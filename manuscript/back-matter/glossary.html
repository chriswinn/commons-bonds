Commons Bonds

Glossary

By Chris Winn

Vocabulary

Abundance Masking
The mechanism by which abundance makes costs invisible to market pricing until scarcity forces them into view. Oxygen feels free because Earth's atmosphere is abundant. Ecological services feel free because ecosystems haven't collapsed yet. Coal-country lung tissue felt free because the affected population was small and dispersed. Abundance Masking names how costs hide; the Commons Inversion Test (CIT) is the methodology that finds what's being hidden. Mechanism + methodology — paired.
Accountability Bond (B)
The framework's action-side variable: the dollars actually forced onto extractors via accountability instruments — reclamation bonds, sovereign wealth funds, carbon taxes, environmental insurance, severance agreements. Accountability Bond (B) is what closes (or attempts to close) the cost-severance gap. The central equation: Cost Severance = Residual Commons Value − Accountability Bond posted. Under honest accounting, B = RCV; current regimes leave B ≪ RCV by orders of magnitude. Decomposes into B = B₁ + B₂: Restitution Bond (B₁), backward-looking, closes the realized-harm gap; Foreclosure Bond (B₂), forward-looking, closes the future-foreclosure gap.
Lineage: Hotelling 1931; Hartwick 1977; Solow 1974; Pigou 1920; Darity & Mullen 2020; Yang & Davis 2021; Balboa 2016; reclamation-bond + financial-assurance literature (OSMRE; GAO-17-207R); restitution-law tradition.
Asymmetric Regret Rule (ARR)
The framework's operational decision rule for cases where future costs can't be precisely quantified. We can always extract later. We can never un-extract. When in doubt, default to the action that's reversible. The rule flips by context: on a comet flyby (access window closes permanently), extraction is the reversible choice. On Europa-class biosphere risk, preservation is the reversible choice. Same rule, opposite verdicts. Specialization of regret-theory + minimax-regret tradition (Savage 1951; Loomes & Sugden 1982) with irreversibility-weighted regret tails.
Lineage: Savage 1951; Loomes & Sugden 1982; Lempert, Popper & Bankes 2003 (Robust Decision Making); Dixit & Pindyck 1994; Rio Declaration 1992 (precautionary tradition).
Commons Bonds
The framework's identifying name and the book's title. The phrase carries three meanings working at once: the formal accountability bonds that would close the gap between what extraction costs and what extractors pay; the relational bonds between people, generations, and place that extraction without accountability frays; and the structural bonds of ecosystems and economies that hold what we share. The book's central question is how to keep all three intact.
Commons Inversion Test (CIT)
The framework's discovery method for identifying which costs of extraction belong in the accounting. The Commons Inversion Test asks of a candidate cost: imagine the commons that grounds it is absent or unconsumed. If the cost becomes visible only against that imagined inversion, it is commons-grounded and admits to the framework's accounting. If the cost would persist regardless, it is intrinsic to the activity, not to the commons-state, and is excluded.

Two sub-forms make the routing explicit:

  • Commons-Absence Inversion (CAI) — imagine the commons not existing. Operational question: what would it take to replace the commons? (Paradigm: oxygen in space; McDowell habitability destroyed by coal extraction.)
  • Commons-Consumption Inversion (CCI) — imagine the commons not being consumed. Operational question: what could the commons do if not drawn down? (Paradigm: commute time returned; forest uncut; medical-commons of dying-with-dignity.)

CIT is the operational protocol that detects the abundance-masking phenomenon Theorem 10.3 establishes analytically.

Lineage: Ostrom 1990; Hess & Ostrom 2007 (knowledge commons); counterfactual reasoning + minimax-regret (Lewis 1973; Savage 1951; Loomes & Sugden 1982).
Cost (Cᵢ)
The framework's atomic unit of measurement — a specific cost term that gets summed into the framework's accounting. Each Cᵢ is a named cost (Direct Health Cost, Foreclosure Cost, Community Disruption Cost, Lineage Labor Cost, Knowledge and Cultural Cost, Political Capture Cost, Lifetime Survival Cost, Actuarial Risk Cost, Mission Failure Reserve, Community Transition Reserve, Ecological Carrying Cost, etc.) that the Commons Inversion Test has identified for a specific extraction. The class is open: every extraction surfaces its own Cᵢ set; the methodology is universal, the components are case-specific.
Cost Severance
The mechanism by which extraction separates value capture from cost bearing. When a company extracts value from a commons — a community, an ecosystem, a future generation — and the costs of that extraction stay with the source rather than the extractor, the result is cost severance. The framework's term builds on the familiar idea of employment severance, where a company that sheds a worker pays that worker for the cut tie. Cost severance is the same logic applied to commons extraction — except the payment architecture is missing.
Why “severance,” not the alternatives: externality names a cost that falls outside the transaction, but not who now carries it or that an accounting architecture is missing; displacement names that the cost moved, but not that it was cut loose from a value-capturer who declined to absorb it; transfer names an active routing, whereas severance names what happens when no routing exists; appropriation moralizes a structural mechanism into an actor’s intent, whereas cost severance operates regardless of intent, by the architecture of extraction-without-accountability. Severance names what the others do not: the cut tie, and the missing payment for it. (Term-choice rationale relocated from Ch 2, 2026-06-05.)
Cost Severance Damages (CSD)
The framework's measurement instrument for already-realized human harm from cost severance — health damages, intergenerational disadvantage, community-level suffering, foreclosed life-trajectories. Cost Severance Damages (CSD) is backward-looking; it counts what extraction has already done to people. Companion to Residual Commons Value, which is forward-looking and counts what extraction forecloses for future generations. Together: CSD prices what's been taken from people; RCV prices what's been taken from the resource.
Lineage: Darity & Mullen 2020; VSL/DALY methodology; Chetty et al. (intergenerational mobility); Bullard 1990 (environmental justice).
CS = RCV − B equation
The framework's flagship equation. Cost Severance equals Residual Commons Value minus Accountability Bond — what's truly owed minus what's actually paid. When B = RCV, severance is zero (honest accounting). When B is below RCV, the difference is what gets passed to communities, ecosystems, or future generations. Norway's sovereign wealth fund is the standard real-world example of B approaching RCV; the American extraction economy operates with B ≪ RCV. Three words, one operator — the book's central assertion in compressed form.
Externality Tail (E)
The damage that lingers after extraction stops. A substitute for coal doesn't clean up the mine. A substitute for oil doesn't remove carbon from the atmosphere. Externality Tail is the framework's name for costs that persist independently of whether substitutes appear — climate damage from past emissions, mine-tailings dispersion, biological extinction. Mathematically: E(R, t) — a time-indexed function that runs on its own clock. Specializes Pigou's externality concept (1920) for post-extraction persistence.

"Tail" here is temporal-persistence (the damage's long tail in time), distinct from statistics-distribution-tail conventions (extreme-value-theory; fat-tailed distributions per Mandelbrot 1963; Taleb 2007). E(R, t) is a deterministic flow function, not a probability density's tail region.

Lineage: Pigou 1920; Nordhaus & Boyer 2000 (DICE model); Stern Review 2006.
Foreclosure Bond (B₂)
Forward-looking accountability instrument — sovereign wealth funds operationalizing Hartwick's rule (Norway is the standard existing example), reclamation bonds, Environmental Impact Bonds, Pigouvian carbon taxes, cap-and-trade schemes, decommissioning bonds for oil and gas. Foreclosure Bond (B₂) closes the residual-commons-value gap: dollars forced from extractors to internalize the permanent foreclosure of future-generation options. Term-pair coherence with Foreclosure Cost (C₁) — what the bond internalizes. Pairs with B₁ Restitution Bond (backward-looking) in the framework's two-instrument architecture.
Lineage: Hotelling 1931; Hartwick 1977; Solow 1974; Pigou 1920; Yang & Davis 2021; Balboa 2016; reclamation-bond + decommissioning-bond + Environmental-Impact-Bond literature.
Hotelling Identity
The framework's mathematical bridge to standard resource economics. Hotelling 1931 priced the rent that accrues to a private extractor as a finite resource depletes — the gap between price and extraction cost, rising over time. The framework's Hotelling Identity says: that same rent, looked at from the commons' side, is the cost severance per unit. Per-unit Residual Commons Value minus per-unit standard Hotelling rent equals per-unit Cost Severance. Hotelling's math; framework's commons-side reading.
Lineage: Hotelling 1931; Hartwick 1977; Solow 1974; Pearce & Atkinson 1993; Daly stock-flow accounting.
intergenerational cost severance (lowercase prose phrase)
The intergenerational subtype of Cost Severance — value captured now, costs borne by future generations. Used as a lowercase descriptive phrase composing the framework's existing "intergenerational" adjective and "cost severance" mechanism term. Not capitalized as a proper-noun compound: capitalizing the phrase would reintroduce the jargon-inflation problem that adjective-precision retired the older "Temporal Cost Severance" term over.
Slots into established intergenerational-equity discourse: Brown Weiss 1989; Rawls; Parfit; Broome; Stern; Nordhaus.
Intergenerational Option Value
The value of preserving the option that future generations might discover or develop a use for a resource we currently extract. Intergenerational Option Value specializes real-options theory (Henry 1974; Arrow & Fisher 1974) to the temporal scope where future generations cannot bid in present markets. When extraction is irreversible and substitute discovery uncertain, preserving optionality has positive value even if no substitute is yet known. Ch 4 The Existence Proof uses this concept to establish that future generations have value-claims on present extraction.
Lineage: Henry 1974 (quasi-option value); Arrow & Fisher 1974 (uncertainty + irreversibility + welfare); Dixit & Pindyck 1994 (real-options theory); Howarth & Norgaard 1990, 1992 (intergenerational-equity option-value treatment); Brown Weiss 1989 (international-law tradition of intergenerational equity).
Intergenerational Pricing Gap (IPG)
The ratio of true intergenerational cost (RCV) to market price. IPG = 33 means today's price covers about 3% of the cost being passed forward. A market-pricing-gap diagnostic — distinct from Cost Severance, which measures what accountability instruments fail to cover. IPG > 1 is architectural for extraction; the empirical question is the magnitude. McDowell's IPG ≈ 33 is the book's compression — readers remember a single number.
Residual Commons Value (RCV)
The framework's central quantification — the true intergenerational cost of an extraction that remains unpriced by markets, accountability bonds, and standard externality instruments combined. Residual Commons Value is what's left over when you account for everything ordinary accounting captures and ask: what did this extraction actually cost the future, the community, the ecosystem? It pairs with the accountability bond in the framework's central equation: Cost Severance = Residual Commons Value − Bond posting. Formally: RCV(R, t₀) = ∫[t₀ to ∞] { [1 − S(t|t₀)] · U(R, t, Q(t)) + E(R, t) } · D(t, t₀) dt.

The framework uses RCV for Residual Commons Value exclusively. Three real-world acronym collisions to disambiguate at first encounter: Ranked-Choice Voting (civic-engagement / electoral-policy); Replacement Cost Value (insurance-policy); Risk Capital Value (banking-regulatory). None of these usages fits the integrand-and-integral form, so the framework's reading is unambiguous after first formula encounter.

Lineage: Pigou 1920; Stern Review 2006; Nordhaus & Boyer 2000; Brown Weiss 1989; Howarth & Norgaard 1990, 1992.
Restitution Bond (B₁)
Backward-looking accountability instrument — restitution payments, reparations programs, welfare-state instruments (universal healthcare, public pensions), environmental-justice damages compensation, toxic-tort litigation, truth-and-reconciliation processes. Restitution Bond (B₁) closes the cost-severance damages gap: dollars that flow to people, communities, and ecosystems already harmed by extraction. Pairs with B₂ Foreclosure Bond (forward-looking) in the framework's two-instrument architecture. Underdeveloped territory globally; varies by welfare-state design.
Lineage: Darity & Mullen 2020 (reparations economics); Bullard 1990 (environmental-justice damages); Esping-Andersen 1990 (welfare-state design); restitution-law tradition; toxic-tort + class-action damages tradition.
severed cost (lowercase prose phrase)
The result of cost severance — a specific cost cut loose from the transaction that created it and transferred to a third party who did not capture the value. The Black Lung Trust Fund's $4.6 billion deficit is a severed cost. The 13-year life expectancy gap in McDowell County is a severed cost. Severed cost is the framework's adoption target — the phrase the book hopes a labor lawyer or judge will use in court without needing it explained.
spatial cost severance (lowercase prose phrase)
The geographic subtype of Cost Severance — value extracted from an area disperses to distant consumers while costs (health, environmental, community) concentrate locally where extraction occurred. McDowell County's 13-year life expectancy gap is the human face of spatial cost severance. Used as a lowercase descriptive phrase, not capitalized as a proper-noun compound. Distinct from the Spatial commons category (one of the ten kinds of commons in the framework's case work); the two have separate operational meaning.
Substitutability Function (S)
A probability that says, for any future time, whether an adequate substitute for the resource being extracted will exist. Substitutability Function S answers the question every foreclosure calculation needs: how likely is it that the people of 2200 will have a non-petroleum way to do what petroleum currently does? When S = 1, foreclosure cost vanishes (a perfect substitute exists). When S = 0, foreclosure cost is the full intergenerational claim. Most cases sit between, and the function tracks where.
Lineage: Hicks 1932 (elasticity of substitution); Dixit & Pindyck 1994 (real-options theory); intertemporal substitution in macro theory.
Three Ways of Counting
The framework's discipline for pricing Residual Commons Value: instead of a single point estimate, three methods approach the same identity, CS = RCV − B, from different sides. Three Ways of Counting runs Replacement Cost (what would it cost to replace what was lost?) and Scarcity-Adjusted Option Value (what is the option value of leaving it in situ?) as the two independent estimators of RCV, and reads the Realized Bond (what internalization actually happened: fund accumulation, taxation captured, restitution paid, restraint exercised) as a revealed floor under both, because a rational polity internalizes no more than it values. Where the estimators converge above the floor, the answer is robust. Where they diverge, the parameter pulling them apart names the real argument.
Lineage: Costanza et al. 1997 (Method 1 replacement-cost); Hartwick 1977 + Solow 1974 (Method 2 realized-Bond reading); Dixit & Pindyck 1994 (Method 3 scarcity-adjusted option value).
value extraction (lowercase prose phrase)
The act by which value is separated from its source — a community, an ecosystem, or a future generation — and taken by someone who did not produce it. Value extraction is the causal event that produces cost severance: the same act that captures the value severs the cost from the capturer and leaves it with the source. The framework's causal chain runs Value Extraction → Cost Severance → Severed Cost. The framework extends Mariana Mazzucato's value-extraction critique (Mazzucato 2018) — developed there for rent-seeking in financial capitalism — into the physical-resource domain. Echoes David Harvey's accumulation by dispossession (Harvey 2003) rendered in accessible accounting-register language.
Lineage: Mazzucato 2018, The Value of Everything; Harvey 2003, The New Imperialism.

Architecture

Two-Instrument Architecture

The framework measures two orthogonal aspects of an extraction event:

  • CSD (Cost Severance Damages) — backward-looking; realized human harm; reparations-economics-grounded; pairs with B₁ (Restitution Bond).
  • RCV (Residual Commons Value) — forward-looking; resource permanent-foreclosure; triangulated estimation; pairs with B₂ (Foreclosure Bond).

Total Extraction Damages = CSD + uncaptured RCV. You could fully compensate every Appalachian descendant — make them whole on health, education, housing, income — and the coal is still gone. Two distinct measurements of the same extraction event from different directions.

Four Gates (admission apparatus for Cᵢ)

Four conditions a candidate cost must pass to be admitted to RCV as a Cᵢ. Naming asymmetry honors the structural distinction: Gate 1 (CIT) is a TEST (procedural methodology); Gates 2–4 are CONDITIONS (static mathematical properties).

  1. CIT (Commons Inversion Test) — content gate; is the cost commons-dependent? (Two sub-forms: Commons-Absence Inversion + Commons-Consumption Inversion.)
  2. Units Consistency — mathematical condition; cost in dollars × time-domain commensurable with rest of RCV integrand.
  3. Boundedness — mathematical condition; cost integrand converges (Theorem 10.4 establishes the convergence conditions; active on climate / intergenerational cases requiring discount-rate convention).
  4. Independence — mathematical condition; cost not redundantly correlated with already-admitted Cᵢ (no double-counting).

Commons categories — 10 examples

The framework's case work surfaces ten kinds of commons across 18 cases. They are examples, not a closed list — different political traditions disagree about what counts as a commons, and the framework's methodology works within any of them. The framework's universality is about method (CIT applies anywhere extraction operates on commons-like resources), not about enumeration coverage.

"Abundance" is preserved as the state-name for each commons (abundant vs scarce — the state CIT inverts), distinct from the commons category label.

  1. Habitability — life-supporting conditions of place. Paradigm: McDowell coal extraction stripping clean air, healthy bodies, intact community life-support.
  2. Spatial — geographic shared infrastructure (mobility access; public space; geographic commons). Paradigm: Chesapeake Bay watermen's geographic commons.
  3. Temporal — shared time-budget; intergenerational time-commons. Paradigm: Social Security cohort-succession commons.
  4. Institutional — governance + legal architecture as shared infrastructure. Paradigm: 2008 financial-crisis institutional commons damage.
  5. Kindred — kinship + chosen-family + specific-attachment-bond infrastructure. Paradigm: opioid Appalachia family-destruction.
  6. Ecosystem — ecological services + biodiversity (Ostrom commons-governance territory). Paradigm: Deepwater Horizon Gulf ecosystem.
  7. Political — political agency + voice + representation as shared infrastructure. Paradigm: political capture cost in extraction industries.
  8. Cohesion — civic / community / social-infrastructure binding (Klinenberg Palaces for the People territory).
  9. Epistemic — knowledge + epistemic-authority commons (Hess & Ostrom Understanding Knowledge as a Commons 2007 territory). Paradigm: opioid case truthful-info-suppression by Sackler/Purdue.
  10. Autonomy — autonomy-supporting infrastructure (Anderson Private Government 2017 — civic-republican framing). The most politically contested example; the framework treats it as a commons in traditions where it operates as one, and remains agnostic in traditions where it does not.

The framework doesn't argue that autonomy is universally a commons, or that habitability is, or that time is. It argues that when any of these operate as commons in a given society — shared, depended-on, collectively enabling — extraction can sever cost from that commons, and this methodology measures the severance. Different political traditions have different commitments. The framework works within any of those commitments. What it doesn't accept is extraction without accountability, regardless of which commons is being extracted from.
