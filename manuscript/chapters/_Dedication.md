10. THE BOOK NEEDS A DEDICATION THAT DOES WORK

Consider dedicating the book to your grandfather at Langley — the model maker in the wind tunnels. Not because it's sentimental (though it is) but because it frames the entire book as a continuation of his work: building instruments that measure what the real world actually does, regardless of what we wish it would do.

Or dedicate it to the McDowell County miner and the Chesapeake waterman — the people whose costs were severed. Or to your son — the next generation, the one whose future the model prices.

The dedication is the first thing a reader sees. It sets the emotional register for everything that follows.
