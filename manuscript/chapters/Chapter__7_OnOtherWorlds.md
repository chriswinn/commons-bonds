# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*




## Chapter 7: On Other Worlds

---

Imagine, for a moment, that the argument this book has been making is wrong — not in its moral framing, not in its sympathy for the communities the framework is meant to protect, but in its technical claim. The claim is that extraction is systematically underpriced because no existing framework captures the full intergenerational cost of foreclosure. Perhaps, a careful reader might say, this is a feature of the particular political economy of extraction on Earth — the specific way lobbyists and governments and industry groups have colluded to keep certain costs off the books — rather than a structural fact about how resource pricing works anywhere.

If that is right, the framework is a political critique of a particular earthly arrangement. It is not a general theory of how rational agents price resources. And it deserves to be read as the former rather than the latter.

There is one way to settle the question. Take the framework off Earth and see whether it still produces the answers a thoughtful planner would recognize as correct.

This chapter does that. The thought experiment that organizes it is a small habitat on Mars, where ten thousand people are trying to make sensible decisions about the water in the ground beneath them. The chapter then runs the same framework through a series of increasingly different scenarios — asteroid iron, lunar helium, comet volatiles, the potential ice of Europa, the hypothetical ecosystems of a distant exoplanet, and a self-imposed commute lease closer to home — to see whether the answers stay sensible as the parameters change.

A note on the choice of setting before the chapter proceeds. Mars is used here because it is the off-world body human civilization has been actively trying to reach for half a century — Mars 3, Viking I and II, Pathfinder, Spirit and Opportunity, Phoenix, Curiosity, InSight, Perseverance, with sample return and crewed proposals discussed for years — which means the parameter ranges for resource extraction in Martian regolith are extensively measured rather than entirely speculative. The thought experiment is not, however, Mars-specific. The framework's behavior on a small Mars habitat is the framework's behavior on any small bounded off-world habitat where survival depends on a small number of finite local resources, whether that habitat eventually exists on Mars, on the Moon, on a free-flying space station, on a rotating asteroid platform, or in a configuration none of us has yet imagined. Mars is the setting because the parameter ranges are real enough to be defensible. The conclusions are not Mars-conclusions. They are bounded-system-with-distance-compressed conclusions, and they hold wherever a habitat fits that description.

The claim the chapter establishes is a strong one. The framework is not a political argument about Earth's particular arrangements. It is a general instrument for pricing extraction from any commons, in any context, under any parameter set, and it produces different answers for different contexts precisely because the parameters drive the output. It works on Mars, on asteroids, on comets. It works, with honest limits that the framework itself names, on worlds whose biospheres we do not fully understand.

And it reveals, along the way, something about why cost severance operates the way it does on Earth — which turns out to be a function of a variable the framework quietly relies on and this chapter will make explicit.

### The habitat

The habitat houses ten thousand people. It is established, self-sustaining, several decades into its existence. It relies on three known subsurface water ice deposits accessible from its habitation zone. At current consumption — drinking, agriculture, oxygen production through electrolysis, industrial processing — the deposits represent roughly a fifty-year supply. The administrator of the habitat's resources has been asked by a trading consortium for permission to accelerate extraction. The request is profitable in the near term. It would deliver a return on the consortium's investment within five years. It would also reduce the habitat's remaining water supply to something closer to a twenty-year horizon.

The administrator has to decide.

She is not, in her situation, burdened by the philosophical debates that have structured terrestrial resource economics for a century. She is not wondering about the social cost of carbon or the appropriate discount rate for intergenerational transfers. She is wondering whether it is acceptable to reduce her habitat's remaining water supply by sixty percent in exchange for a financial return to a specific extraction consortium over the next decade. She knows what happens to a habitat that runs out of water. She also knows that the consortium, having captured its return, will be operating under somewhat different incentives thirty years from now.

Walk the residual commons value framework through her situation, term by term.

Substitutability. The probability that an adequate substitute will exist at any given future time. For drinking, for agriculture, for electrolysis into oxygen, water has essentially no substitute at the chemistries involved. The administrator cannot run the habitat on ammonia. She cannot breathe synthesized oxygen produced by some non-water chemistry at any pace that would keep the life-support systems functional. The substitutability function for Mars water is approximately zero for the applications that matter most — the ones that keep her inhabitants alive. She can, in the very long run, imagine atmospheric water harvesting, cometary water imports, or large-scale synthesis from rarer feedstocks. None of these is currently operational at the scale the habitat requires. None is on a development trajectory that guarantees availability within the extraction horizon being considered.

Stock-dependent utility. The value of each remaining unit as the stock declines. Water is abundant to an uninhabited Mars. Water is not abundant to a habitat of ten thousand people with three known deposits. Each unit of the remaining stock becomes more valuable as the stock depletes — approaching, as Q shrinks, the value of survival itself. The utility function is not smooth here. It is a ramp that steepens as the stock approaches the inhabitants' survival threshold, and then, at some point not known exactly in advance, it becomes a cliff.

Externality tail. The ongoing damage that extraction imposes after it ends. Extraction of subsurface ice risks contamination of the remaining stock with drilling chemicals. It risks destabilizing the geological formations holding adjacent deposits. It risks venting volatiles that the habitat's atmospheric management systems must absorb. Beyond these direct externalities, there is a deeper one. Mars may harbor subsurface microbial life. If it does, extraction that contaminates or destroys the subsurface hydrosphere may end the only confirmed extraterrestrial biosphere in the known universe — a loss that cannot be priced in dollars because it involves scientific and philosophical values that are not denominated in any currency.

Discount factor. The weight placed on future value relative to present value. On Earth, economists argue about the right discount rate for decades without resolution. On Mars, the discussion is different. The inhabitants' survival is contingent, in a very direct way, on resources the administrator manages today. Discounting fifty-year horizons at the rates typically used in terrestrial cost-benefit analysis is suicidal. The future is not a discountable abstraction; it is the same population's own next decade of continued existence. The discount factor approaches zero — which is to say, the future is weighted almost as heavily as the present, because the same people expect to be alive for both.

Every term is at an extreme value. The substitutability is near zero. The utility is life-or-death. The stock is small and fixed. The externalities are potentially lethal to the inhabitants themselves or, at the outer edge, civilizationally significant. The discount factor is essentially unity.

The residual commons value is extraordinarily high, and the administrator has not even reached the end of the calculation. The model's recommendation is not subtle. It says: reserve the deposits, ration aggressively, price each unit at a progressive scale that rises as stocks decline, invest heavily in recycling and closed-loop water management, and under no circumstances permit the consortium to accelerate extraction for near-term financial return. The math says the return the consortium is offering is trivial against the foreclosure cost the inhabitants would bear, and bear themselves, in the decades after the contract completes.

Any thoughtful administrator of any inhabited place on any world would recognize this as obviously correct. That is exactly the point.

### Earth is also a bounded system

Now take the model, with every parameter exactly where it sat for Mars water, and change one number at a time.

Increase the stock. The deposit becomes larger. The stock-dependent utility pressure drops. At some stock size, a unit of extraction no longer meaningfully reduces what remains. The model's extraction recommendation loosens.

Increase the substitutability. The resource becomes one for which alternatives exist. The foreclosure cost component drops — because future generations who need the resource can turn to the substitutes the model is accounting for. The model's extraction recommendation loosens further.

Decrease the externality tail. The extraction process becomes cleaner, the remaining stock uncontaminated, the ecological damage minimal. The model's extraction recommendation loosens again.

Increase the discount factor. The future becomes less weighted relative to the present. The model's extraction recommendation loosens further still.

At some combination of these changes, the model's recommendation converges on market pricing. The resource is abundant, substitutable, clean to extract, and the future can be reasonably discounted. Honest price approximately equals market price. There is no meaningful cost severance. Extract freely.

At another combination, the model's recommendation diverges wildly from market pricing. The resource is scarce, substitution is uncertain, the externality tail is long, and the future is weighted heavily because of some structural feature — ecological, intergenerational, existential — that prevents heavy discounting. Honest price vastly exceeds market price. Cost severance is large. Extract only with extreme accountability, or do not extract at all.

The model does not change in any of this. The parameters change. The recommendations change because the parameters change. That is what a properly calibrated pricing instrument does.

Earth, it turns out, is not categorically different from Mars. Earth is the same kind of system with different parameters. The stock of coal is much larger than Mars's water ice, but it is not infinite. The substitutability of coal for electricity is today much higher than Mars water for drinking, but the substitutability of specific hydrocarbon chemistries for specific industrial applications is lower than a casual reader might expect. The externality tail of coal extraction includes black lung, acid mine drainage, and atmospheric carbon — externalities that persist long after the mine closes. The discount factor for intergenerational carbon harm is the exact subject of decades of economic debate whose underlying question is whether the children of 2200 count as much as the shareholders of 2030.

That debate has structure worth naming. The 2006 Stern Review argued for a near-zero pure rate of time preference on the explicit ethical premise that future generations cannot be discounted simply for being further away in time; William Nordhaus's DICE model and its successors used a higher discount rate calibrated to observed market returns, on the methodological premise that climate policy must be evaluated against what the same capital would have been worth had it been deployed productively elsewhere. The gap between these two approaches — roughly 1.4 percent versus 4 percent in the original exchanges — translates, in present-value terms, into trillions of dollars of difference in what counts as the right level of mitigation effort today. John Hartwick's 1977 rule, which says that the rents from exhaustible-resource extraction should be invested in reproducible capital sufficient to leave future generations no worse off, sits adjacent to both: it is the operationalization of the intergenerational-equity premise Stern's framing depends on, and it is the kind of accountability discipline this framework's restitution-bond logic instantiates. The point of citing the lineage is not to settle the discount-rate debate inside this chapter. The point is that the question — what weight do future people carry against present people — is not avoidable. Mars makes the answer easier because the future people in question are the same habitat's own next generation, and the inhabitants cannot pretend otherwise. Earth makes it harder because the future people in question are strangers, and therefore easier to discount. The framework's discipline, here as elsewhere, is to refuse the easier answer where the easier answer is structurally tempting but logically unsupported.

Every parameter that drove the Mars administrator's decision exists on Earth. The values are different. The structure is the same. The instrument that told her to preserve Martian water is the same instrument that tells us, when applied honestly to Appalachian coal, that the resource has been severely mispriced for a century.

This is the universality claim the chapter is establishing. The framework is not an Earth-specific critique. It is a general theory of pricing resources, tested at the limits where the parameters are stark enough to make the correct answer unambiguous, and applied on Earth where the parameters are moderate enough to have allowed centuries of systematic mispricing without anyone being forced to notice.

### Running the test

The chapter's function is to take the framework through enough different scenarios that the universality claim becomes credible rather than asserted. What follows is a series of extractions, run quickly, with the framework's recommendation noted in each case and the parameters that drove it.

Iron from an uninhabited asteroid. The resource is abundant across thousands of similar bodies in the accessible solar system. Substitutability is high; iron from other asteroids, iron from Earth, iron from recycled orbital structures are all available. The externality tail is minimal — no atmosphere to pollute, no ecosystem to disrupt, no community displaced by the mining operation. Stock-dependent utility is low because the stock is effectively unlimited. The model's output is that residual commons value (RCV) approaches the market price of the extracted material. Cost severance, under any reasonable accountability bond for orbital debris management and energy accounting, is near zero. Extract freely. This is the lowest-RCV extraction the framework can recognize.

Helium-3 embedded in lunar regolith. The resource has essentially no current industrial use. Its potential value depends on whether fusion reactors built on a specific deuterium-helium-3 cycle ever achieve net energy gain — a technology horizon that has moved repeatedly over decades and whose arrival, if it arrives, cannot be dated. Substitutability is the inverse question: for the hypothetical future fusion application, helium-3 has no substitute by definition. Stock is very large (the Moon's regolith contains roughly a million tons), but extraction is energy-intensive and disturbs the lunar surface in ways that would be difficult to reverse. The model's answer here is interesting. It does not pretend to know the probability of fusion viability. It observes that the insurance cost of preserving lunar helium-3 is nearly zero — not mining the Moon costs nothing — and that the Asymmetric Regret Rule applies cleanly. If we preserve and fusion never works, we have lost nothing. If we extract and fusion later becomes viable, we have lost potentially the most important fuel source in the solar system. The model says: preserve. Wait. Reassess as the technology matures.

Common regolith on the Moon, for construction of lunar habitation. The same celestial body, a different resource. The regolith is abundant beyond any meaningful depletion horizon — the Moon is made of it. Substitutability is high for construction applications. The externality tail is modest, limited to local dust management and operational contamination. Stock-dependent utility is negligible. The model's answer on this resource is the opposite of its answer on lunar helium-3: extract freely, manage dust, proceed. The same location produces different recommendations for different resources because the parameters are different for different resources. A framework that said "preserve everything on the Moon" or "extract everything on the Moon" would be useless. The framework's capacity to differentiate within a single location is what makes it an instrument rather than an ideology.

Volatiles from a long-period comet during flyby. The situation inverts. The resource is accessible only during a short extraction window — weeks to months — after which the comet departs for millennia. The externality tail is near zero; no life, no community, no atmosphere disturbed. Substitutability is moderate; other comets exist. The asymmetric regret flips here. The cost of not extracting, once the window closes, is the permanent loss of access to this specific comet's material. The cost of extracting is modest. The model says: extract aggressively during the window. This demonstrates that the framework is not conservation-biased; it is parameter-sensitive. When the parameters favor extraction, the model recommends extraction.

Subsurface ocean of Europa. Here the model encounters its boundary. The scientific and moral value of potentially destroying an independent biosphere, if one exists in Europa's ocean, is genuinely not priceable in dollars. The externality term cannot be filled in with a number. The model does not pretend to fill it in. It says, honestly, that it cannot price this particular externality, and that the Asymmetric Regret Rule therefore dominates: the irreversibility of losing the only other known biosphere is absolute, the cost of waiting and studying is time, and the decision to extract should be deferred until the scientific question can be answered. This is not a failure of the framework. It is the framework behaving correctly — naming a limit where a limit exists rather than manufacturing a price to maintain the appearance of completeness.

Rare earth elements near an inhabited Mars habitat. This is the scenario that reveals the deepest structural insight the chapter has to offer. The inhabitants mine rare earths locally to manufacture electronics rather than importing from Earth. The substitutability question becomes supply-chain dependent — moderate if Earth imports are reliable, near zero if they are not. The utility is high for strategic independence. And here is the crucial part: the externality tail falls on the inhabitants themselves. Dust contaminates the habitat's air filtration. Chemical waste must be managed in a closed system where there is no downstream somewhere-else. Worker health affects the habitat's own limited medical resources. The inhabitants breathe their own emissions.

Cost severance, under these conditions, collapses toward zero. Not because the costs are small, but because the extractor and the affected community are the same people. There is no one to sever the cost to. The model's recommendation is that extraction, if it occurs, must be priced at its full internal cost — because the inhabitants will bear the full internal cost whether or not it is priced honestly. What the inhabitants actually pay and what the extraction actually costs converge automatically.

This is the insight worth pausing on — and one that extends to Earth in ways the off-world scenarios haven't yet shown.

A self-imposed commute lease on Earth. The case has no Mars and no asteroid in it. A person living in suburban Maryland signs a lease that commits them to a five-hour daily commute to their office in Washington, D.C. There is no extracting corporation; there is no community being displaced; the cost-bearer made the decision themselves, with available information about the destination and the travel time. The framework computes the case the same way it computes asteroid iron and McDowell coal: five hours per day, roughly two hundred and fifty work days per year, multiplied by the years committed — thousands of hours of life. Against any defensible valuation — lost wages, lost family time, lost rest, depleted attention, accumulated chronic stress — the dollar figure of what was actually being signed is many multiples of the twenty thousand dollars per year saved on cheaper Maryland housing. The mechanism by which cost was severed from the decision wasn't a corporation extracting from the lease-signer. It was past-self under-pricing future-self's time, through temporal asymmetry (past-self deciding for present-self and future-self), informational asymmetry (the actual two-and-a-half-hour each-way reality not fully internalized at decision time even though the data was available), normative routinization (long American commutes culturally normalized as the way these things go), and abundance masking (hours of life feeling unlimited until the math collapses the illusion). Four mechanisms operating intra-personally across time. The framework's Commons Inversion Test, applied at decision time, would have surfaced the actual price of the lease before signing. Same instrument; no bad actor required; structural test the same.

What the cross-scenario sweep makes visible — from asteroid iron through Mars rare earths to a Maryland commute lease — is that the framework is a pricing instrument that operates across the entire range of cases where costs are systematically under-priced, regardless of whether there is a counterparty doing the under-pricing on purpose.

### What cost severance requires

Cost severance, the framework has argued from the beginning, is the act of cutting the cost of extraction loose from the profit of extraction and transferring it to people who had no say in the transaction. The transfer requires a transferee — people who can be made to bear what the extractor does not. It also requires a structural arrangement that lets value flow in one direction while cost stays in the other. The framework names six common patterns through which this arrangement gets produced, alone or in combination. These six are illustrative, not exhaustive — observed empirical examples drawn from the cases this book examines, in the same spirit as Ostrom's eight design principles, Sen's ten capabilities, and Rawls's primary goods. The structural test is the operational arrangement itself, not whether any specific named pattern is present; readers applying the methodology to their own cases may surface additional patterns or refine these. An extraction practice that addresses each named pattern with surface-level interventions has not addressed cost severance — it has merely simulated addressing it. The chapter's immune-response stress test, later in this chapter, returns to that simulated-reattachment dynamic specifically.

**Geographic distance.** When a ton of coal is extracted from McDowell County and burned to generate electricity in Chicago, the value is captured by shareholders and consumers a thousand miles of highway and regulatory separation away from the people who pay in their lungs and their watersheds.

**Temporal asymmetry.** The intergenerational case — costs borne by grandchildren no one alive today has met — is the same structural arrangement at a different scale. The extractor is not far in space but far in time; the cost-bearer is unborn and cannot bid in the transaction.

**Informational asymmetry.** The 2008 housing collapse is the clearest exemplar. Bankers and underwater homeowners shared a country, a legal system, often a metro region; what severed costs from value was not distance but what one side knew, when, and what the other side did not. The cost-bearer cannot refuse what they cannot see in time.

**Power asymmetry.** Slavery and forced labor sever costs from value through coercion alone. Enslavers and enslaved often shared the same plantation, with eye-contact-distance proximity; the pyramids' conscripted laborers worked within sight of the pharaohs whose monuments they built. Geographic distance was zero. Informational asymmetry was minimal — both parties knew what the work cost. What severed the cost was that the bearer could not refuse. The pattern operates wherever economic systems have no use for the appearance of consent.

**Normative routinization.** When cost-bearing has gone on long enough to become accepted as a baseline — the "cost of doing business," the routine subsidy that everyone knows is there but no one prices — severance can operate with full information and no formal coercion, because the cultural infrastructure has done the work that distance and force did in earlier configurations. The miner accepts the lung damage as part of the job. The town accepts the eventual mine closure as the way these things go. The consumer accepts cheap electricity as priced honestly because the cost has never appeared on any invoice they have seen.

**Abundance Masking.** When a resource appears unlimited, no one prices it — and the costs it bears can flow continuously without anyone computing them. Air on Earth is the paradigmatic case: no one prices the air we breathe; the abundance illusion is total. The same illusion has applied historically to ocean fisheries before collapse, to aquifer water in the Ogallala before drawdown became visible, to the atmosphere's carbon-absorbing capacity, to soil's regenerative capacity. The framework's mathematics formalizes this in Theorem 10.3 (Abundance Masking) of the Tech Appendix; the framework's Commons Inversion Test is the apparatus for detecting it.

The framework's choice of *abundance masking* as the name for the pattern by which structural-finiteness is hidden by a surface-experience of abundance warrants a defense against the closest casual synonyms. *Abundance illusion* — which the prose above occasionally uses interchangeably — implies a perceptual error: a viewer sees abundance because their perception is mistaken, and the corrective is to view more carefully. The framework's claim is structurally different. The abundance is real. The air over McDowell County in 1960 was abundant; the atmosphere did absorb the combusted carbon for two centuries without observable saturation; the Ogallala aquifer was abundant in the 1940s when central-pivot irrigation began drawing from it. What the abundance hid was not its existence but its finiteness — and the hiding was structural, not perceptual. Industrial architectures were built on the working assumption of unlimited atmospheric absorbing capacity; agricultural architectures were built on the assumption of unlimited aquifer recharge. The mask is the architecture, not the perception. *Mask* is the right word for what an architecture does to underlying structure — it covers without removing; it hides without dissolving; it produces a surface experience that operates the same way for every observer regardless of viewing care. Other names were available and each fails for a specific reason. *Abundance illusion* misroutes the diagnosis from architecture to perception. *Abundance-as-cover* is mechanically accurate but loses the active sense — masking is a thing the architecture does; cover is a passive state. *False abundance* implies the abundance was fake, which is empirically wrong — the abundance was real but local and time-bounded. *Abundance concealment* implies an actor doing the concealing, which moralizes a structural mechanism. The Mars-habitat thought-experiment the framework's earlier prose constructed demonstrates the structural feature directly: abundance masking collapses *by construction* in a closed habitat where every cubic meter of air and every gallon of water is enumerated. What collapses by physical construction cannot be a perceptual error; perception does not collapse by closed-system architecture. Structural features do. *Masking* names the structural feature; *illusion* names something close-but-not-the-same the framework specifically does not claim.

On Mars, in a habitat where ten thousand people share the same atmosphere, none of the six patterns can produce concealment. Geographic distance collapses by construction. Informational asymmetry collapses — the closed system is small, feedback is direct, no party can know less than the others know. Power asymmetry collapses — the administrator's family is among the cost-bearers, with no coercive infrastructure available to deploy. Normative routinization has not had time to develop in a 10,000-person system. Temporal asymmetry remains technically available — present-self can under-price future-self — but the closed feedback loop makes the consequences visible immediately, in a way the same mechanism on Earth would conceal for years or generations. And abundance masking collapses *by construction* — there is no possible illusion of unlimited atmosphere or water in a closed habitat. Every cubic meter of air and every gallon of water is enumerated. Every breath and every drink visibly counts against finite stock.

Severance is therefore not impossible on Mars. It is impossible to *hide*. The administrator who under-prices for short-term gain still produces severance against future-self and future-population — but the closed feedback loop makes the cost visible immediately, and the framework's logic requires that what is visible be priced. What Mars exposes by removing concealment is not just the scarcity of specific resources but the entire ambient abundance Earth provides. A Mars resident visiting Earth wouldn't see Earth as a place with abundant resources. They'd see free air. Free water falling from the sky on a regular schedule. Free temperature regulation. Free solar irradiance, continuously and without maintenance. Free gravitational stability provided by the Moon, including the tidal cycle that drives nutrient circulation through the oceans. Free magnetic-field shielding against radiation that would otherwise strip the atmosphere. Free atmospheric filtering of ultraviolet wavelengths that would otherwise sterilize the surface. All the conditions Earth provides as ambient — the conditions a closed-habitat resident would calculate maintenance budgets for. Earth's ambient abundance is the dominant cost-hiding apparatus on this planet, and the framework's Commons Inversion Test is the tool for detecting which parts of it are actually finite-and-being-drawn-down.

On Earth, the compression isn't available. Cost severance is hidden through some combination of the six patterns, and the specific combination shapes which interventions can address it. McDowell County coal is severed primarily through geographic distance and informational asymmetry. The 2008 housing collapse was severed primarily through informational asymmetry. The pyramids were severed through power asymmetry. The "cost of doing business" pattern in modern industrial subsidy is severed through normative routinization. The atmosphere's carbon-absorbing capacity has been severed through abundance masking — the illusion that the sky could continuously absorb our externalities held for two centuries before the math caught up. What the Mars habitat makes visible is that the underlying structural test is the operational arrangement itself, not the patterns by which the arrangement gets produced.

This has an implication for the policy work the later chapters take up. The closer the policy brings cost-bearers and value-capturers — by collapsing geographic distance through spatial cost severance pricing, by collapsing temporal asymmetry through intergenerational accounting, by collapsing informational asymmetry through transparent supply-chain disclosure, by collapsing power asymmetry through institutional accountability requirements, by collapsing normative routinization through legible pricing of what the baseline has hidden, by collapsing abundance masking through honest stock accounting — the more automatic honest pricing becomes. Place-based transition-cost accounting, spatial cost severance charges, border carbon adjustments, transparent supply chain disclosure — all of these address one or more of the six patterns. None of them addresses the structural test by itself, which is why a policy regime that addresses each named pattern with surface-level interventions can still leave cost severance fully operational. The framework's logic is clear about that.

### Not all resources matter equally

A natural consequence of the cross-scenario testing is a recognition that the framework does not treat all resources the same, because the framework's parameters do not treat all resources the same. For some resources, the residual commons value collapses to near the market price — the framework adds little, and market pricing is approximately correct. For others, it is orders of magnitude higher than the market price — the framework reveals severe and systematic underpricing. The difference between these two classes is a property of the resource itself, not a choice made by the analyst.

The book calls this property the *existential substitutability gap*. A resource's existential substitutability gap is the difference between its substitutability for current industrial applications and its substitutability for possible long-term civilizational applications — the applications, known and unknown, that a species with a two-hundred-year planning horizon would want to keep options open for. Per the framework's Substitutability Function S, this gap is the difference S_max(industrial) − S_max(existential).

The terminology here engages the existential-risk economics lineage — Bostrom 2002 (the existential-risk concept's origination); Bostrom 2014 *Superintelligence*; MacAskill 2022 *What We Owe the Future*; Ord 2020 *The Precipice*; and the Future of Humanity Institute / Centre for the Study of Existential Risk literatures — as economic-pricing apparatus for resources critical to civilizational continuity. The framework does not adopt longtermism's specific ethical commitments; no commitment to total-utilitarian aggregation across futures, no commitment to particular population-ethics conclusions. The framework's existential-substitutability concern is one input to existential-risk reasoning, focused specifically on resource-availability across civilizational timescales — the chapter's two-hundred-year planning horizon is illustrative, with the underlying concern extending to the timescales (10,000+ years for space-travel options; 500 million to 1 billion years for sun-expansion contingencies) that civilizational-continuity option-preservation actually requires.

Coal has an existential substitutability gap near zero. Electricity from coal is readily substitutable today; no foreseeable civilizational application requires coal as a specific input. Sand, common iron ore, and common stone are similar — abundant, substitutable, zero existential substitutability gap.

Conventional oil, copper, and zinc have low existential substitutability gap. Substitutable for most current applications; no clear civilizational applications that uniquely require them.

Uranium, lithium, and cobalt have moderate existential substitutability gap. Substitutable for some applications; important strategic roles in specific technologies; future applications plausibly but not certainly dependent on these particular materials.

Rare earth elements and platinum group metals have high existential substitutability gap. Their specific magnetic, catalytic, and electronic properties are required for technologies that have not yet been invented but which almost any civilization with advanced manufacturing will need. Substitutes exist for some current applications; no clear path to substitutes for all applications; high probability that at least some future applications cannot be served without them.

Helium-3 occupies a unique category — conditionally high existential substitutability gap. If deuterium-helium-3 fusion becomes viable, helium-3 becomes one of the most strategically important resources in the accessible solar system. If it does not, helium-3 is a curiosity. The classification is contingent on a technology that has not yet been demonstrated.

The existential substitutability gap ranking is not a complete map of what to reserve and what to extract. It is the input that, combined with the stock estimate and the externality tail for each resource class, produces the reservation policy the later chapters will develop. For the moment, it is enough to observe that the ranking produces intuitively correct answers and that the framework's logic supports those intuitions: the things we should worry about reserving for our descendants are the things whose existential substitutability gap is high, because those are the things our descendants might need and cannot replace.

Chapter 9 will return to this ranking when it asks what the policy architecture for the renewable-energy transition has to actually accomplish. The high existential substitutability gap of rare earths, platinum-group metals, cobalt, and nickel — the inputs to the motors, magnets, electrolyzers, fuel cells, and battery cathodes the transition runs on — is not an academic curiosity. It is the reason that getting the transition wrong on these specific resources would foreclose the very civilizational capabilities the transition is meant to deliver. The framework's pricing logic and the framework's policy logic meet, in that chapter, on this list.

### The cross-scenario result

The summary of the universality test fits in a small table. Each scenario is run against the same framework, with the same terms, under different parameters. Each produces a different answer. Every answer is one that a thoughtful administrator of the scenario would recognize as correct.

The framework is universal. The same formula, the same terms, the same logic, applied everywhere, producing different answers because different parameters describe different situations. This is what a calibrated instrument looks like.

### Mars is a false analogy

A careful reader will push back on the universality claim in a specific way that deserves a direct response.

Mars is a simple system. Ten thousand people, three ice deposits, a closed habitat with transparent feedback loops. Misprice the water and people die within a decade — the consequence is immediate and lethal. Earth is a complex system with unclear boundaries, vast and partially-understood stocks, continuous discovery of new deposits, ongoing technological innovation that can change the substitutability of almost any resource, and political and economic feedback loops that operate on timescales the Mars thought experiment does not reproduce. Saying "the math is the same" ignores that the systems are qualitatively different. On Mars, mispricing kills quickly. On Earth, mispricing kills slowly, distantly, in populations whose deaths are not easily attributed to the specific extraction that contributed to them. The habitat administrator gets the answer right because she cannot afford to get it wrong. The terrestrial policymaker, the terrestrial extractor, the terrestrial consumer operate under no comparable constraint.

The objection is legitimate and deserves a specific response.

The objection is correct that Earth is more complex than Mars. The objection is not correct that this complexity makes the framework inapplicable. Complexity affects the parameters the framework uses — the stock estimates are less certain, the substitutability curves are more contested, the externality tails are harder to bound, the discount factor is the subject of decades of unresolved argument. Complexity does not affect the structure of the pricing problem, which is: a finite commons is being extracted, costs and benefits are being distributed across space and time, and the question is how to price the extraction in a way that reflects those distributions.

The reason the Mars thought experiment is useful is not that Mars is Earth. It is that Mars strips away the layers of uncertainty that allow Earth's cost severance to persist under the cover of ambiguity. On Mars, the parameters are stark enough that the correct answer is unmistakable. On Earth, the parameters are moderate enough that reasonable people can disagree about them — which is precisely the condition under which cost severance has been permitted to operate at the scale it has. The framework's function is not to pretend Earth is simple. Its function is to make the structural logic visible where Earth's moderate parameters would otherwise obscure it.

This is how physics uses frictionless planes and thermodynamics uses perfect insulators — not because the world contains such things, but because the limiting case exposes the underlying law. The law, once exposed, applies in the messy real world with the appropriate adjustments for friction and leakage. The Mars administrator's water ice problem is the frictionless plane of resource economics. The principles it reveals apply, imperfectly, messily, with all the real-world noise Earth provides, to every extraction decision that has ever been made on this planet.

A different reader will object on different grounds. Mars is a thought experiment. There is no actual habitat, no administrator, no consortium, no decision pending in 2026. The entire scenario is, in the dismissive sense of the word, science fiction. Importing fictional scenarios into a serious argument about resource economics, this reader will say, is a category error.

The objection is one this book takes seriously, and one the methodological literature took up long ago and answered. Thought experiments are not predictions; they are stress tests of the logical apparatus. A frictionless plane is fictional. So is an ideal gas, a perfectly rational agent, a market without transaction costs, an electron at rest in an inertial frame. Each of these has been more useful to the development of its discipline than any number of empirically-attested cases, and each is foundational to disciplines that are now, in the most banal real-world sense, obviously not science fiction. The point is not that Mars is real. The point is that the framework's structure produces outputs that can be checked against intuitions that thoughtful readers actually hold. If the framework gave the wrong answer in the Mars habitat — if it told the administrator to accept the consortium's terms, or if it said the rare-earths-as-self-bearing case was indistinguishable from the asteroid-iron case — readers would notice, and the framework would be falsified by an exercise that costs nothing but careful reading. The thought experiment does work the empirical record cannot do: it subjects the framework to scenarios where the right answer is unambiguous, and checks whether the framework gets there. The framework that passes the test is the framework worth applying to the harder, noisier cases the empirical record actually contains.

The thought experiment also has, on a long enough horizon, a structural reason for being non-fiction. The Sun's main-sequence luminosity is increasing on a timescale that mainstream solar-physics models project will make Earth uninhabitable for biological civilizations on the order of 500 million to 1 billion years from now — well before the red-giant phase that fully engulfs the planet roughly 7.5 billion years out. On those timescales, the question of whether human civilization or its descendants will attempt to inhabit other worlds in order to continue surviving as a species is not a question of *whether* but a question of *when*. The framework's behavior on bounded off-world habitats is therefore not a methodological abstraction; it is a coherent forward-looking civilizational concern, even if the timescale is far enough out that no present-day decision turns on it.

A reader should be careful here. This argument is methodological, not advocational. The framework is using off-world habitats as test cases for a pricing instrument; it is not arguing that humanity must accelerate the inhabitation of Mars or treat off-world settling as a present-day civilizational priority. There are good arguments on both sides of that policy question — from the existential-risk literature that frames off-world inhabitation as one of several long-horizon mitigation strategies, to the skeptical academic lineage that argues off-world expansionism is geopolitically destabilizing and distracting from near-term Earth-stewardship priorities — and the framework holds whatever the answer to that policy question turns out to be. The point is only that off-world habitats are a coherent scenario worth taking seriously as a stress test, not a fanciful conceit that disappears when the reader closes the book.

And to the related objection — that for an uninhabited asteroid, the framework reduces to market pricing because the externality terms collapse to zero — the response is that this is not a weakness. It is a strength. A well-calibrated instrument should agree with existing instruments where those instruments are correct, and diverge only where they are incorrect. The framework agrees with market pricing for asteroid iron because in that specific case, market pricing is approximately right — there are no externalities, no community displaced, no ecosystem disrupted, no foreclosure of access for future generations. When all the novel terms collapse to zero, the residual commons value collapses to the market price, and the framework's recommendation is the market's recommendation. The framework adds value only where the market misses something. It correctly adds nothing where the market misses nothing. That is what a calibrated instrument is supposed to do.

A framework that always disagreed with market pricing would be as useless as one that always agreed. The one that correctly identifies when and by how much the market errs is the one worth having.

### The framework across time, and the immune response

Two more pressure points on the universality claim deserve a direct response.

The first is temporal. This book has described cost severance in the industrial and post-industrial economies of the last two centuries. A careful reader might ask whether the pattern is a feature of capitalism specifically — whether a different economic system would produce a different result. The answer is no. The clearest way to see it is to look at the pattern operating in civilizations that had no contact with each other and no shared cultural or ideological inheritance.

The pyramids at Giza were built, over a period of roughly seventy years in the Egyptian Old Kingdom around 2550 BCE, by a workforce of skilled craftsmen and conscripted rotational laborers drawn from the agricultural population. The archaeological consensus that emerged from Mark Lehner's Giza Plateau Mapping Project and Zahi Hawass's workers' cemetery excavations has substantially revised the older Herodotean image of chattel slavery under the lash: the workers were paid in rations, medically cared for, and properly buried. They were not slaves in the plantation-economy sense. They were also not free in any sense a modern reader would recognize. The centralized pharaonic economy left no alternative employment, no geographic mobility beyond the Nile Valley, no political structure within which refusal was possible. The pharaoh extracted the value — political legitimacy, divine kingship, monumental architecture guaranteeing afterlife provisions. The workers bore the cost. The monument remains visible today, and its visibility is the permanent evidence that the mechanism operated four and a half millennia before capitalism existed.

Twenty-three hundred years later, on the other side of the world, Qin Shi Huang unified the warring states into the first centralized Chinese empire and commissioned two construction projects whose remains are also still visible: the initial unified Great Wall, spanning five thousand kilometers of northern frontier, and his own mausoleum complex near modern Xi'an, of which the Terracotta Army discovered in 1974 is one fragment. Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian*, compiled approximately a century after the Qin's fall, reports that the Wall was built by three to five hundred thousand concurrent conscripted laborers drawn from peasants, prisoners, and convicts, with documented mass mortality; the mausoleum employed by his estimate upwards of seven hundred thousand. The legalist political philosophy that organized this extraction was explicit in its doctrine: strict penalties for failing conscription quotas, merit-based administration, efficient production regardless of cost-bearer welfare. The Qin dynasty's harshness was so extreme that it collapsed within four years of the First Emperor's death, under peasant rebellions that swept away the legalist regime. But the pattern itself — value extracted by imperial state, cost borne by labor whose exit was structurally foreclosed — is recognizable across every line of Sima Qian's text. The Great Wall is still standing. So are the figures.

Egypt and Qin China had no contact. Their cosmologies, their religions, their political systems, their philosophies of rulership were incommensurable. The mechanism operated in both. Closer to our own time, feudal lords extracted agricultural surplus from serfs who worked land they did not own, bearing the costs of that extraction in their bodies, their families, and a poverty from which exit was structurally foreclosed. The lord extracted the value. The serf bore the cost. The cost-bearer-blaming narrative that accompanied it — divine order, natural hierarchy, the Great Chain of Being, the serf's station as God-ordained — is structurally the same rhetorical move that later ideological languages continue to perform with updated vocabulary. The lord called it protection. The twentieth-century company town called it paternalism. Today's extraction apologists call it culture. The function is identical: a narrative that frames the cost-bearer's condition as a property of the cost-bearer rather than a product of the extraction.

Cost severance is not a feature of capitalism. It is a pattern that finds whatever economic system is available, and the system that happens to be in place becomes the delivery vehicle, not the root cause. Capitalism is the current and most efficient delivery vehicle. Feudal agriculture was a previous one. Pharaonic divine kingship was an earlier one. Qin legalism ran its own compressed version. Soviet industrial planning ran a post-capitalist version. The mechanism does not require a specific ownership structure — it requires the conditions Chapter 5 named, and those conditions are not ideologically parochial. If a reader wants evidence that the mechanism is older than any of the institutions we argue about, the pyramids and the Great Wall are that evidence. They have been standing longer than any of our political vocabularies.

The second pressure point is the mechanism's ability to generate its own immune response. When cost severance becomes publicly visible and consumers demand accountability, the extraction architecture can manufacture a performance of accountability that does not actually reattach the costs it claims to. The Kimberley Process certification of "conflict-free" diamonds is the textbook case. The consumer pays an ethical premium. The diamond is certified. The premium stays with the retailer and the certification infrastructure; it does not reach the miners whose conditions the cost was born in. The bond is severed. The certificate is a performance of non-severance. The miner is no better off than before, and the retailer captures a larger margin than before. The pattern generalizes — fair-trade coffee certification that does not substantively raise farmer income, carbon offsets that reduce the marketing cost of emissions without reducing emissions, ESG screens that satisfy fiduciary appearance without altering capital flow to problematic industries. In each case, the extraction architecture has adapted to frameworks like this one by building a simulated-reattachment layer that performs accountability without delivering it. A framework that ignores this adaptation will be captured by it. The discipline the remaining chapters try to maintain — distinguishing accountability bonds that actually reach the cost-bearing party from mechanisms that merely appear to — is the discipline this second stress test is pointing at. Chapter 9, in particular, takes up the design problem this stress test surfaces: how to architect accountability instruments whose verification structure resists the simulated-reattachment adaptation, and what the existing partial-implementations — the Norwegian sovereign-fund discipline, the early-twentieth-century Nordic policy architectures, the reachable-horizon institutions Chapter 9 will catalog — already get right and where they remain vulnerable.

Together these two pressure points show that the mechanism is older than its most visible contemporary forms and more sophisticated than its most visible contemporary critics. Which is exactly why the framework needed to be tested at the limits — on Mars, on asteroids, across centuries, and against the system's own immune responses. What survives these tests is an instrument that holds where the parameters change, and names its limits where it cannot.

### What comes next

The universality test establishes, as cleanly as a thought experiment can establish anything, that the framework is an instrument rather than an ideology. It produces different answers for different situations. It agrees with market pricing where market pricing is correct, and diverges where market pricing is wrong. It names its own limits where its externalities are not priceable. And it reveals, in passing, that cost severance requires operational arrangements that hide the cost from honest accounting — which means that the policy response on Earth is, in part, a project of collapsing the patterns that produce the hiding, across whichever combination is doing the load-bearing work in the case at hand.

Before turning to what that response would look like in practice — which is the subject of the remaining chapters — the book has one more accounting to do. The framework has been presented in abstract terms, validated against terrestrial cases, and stress-tested across the solar system. What has not yet been done, in concrete aggregate, is to ask what the honest invoice looks like when the framework is applied to a single real extraction that we can walk through tier by tier.

The next chapter does that, for the resource and the community the book opened with. One ton of Appalachian coal, from a specific mine, in a specific county, in a specific year. Every cost category named, every tier priced conservatively, every number defensible. And the result placed against the four-dollar-and-fifty-cent figure the market cleared the transaction on.

Look at the number. Look at the price on the invoice. Notice the gap.

### A note on the numbers behind the thought experiment

The asteroid-iron, lunar, and Mars-water scenarios this chapter walks through are not pulled from no-where. Current Earth-launch infrastructure has reduced the cost of getting a kilogram into low-Earth-orbit by roughly an order of magnitude over the past fifteen years, from the Space Shuttle era's approximately thirty thousand dollars per kilogram down to the current commercial-launch baseline of roughly two thousand to five thousand dollars per kilogram (Falcon 9 and Falcon Heavy operating; Vulcan Centaur and New Glenn now operational). A further order-of-magnitude reduction to roughly two hundred to one thousand dollars per kilogram is plausible-but-not-yet-validated for the next decade, contingent on Starship operational maturity. These figures are not fantasy. They are the operational economics the next phase of space activity will be conducted under.

Mars-surface presence is currently far more expensive than low-Earth-orbit access. The Curiosity, Perseverance, and InSight missions delivered about a kilogram of operational hardware to the Martian surface for approximately two and a half million dollars apiece, dominated by interplanetary cruise and entry-descent-landing infrastructure rather than per-kilogram launch from Earth. A small Mars habitat at the scale this chapter imagines — perhaps ten people, perhaps fifty tons of delivered surface mass — would carry, under current operational economics, an order-of-magnitude delivery cost of fifty to several hundred billion dollars before any consideration of in-situ resource utilization or Starship-class delivery cost reductions. The asteroid-mining commercial proposals in the literature (the Keck Institute Asteroid Retrieval Mission Study of 2012; pre-acquisition projections from Planetary Resources and Deep Space Industries) target costs per kilogram extracted that are five to seven orders of magnitude below current science-mission economics. Those projections are actor-stated targets, not validated operational figures.

None of this is offered as a delivery-cost forecast. The framework's claim about asteroid iron — that residual commons value approaches market price under any reasonable accountability bond — does not depend on which specific cost-per-kilogram number eventually settles. The structural argument is about substitutability and externality, both approximately zero for asteroid iron, regardless of the delivery economics. The numbers are noted here only so that a reader who wants to know whether the parameter ranges in the chapter are grounded in real exploration economics has the answer: they are.
