## Chapter 2: The Miner

---

In May of 1960 a young senator drove through McDowell County, West Virginia, and what he saw there did not leave him.

He had come for votes. The primary was on and the state was poor and the county was the poorest stretch of it, and he drove the hollows where the road ran along the creek because there was nowhere else for a road to run. He saw children standing in doorways with the swollen bellies of the underfed. He saw the surplus commodities the government handed out, the cheese and the flour, stacked in the cabins because there was no work and no money and nothing else coming in. He saw men sitting on porches in the middle of a working day. Some of those men had carried rifles in a war and come home to a country that had won it, and now they sat. The richest coal on the continent lay under their feet. They had cut it out of the ground with their bodies. And they sat.

The county had given the nation its metallurgical coal, the hard, hot-burning coal that the steel mills could not run without, the coal that built the rails and the ships and the bridges and the cities. At its peak McDowell shipped more of it than anywhere in America. By every measure of what came out of the ground, it was rich. By every measure of how the people who took it out of the ground lived, it was the poorest place in one of the poorest states in the country.

That was the paradox, and the senator felt it, and he carried it with him out of the hollows.

Four months later, in September, he stood at a podium in Canton, Ohio, and named the thing he had seen. He spoke as a man builds a case. He named the want, and the idleness, and the children, and he laid the blame where a man laying blame from a podium lays it, on an opponent, on an administration, on a policy that had failed. He had the want right. He had the suffering right. He had seen it with his own eyes and he did not exaggerate it.

He had the wrong defendant.

The men on the porches had not been robbed by a party or a policy. They had been emptied out by an arrangement so ordinary that it had no name, an arrangement that took the value of what they dug and sent it up and out of the county and left the cost of the digging behind in their lungs and their creeks and their children. The senator was looking at the result of that arrangement. He just had no way to point at the arrangement itself, because no one did. There was no defendant to point at. There was only the empty chair where the defendant should have been.

This chapter is about what should have sat in that chair.

---

The last chapter ended with a ledger and a set of empty columns. This chapter fills in the first one.

The organizing question is small and it is the whole book. What did a ton of that coal actually cost?

Not the price. The price is easy and it is in the records. In 1960 the coal sold at the mouth of the mine for just under five dollars a ton. That is the price. The price is the number the buyer paid the seller, and it is the number everyone means when they say what something costs, and it is not what the coal cost. The cost is the price plus everything the price left out. The cost is the price plus the bill that never came.

This is the miner I meant in the last chapter, when I said I had options a coal miner does not have. I had skills that several employers wanted and the freedom to move between them.

The miner had a hole in the ground and a county with no other work in it and a body that the work would use up. A constrained choice is still a choice. It is not a free one. And the cost of his is the cost I want to set down now, one column at a time, the way you read a bill you did not know you had run up.

---

Begin with the body, because the first column is written in a man's lungs.

Robert Bailey worked underground. The dust came off the coal face and off the machines that cut it, fine enough to ride the air and reach the deep tissue of the lung where the body cannot clear it, and the body, finding it there, walls it off, scars around it, and the scar tissue spreads until the lung is a thing that cannot do the one thing a lung does. The doctors call it pneumoconiosis. The miners call it black lung. Bailey, when he tried to say what it was, said it was almost like drowning.

It is almost like drowning.

He kept working at it. The disease is not fast in the way a wound is fast. It is slow the way water is slow against stone. He needed, at the end, a double lung transplant, two new lungs to replace the two the coal had filled, and he got one, and the transplant kept him alive on a regimen of drugs that hold the body back from rejecting what is not its own.

Here the ledger does a thing that the price never does. The company Bailey had worked for went bankrupt. When a company goes bankrupt the corporate shell dissolves, and when the shell dissolves the obligation inside it dissolves too, and the obligation that dissolved was the company's duty to the man whose lungs it had used. That debt did not disappear. A debt that real does not disappear. It moved. It moved onto a federal fund that the rest of us pay into, which is to say it moved onto the rest of us, who never dug the coal and never bought the steel and never sat in the room where the choice to mine it cheap was made. The miner's body carried the disease. The public carried the bill. The company carried neither, because by then the company was a name on a closed file.

Bailey testified before Congress about the backlog of claims, the men waiting and dying while their paperwork moved through the machine. He testified twice. He said that most laws have been written with blood.

He died twenty-eight days after his last interview, in February of 2019. The cancer that killed him was brought on, in part, by the drugs that had kept the transplanted lungs alive. The county had taken his lungs through the coal and then the medicine that replaced his lungs helped take the rest of him. There is no column on any invoice for that.

I will not turn him into a number. The numbers are coming, and they are real, and he is not one of them. He is the reason the numbers matter.

The disease is not behind us. It is rising again, and rising fastest in the younger miners, the men with fewer than twenty years underground, which means the new machines that cut faster throw a finer and more dangerous dust. In central Appalachia, in the Kentucky and Virginia and West Virginia coalfields, around one in five miners with twenty-five years and more in the mines carries it. Each man who dies of it loses, by the careful count, somewhere between eight and thirteen years of the life he would otherwise have had. Set that number down and look at it. Eight to thirteen years, per man, taken at the end, where the years are most precious because there are fewest left.

The federal program that pays the benefits has paid out, by a conservative and honestly dated count, around forty-four billion dollars in the years through 2009. That is a floor, not a total. There is no trustworthy figure for the years since, only the certainty that the floor keeps rising. The fund that pays it has run in debt almost every year since 1979, and the debt stood at five point one billion dollars as of September of 2024. When three coal companies went bankrupt between 2014 and 2016, Alpha Natural Resources and Patriot Coal and James River Coal, the black lung benefit obligations they carried, eight hundred and sixty-five million dollars of duty to sick and dying men, came off the companies and onto the public. The shell dissolved. The debt moved. It always moves the same way.

That is the first column. A ton of coal cost a portion of a man's lungs, and the man paid it, and the public paid the bill for the man, and the company paid nothing, and none of it appeared in the just-under-five-dollars.

---

The second column runs orange in the creeks.

When mining cuts into rock that holds sulfur, the sulfur meets air and water and makes acid, and the acid leaches the metals out of the disturbed ground, and the metals come down into the streams. The creeks run the color of rust, iron sulfide orange, or they run gray with the sediment, and the things that lived in them stop living in them. This is acid mine drainage. It does not stop when the mining stops. It starts when the rock is opened and it runs for decades after the last man has left the last mine, a wound in the ground that keeps bleeding into the water long after the body that opened it has gone.

The law says the land is to be reclaimed, the mines closed out, the drainage treated, the ground brought back toward what it was. The law is right and the law is underfunded. Across the coalfields there are more than six hundred and thirty-three thousand acres waiting on reclamation. The cost of doing it runs somewhere between seven and a half and nine and three-quarters billion dollars. The bonds the companies posted against that cost hold around three point eight billion. The gap, the distance between what reclamation costs and what the companies set aside to pay for it, runs four to six billion dollars. That gap is a column on the bill. Someone fills it or no one does, and if no one does, the creek stays orange, and the orange is the cost, sitting in the water where the price put it.

A ton of coal cost a measure of a watershed, and the watershed paid it, and the price did not mention the water.

---

The third column is the emptied town.

Welch had department stores and theaters and hotels and a daily paper. Keystone had its own life, and Kimball, and a town called War. They had the look of permanence that a place gets when the money is flowing and no one yet imagines it will stop. The money was the coal money and the coal money flowed and the towns grew up in its course like grass along a stream.

Then the work thinned. The mines mechanized, and a machine cuts coal with a fraction of the men, and the men who were no longer needed were no longer paid, and the steel that had bought the coal began buying it from elsewhere or not at all. The big steel operation that had anchored the county wound down across the 1980s, more than twelve hundred jobs going out of it over those years, not in a single announced closing but in the slow way these things actually happen, a shift cut here, a section closed there, until the work that had been the spine of a town was simply gone.

The income of the county did not fall off a cliff. It bled. In the worst single year it dropped around thirteen percent, and from the peak in the mid-1980s it lost around fifteen percent over three years, and then it kept going, down and down across forty years, while the people went with it. In 1950 nearly a hundred thousand people lived in McDowell County. By 2020 the number was under twenty thousand. Four out of five people, gone. The stores closed because the people left and the people left because the work was gone and the work was gone because the coal that remained no longer needed them to take it.

What is left in Welch and Keystone and Kimball is not ruins, in most cases. Ruins are dramatic. This is quieter. These are empty lots where the stores were, and storefronts with the names still faintly readable above doors that no one opens, and a Main Street with the traffic of a Sunday on every day of the week. The town did not fall. It emptied.

A ton of coal cost a share of a town's future, and the town paid it across forty years, and no single year on any ledger shows the size of it, because the cost was spread thin enough to look like ordinary decline.

---

The fourth column is the children, and it is the hardest one to see, because the people who paid it are not in McDowell to be counted. They left.

A family raises a child the way every family does, by spending on the child. The breakfast cooked at seven in the dark before a shift. The cleats for the team. The lesson on the secondhand piano. The years of attention and money and worry that a family pours into a child are an investment, in the plainest sense, a present cost laid down against a future return. In an ordinary place the return comes back to the place. The child grows up and works there and raises children there and the value the family invested circulates and stays.

In McDowell the child grew up and left, because there was no work to stay for, and the value the family had invested got up and walked out of the county on the child's own two feet and went to produce its return somewhere else, in some city that had paid nothing to raise it. The county fed the seed and another place ate the fruit. This is a cost too, the deepest and quietest of them, the cost of raising a generation for the benefit of the places that generation moves to. It does not run orange and it does not show in a lung. It shows in a county of old people and empty schools, in a place that spent itself raising the very people who had to leave it to live.

A ton of coal cost the county its own children, raised at the county's expense and harvested by everywhere else.

---

There is a fifth column and I am going to leave it nearly empty on purpose, because it belongs to a later chapter.

The coal did not stop costing when it left McDowell. It was metallurgical coal, most of it, bound for the furnace, and what goes into a furnace comes out as heat and as the gas that warms the air for everyone who will ever live after the burning. That cost lands nowhere near McDowell and nowhere near 1960. It lands on people not yet born, in a column that does not come due in the lifetime of anyone who profited from the ton. The price of the coal said nothing about it, because the price is paid by the living and this cost falls on the unborn, who cannot be at the table where the price is set and cannot send a bill back through time.

We will return to that. The full accounting of it, the actual number, belongs to a later chapter that does nothing but compute what these things cost. For now I only set the column on the page and leave it open. The furnace is still warm.

---

Now the numbers, flat.

A male child born in McDowell County could expect to live to around sixty-three and a half years, against a national figure near seventy-six and a half. Thirteen years. The drug-death rate ran around one hundred and forty-one per hundred thousand, against a national rate near sixteen. The poverty rate stood at thirty-seven and six tenths percent, the median household income at twenty-eight thousand two hundred and thirty-five dollars, as of 2022. Of West Virginia's fifty-five counties, ranked by the health of the people in them, McDowell ranked fifty-fifth. Last.

It was here, in Welch, that the first food stamps under the modern program were issued, in a 1961 pilot, the first recipient a man named Alderson Muncy, on the twenty-ninth of May. The county that had fed the nation's furnaces became the county where the nation first tested how to feed the people the furnaces had used.

These numbers are real. But the numbers are not the argument. The numbers are what the argument explains.

---

Now the word, because the chapter has been circling a word and it is time to set it down.

When value is generated in one place and the cost of generating it lands in another, and the two are never reconciled, never billed back, never made to meet, I call that cost severance. The cost is severed from the value. The man who made the value does not bear the cost, and the place where the cost lands did not keep the value, and the gap between them is where the whole sorrow of this lives.

The cost that gets left behind, the lung and the creek and the emptied town and the departed child, that is the severed cost. It is real and it is owed and there is no one to send the invoice to.

And the act of taking the value while leaving the cost, of carrying off the worth and setting down the bill, that is value extraction. Not value creation, which makes something and is paid for it. Extraction, which takes what was there and bears none of the cost of the taking.

There is a lineage to this idea and I want to mark it without leaning on it. The economist Mariana Mazzucato has drawn the line between making value and merely taking it, between the activity that adds to the common store and the activity that draws from it while dressed in the language of contribution, and what I am describing is a taking of that second kind, a value carried off without the cost of it borne, which echoes what David Harvey called accumulation by dispossession, the old pattern of wealth gathered by stripping it from somewhere that cannot defend the claim. The pattern is old and has been named before. What this book tries to add is the application of it to the physical extraction of a resource from the ground, and the method, later, for putting a number on the severed cost.

There is also a century-old name for the part of this that economics already half-saw. Arthur Pigou, writing before any of these mines had reached their peak, named the externality, the cost a transaction imposes on someone outside it, the smoke that falls on the neighbor's wash. The word is technically accurate. The black lung is an externality. The orange creek is an externality. And the word is practically insufficient, because to call a man's drowned lungs an externality is to file the worst thing in the chapter under a clerk's heading, a thing external to the transaction, off to the side, not really the point. It was always the point. Pigou's tradition saw the cost and lacked the instrument to do anything with it. The instrument came later, and it is what the rest of this book is for.

These three words, cost severance and severed cost and value extraction, are the load-bearing vocabulary of everything that follows. The book will use them in every chapter. I am coining them here, against this county, because this is the place where their meaning is plainest, and then I am going to stop, because naming the mechanism is enough work for one chapter, and the chapters after this one will build what the naming makes necessary.

---

There is a particular feature of cost severance that this county shows better than anywhere, and it has to do with distance.

In the last chapter the man on the rooftops climbed above each city so he could see the shape of it, the bends of the river and the run of the old roads and where the money flowed and where it flowed past. You have to get above a thing to see its shape. The people inside it, living their streets and their stops and their doors, never do.

The coal worked the same shape in reverse. The value of McDowell's coal went up and out, to the mills and the cities and the accounts that bought the steel, and the people who lived on the receiving end of that value, who drove on the rails it built and crossed the bridges it framed and lived in the cities it raised, never once had to look down on McDowell and see the shape of what their steel had cost. The cost stayed in the hollows. The value left them. And because the value left and the cost stayed, the people who got the value were spatially severed from the people who paid for it, far enough away that they never had to climb above the thing and see it whole. Distance did the hiding. You do not feel the cost of a place you never have to look at.

Call that spatial cost severance. The geography itself does the work of keeping the bill out of sight. The value and the cost end up in different places, and the different places never meet, and the distance between them is exactly the distance across which no invoice is ever sent.

---

Now the objections, because a chapter like this one invites three, and each of them is worth taking seriously, and none of them lands where it is aimed.

The first objection is that the companies were evil. That a chapter full of dead miners and orange creeks and emptied towns is building toward a villain, and the villain is the coal operator, and the moral of the story is that bad men did bad things.

It is not. There may have been bad men. There usually are, somewhere. But the argument does not need them and does not rest on them. The cost severance would have happened with saints in the boardroom. The mechanism does not run on malice. It runs on the simple fact that the price of the coal had no line for the lung or the creek or the town, and a transaction that has no line for a cost does not record that cost, and a cost that is not recorded is not paid by the parties to the transaction, and so it lands on whoever is standing where it falls. That is a property of the arrangement, not of the character of the men inside it. This is the mechanism, not the motive, and the whole point is that it does not need a motive to work.

The second objection is the opposite of the first. It says that industrialization itself was the cost, that you cannot have steel without mining and you cannot have mining without harm, that the coal built the modern world and the modern world is worth its price, and that to total up the bill like this is to wish the rails and the ships and the bridges away.

I am not wishing them away. The coal did build the world, and the world is worth a great deal. The question this chapter asks is not whether the coal should have been mined. The question is who should have borne the cost of mining it. Grant everything the objection wants to grant, that the steel was necessary and the cities are good and the bridges hold. The lung was still real and somebody still paid for it, and the only question on the table is whether the somebody should have been the miner who could not bill it back, or the arrangement that took the value and skipped the cost. The compensation question survives the concession entirely. You can believe the coal was worth mining and still ask why the bill went where it went.

The third objection is that the people were passive. That if the cost was this severe, the miners and their families would have fought it, and since they did not bring down the arrangement, they must have consented to it, or at least acquiesced.

They fought it. The history is not one of passive people. The miners organized, and struck, and bled for the union that gave them what protection they got. When black lung was being denied as a real disease, miners across the coalfields walked out in a wildcat strike in 1968 to force the issue, and the Black Lung Association carried it into the law, and the movement that became Miners for Democracy fought to take their own union back when it had stopped fighting for them. These were not passive people. They had agency, and they used it, and they won real things with it. What they did not have was the power to repeal the mechanism. You can win a strike and still not be able to put a price on a lung. Agency inside a structure is real, and it is still inside the structure. A constrained choice is still a choice. It is not a free one, and the constraint is the thing the chapter is about.

So the three objections clear the ground rather than blocking it. The companies need not be evil. The coal can have been worth mining. The people were not passive. And what is left standing, when all three have had their say, is the arrangement, the mechanism that takes the value and severs the cost, sitting in the chair where the senator could not see it.

The indictment the senator delivered in Canton stands. The want was real, the suffering was real, the case was true. Only the name of the defendant changes. It was never the party in power and never the policy that failed. What belongs in the dock is an instrument that does not exist, a way of making the cost meet the value, of pricing the severed cost and routing it back to where it came from. There is no such instrument. That is precisely the point. The argument of this book is that we have to build it.

---

I have been writing as though this were a thing that happened, past tense, to a county that the world has finished with. It is not. The mechanism does not retire with an industry. It moves to the next vivid thing. And to see that it is a mechanism and not a story about coal, watch it run a second time, on the same county, in our own years, with a different product and the identical shape.

---

When the work left McDowell, it left behind exactly what an extraction leaves behind, a population in pain and out of options, with bodies worn by labor and a county with no economy to absorb them. The first extraction had taken the value of the coal and left the cost in the place. The second extraction came for what the first one had left.

It came as a medicine. In the late 1990s a company called Purdue Pharma brought to market an opioid painkiller, OxyContin, and sold it hard into places like McDowell, and the places like McDowell are where it did the most. This is the part where the temptation is to say that the company drew a map of the broken places and aimed for them, and I am not going to say that, because the honest account is more careful and more damning. There are two records here. There is the record the companies wrote down themselves, the litigation record, the internal documents and the settlements, which says what it says under oath. And there is the record that researchers and prosecutors reconstructed afterward, piecing together where the pills went and why they went there, from prescriber data and public-health studies and the geography of distress. Put the two records together and a pattern stands out that no single document confesses to: the drug found its largest markets in the places where the wage economy had already collapsed, where there was chronic physical pain from physical work, where there was little to lose and less to do, where the medical and economic supports that might have caught it had thinned along with everything else. The places the first extraction had hollowed out were the places the second extraction filled.

Notice what kind of taking this was. The coal company at least employed the miner. It paid him a wage, took the value of his labor, and left the cost in his lungs, but there was a wage contract in it, an exchange, however lopsided. The second extraction did not employ McDowell at all. It did not take the value of the county's labor. It found the wreckage the wage economy had left and made a market of the wreckage, and what it took, it took outside any contract of work, from people it did not employ, by selling into their pain. This is what Nancy Fraser calls expropriation, accumulation by means other than the wage contract, and once you have the term you can see that the second extraction was the purer case, value drawn out of a place by selling into the damage the first extraction did, with no wage contract anywhere in it to disguise the taking.

The family that owned Purdue, the Sacklers, drew out of the company something on the order of eleven billion dollars. The arithmetic of the harm is in the record. Purdue pleaded to a federal felony of misbranding in 2007 and paid six hundred million dollars, and three of its executives, Friedman and Udell and Goldenheim, pleaded to misdemeanor counts. In 2020 the company pleaded again, to three federal felony counts. The largest drug distributors, McKesson and Cardinal Health and AmerisourceBergen, the companies that moved the pills by the railcar load into counties whose populations could not have consumed them honestly, agreed in 2022 to a settlement of around twenty-one billion dollars. Set the drug-death number from a few pages back beside these figures and the cost severance is exact: the value came out as the eleven billion and the twenty-one billion, and the cost stayed in the county as the bodies, and the two were never on the same ledger until a court forced them there, decades late, for cents on the dollar of what was taken.

This is why the second cycle matters more than its own facts. It is the proof that the first cycle was not about coal. If it were about coal, it would have ended when the coal did. Instead the same shape arrived in a different industry and ran the same way, value out, cost down, no instrument to make them meet, and it will keep arriving, in whatever the next product is, as long as the instrument does not exist. The mechanism is not a fact about mining. It is a fact about how we price things, and it will run on anything we price the way we priced the coal.

---

The water I grew up on is a different place with the same defendant.

The Chesapeake watermen go out before light and come back with what the bay gives, and what it gives has been thinning, and the thinning has a cause, and the cause is costs the bay has been made to carry that no one ever put on an invoice. The nutrients that run off the land and into the water, the warming, the sediment, the long slow loading of the bay with the byproducts of everyone upstream, all of it lands on the oyster and the crab and the men who chase them, while the value of all that upstream activity floats off inland and out of reach. Same structure. Different details. The defendant in the bay is the same defendant as in the hollows, an instrument that does not exist, a way of making the cost meet the value that we have not built.

There is one difference between the two places, and it matters, and I want to name it without softening it. The coal is finite. What McDowell lost, it lost for good; the mountain does not refill. The bay is different. A bay can keep making what is taken from it, oyster after oyster, crab after crab, generation into generation, if the rate of taking stays under the rate of making. The severance there is not built into the resource the way it is built into the coal. It is conditional. It arrives only when we take faster than the water can make, and it can be held off, even reversed, if the instrument we lack in McDowell is built in time for the bay. That is the whole difference between a place that has been emptied and a place that still could be. It is the difference between an elegy and a warning.

I have not said a word against the miners and I am not going to. The pride in that work was real and it was earned. A man who cut metallurgical coal out of a mountain with his body did a hard and skilled and necessary thing, and he did it well, and he had every right to the pride he took in it. Nothing in this chapter is a verdict on the men. The men were not the problem. The men were the best of it. This is a verdict on an arithmetic, on a way of pricing a ton of coal that had no line for the lung or the creek or the town or the child, and sent the value up and out and left the cost at home, and could not, in its own terms, do otherwise.

The senator saw the suffering and named the wrong defendant. The right defendant is an instrument that does not exist. The rest of this book is the work of building it.
