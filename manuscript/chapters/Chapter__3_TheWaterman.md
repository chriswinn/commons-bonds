# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 3: The Waterman

---

The harbor at Old Point Comfort wakes a long time before the city does. The first work boat eases out of its slip in the dark that comes just before gray. A diesel coughs and settles. On the next pier a man says something to a man on the boat, neither voice raised, because at that hour the water carries a voice farther than the voice was meant to go. The Fort sits dark behind them. Ahead, the mouth of the Bay opens gray on gray, the channel marker blinking red. Somewhere on the far side of the channel a tug is moving supplies west toward Newport News, another load for the HRBT expansion that has dragged on for years.

I live aboard a boat now, but I did not grow up on the water the way a waterman's son would have. When I went out as a kid, it was for a day of fun. Maybe the fish bit and maybe they didn't. A waterman is deep into work before dawn and stays out until there is enough aboard to feed a family and put money in a pocket. He comes back in the afternoon, tired. Some come back with a hold full of crabs and some with a hold full of nothing, and the men coming home the second way are quieter than the men coming home the first, though neither set says much. They tie up, hose down, walk to a truck. At four the next morning they will be at the slip again, or they will not, and the Bay will not notice the difference either way. The slip will notice, and the dock, and the man two slips over who has been doing this since before any of them were born. That is how the working waterfront keeps its books.

This chapter is about what those books are not allowed to record.

---

I am writing from Hampton, which once held one of the largest oyster-packing operations in the country. I grew up on this bay. The firm of J.S. Darling and Son took that name in 1884, when Frank Darling joined his father's existing oyster business. At its peak, 160 shuckers handled 200,000 bushels of Chesapeake oysters every year, and the meat went out in iced tubs to dinner tables and restaurants in Baltimore and New York and other cities. The empty shells stayed. They left the plant on conveyor belts and went onto piles out back that rose more than four stories high, mountains a town could see and smell from the waterfront. The Darlings also ran a menhaden factory at Factory Point in Fox Hill, rendering thousands of tons of menhaden a year into fish meal for fertilizer and oil for industrial uses. Hampton was known up and down the coast as Crabtown; in the 1870s, James McMenamin invented and patented a canning process that put cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in airtight tins and shipped it to tables far from the Bay.

The meat left town, and the shells were dealt with. Most were sold and crushed into roads and driveways across the lower Bay, shell being cheaper than rock for keeping a dirt lane out of the mud. My father's family's own driveway was paved with them. Some were hauled out to Back River and dumped, and new oysters anchored on them and grew, so the bulk of what was lost was not lost to ignorance; it was lost to the going price of gravel. My father, working every summer to pay his way through college, spent one of those summers pouring concrete for the boardwalk at Buckroe Beach. It was concrete and rock, no shell in it, and it still stands. The driveway is the part I keep returning to: the material a reef is rebuilt from, spread under a family's cars.

In the summer of 2020, at the Navy's own installation on the Hampton Roads shoreline, Navy personnel and volunteers stacked concrete blocks called oyster castles into the water, structures built to give young oysters something hard to cling to. We paved with the thing that builds reefs, and a generation later we are casting concrete to do what the shell would have done for free.

The company shut down in 1979. I was born in 1980. I never saw the shell mountains, and there are not enough oysters coming out of the water to pile them that high anymore. There is still seafood pulled from the Chesapeake today, but nothing like what came out of these docks then. The story of a renewable resource turning non-renewable has never needed explaining to me. I have watched some of it happen here.

---

The community on this water has a name. The neighborhood of Hampton I am closest to is called Fox Hill, and Fox Hill is, or was, an island fishing community, a place where for most of the twentieth century almost every family in every house had at least one person on the water. My grandfather, who worked at NASA Langley just up the road, building pieces of a different kind of America in a different kind of building, was an exception. The neighbors on either side of him were on the water. The men my father grew up with were the sons of watermen. My father did not become a waterman, but the men who taught him how to handle a small boat, where the fish were running, what the smell of the channel meant in late August, were men whose lives were the water. My family is one generation removed from the working life and zero generations removed from the community.

Most days at most hours, the Chesapeake looks like a postcard: flat water, soft light, a bay you would put a child on. Some days it is not, and the difference is a thing the watermen know the way they know their own hands. Ask any waterman about the chop and you will hear about the other days. On the ocean, a five-foot wave is barely worth a comment, a slow rocking that can put a man to sleep, because the tops of those waves run thirty feet apart or more; the distance between crests is the wavelength, and on the Atlantic the wavelength runs long. The Bay does not build waves that shape. The Bay's wavelength runs short, short enough that a five-foot wave can come at you on something close to a five-foot wavelength, a wave very nearly square, and a square wave will turn the hardiest seaman green. The boat built for this water is the deadrise, the waterman's boat, nothing fancy; the men who run them will tell you it rides the chop more steadily than most anything else out here. There are no superyachts on this water; those are somewhere else in the world, which is its own small fact about where the water's value went. The men who have worked this water for forty years know which days you do not go out, which days you go out but stay close to home, and which days the sky is lying about what the water is going to do in three hours, and they know all of it without consulting anything outside themselves. The water grants the knowledge in exchange for one obligation: never mistake which day you are looking at.

Forty years of going out every day is what it takes to know what the watermen know, and no part of it is learned on weekends: the sweltering summer days when the heat takes the air, the winter mornings when the gale comes off the cape and blows through whatever you are wearing and shakes your very soul. The men go out anyway, because the houses are not paid for and the families do not eat when nothing comes home. Their rain slicks keep the water out and hold no warmth, so under them go thick base layers and sweaters against the brutal cold, and every man dressed that way knows what the clothes weigh. He knows that if he goes into the freezing water he will not get them off in time to reach the surface. He knows it, and he goes.

The community that grows up around that obligation has a particular shape. When the watermen of a place like Fox Hill say they look out for each other, they are describing the structure of the work, not a sentiment. A waterman whose nets need rebuilding before the next season needs help rebuilding them, because the nets cannot be done by one man in one season's worth of available time, and there is no industry of net-rebuilders to hire. So a morning is set. At three or four, when every man there would normally be on the water or on the way to it, the boats stay tied up and the pickups come instead to whoever's place needs the nets. They bring their own coffee. They work until it is done. The understanding under the morning is not that of a favor done but the knowledge, sure as the sun rising and the sun setting, that a morning is coming when the man who needs the help is you, or your son, or your father, and that a community that fails to come on the day you need it failed long before that day. That is the bargain, and it holds for as long as the community holds.

The most precise thing the bargain produces, the thing hardest to render for anyone who has not been around it, is what happens when one of the men who said he would be there is not. Everyone stops. The nets go down. Somebody picks up a phone and calls somebody else, and within ten minutes the whole morning has reorganized itself around the question of where the missing man is, because everyone there has done this work long enough to know that the only thing that keeps a waterman from being where he said he would be is something serious enough that he cannot tell anyone about it. It might be his truck or his health, or it might be that he was on the water at four-thirty and did not come back, and the water is the part of the morning the rest of the men go check on first. The older men carry that possibility in their bodies the way a cook carries the possibility of burns.

This is the community Fox Hill was for most of my father's life, and the community I have watched, in my own life, slowly thin.

---

To understand what is happening on the Chesapeake right now, you have to start with what the Chesapeake was. Not in some prehistoric sense, not before the Powhatan, not before the English came up the James in 1607, but in a register the people working the water still carry in their bodies. I have lived near this water long enough to have known three rough generations of the men who work it. The Bay the grandfathers fished was not the Bay the fathers fished, and the Bay the fathers fished is not the Bay the sons would have fished, had there been a Bay to fish. Across three generations the resource has been visibly thinning, everywhere, in every season.

The oyster is the easiest place to begin, because the oyster is the species the historical record carries most clearly. In the late 1880s the Chesapeake produced something on the order of fifteen million bushels of oysters a year. By the 1990s, that number had fallen to under two hundred thousand. Two orders of magnitude in a century, a 99 percent decline. Not many fisheries in the world have lost that much of themselves and remained recognizable as the same fishery, and there is a real argument that the Chesapeake oyster fishery did not. What the men were going out for in 1990 was a different thing from what their grandfathers had gone out for in 1890, even though the boats looked similar, and the tongs, and the dock at the end of the day.

Nothing in a 99 percent decline is luck or weather. The mechanisms are well documented now and were largely understood at the time. The first wave was overharvesting: dredges scraping the reefs flat, season after season, faster than the reefs could rebuild. The second wave was disease, MSX in the late 1950s and Dermo in the years that followed, parasites the weakened, low-density populations had no resilience against. The third wave was sediment, agricultural runoff smothering the oyster spat where they tried to set, and nutrient loading that fed algal blooms that consumed the dissolved oxygen the rest of the system depended on. Each wave compounded the last. Reefs that had once been visible at low tide, that ships in the colonial period had to chart around, were ground down to flat, silt-buried bottom. An oyster needs something hard to set on. By the late twentieth century, much of the bottom the oysters had built across centuries had been ground into the same mud the runoff was laying over it.

A coal seam is finite from the day it forms. You can extract it faster or slower, but you cannot extract more of it than was laid down. An oyster reef is another kind of thing entirely: an instrument that produces more oysters every year, as long as you do not damage the instrument. Take some, leave the rest to spawn, and come back next year. Done correctly, the harvest does not diminish across the generations. You can do it, and your son after you, and his son after him. The fishery makes more of itself.

What happened in the Chesapeake is what happens when the rate of taking exceeds the rate of making. For a long while the difference is invisible. The harvest is still good, the price still feels right, the boats come back full-ish most days, and there are good seasons and bad seasons, the way there have always been. Then comes the season that cannot be excused, and the difference turns out to have become visible and irreversible at the same time, because what was being taken all along included the structure that produced the renewal, written off as a cost of doing business. The reef the oysters built was the bottom the next generation would have set on. Grind the reef down and you have not just taken this year's oysters; you have taken the capacity of the bottom to make any oysters at all. From that point on, leaving the system alone is no longer an answer. You put something back, or you accept that what was lost stays lost.

Nobody marks the day this happens. Decline on this water moves the way a sunset moves: you would swear it is taking forever, and then you blink. The men going out the next morning go out the same way they went out the morning before. But something has been done that cannot be undone by any decision the next morning's men can make.

One of the oystermen my father knew, growing up on Fox Hill, was a man called Biggie. Biggie had the build of an oak, not tall, in the way some big men are not tall, but solid through the shoulders and the back, the build of a man who had been doing one specific kind of work for most of his adult life. He worked oysters. He had worked them since he was old enough to be useful in a boat with whoever brought him into one. The bars he worked were bars he had worked his entire life, and the boat he ran was a boat suited to the work. He went out before the day. He came back when the bushels were what the bushels were going to be. You could watch him shift among his beds with one oar in the water, the subtlest corrections, an effortlessness with no wasted motion in it, and see at once that his family had been doing this for generations.

Every morning he went out, Biggie carried aboard a six-pack of Coca-Cola in a wire carrier. No ice, no cooler, and no lunch. The first time my father asked him about the lunch, my father was a small boy, still young enough to be honestly puzzled, and Biggie smiled and told him his lunch was waiting for him in the water, as fresh as it could be. Once the sun was high and the work had found its rhythm, Biggie ate oysters as he worked. He kept a spot on the boat, near his body, where he set the oyster he was about to shuck, so that he never had to reach or lift his arm. The motion he shucked with was the motion of a man who had reduced the work to its smallest possible movement over a working life of doing it: a quarter turn of the wrist, the shell parting, the meat coming up clean. My father watched him, more than once, and tried to count how many oysters Biggie put away in a single day on the water. He told me, when I was old enough to ask, that he never managed to keep count, not because there were too many to count, though there may have been, but because the eating was so woven into the work that there was never a moment when an oyster started or stopped being eaten. It was simply something Biggie was doing alongside everything else he was doing, the way breathing is. My father has told that story so many times that I finish it for him now whenever he tells it again.

Biggie has been dead for more than thirty years. He died before Virginia stopped its commercial shad fishery in 1994, a date the men still on the water sometimes use to mark when things were still working. He went out with what an earlier generation of men had gone out with, and he came back with what they had come back with, and the bars sounded under his tongs the way they had sounded under the tongs of the men before him.

The bars do not sound that way anymore. The motion Biggie's body had worn down to a quarter turn of the wrist is not being shown to any younger man at Fox Hill, because no younger man at Fox Hill is going out to bars that would let him learn it.

---

The men who would tell this story, if you sat with them long enough on a dock late in a season that had not gone the way a season is supposed to go, do not call it any of these things. They do not say renewable. They do not say regeneration rate. They say what they remember. Their grandfather went out of a place that does not exist as a working dock anymore. The boats their fathers ran were named for wives and daughters, and some of those names are still on boats and some are not. When they were small they could stand in the bow on a calm summer day off the mouth of the Poquoson River and see the bottom, actually see it, the grass and the crabs walking through the grass, and a child cannot do that anymore in most parts of the Bay where it could once be done. The water is greener than it used to be. The smell at the dock has changed, slightly, in late August. Their fathers came home with a kind of catch that does not come home anymore.

A man in his sixties who has worked the Lower Bay since he was twelve does not need a stock assessment to know that something has changed. He has been making the comparison every morning for fifty years.

This is the texture of generational witness on the water, and there is nothing nostalgic in it. In tone it sits closer to the way you might describe a relative's slow illness: small, precise observations from someone who has been paying attention long enough that the attention has become part of how they see. Around men like this the feeling is not anger so much as a patient, unsentimental clarity. They are not asking to be pitied; most of them would refuse the offer. They are telling you what they have seen.

What they have seen is the disappearance of margins. Seasons that used to carry a year now carry only part of one. Species that used to fill in for each other when one ran short do not fill in the same way. The crabs are smaller, on average, than the crabs they used to bring in. The runs of striped bass their fathers had timed to the calendar do not run on the calendar anymore. The water itself seems to have a different temper. The observations run across forty and fifty years, one of the longest continuous attentional records any working person in America keeps. The bodies are the instrument. The memory is the data.

J.C. Hudgins, who runs the Virginia Watermen's Association and works the Piankatank River in the Middle Bay, has done the math out loud. *You're lucky if you can put $150 in your pocket per day,* he told *Maryland Matters*. *And it ain't no easy job. You're freezing your butt off. It's cold.* On the buying end: *People aren't going to spend $20 for a pint of oysters. It's pretty expensive eating. I think people are feeding their families in other ways.*

---

The striped bass, what they call rockfish on the Bay, is the species that taught Maryland and Virginia, in the 1980s, what intervention can do. I am old enough to remember it. The fish I loved to eat as a boy were striped bass and red snapper and grouper, and what those years taught me first was how quietly such a thing goes: you notice yourself catching one of them less often, then it crosses your mind that you have not seen it on a menu in a couple of years, and the difference gets waved away as a bad season or two. I understand now that the bad season or two was the last of the seasons they were there in any meaningful quantity, and that we went on taking as much as we could carry to the shore. The watermen I knew as a boy at the marinas where my family kept boats were talking, as the moratorium years came on, about something they had not had to talk about before. I did not understand the politics of it then. I understood, the way a child understands without being told, that a thing had reached a point where the men whose work depended on it had agreed, with whatever reluctance, to stop the work for a long time so that the thing could come back.

By the late 1970s, the population had collapsed badly enough that Maryland and Virginia, working with the federal government, agreed to a moratorium. For five years on the Maryland side, beginning in 1985, no one fished commercially for striped bass at all: not the watermen, not the charter fleet, not the recreational anglers. The species was given a generation off. It was a hard ask of the people whose income depended on the fishery; many of them lost a livelihood for the duration, and some did not come back to the work. But the moratorium worked, in the narrow sense it had been designed to. The spawning population recovered. By 1995 the fishery had been declared restored, and the men who had given up five years saw, at the end of them, an instrument brought back from a place very few fisheries get brought back from.

That recovery is part of why what is happening now is hard for the men who remember it, because what is happening now to the rockfish is what was happening to the rockfish before the moratorium. The juvenile recruitment numbers, year after year, are coming in below the long-term average. In 2024 the Maryland young-of-year index was 2.0 fish per sample, against a long-term average of 11.0. It was the sixth such season in a row. Six in a row stop being weather and start being structure.

Allison Colden, the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's Maryland executive director, watching that signal accumulate across the years, has put the situation in terms the watermen would recognize. *Maryland's striped bass surveys have made it clear,* she has said in public statements; *we're facing a looming disaster. Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future.* In a different interview, more bluntly: *there are very few levers left to pull.* Many people in Maryland, she has said elsewhere, *rely on striped bass for their food, their businesses, and their futures. Without action, that will all be at risk.* The rising water temperatures and the low-oxygen periods of summer, both worsened by warming, are *pummeling* the species, in her word, during the months it can least afford to be pummeled.

Chris Moore, the foundation's Virginia executive director, has said the harder thing in response to the 2024 stock assessment. *Despite recent management actions, striped bass continue to struggle. It is obvious that conditions have changed and striped bass will not have the same recovery they had in the 1980s. Warmer water from climate change, habitat loss, and invasive species are all making it very hard for striped bass to bounce back.*

The men on the water do not need to be told this. They felt it before the survey numbers came in. What the surveys do is force the institutions to acknowledge what the watermen have been carrying as a kind of private foreknowledge, the way miners know what the dust is doing to them years before the X-rays come back. The institutional acknowledgment, when it comes, is always late: later than the data would have allowed, later than the watermen had warned, late in a way the moratorium of 1985 was not, because in 1985 there was a political will that has thinned in the decades since, and the regulatory architecture that once could ask a fishery to take a generation off has lost some of the capacity to ask.

When the watermen read the rockfish, what they are really reading is whether the institutional system built to manage the Bay can act on what its own science is telling it. So far in this round, the answer has been mostly no. Catch limits get tightened in increments smaller than the science has indicated are warranted. Emergency actions get debated longer than the species can spare. The committees meet, the substitute motions are offered, and the language of erring on the side of conservation gets answered with the language of not yet being certain enough to act. When the offer was to increase the harvest, the committees acted with confidence. When the offer is to reduce it, the committees discover an unfamiliar caution. The asymmetry is the point.

Bill Goldsborough, the fisheries director at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation through the rockfish moratorium years, told *Smithsonian Magazine* how the institutional time-scale looks from inside the science. *Our objective is to try to restore the health of bay and aquatic life so that it can support viable fisheries,* he has said. *We see that task taking place over a longer term [...]. But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment.*

A retired waterman who worked the Bay through the 1985 moratorium, into the recovery, and into the present decline can tell you in a single sentence how this looks from his deck. He gave up five years of his life so that a fishery could come back, watched it come back, and is watching it begin to go again, and the people whose job is to manage the resource have told him, gently and not so gently, that the political conditions for another moratorium do not exist. Nobody has told him what the political conditions for another collapse are.

---

The rockfish recovery is not the only intervention story this Bay has produced. In January 1994, a few years after the rockfish moratorium ended, Virginia closed its commercial shad fishery, the spring run that had been one of the Bay's reliable fisheries through most of the twentieth century. The men whose fathers had timed the shad run to the calendar were still on the water when the runs stopped coming; they could tell you the spring the nets in late March began to come up empty. The closure was meant, like the rockfish moratorium, to give the species a generation off so the runs could rebuild. Shad live most of their lives in the open Atlantic and come into the Bay each spring to spawn, running up the tributary rivers to lay their eggs in fresh water. The Bay is the spawning ground for the shad of an entire region; when its rivers fail the shad, the shad fail in the ocean too. The runs did not come back after 1994. The science on why has not converged on a single explanation. What it has converged on is the absence of a recovery. There are not, in any working sense, shad in the Bay anymore. The same instrument that worked once for rockfish was tried for shad, and arrived too late.

---

Then there is the blue crab.

The blue crab does not build a reef. Its life cycle is short, a year and a half or sometimes two, which means in principle the population can rebound from a bad year quickly if the next year is good. For a long time this made the Chesapeake blue crab fishery the Bay's most reliable seasonal income for working watermen. The crabs were here. They came back in spring. They were tonged and trotlined and potted through the warm months, and sold to picking houses up and down the Bay, where mostly women picked the meat by hand into containers that ended up in restaurants in cities where most of the people eating them had never seen a crab pot in the water.

The fishery is now in a kind of trouble the oyster fishery has already lived through and the rockfish fishery is living through. The 2025 winter dredge survey put the Bay-wide population at around 238 million crabs, the second-lowest level since the survey began in 1990, and the sixth consecutive year of below-average numbers. *The red flags are flying for blue crabs,* Colden has said. And the structural framing: *with more than five years of below average crab numbers, it is clear that changing conditions in the Bay are undermining the current management of this important species.*

That sentence carries more than it appears to. Changing conditions are undermining a management regime that was designed for an earlier Bay; the regulatory architecture is being outrun by the system it was built to govern. The rules are not wrong so much as calibrated to a Bay that no longer exists. The committees that set the limits still meet on the schedule the previous Bay had. The crabs are no longer on that schedule, and neither is the Bay.

The men on the water have been reading this from the deck for decades. They do not call it any of that. They say what they see. They aren't where they used to be. They aren't running when they used to run. The bigger ones are gone. We aren't catching what we used to catch.

---

There is a fish you have probably never eaten. It is small, oily, and silver. It runs in schools so dense that their movement can flatten the surface of the water like a passing wind, and it is, by weight, one of the most important fish in the Atlantic ecosystem.

The menhaden. Bunker, on the New Jersey side. Pogy, in New England. On the Bay, just menhaden.

You do not eat it because it is not pleasant to eat. It is, instead, the thing that almost everything else eats: the forage fish at the base of the Bay's food web, the species the striped bass and the bluefish and the ospreys and the loons and the dolphins all come for. It is also the species harvested, in the largest single fishery on the East Coast, by an industrial reduction operation out of Reedville, Virginia, which catches it at scale and grinds it down into fish meal, fish oil, omega-3 supplements, pet food, and aquaculture feed. The catch leaves the Bay in tank trucks and railcars and on ships. People do not eat it. An industrial process converts it into inputs for industries somewhere else.

Structurally, this is an interesting case, because the menhaden is the food web. Remove enough of it and the rockfish do not have enough to eat in summer, when they are heat-stressed and oxygen-starved and trying to put on weight. In some Bay tributaries the ospreys have begun to fail to fledge their young, and the suspicion among the people who watch ospreys is that the parent birds are not bringing in enough small oily fish to feed a growing brood. People who like the phrase describe the Bay-wide signal as *a hollowing*: the bigger species look fine until you ask whether they are reproducing and whether their young are surviving, and then the picture shifts.

How much menhaden to take out of the Bay is the longest-running fisheries-policy fight in the region. There is a Bay-specific cap of 51,000 metric tons, set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission in 2017, a cut of roughly forty percent from the limit that preceded it. The conservation side regards the cap as a floor rather than a ceiling; the industry side regards it as a hard-fought concession. The relevant scientific committee has, in recent years, recommended coastwide reductions and Bay-specific precautions; the management board has, in recent years, adopted reductions smaller than the committee recommended, on schedules slower than the committee proposed. *Just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should,* Colden has said, *we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that that's warranted.*

The gap is no abstraction to the people working the science. Genny Nesslage, an associate research professor at the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science, said it plainly to WHRO: *We don't know the abundance of menhaden in the bay at any given time relative to the coast.* Rob Latour, a Virginia Institute of Marine Science menhaden researcher, said it more bluntly to the same outlet: *We have no data to really do this in a meaningful way, from a biological perspective.* In 2023, the Virginia Institute of Marine Science drafted a $3 million proposal for a Bay-specific menhaden study. The Virginia General Assembly declined to fund it.

Increases get adopted at the speed of the appetite for them, reductions at the speed of the resistance to them. The watermen know the asymmetry. The ospreys feel it.

More than any other Bay-management debate, the cap on menhaden makes visible who the system is built to serve. The fishery is not feeding people in any direct sense. It supplies intermediate inputs to industries whose end customers have no idea that the small oily fish at the base of the Chesapeake food web are being moved through a Reedville plant and into their lives. Reedville sits at the mouth of the Great Wicomico River in Northumberland County, and the plant has employed several hundred people, in shifts that follow the seasons the menhaden run. The end customers are the consumer of an omega-3 capsule in Indianapolis, the owner of a salmon farm in Chile, the buyer of a forty-pound bag of dog food in a Phoenix grocery. The value disperses across a continent and across industries. The cost concentrates here, on the rockfish, the ospreys, and the watermen who depend on a Bay that depends on the menhaden.

On the last day of 2025, a Virginia resident named Tonya O'Connor filed a 349-page petition with the Virginia Marine Resources Commission asking the agency to declare a temporary moratorium on the Bay's reduction fishery until it produced Bay-specific science. The petition's central finding put the absence in regulatory language: the commission *did not have*, and *still does not have*, *a Bay-wide estimate of menhaden biomass, age structure, spatial distribution, recruitment trends, or localized depletion risk.* The legal grounding cited Virginia's public-trust doctrine: *Commonwealth v. City of Newport News*, 158 Va. 521 (1932); *Taylor v. Commonwealth*, 102 Va. 759 (1904); *Bradford v. Nature Conservancy*, 224 Va. 181 (1982). The commission held a hearing on April 21, 2026, and received 1,266 public comments. It voted six to two against the petition, and the moratorium did not move forward.

The shape is familiar. Coal had it.

---

The Chesapeake watershed covers some 64,000 square miles across six states and the District of Columbia. The water that makes the Bay starts as rain on a corn field in central Pennsylvania, a soybean field on the Eastern Shore, a parking lot in Northern Virginia, a chicken house on the Delmarva, a wastewater plant in Baltimore, a suburban lawn in Loudoun County. By the time it reaches the Bay it is carrying everything those landscapes have given it: nitrogen from fertilizer, phosphorus from manure and runoff, sediment from disturbed ground, pesticides, pharmaceuticals, whatever the upstream economy has not contained.

The science of this has been understood for forty years. The Chesapeake Bay Program, established in 1983, has been measuring nutrient loads, modeling them, allocating them, and asking the upstream parties to reduce them ever since, with partial successes and significant failures. Point-source pollution from wastewater plants and factory discharges has, in many places, been reduced. The recent review by the Program's own science advisory committee, evaluating the forty-year effort, was as plain as such reports are allowed to be: the cleanup is not on track to meet its goals. The agricultural sector has not been required to do what the science said the agricultural sector needed to do. The political will to require it has not formed.

The science was right. The targets were modeled, the schedule was set, the jurisdictions agreed. And the loads, in the places where the loads were largest, did not come down at the rate the agreement said they would, because the political weight of the upstream agricultural economy was greater than the political weight of the downstream fishery economy, and the decisions accumulated, year by year, in the direction of the heavier weight.

From the deck of a sailboat in summer, in the years I have been on it, I have watched the green of the water change in a way that does not feel like life. The grass beds I could see through the water in the early 2000s are not visible the same way anymore. The watermen know this in a longer register; they have been watching it for decades. They know that the runoff hitting the grass beds in Mobjack Bay started as fertilizer on a field they have never seen, owned by a farmer they will never meet, in a state none of their children have lived in. They know that the dead zones, the volumes of low-oxygen water that form in the deeper parts of the Bay every summer, suffocating the bottom-dwelling species that cannot move and stressing the swimming species that can, are the downstream signature of an agriculture-and-development pattern over which they have never had any voice.

They live at the bottom of a watershed whose accounting does not include them. Everything every upstream actor does has to come past their boats; nothing they do reaches the upstream actors at all. The asymmetry is the geography, and the geography does the work the accounting will not.

There is a man on Fox Hill I am leaving unnamed here. He is alive, still on the water, and the question of how he wants to appear in a book is not yet settled between us. He is a large man, not tall, on the Bay since he was a boy, from the same Fox Hill world Biggie was from, and his work and his life have unfolded in the next stretch of the same arc.

He is a crabber and a fisherman, and he has been one all his working life. The water near Fox Hill and Poquoson is the water he has always known. What he has watched, across his decades on it, is the math change for a man like him. The catches have come back thinner. The costs of fuel and gear and the slip have moved in the other direction. The species he came up working are not all of them still running. None of this is particular to him; these are the conditions every man still working this water has had to absorb in his own way. What is particular to him is what he has done with the absorption.

He still gets up at two or three in the morning. He still goes out and pulls his crab pots, and he runs them the way he ran them when he was younger, except that the math at the end of the day is different now than it was then. So he comes back to the dock by mid-morning, hoses down the boat, and goes to the mechanic shop he owns on the Peninsula, where his name is on the sign and his crab pots are stacked behind the locked fence in the back lot, behind the cars he is working on for customers. He works the shop until five or six in the evening. What he calls this is between him and the people who know him. What it does is keep the family math balancing, the boat in the slip, the license current, the presence on the water continuing.

He did not quit the water. He could not. What he did instead was spend decades building, on his own time and his own dime, the second economy that lets him stay. The accounting that made the older men's lives possible, the accounting in which the crab and the boat and the long days were enough, does not work for him, and he has filled the gap with the only thing he had left to fill it with, which is more of himself.

I have not asked him what he would say about all this, and the chapter does not have his answer to put on this page. My guess, from watching him and from knowing the men around him, is that over a beer at the end of a long day he would say he is glad to still be on the water and grateful for the shop. The accounting this book is trying to make visible is not, necessarily, the accounting he would put on himself. It is what the surrounding economy has been accepting from him, and from many men like him up and down this Bay, in exchange for a continued supply of the seafood the surrounding economy has not stopped wanting.

---

There is a particular kind of community that grew up on the working waterfronts of the Bay, and a particular kind of decision the people in those communities are now being asked to make.

Tangier Island, in the middle of the Lower Bay, is two square miles of low-elevation marsh and sand, continuously inhabited since the 1770s. The accent spoken there is closer, by some accounts, to a colonial-era English than to anything else still spoken in North America. The economy has been the water, crabs in the warm months and oysters in the cold ones, the rhythms and the vocabulary carried through eight generations of the island's families. The island is also losing about fifteen feet of shoreline a year to erosion, and the projections suggest most of it will be uninhabitable within the lifetime of children currently in the school. The mayor and the men on the docks have asked, repeatedly, for the engineering that would buy them another few decades. The asks have, so far, mostly not been answered at the scale that would matter.

James "Ooker" Eskridge, the mayor of Tangier and a working waterman for more than fifty years, told the Thomson Reuters Foundation what was at stake: *We're talking about saving a culture, the people, (a) way of life.* The whole package, in his words. Cook Cannon, a former Tangier waterman who has spent the years since at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation, told *Smithsonian Magazine* what the prospect looked like from inside the island, sitting in his island home on a cold January night. *I could cry to think about what's going to happen to Tangier.*

Smith Island, the Maryland counterpart twelve miles to the north, is in roughly the same shape. The picking houses there have been thinning. For generations, mostly older women picked the meat from soft-shell crabs that the men on the boats brought in. The young people are leaving for the mainland. There is a saying on Smith that you get mud between your toes when you spend time there, and that the mud means the island never leaves you. The women who say it are mostly past the age where leaving was an option. The children who hear it have been on the mainland for a decade.

Up the Bay on Tilghman Island in Maryland, the same work continues. Pat Whewell was photographed culling oysters aboard Lonnie Gowe's boat in Broad Creek in 2023; Chumley Fisher and Chet Schwartz were photographed offloading oysters on Tilghman three years later. The pictures ran in *Maryland Matters*. The picking houses on Tilghman, like the picking houses on Smith, have been thinning.

Hampton, where I sit, is not Tangier or Smith. Hampton is bigger, more diversified, more capable of absorbing the slow contraction of its working waterfront into the larger economy of the city. The boats are still here, the slips still rented; the dockmasters still know who is going out tomorrow and who is not. In the years I have kept my own boat at my marina, I have watched it slowly tip from a place where the working operators had most of the slips toward a place where the recreational owners do. On a long enough horizon, the trajectory is the same trajectory. The number of working watermen on the Lower Bay has been falling for a generation. The picking houses are closing. The boatbuilders who used to build wooden deadrises are mostly gone. The supply chain that supports a working waterfront thins by one capacity at a time, the welders, the engine shops, the ice plants, the buyers, the haulers, and the community that used to come at three in the morning to help with somebody's nets does not, in some places, have the bodies to come anymore. The fabric thins one connection at a time until the thinning becomes a tipping point, past which the next year's young person who might have stayed cannot find the work that would have made staying possible. They leave. The waterfront thins by one more capacity. The next year, someone else faces the same choice, thinner.

What is being asked of these communities is something the broader economy does not have a name for. They are being asked, in effect, to disperse: to absorb their own transition costs, to relocate their families on their own dime, to retrain their young into industries that are not available locally, to abandon places their ancestors have lived in for two and three centuries, to give up languages and skills and seasonal rhythms that took those generations to develop. They are being asked to do this without compensation, without acknowledgment, and largely without notice from the upstream economy whose runoff and whose climate forcing and whose harvest pressure have, between them, made the staying impossible. The people in these places are not asking for sympathy; mostly they are asking to be allowed to keep doing what they know how to do, in the place they know how to do it, for as long as the water and the regulation and the market allow it.

The chapter on McDowell County described this same pattern from the inside of the hollow. The accent is different, and the work. The pattern is not.

---

At the marina in October, I asked a waterman I know, a man in his fifties on the Lower Bay, what he was telling his children. He paused a long time before answering. He glanced at the water. What he said was something about how the work is hard and the money is uncertain and the regulations are unpredictable. The rest I have heard, in pieces, from him and from men like him along this waterfront. That nobody is encouraging a son into the work, and that some sons go anyway, because the work is what they know and what their grandfathers did, and the choice is the son's. That a daughter in nursing school in Norfolk has more options than her father had at her age, and that he is glad of it, and that there is something in the gain he does not, in his bones, fully celebrate. The thinning of the family-on-the-water, across the generations, is a kind of liberation that is also a kind of loss.

Added up across the Lower Bay and the Eastern Shore and the small islands, the discouragement amounts to a generation-scale withdrawal of the human capital that would have continued the fishery. The fathers are not investing in the sons the years of patient teaching their own fathers invested in them. They are withholding the investment because the return, for the son, is not what the return was, and they are not willing to teach a son into a trade whose floor they cannot promise. The community that took five generations to build the knowledge of where the crabs run in late October is not building a sixth generation that will know it the same way. The knowledge is dying with the men who hold it.

This is its own kind of cost. It shows up on no stock assessment. It is the slow draining of an inheritance that took centuries to accumulate, into a generation that has been quietly told it cannot be passed on.

The children, for their part, are mostly going: to Norfolk, to Richmond, to the Hampton Roads tech corridor, to the medical centers, to the military bases, wherever a paycheck is more predictable than a season on the water. Most of them carry, somewhere, a respect for what their fathers did that they will not put into words. Some of them come back on weekends and ride out on the boat one more time to remember how it felt. Some of them never come back. The slip stays empty after the father retires, and the waterfront becomes one boat thinner, and the next father and the next son hear the news and run their own arithmetic.

Cameron Evans, a Tangier native then studying at Virginia Wesleyan, named the question for the Thomson Reuters Foundation in his own words. *If you can't save an island a mile and a half long, how are you going to save the East Coast...* Andy Harris, who represents Maryland's Eastern Shore in Congress, has framed the same arithmetic from the other side of the aisle. *Our watermen are pretty resilient,* he has told *Maryland Matters*. *They're like our farmers. They have good years and bad years. But if you have a couple bad seasons in a row, then you're in trouble.*

---

A paradox has emerged in the most recent years, and it is worth setting down carefully, because it is the most precise illustration the Bay has produced of the cost-severance pattern this book is tracking.

The Maryland oyster restoration program has succeeded, by the metrics it set for itself: five tributary-scale sanctuaries, hundreds of acres of rebuilt reef, tens of millions of oysters per year added to the rebuilt structures. The oyster population in Maryland has roughly tripled since 2005. *Large-scale oyster restoration is working,* Colden has said. *Our effort to bring oysters back from the brink in Chesapeake Bay is working,* she has said elsewhere, adding that *continued recovery is not guaranteed.*

Mitch Tarnowski, who leads Maryland's Department of Natural Resources oyster reef surveys, summed the paradox up in twelve words. *There are plenty of oysters out there,* he told *Maryland Matters*. *They're just not being harvested.*

He meant that the wild bars outside the restoration sanctuaries are not being worked. The watermen with the licenses are not on the water. The boats that used to take the oysters off those bars are mostly tied up.

In the same year the program was being celebrated as one of the largest successful ecosystem-restoration efforts in North America, the State of Maryland filed for a federal disaster declaration for the oyster fishery. The harvest had collapsed in volume and in value. The watermen who depend on the wild harvest were in financial crisis, the market for Chesapeake oysters hollowed out by Gulf Coast farm-raised competition and by extreme-weather seasons that had disrupted the harvest windows. *Despite a growing oyster population,* Colden said, in support of the disaster request, *Maryland's watermen are feeling the pinch of extreme weather and market competition.*

Jason Ruth, who owns Harris Seafood Co., an oyster processor in Grasonville, Maryland, told the same paper how the longer arc looks from inside an oyster house. *While the demand for boutique aquaculture oysters has grown over the last decade,* he has said, *the shucked oyster sector,* which is the larger part of the industry, *has seen a 60-year nationwide decline.*

The population is recovering. The watermen are in disaster. The two facts coexist because the recovery is happening in sanctuaries the watermen are not allowed to harvest, and because the harvest from the wild bars outside the sanctuaries is being undercut, in the marketplace, by aquaculture operations the watermen mostly do not own and by Gulf Coast operations that did not bear the costs the Bay's recovery is now being asked to carry. The ecosystem is being asked to come back. The watermen are being asked to absorb the cost of its coming back. The consumers eating the recovered oysters in restaurants are paying for an oyster, and they are not paying for what the watermen have absorbed in order for the oyster to be on the half-shell in front of them. They cannot pay for it. The price is not constructed to carry it.

*One of the most important ways people connect with the Chesapeake Bay,* Colden has said, *is through eating seafood and fishing.* Without watermen, *there's certainly a lot fewer eyes on the water and a lot fewer people caring passionately about the Bay.* The very people whose attention has held the Bay accountable for centuries are being squeezed out of the relationship by the same accounting that is, at the species level, beginning to recover what was lost. The recovery and the squeeze are happening at the same time. They are the same process, seen from the two sides of the ledger.

What the Chesapeake is teaching, in this paradox, is that recovery without an honest accounting of who paid leaves the people who paid still paying. The oysters can come back without the watermen coming back. The reef can be rebuilt, and the working waterfront not.

So let me do for the oyster what this book refused to leave undone for the miner. Let me add up the column.

The instrument that does the adding is one I am going to name here and build properly in a later chapter. I will call it the Restitution Bond, and the idea of it is simple even though the machinery is not. It looks backward at a commons that was drawn down, prices the physical cost of making it whole, and names who should hold that bill. It is the backward-looking partner to an instrument that looks forward, and the forward one will keep until later. Right now I only want to run the backward one, once, on this reef, and take it to a floor I can defend against anyone who wants to fight me about it.

Here is the first discipline of the floor. I am not going to price the lost fishery, or the menhaden, or the livelihoods of the watermen, or the value of all the oysters the bars would have grown if they had been left whole. Every one of those is a real cost, and every one of them invites an argument I do not need to have. I am going to price one thing only, the thing that is physical and documented and beyond dispute: the reef bottom itself, the hard foundation that was stripped out of the water and never put back. What does it cost to physically rebuild the reef that was foreclosed?

And I am not going to build the number out of Darling's shell mountains, tempting as it is, because the mountains are the wrong measuring stick. Raw shell is cheap, and the dramatic figure you would get by multiplying a peak year across a century is the kind of figure a careful critic takes apart in an afternoon, because no operation produces its peak every year for a hundred years. The four-story piles are the image of the severance. They are not the price of it. The price is built from acres.

So here is the floor. Scientists who study the James River, the river system the Hampton bars belong to, have measured how much high-quality oyster reef bottom was lost there across the twentieth century. Between 1910 and 1981 the James lost about 2,700 acres of it. I use the James figure, and I tell you plainly that I am using it as the stand-in for the Hampton bars, because the careful historical acreage exists for the James and not for Hampton Bar alone, and I would rather name my proxy than hide it. Now multiply those 2,700 acres of foreclosed reef by what it costs to rebuild an acre of reef on lower-Bay bottom where oysters still set on their own, which the restoration record puts between about $7,300 and $13,500 an acre.

That gives a floor of roughly $20 million to $37 million.

That is the conservative number, deliberately the smallest defensible figure, built from the smallest documented loss and the cheapest documented rebuild cost, because a floor that errs low is a floor nobody can knock over. Price the same acreage instead at the average cost of completed reef restoration across the whole Bay, about $77,000 an acre, and the figure comes to roughly $211 million. That is the robust center of the estimate, not the floor, and I am not leading with it. I am leading with the floor: $20 million to $37 million to physically rebuild the reef bottom foreclosed in this one river system. That is the column, added up.

Now set the number next to what the reef was worth while it stood, because the gap between the two is the whole argument.

The watermen who brought the oysters up were paid by the bushel at the dock. Price a Darling peak year at today's dockside rate, around $35 a bushel, and two hundred thousand bushels comes to about $7 million, and I will not multiply that across the decades for the same reason I would not multiply the shells. Seven million dollars for a peak year, paid to the men who did the bringing up. But the dockside fee was never what the oyster was worth. It was the fee Hampton charged to ship its own wealth away. By the time those oysters reached the tables they were sold to, at roughly two hundred oysters to a bushel and a few dollars an oyster on the plate, the same harvest was worth on the order of $120 million a year, realized in the dining rooms of Baltimore and New York and beyond. The watermen got the dockside fee. The cities got the oyster and the restaurants and the jobs and the tax base, the entire downstream economy of the Bay's own abundance, built everywhere except the Bay.

Hold the three numbers together. The value the reef produced, around $120 million a year, dispersed up and out to other regions' tables a century ago. The labor fee, around $7 million in a peak year, stayed at the dock. And the cost, the $20 million to $37 million it takes to rebuild the foreclosed reef, stayed home and went unpaid, and is coming due now. Value goes up and out. Cost stays and settles down. It is the same sentence this book wrote about a county under coal, written one more time in water.

And here is the fact that makes the case hard to argue with. The reef is already being rebuilt, right now, and the public is paying for the whole thing.

In Maryland alone, more than $92 million in combined state and federal money has gone into rebuilding oyster reef across the sanctuaries, roughly 1,300 acres of it, all of it public money. Across the whole Bay, the public total comes to roughly $115 million: the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration's own itemized accounting of the completed ten-tributary restoration puts Maryland's share at $93.52 million and Virginia's at $21.77 million, paid by NOAA and the Army Corps of Engineers and the Maryland and Virginia resource agencies, every dollar of it public. A rounder program-level figure of about $108 million is sometimes quoted for the same effort; it corroborates the magnitude, nothing more. And out at the Navy's Hampton Roads installation, the oyster castles went into the water on the Navy's account and the public agencies', the spat supplied by the Chesapeake Bay Program. I cannot give you the dollar figure for that one installation, because it was never published, and I am not going to invent a number to make a point. But I can tell you exactly who paid for it and exactly who did not. The Navy paid. The taxpayers paid. The industry that took a world's-largest oyster operation's worth of value out of this water and shipped it to other people's cities has paid nothing into the reef's return. The industry's line in this column is zero.

It is the same shape as McDowell County, and the rhyme is the proof that this is a mechanism and not an accident. In the coalfields, the value of the coal dispersed across a continent, and the cost of the black lung and the orange creeks and the wrecked land stayed in the hollows, and when the bill came due it was the public trust fund and the public reclamation money that paid it, because the companies that had collected the value were gone or shielded or simply never on the hook. Here the value of the oyster dispersed to the cities, the cost of the foreclosed reef stayed in the water, and when the bill came due it was public money and Navy labor that paid it. The defendant is the same in both, and in both it is not in the room. Coal or oyster, dry land or open water, the instrument that should have priced the cost into the value at the moment of the transaction did not exist, so the cost was severed loose and floated free until it landed, decades later, on whoever happened to be standing nearest when it came down. Usually that is the public.

What the Restitution Bond does is more modest than it sounds, and the modesty is its strength. It does not invent a new bill. The bill already exists. We are already paying it: $92 million in Maryland, roughly $115 million across the Bay, a Navy crew in the Hampton Roads mud stacking concrete blocks. The Restitution Bond does the one thing the present arrangement refuses to do. It names who should have been holding the bill all along, and it moves the cost back onto the value instead of leaving it severed onto the public. It does not pay a single waterman a single dollar, because the moment you start cutting checks to individuals you start an argument about which ones and how much that drowns the whole idea. It pays into the reef. It funds the foundation. It rebuilds the commons that was drawn down, and it asks the parties who drew it down, and their successors, to fund the rebuilding, rather than handing the invoice, one more time, to everyone.

Twenty to thirty-seven million dollars. That is the floor. The real figure is higher, and the true social cost, the fishery and the menhaden and the watermen's lost living and the hundred years of dispersed wealth, is higher still, and I have left all of it off the books on purpose, because the floor is the part nobody can take away from me. It is the smallest honest number in a very large injury, and I would rather stand on a number I can defend to the last decimal than reach for a bigger one and hand a critic the lever to dismiss the whole.

---

I started this chapter with the harbor at Old Point Comfort because that is the harbor I know. But what is happening here is happening up and down the Bay, and what is happening up and down the Bay is happening in the Gulf of Maine, in Bristol Bay, on the coast of West Africa, in the Mekong Delta. The pattern repeats wherever a fishery has been pushed past the rate at which it could replace itself. The thinning comes in stages, first in the margins, then in the center, then in the structure that produced the catch, and by the third stage the question is no longer whether the harvest will return but whether anything about the place can be saved.

The men who work the Bay are reading a slow version of this story. The boats go out. The catch is what the catch is. The picking houses thin. The young people leave. The fathers, on a Tuesday afternoon in early November, watch a son tie up after a long day and think the thought they are not going to say out loud.

The thought is that what is being lost is not just an income or an industry but a way of knowing the world, one that took generations of careful attention to develop and that cannot be rebuilt by any decision the children's generation will be in a position to make. The thought is that the men whose labor and whose communities produced the value that fed the Atlantic seaboard for three hundred years are being asked, now, to absorb the cost of the value-stream's continuation, under conditions they did not design and do not control.

It is the same thought the men in McDowell County had been thinking for two generations before the books got written about them.

While I was finishing this chapter, I went back to McDowell County in my mind. The miner who developed black lung was not compensated for the lung; he was paid for the coal. The community that emptied out around him was not compensated for the emptying. The river that runs orange below the closed mine was not compensated for the orange. These are accounting facts, not metaphors.

The waterman has been having the same accounting done to him for longer, in a different register. He is paid for the crab and not for the Bay. He is paid for the oyster that comes up in the tongs, and not for the reef the tongs ground down across a century, or for the grass beds the runoff smothered, or for the menhaden the reduction fleet is removing from the food web his life depends on, or for the Tangier shoreline going under at fifteen feet a year, or for the daughter who has gone to nursing school in Norfolk because there was no boat for her to come back to. He is paid for what comes up in the tongs.

What does not come up in the tongs gets left in the water, and what gets left in the water has to be carried by something. By the Bay, while the Bay can carry it. By the watermen, after the Bay can no longer carry it alone. By the working waterfront, and the families, and the daughters who do not come back, and the children who never had a chance to learn the late-October crab runs, because by the time they were old enough to learn, the runs were no longer running on a schedule anyone could teach. The resource is different, and the geography. The mechanism is the same.

Put the miner and the waterman in a room together and they would not recognize each other immediately. They live in different states. They watch different news. They vote, when they vote, for different people, and they have been told different stories about who is to blame for what has happened to them. The waterman has been told that the people who would clean up the runoff are the same people who would shut down his harvest. The miner has been told that the people who would clean up the air are the same people who would close his mine. Each of them has been given an enemy who is, in fact, the other one. The system has been extracting from them both, pricing the value of what they produce without pricing the cost of producing it, and it is maintained, in part, by keeping them in those rooms with those stories.

What the Bay and the hollow have in common is not the resource but the position. They are both at the bottom of a watershed of value that flows somewhere else.

---

There is, in the world, a place that has handled this question differently.

A small country in northern Europe, smaller in population than the state of Virginia, came into possession of a finite resource at the bottom of one of its territorial seas in the late 1960s and early 1970s. The country's leaders made a different decision than most other countries had made. They had looked at what happened to other resource-rich nations in similar circumstances, the way the rents dispersed, the way the costs concentrated, the way the future was spent in the present and the next generation was left to absorb the bill. They built an instrument: rules that held the value coming out of the seabed in trust for the people of the country and for the generations not yet born, a fund with limits on what could be drawn. Against more pressure than is usually acknowledged, they have kept the fund intact for more than thirty years.

That country is Norway, and it is the closest thing the world currently has to an existence proof: a working demonstration, in a real economy with real political pressures, that the mechanism this book is naming is not inevitable. The rents from a finite resource can be captured at the source. The costs to the present can be honored without spending the future. A country can extract from a commons without severing the costs of the extraction onto the people the commons belongs to. The instrument is not perfect; no instrument is. But it answers the most important question, which is whether the alternative is even possible. It is. They have been doing it for more than three decades.

The watermen of the Chesapeake do not know about Norway. Most of them have never heard of the fund. If you described it to them, at the marina at the end of a long day, they would listen quietly, the way watermen listen to most things, and at the end they might say something measured and short. That it is a long way away, maybe. Or that their grandfathers fished the Bay before there was a Norway anyone had heard of, and that the Bay is what they have.

The chapter that follows asks whether the instrument that was built in Oslo could, in some adapted form, be built for the Bay; whether what was done for the oil could be done for the oyster; whether the rents that have been quietly leaving this watershed for two centuries might, in some future this book is trying to make conceivable, begin to stay.

For now, the boats are out at the mouth. The light has crossed the channel and reached the top of the Fort. A man two slips over has come in early, empty, and is hosing down. A truck door closes somewhere on the parking lot. The day is beginning. Within my father's lifetime, on this same water, a man stepped aboard at this hour with six bottles of Coca-Cola in a wire carrier and nothing else, because his lunch was waiting for him in the water, as fresh as it could be. It is the day a waterman has, on the Bay he has, with the accounting the country has agreed to do. It is not yet the day he could have had.
