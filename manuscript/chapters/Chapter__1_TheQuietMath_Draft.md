# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 1: The Quiet Math

---

The first thing he did in any new city was find the highest place in it and climb.

A rooftop, a tower, a hill on the edge of town with a road that switched back on itself until the whole city lay open below. He went up so he could look down on a place the way the people who had lived there their whole lives never had. He told me once that you do not really see a city from inside it. You see your street, your stop, the door you walk through every morning. You have to get above it before the shape of the thing shows itself, before you can see where the river bends and why the old roads run the way they do and which neighborhoods the money flowed to and which it flowed past. He had done this in more countries than I had ever set foot in, and I have now traveled to fifty-eight of them as of this writing. He was better at languages than I will ever be. When he learned that I carry fifteen nationalities in my blood, he started greeting me each morning in the native dialect of one of them, rotating through, so that I never knew which tongue would meet me at the door. He did this for everyone he cared about. He made a point of learning hello and goodbye, good morning and good evening, in the language of every person he befriended, because he understood, better than anyone I have known, that hearing a greeting in your mother's tongue even once a week brings a smile to a person living far from home. He had been far from home. He knew the ache, and he had decided to spend his life easing it in others, one small word at a time.

He was the most credentialed person in the building.

I was not. I was a junior developer, about two years into the work, no certifications, no college degree, twenty-something and learning on the job. He held degrees and standing that the company could not have replaced if it had tried. The contracts that kept the lights on, that kept the rest of us employed, leaned on his name the way a roof leans on a single load-bearing wall. Take him out and the structure does not sag. It comes down. Everyone there understood this without ever saying it.

He made about half of what I made.

The most credentialed person in the building made about half of what the least credentialed person in the building made, and there was nothing he could do about it. I do not mean he chose to do nothing. I mean there was nothing he could do. He was not a citizen. His right to remain in the country was tied to his job. His family was here, his children were here, the whole life he had built was on this side of a line he could be sent back across the moment the company decided it no longer wished to hold the other end of the cord. So he came to work, and he did the work, and he did it beautifully, and the company collected the long difference between what his work was worth and what it paid him, and that difference went somewhere, and it did not go to him.

I had no word for what I was looking at. I want to be precise, because it is the reason I am telling you any of this. I did not lack the word because I was slow. I lacked it the way everyone around me lacked it. We could all see him. We all knew, more or less, the shape of his situation. It was not a secret. There was simply no place in our ordinary way of talking about work and pay and fairness and the market where the thing I was looking at could be set down and named. A free employee, freely working, freely paid, free to quit whenever he liked. That was the language we had. And the language was a lie, not because anyone was lying, but because it had no room in it for a man who could not leave.

What I felt standing near him was older than language. I felt that something was being taken. Not stolen the way a thief takes, with a hand in a pocket. Taken in a quieter way, a way built to run smoothly and leave no fingerprints. The value he generated went up and out and settled into accounts that were not his. The cost of the arrangement, the gap between his worth and his pay, the constrained years, the daily knowledge that he could not go, all of that settled down onto him and stayed. He carried it. He could not bill it back to anyone. There was no invoice he could send and no court that recognized the debt. The whole thing was legal and ordinary, and it sat in my chest like a stone.

And here is the part I did not understand at the time. I recognized it on sight. I looked at this man, a near stranger, separated from me by language and origin and circumstance, and what moved in me was not pity and was not guilt. It was recognition. The recognition ran both ways, I think. He had spent his life seeing the people the world walked past, learning their words, honoring what usually goes unhonored, and so he saw me, and named me each morning in a tongue of my mothers. And I saw him. I knew this thing I was looking at. I had no idea, then, how I knew it.

---

I would learn how I knew it. But the knowing came in pieces, and the first piece had nothing to do with him. It had to do with a lease.

When I moved to the region around Washington, the one the people who live there call the DMV, I did what every new arrival does. I looked for somewhere to live and I ran the math that everyone runs, which is rent against square footage against distance from work. Farther out was cheaper. Everyone knew this. I knew it. So I found a place farther out, more room, an extra bedroom, three hundred dollars a month under anything I could find near the office. I drove the route once before I signed, late at night, the roads empty, and it took forty-eight minutes end to end, and I believed I understood what I was agreeing to.

I did not understand what I was agreeing to.

The first day I drove it during working hours it took two and a half hours each way. Not the driving alone. The drive to the train, the parking, the ride into the city, the walk. Two and a half hours in the morning and two and a half hours at night, plus forty dollars to park, until I learned that parking in the alleys and eating the occasional ticket came out cheaper than the lot. I got home that first night emptied of whatever optimism I had carried into the lease. I worked five days a week, six when we were busy.

Run the arithmetic with me, because the arithmetic is the thing. Five hours a day. Five days a week. Fifty-two weeks. Thirteen hundred hours. In one year, in exchange for the cheaper apartment, I spent thirteen hundred hours in a car and a train and a station, going nowhere, generating nothing, becoming no one. Thirteen hundred hours is enough time to learn a language. It is enough time to learn an instrument well enough to play for people. It is enough time to fall in love, and, if you are not careful, it is exactly enough time to fall out of it.

I had saved three hundred dollars a month. Thirty-six hundred dollars across the year. And against that thirty-six hundred dollars I had spent thirteen hundred hours of my one life, and not one of those hours appeared anywhere when I signed. They were not on the listing. They were not in the rent. They were not in the calculation everyone runs, the calculation I had run, the one that told me, correctly, that the apartment was cheaper. The apartment was cheaper. It was also the most expensive place I have ever lived, and it would be years before I could say both of those things at once and have them both be true.

Nobody did this to me. There was no villain at the leasing office, no scheme. I did it to myself, freely, with full information about everything except the one thing that turned out to matter most, which was the cost no one had put on the invoice. And once I had felt that cost in my own body, once I had spent the thirteen hundred hours and understood too late what they had been worth, I could not unsee the shape of it. I started looking for the missing number everywhere. Not as a theory. As a flinch, the way you favor a joint you have hurt.

Seeing became a method. Without my deciding it, I had started running a kind of math nobody taught me, the math of what a thing actually costs, including the part nobody writes down, including the part that lands on someone who never agreed to carry it. I did not name it. I just ran it, under everything, all the time.

---

And then I saw it everywhere.

I saw it in the watermen of the water I grew up on, the men who work the Chesapeake, out before light and back with what the water gives, and increasingly with less of what it used to give, because the water has been made to carry costs no one ever put on an invoice, costs that settle onto the oyster and the crab and the men who chase them while the value floats off upstream and inland and out of reach. I will tell you about them properly later. They deserve a chapter, not a sentence. For now I only want you to feel the recognition arrive, fast and without permission.

I saw it in the worker, any worker, who generates a value that does not stay near the worker, that flows past and settles as someone else's fortune. The hand that builds the thing, paid for an hour of building, while the thing goes on earning for years into accounts that are not the builder's.

And I saw it in the deepest version, the one that is the floor under all the others, the one I cannot speak honestly about any of this without naming. People whose labor was taken from them entirely, by force, for another's fortune. Not only their labor. Their freedom, and beneath their freedom, the thing harder to name and worse to lose, the freedom to choose how to live a life at all. I am the child of a place that sits on that history. I think of Harriet Tubman, who escaped slavery and reached her own freedom and then went back, again and again, into the country that would have re-enslaved her, to carry others north along the secret road. The wrongness there is not a missing line on a ledger. It is the ledger torn up and the person who kept it sold. The shape rhymes with everything else in this book. It is the same vector, value generated by one person and captured by another. But the weights are not the same and will never be the same. A commute and a stolen life do not belong on the same line, and they will not share one here. Naming the rhyme is not the same as making them equal.

I am moving quickly through these, and that is how it happened. Once the lens had formed, the examples did not arrive one at a time, politely, in order. They arrived all at once, each one calling up the next, until I could not walk through an ordinary day without three or four of them surfacing on their own. I suspect that as you read, your own have started to arrive too. The job you took or left. The person you watched carry something that was never theirs. The thing you bought cheap that someone down a chain you could not see paid the real price for. You have witnessed this your whole life. So had I. The seeing was not new. What was new was that I had finally been handed a way to see it on purpose, instead of feeling it as a stone in the chest and reaching, every time, for a word that was not there.

---

I had been handed the feeling, it turns out, long before I was handed any of the words.

The man my own son would never meet was my grandfather. The family called him Pooh, after the bear in the children's story, a thing my father did not tell me until I was grown. He spent his entire working life at NASA Langley, in Hampton, Virginia, from the years when the agency was still called NACA, through the change to NASA in 1958, until he retired not long before he died. He was a model maker in the wind tunnels. He was an inventor, and his name is in the patent record.

Let me tell you what it means to be a model maker in a wind tunnel, because it turns out to be the quiet center of this whole book, and I did not understand that for most of my life.

A wind tunnel tells the truth about an aircraft that does not yet exist. You cannot fly the real thing to find out whether it will fly, because if the answer is no, the answer is paid for by people who cannot recover from it. So instead you build the world in miniature. Wood and metal and resin, a scale model of a craft that lives nowhere yet, and you set it in the moving air and let the air tell you the truth. But the air only tells you the truth if the model was built faithfully enough for the truth to come through. A careless model gives back a confident, beautifully measured, completely false answer, and the false answer kills people. So the discipline is specific and unforgiving. Build it true. Build it true enough that the truth can be seen while there is still time to act on it. My grandfather's work had to hold up where being wrong is paid for by someone who cannot survive the error.

He built faithful models so the truth could be seen in time, and he did not think much of it.

I know the not-thinking-much-of-it from the story of the air compressor, which my father told the way you tell a thing that has become a small family scripture. My father had stopped by the workshop behind the house, the place where my grandfather spent the hours he did not spend at the tunnels, every tool in its assigned spot. My father noticed the air compressor was sitting out of its place, which in that workshop was like finding a word misspelled in a book my grandfather had written. So he asked, casually, whether he might borrow a drill bit, his eye on the compressor the whole time. The bits were worn out, my grandfather said. Not ready to use. And then, almost as an afterthought, that the compressor's engine had given out on him.

A week or two later my father came by again and the compressor was running. He asked what had happened to it. My grandfather shrugged without turning his head from whatever was in front of him and said he had machined new pistons and new rings and rebuilt the engine. Then he nodded toward the drill bits on the bench. Those are good to go now, he said. He had sharpened them too. He had machined an engine back to life and sharpened a set of bits nobody had asked him to sharpen, and he mentioned both the way you mention the weather. He did not think anything of it. That was the man. The value he generated was real and large, and the fame and the fortune of the enterprise he served went, as it always does in the patient ordinary machinery of salaried invention, to the enterprise. That is how it is built to work. No one reached into his pocket. The value simply went up and out and settled somewhere that was not the man who made it, and the man who made it carried the difference and did not think anything of it.

My father carried it forward. He was a naval architect, a civil servant who worked for the Navy, who held a high clearance and traveled on a passport that read, across the top, DIPLOMATIC PASSPORT. He was tight-lipped all my childhood about what he actually did. He sat in rooms with captains and admirals. What he passed to me was never subject matter, which I never heard, but a way of being in the world.

He taught it to me almost without seeming to teach. He told me, when I was a boy, that even if I grew up to dig ditches for a living, I should be able to look at my work at the end of the day and know in my heart that no one had ever done it better. He said pride in your own work makes the hard days a little easier, and that knowing you did well is often enough, even when no one else will ever see what you did. And he had a way of asking, before he came to look at something I had done, whether I would be proud of what he was about to see, and then never forcing me to answer, because the question did the work without one.

I remember an afternoon. He had given my sister and me some chore, the living room or the backyard, and we were small and we hurried it. We swept the dust under the corner of the rug. We pushed the sticks from the yard under the old shed. Then we came back and announced we were done and asked whether we could go play. He started to rise from his chair, and then he paused with his hands still on the armrests, and he looked at us, and he said, Before I get up, when I go to check the work, will you be proud of what I am about to see? My sister and I looked at each other. Wait, we said. Wait, do not get up yet. Let us do a quick walk through. We ran for the broom and the dustpan and carried the dust under the rug to the trash. We crawled under the shed and pulled the sticks out and broke them down. We edged the lawn and swept the sidewalks, and by the time we walked back to him we were standing taller, and we were ready, finally, for him to look.

He had a way of holding a room, too, that I have used my entire career. He sat with men who carried large personalities, and he never raised his voice. He did not even speak at the room's volume. He spoke a hair below it, and only when he had something worth saying, and the strange thing that happens when a person with something worth hearing begins to speak just under the volume of the room is that you do not have to exercise any power over anyone. They quiet themselves down to hear you.

So three generations of men in my family pulled long hours and generated value and watched the fortune of it settle into someone else's accounts, and did not, on the whole, think very much of it, because the not-thinking-much-of-it was itself the inheritance. The grandfather was the first instance I can name. And when I stood near the colleague years later and felt the stone settle in my chest, I was not feeling something new. I knew the shape of value flowing away from the person who made it because it was the shape of my own family, three generations deep. That is why I recognized him on sight. The bone-sense was inherited. I had been given the feeling long before I was given the words.

There was one more handing-down, and it came from the smallest member of the family. Years after my grandfather died, my sister had a son. He was at the age of learning everyone's names. He pointed at her and said momma. He pointed at me and said Uncle Chris. He pointed at my father and reached for Pappou, the Greek word for grandfather, and what came out of his mouth instead was Pou. My father got a particular kind of smile on his face, and something moved in his eye that I did not, at the time, understand. He nodded. Pou it is, he said, and he did not explain anything to anyone in the room. I did not know, then, that his own father had been called Pooh, and that this small boy had just handed him, by accident, the sound of his father's name. I would not understand it for another six or eight years. The boy was too young to know what he had given my father back.

---

I started my own working life downstream of greatness, which is a useful place to learn humility. I was twenty-two, on a contract at the National Institutes of Health that was supposed to last three months. The Human Genome Project was racing toward completion while I was there. I make no claim on that work. It belonged to researchers I still count among the finest minds alive. My job sat downstream of theirs. I built the things that took their research, ran it through peer review, and translated it for the people who needed it.

That sounds simple and it was deceptively hard. The same finding about a marker for some rare genetic condition had to be rigorous enough that the specialists in the field would take it seriously, and clear enough that a child living with that very condition could understand, on the same page, what had been found and what it might mean for her. There were, at a baseline, around fifteen of these versions for around fifteen different audiences, sometimes climbing into the mid-twenties, running from the researchers who would take the finding and build on it all the way down to the children themselves. We were not promising those children a cure. We were telling them that people were working, and that the work might one day mean a different kind of life for children like them. There is a particular look on a child's face when she understands that someday a child might not have to be sick the way she has been sick. That look is part of why I stopped sleeping properly for years.

The project I had been hired to look at for three months had already been running for two. It had been planned for a team of around forty people, one year, about a million dollars. I am rounding the team down, by the way. It could have been thirty-six. It could have been closer to fifty. Even then, in my own head, I was reaching for the smallest honest number I could stand behind, a habit I did not know I had until much later, when I found I had built an entire way of thinking on top of it. By the time I arrived the project was at about a million and a half and a year past due, and the plan was to ask for another year and another million and more people. I noticed that even while people were explaining the project to me, most of the explaining was a defense of all the work that had been piled onto it. So I cut everything that had drifted away from the original scope, and three months later there was a working proof of concept, and the contract was renewed, and they handed me another project a different team had given up on, and then another, and the renewals kept coming.

What made me good at it was not special knowledge. It was the same thing my grandfather used when he sharpened the drill bits nobody had asked him to sharpen. It was a willingness to ask the question my role did not include, to work at the seams the documentation never covered, to refocus on what the actual problem was instead of the problem everyone had agreed to keep having.

Years later, a CEO by then, I drove up to a cable television station on the East Coast at night. The lot was empty under the sodium lights and the building was lit from inside, the broadcast running on a queue that would run dry in three days and then play dead air on every commercial break for the rest of the week, which would have meant refunding the revenue and likely losing the clients for good. The system that played the commercials worked perfectly. The system that loaded new ones into it was dark, and nobody in the building knew where the server even was. I asked the only question I have ever really known how to ask, which is whether anything had changed in the last week. A new guy pointed at a dusty machine in the corner and said that a few days back there had been a storm, and the little red light on that one had stopped blinking. He did not know what it did. Nobody there did. They were not careless. They were competent people who had inherited systems other people installed, following the startup and shutdown sequences they had been handed, doing exactly the work their roles defined. The question I happened to walk in willing to ask was simply not in any of their job descriptions. It could not be. That is how institutions are built. The machine turned out to be a decade-old server from a famous broadcasting company, and the company found a warehouse with parts that had never been used, and put me on the phone with a worker in Chicago who said he would get on a plane if I bought him a round-trip ticket and put cash in his hand. Across the warehouse search and the meetings to release the funds and the part's flight in, I slept on the production suite floor under the mixing board in stretches, three hours and then half an hour at a time. We were back up in three days. The station thanked us and asked about further work, and when I gave them a number for a retainer, they declined.

I tell you this not to praise the skill but to be honest about its price, because the skill and the price are the same thing seen from two sides. I billed over a hundred and twenty hours in a week for stretches that ran into months, and there were three weeks back to back, before a deadline, where I billed close to a hundred and thirty-eight hours every seven days, out of the hundred and sixty-eight a week contains. I delivered early. There was a stack of unopened paper paychecks on my desk. My hair grew long because there was no time for a haircut, so I started shaving my own head in the bathroom with the phone on mute during conference calls, and I ate, when I remembered to eat, from a drive-through window on mute during conference calls. I woke one morning at my desk with the laptop still open and realized I had fallen asleep mid-sentence in an email, and the thing I remember is cursing at myself, not for falling asleep, but for not having clicked send before sleep took me, because someone needed it before they woke so they could keep the work moving. Sleep took me. I had stopped choosing it.

That pattern, the one I learned at the NIH and carried the rest of my career, the inherited willingness to absorb the whole cost of the work into my own body without thinking much of it, would one day cost me the early years with my son, and cost me my marriage.

---

By the time I was deep into my best years, the company I worked for told me, correctly and without cruelty, that the leave had run out, that the protected time was over, that it was time to do what I was good at again. There was no villain in this. There almost never is. The company did precisely what its protocols told it to do. My colleagues were not indifferent. The system did exactly what it was designed to do, which is the entire problem, and the entire reason I needed, eventually, to build a way of seeing it. A free-market economist would tell me, correctly, that I had made voluntary trade-offs, and I had. I had options a coal miner does not have. I had rare skills, several possible employers, the freedom to move, and significant pay. And with every advantage the language of voluntary choice could grant me, I still ended up doing the thing the pattern had always pulled me toward.

So I went, when the leave ran out. I pulled away from my family at the worst moment of my life and got on a plane and flew north, and as the plane climbed I looked out the window and watched Savannah, Georgia fall away beneath me, the river and the marsh and the city shrinking until I could see the whole shape of it at once, the way you can only see a place once you have left the ground.

And I thought of him. The colleague, on his rooftops and his hills, looking down on Washington and Paris and Taiwan, on cities all over the world, seeing what the people who lived their whole lives inside them had never climbed high enough to see. I had finally reached his vantage. I had it now, pressed to a small oval window at altitude, and from it I could see, all at once and too clearly, the exact shape of the cost I was living inside. I had spent my life learning how to get above a thing to see it. I just had not understood, until that plane lifted off, that the thing I most needed to see from above was my own life, and that I would only get the altitude at the moment I was being carried away from everything in it that mattered.

What I was being carried away from was a small room full of colored light where, on the mornings I could get there, I held my son.

He had come early, and he was very small, and the nurses had told me, almost apologetically, that it is not good for a baby to go without being held by a human, that they tried, they always tried, but there were so many babies and so few of them, and could I come and lie back in the chair beside his bed and let them set him on my chest. Getting the chair and the wires arranged safely was slow work, so it was only two or three mornings out of seven. But on those mornings I held him. Left hand resting on top of him, right hand underneath, supporting his bottom and his weight, the whole of him almost nothing in my two hands. At first the feel of him was unfamiliar to me, the way anything is the first time. And then something turned over in me, and I understood that my chest was not just another kind of bed for him. It was warm to the touch in a way no plastic bed could ever be. That was the whole of it. A warm chest instead of a plastic bed. I have never in my life been more completely in one place than I was on those mornings, counting my breaths, his weight in my hands, with nowhere else I was and nothing else I was doing.

Then the leave ran out, and the deals had to close, and the mortgage had to be paid, and the lights in the house had to stay on. So I flew north.

The marriage did not make it through. I will only tell you my side of that, because the rest is not mine to tell, and I am going to hold that boundary as carefully as I held the boundary around the weights of slavery, because the same honesty is at stake. It did not make it through the distance and the trauma. There were other reasons. There always are. It would be a lie, and a kind of self-flattery dressed up as confession, to say the work alone ended it. It did not. But the distance and the trauma and the absence at the moment that most needed presence were in it, and in the end it did not make it through.

The house is not the thing I miss.

I had bought a beautiful house, and the beautiful house had a mortgage, and the mortgage had to be paid, and the only way to pay it was to keep doing the thing that pulled me away. I have thought, many times since, about the smaller life I did not choose. A tiny house on the coast. A cabin in the woods. A boat. Any of them would have cost so little to keep that I could have stopped working when it mattered most and stayed afloat for a while, long enough, maybe, to be where I was needed instead of on a plane. The beautiful house carried a cost that never appeared on the listing, the same kind of cost the cheaper apartment had carried, the kind nobody writes down, the kind that lands on a life and stays there. The house was a thing I had to keep feeding, and feeding it took the one thing it was supposed to shelter.

And here is the harder number, the one that closes the door on the comfortable idea that I simply earned too little, that more money would have fixed it. In my best years, the peak of them, salary and commission together touched around four hundred thousand dollars. That was not a steady annual figure. It was the ceiling, the highest the earning ever reached, in the best years only. And even at that ceiling, even at the very top of everything my skill and my inheritance and my refusal to sleep could generate, it would not have been enough to cover the year and more of surgery and rehabilitation that life eventually required. Not at the peak. Not at the absolute top of what I could earn. The system that priced my labor so well, that rewarded the inherited pattern so handsomely, could not, at its most generous to me, cover the thing a life will eventually need covered. If it could not cover me at the ceiling, sit for a moment with what that means for everyone earning beneath it, which is to say almost everyone.

---

What the years finally gave me was not the satisfaction of righting the things that felt wrong, it was the ability to shine a light on them. To make the unseen, seen. It took me back to the morning that man greeted me in my mother's language, and it gave me the arithmetic that shows the system as it is, and that proves the decisions we made would not all have been the same if we had known the real cost.

This is an honest accounting. It does not undo the damage.

The son I once held is somewhere in the world now. I do not know where. I wish I knew. We have no contact.

I am writing for the ones still ahead of us, for the unseen costs of the decisions not yet made, so that we can see the true cost before the truth is too late to use.

This is the ledger. I can only point out the empty columns. The numbers are yours to enter.
