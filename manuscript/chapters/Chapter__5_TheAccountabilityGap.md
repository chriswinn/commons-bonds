# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*

## Chapter 5: The Accountability Gap

There is a pattern that, once you have seen it clearly, you cannot stop seeing. It is not a conspiracy and it has no single author. It works like this. An entity, a company, a generation, a political system, draws value from something it depends on but did not produce: a mineral seam, a fishery, a river, an atmosphere, a body of labor, the trust between citizens, the future itself. The drawing of that value generates a cost. And then the cost is detached from the party that benefited and pushed onto a party with less power, less mobility, and less ability to refuse. By the time the cost comes due, the party that benefited has usually moved on. The mine has closed. The company has reorganized. The executives have retired. The politicians who made the promises have left office. The cost remains, and it has been routed to whoever happened to be standing where it landed.

The reckoning always arrives. That is the one reliable thing about this pattern. The bill is never cancelled, only deferred and reassigned. The only open question, ever, is who is still standing in the place where it lands.

This chapter walks the pattern across a deliberately scattered set of cases. A vermiculite mine in a small town in Montana. An oil platform in the Gulf of Mexico. A tanker on a reef in Alaska. A rare-earth processing complex in Inner Mongolia. A national financial system that nearly stopped working in 2008. The retirement compact between American generations. And the healthcare system that, when it cannot pay, routes its shortfall through online fundraising pages and the unpaid labor of exhausted families. The cases are separated by geography, by industry, and by decades. They have different victims and different beneficiaries. They run on completely different arithmetic. A mine is not a mortgage. A herring fishery is not a pacemaker. And yet, beat for beat, the mechanism is identical. Something shared and depended upon is treated as if it were free, the cost of treating it that way is real, and that cost is severed from the people who gained and fastened to the people who cannot get away.

The register climbs as we go. We start with a town and end with a single dying body, and in between we pass through an ecosystem, a global supply chain, an entire national economy, and time itself. The economy in 2008 is the hinge, the point at which the pattern becomes hardest to look away from, because it strikes everyone at once and no story about distance or foreign culture or someone else's bad luck survives contact with it. But the town comes first, because the town is where the mechanism is small enough to hold in one hand.

## Libby, Montana

For roughly twenty-seven years, from 1963 to 1990, the W.R. Grace company operated a vermiculite mine above the town of Libby, in the far northwest corner of Montana. Vermiculite is a useful mineral. Heated, it puffs up into a light, fire-resistant material that makes excellent insulation, and Grace sold it under the brand name Zonolite. The Libby mine was not a marginal operation. For much of its life it produced the large majority of the vermiculite used in the United States, and Zonolite insulation found its way into the attics and walls of tens of millions of American homes. The operation was profitable. By the ordinary measures a company uses to judge itself, it was a success.

The vermiculite at Libby was contaminated. Threaded through the ore was a particularly lethal form of asbestos, the kind that lodges in the lining of the lungs and, decades later, produces cancers and scarring that suffocate a person slowly. This was not a hidden geological surprise that no one could have known. The internal company documents later released in litigation show that the contamination was identified early and that asbestos exposure among the workers was measured throughout the operation's life. The mining continued. Miners carried the dust home on their clothes, into kitchens and onto the laps of children. Children played on the waste piles, which the company had let the town use as fill for the high-school running track and for residential gardens. The dust did not stay at the mine. It became the air the town breathed.

The human toll is documented and it is large. Hundreds of Libby residents have died of asbestos-related disease. Thousands more have been diagnosed with the lung abnormalities that mark exposure. The Environmental Protection Agency eventually declared a public-health emergency in Libby, the first time the agency had ever made such a declaration for a disaster made by human hands. A town of a few thousand people had become a place where neighbors carried oxygen tanks to the grocery store and where funerals for the relatively young were not rare. The mortality from asbestos disease in Libby has run at many times the national rate.

Now set the company's gains beside the town's losses, and the severance becomes legible. Over its operating life the Libby mine generated revenue on the order of one hundred million dollars. Against that sits a long and still-lengthening tail of cost: the medical care for the sick and dying, the environmental remediation of a town saturated with fibers, the legal settlements, the collapse of property values, and the open-ended health monitoring a poisoned population requires. Pinning a single clean ratio to this is harder than the rounder numbers that sometimes circulate suggest. The documented public spending on Libby, the federal Superfund remediation and the related government outlays, sums to something on the order of nine hundred million dollars to a billion, which against the mine's revenue is already a ratio of roughly nine or ten to one. That figure does not yet count the uncompensated cost of the deaths and the disease, the years of life lost, the caregiving hours, the futures foreclosed, which no settlement ever fully priced. Add even a conservative valuation of those and the total cost plausibly reaches into the low billions. The honest way to state it is as an order-of-magnitude estimate built from disclosed components rather than as a single authoritative number: a company that took roughly a hundred million dollars in revenue left behind costs at least an order of magnitude greater, borne almost entirely by people who never saw the revenue.

And here is the part of the Libby story that matters most for everything that follows, because it is not about the harm but about the architecture that absorbed it. In 2001, with the liabilities mounting, Grace declared bankruptcy. The corporation reorganized. Its assets were restructured and its obligations channeled into the slow machinery of a bankruptcy trust. Shareholders and executives moved on with their lives. The town did not move on, because the town could not move; the fibers were in the soil and the lungs of the people who lived there.

In 2005 the federal government indicted W.R. Grace and several of its senior executives, naming them by their roles in the operation. It was, at the time, the largest environmental criminal prosecution the country had attempted. After a long trial, in 2009, the defendants were acquitted.

It is important to read that acquittal correctly. It is not evidence that the harm did not happen. The harm is documented in death certificates and remediation budgets and the EPA's own emergency declaration. The acquittal is evidence of something else: that the criminal law, as it is built, is not structurally suited to this kind of mechanism. Criminal prosecution is designed to find an identifiable act, committed with identifiable knowledge, at an identifiable moment, by an identifiable person. It is built to catch a decision. But the mechanism at Libby did not live in a single decision. It lived in the accumulation of thousands of ordinary operating choices spread across twenty-seven years, each defensible on its own, none of them the single provable act that the law requires. The law looked for the moment and could not find it, because there was no moment. There was a process.

This is the point the legal scholar Katharina Pistor makes with great force in her book *The Code of Capital*. Capital, she argues, is not a natural substance; it is a legal artifact, assembled out of legal modules, property, collateral, trust, the corporate veil, bankruptcy, and the choice of which jurisdiction's law applies. These modules do not merely describe wealth; they manufacture and protect it, and they decide who can be reached when something goes wrong. Seen through Pistor's eyes, the bankruptcy discharge that capped Grace's liability and the acquittal that ended the prosecution are not failures of the system. They are the system performing exactly as designed. The modules that let a corporation limit its liability, shed its obligations, and dissolve the identifiable actor into a process are the same modules that make large-scale extraction financeable in the first place. The discharge and the acquittal are design features of the code that encodes capital, not accidents within it. Where Pistor diagnoses the modules that encode and protect the severance, the work of this book is to price what those modules sever.

## Deepwater Horizon

Move from the town to the ocean. On the twentieth of April, 2010, the Deepwater Horizon drilling rig, working a well operated by BP in the deep Gulf of Mexico, suffered a blowout. Eleven workers were killed. Over the following eighty-seven days, before the well was finally capped, roughly 4.9 million barrels of oil were released into the Gulf. The rig had been drilling through geological formations that BP's own engineers had flagged as carrying extreme risk. The pressure that the formations could not safely hold was known to be there.

BP had extracted enormous value from the Gulf over the years, and from this well and its neighbors specifically. The value was real, banked, and distributed to shareholders. Then the well blew, and the cost of that value went looking for somewhere to land.

It landed in every dimension that the word severance can carry. There was spatial severance: the oil from the Gulf was refined and sold and consumed in cities far from the water, and the poison stayed behind to foul the marshes and estuaries where local fishermen had worked the same grounds for generations. There was temporal severance: a quarterly earnings report has a horizon of three months, but oil in a marsh and in the tissue of fish persists for years, long after the corporate attention has moved to the next quarter. And there was ecological severance: the toxins entered the bodies of keystone species, the small organisms at the base of the food web and the larger animals that depend on them, in ways that depressed reproduction for years and rippled outward through the whole system, costs that no balance sheet was built to register because the creatures bearing them cannot file a claim.

What did BP pay against what the Gulf lost? The honest answer requires care, because the round numbers that circulate do not survive scrutiny. There is no credible source that produces a single figure of total economic loss to the Gulf, and the nearest large aggregates in the literature measure the cost to BP, which is the wrong thing to measure, because the question is the cost to the region. What can be built is a defended range from documented components. The natural-resource damage assessment alone reached roughly seventeen billion dollars. To that the region added tourism losses, which in the lower estimates run to several billion over the year-and-a-bit after the spill and in the higher multi-year estimates run considerably above twenty billion, and fishery losses, and depressed property values along the coast that the peer-reviewed work places in the range of roughly four to five billion dollars rather than the larger figures sometimes cited. Set against that, BP's penalties, settlements, and the compensation fund it established came to somewhere in the order of sixty to sixty-five billion dollars. Lay the documented costs to the region against what BP actually paid and you do not get full recovery; you get a recovery ratio well below one hundred percent, a fraction that, depending on which defensible components you include, leaves a wide band of cost simply unrecovered. The point does not depend on a single decimal. The point is that the band of unrecovered cost is large, and the people inside it are the people who could not move.

There is a sharper version of the same point at the border. The slick did not respect national lines. The same spill that fouled Louisiana and Florida also reached Mexican waters in the southern Gulf. The settlement BP reached with Mexico was on the order of twenty-five million dollars, which is smaller by orders of magnitude than the U.S. settlements when measured against comparable lengths of damaged coastline. The oil did not discriminate by nationality. The accounting did. The difference was not in where the slick went; it was in the legal architecture available to each set of claimants. American claimants could reach for the Oil Pollution Act of 1990, for state tort and environmental law, and for the machinery of class-action aggregation that lets many small injuries combine into a force a corporation must answer. Claimants on the Mexican side reached for a far weaker architecture for pressing claims across a border against a foreign-domiciled corporation. Same oil, same chemistry, same fish. Two completely different prices, set entirely by which set of legal modules the injured party could invoke. Pistor's point about the code of capital is not abstract here; it is the difference between twenty-five million and tens of billions.

Close the case on the costs that never appeared on any ledger BP was made to balance. The fishing knowledge held in particular families, the reading of tides and grounds passed from a grandfather to a grandchild, broke where the fishery broke. Communities organized for generations around the water lost the thing they were organized around. Relationships between people and a living system, built over a century, were severed in a season. None of these had a line on any document BP ever had to reconcile, which is precisely why they were free to lose.

## Exxon Valdez

Stay in the water, but only briefly, because this case belongs more fully to a later chapter and is named here mainly so that the spread of cases promised earlier in the book actually delivers. In 1989 the tanker Exxon Valdez ran aground on a reef in Prince William Sound, in Alaska, spilling millions of gallons of crude into one of the richest cold-water ecosystems on the continent. What followed was a legal aftermath that ran for the better part of two decades, at the end of which a very large punitive damages award was sharply reduced on appeal, cut to a fraction of the figure the original jury had set. And the Pacific herring, a foundation species of that sound and of the fishing economy built on it, collapsed in the years after the spill and did not recover, while the fishing families who depended on it found there was no architecture through which to file for the loss of an entire fishery, because a fishery that simply stops returning is not the kind of harm the law is built to compensate. That is the whole of it for now. The full accounting waits for Chapter 6, which gives Valdez the complete decomposition it deserves.

## Baotou

Now to a place most readers downstream of it have never heard named, though they hold its consequences in their hands every day. Baotou, in Inner Mongolia, sits at the center of a vast rare-earth processing complex, drawing on the Bayan Obo deposit nearby. Rare-earth elements are the unglamorous foundation of modern technology. They are in the magnets that drive electric-vehicle motors and wind turbines, in the screens and speakers of phones, in a long list of the devices that wealthy economies regard as ordinary and as clean. China supplies the large majority of the world's rare earths, on the order of sixty percent of global production, and the Bayan Obo and Baotou complex accounts for roughly half of China's own output. So the correct way to say it is not that this one place produces sixty percent of the world's rare earths; it is that this one place is a very large share of the production of the country that itself supplies most of the world.

The processing is filthy in a way the finished products are not. Separating the valuable elements from the ore requires large volumes of strong acids and generates enormous quantities of toxic waste, laced with heavy metals, residual acids, and, because rare-earth ores are often bound up with thorium and other radioactive elements, low-level radioactivity. A conservative accounting puts the wastewater at something like ten tons for every ton of rare-earth oxide produced, and that is the low end of the published estimates. The waste flows into vast tailings impoundments. Residents of the villages near the complex report elevated rates of cancer, of respiratory disease, and of birth defects, and farmland within a radius of the facilities has gone out of use, the soil no longer fit to grow food.

The severance runs in every direction at once. It runs across space: the waste that makes the distant consumer technology possible sits in Inner Mongolia, entirely invisible to the engineer designing the device and to the household charging the car. It runs across time: today's price for the feedstock is paid now, while the health and environmental costs unfold over a horizon measured in human generations and, for the radioactive fraction, in something closer to geological time. And it runs across the social distance between who gains and who pays: the value flows to shareholders and to consumers in wealthy economies with strong institutions, while the cost settles on rural communities with little political voice and few channels through which to refuse.

Honesty requires a word about the evidence here. Data on health and environmental conditions around Baotou is constrained, because the information environment in China limits what can be gathered and published, and a careful writer should say so plainly rather than overstate what is known. But the constraint is not the same as silence. Peer-reviewed environmental work exists despite it, and it deserves to be cited specifically rather than gestured at by waving in the direction of journal names. Studies such as the work of Zhang and colleagues in 2022 on the environmental costs of the rare-earth industry, and of Zhou and Ge in 2021, document the waste intensity and the regional burden. What cannot be honestly stated is a tidy total remediation figure. A round number sometimes attached to Baotou cleanup, on the order of a hundred billion dollars, does not trace to a source that survives checking, and the right thing to do with an unsourceable number is to set it aside. What can be said is narrower and defensible: that the annual environmental cost of the industry has been estimated in the billions, that the industry's revenue is on the order of ten to twelve billion dollars a year, and that the share of that revenue directed at remediation has historically been a small fraction of the harm. If one wants a figure for the full centuries-long cleanup, it can only be offered as an explicit framework estimate with its inputs disclosed, not as a documented fact.

And then the beat that matters most, because it is the one the whole book turns on. The person at the end of this supply chain, the engineer designing the phone, the driver charging the electric car, the household drawing power from a wind turbine, is not a villain. There is no villain here. That is not a softening of the case; it is the center of it. The consumer shares in the value that the extraction produced, and the consumer is separated from the cost by the entire length of the supply chain, and the consumer's private virtue or guilt is simply beside the structural point. You could be a saint and still be the person on the bright end of this severance. Spatial severance through a global supply chain is not an unfortunate byproduct of the arrangement; it is the thing that makes the arrangement livable for the people who benefit, because it keeps the cost-bearer permanently out of view. The mechanism does not need anyone to be cruel. It needs only distance, and it manufactures distance for a living.

## 2008 and the double transfer

Here the register changes. The cases so far have had a geographic alibi, or a cultural one, or the alibi of a remote supply chain. A reader in a comfortable suburb could hold Libby and Baotou at arm's length, as someone else's tragedy in someone else's place. The financial crisis of 2008 took the alibi away, because it struck the entire economy at once and reached into the retirement accounts of the same comfortable readers who had felt safe. This is the hinge of the chapter. It is also the place where this book absorbs a piece of the crisis story that might otherwise have been told later, the household side of the ledger, because it belongs here, with the rest of the mechanism.

The crisis was the meeting of two transfers, set in motion decades apart.

The first transfer began quietly in 1978, when a provision of the tax code, section 401(k), created the legal scaffolding for a new kind of retirement saving. Over the following years, American employers steadily replaced the defined-benefit pension, in which the company promised a worker a fixed income in retirement and bore the responsibility of funding it, with the defined-contribution account, in which the worker saved into an individual account and bore the outcome. This was sold to workers as freedom and as ownership. You would control your own money. You would not be tied to one employer for a pension. You would be an owner of capital rather than a supplicant for a promise.

What was not said as loudly was whose freedom it really was. The pension had placed three serious risks on the institution: market risk, that investments might fall; longevity risk, that retirees might live longer than funded for; and behavioral risk, that people might save too little or sell at the wrong moment. The defined-contribution account moved all three of those risks off the corporate balance sheet and onto the individual, who, unlike the institution, has no way to pool risk across thousands of lives and no way to hedge a thirty-year horizon. The freedom that mattered most in the transfer was the corporation's freedom from liability. This ran quietly for about thirty years, an enormous reassignment of risk that produced no crisis on its own, because no shock had yet arrived to reveal where the risk now sat.

The second transfer was compressed into a few years, roughly 2001 to 2008. During that window, the financial industry built an apparatus for turning home mortgages into securities, mortgage-backed securities, and then bundling and slicing those into still more elaborate instruments, collateralized debt obligations, whose internal complexity was not an accident. The complexity was structural and necessary to the business, because only an instrument too complicated to evaluate could be sold at scale to investors who could not actually judge what they were buying. And these instruments were packaged into, among other places, the very retirement accounts that the first transfer had filled with ordinary workers' savings. When the housing market turned and the instruments collapsed in 2008, the losses did not stop at some abstract institution. They landed in those accounts.

Now bring in the household side, because the aggregate damage to ordinary people is where the crisis stops being a story about banks. American household net worth fell by roughly eleven trillion dollars, by the Federal Reserve's own flow-of-funds tally. The economists Atif Mian and Amir Sufi, in their book *House of Debt*, reconstructed how that loss was distributed, and the distribution is the whole point, because the headline figure conceals the cruelty in it. Mian and Sufi show that the losses fell hardest, by a wide margin, on households with the least wealth and the most debt. The mechanism is the brutal arithmetic of leverage. Picture two homeowners. One owns a house outright; the other has put down a tenth of the price and borrowed the rest. Let the price of the house fall by a tenth. The unleveraged owner has lost a tenth of the value, a real loss but a survivable one. The leveraged owner has lost the entire down payment; the equity is wiped out completely. The same price decline that dents the wealthy balance sheet annihilates the modest one. Across the country, the families with the thinnest cushions of wealth and the heaviest mortgages lost a multiple of what wealthier families lost, not because they had behaved worse but because leverage transmits a price decline through to equity with merciless efficiency. Mian and Sufi's analysis of how households respond to such losses shows a sharp difference in spending behavior across the income and leverage distribution, roughly a threefold difference in the marginal propensity to consume across the housing-net-worth distribution, which is why a shock concentrated on the indebted poor propagated into a general collapse of demand and a deeper recession for everyone.

Set against that household devastation, look at what the system did mobilize, and how fast. The Troubled Asset Relief Program committed seven hundred billion dollars. The Federal Reserve's emergency lending, when the Government Accountability Office audited it in 2011 and added up the cumulative total across all the programs, exceeded sixteen trillion dollars. That figure, the GAO's sixteen trillion, is the one large 2008 number that is genuinely well sourced and can be stated with confidence. Within roughly eighteen months, bonuses at the rescued institutions had resumed. And on the other side of the ledger, across the years 2008 to 2012, there were millions of foreclosure filings in the United States, of which roughly four million ended in completed foreclosures, about 4.1 million through 2012 by CoreLogic's count, families put out of their homes. The system found trillions of dollars in a matter of days for the institutions at the center of the crisis. For the households whose retirement accounts had absorbed the manufactured complexity, and whose homes were being taken, it organized nothing of remotely comparable scale or speed. The Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission, reporting in 2011, concluded that the crisis had resulted from human action and inaction, from choices, not from forces beyond anyone's control.

It would be too easy to leave the case there, with the contrast stated and the indignation invited. The case is stronger if it survives its own best objections, so take the four most serious ones in turn, each at full strength, before answering.

The first objection is about agency. Many of the mortgages that defaulted had gone to borrowers who could not have serviced them under any reasonable assumption, and those borrowers signed the papers. No one held a pen to their hands. This is true, and it should not be waved away. But agency is not a free-floating thing; it requires information, and the information architecture surrounding these loans was structurally adversarial. The commissions that paid the people originating the loans were tied to the volume written, not to whether the loans were ever repaid, which is an engine for writing loans that should not be written. The terms disclosed in the marketing routinely differed from the terms buried in the contract. Adjustable-rate products were marketed precisely to borrowers whose grasp of how the rate would reset was expected to be incomplete, and the later enforcement record of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau documents the pattern in case after case. The borrower's agency was real. The asymmetry of information was the lever that turned that real agency against the borrower's own interest. You do not dissolve the responsibility of the borrower by saying this; you locate it in its actual proportion, beside the responsibility of the people who engineered the asymmetry on purpose.

The second objection is about geography. The crisis, this objection runs, was global, not a story about American policy. European banks were heavily exposed; Asian exporters were hammered by the collapse in demand; mortgage-backed instruments were held across borders; the international Basel capital framework shaped how banks everywhere behaved. All true. But the global character of the crisis amplifies the finding about the American mechanism rather than dissolving it. The instruments that propagated the damage around the world were predominantly originated in the United States. The fee-on-origination architecture that rewarded volume over quality was an American design. The conflict at the heart of the rating agencies, in which the issuer of a security paid the agency that rated it, was permitted by American policy. The exemptions that let the whole apparatus scale were American choices. That the poison spread worldwide does not make it less American in origin; it makes the origin more consequential.

The third objection is about repayment. TARP, the rescue fund, was largely repaid; the Treasury's accounting shows substantial recovery and, on some measures, even a modest profit, and the later retrospectives from the GAO and the Congressional Budget Office bear this out. Did the public not, in the end, get its money back? The answer is that TARP is simply the wrong unit of account. What this chapter is pricing is not the federal government's cash flow on a particular rescue facility. It is the welfare lost by the households put out of their homes, the lifetime compounding lost by the workers whose account balances reset to a lower base and never caught up, the regional economies that contracted and stayed contracted, and the trust between citizens and their institutions that the spectacle of an asymmetric rescue depleted. None of that is recovered when TARP is repaid. None of it even appears on the balance-sheet flow accounts that the repayment argument cites. The money the government lent and got back is a real fact about one ledger. It says nothing about the ledger this book is keeping.

The fourth objection is about reform. The Dodd-Frank Act, passed in 2010, was a serious response. It created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and the Financial Stability Oversight Council, restricted banks from certain proprietary trading through the Volcker Rule, and imposed tougher capital and liquidity requirements. Was the gap not, then, closed? Here the honest answer is that Dodd-Frank is best read as a partial and early installment of exactly the kind of accountability architecture this book is arguing for, which is a genuine compliment, not a dismissal. But a reform that is partial and reversible has not closed the gap and has not been consolidated to the point where its survival is structurally guaranteed. The Volcker Rule was later relaxed. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau was later weakened institutionally. The designations of which institutions count as systemically important have been contested and rolled back. A door that has been pushed partway open and can be pushed shut again is not the same as a wall rebuilt. Chapter 9 returns to Dodd-Frank by name and treats it as one of the entering wedges, the partial, real, but unfinished beginnings from which a fuller architecture might be built.

There is one more thing to say about 2008, and it is the most important thing, because it answers the quiet assumption underneath all four objections, the assumption that what the system did was simply what any system would have had to do. It was not. The scope of a rescue is a choice, and a society can choose to rescue both the institutions and the households. The proof is historical. In the 1930s, Sweden faced banking-crisis pressure structurally similar to what the United States faced in 1929 to 1933 and again in 2008 to 2009, and Sweden responded by pairing a bank rescue with substantial household relief. In 1931 it left the gold standard and let the krona devalue, which restored export competitiveness and eased the real burden of household debt. It coordinated the rescue of its banks with the modification of mortgages, so that the relief reached households rather than stopping at balance sheets. Through the middle of the decade it ran public-works employment programs that put income back into households directly. And in 1938 the Saltsjobaden Agreement institutionalized the labor-and-capital settlement that became the architecture of the Swedish welfare state. This comparison is not a stray anecdote; it is drawn from the comparative literature on banking crises, the tradition of Kindleberger and Eichengreen, which treats it as a standing demonstration that bank rescue and household relief are not an either-or. A society can choose both. The United States chose otherwise in 2008 and 2009, and chose similarly again in 2020. The choice was contingent then and remains contingent now. Chapter 9 develops the fuller cross-country comparison; what matters here is only that the road taken was a road chosen, not a road forced.

Three settings remain to pass through, and they are the hardest, because in each of them the distance that severs the cost is not a border or a supply chain but something more difficult to see across. In the next case the distance is time itself. In the one after, it is the boundary of a single human body.

## Social Security

The most devastating example hides inside the system Americans treat as the opposite of extraction. Social Security is the promise that each working generation will care for the one before it, a compact deliberately built to express the idea that a society does not abandon its old. And it is, genuinely, that. It is not a fraud and it is not a Ponzi scheme, and the critique that follows is not a critique of the program but of one specific feature of how it was funded. Hold that distinction firmly, because the mechanism this book traces is precisely the kind of thing that, named carelessly, gets weaponized against a program worth defending.

The mechanics are straightforward. Designed in 1935, Social Security was built as a pay-as-you-go system: the payroll taxes of today's workers fund the benefits of today's retirees. For decades the system collected more than it paid out, and the surplus was held in special-issue Treasury obligations in a trust fund. The arrival of the baby-boom generation at retirement age was visible on the horizon for forty years; the demographers and actuaries saw it coming and said so in report after report. By the early 2020s, the system's outflows had come to exceed its payroll-tax inflows, and under current law and projection the trust fund is expected to deplete around 2033, after which incoming payroll taxes would cover roughly three-quarters, about seventy-seven percent, of the benefits that have been promised.

Now we come to a number that has done real damage to honest discussion of this question, and it has to be handled with discipline. There is a figure that circulates, a claim that something like one hundred and eight trillion dollars in compounding returns was foregone because the trust-fund surpluses were held in low-yielding Treasury bonds rather than invested in the stock market. It is sometimes paraded as the largest cost severance in human history. That figure is dishonest, and naming it as dishonest is part of the work here, because a framework that prices severed costs has no business smuggling in a number it cannot defend. The hundred-and-eight-trillion figure cannot be reconstructed from its own inputs. It sits roughly two orders of magnitude above the nearest serious counterfactual estimate. It exceeds the peak size the trust fund ever reached. It exceeds the cumulative income the entire program has ever taken in. It exceeds even the ceiling you would get by applying the full historical gap between equity and bond returns to any plausible base. It is a number generated by compounding a figure that was never actually available to compound, and it should never appear as a real magnitude of severed cost.

So set it aside, and build the honest version from the parts. Over the whole life of the program, cumulative contributions add up to something on the order of twenty-six trillion dollars, and cumulative benefits paid out come to roughly the same, on the order of twenty-six trillion. Those two enormous flows very nearly cancel, because the system was designed for them to: money came in from workers and went out to retirees, year after year, by design. The money that was ever actually available to invest was not the gross flow; it was only the running surplus, the accumulated reserve that built up when inflows exceeded outflows, which at its peak was on the order of two-point-seven trillion dollars. That is the base on which a foregone-return counterfactual can honestly be computed. Compound that investable surplus at a disclosed, realistic real equity return, net of the pay-as-you-go payouts the system actually had to make along the way, and the foregone return lands at a few trillion dollars, not a hundred trillion. The honest qualitative floor is this: the severed cost here runs into the trillions. That is the headline. The foregone return runs into the trillions. Not a hundred. The method matters and is worth stating plainly, which base compounds, at what assumed real return, netting the payouts, because a number you cannot show your work for is a number you should not assert.

One more figure needs to be kept distinct, because conflating the two is its own kind of dishonesty. There is a separate, genuinely large number attached to Social Security, the seventy-five-year actuarial shortfall the program's Trustees publish, which is on the order of twenty-two-point-six trillion dollars. That is the projected gap between promised benefits and projected revenue over the long horizon. It is a real and important figure, but it is a different quantity from the foregone-investment counterfactual, and the two must never be blurred together into a single impressive-sounding total. The shortfall is what the program is projected to owe beyond what it is projected to collect. The foregone return is what the investable surplus might have earned. Keep them labeled and keep them apart.

Here is the move that makes Social Security the sharpest case in this chapter, because it relocates the severance away from where people instinctively look for it. The instinct is to say that the cost severance lives in the investment decision, in the choice to hold the reserves in Treasuries instead of equities. It does not. Holding the reserves in Treasury obligations was administratively prudent; a public fund that gambled retirees' security on the stock market would be exposed to exactly the market and political-economy risks a retirement system exists to absorb. The portfolio choice was sound. The severance lives somewhere else entirely. It lives in how the obligations were generated in the first place.

Across the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, Congress repeatedly expanded Social Security benefits. Those expansions were enacted by legislators who, in nearly every case, would have left office long before the demographic transition arrived and the obligations they were creating came fully due. They were funded comfortably by the favorable ratio of working-age contributors to retirees that happened to prevail in exactly those decades, a ratio that the actuaries openly projected would deteriorate. Every actuarial report of the period named the deficit window the expansions would eventually run into. The expansions were made anyway, during the demographic surplus, without funding the deficit window the actuaries had identified. That is the severance. The electoral value of generous promises was harvested in the decades when the promises were cheap to make, by people who would be gone before the bill arrived, and the funding of those promises was severed onto the future taxpayers who would face the deteriorated ratio. Time is the distance here. The value, the political reward of the promise, was extracted at one moment; the cost, the funding of the promise, was assigned to a cohort that did not yet exist and could not refuse.

There is a contrast available that proves the architecture was a choice and not a fate. The 1935 design, pay-as-you-go with surpluses parked in Treasury obligations, was one option among several. Chapter 4 described Norway's sovereign wealth fund, which channels the rents from the country's oil and gas into a compounding investment fund that accumulates real capital for future generations rather than spending it as it arrives. Same structural problem, the duty of one generation to those that follow, met with a different architectural answer, and the two answers produce different outcomes. The difference is not explained by national wealth or by demography; it is explained by the architecture chosen at a specific moment by specific people. There is no need to re-derive how Norway's fund works; the point is only that a fund-based design existed as a live alternative, which means the pay-as-you-go design was selected, not inevitable.

Two objections deserve a full hearing here as well. The first is the public-finance objection, and it should be conceded entirely: holding the reserves in Treasuries is exactly what a sound fund manager does, and exposing retirees' core security to equity markets would have introduced precisely the volatility a retirement system is built to remove. That objection is correct, and it leaves the argument untouched, because the argument operates upstream of the portfolio. It is not a claim about how the surpluses were invested. It is a claim about how the obligations were generated. A defense pitched at the portfolio level does not reach a claim pitched at the obligation-generation level; the two pass each other.

The second objection is about tractability. The gap is solvable, this objection runs, through ordinary and well-understood adjustments: raising the payroll-tax rate, lifting or removing the cap on taxable wages, refining the benefit formula at the high end, raising the eligibility age to track rising longevity. The Trustees and a long line of commissions have laid out the menu. The arithmetic is conceded: the gap is mathematically closeable, and closing it is the responsible thing to do. But notice what every item on that menu does. Each one transfers the obligation onto the cohort living in the demographic-deficit window, the people who will pay a higher rate or receive a smaller benefit or work additional years, and not onto the cohorts who extracted the electoral value of generating the obligation in the first place, who are by now mostly beyond the reach of any adjustment. Tractability is not the absence of severance. It is severance unfolding exactly on the schedule the actuaries drew decades ago. The fact that the bill can be paid does not change who is being handed the bill.

## The healthcare commons

The last setting is the smallest and the most intimate. Here the mechanism operates not on a town or an ecosystem or an economy but on individual human bodies, at the most vulnerable stretches of life, and the severed costs are not abstract externalities floating somewhere in an economy. They are the specific financial ruin of specific households, named on specific fundraising pages.

Consider what has happened to medical crowdfunding in the United States. Over the 2010s, the practice of raising money for medical bills through online fundraising platforms grew from a curiosity into an institution. By 2020, more than a third of the campaigns on the largest such platform were medical. Across the window from roughly 2016 to 2020, there were on the order of four hundred thirty-eight thousand medical campaigns, drawing more than two billion dollars across some twenty-one million donations. And yet the structure of the outcomes tells the real story: only about twelve percent of campaigns met their stated goals, and roughly sixteen percent received nothing at all. Read the first figure from the other side and the failure rate, the share that fell short of what they asked for, runs near ninety percent. The chief executive of the platform itself has acknowledged that it was never designed to be a healthcare financing system and that it became the default backstop by accident, filling a hole that a functioning safety net would have filled. It is worth being precise rather than sweeping about the comparison: crowdfunding for medical costs exists in other wealthy countries too, in Canada and the United Kingdom and Germany among them. What is distinctive about the United States is the scale and the role, that the country runs medical crowdfunding at a volume and as a default substitute for coverage that the peer-reviewed comparative work, such as the analysis by Saleh and colleagues published in JAMA Network Open in 2020, finds far above the levels in those comparison countries.

There is a further layer, and it is the cruelest, because it shows the informal backstop reproducing the very inequities the formal safety net was supposed to reduce. Studies of medical crowdfunding find that campaigns run by people with distinctively Black or Hispanic surnames raise less money and are less likely to meet their goals, even after controlling for geography and time period. The work of researchers including Kenworthy and Igra, in their 2022 analysis in the American Journal of Public Health, documents the scale of these campaigns and the structure of who succeeds. A system of mutual charity that depends on the size and wealth of a person's social network will, with grim reliability, deliver the least to those whose networks were already thinnest, which is to say it routes the most help to those who need it least and the least to those who need it most. The backstop does not correct the inequity. It launders it.

Underneath the crowdfunding sits a slower structural pressure. The economists Lawrence Mishel and Josh Bivens, in their work at the Economic Policy Institute, document the decoupling of the typical worker's wages from overall productivity that began in the early 1970s, after which the economy kept growing more productive while the median wage stalled. Across the same span, healthcare costs grew at multiples of general inflation. The financial pressure that lands a family on a fundraising page is, structurally, the widening gap between those two curves, flat wages below, soaring medical costs above, with households caught in the opening space. The political scientist Jacob Hacker named the broader version of this in his book *The Great Risk Shift*, the long, systematic transfer of economic risk off institutions and onto families, of which healthcare cost-severance is one of the sharpest instances. The pension-to-401(k) transfer from the 2008 case and the medical-bill-to-fundraising-page transfer here are the same shift wearing different clothes.

To see the mechanism on a single body, turn to the account the journalist Katy Butler set down of her own father's last years, a documented case that should be rendered strictly within what she published and not embellished a single detail beyond it. The architecture of medical reimbursement, as her account makes plain, points relentlessly in one direction. A long office visit spent discussing whether a particular intervention is wise, weighing alternatives, talking through what a frail patient actually wants, is reimbursed at a small fraction of what a procedure earns. By Butler's account, Medicare would have paid her father's doctor fifty-four dollars for that kind of long conversation, and it paid four hundred sixty-one dollars to the surgeon who implanted the pacemaker, on top of a hospital fee of roughly twelve thousand dollars, nearly half of which flowed to the company that made the device. The liability architecture points the same way: performing the procedure protects the clinician against the malpractice exposure that declining it would create. And so the device goes in, and it keeps the heart beating reliably through years of subsequent cognitive decline that the patient, had the long conversation been the well-compensated act, might have chosen to forgo. Through those years the unpaid labor of caregiving falls on an elderly spouse, hour upon hour of it. Butler's mother, at seventy-seven, found herself on duty more than eighty hours a week, work that appeared on no ledger anywhere, counted by no system, reimbursed by nothing. The cost was severed from the institutions that profited by the procedure and fastened onto a household at its most defenseless.

The surgeon and writer Atul Gawande gave the systemic version of this in his book *Being Mortal*. The reimbursement architecture rewards intervention at every decision point while it underfunds the palliative-care infrastructure and the training that would let medicine attend well to the end of a life. The aggregate of that incentive structure is the over-treatment of the dying, the individual case reproduced at the scale of a whole system. And crucially, no individual clinician need ever choose extraction over compassion for the pattern to appear. The pattern is produced by aggregating the rational responses of decent people to the incentives in front of them, and the incentives are themselves the policy-design choice. Change the reimbursement architecture and the aggregate behavior changes without anyone's character changing at all. The same logic extends well beyond the dying: into maternal care, into mental health, into the long management of chronic disease, wherever the architecture pays for the procedure and starves the conversation.

## The pattern made visible

Set the cases side by side and the shared skeleton stands out from the different flesh.

In each case an entity, a company, a generation, a political system, drew value from a resource or a system that could have been managed for the long term. The drawing of that value generated a cost, environmental at Libby and in the Gulf and at Baotou, financial in 2008, intergenerational in Social Security, bodily and financial in the healthcare cases, and in every case the cost was real. And in every case that cost was severed onto a party with little or no power to prevent the transfer: the workers and families of Libby, the fishing communities of the Gulf, the villages around Baotou, the foreclosed households of 2008, the future taxpayers of the Social Security obligation, the families failing to fund their campaigns and the spouse keeping vigil over the maintained heart.

There are a handful of recurring covers under which the party that benefited escapes the full reckoning, and the cases display them without needing to be filed into a tidy list. There is distance, the spatial separation that keeps the Baotou tailings out of the consumer's sight. There is time, the temporal separation that lets a 1970s Congress harvest the value of a promise whose bill falls due in the 2030s. There is the asymmetry of what is known, the gap between the company that measured the asbestos and the family that breathed the dust, between the lender who structured the loan and the borrower who signed it. There is the asymmetry of who can refuse, the fact that capital is mobile and a poisoned town is not. There is the normalization of a cost as routine, the way a fishery's decline or a family's bankruptcy becomes background noise. And there is the appearance of abundance, the way cheap insulation and cheap electronics and a rising stock market make the underlying cost register, for a while, as no cost at all. Chapter 7 returns to these covers and names the canonical set of them precisely; here they are only illustrations of how the escape is managed, not a closed enumeration.

Walk the cases one more time as a single roll-call of who absorbed the cost. The town that could not move. The ecosystems and the fishing communities that could not relocate. The rural villages with no exit. The foreclosed households. The future Americans who cannot opt out of an inherited obligation. The families whose fundraising pages fall short, and the elderly spouse whose labor appears on no ledger. In each case the original beneficiary departs. The company declares bankruptcy. The politicians leave office. The promise-making generations pass away. And in each case the cost does not depart with them. It stays, transferred to whoever could not escape it.

The structural claim, stated without any villain in it, is this: what these cases show is not a failure of the system but the system working as it was built to work. Not through anyone's conscious intention to harm, but through the accumulated logic of structures that reward the extraction of value and externalize its cost onto the people least able to resist the transfer. This is the claim that extractive economies, wherever the taking has outrun the restoring, have at bottom operated this way, and that claim cannot be left hanging as an assertion. It has a lineage, and naming the lineage is the work of the next section, because the people who first saw this pattern saw it nearly a century ago and gave it names this book is obliged to use.

The forward question is not whether any of this is happening. The cases settle that. The question is what it would look like to build accountability that interrupts the pattern before the cost is severed, rather than chasing it afterward, once the beneficiary has already gone. That is the question the rest of the book is built to answer.

## The same object, named honestly

Step back from the cases to ask what, exactly, was being extracted from in each of them, because the answer is more unified than the surface variety suggests, and naming it is the most important single move this chapter makes.

A reef. The atmosphere. A community of fishing families. A body of human labor. A generation not yet born. These look like utterly different kinds of things. But define a commons not by its substance but by its structure, and they collapse into one. A commons, structurally, is anything that is depended upon and shared but was not produced as a commodity for sale. A reef was not manufactured; it was inherited. The atmosphere was not built to specification. A community's web of knowledge and trust was not made to be sold. The labor that reproduces a society, the care that raises the next generation, was never produced as a market good. And a future generation is the ultimate un-produced inheritance. On that structural definition the reef and the atmosphere and the community and the body of labor and the unborn generation are the same object: a commons whose very abundance disguised the fact that drawing on it carried a cost. And one apparatus can price all of them. It prices forward, against the foreclosure of an ecological or atmospheric future, and it prices backward, against extraction from human communities that has already happened.

To name a price for any of these is to invite a serious objection, and the philosopher Michael Sandel has stated the serious version of it as well as anyone, in his book *What Money Can't Buy*. To put a price on a sacred landscape, on a community, on the unborn, Sandel warns, is to launder the priceless into a transaction. It concedes the extractor's own frame, the frame in which everything is fungible and everything has a number. Worse, monetizing a harm can legitimate it, turning a violation into a fee, a thing you are permitted to do as long as you pay for it. This worry has to be taken on directly and answered here, inside this passage, because there is no later chapter where it gets answered instead.

The answer turns on what the alternative to pricing actually is. The choice is not between a sacred thing left unpriced and a sacred thing reduced to a number. The choice is between two numbers, because the thing already has a number, and that number is zero. A cost set at zero is not a cost left sacred and untouched. Zero is the number the extractive accounting already assigned to the reef and the community and the future, the number that made the extraction profitable in the first place. The reef's destruction priced at zero is exactly how the extraction got financed. The framework refuses that zero. It insists that the cost is not zero and never was, and it sets out to say what the cost actually is. There is a world of difference between pricing something in order to sell it and pricing something in order to reserve it, to hold it back, to re-embed it in the obligations that the market had stripped away. Pricing-to-sell turns a forest into board feet. Pricing-to-reserve names what the forest is worth so that the destruction can no longer be booked as free. They are opposite operations that happen to share the word price.

This refusal of the zero has a direct ancestor, and it is time to name him. In 1944 Karl Polanyi published *The Great Transformation*, and at its center is the idea of the fictitious commodity. Labor, land, and money, Polanyi argued, are treated by the market as if they were commodities, things produced for sale, when in fact none of them was produced for sale at all. Labor is human life; land is nature; money is a token of social trust. To run a society as though these were ordinary goods, Polanyi saw, is to do violence to the things themselves and to the people and places they are. The structural object-definition this book uses, a commons as anything depended-upon and shared but not produced as a commodity, is the operational descendant of Polanyi's thesis. The diagnostic move this book makes, imagine the commons absent and ask what cost appears, is a way of operationalizing exactly Polanyi's intuition that the market had disguised a fictitious commodity as a real one. And Polanyi's deepest idea, the double movement, the way a society that has had something marketized out from under it pushes back to re-embed that thing in social obligation, describes precisely what the bond apparatus of this book is. The double movement, in Polanyi, was a historical tendency he could only diagnose. Here it becomes an instrument. The accountability bond is Polanyi's double movement made into an accounting tool: it prices and reserves against what Polanyi could only point to and call by its true name.

The closest living anchor for this argument is Nancy Fraser, and her work pays off a debt this book incurred several chapters ago. Fraser draws a distinction that the cases in this chapter have been quietly illustrating all along: the distinction between exploitation and expropriation. Exploitation, in the older sense, takes from a worker through the wage relation, through a contract, however unequal. Expropriation is something more naked. It takes without even the form of a contract. It is capital's reliance on the unpaid appropriation of the labor of social reproduction, the care work that keeps a society going, and on the unpaid appropriation of nature, the resources and sinks that sit behind the wage relation entirely, never priced, never contracted for, simply taken. In her essay "Behind Marx's Hidden Abode" and in her book *Cannibal Capitalism*, Fraser maps the terrain on which this whole chapter has been standing. The reef taken without contract, the community's knowledge taken without contract, the caregiving spouse's labor taken without contract, the future generation's options taken without any possibility of a contract, these are expropriation in Fraser's exact sense, and the same-object meta-claim of this book, that they are all one commons priced backward or forward, is a claim staked on Fraser's ground. Chapter 2 left a marker in its discussion of the etymology of severance and the logic of expropriation, a thread deliberately left loose. That marker comes due here, and the name it was waiting for is Fraser's.

There is a caution to state honestly, because it is also the answer to the sharpest hostile reading of this whole project. A critic might say that pricing the commons is just financialization by another route, the commons repackaged as a new asset class for someone to trade. The defense against that reading is precisely the lineage just named. To claim the inheritance of Polanyi and Fraser, the very thinkers who diagnosed commodification as a harm, is to mark the difference between pricing-cost-severance-to-force-honest-accounting and financializing-the-commons-as-an-asset. Accounting a cost is not coding an asset. Pistor's modules code assets; this book accounts costs. The whole point of standing in the Polanyi-Fraser tradition is that the tradition is the one that refused the asset frame, and a framework standing inside it is using their refusal, not betraying it.

That is why this passage carries more weight than its length suggests. In naming the structural-commons identity it does two things at once. It anchors the meta-claim that the reef and the community and the future are the same object, and it simultaneously disarms the whole cluster of objections that the framework's own pricing is the very commodification it set out to critique: Sandel's worry, the financialization worry, the worry that the cost-severance language is just a metaphor dressed as accounting, the worry that pricing flattens the distinct coercions of different harms into a single ledger. The passage answers all of them from one position, the position Polanyi and Fraser built, which is why it is the load-bearing addition of the chapter.

## Who shaped the system?

There is a question the framework so far has left to one side, and it is worth restoring deliberately, because it complements everything above rather than competing with it. The apparatus of this book accounts for who bore the cost. That is the ledger. But there is a distinct and necessary question alongside it: who shaped the architecture that distributed the cost the way it did? The ledger tells you where the cost landed. It does not, by itself, tell you who built the chute it slid down.

For that second question there is a rigorous toolkit already in existence, and it would be a mistake of pride not to use it. The Public Choice tradition, built by James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock and developed by their successors, is precisely an apparatus for analyzing how concentrated interests shape institutions to serve themselves at diffuse cost to everyone else. Tullock's analysis of rent-seeking, the expenditure of real resources to capture a privilege rather than to create value, is the analytical engine. Where the people who extract value and the people who bear the cost are distinct populations, as they are in most of this chapter's cases, the rent-seeking chain runs cleanly: identify the concentrated-interest actors who shaped the institutional architecture, identify the lobbying expenditure and the political coalitions that produced the current design, and identify whose interests the design serves and whose it costs.

Run that chain across the cases and a roster appears. At Libby, the company's own released internal documents reveal documented lobbying and an effort to delay regulatory action on asbestos, an interest in keeping the architecture loose long enough to keep mining. In the Gulf, the Minerals Management Service, the agency that was supposed to regulate offshore drilling, had become a textbook captured regulator, with documented industry funding of personnel travel and gifts and a revolving door between the agency and the industry it oversaw, so thoroughly compromised that after the blowout the agency was broken up and reorganized into successor bodies, and the Interior Department's own Inspector General had reported on the capture before the disaster, not only after. In 2008, the deregulatory architecture that permitted the crisis, the repeal of the separation between commercial and investment banking through Gramm-Leach-Bliley in 1999, the exemption of derivatives from regulation through the Commodity Futures Modernization Act in 2000, the preemption rulings that pushed aside state consumer protections, was shaped by financial-industry lobbying documented at the time by the Center for Responsive Politics and in congressional hearings. In healthcare, pharmaceutical-industry lobbying has consistently led American industry totals, and the long-standing prohibition on Medicare negotiating drug prices, only recently modified, is as clean a rent-seeking outcome as the literature contains. And forward to Chapter 8, where the coal economy of McDowell County, West Virginia, will be examined in full, there sits a multi-generational history of an industry shaping the legislative and regulatory architecture of an entire state to its own advantage.

The chain does not run cleanly everywhere, and it is more honest to say so than to force it. In the Baotou case the extraction is shaped by global consumer demand as much as by any identifiable lobby, and the actors blur. In Social Security the cost-bearers of the future were, in part, the very payers of an earlier period, and the intertemporal dynamic does not reduce to a roster of concentrated interests. Where the rent-seeking chain blurs like this, it is the framework's structural accounting of who bore the cost that carries the weight, because the structural account does not depend on identifying a lobby.

A word is needed on how this toolkit is being used, because it matters and is easy to misread. This book draws on the rent-seeking vocabulary; it does not endorse the larger Buchanan political project, much of which points in directions this book does not follow. The rent-seeking tools are in the toolkit, not in the opposing camp. The framework's accounting of who bore the cost supplies exactly the ledger to which Public Choice's analysis of who shaped the architecture applies, and the two fit together without either swallowing the other. A reader who wants the architecture-shaping analysis developed in full should read Buchanan and Tullock and their successors directly and in parallel, and will find that the rent-seeking analytical vocabulary survives many different readings of Buchanan's broader commitments. This is an invitation extended deliberately to readers at institutions such as the American Enterprise Institute, Cato, Mercatus, and George Mason University, where the Public Choice tradition is most at home: the ledger this book builds is the natural complement to the rent-seeking analysis they already do, and the two halves are stronger joined. Chapter 9 develops this complement at greater length, where the framework supplies the ledger of what the reasoning actually costs and to whom.

## Restitution and foreclosure

The cases produce a question, and the question is the pivot from diagnosis to prescription. If all of this is one mechanism, severance of a real cost from the party that gained onto a party that could not refuse, then what would honest accounting of these severances actually require? The framework's answer is a coordinated pair of instruments, named here for the first time and formalized in the chapters that follow. Chapter 6 builds the methodology; Chapter 9 handles the politics of implementation. Here the task is only to name the two instruments at the level of structure and to set out the intellectual and legal lineage they stand on, woven through the cases that ground each claim rather than piled up as a list of authors.

The accountability gap has two temporal faces. There is harm already done, the realized extraction whose victims are alive or whose communities and ecosystems are already damaged: the lungs of Libby, the marshes of the Gulf, the herring of Prince William Sound, the foreclosed households. And there is harm not yet done, the future-generation cost that current extraction is foreclosing right now: the habitable atmosphere being spent, the irreplaceable materials being drawn down, the options being closed off for people who do not yet exist. An architecture that addresses one face without the other addresses only half the problem.

So the framework proposes two instruments. The Restitution Bond discharges harm already realized; it is the backward-looking, person-affecting instrument, aimed at people and communities who exist and have been hurt. The Foreclosure Bond discharges the future-generation cost that current extraction forecloses; it is the forward-looking, impersonal instrument, aimed at outcomes for people who do not yet exist. Together they form the aggregate accountability bond the framework's central relation requires, and each instrument is measured against its own face of the cost: the Restitution Bond against the damages already realized, the Foreclosure Bond against the residual value of the commons minus what has actually been posted to protect it.

Why call them bonds at all? The word is chosen deliberately, against the alternatives the framework rejected. A tax is paid at the moment of a transaction into general revenue, with no architecture connecting the amount paid to any specific future obligation; the money disappears into the general fisc and matches nothing. A fee licenses an activity, grants permission to proceed, rather than discharging the harm the activity causes; it is a toll, not a remedy. A fund aggregates money without discharging any particular liability; it is a pool, not a promise kept. A bond is none of these. A bond is posted before the full cost is borne, it carries forward across the long tail of that cost, and it discharges observably, a specific posted amount set against a specific identified liability. That structure already exists in the world in several forms, which is part of why the term is honest rather than invented. Reclamation bonds are posted under the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977, money held against the cost of restoring mined land. Decommissioning bonds are posted in the oil and gas industry against the future cost of closing a well. Norway's sovereign fund is a bond-like instrument at national scale, capital reserved against future need. The economics of reparations treats a historical injustice as a documented debt to be discharged, which is bond-like in its structure. And the civic-republican tradition's standing obligation toward future generations finds in bond-posting its natural accounting form. The term gathers these together.

A note is owed on why the backward instrument is called Restitution when its primary informing tradition is Reparations, because the choice is intentional and the distinction matters. Restitution, in the old legal sense of restitutio in integrum, the restoration of what was taken, is the broader and older legal-philosophical term, and the framework needs an umbrella wide enough to cover the full field of cost-severance contexts: environmental damage, occupational disease, the destruction of a community, the loss of an ecosystem. The reparations tradition, the line of work running through Coates and Darity and Mullen and Hamilton and Conley, is the primary informing source; it is the methodology through which the framework knows what it knows about backward-looking accountability. But to call the instrument the Reparations Bond would be to narrow the umbrella to one historically specific application and to make the methodology's reach into other contexts read as a loose analogy rather than a direct application of the same discipline. The narrower alternatives fail for their own reasons. Restoration presupposes that the harm can be physically undone, which many of these harms cannot. Indemnification routes the obligation through an insurance register the framework does not use. Damages collapses the bond into the measurement instrument and loses the forward-posting structure that is the whole point. So the framework joins the reparations tradition rather than translating it or rebranding it. In the language of Darity and Mullen's own three-part account of what redress requires, acknowledgment and atonement, then redress, then closure, the Restitution Bond is the instrument that operationalizes the material-restitution component of redress. It works inside their typology, using their language, not against it.

The forward instrument's name does similar work. A reader hears Foreclosure and thinks first of 2008, of a family barred from its home, and that association is not a coincidence to be apologized for but a continuity to be claimed. Foreclosure, from the old forclore, to bar from, describes both events exactly. The mortgage foreclosure is a household barred from its primary asset by a legal architecture that permits the barring to happen at scale. The framework's Foreclosure Bond addresses future generations barred from the commons they should inherit, a habitable atmosphere, intact ecosystems, irreplaceable material reserves, by a legal and economic architecture that permits that barring to happen across time. Same mechanism of barring-from, different substrate, one a household and one a generation. The alternatives miss the feature that matters. Future-Generations reads as soft philanthropy. Sustainability has been co-opted into corporate environmental branding. Reclamation is already a narrower statutory term. Conservation and Intergenerational both miss the specific bar-from action the word foreclosure names.

The lineage these instruments stand on can now be placed, grounded in the cases rather than recited. Pistor's *Code of Capital*, already met at Libby, supplies the legal-architecture half of the diagnosis: where Pistor shows how the modules of law encode and protect the severance, the framework prices what the modules sever. For the forward instrument's moral grounding, Derek Parfit's *Reasons and Persons*, from 1984, supplies the intergenerational standing: a way of taking seriously our obligations to future people that operates at the level of outcomes rather than the identities of specific persons, which is what an obligation to generations not yet born requires. The framework leans on Parfit here without claiming to have solved the non-identity problem he left famously open; that problem is named as a standing limit, not a quietly defeated one. The civic-republican tradition of Philip Pettit's *Republicanism*, from 1997, and Quentin Skinner's *Liberty Before Liberalism*, from 1998, extends the idea of freedom-as-non-domination across time: a society that extracts now in ways that subject future generations to dominated conditions violates the commitment to non-domination regardless of any specific contract broken, and the Foreclosure Bond is the accounting form of the commitment not to dominate the future. The framework does not require a reader to accept civic-republican philosophy as the only valid frame; it offers it as one strong grounding among several. The environmental-economics lineage runs through John Hartwick's rule, from 1977, the principle that the rents from a depleting resource should be invested in reproducible capital so that consumption need not decline, of which Norway's fund is the cleanest real-world application; these are foreclosure-prevention instruments, not reparations instruments, and the distinction keeps the two faces of the bond properly separate.

The backward instrument's lineage runs through the reparations tradition. Ta-Nehisi Coates, in *The Case for Reparations*, his 2014 essay in *The Atlantic*, supplied the argument-architecture, name the mechanism, name the harm, name the debt, that is the rhetorical companion to the economics, and the framework's backward instrument extends that architecture structurally into the contexts of extraction communities. The economics itself runs through Darity and Mullen's *From Here to Equality*, from 2020, the contemporary landmark and the framework's primary informing source; through *Umbrellas Don't Make It Rain*, the 2015 work by Hamilton, Darity, Price, Sridharan, and Tippett, whose matched-comparison method produced the finding that the structural variable is wealth and not behavior, that Black households at the highest stratum of educational achievement hold less wealth than white households at lower strata; and back through Dalton Conley's *Being Black, Living in the Red*, from 1999, the earlier articulation of wealth as the structural variable. The framework's backward instrument applies that same matched-comparison discipline to extraction-community contexts: McDowell County's wealth collapse measured against comparable non-extraction Appalachian counties, Libby's cumulative health-cost burden measured against comparable communities that had no Grace mine, and the opioid mortality of Appalachia measured against comparable counties that the documented marketing maps of Purdue's product did not concentrate on. That third comparison, the opioid leg, is carried forward to Chapter 8, where the full matched comparison is run.

A boundary has to be drawn honestly here, and drawn concisely. The same apparatus runs in reverse, and the most developed backward work in the field is Darity and Mullen's pricing of the racial wealth gap and of the longevity gap, the measurable difference in life expectancy that is a legacy effect of historical injustice, with McDowell's own life-expectancy gap standing as the coal-extraction variant of the same legacy-effect method. But there is a genuinely unresolved question at the edge of this work, and pretending otherwise would be the kind of overclaiming this book refuses. The direct pricing of the coercion vector itself, what cost to assign to coerced labor as a dimension distinct from its downstream legacy effects, remains methodologically unresolved in the reparations-economics field, and it lies outside the empirical scope of this book. The framework reaches coerced cases through their legacy effects, the wealth gaps and longevity gaps and disrupted knowledge transmission and intergenerational trauma that can be measured, and it defers the direct pricing of coercion as an open question, one the reparations-economics field is already carrying forward and one this book does not undertake. Indigenous land dispossession and the cultural knowledge severed by colonization sit in the same deferred territory: structurally accessible to the methodology applied backward, but to be computed in subsequent work, not legislated in this book.

Where, finally, does bond-posting actually stand across American extraction today? Far below the residual value of the commons it is meant to protect, by orders of magnitude. The federal black-lung program had paid out roughly forty-four billion dollars through 2009, the most recent authoritative cumulative tally, from the Government Accountability Office and the Congressional Research Service, which is a real and far-from-small number and a conservative floor for the program's full run, and yet the program's trust fund still carries debt outstanding of $5.1 billion as of September 2024. The deeper finding is not that the instrument is missing. It exists, at scale, and it is mis-assigned in part: when the coal excise tax falls short, the difference is borrowed from the federal general fund, which is to say from the public, rather than recovered from the extraction that severed the cost in the first place. The reclamation bonds posted under the 1977 statute cover, in the aggregate, several billion dollars less than the actual cost of the reclamation they are supposed to guarantee. Against the Social Security intergenerational obligation there is no Foreclosure Bond at all. This is the accountability gap, stated as a measurable fact rather than a metaphor: the bond posted is a small fraction of the cost severed. Chapter 6 supplies the methodology for measuring the gap, and Chapter 9 takes up the policy of closing it.

One discipline must close this out, because it guards against a misreading the whole argument is vulnerable to. The question of what is owed is separate from the question of what any society is currently willing or able to collect. The methodology applies regardless of whether anyone is presently prepared to post the bond; its contribution here is accounting, not advocacy. What the bond would have to be, if the severance were accounted honestly, is the methodology's question, and it has an answer whatever the political weather. The path from today's near-empty bond levels toward the residual value of the commons is a different question, a political and political-philosophical one, and it belongs to Chapter 9, where it is treated as an open matter rather than a single prescription this book pretends to legislate.

The chapter has done its work. It has named the severance and traced it across a town, an ecosystem, a supply chain, a national economy, the relationship between generations, and the individual dying body. It has met the strongest counter-positions on their own terms, on the financial crisis and on Social Security alike. It has engaged the deepest objection, that pricing the commons is itself the commodification it critiques, through Polanyi and Fraser and Sandel, and it has restored the complementary question of who shaped the architecture through the rent-seeking tradition. And it has named, for the first time, the two-instrument response that the rest of the book elaborates. What remains is the methodology that formalizes the apparatus, the stress-test that asks whether the pattern is truly universal, the single fully worked number that proves it can be computed, the political economy of implementation, and the close. The question the cases leave is no longer whether cost severance is real. The cases have settled that. The question is what we are going to do about it.
