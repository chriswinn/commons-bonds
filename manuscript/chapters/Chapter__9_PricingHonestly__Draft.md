# Commons Bonds

By Chris Winn

## Chapter 9: Pricing Honestly

Chapter 8 put a number on the invoice.

One ton of McDowell County coal, sold at the mine mouth in 1960 for four dollars and fifty cents, actually cost — conservatively, floor-estimated, cost-component by cost-component — somewhere between five hundred and six hundred dollars when every severed cost was priced honestly. The gap between those two numbers is not a rhetorical flourish. It is arithmetic. And it is replicated, with different parameters, across every extraction the economy runs.

The honest response to arithmetic like that is not outrage. It is to ask what to do about it.

This chapter is that answer — not a legislative program, not a manifesto, not a policy blueprint, but the set of practical moves a society would make if it decided to close the gap between what things cost and what we pay. The moves are sequenced. They are operable with institutions that already exist. They do not require consensus on every contested philosophical question about markets, or property, or the obligations of one generation to the next. They require only the decision to start pricing honestly, and the discipline to keep pricing honestly as we learn.

The sequence is four steps. Classify. Reserve. Invest. Reassess.

---

### Step 1 — Classify

Before anything else, sort the cabinet.

Not every resource carries the same foreclosure risk. A ton of iron ore extracted today imposes externality costs — the mine pit, the tailings, the emissions of processing — but iron is abundant on this planet and on most of the rocky bodies around it, substitutes are conceivable for almost every application, and extracting more iron tomorrow does not meaningfully reduce what remains for the century after. Coal is similar in abundance, different in externality tail, but substitutable in principle — we already know how to heat a house and power a grid without it. These resources are low-existential substitutability gap. Price their externalities, tax their extraction at a rate that reflects the damage tail, and the framework's job is mostly done.

Other resources are not like that.

Rare earth elements — the seventeen metals whose magnetic and optical properties underwrite nearly every piece of advanced electronics — are geologically concentrated in a small number of deposits, chemically difficult to substitute, and required for technologies humanity has not yet invented but will almost certainly need. Platinum group metals are similar. Helium-3, should fusion reactors ever run on it, is effectively absent from Earth's atmosphere and abundant in lunar regolith, which means terrestrial reserves of helium in general are a resource we may want our descendants to still have access to. Phosphorus, essential for agriculture, exists in workable concentrations at only a few places on the planet and has no substitute for the biological role it plays. Groundwater in fossil aquifers — the Ogallala, the North China Plain, the Guarani — regenerates on timescales of millennia and is being withdrawn on timescales of decades.

These are high-existential substitutability gap resources. They are the ones a civilization that plans to exist in two hundred years would want to hold in reserve.

A third category sits in the framework's classification scheme worth naming explicitly. Sunlight, wind, atmospheric oxygen, regional rainfall, and the broader atmospheric-circulation patterns that distribute them are commons too — shared by everyone in a region, regenerating continuously on timescales relevant to ordinary use, with substitutability functions that approach unity for most applications. The framework reads them as low-existential substitutability gap by construction: renewable substitution is not a question because they are themselves the renewable substrate the rest of the framework's renewable-substitute investments rest on. The interesting cases inside this category are the edge cases where renewable commons can be captured or contested — solar-access easements, wind-rights as land-use rights, rainwater-harvesting laws, atmospheric-oxygen depletion via deforestation — and the framework prices those the same way it prices any commons capture: by the cost severance the capture imposes on the bearers. The general rule for renewable commons is "use freely"; the edge cases are where the framework has the most to say.

Classification is not a mysterious exercise. Materials scientists, energy economists, agronomists, aerospace engineers, and hydrologists already have most of the information required to rank any given resource by its substitutability function, its stock dynamics, its externality tail, and the criticality of its likely future uses. The European Union publishes a Critical Raw Materials list that does a portion of this work. The United States Geological Survey publishes annual assessments that do another portion. What does not exist yet is a single, legible ranking that explicitly integrates all four components of residual commons value into a single existential substitutability gap score. Producing one is a matter of months, not decades. It has not been done mostly because no one has asked for it in exactly this form.

The classification need not be perfect to be useful. A rough sort into three bins — low, medium, high existential substitutability gap — is enough to drive every policy decision that follows. Low-existential substitutability gap resources get the carbon-tax treatment: price the externality tail, require adequate reclamation bonding, let the market allocate within a corrected price signal. Medium-existential substitutability gap resources get progressive extraction rates and sovereign wealth mechanisms like Norway's. High-existential substitutability gap resources get the one move the framework is organized around: reservation.

### Step 2 — Reserve

Here is the idea that sounds strange on first hearing and obvious on second.

For high-existential substitutability gap resources, designate a fraction of known economic deposits as strategic reserve. Not extract them and warehouse them. Not stockpile them against a shortage. Leave them in the ground.

The ground is free, permanent storage. It requires no refrigeration, no security, no insurance premiums against the warehouse burning down. Platinum that has sat in South African bushveld for two billion years will sit there another two billion unless someone goes in and takes it out. Rare earth elements in the Bayan Obo deposit are as stable today as they were when the deposit formed. Helium trapped in natural gas reservoirs requires no attention until the moment it is extracted and vented. The cost of in-situ preservation, for almost every resource in this category, is exactly the foregone revenue from the reserved fraction. Nothing else.

That cost, for most critical minerals, is small. The reason it is small is that extraction capacity is usually constrained not by total deposit size but by current demand and current mining throughput. If the United States Geological Survey identifies, say, thirty million metric tons of economically recoverable rare earth reserves and current global annual production is around three hundred thousand metric tons, designating twenty percent of reserves as strategic — six million tons, twenty years of current production held in reserve — costs essentially nothing in the near term. The thirty-year supply that remains available for extraction still dwarfs the demand schedule, and the preserved fraction simply becomes unavailable to future extraction decisions that our descendants would in any case have made for themselves.

The reserve is, in effect, a free option. It costs the foregone revenue on whatever fraction would have been extracted anyway within the current window, which — at realistic discount rates over the relevant horizon — is a small fraction of total resource value. Its payoff is the full substitution value of the preserved stock if and when future scarcity develops. It appreciates as the non-reserved stock depletes. It depreciates only if and when successful substitution makes the resource unnecessary, at which point the reserve can be reclassified and released. In financial terms, it is a hedge that costs almost nothing to hold and pays off exactly when needed.

And it requires no new international institution. Strategic petroleum reserves already exist in most industrial countries. China has been quietly building rare earth reserves for two decades. The United States has at various points held strategic stockpiles of helium, tungsten, cobalt, and several dozen other materials. The institutional architecture for reservation exists. What the framework adds is a coherent principle for deciding what to reserve and how much — the existential substitutability gap ranking from Step 1, applied to every high-existential substitutability gap resource class, with reserved fractions sized to the asymmetric regret of the decision.

Asymmetric regret is the principle that does most of the work. If the reserve turns out to have been unnecessary — substitutes mature, demand falls, new deposits are discovered elsewhere in the solar system — the cost of having reserved was a modest amount of foregone revenue, distributed across a century, spread across the global economy. If the reserve turns out to have been necessary — some critical application is identified in 2150 for a material our grandchildren can no longer access because we dug all of it up — the cost of not having reserved is civilizational. The first error is measured in dollars. The second is measured in possibilities foreclosed forever. When the insurance is cheap and the downside is unbounded, the rational move is obvious.

### Step 3 — Invest

Reservation alone does not complete the framework. It buys time. The question is what we do with that time.

The answer, the model insists, is two simultaneous investment priorities.

The first is renewable substitutes — not only renewable energy, though that is the most visible case, but the full substitutability research agenda. Photovoltaic and wind and storage to displace coal, oil, and natural gas. Alternative battery chemistries — sodium, iron-air, solid state — to reduce pressure on lithium and cobalt. Recycled and synthesized material flows to reduce pressure on virgin rare earths. Regenerative agricultural practices to reduce pressure on fossil aquifers and phosphate deposits. Plant-based and cellular protein to reduce pressure on collapsing fisheries. The details vary by resource. The structural claim does not.

Every dollar of renewable research does three things at once. It raises the substitutability function — the probability that a functionally adequate substitute exists at any given future time — which directly lowers the residual commons value of every high-existential substitutability gap resource whose applications that substitute touches. It preserves the optionality of the strategic reserve, because a reserve that is never needed can eventually be released for other uses. And it reduces the externality tail of future extraction, because the less we need to extract, the less damage our extraction inflicts on the communities and ecosystems that bear the direct costs. No other investment class does all three at once. Not infrastructure. Not defense. Not even education, though education compounds with all of them. Renewable substitution research is the only expenditure that simultaneously reduces present harm, future harm, and future need.

The second investment priority is alternative supply sources — what gets called, in compressed form, space access. This is the piece of the framework that often sounds science-fictional and isn't. The argument is purely one of comparative residual commons value. An asteroid has no communities bearing spatial cost severance. It has no ecosystems whose collapse cascades through a biosphere. Its externality tail is measured against an empty rock in an empty orbit. The existential substitutability gap of a lifeless body is, by construction, very low. A kilogram of nickel lifted from 16 Psyche and returned to Earth orbit will eventually, as launch costs continue to decline, compete on total cost with a kilogram of nickel mined from a terrestrial deposit whose full residual commons value has been priced honestly. The model says nothing about when that crossover happens. It says only that the eventual destination of industrial extraction, for the most abundant materials we use, is off this planet. Investing in the capabilities that reach that destination sooner is investing in a future where Earth's deposits do not need to be extracted at all.

This is what pricing honestly demands, stated plainly. The model does not say never extract. It says extract from the lowest-RCV source first. For most industrial materials, over the next century, the lowest-RCV source will be renewable substitution. For a narrower set of materials — the ones we cannot substitute and cannot recycle indefinitely — the lowest-RCV source will eventually be extraterrestrial. Neither conclusion was arrived at by politics. Both fall out of the arithmetic once extraction is priced honestly.

### Step 4 — Reassess

Nothing about this is static.

A resource classified today as high-existential substitutability gap may become low-existential substitutability gap in thirty years if a substitutability breakthrough matures. Rare earth demand could be halved by a successful non-rare-earth magnet chemistry. Lithium demand could be structurally reduced by sodium-ion batteries reaching grid-storage parity. Helium could be synthesized economically, or made unnecessary by a fusion approach that doesn't use it. When that happens, the reserved fraction can be reclassified. Some of it may be released to the extraction market. Some of it may be permanently preserved as a hedge against the substitute's failure modes. The point is that the classification is a living document, maintained by the same scientific bodies that produce it, reassessed on a regular cadence — every five or ten years, probably, at the pace at which materials science actually moves.

Reassessment also runs in the other direction. A resource classified today as low-existential substitutability gap may turn out to be higher than we thought, as new uses are discovered or as the externality tail lengthens beyond what current models capture. Cobalt looked abundant twenty years ago. It has since been reclassified by most industrial nations as strategically critical. Helium was once so cheap that the United States government literally gave it away; it is now priced with the seriousness its unique properties deserve. The framework does not require us to get the classification right on the first try. It requires us to keep learning, and to adjust the reservation policy as the learning matures.

The discipline this imposes is modest. Most of the institutional machinery already exists — the USGS, the European Commission's Joint Research Centre, the International Energy Agency, the national strategic stockpile programs. What is missing is not the capacity to reassess but the framework within which reassessment matters. The existential substitutability gap-driven reserve is that framework.

### The international dimension

Cost severance does not stop at national borders. In fact, borders are among the mechanisms by which it operates.

The rare earths in an iPhone manufactured in Zhengzhou and sold in California were mined in Baotou, in Inner Mongolia, where the tailings pond — seven square miles of radioactive slurry — borders villages whose residents breathe the airborne dust. The oil refined into diesel for a European truck may have been pumped from the Niger Delta, where fifty years of spillage have rendered thousands of square kilometers of mangrove and farmland uninhabitable. The cobalt in nearly every lithium-ion battery on the planet was, until very recently, overwhelmingly extracted from the Democratic Republic of Congo, where an estimated forty thousand children have worked in artisanal mines. The people who capture the value of these materials do not share a border with the people who bear the costs. They often do not share a continent.

International trade, as it currently operates, is among other things a cost severance delivery system. It permits a consumer in a wealthy country to purchase a product whose full residual commons value has been paid entirely by people the consumer will never meet, in places they will never visit, in amounts they have no way of perceiving. The geographic distance between beneficiary and bearer is what makes the severance possible. If the tailings pond were in New Jersey and the refinery in Connecticut, the framework's effects would be felt immediately in domestic politics. Because the tailings pond is in Inner Mongolia and the refinery in the Niger Delta, the politics are displaced.

Honest pricing at the national level does not, by itself, solve this. A country that internalizes the residual commons value of domestic extraction while continuing to import materials whose foreign extraction costs were never priced has not actually reduced severance — it has merely relocated where the severance enters its own economy. The import gap is the new problem.

The framework's response is not a treaty, which the book does not pretend to draft. It is a principle: a country serious about closing its cost severance gap must apply the pricing to imports as well as to domestic extraction. Mechanically, this looks like a border adjustment — a tariff scaled to the residual commons value that the exporting country failed to price. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, introduced in staged form beginning in 2023 and fully operational in 2026, is a partial version of exactly this idea for carbon alone. It is not theoretical. It exists. It works imperfectly, as every institution built inside political constraints works imperfectly. But it demonstrates that border adjustment is legally, administratively, and diplomatically feasible when the jurisdiction doing it is large enough to matter.

A generalized border adjustment — scaled to existential substitutability gap, applied to a widening set of resource categories as the classification matures — is the international-dimension analog of what national pricing does domestically. It makes imported goods bear the same cost severance charge that domestic production would. It removes the incentive to relocate extraction to the jurisdictions with the weakest accountability. Over time, it raises the floor for every exporting economy, because the cheapest path to retaining market access in the border-adjusting countries is to internalize the costs at the source and capture the revenue domestically rather than surrendering it to the tariff.

Is this a treaty? No. Is it coordination? Partially. Is it unilateral action that eventually drags other jurisdictions along? Mostly. The international dimension of the framework does not wait for the world to agree. It begins where any country or bloc decides to begin, and it expands by the ordinary mechanics of market access. That is how carbon pricing has spread from a handful of jurisdictions in the early 2000s to more than seventy today. existential substitutability gap pricing can follow the same path.

### The political economy of resistance

It is worth saying aloud why none of this has happened yet, and why the inertia is deeper than the idea is new.

The ten to fifteen trillion dollars per year of severed costs that Chapter 8 identified are not a deadweight loss. They are a wealth transfer. Someone receives them. The beneficiaries are concentrated, organized, well-represented in every jurisdiction that matters, and capable of deploying resources in defense of the arrangement. The bearers of severed costs — coal country residents, the Baotou villagers, the Niger Delta farmers, future generations who do not yet exist and cannot currently vote — are diffuse, unorganized, and, in the case of the unborn, entirely unrepresented. The arithmetic of the political economy is simple. A concentrated interest defending a billion-dollar position reliably defeats a diffuse interest trying to reclaim a hundred-dollar share, even when the aggregate loss is orders of magnitude larger than the aggregate gain.

This is not a discovery. Mancur Olson described it in 1965. The reason honest pricing has not happened is not that the case has not been made; it is that the case has been made against resources, organization, and institutional access that the countervailing interest does not possess. No individual coal-country resident or Baotou villager can outspend the extractive industries whose pricing they are opposing. No coalition of future generations can lobby.

The framework does not solve this problem by wishing it away. It solves it, partially, by changing the terms of the debate in two specific ways.

First, by pricing the severance in dollars, it converts a moral argument into a fiscal one. "The coal industry is harming Appalachian communities" is a sentence that has been uttered for a hundred years without producing durable policy change, because it asks for action on behalf of a group the decision-makers do not identify with. "The coal industry extracted $550 per ton of value in unpriced damage while selling the coal for $40" is a sentence that describes a subsidy. Subsidies have constituencies on both sides. The side that pays — which, in the case of coal, is the public broadly — has reasons to care that the side receiving the morality lecture did not. When the severed cost is denominated in money, a different political coalition becomes possible.

Second, by making the beneficiaries concrete and local, it shifts the constituency for reform. Costs that aren't priced are still costs. The framework's pricing logic identifies them; the question is what architecture would address the distributional problem they create. The plain-accounting name for the distributional problem is subsidy in the wrong direction: bearers subsidizing extractors, communities subsidizing supply chains, the future subsidizing the present. The architecture's structural shape is clear from the framework's accountability-bond logic: capture the rents at the source of extraction, designate disbursement to the communities bearing the direct spatial cost, match the disbursement schedule to the full life cycle of the externality tail. Alaska's Permanent Fund operates a domestic version at state scale: since 1976 the state has captured 25 percent of mineral royalty income and invested it as an irrevocable constitutional trust, which now holds roughly $80 billion and has distributed an annual dividend to every resident since 1982, most recently in the $1,300–$1,700 range. The Fund has survived boom, bust, and every administration the state has produced; its political third-rail status is the evidence that once a community is the actual beneficiary of resource rents, the constituency for preserving the arrangement forms and stays. Norway's sovereign wealth fund is a larger, more disciplined national version operating on the same principle. The specific design of an architecture that sits between these two — not a dividend to every citizen, but a long-horizon fund structurally tied to the community whose commons was drawn from — is the work of separate applied-policy analysis the framework does not attempt to legislate. What this book names is the structural shape: rent-capture at extraction, community-designation at the bearer level, life-cycle-matched disbursement across the externality tail. That community then becomes the constituency for pricing honestly. Reform has a local beneficiary to argue for it, not only a diffuse population to receive abstract improvements.

The political-economy-of-resistance dynamic does not remain abstract. It operates at scale, in real crises, when the architecture of response is being decided in real time. Two cases from the past century illuminate what the mechanism produces — one as negative example from the United States in 2008, one as positive analog from Sweden in the nineteen-thirties.

The U.S. response to the 2008 housing-bubble collapse demonstrates the dynamic at maximum scale. The collapse destroyed approximately eleven trillion dollars in U.S. household wealth and triggered roughly ten million foreclosures over the following five years. The federal response — the Troubled Asset Relief Program, the Federal Reserve's emergency-lending facilities, the AIG and Citigroup bailouts, and the orchestrated bank-recapitalization through ad hoc instruments — deployed roughly seven hundred billion dollars in direct authorization and trillions of dollars in liquidity and guarantees. Almost all of the deployed capital went to recapitalize bank balance sheets rather than to restructure household debt. The scope-choice was contestable at the time and remains contested now. Mian and Sufi's 2014 *House of Debt* analyzed the welfare cost of the chosen architecture against an alternative architecture that would have used the same fiscal capacity to restructure underwater mortgages — writing down household debt to a fraction of its bubble-era nominal value, preserving more household wealth, and producing a faster recovery in aggregate consumption. Their specific welfare calculation — that household-debt restructuring at the scale the crisis required would have produced substantially more consumption recovery in the 2009-2011 period than the bank-rescue architecture actually produced, with redistributive effects benefiting the bottom forty percent of the income distribution by a factor of three to four relative to top-quintile households — quantifies the cost of the chosen architecture in welfare terms specific enough to defend or rebut on empirical grounds.

The household-debt-restructuring architecture was politically reachable in 2008 and 2009; it was not selected. The reason it was not selected is the dynamic this section opens with. The bank-rescue architecture had a concentrated beneficiary class — the financial sector itself — capable of organizing rapid response and providing the political legitimacy required to deploy fiscal capacity at the scale and speed the moment demanded. The household-debt-restructuring architecture would have had a diffuse beneficiary class: ten million foreclosed households spread across every congressional district, with no organized political vehicle to demand the architecture or to defend it through the moments when its costs became visible. The architecture that won was the architecture whose beneficiary class could fight for it. The framework's reading of this case is not that the bank-rescue architecture was wrong as a matter of policy; reasonable people can defend the choice, and the financial-system collapse risk in 2008 was real. The framework's reading is that the choice was a choice — a policy decision made under specific political-economy constraints, producing a specific distribution of the crisis's costs between bank balance sheets and household balance sheets, that an honest pricing system could read in advance and a different political-economy could have reversed.

The case has a positive analog from a different country and a different century. Sweden's response to the 1929-1932 financial crisis combined bank rescue with substantive household relief: a 1933 currency devaluation that reduced real debt burdens; mortgage-modification programs that wrote down nominal mortgage debt for households unable to service it; public-works employment programs that absorbed unemployed labor at wages maintaining household solvency; and the 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement framework that institutionalized labor-employer wage-bargaining at a level designed to maintain household consumption capacity. The architecture combined bank-stability with household-stability rather than choosing between them. The Saltsjöbaden Agreement specifically institutionalized a wage-coordination architecture that survived through World War II, the 1973 oil shock, the 1992 Swedish banking crisis, and into the present — a multi-generational durability that demonstrates the architecture is not specific to a one-decade response but represents a sustained political-economy settlement. The 1992 Swedish banking crisis itself, when several major Swedish banks collapsed and required government recapitalization, was managed through an explicit bank-rescue-plus-household-protection architecture that drew directly from the lessons of 1932-1937; the recapitalization captured equity in the rescued banks, recovered most of the deployed capital within five years, and combined with countercyclical fiscal policy that maintained household consumption through the recession.

The Swedish architecture was not Norway's sovereign-wealth-fund — that came later, in a different context, for a different problem. But it shared the structural feature that distinguishes the U.S. 2008 response from the Swedish 1932-1937 response: it captured the crisis's cost as an intergenerational claim and distributed the cost across multiple constituencies rather than allowing the political-economy mechanism to concentrate the cost on the diffuse household sector. The Swedish architecture had multiple political constituencies — the SAP-led government, the trade-union federation, the employers' federation, and the bank sector itself — and produced an outcome none of them could have produced unilaterally. The U.S. 2008 architecture had a more concentrated political coalition and produced a less distributed outcome.

What both cases demonstrate is that financial-crisis architecture is not a market outcome; it is a policy choice, made under specific political-economy constraints, that the framework's accountability-bond logic prices structurally. The choices were not inevitable. Different political-economies produced different distributions of the crisis's costs. The framework's contribution is to make the choice visible as a choice, and to apply the same accountability-bond logic to financial-crisis architecture that it applies to resource extraction. What was chosen once can be re-decided when the next crisis arrives.

Neither of these moves is decisive on its own. Both of them, combined with the entering wedges already in place, begin to shift the arithmetic.

### Property rights, and their limits

The most intellectually serious objection to anything like this framework comes from the property rights tradition, and it deserves an honest hearing before the chapter closes.

The argument is that well-defined private property rights, enforced by credible institutions, already solve the commons problem for every resource that matters. Ronald Coase's 1960 demonstration — that in the absence of transaction costs, externalities will be resolved through bargaining regardless of initial entitlement — is one version of the claim. Elinor Ostrom's work on common-pool resources governed by local institutions is another. The free-market environmental tradition argues, with real empirical support in some cases, that assigning ownership to something is usually the best way to ensure its long-term preservation. Fish populations stabilize when fisheries are quota-allocated. Forests regrow when landowners internalize the value of standing timber. Aquifers survive when the water is priced and metered. Private stewardship, the argument runs, reliably beats public neglect.

The framework does not deny any of this. It agrees, in fact, with most of it. Property rights do extraordinary work for a specific class of resources — those that are rivalrous, excludable, and valued mostly in the present tense. For those resources, the price system aggregates dispersed information and incentivizes stewardship far better than any central planning alternative. The argument against the property rights solution is not that property rights fail; it is that they structurally cannot reach three specific problems that the residual commons value framework exists to address.

The first is intergenerational foreclosure. Property rights are held by living agents. Future generations hold no deeds, sign no contracts, cannot participate in the bargaining Coase described, and cannot bid against current uses of a finite stock. A mine owner and a mining community can negotiate, under the property rights regime, every term of the current extraction. Neither can negotiate with the humans who will be alive in 2200 whose access to the deposit is being foreclosed by today's decision. The property rights solution, in its pure form, assumes that if those future humans mattered they would bid — and the absence of their bid is taken as evidence that they do not matter at the margin. The framework rejects this assumption. Substitutability, stock depletion, and irreversibility are real constraints whether or not anyone alive today chooses to price them, and a pricing system that ignores them is not "efficient" in any defensible sense; it is incomplete.

The second is transboundary externality. Property rights work when the property owner bears the costs and benefits of the asset's use. They work less well when the costs escape the property line. Acid mine drainage crosses property lines. Atmospheric carbon crosses property lines. Aquifer depletion crosses property lines. The Baotou tailings pond sits on a specific parcel of land, but the radioactive dust that blows off it respects no fence. Property rights solve internal optimization, not external leakage. The residual commons value framework is, at its core, a systematic accounting of the leakage — the component of value that cannot be captured by any single property owner because it is held in common by everyone downstream, downwind, and downstream in time.

The third is information asymmetry at civilizational scale. Property markets price what participants currently know. They do not price what we will discover we needed only after the resource is gone. A helium-3 deposit would have been priced at zero in 1900, because helium-3 had no industrial use at the time. Rare earth deposits were of limited commercial interest until the late twentieth century, when their electronic applications emerged. The current market price of any high-existential substitutability gap resource is a function of current substitutability estimates and current demand. The price does not, because it structurally cannot, incorporate applications that have not yet been invented. The framework's insurance logic — hold a reserve when substitutability is uncertain, because the downside of under-reservation is potentially unbounded — is the move that property rights cannot make, because the information required to make it does not exist at the moment of pricing.

This is not a refutation of property rights. It is an identification of the domain within which they are complete and the domain within which they are not. For most goods, property rights are the right tool and we should leave them alone. For high-existential substitutability gap resources, intergenerational stocks, and transboundary externalities, property rights are a tool that is doing what it can do but cannot reach the full problem. The residual commons value framework is the supplement, not the replacement.

Adam Smith, who spent a good portion of The Wealth of Nations arguing against producers who externalized costs onto the public, would have recognized what this framework does. It is not a departure from the market mechanism. It is the completion of it.

### The Nordic objection, taken seriously

A reader following the argument toward this chapter's reachable-horizon cluster will hold a particular objection at arm's length, and the framework cannot earn the citation of Nordic examples without engaging it specifically. The objection is not that Nordic outcomes are undesirable. The objection is that the conditions under which Nordic outcomes were achieved are not replicable in a continental democracy of three hundred and thirty million people.

The serious version of the objection rests on four conditions. Scale: Norway has five and a half million people; Sweden has ten and a half million. The institutional-design problem scales nonlinearly. A policy that produces universal-healthcare consensus in a country smaller than Los Angeles metropolitan does not automatically produce consensus in a continental democracy spanning forty-nine legislative jurisdictions with constitutionally guaranteed regional variation. Demographic homogeneity: the Nordic social-democratic consensus was built among populations with centuries of shared ethnic, linguistic, religious, and cultural context. The high-trust institutional environment that underwrites Nordic governance is not recent political work; it is accumulated civic inheritance. The United States is the largest experiment in multi-ethnic, multi-cultural, multi-religious polity-construction in human history. The resource base: Norway's sovereign wealth fund operates on oil rents at a per-capita scale the United States cannot reproduce, and the U.S. states with comparable resource extraction operate under different political-economic conditions and have implemented partial analogs that have not achieved Nordic-scale outcomes. Historical contingency: Nordic social-democracy developed over a specific century-and-a-half arc — labor movements in the late nineteenth century, social-democratic political victories in the early twentieth, post-WWII consolidation, mature welfare-state design in the nineteen-sixties and seventies. The United States was on a different trajectory in each of those periods, and the trajectory that would have brought the U.S. to Nordic-equivalent social-democracy is not reconstructable; the decision points have passed.

The serious critique is not that Nordic outcomes are wrong. It is that pointing at Nordic examples without engaging these structural differences invites a policy misapplication: building a version of the Nordic architecture in the United States that lacks the Nordic conditions and therefore produces different, possibly worse, outcomes.

On scale, the United States does not need to replicate Nordic governance at national scale. Every Nordic welfare-state feature has U.S.-domestic partial implementations at state or municipal scale. Medicare and Medicaid demonstrate federal-level universal-coverage administrative capacity at the sixty-five-and-older population segment. Massachusetts, California, and Washington operate state-level healthcare-access programs. The Alaska Permanent Fund, examined in this chapter's reachable-horizon cluster, is the U.S.-domestic state-scale sovereign-wealth mechanism. Vienna-scale municipal social-housing has Mitchell-Lama in New York City and Montgomery County Housing Opportunities Commission parallels. The United States has already implemented components of the Nordic architecture at sub-national scale. The scale objection becomes empirical, not theoretical: can these components be integrated at national scale? The answer is contingent on political work, not on structural impossibility.

On demographic homogeneity, the steelman's strongest claim. Three responses pressure-test the claim. First, Nordic countries have integrated substantial post-WWII and post-nineteen-nineties immigration and maintained their social-democratic architecture; the ethnic-homogeneity thesis is partially historically constructed rather than essentially permanent. Second, U.S. states with high social-democratic policy adoption — Minnesota, Oregon, Vermont, California, Massachusetts — produce outcomes that correlate with the policy architecture, not with the demographic composition, across varied demographic environments. Third, the framework's claim is not that the United States must become ethnically homogeneous. The claim is that cost severance operates structurally regardless of ethnic composition, and can be partially corrected regardless of ethnic composition, through policy architecture that is cluster-replicable. The demographic objection establishes a real difficulty, not a structural impossibility.

On resource base. The U.S. has a different but equivalent revenue base; twenty-two-trillion-dollar-plus GDP produces a vastly larger tax base than Norway's oil wealth on absolute terms. The constraint is not revenue-generation capacity; it is political will to direct revenue to public provision. The framework's contribution is naming the structural mechanism; the political-will question is downstream of the framework and is the ordinary work of democratic politics, which the framework does not attempt to legislate.

On historical contingency. Yes, specific decision points have passed. Yes, the conditions that would have produced different outcomes at those decision points have changed. This is precisely why a contingency argument that names specific moments — and the framework will name them in the next section — is more useful than a counterfactual rebuild from the eighteen-nineties. The contingency does not extend infinitely backward; it extends from the present forward. The United States can make different decisions now, at identifiable decision points the next chapter will name, and the framework's accountability architecture is what those decisions would be making honest.

The framework's response to the Nordic objection is more specific than "Nordic outcomes can be produced in the United States with current conditions." The cost-severance mechanism the framework names is not a Nordic-culture-dependent phenomenon. The accountability architectures that correct for the mechanism have U.S.-domestic partial-implementations that work. Integrating those partial-implementations at national scale is an empirical-political question, not a structural-impossibility question. And the serious response to Nordic examples is not "those conditions don't exist here" but "which components of those architectures can be integrated at U.S. scale, starting from current conditions, and what would that look like." That work is what this chapter's policy architecture, and the next chapter's closing reflection, attempt to make legible.

### What the eleven-cent difference actually buys

There is one further empirical observation, and it is the structural observation Nordic comparisons are usually made to support but rarely make precisely. A common framing of American tax policy holds that Americans pay less in taxes and accept less in services in exchange — that the "freedom" the lower tax rate purchases is the individual's choice over how to spend the retained income. The structural reality contradicts this trade-off. Americans pay nearly as much as Swedes in total tax burden and receive almost none of it back as guaranteed services.

The numbers, drawn from the Tax Foundation's twenty-twenty-four cross-national comparison, are these. The tax wedge for a single average-wage worker is approximately thirty percent in the United States and approximately forty-two percent in Sweden — an eleven-point difference. The tax-to-GDP ratio is approximately twenty-six percent for the United States and forty-one percent for Sweden — a fifteen-point gap in what the whole economy contributes to public goods. What the eleven-cent difference actually purchases on the Swedish side is universal healthcare; free university education with monthly student stipends; four hundred and eighty days of parental leave per family; subsidized childcare; housing support; substantially longer life expectancy with less variance across class lines; and a measurably stronger social-trust environment that operates as civic infrastructure across every other domain of public life.

The healthcare-financing component of that eleven-cent difference deserves explicit engagement, because it is the dimension on which the United States is uniquely positioned among wealthy nations. The U.S. healthcare-financing architecture combines employer-tied insurance for the working population, Medicare for the over-sixty-five population, Medicaid for the lowest-income population, ACA-marketplace coverage for the in-between population, and an estimated twenty-eight million people without coverage at any given time. Among the wealthy nations whose tax burdens are comparable to or exceed the U.S. burden, none produces this configuration. Sweden, Denmark, Norway, Germany, the United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia each implement different financing architectures — single-payer government-funded; multi-payer regulated-private-insurance; mixed-system employer-and-state — and each delivers universal coverage at outcomes that compare favorably with U.S. outcomes on every standard health-quality metric (life expectancy; infant mortality; maternal mortality; preventable mortality; treatment outcomes for specific conditions tracked by OECD Health Statistics and the Commonwealth Fund's Mirror Mirror cross-national report).

The empirical pattern this produces in the U.S. is the medical-crowdfunding default. GoFundMe and equivalent platforms have become, since roughly 2010, a structural feature of U.S. healthcare-financing — a routinely-deployed safety-net layer that compensates for the gaps the formal architecture produces. Berliner and Kenworthy's 2017 study documented over 250,000 medical-crowdfunding campaigns annually with average campaigns covering 25-40 percent of stated need; subsequent research (Ly and Soman 2020; Kenworthy and Igra 2024) confirms the pattern at scale and documents differential success rates that mirror existing healthcare-access disparities. None of the comparison countries produces medical crowdfunding at this scale, because none of them produces the financing-architecture gap the U.S. architecture leaves uncovered.

The framework's reading of this is the same as its reading of the retirement-security comparison Chapter 4 develops. The U.S. healthcare-financing architecture is a contingent choice, made at specific historical moments under specific political-economy conditions, that produces a distribution of healthcare-cost severance specific to that architecture. What was constructed once can be reconstructed; what specific reconstruction would address the structural problem is the work of ordinary democratic politics, which the framework does not attempt to legislate.

The structural claim is not "Americans should adopt Swedish tax rates." The structural claim is that Americans already pay comparable tax burden and merely fail to receive comparable provision. The trade-off was sold as freedom. It functioned as the elimination of the floor beneath workers while preserving the ideological narrative that the floor was never needed. The architecture that underwrites the difference is the rent-extractor cluster — insurance, pharmaceutical, private-education-finance, student-loan-servicing — that captures the value public provision would have absorbed; the political-capture shield that prevents architectural revision; and the information shield that keeps the cross-national comparison out of ordinary American political discourse.

The framework's role here is structural observation, not advocacy. The accounting demonstrates that the trade-off the lower-tax framing claims to purchase is not what is actually being purchased. What is actually being purchased is a different value-capture architecture, which routes the same total tax burden through different intermediaries to different beneficiaries. Naming this is the framework's contribution. What political path leads from the current architecture to one that produces Nordic-comparable provision at Nordic-comparable burden is the work of ordinary democratic politics — and the work of the contingency argument the next section opens with.

### Entering wedges

One useful way to see that none of this is utopian is to count the pieces of it that already exist.

Carbon pricing, in the narrow sense of a tax or trading scheme on greenhouse emissions, operates in more than seventy jurisdictions as of 2025, covering roughly a quarter of global emissions. The schemes are imperfect. The coverage is uneven. The prices, in most cases, are below the social cost of carbon as estimated by every major economic body that has tried to estimate it. None of this changes the fact that a substantial, legally functional, politically defended mechanism for pricing part of what residual commons value would account for — the externality tail of fossil fuel combustion on the atmospheric commons — already exists and expands, year by year, to cover more sources and more jurisdictions.

Sovereign wealth funds built on resource rents exist in Norway, Alaska, several Gulf states, and in smaller form across dozens of other jurisdictions. Each is a partial implementation of the principle that the rents of finite resource extraction belong, in some meaningful sense, to the political community whose commons was drawn from — not to the individual entity that extracted it. Norway has demonstrated, over fifty years, that it is politically possible to sustain the discipline required to capture resource rents and preserve them for intergenerational equity. The discipline has survived changes of government, pressure from industry, and the ordinary temptations of a wealthy society with a large pool of available capital. The architectural-choice frame extends beyond resource rents: Chapter 4 develops the comparison between Norway's sovereign-fund architecture and the United States Social Security system, demonstrating that retirement-security architectures are themselves contingent choices made in particular political-economy conditions, and that the framework's accountability-bond logic prices both. It is not a miracle. It is an institution, built deliberately, maintained deliberately.

The collective-capture pattern is not confined to resource rents, and it is not confined to one scale. It operates in infrastructure, in labor, and in housing, each of which has a working example of a commons held at the level of the community that uses it.

In Chattanooga, Tennessee — in the heart of the kind of political economy where the framework is supposed to be impossible — the municipally-owned electric utility EPB launched a fiber-optic network in 2010 that now delivers gigabit service city-wide and speeds up to twenty-five gigabits for those who need them, at prices a fraction of what the regional private incumbents charge. The network is profitable. It cross-subsidizes the electric smart grid that was the original rationale for the fiber. It has done this while being sued by Comcast and while the Tennessee state legislature has passed laws specifically to prevent EPB from extending the service beyond its electric territory to neighboring communities that have been asking for it. The service survived. The service expanded within the permitted footprint. The value that would otherwise have been captured as rent by national broadband carriers is now instead funding Chattanooga's grid modernization and closing the city's digital divide. It is the reachable-horizon case for infrastructure commons at municipal scale, demonstrated in a jurisdiction with no obvious political predisposition to favor it.

In the Basque region of northern Spain, the Mondragon Corporation — a federation of more than ninety-five worker-owned cooperatives, employing roughly eighty thousand people and producing annual revenues in the tens of billions of euros — has operated continuously since 1956. It is the largest worker-cooperative system in the world, it manufactures everything from washing machines to bank services to university education, and it has survived, adapted to, and continued to grow through nearly seventy years of European economic history that includes the end of Franco's dictatorship, Spain's integration into the European Union, the 2008 financial crisis, and the 2013 bankruptcy of its flagship appliance manufacturer Fagor. The cooperatives operate under an internal pay ratio cap — the highest compensation in most cooperatives is limited to roughly six to nine times the lowest — and reinvest a substantial portion of surplus in a shared federation bank and technological institute that function as collective capital. Mondragon is not utopia. It has limits and internal debates the rest of Europe has been watching closely for decades. But it demonstrates, at cooperative-sector scale, over a multi-generational time horizon, that the productive capacity of an industrial firm can be captured by the workers who produce it rather than by external shareholders, without collapsing the firm's ability to compete. The labor-ownership commons is an operational architecture, not a thought experiment.

In Vienna, roughly sixty percent of the population lives in housing that is either municipally owned — the Gemeindebau, a stock of more than two hundred thousand apartments built and maintained by the city — or operated by non-profit cooperative associations that serve the same function under a slightly different legal structure. The program was initiated in 1919 by the Red Vienna government, it survived an interwar fascist coup and Nazi occupation, and it has been maintained across every administration and every government that has governed Vienna since the Second World War. Rents are substantially below private-market comparables. The buildings are not stigmatized as poverty housing; middle-class and working-class residents live in the same stock, and the architecture is in some cases of explicit historical and aesthetic importance. The system is funded through a one-percent payroll contribution — the Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag — combined with tenant rents and subsidized federal loans, and it has sustained itself through a century of use. Vienna is the counter-evidence to every argument that says public housing inherently concentrates poverty and decays. It demonstrates that a municipal-majority housing commons is operationally achievable across century-plus horizons at a scale large enough to stabilize an entire capital city's cost-of-living structure.

None of Chattanooga, Mondragon, or Vienna implements the full Commons Bonds framework. All of them are partial. Each demonstrates a different answer to the same objection — that the collective-capture architecture cannot work at scale, cannot work durably, cannot work in a diverse political economy, cannot work without stigmatizing its beneficiaries, cannot work against the opposition of concentrated private capital. In each case, it does. The burden of proof on the objection has shifted. The framework's claim is not that the architecture is easy. The claim is that it is reachable from where we currently stand, which is the only useful claim a practical framework can make.

Strategic petroleum reserves, rare earth stockpiles, and critical minerals stockpiles exist in most industrial nations, though rarely organized around a coherent framework. The infrastructure for designating, storing, and managing strategically reserved quantities of specific resources is in place. What is missing is the principle that selects which resources to reserve and how much — which is exactly what existential substitutability gap classification provides.

The reservation logic the framework's classification produces is not theoretical even at the narrow-domain level. Multiple jurisdictions have implemented rare-earth strategic-resource architectures in response to supply-chain dependency on Baotou and the broader Chinese rare-earth processing infrastructure. The United States restored Mountain Pass mine production through MP Materials' acquisition in 2018, recovering domestic supply capacity that had been lost to consolidation in the 2000s. The European Union enacted the Critical Raw Materials Act in 2023, formally classifying thirty-four materials as strategically critical and requiring stockpile-and-alternative-sourcing programs. Japan responded to the 2010 Senkaku-Islands rare-earth-export-restriction crisis by funding Lynas Corporation's Australian-Malaysian processing infrastructure as alternative-sourcing capacity; South Korea built equivalent reserves following the 2019 Japan-Korea trade dispute. None of these programs implements the framework's full reservation discipline, and none was designed with the framework's vocabulary in mind. Each demonstrates that the IES-gap-driven reservation logic is operationally reachable when supply-chain dependency raises the visibility of the underlying classification problem.

One other partial-implementation case is worth naming, because it is live, contested, and exercises the framework's full apparatus on a near-term policy decision. The Clarion-Clipperton Zone in the eastern Pacific Ocean — a four-and-a-half-million-square-kilometer international-waters area — holds the largest known accessible deposits of polymetallic nodules containing cobalt, nickel, manganese, and rare-earth elements. The International Seabed Authority, established under UNCLOS in 1982, has issued exploration contracts since 2014 and is currently negotiating the commercial-mining code that would authorize extraction. The Nauru-sponsored 2-year-rule trigger in 2021 set a deadline pressure on those negotiations that has structured the debate since.

The framework's reading of the case follows from Steps 1 through 4 directly. Cobalt, nickel, manganese, and rare earths are high-existential substitutability gap resources. The CCZ ecosystem is in the externality-tail-unbounded category similar to Chapter 7's Europa-ocean treatment — the recovery timescales of deep-sea ecosystems following disturbance are unknown on multi-decadal-to-multi-century scales. Asymmetric-regret reasoning under these parameters produces a clear preserve-and-study verdict. The framework's logic does not depend on prescribing what the ISA should specifically rule; it depends on whether the ISA architecture's verification structure resists the simulated-reattachment adaptation Chapter 7's immune-response stress test surfaced. That question is being answered in real time by the negotiating parties.

The framework is structural-observational here, not prescriptive. It does not take a position on the mining decision. It reads the case the way it reads any extraction case — through the IES-gap classification, the externality-tail bounding, the asymmetric-regret math, and the architectural-test-of-accountability question. What the world does with that reading is the work of the parties to the decision, and is not the framework's place to legislate.

Mining reclamation bonds exist in the United States under the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977, and in various forms in most mining jurisdictions worldwide. They are structurally underpriced, as Chapter 2 documented — the current bond pool covers a fraction of actual reclamation costs, and the gap has been papered over through bankruptcy-driven transfer of liability to the public — but the mechanism itself is sound. Price the bonds correctly, index them to actual remediation costs rather than nominal amounts set decades ago, require refresh as the liability tail lengthens, and the reclamation bond becomes a serious accountability instrument rather than a token gesture.

Environmental impact assessments, established under NEPA in the United States in 1970 and adopted in similar forms by most jurisdictions, are a partial implementation of pricing the externality tail — a procedural, non-monetized version, admittedly, but a legally durable one. The border carbon adjustment now partially operating in the European Union is a partial implementation of transboundary pricing. Health-impact fees on extractive industries exist in several U.S. states, funding black lung trust funds and related mechanisms. The Community Reinvestment Act, for all its limitations, is a partial implementation of the spatial justice principle — the idea that capital extracted from a place owes something to the place it was extracted from.

Each of these is a partial, imperfect, politically achievable implementation of one slice of the residual commons value framework. None of them was designed with the framework in mind; they evolved piecemeal, in response to specific crises, under specific political coalitions, and they reflect all of the compromises that such politics produce. Taken together, they demonstrate something important: every component of honest pricing exists in some form, in some jurisdiction, and has survived the political process long enough to become institutional furniture. The framework's task is not to invent the instruments. It is to connect them, strengthen them, align them around a coherent principle, and extend them to the resources they do not currently cover.

This is the path forward. It is neither revolutionary nor utopian. It is incremental institutional work, made strategic by the framework that organizes it.

### What a framework does

The hardest question this chapter has to answer is the most pragmatic one, and it is usually asked by people who have actually tried to pass legislation.

Carbon pricing has been debated for thirty years. The most ambitious implementation — the EU Emissions Trading System — covers a portion of emissions, is riddled with exemptions, and was nearly destroyed by lobbying during the 2008 financial crisis. If the world cannot implement a simple carbon tax after three decades of scientific consensus on climate change, the argument runs, how exactly do you propose to implement a comprehensive residual commons value pricing regime covering every high-existential substitutability gap resource, every externality tail, every intergenerational foreclosure, every transboundary externality, and every coordination problem across every jurisdiction on Earth? Show me the legislative pathway. Show me the political coalition. Show me the international treaty framework. Without those, the argument concludes, this is another book by another person who understands the problem and cannot solve it.

The objection deserves the most honest answer I know how to give.

This is not a legislative blueprint. It is a framework. The political pathway to implementation — which laws, which jurisdictions, which coalitions, which treaty architecture — is beyond the scope of what any single book can produce, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest about the nature of what a framework does and does not do.

But note what frameworks do that legislation cannot. They change how people think, which eventually changes what legislation becomes conceivable.

David Harvey's "accumulation by dispossession" did not pass a single law. It gave people a vocabulary for naming a structural pattern they had been experiencing for decades without a word for it. Once the word existed, the political conversation around gentrification, enclosure, and privatization changed. Legislation — uneven, partial, contested — followed.

Elinor Ostrom's commons governance framework did not create any new institution. It identified design principles that dozens of existing local institutions had independently evolved to govern common-pool resources sustainably. Once the principles were named and systematized, they legitimized the local approaches against the twin pressures of privatization and state management, and they have since informed fisheries policy, forestry policy, and water governance in more jurisdictions than can easily be counted.

Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics has not yet, to my knowledge, passed a single law. It has been adopted as a guiding framework by the city of Amsterdam, by several regional governments, by parts of the European Union climate architecture, and by a growing number of development agencies, because it gives those institutions a coherent way to think about what they were already trying to do. The laws those jurisdictions pass from here forward will be shaped by the framework, even if no law cites it directly.

Matthew Desmond's Evicted did not pass a law. It produced, through the force of its narrative and the specificity of its reporting, a change in how housing instability is discussed in American political life, and that change has made housing policy experiments — rent support, eviction moratoriums, right-to-counsel — politically conceivable in ways they were not a decade earlier.

Commons Bonds is, and can only be, an attempt to do the same. It gives people a vocabulary — cost severance, residual commons value, severed cost, accountability bond, the existential substitutability gap. It gives them a measurement tool — the RCV model, in its three convergent forms, testable against any extraction scenario in any jurisdiction. It gives them a framework for thinking about resource pricing they can apply in their own contexts: a coal-country resident asking what their community was worth; a municipal council setting reclamation bonds; a state legislature designing a sovereign wealth fund; an international body debating a critical minerals agreement. The book does not need to pass a law. It needs to change the terms of the debate so that the right laws become thinkable.

And it needs to point, honestly, at the entering wedges: the carbon pricing regimes that already exist, the sovereign wealth funds that already exist, the reclamation bonds that already exist, the critical minerals stockpiles that already exist, the environmental impact assessments that already exist, the border carbon adjustments that already exist. The work is not to build everything from scratch. The work is to strengthen what exists, connect what is currently disconnected, align the fragments around a coherent principle, and extend the principle to the resources and externalities it does not yet cover. That work is difficult. It is not impossible. People who have done harder institutional work before have done it with less.

The framework will not do this on its own. No framework ever does. What it does is give the people who do the work a common language and a common instrument to do the work with. Whether the work gets done is the question every generation answers for the one that follows.

---

And so, with the framework laid out and the entering wedges named, the book turns toward its closing question — which is not, any longer, what extraction actually costs, or what honest pricing would look like, or how the institutions might be bent toward it. Those questions have been answered, within the limits of what a single volume can answer.

The remaining question is a different kind of question.

The miner in McDowell County. The waterman on the Chesapeake. The villager in Baotou. The farmer irrigating from the Ogallala. The hypothetical off-world administrator on Mars, making the resource decisions that will determine whether the habitat survives.

They are not, the book has argued, separate problems. They are the same problem in different parameters. The framework that prices one prices all of them. The mechanism that severs cost from value in one context operates by the same structural logic in every other.

If that is true, then the last chapter has to ask what follows from recognizing it — not as policy, but as something closer to a fact about ourselves and the generations we belong to, and the ones we owe.

That is where the book goes from here.
