## §AUTHOR-ZONE-2: The 120-hour week — what the contract did not capture

[*Guidance line 117 already has Chris's authored draft of this passage. Reproduced below as starting point for placement; Chris's revision welcome.*]

There is a specific kind of 120-hour week that does not appear in any ledger. Not the hours that were billed — those were billed accurately, at rates both parties had agreed to. What does not appear is the cognitive overhead that followed the hours home, the pattern-matching fatigue that meant I was not present even when I was physically home, the compounding weight of problems that did not fit in the contract but followed me anyway because I was the person hired to carry them. The contract captured the billable time. It did not capture what the billable time was displacing. My body was in the meeting. My attention was partitioned across four open problems, six stakeholder relationships, and two deadlines that would arrive before the plane landed. The work captured my attention. The attention it captured was taken from somewhere — from my wife recovering from dying, from my son who could have been held longer, from the version of my life that required me to be physically, cognitively, emotionally present. What was taken was not billed. The party that took it did not pay for it. I did.

## §AUTHOR-ZONE-3: The consent problem in its sharpest form

[*Guidance line 132-136 already has Chris's authored draft. Reproduced for placement.*]

A free-market economist will say you made voluntary trade-offs. The chapter should address this directly — and the specific form the addressing should take is: *you had options a coal miner in McDowell County does not have*. You had elite technical skills, multiple employers, geographic mobility, and significant compensation. And you still ended up on a plane while your wife recovered from dying.

That is the most interesting version of the consent problem in the entire book, because it rules out the easy explanation. You did not lack alternatives. You did not lack information. You were not coerced in any conventional sense. And the framework still applies, because the voluntariness of the exchange was nominal rather than real — the contract priced the billable time, not the displacement. The choice was not between mining coal and investment banking. It was between billing 120 hours and losing the income trajectory that funded the healthcare the family depended on. A choice made under those conditions is a choice, but it is not the free choice that free-market theory presupposes.

This is the sharpest demonstration of the framework you can make, because it forecloses the easiest dismissal. If the framework applies to someone with your resources, it applies with greater force to people with fewer.
