# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 3: The Waterman

---

The harbor at Old Point Comfort wakes a long time before the city does. The first work boat eases out of its slip in a light that is not yet light. A diesel coughs and settles. A man on the next pier says something to a man on the boat in a voice neither of them needs to raise, because at that hour sound carries across the water as if the water itself were an instrument. The Fort sits dark behind them. The mouth of the Bay opens out ahead, gray on gray, the channel marker blinking red. Somewhere on the far side of the channel, a tug is moving north toward Newport News. You hear it before you see it.

I have watched this happen a thousand times. I did not grow up on the water in the way the men going out before dawn grew up on it — my hands are wrong for it, my back is wrong for it — but I have lived close enough to it for long enough that the rhythm has become part of how I keep time. The boats go out before sunrise in any season the regulations allow. They come back in the afternoon, tired. Some come back with a hold full of crabs; some come back with a hold full of nothing, and the men coming home in the second case are quieter than the men coming home in the first, but neither set is talkative. They tie up. They hose down. They walk to a truck. They will be back at the slip at four in the morning, or they will not, and the Bay will not notice the difference one way or the other, but the slip will, and the dock will, and the man two slips over who has been doing this since before any of them were born will notice, because that is how the working waterfront keeps its books.

This chapter is about what those books are not allowed to record.

---

The community on this water has a name, in the place I am writing from. The neighborhood of Hampton I am closest to is called Fox Hill, and Fox Hill is, or was, an island fishing community — a place where, for most of the twentieth century, almost every family in every house had at least one person on the water. My grandfather worked at NASA Langley — twelve miles or so up the road — building pieces of a different kind of America in a different kind of building, and he was an exception. The neighbors on either side of him were on the water. The men my father grew up with as boys were the sons of watermen. My father, who is from Fox Hill, did not become a waterman, but the men who taught him how to handle a small boat and where the fish were running and what the smell of the channel meant in late August were men whose lives were the water. My family is one generation removed from the working life. We are zero generations removed from the community.

Most days at most hours, the Chesapeake looks like a postcard — flat water, soft light, the kind of bay you would put a child on. Some days it is not. If you have never worked a small boat in the Chesapeake you should know one thing about the water that the watermen know in the way they know their own hands. The Bay is not the ocean. The waves on the Bay are different from the waves on the ocean in a specific way that matters. In the open ocean, a five-foot swell is almost not worth a comment, because the wavelength — the distance and the time between one peak and the next — is long. Twenty seconds between waves on the Atlantic feels like a slow rocking. The Bay does not give you twenty seconds. The Bay's wavelength runs three or four seconds, sometimes less, because the body of water producing the waves is shallow and finite, and the energy in those waves stacks differently than open-ocean energy. A three-foot swell on the Chesapeake can be steeper than a much larger swell on the Atlantic. A Bay chop on a bad afternoon will roll a small boat in ways an oceangoing yacht in much larger seas does not get rolled. The men who have worked this water for forty years know which days are the days you do not go out and which days are the days you can go out but you stay close to home and which days the sky is lying about what the water is going to do in three hours, and they know all of it without having to consult anything external to themselves. The water grants them this knowledge in exchange for the obligation never to mistake which day they are looking at.

What the watermen know is not what someone learns by going out on weekends. It is what someone learns by going out every day for forty years — the sweltering summer days when the heat takes the air; the winter mornings when the gale wind comes off the cape and blows through whatever you are wearing and shakes your very soul. The men go out anyway, because the houses are not paid for and the families do not eat when there is nothing to bring home. You go out early. You stay out until you bring food back.

The community that grows up around that obligation has a particular shape. It is not a metaphor when the watermen of a place like Fox Hill say that they look out for each other. It is a structural condition of the work. A waterman whose nets need rebuilding before the next season is a waterman who needs help rebuilding them, because the nets cannot be done by one man in one season's worth of available time, and there is no industry of net-rebuilders to hire. So a morning is set, and at three or four in the morning, when everyone in this community would normally already be on the water or on the way to it, the boats stay tied up and the men come instead to whoever's place needs the nets. They come in pickups. They bring their own coffee. They work until it is done. The understanding behind the morning is not the understanding of a favor done. It is the understanding that, as surely as the sun rises and the sun sets, there will be a morning when the man who needs the help is you or your son or your father, and the community that fails to come on the day you need it is a community that has already failed before you needed it. That is the bargain, and the bargain holds for as long as the community holds.

The most precise thing the bargain produces, the thing that is hardest to render to anyone who has not been around it, is what happens when one of the men who said he would be there is not. Everyone stops. The nets get put down. Somebody picks up a phone and calls somebody else. Within ten minutes the whole morning has reorganized itself around the question of where the missing man is, because everyone there has done this work for long enough to know that the only thing that keeps a waterman from being where he said he would be is something serious enough that he cannot tell anyone about it. It might be his truck. It might be his health. It might be — in the older men's bodies, which carry this possibility the way a cook's body carries the possibility of burns — that he was on the water at four-thirty and did not come back, and the water is the part of the morning the rest of the men have to go check on first.

This is the community Fox Hill was for most of my father's life, and the community I have watched, in my own life, slowly thin.

---

To understand what is happening on the Chesapeake right now, you have to start with what the Chesapeake was. Not in some prehistoric sense — not before the Powhatan, not before the English came up the James in 1607 — but in a register the people working the water still carry in their bodies. I have lived near this water long enough to have known three rough generations of the men who work it. The Bay that the grandfathers fished was not the Bay that the fathers fished, and the Bay that the fathers fished is not the Bay that the sons would have fished if there had been a Bay to fish. Across three generations, the resource has been visibly thinning. Not in some places, not in some seasons. Everywhere. Every season.

The oyster is the easiest place to begin, because the oyster is the species the historical record carries most clearly. In the late 1880s the Chesapeake produced something on the order of fifteen million bushels of oysters a year. By the 1990s, that number had fallen to under two hundred thousand. Two orders of magnitude in a century. A 99 percent decline. There are not many fisheries in the world that have lost that much of themselves and remained recognizable as the same fishery, and there is a real argument that the Chesapeake oyster fishery did not, in fact, remain recognizable — that the thing the men were going out for in 1990 was a different thing from what their grandfathers had been going out for in 1890, even though the boats looked similar and the tongs looked similar and the dock at the end of the day looked similar.

A 99 percent decline is not a fluctuation. It is not bad luck. It is not the weather. The mechanisms are now well documented and were largely understood at the time. The first wave was overharvesting — dredges that scraped the reefs flat, season after season, faster than the reefs could rebuild. The second wave was disease, MSX in the late 1950s and Dermo in the years that followed, parasites that the weakened, low-density populations had no resilience against. The third wave was sediment, agricultural runoff smothering the oyster spat where they tried to set, and nutrient loading that fed algal blooms that consumed the dissolved oxygen the rest of the system depended on. Each wave compounded the last. The reefs that had once been visible at low tide — that ships in the colonial period had to chart around — were ground down to flat, silt-buried bottom. An oyster needs something hard to set on. By the late twentieth century, much of the bottom the oysters had built across centuries had been ground into the same mud the runoff was depositing on top of it.

An oyster reef is not a coal seam. A coal seam is finite from the day it forms. You can extract it faster or slower, but you cannot extract more of it than was laid down. An oyster reef is something else. An oyster reef is, in principle, an instrument that produces more oysters every year, as long as you do not damage the instrument. Take some — leave the rest — let the rest spawn — come back next year. Done correctly, the harvest does not diminish across the generations. You can do it, and your son can do it, and your son's son can do it. The fishery makes more of itself.

What happened in the Chesapeake is what happens when the rate of taking exceeds the rate of making. For a while — sometimes a long while — the difference is invisible. The harvest is good. The price is right. The boats go out. Then the difference becomes visible and irreversible at almost the same moment, because the structure that produced the renewal is itself part of what was being taken. The reef the oysters built is the bottom the next generation would have set on. When you ground the reef down, you did not just take this year's oysters. You took the capacity of the bottom to make any oysters at all. From that point on, leaving the system alone is no longer the answer. You have to put something back, or accept that what was lost stays lost.

Nobody marks the day this happens. The men going out the next morning go out the same way they went out the morning before. But something has been done that cannot be undone by any decision the next morning's men can make.

One of the oystermen my father knew, growing up on Fox Hill, was a man called Biggie. Biggie had the build of an oak — not tall, in the way some big men are not tall, but solid through the shoulders and the back in a way that registered as the build of a man who had been doing one specific kind of work for most of his adult life. He worked oysters. He had been working oysters since he was old enough to be useful in a boat with whoever brought him into one. The bars he worked were bars he had worked his entire life. The boat he ran was a boat suited to the work. He went out before the day. He came back when the bushels were what the bushels were going to be that day. You could watch Biggie work his bars with one oar between the beds, the kind of effortlessness that has no wasted motion in it, and you could see immediately that his family had been doing this for generations.

Biggie carried, every morning he went out, a six-pack of Coca-Cola onto the boat. He did not carry a lunch. The first time my father asked him about the lunch — my father was a small boy then, still young enough to be honestly puzzled about it — Biggie told him that his lunch was waiting for him in the water. Once the sun was high enough in the day and the work had hit its rhythm, Biggie would settle into eating oysters as he worked. He had a particular spot on the boat, near his body, where he would set the oyster he was about to shuck. He did not have to reach. He did not have to lift his arm. The motion he used to shuck was the motion of a man who had reduced the work to its smallest possible movement after a working life of doing it — a quarter turn of the wrist, the shell parting, the meat coming up clean. He ate as he worked. My father watched him as a boy, more than once, and tried to count how many oysters Biggie put away in a single day on the water. My father told me, when I was old enough to ask, that he had never managed to keep count. Not because there were too many to count, although there may have been. Because the eating was so woven into the work that there was never a single moment when an oyster started or stopped being eaten — it was simply something Biggie was doing alongside everything else he was doing, the way breathing is something a person is doing alongside everything else.

Biggie has been dead for more than thirty years. He died before Virginia stopped its commercial shad fishery in 1994, which is a date the men still on the water sometimes use as a marker for when things were still working. He went out with what an earlier generation of men had gone out with. He came back with what they had come back with. The bars sounded under his tongs the way the bars had sounded under the tongs of the men before him.

The bars do not sound that way anymore. The motion of the wrist that Biggie's body had reduced to its smallest possible movement is a motion that no younger man at Fox Hill is being shown how to make, because no younger man at Fox Hill is going out to bars that would let him learn it. The motion is dying with the men who hold it.

---

The men who would tell this story, if you sat with them long enough on a dock late in a season that had not gone the way the season was supposed to go, do not call it any of these things. They do not say *renewable*. They do not say *regeneration rate*. They say what they remember. They say their grandfather used to go out of a place that doesn't exist as a working dock anymore. They say the boats their fathers ran were named after wives or daughters and that some of those names are still on the boats and some are not. They say that when they were small they could stand in the bow on a calm summer day off the mouth of the Poquoson River and see the bottom — actually see it, see the grass and the crabs walking through the grass — and that this is not something a child can do anymore in most parts of the Bay where it could once be done. They say the water is greener than it used to be in a way that does not feel like life. They say the smell at the dock has changed, slightly, in late August. They say their fathers used to come home with a kind of catch that does not come home anymore.

A man in his sixties who has worked the Lower Bay since he was twelve does not need to read a stock assessment to know that something has changed. He has been making the comparison every morning for fifty years.

This is the texture of generational witness on the water. It is not nostalgic. It is closer in tone to the way you might describe a relative's slow illness: the small precise observations of someone who has been paying attention long enough that the attention has become a part of how they see. The sense one gets, around men like this, is not anger so much as a kind of patient, unsentimental clarity. They are not asking to be pitied. Most of them would refuse the offer. They are simply telling you what they have seen.

What they have seen is the disappearance of margins. The seasons that used to carry a year now carry only part of one. The species that used to fill in for each other when one ran short do not fill in the same way. The crabs are smaller, on average, than the crabs they used to bring in. The runs of striped bass that their fathers had timed to the calendar do not run on the calendar anymore. The water itself seems to have a different temper. None of these are stories. They are observations across forty or fifty years of one of the longest continuous attentional records any working person in America keeps. Their bodies are the instrument. Their memory is the data.

J.C. Hudgins, who runs the Virginia Watermen's Association and works the Piankatank River in the Middle Bay, has put the math in his own words. *You're lucky if you can put $150 in your pocket per day,* he has told a reporter for *Maryland Matters*. *And it ain't no easy job. You're freezing your butt off. It's cold.* On the buying end: *People aren't going to spend $20 for a pint of oysters. It's pretty expensive eating. I think people are feeding their families in other ways.*

---

The striped bass — what they call rockfish on the Bay — is the species that taught Maryland and Virginia, in the 1980s, what intervention can do. I am old enough to remember it. The watermen I knew as a boy at the marinas where my family kept boats were talking, as the moratorium years came on, about something they had not had to talk about before. I did not understand the politics of it then. I understood, the way a child can understand without being told, that a thing had reached a point where the men whose work depended on it had agreed, with whatever reluctance, to stop the work for a long time so that the thing could come back.

By the late 1970s, the population had collapsed badly enough that Maryland and Virginia, working with the federal government, agreed to a moratorium. For five years on the Maryland side, beginning in 1985, no one fished commercially for striped bass at all. Not the watermen. Not the charter fleet. Not the recreational anglers. The species was given a generation off. It was a hard ask of the people whose income depended on the fishery. Many of them lost a livelihood for the duration. Some of them did not come back to the work. But the moratorium worked, in the narrow sense that the moratorium had been designed to do. The spawning population recovered. By 1995 the fishery had been declared restored. The men who had given up five years saw, at the end of that five years, an instrument that had been brought back from a place very few fisheries get brought back from.

That recovery is part of why what is happening now is hard for the men who remember it. Because what is happening now to the rockfish is what was happening to the rockfish before the moratorium. The juvenile recruitment numbers, year after year, are coming in below the long-term average. In 2024 the Maryland young-of-year index was 2.0 fish per sample, against a long-term average of 11.0. That is not a bad season. That is a structural signal. It is the sixth such season in a row.

Allison Colden, the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's Maryland executive director, watching that signal accumulate across the years, has put the situation in terms the watermen would recognize. *Maryland's striped bass surveys have made it clear,* she has said in public statements; *we're facing a looming disaster. Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future.* In a different interview, more bluntly: *there are very few levers left to pull.* Many people in Maryland, she has said elsewhere, *rely on striped bass for their food, their businesses, and their futures. Without action, that will all be at risk.* The rising water temperatures and the low-oxygen periods of summer — both worsened by warming — are *pummeling* the species, in her word, during the months it can least afford to be pummeled.

The men on the water do not need to be told this. They felt it before the survey numbers came in. What the survey numbers do is force the institutions to acknowledge what the watermen have been carrying as a kind of private foreknowledge — the way miners know what the dust is doing to them years before the X-rays come back. The institutional acknowledgment, when it comes, is always late. Later than the data would have allowed. Later than the watermen had warned. Late in a way the moratorium of 1985 was not, because in 1985 there had been a political will that has thinned in the decades since, and the regulatory architecture that used to be capable of asking a fishery to take a generation off has lost some of the capacity to ask.

What the watermen are reading, when they read the rockfish, is not really the rockfish. It is whether the institutional system that was built to manage the Bay is capable of acting on what its own science is telling it. So far in this round, the answer has been mostly no. Catch limits get tightened in increments smaller than the science has indicated are warranted. Emergency actions get debated longer than the species can spare. The committees meet, the substitute motions are offered, and the language of *err on the side of conservation* gets answered with the language of *we are not yet certain enough to act.* The asymmetry is the point. When the offer was to increase the harvest, the committees acted with confidence. When the offer is to reduce it, the committees discover an unfamiliar caution.

Bill Goldsborough, a fisheries scientist at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation through the rockfish moratorium years, has put the institutional time-scale to a reporter for *Smithsonian Magazine*. *Our objective is to try to restore the health of bay and aquatic life so that it can support viable fisheries,* he has said. *We see that task taking place over a longer term [...]. But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment.*

A retired waterman who worked the Bay through the 1985 moratorium and into the recovery and through the present decline can tell you in a single sentence how this looks from his deck. He gave up five years of his life so that a fishery could come back. He watched it come back. He watched it begin to go again. He has been told, gently and not so gently, by people whose job is to manage the resource, that the political conditions for another moratorium do not exist. He has not been told what the political conditions for another collapse are.

---

The rockfish recovery is not the only intervention story this Bay has produced. A few years after the rockfish moratorium ended, in January 1994, Virginia closed its commercial shad fishery — the spring run that had been one of the Bay's reliable fisheries through most of the twentieth century. The closure was meant, like the rockfish moratorium, to give the species a generation off so the runs could rebuild. Shad live most of their lives in the open Atlantic but come into the Bay each spring to spawn, running up the tributary rivers to lay their eggs in fresh water. The Bay is the spawning ground for the shad of an entire region. When its rivers fail the shad, the shad fail in the ocean too. The runs did not come back after 1994. The science on why has not converged on a single explanation. What it has converged on is the absence of a recovery. There are not, in any working sense, shad in the Bay anymore. The same instrument that worked once for rockfish was tried for shad and arrived too late.

---

Then there is the blue crab.

The blue crab is not the oyster. It does not build a reef. Its life cycle is short — a year and a half, sometimes two — which means in principle the population can rebound from a bad year quickly if the next year is good. This is one of the reasons the Chesapeake blue crab fishery was, for a long time, the Bay's most reliable seasonal income for working watermen. The crabs were here. They came back in spring. They were tonged and trotlined and potted through the warm months, sold to picking houses up and down the Bay where mostly women picked the meat by hand into containers that ended up in restaurants in cities where most of the people eating them had never seen a crab pot in the water.

The blue crab fishery is now in a kind of trouble the oyster fishery already lived through and the rockfish fishery is living through. The 2025 winter dredge survey put the Bay-wide population at around 238 million crabs — the second-lowest level since the survey began in 1990. It was the sixth consecutive year of below-average numbers. *The red flags are flying for blue crabs,* Colden has said. And the structural framing: *with more than five years of below average crab numbers, it is clear that changing conditions in the Bay are undermining the current management of this important species.*

That sentence carries more than it appears to. *Changing conditions* are *undermining* a management regime that was designed for an earlier Bay. The regulatory architecture is being outrun by the system it was built to govern. It is not that the rules are wrong. It is that the rules were calibrated to a system that no longer exists. The committees that set the limits are still meeting on the schedule the previous Bay had. The crabs are not on that schedule anymore. The Bay is not on that schedule anymore.

The men on the water have been reading this from the deck for decades. They do not call it that. They say what they see. *They aren't where they used to be. They aren't running when they used to run. The bigger ones are gone. We aren't catching what we used to catch.*

---

There is a fish you have probably never eaten. It is small. It is oily. It is silver. It runs in schools so dense that their movement can flatten the surface of the water like a passing wind, and it is, by weight, one of the most important fish in the Atlantic ecosystem.

The menhaden. Bunker, on the New Jersey side. Pogy, on the New England side. On the Bay, just menhaden.

You do not eat it because it is not pleasant to eat. What it is, instead, is the thing that almost everything else eats — the forage fish at the base of the Bay's food web, the species the striped bass and the bluefish and the ospreys and the loons and the dolphins all come for. It is also the species that is harvested, in the largest single fishery on the East Coast, by an industrial reduction operation out of Reedville, Virginia, that catches it at scale and grinds it down into fish meal, fish oil, omega-3 supplements, pet food, and aquaculture feed. The catch leaves the Bay in tank trucks and railcars and on ships. It does not get eaten by people. It gets converted, by an industrial process, into inputs for industries somewhere else.

This is, structurally, an interesting case. The menhaden is the food web. If you remove enough of the menhaden, the rockfish do not have enough to eat in summer when they are heat-stressed and oxygen-starved and trying to put on weight. The ospreys, in some Bay tributaries, have begun to fail to fledge their young, and the suspicion among the people who watch ospreys is that the parent birds are not bringing in enough small oily fish to feed a growing brood. The Bay-wide signal is sometimes described, by people who like the phrase, as *a hollowing*. The bigger species look fine until you ask whether they are reproducing and whether their young are surviving, and then the picture shifts.

The argument over how much menhaden to take out of the Bay is the longest-running fisheries-policy fight in the region. There is a Bay-specific cap, set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission in 2006 and reduced in 2017 to its current level of 51,000 metric tons, that the conservation side regards as a floor not a ceiling and the industry side regards as a hard-fought concession. The relevant scientific committee has, in recent years, been recommending coastwide reductions and Bay-specific precautions; the management board has, in recent years, been adopting reductions smaller than the committee recommended and on schedules slower than the committee proposed. *Just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should,* she has said, *we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that that's warranted.*

The gap is no abstraction to the people working the science. Genny Nesslage, an associate research professor at the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science, has put it plainly to a reporter for WHRO: *We don't know the abundance of menhaden in the bay at any given time relative to the coast.* Rob Latour, a Virginia Institute of Marine Science menhaden researcher, has said it in his own terms in the same piece: *We have no data to really do this in a meaningful way, from a biological perspective.* The Virginia Institute of Marine Science drafted, in 2023, a $3 million proposal for a Bay-specific menhaden study. The Virginia General Assembly declined to fund it.

The sentence points at an asymmetry — between increases adopted at the speed of the appetite for them and reductions adopted at the speed of the resistance to them. The watermen know the asymmetry. The ospreys know it, in the way ospreys know things.

What the cap on menhaden makes visible, more than any other Bay-management debate, is who the system is built to serve. The fishery is not feeding people in any direct sense. It is supplying intermediate inputs to industries whose end customers — the consumer of an omega-3 capsule in Indianapolis, the owner of a salmon farm in Chile, the buyer of a forty-pound bag of dog food in a Phoenix grocery — have no idea that the small oily fish at the base of the Chesapeake food web are being moved through a Reedville plant and into their lives. The value disperses across a continent and across industries. The cost — to the rockfish, to the ospreys, to the watermen who depend on a Bay that depends on the menhaden — concentrates here.

On the last day of 2025, a Virginia resident named Tonya O'Connor filed a 349-page petition with the Virginia Marine Resources Commission asking the agency to declare a temporary moratorium on the Bay's reduction fishery until it produced Bay-specific science. The petition's central finding put the absence in regulatory language: *[VMRC] did not have — and still does not have — a Bay-wide estimate of menhaden biomass, age structure, spatial distribution, recruitment trends, or localized depletion risk.* The legal grounding cited Virginia's public-trust doctrine — *Commonwealth v. City of Newport News*, 158 Va. 521 (1932); *Taylor v. Commonwealth*, 102 Va. 759 (1904); *Bradford v. Nature Conservancy*, 224 Va. 181 (1982). The commission held a hearing on April 21, 2026, and received 1,266 public comments. The decision is pending.

It is a familiar shape. It is the same shape coal had.

---

The Chesapeake watershed covers some 64,000 square miles across six states and the District of Columbia. The water that makes the Bay starts as rain on a corn field in central Pennsylvania, or a soybean field on the Eastern Shore, or a parking lot in Northern Virginia, or a chicken house on the Delmarva, or a wastewater plant in Baltimore, or a suburban lawn in Loudoun County. By the time it reaches the Bay, it is carrying everything those landscapes have given it. Nitrogen from fertilizer. Phosphorus from manure and runoff. Sediment from disturbed ground. Pesticides. Pharmaceuticals. Whatever the upstream economy has not contained.

The science of this has been understood for forty years. The Chesapeake Bay Program, established in 1983, has been measuring nutrient loads, modeling them, allocating them, and asking the upstream parties to reduce them ever since. The program has had partial successes — point-source pollution from wastewater plants and factory discharges has, in many places, been reduced — and significant failures. The recent review by the Program's own science advisory committee, evaluating the forty-year effort, was as plain as such reports are allowed to be: the cleanup is not on track to meet its goals. The agricultural sector has not been required to do what the science said the agricultural sector needed to do. The political will to require it has not formed.

The science was right. The targets were modeled. The schedule was set. The jurisdictions agreed. And the loads, in the places where the loads were largest, did not come down at the rate the agreement said they would, because the political weight of the upstream agricultural economy was greater than the political weight of the downstream fishery economy, and the decisions accumulated, year by year, in the direction of the heavier weight.

I have watched the green of the water change, in the years I have been on it from the deck of a sailboat in summer, in a way that does not feel like life. The watermen know this in a more sustained register. They have been watching it for decades. They know that the runoff hitting the grass beds in Mobjack Bay started as fertilizer on a field they have never seen, owned by a farmer they will never meet, in a state none of their children have lived in. They know that the dead zones — the seasonal volumes of low-oxygen water that form in the deeper parts of the Bay every summer, that suffocate the bottom-dwelling species that cannot move and stress the swimming species that can — are the downstream signature of an agriculture-and-development pattern over which they have never had any voice.

They live at the bottom of a watershed whose accounting does not include them. Everything every upstream actor does has to come past their boats. None of what they do reaches the upstream actors at all. The asymmetry is the geography, and the geography does the work the accounting will not.

There is a man on Fox Hill I am leaving unnamed here. He is alive, still on the water, and the question of how he wants to appear in a book is not yet settled between us. He is a large man, not tall, who has been on the Bay since he was a boy. He is from the same Fox Hill world Biggie was from, and his work and his life have unfolded in the next stretch of the same arc.

He is a crabber and a fisherman, and he has been one all his working life. The water near Fox Hill and Poquoson is the water he has always known. What he has watched, across his decades on it, is that the math for a man like him has changed. The catches have come back thinner. The costs of fuel and gear and the slip have moved in the other direction. The species he came up working are not all of them still running. None of these are observations particular to him; they are the conditions every man still working this water has had to absorb in his own way. What is particular to him is what he has done with the absorption.

He still gets up at two or three in the morning. He still goes out and pulls his crab pots. He runs them the way he ran them when he was younger, except the math at the end of the day is different now than it was then. So he comes back to the dock by mid-morning. He hoses down the boat. He goes to the mechanic shop he owns, on the Peninsula, where his name is on the sign and his crab pots are stacked behind the locked fence in the back lot, behind the cars he is working on for customers. He works the shop until five or six in the evening. What he calls this is between him and the people who know him. What it does, in any case, is the work that keeps the family math balancing and the boat in the slip and the license current and the presence on the water continuing.

He did not quit the water. He could not. He has spent decades building, on his own time and his own dime, the second economy that has allowed him to not quit it. The accounting that made the older men's lives possible — the accounting in which the crab and the boat and the long days were enough — does not work for him. He has filled the gap with the only thing he had left to fill it with: more of himself.

I have not asked him what he would say about all this, and the chapter does not have his answer to put on this page. What I can guess, from watching him and from knowing the men around him, is that if asked over a beer at the end of a long day he would say he is glad to still be on the water and that he is grateful for the shop. The accounting this book is trying to make visible is not, necessarily, the accounting he would put on himself. It is what the surrounding economy has been accepting from him, and from many men like him up and down this Bay, in exchange for a continued supply of the seafood the surrounding economy has not stopped wanting.

---

There is a particular kind of community that grew up on the working waterfronts of the Bay, and a particular kind of decision the people in those communities are now being asked to make.

Tangier Island, in the middle of the Lower Bay, is two square miles of low-elevation marsh and sand that has been continuously inhabited since the 1770s. The accent the people there speak is closer, by some accounts, to a colonial-era English than to anything else still spoken in North America. The economy has been the water — crabs in the warm months, oysters in the cold ones, the rhythms and the vocabulary the families on the island have carried through eight generations. The island is also losing about fifteen feet of shoreline a year to sea-level rise and erosion, and the projections suggest most of it will be uninhabitable within the lifetime of children currently in the school. The mayor and the men on the docks have asked, repeatedly, for the engineering that would buy them another few decades. The asks have, so far, mostly not been answered at the scale that would matter.

James "Ooker" Eskridge, the mayor of Tangier and a working waterman for more than fifty years, has named the stakes to a reporter for the Thomson Reuters Foundation. *We're talking about saving a culture, the people, (a) way of life — the whole package.* Cook Cannon, a former Tangier waterman who has spent the years since at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation, has put it more plainly to a reporter for *Smithsonian Magazine*. *I could cry to think about what's going to happen to Tangier,* he has said, sitting in his island home on a cold January night.

Smith Island, the Maryland counterpart twelve miles to the north, is in roughly the same shape. The picking houses there — where mostly older women have, for generations, picked the meat from soft-shell crabs that the men on the boats brought in — have been thinning. The young people are leaving for the mainland. There is a saying, on Smith, that you get mud between your toes when you spend time there, and that the mud means the island never leaves you. The women who say it are mostly past the age where leaving was an option. The children who hear it have been on the mainland for a decade.

Up the Bay on Tilghman Island in Maryland, the same work continues. Pat Whewell, photographed culling oysters in Broad Creek aboard Lonnie Gowe's boat alongside Chumley Fisher and Chet Schwartz, is one of the named on-water presences in the Maryland working-waterfront record. The picture ran in *Maryland Matters*. The picking houses on Tilghman, like the picking houses on Smith, have been thinning.

Hampton, where I sit, is not Tangier or Smith. Hampton is bigger, more diversified, more capable of absorbing the slow contraction of its working waterfront into the larger economy of the city. The boats are still here. The slips are still rented. The dockmasters still know who is going out tomorrow and who is not. I have watched the marina I keep my own boat at, in the years I have kept her there, slowly tip from a place where the working operators had most of the slips toward a place where the recreational owners do. But the trajectory, on a long enough horizon, is the same trajectory. The number of working watermen on the Lower Bay has been falling for a generation. The picking houses are closing. The boatbuilders who used to build wooden deadrises are mostly gone. The supply chain that supports a working waterfront — the welders, the engine shops, the ice plants, the buyers, the haulers — thins by one capacity at a time. The community that used to come at three in the morning to help with somebody's nets does not, in some places, have the bodies to come anymore. The fabric thins one connection at a time. At some point the thinning becomes a tipping point past which the next year's young person who might have stayed cannot find the work that would have made staying possible. They leave. The waterfront thins by one more capacity. The next year, someone else faces the same thinner version of the choice.

What is being asked of these communities is something the broader economy does not have a name for. It is being asked, in effect, to disperse — to absorb its own transition costs, to relocate its families on its own dime, to retrain its young into industries that are not available locally, to abandon places its ancestors have lived in for two and three centuries, to give up languages and skills and seasonal rhythms that took those generations to develop. It is being asked to do this without compensation, without acknowledgment, and largely without notice from the upstream economy whose runoff and whose climate forcing and whose harvest pressure have, between them, made the staying impossible. The people in these places are not asking for sympathy. They are asking, mostly, to be allowed to keep doing what they know how to do, in the place they know how to do it, for as long as the water and the regulation and the market allow it.

The chapter on McDowell County described this same pattern from the inside of the hollow. The accent is different. The work is different. The pattern is not.

---

I want to say something now about the choice the men on the dock are making, and about what they are telling their sons.

A waterman in his fifties on the Lower Bay, asked what he is telling his children, will often pause before answering. He may glance at the water. He may say something about how the work is hard and the money is uncertain and the regulations are unpredictable. He may tell you that he has not encouraged his son to go into it. He may tell you that he tried to discourage him and the son went anyway, because it is what the son knows and what his grandfather did, and that the choice is the son's. He may tell you that his daughter, now in nursing school in Norfolk, has more options than he had at her age, and that he is glad about that, and that he is also aware there is something in the gain that he does not, in his bones, fully celebrate. The thinning of the family-on-the-water, across the generations, is a kind of liberation that is also a kind of loss.

What the discouragement amounts to, when you add it up across the Lower Bay and the Eastern Shore and the small islands, is a generation-scale withdrawal of the human capital that would have continued the fishery. The fathers are not investing the years of patient teaching into the sons that their fathers invested into them. They are withholding the investment because the return, for the son, is not what the return was, and they are not willing to teach a son into a trade whose floor they cannot promise. The community that took five generations to build the knowledge of where the crabs run in late October is not building a sixth generation that will know it the same way. The knowledge is dying with the men who hold it.

This is its own kind of cost. It does not show up on a stock assessment. It is the slow draining of an inheritance that took centuries to accumulate, into a generation that has been quietly told it cannot be passed on.

The children, for their part, are mostly going. To Norfolk, to Richmond, to the Hampton Roads tech corridor, to the medical centers, to the military bases — wherever a paycheck is more predictable than a season on the water. Most of them carry, somewhere, a respect for what their fathers did that they will not put into words. Some of them come back on weekends and ride out on the boat one more time to remember how it felt. Some of them never come back. The slip stays empty after the father retires. The waterfront becomes one boat thinner. The next father, the next son, hear the news and run their own arithmetic.

Cameron Evans, a Tangier native then studying at Virginia Wesleyan, has put the question to a reporter for the Thomson Reuters Foundation in her own words. *If you can't save an island a mile and a half long, how are you going to save the East Coast?* Andy Harris, who represents Maryland's Eastern Shore in Congress, has framed the same arithmetic from the other side of the aisle. *Our watermen are pretty resilient,* he has told *Maryland Matters*. *They're like our farmers. They have good years and bad years. But if you have a couple bad seasons in a row, then you're in trouble.*

---

There is a paradox that has emerged, in the most recent years, that I want to set down carefully because it is the most precise illustration the Bay has produced of what this book is trying to name.

The Maryland oyster restoration program — five tributary-scale sanctuaries, hundreds of acres of rebuilt reef, tens of millions of oysters per year added to the rebuilt structures — has succeeded, by the metrics it set for itself. The oyster population in Maryland has roughly tripled since 2005. *Large-scale oyster restoration is working,* Colden has said. The reefs are setting, the oysters are spawning, the structures are accumulating. *Our effort to bring oysters back from the brink in Chesapeake Bay is working,* she has said elsewhere — *but continued recovery is not guaranteed.*

Mitch Tarnowski, who leads Maryland's Department of Natural Resources oyster reef surveys, has put the paradox in twelve words. *There are plenty of oysters out there,* he has told a reporter for *Maryland Matters*. *They're just not being harvested.*

In the same year the program was being celebrated as one of the largest successful ecosystem-restoration efforts in North America, the State of Maryland filed for a federal disaster declaration for the oyster fishery. The harvest had collapsed in volume and value. The watermen who depend on the wild harvest were in financial crisis. The market for Chesapeake oysters was being hollowed out by Gulf Coast farm-raised competition and by extreme-weather seasons that had disrupted the harvest windows. *Despite a growing oyster population,* Colden said, in support of the disaster request, *Maryland's watermen are feeling the pinch of extreme weather and market competition.*

Jason Ruth, who owns Harris Seafood Co., a Grasonville, Maryland oyster processor, has put the longer arc on the same paper's record. *While the demand for boutique aquaculture oysters has grown over the last decade,* he has said, *the shucked oyster sector — the larger part of the industry — has seen a 60-year nationwide decline.*

Pause on this for a moment. The population is recovering. The watermen are in disaster. The two facts coexist. They coexist because the recovery is happening in sanctuaries the watermen are not allowed to harvest, and because the harvest from the wild bars outside the sanctuaries is being undercut, in the marketplace, by aquaculture operations that the watermen mostly do not own and by Gulf-Coast operations that did not bear the costs the Bay's recovery is now being asked to carry. The ecosystem is being asked to come back. The watermen are being asked to absorb the cost of its coming back. The consumers eating the recovered oysters in restaurants are paying for an oyster but they are not paying for what the watermen have absorbed in order for the oyster to be on the half-shell in front of them. They cannot pay for it, because the price is not constructed to carry it.

*One of the most important ways people connect with the Chesapeake Bay,* Colden has said, *is through eating seafood and fishing.* Without watermen, *there's certainly a lot fewer eyes on the water and a lot fewer people caring passionately about the Bay.* This is the structural risk: that the very people whose attention has held the Bay accountable for centuries are being squeezed out of the relationship by the same accounting that is, at the species level, beginning to recover what was lost. The recovery and the squeeze are happening at the same time. They are the same process, viewed from two sides of the ledger.

What the Chesapeake is teaching, in this paradox, is that *recovery* without *honest accounting for who paid* leaves the people who paid still paying. The oysters can come back without the watermen coming back. The reef can be rebuilt without the working waterfront being rebuilt.

---

I started this chapter with the harbor at Old Point Comfort because that is the harbor I know. But what is happening here is happening up and down the Bay, and what is happening up and down the Bay is happening in the Gulf of Maine, and in Bristol Bay, and on the coast of West Africa, and in the Mekong Delta. The pattern is the same wherever a fishery has been pushed past the rate at which it could replace itself. The thinning happens in stages — first in the margins, then in the center, then in the structure that produced the catch. By the third stage, the question is no longer whether the harvest will return but whether anything about the place can be saved.

The men who work the Bay are reading a slow version of this story. The boats go out. The catch is what the catch is. The picking houses thin. The young people leave. The fathers, on a Tuesday afternoon in early November, watch a son tie up after a long day and think the thought they are not going to say out loud.

The thought is that what is being lost is not just an income or an industry but a way of knowing the world that took generations of careful attention to develop and cannot be rebuilt by any decision the children's generation will be in a position to make. The thought is that the men whose labor and whose communities produced the value that fed the Atlantic seaboard for three hundred years are being asked, now, to absorb the cost of the value-stream's continuation under conditions they did not design and do not control.

The thought is the same thought the men in McDowell County had been thinking for two generations before the books got written about them.

---

I went back to McDowell County, in my mind, while I was finishing this chapter. The miner who developed black lung was not compensated for the lung. He was paid for the coal. The community that emptied out around him was not compensated for the emptying. The river that runs orange below the closed mine was not compensated for the orange. These are not metaphors. These are accounting facts.

The waterman has been having the same accounting done to him for longer, in a different register. He is paid for the crab. He is not paid for the Bay. He is paid for the oyster that comes up in the tongs. He is not paid for the reef the tongs ground down across a century, or for the grass beds the runoff smothered, or for the menhaden that the reduction fleet is removing from the food web his life depends on, or for the Tangier shoreline that is going under fifteen feet a year, or for the daughter who has gone to nursing school in Norfolk because there was no boat for her to come back to. He is paid for what comes up in the tongs.

What does not come up in the tongs gets left in the water, and what gets left in the water has to be carried by something. By the Bay, while the Bay can carry it. By the watermen, after the Bay can no longer carry it alone. By the working waterfront. By the families. By the daughters who do not come back. By the children who never had a chance to learn the late-October crab runs, because by the time they were old enough to learn, the runs were no longer running on a schedule anyone could teach.

Different resource. Different geography. Same mechanism.

The miner and the waterman would not, if you put them in a room together, recognize each other immediately. They live in different states. They watch different news. They vote, when they vote, for different people, and they have been told different stories about who is to blame for what has happened to them. The waterman has been told that the people who would clean up the runoff are the same people who would shut down his harvest. The miner has been told that the people who would clean up the air are the same people who would close his mine. Each of them has been given an enemy who is, in fact, the other one. The system that has been extracting from them both — that has been pricing the value of what they produce without pricing the cost of producing it — is maintained, in part, by keeping them in those rooms with those stories.

What the Bay and the hollow have in common is not the resource. It is the position. They are both at the bottom of a watershed of value that flows somewhere else.

---

There is, in the world, a place that has handled this question differently.

A small country in northern Europe, smaller in population than the state of Virginia, came into possession of a finite resource at the bottom of one of its territorial seas in the late 1960s and early 1970s. The country's leaders, looking at what had happened to other resource-rich nations in similar circumstances — at the way the rents had dispersed, the way the costs had concentrated, the way the future had been spent in the present and the next generation had been left to absorb the bill — made a different decision than most other countries had made. They built an instrument. They wrote the rules in such a way that the value coming out of the seabed was held in trust for the people of the country and for the generations not yet born. They set up a fund. They imposed limits on what could be drawn. They have, against more pressure than is usually acknowledged, kept the fund intact for more than thirty years.

That country is Norway. It is the closest thing the world currently has to an existence proof — a working demonstration, in a real economy with real political pressures, that the mechanism this book is naming is not inevitable. That the rents from a finite resource can be captured at the source, the costs to the present can be honored without spending the future, and a country can extract from a commons without severing the costs of the extraction onto the people the commons belongs to. It is not perfect. No instrument is. But it does answer the most important question that someone who has read this far is entitled to ask, which is whether the alternative is even possible.

The alternative is possible. They have done it. They have been doing it for more than three decades.

The watermen of the Chesapeake do not know about Norway. Most of them have never heard of the fund. If you described it to them, they would, I think, listen quietly, the way watermen listen to most things, and at the end they might say something measured and short. They might say it is a long way away. They might say their grandfathers fished the Bay before there was a Norway anyone had heard of, and that the Bay is what they have.

But the question the chapter that follows is going to ask is whether the instrument that was built in Oslo could, in some adapted form, be built for the Bay. Whether what was done for the oil could be done for the oyster. Whether the rents that have been quietly leaving the watershed for two centuries might, in some future the framework is trying to make conceivable, begin to stay.

That is the question the next chapter takes up.

For now, the boats are out at the mouth. The light has crossed the channel and reached the top of the Fort. A man two slips over has come in early — he is empty — and is hosing down. A truck door closes, somewhere on the parking lot. The day is beginning. It is the day a waterman has, on the Bay he has, with the accounting the country has agreed to do.

It is not yet the day he could have had.
