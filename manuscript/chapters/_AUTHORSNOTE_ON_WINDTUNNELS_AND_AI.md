## Author's Note: On Wind Tunnels and AI

My grandfather worked at NASA Langley as a model maker — a title he was proud of — and an inventor whose name is in the patent record. He filed under L. E. Winn — family and colleagues called him only Pooh.

Model-making at Langley meant building scale aircraft and spacecraft components — wood, metal, resin, accurate to a thousandth of an inch — so the wind tunnel would tell the truth about full-size designs that did not yet exist. His patented work extended the same discipline beyond the tunnel: a tool bit for machining the resin-impregnated fiberglass laminates that went into spacecraft components, a zero-gravity liquid-waste feed system for crewed missions. The territory fed the lunar program. The discipline was specific. His tools and systems had to work in spaceflight, where reality is unforgiving and the cost of being wrong is borne by people who cannot recover from the error.

I grew up around men who spoke of his work with a particular kind of respect. Not because of rank — he did not have much of that — but because of accuracy. The discipline he practiced, in the model shop and in the tools he invented, was the discipline of building something faithful enough that the world will not lie about it: the model has to behave the way the full-size craft will, the fiberglass part has to machine clean, the waste system has to function in zero-G. Everything downstream — the spacecraft assembly, the mission, the crew — depends on whether the people at the start of the chain measured faithfully.

This book is a model. The framework I have built across ten chapters is an instrument for measuring what extraction actually costs. Like the work my grandfather did, its value depends on whether it was built faithfully enough that the world will not lie when run against it.

I built the model. An AI was the wind tunnel.

Specifically, beginning in March 2026 and continuing through publication, I worked extensively with Claude, an AI system made by Anthropic. The collaboration did three things I want to name clearly, because they are not the same thing.

The AI ran the rigor. It executed the stress-tests an academic peer-review process would have executed for a book published through institutional channels. I do not have a faculty advisor, a writing group, or a network of economists checking the math. I have a repository of rigor-pass documents, ratification commits, and a Working Principles log — all preserved, all readable — that records what was tested, what survived, what was revised, and what was discarded. The AI was the peer review. A reader who wants to verify can read the logs. They are not hidden.

The AI surfaced what a solo author cannot. Cross-cutting observations across vocabulary, structure, and ten chapters of decisions — the kind that emerge from holding the whole project at once. Many of the framework's later choices came from the AI noticing what I had missed and surfacing it for ratification. I made the decisions; the AI surfaced the questions.

The AI helped formalize what I had seen but could not on my own express. The residual commons value integral, the three-method convergence, the existential substitutability gap ranking — formalizations of intuitions I brought, executed at a level of mathematical rigor my training does not include.

What the AI did not do, I did. The originating intuitions are mine: the $100 barrel of oil that struck me as a foreclosure on every future generation; the felt sense, accumulated over twenty years inside institutions that managed costs by passing them to others, that the accounting was missing something fundamental; the recognition that the people I had spent a working life watching get taken advantage of were not exceptions to the system but the unpriced subsidies the system runs on. The vocabulary was workshopped together but seeded by what I saw: *cost severance* as a name for the mechanism I had been watching for years; *residual commons value* as the thing no framework was pricing. The title — *Commons Bonds*, with the dual meaning of obligations owed to the commons and the bonds tying together those who have been extracted from — is mine. The case selection is mine: McDowell County coal, the harbor at Old Point Comfort, the miner and the waterman, the call to honor what the ledger does not carry. The ratification decisions are mine. The lived experience that gave the framework its moral weight is mine. The AI helped me build the model. It did not give me the reason to build it.

The framework I have built also applies to its own production. The training of large language models raises an unsettled question about whether the source-data the model learned from was compensated. The cost-severance logic of this book is exactly the apparatus a serious analysis of that question would deploy. I am not the person to settle it. I am someone who used the tool while the question remained open, and who has tried to leave traceable artifacts a future analyst could read against the framework I am offering them.

I do not have an economics degree. Ostrom did not have one either. Desmond is a sociologist. Raworth drew her doughnut because she was frustrated with how economists measured. The most useful frameworks in social science have often come from people who could see the gap precisely because they were not trained to look past it. Standing in McDowell County, you can see what the textbook does not. The lack of training is what let me see. The collaboration with the wind tunnel is what let me build.

What you are reading is what held.
