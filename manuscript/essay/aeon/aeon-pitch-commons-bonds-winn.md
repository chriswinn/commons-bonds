## Working title

**Pricing the Air**

## Pitch (~290 words)

Imagine an administrator running a closed habitat on Mars. They must ration food, water, energy — even the air they themselves breathe. Now imagine that administrator visiting Earth. They would not see Earth's resources as we do. They would see free air. Free water falling from the sky. Free temperature regulation. Free solar irradiance. Free gravitational stability provided by the Moon. Free magnetic shielding against radiation. Free ultraviolet filtering. All conditions a closed-habitat resident would calculate the cost of.

This essay walks a pricing instrument through that thought experiment. The instrument — built outside the academy across a decade of work in healthcare IT, federal research, and consulting — names six common patterns through which costs get hidden in transactions: geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry, power asymmetry, normative routinization, and abundance masking. On Earth these combine to make extraction systematically under-priced. On Mars all six collapse simultaneously by construction — which is what makes the closed habitat the limiting case that exposes the open one. The framework is not anti-extraction. Asteroid iron, with no community to bear cost: extract freely. Coal in McDowell County in 1960: severely mispriced. A self-imposed commute lease whose hours of life were never computed: same instrument, no external bad actor, structural test the same.

The central claim is that the framework is universal — a pricing instrument first, a critique second. Most discussions of resource pricing are political. This one is structural. If the apparatus is right, the price tag on Earth's ambient abundance is many multiples of what we've been paying. The math, finally done, is what a thoughtful reader on any of these worlds — including this one — would recognize as obviously correct.

## Bio

Chris Winn is a nursing student in Hampton, Virginia, and a former healthcare IT executive. He built the framework this essay applies across a decade of consulting work that took him through fifty-eight countries — until his son's birth and a family caregiving crisis made him reconsider what the work was actually costing. He is not an economist. The framework grew from inside the systems it engages, not above them.
