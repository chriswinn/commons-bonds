## Pitch — Version B (~322 words after Option-2 airlock-question rewrite; tightening continues)

Imagine landing at a closed habitat — Mars, the Moon, an orbital station, the analog at Devon Island or HI-SEAS. At the airlock, you are asked how you will be paying for your air. You understand viscerally, then and there, what you have been living with all your life — someone else has been paying.

Then you come home. The bill doesn't arrive. The air is free again. The water is free. Light, heat, the cubic meter you occupy — all of it suddenly costless. You laugh, the first morning back, at the absurdity of it. Then you discover, slowly, that the costs are still being paid. Just not by you.

This essay walks a pricing framework through what you discover. The framework — built outside the academy across a decade of work in healthcare IT, federal research, and consulting — names common patterns through which costs get hidden in transactions, including geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry, power asymmetry, normative routinization, and abundance masking. On Earth these patterns thicken the mask of abundance — routing the costs that stopped at the airlock to communities downstream of every extraction site, to atmospheres a generation has not yet been born to breathe, to ecosystems that cannot send a bill. The framework is not anti-extraction. Asteroid iron, with no community to bear the cost: extract freely. Coal in McDowell County in 1960: severely mispriced. A self-imposed commute lease whose hours of life were never computed: same framework, no external bad actor, structural test the same.

The central claim is that the framework is universal — a pricing framework first, a critique second. Most discussions of resource pricing are political. This one is structural. If the framework is right, the price tag on what stopped at the airlock is many multiples of what we have been paying as a species. The math, finally done, is what a thoughtful reader on any of these worlds — including this one — would recognize as honest accounting.

## Composition notes — Version B specific (for author review; remove before submission)

**Lead choice — visceral consumer experience ("you" are billed for breathing):** central rhetorical engine. The reader stays in their own perspective rather than being asked to imagine someone else's. The "your body is the most expensive thing you have ever owned" beat lands physically rather than conceptually. Strongest single move in this version.

**Come-home pivot mechanism:** "the bills stop" is the perspective-reversal moment, internalized via lived experience rather than asserted via thought experiment. The "where do they go on Earth?" question pulls the reader into the framework's central insight before the framework is named.

**Title-phrase threading:** "the mask of abundance" appears once in para 2 ("On Earth these patterns thicken the mask of abundance"). Threads back to title; bills-stopping-at-airlock metaphor in para 1 does the unmasking work without naming it.

**"Stopped at the airlock" recurring image:** appears in para 2 and para 3 as a load-bearing concrete anchor for "what costs the framework prices." More memorable than abstract "Earth's ambient abundance" of Version A.

**Sci-fi register risk:** "closed habitat" is sci-fi-coded but less than Mars-specific. Version B opens with a list of locations (Mars / Moon / orbital station) which signals the variant is not strictly Mars-anchored. Slightly broader genre signal than A; slightly less novel angle.

**Lost from Version A:** the cumulative "Free X" list of seven Earth conditions (gravitational stability from the Moon / magnetic shielding / UV filtering / etc.). Version B replaces that list with the inverse — what you'd be billed for in a closed habitat — which is shorter but does different rhetorical work. The "free things" cumulative shock from A is genuinely lost; B substitutes "your body is the most expensive thing you have ever owned" as the equivalent visceral move.

**Word count:** ~300 words (right at self-imposed convention; Version A is ~283 words). Compression cost of the visceral move; B's lead requires more setup than A's perspective-reversal.

## Comparison to Version A — head-to-head

### Version A strengths
- More distinctive opening (Mars admin visiting Earth is unusual; reader visiting Mars is standard sci-fi premise)
- Tighter (~283 vs ~300 words)
- "Free X" cumulative-shock list is rhetorically powerful
- Mars admin character mirrors the framework (a pricer/calculator)

### Version B strengths
- Visceral consumer experience (reader stays in own perspective)
- "Your body is the most expensive thing you have ever owned" lands physically
- Come-home moment does perspective-reversal viscerally rather than conceptually
- Reader experiences abundance masking in their own body before the framework names it

### Equal between versions
- Title: *The Mask of Abundance* (frame-agnostic)
- Six-mechanism enumeration (open framing)
- Three case-walks (asteroid iron / McDowell / commute)
- Universalizability move + "honest accounting" close
- Cultural-engagement commitment (Dunbar / Du Bois / Ellison / Fanon)
- Bio + AI disclosure
