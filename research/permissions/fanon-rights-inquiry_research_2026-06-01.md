### §1.1 Grove Atlantic, Inc. — English-translation rights (PRIMARY)

**Pathway summary.** Grove Press published the Constance Farrington English translation of *Les
Damnés de la terre* in 1963 under the title *The Wretched of the Earth*. Grove Press merged with
The Atlantic Monthly Press in 1993 to form Grove/Atlantic, Inc. (publicly branded as Grove
Atlantic). Grove Atlantic commissioned the Richard Philcox retranslation in 2004 — published
with new preface by Homi K. Bhabha + foreword by Jean-Paul Sartre (from the 1961 original).

For the Noema essay's English-language publication context, Grove Atlantic is the operational
rights-holder for direct quotation of EITHER the Farrington or the Philcox text. This is the
primary letter to send.

**Standard Grove Atlantic permissions process (training-set general knowledge):**

- Brief quotation requests (<300 words) for academic-essay use historically processed via
  email or web form; turnaround typically 2-8 weeks.
- Fees scaled to use (non-commercial academic essay ≪ commercial print-book first edition);
  some quotations granted gratis for clearly fair-use-adjacent academic uses; others charged
  per-word or flat-fee.
- Combined essay + book permission COMMON to request; sometimes granted in one transaction,
  sometimes split into essay-now + book-on-contract-signing.
- For Fanon specifically: Grove Atlantic has been historically generous with brief academic-
  essay quotation; *The Wretched of the Earth* is one of their evergreen backlist titles with
  steady postcolonial-studies citation volume.

**Fair-use threshold per Grove Atlantic stated practice (training-set general knowledge,
NOT legal advice):** [VERIFY against current Grove Atlantic permissions FAQ if published.]
The general US-academic-publishing practice is that quotations of ≤300 words from a substantial
prose work for academic-register critical commentary fall within fair use under Section 107 of
the US Copyright Act (the four-factor test). Grove Atlantic has historically respected this
threshold without requiring formal permission. The proposed Noema-essay quotation is ~25-40
words, well within any plausible fair-use threshold. **The author may proceed with brief
quotation under fair use without permission if they wish; the permissions inquiry is the
conservative discipline that eliminates the risk of any rights-holder challenge.**

### §1.2 Éditions La Découverte — Original French publication rights (PARALLEL)

**Pathway summary.** *Les Damnés de la terre* was first published in 1961 by Éditions François
Maspero in Paris, with the foreword by Jean-Paul Sartre. Maspero ceased publishing in 1982; the
catalogue was acquired by Éditions La Découverte, founded in 1983 by editors leaving the Maspero
firm [VERIFY: confirm specific catalogue-transfer date and corporate continuity]. La Découverte
has kept Fanon's titles in active print, both in their main catalogue and in the Poche
small-format paperback series.

For an English-language essay quoting from an English translation, La Découverte is NOT
strictly the operational rights-holder — Grove Atlantic handles the translation. But under
French droit d'auteur, La Découverte holds the underlying-work rights. Best-practice
permissions discipline runs both letters in parallel because:

1. Grove Atlantic may route the request back to La Découverte for underlying-work clearance.
2. If the essay is later translated for La Découverte's own region or for a non-Anglophone
   reprint, La Découverte's clearance becomes the operative gate.
3. La Découverte's clearance also positions the downstream book project (Commons Bonds) for
   eventual French-language rights inquiries with relationship pre-established.

**Copyright duration.** French droit d'auteur runs life-plus-70-years. Fanon died 1961-12-06.
French rights to *Les Damnés de la terre* expire 2031-12-31 (calendar-year-after-life-plus-70
convention) [VERIFY: confirm no special wartime-extension provisions apply that would push the
term further — France has historically applied wartime extensions to works affected by WW1 + WW2,
though *Les Damnés de la terre* was published 1961 and would not normally qualify].
