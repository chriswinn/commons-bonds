## Why this case matters

The feudalism case is not primarily a contemporary-policy illustration (the feudal system is long dismantled in most of the world). Its unique analytical contributions are:

1. **The mechanism predates capitalism.** The extraction / severance pattern was operating at scale in medieval European agrarian economies. Capitalism did not invent cost severance; it delivered it more efficiently.
2. **The cost-bearer-blaming narrative is ancient.** The feudal lord framed extraction as *protection* (from competing lords, from "the lawless"), as *divine order* (the Great Chain of Being; the serf's position was God-ordained), and as *natural hierarchy* (people were born to their station, and disruption was disorder). Contemporary "culture of poverty" narratives about Appalachia, the Rust Belt, the Niger Delta — these are the same rhetorical move with updated vocabulary.
3. **The serf could not exit.** Physical coercion + legal constraint + cultural absence of alternatives made exit functionally impossible. This is Autonomy-dimension cost-severance (consent-normalization shield) at historical scale: "you chose to stay" is not meaningful consent when exit is structurally foreclosed.

## Strategic framing value

Including feudalism in the book's cases (even briefly) does several important things:

- **Forecloses the "this is a socialist critique" dismissal.** Feudal extraction was pre-capitalist. Soviet industrial planning (also mentioned in the broader-than-capitalism framing from file 4) was post-capitalist. Both severed cost the same way. The critique transcends capitalism-vs-socialism.
- **Shows the mechanism as structural, not ideological.** "Cost severance is what allocation-architectures do under specific shielding conditions" — feudalism demonstrates this in an economic system whose ideology was explicitly non-capitalist and anti-market.
- **Connects historical narrative-blaming to contemporary narrative-blaming.** Vance's "Appalachian culture" framing has a thousand-year pedigree. Naming this pedigree disarms the framing by showing it as a reusable rhetorical template rather than a specific insight about Appalachia.

## Broader-than-capitalism insight (paired source passage)

From the same April 19 session notes source (file 4):

> The mechanism is broader than capitalism. It predates capitalism. It will likely survive capitalism. Feudal lords did it. Colonial empires did it. Soviet industrial planners did it. A nonprofit can do it. A government agency can do it.
>
> The system is almost incidental — the mechanism is what matters. Capitalism is the current and most efficient delivery vehicle, not the root cause.

This is load-bearing for the book's cross-spectrum positioning. Feudalism is the case that anchors the "predates capitalism" claim. Colonial empires and Soviet planning anchor the "transcends capitalism" claim.
