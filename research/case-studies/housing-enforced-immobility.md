## The case in brief

The bootstrap narrative — "if the conditions in your community are bad, move" — requires geographic mobility. Geographic mobility requires a savings cushion sufficient to absorb relocation costs and a period without income. Neither is available to a cost-burdened household. **Housing costs have become the structural mechanism that forecloses the mobility the bootstrap narrative assumes is available.**

**The headline numbers:**
- **49% of all renters are cost-burdened** (30%+ of income on housing) — record high for the 4th consecutive year (Harvard JCHS, 2024)
- **Renters' median housing costs rose 38%** from 2019–2024; incomes rose 28% (10-point compression)
- **90%+ of Americans live in counties** where housing costs outpaced income growth from 2000–2020 (US Treasury, 2021)

**The structural argument:** when housing consumes 30–50% of income, there is mathematically no path to savings accumulation, no capacity to absorb a medical emergency, and no realistic possibility of financing relocation to a region with stronger economic opportunity. The bootstrap narrative's escape hatch — move where the work is — is closed. The landlord class captures the surplus that wages cannot absorb, and the captured surplus is precisely what would have funded mobility.

## Framework application

### Cost severance operating as spatial-immobility mechanism

The Appalachian coal case (`appalachian-coal.md`) handled the "miners chose that work" counterargument by noting that constrained choices are still choices but not the same thing as free choices. This case operationalizes that abstract claim with specific macro-data:

**At the household level:** housing as 30–50% of income means relocation costs (first-last-deposit = typically 2–3 months rent at destination + moving costs + period-without-income during job transition) cannot be funded from current income streams. The savings cushion that would finance relocation does not exist because housing consumes the discretionary surplus that would have produced it.

**At the county level:** 90%+ of US counties have housing outpacing income over 20 years. This means there is essentially no destination county where mobility is cheaper than the origin — the bootstrap narrative's implicit assumption that struggling workers can move to a better market is false at macro scale. The markets have all moved in the same direction.

**At the system level:** the landlord class captures the surplus that wages cannot absorb. This is cost severance operating on the labor side of the economy: value produced by workers flows through wages into rent, and the rent captures what would have been savings, and the savings is what would have produced mobility. The value is captured by housing-asset owners. The cost is the foreclosure of the escape hatch.

### The connection to Chetty mobility data

Raj Chetty's intergenerational mobility research documents that children's economic outcomes are highly correlated with their parents' — and the correlation varies significantly by geography. High-mobility counties exist; they are generally expensive. Low-mobility counties are cheaper but produce the outcomes they produce. A family in a low-mobility county cannot easily relocate to a high-mobility county because the high-mobility county's housing costs exceed the low-mobility county's income potential.

This is the Chetty data reading onto the Commons Bonds framework: intergenerational mobility is not only about "effort" or "talent" or "cultural values" — it is structurally constrained by housing-market dynamics that foreclose the mobility that would be required to access higher-opportunity environments.

### The racial layer

Housing-market history in the US — redlining (1934+), exclusionary zoning, restrictive covenants, blockbusting, subprime-lending concentration during the 2008 pre-crisis years — produces housing-cost distributions that fall disproportionately on Black and Hispanic households. The 2008 foreclosure wave reset homeownership rates for these households specifically, and the post-2008 rental-cost surge landed disproportionately on them as well. The enforced-immobility case has a racial concentration signature that the sociology-paper source notes implicitly but does not develop. The existing research base (Desmond, Darity, Hamilton) covers this extensively and would inform chapter integration.

### Refutation of the "miners chose that work" counter

Ch 2 currently handles this counter at two levels: (1) the structural argument (company-town ownership of land/stores/houses made leaving costly); (2) the informational argument (miners were not told what black lung would do to them). This case adds a third level:

**(3) The macro-housing argument:** even when miners conceptually *could* have left, the housing costs at destination (any destination that offered better work) exceeded the savings capacity of miners' wages. The coal industry's maintenance of wage levels that permitted survival-but-not-savings is the same mechanism as the contemporary landlord class's capture of surplus. Different industries, same structural function — value extraction that keeps the extraction's targets economically immobile.

The "miners chose" counterargument is vulnerable to this third level because it assumes geographic mobility was a real alternative. It wasn't. Not for the miner-era population in their time, and not for the contemporary cost-burdened-renter population now. The structural immobility is a feature of how American labor markets have been organized, not a temporary condition.

## Expansion notes

**1. Connection to 2008 foreclosure wave.** The 10M foreclosures 2008–2012 document the acute version of this case's chronic pattern. People who had bought housing (often via predatory subprime loans that the MBS machinery required to generate sufficient mortgage volume) lost the housing in the crisis. The foreclosure removed the household from the neighborhood, destroyed the household's accumulated housing-equity wealth, and reset the household's rental-market exposure. The post-foreclosure rental surge in many markets was partly driven by private-equity firms buying foreclosed stock at auction and converting to rentals at premium prices. Integration opportunity: the 2008 case and this enforced-immobility case form a "housing as commons" bookend pair — how the commons was captured (2008 narrative), how the captured commons now enforces immobility (this case).

**2. Zoning and supply-constraint arguments.** The YIMBY movement's argument — that zoning constraints on housing supply are the driver of housing-cost acceleration — is the market-side counter-argument to the landlord-capture frame this case presents. Worth engaging: both explanations have partial truth (supply constraint + rent-seeking capture), and they produce different policy implications (liberalize zoning vs. constrain rent capture vs. fund public housing). Integration opportunity: engage the YIMBY frame at chapter scale rather than dismissing it, to preserve cross-spectrum credibility.

**3. Mondragon / cooperative housing as alternative.** Per sociology-paper source todo list: "Alaska Permanent Fund, municipal broadband cooperatives, Mondragon-model worker cooperatives in former extraction zones" — these are the reachable-horizon alternatives suggested. Mondragon specifically has housing-cooperative components. Vienna's social housing model (a majority of residents in municipal housing, not as stigma but as default) is another. Integration opportunity: the reachable horizon this case needs is a functional housing-as-commons model that already exists somewhere and can be pointed at.

**4. Eviction data (Desmond's Evicted territory).** Matthew Desmond's *Evicted* (2016) is the deep-research complement to this case. Desmond's dataset (Milwaukee 2008–2011) documents the specific mechanism by which eviction perpetuates rather than resolves housing-cost exposure for affected households. Integration opportunity: Desmond-style methodological grounding — the framework-level argument made concrete in named human stories. Ch 2's `[INTERVIEW NEEDED]` slots could be filled with eviction-case interviews if field access is available.

**5. Rural housing and extractive-community-specific dynamics.** McDowell County and similar Appalachian counties have a different housing pattern than urban cost-burdened-renter dynamics — abandoned housing stock from the population drain, property values collapsed to near-zero, but no jobs to keep residents in the housing that exists. This is housing as commons-after-extraction: the commons was the community that gave the housing value; the community collapsed; the housing remained as physical structures without their economic context. Different from the urban pattern, but same cost-severance mechanism operating in inverse: the value was severed from the housing by extraction, rather than the housing severing value from its residents.

**6. Climate-driven housing risk adding to the pattern.** Insurance markets in Florida, coastal Louisiana, and wildfire-prone California counties are showing the 2008-style pattern in slow motion: insurance unavailability / extreme rate increases / mortgage unavailability / property value collapse / resident inability to leave or stay. This is the climate-foreclosure extension of the enforced-immobility case. Integration opportunity: if Ch 9 (*The Renewable Imperative*) picks up climate-policy-economy content, this is a natural home for the enforced-immobility mechanism operating under climate pressure.
