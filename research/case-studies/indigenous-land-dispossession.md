## Role in source (cross-domain test framing)

From the RCV formal-model framework's validation strategy:

> **Cross-domain test — Indigenous land dispossession:** Land is non-extractable in the oil sense but was permanently transferred. The resource isn't consumed — it's enclosed. Tests whether the model is truly about permanent foreclosure or only about physical depletion. If it can't accommodate enclosure, it's too narrow. If it can, it's more general than initially scoped.

Indigenous land dispossession serves a specific analytical function: it tests whether the RCV formula handles *enclosure-without-consumption* as a form of permanent foreclosure. If the formula's option-value term (Method 3) requires physical depletion, it's too narrow. If it generalizes to enclosure (legal-transfer-of-ownership without physical consumption of the resource), the framework is more general than initially scoped.

## Why this case is structurally important to the framework

Indigenous land dispossession is not primarily a validation case — it's a **canonical case for multiple framework claims simultaneously:**

- **Permanent foreclosure without physical depletion** — the land still exists; it's just been legally enclosed. This is the generalization test the source describes.
- **Tier 5 (Community Transition Reserve) at historical scale** — entire peoples displaced from homelands; no transition reserve existed or was funded; communities absorbed total transition cost across generations.
- **Tier 7 (Knowledge and Culture Cost) at its most severe** — language, ceremonial knowledge, land-management practice, traditional ecological knowledge all lost when carrying communities fell below transmission threshold. Threshold-dynamic collapse is visible across centuries.
- **Kindred dimension cost-severance at maximum intensity** — specific identity-deep attachment to specific land (not substitutable); severed definitively; chosen-family / fictive-kin networks in displaced communities preserved some bonds but not the land-specific ones.
- **Political Capture Cost foundational** — the Doctrine of Discovery, terra nullius, the legal architecture justifying dispossession was itself constructed by the dispossessing parties; indigenous legal traditions had no standing in the imposed legal system.
- **Epistemic dimension cost** — indigenous epistemologies (oral history, traditional ecological knowledge, kinship-mediated authority) were delegitimized wholesale; what counted as "evidence" was defined by the dispossessing system.

## Strategic framing considerations

- **This case is not optional for Book 1.** Omitting it would be a Book-One-level gap; the framework's claims about "cost severance" fail as a universal framework if they don't account for the foundational dispossession of indigenous peoples.
- **Treatment requires care.** The academic and political discourse around indigenous land dispossession is well-developed; framework integration must engage existing scholarship rather than appear to be reinventing or neutralizing it. Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz (*An Indigenous Peoples' History of the United States*), Patrick Wolfe ("Settler Colonialism and the Elimination of the Native"), Glen Coulthard (*Red Skin, White Masks*), Leanne Betasamosake Simpson, Robin Wall Kimmerer are canonical starting points.
- **Scale challenge.** Indigenous land dispossession is not a single case but a global-scale historical pattern across the Americas, Australia, New Zealand, parts of Africa and Asia. The framework can't treat it as one case; it has to either pick exemplars (specific treaty violations, specific tribal experiences) or address the category at a more abstract level with exemplar illustrations.
