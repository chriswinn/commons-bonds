## §1. The case in brief

In 2010, the Electric Power Board of Chattanooga (EPB), a municipally-owned electric utility, launched EPB Fiber Optics — a municipal fiber-optic broadband network. The network offered gigabit internet service at prices substantially below private-sector comparable offerings, ran at operational profitability, and reached full city-wide coverage. As of 2024, EPB Fiber Optics serves approximately 125,000 customers, offers up to 25-gigabit service (well beyond what most private providers make available), and has been profitable since roughly 2015.

The case is important for Book 1's reachable-horizon cluster because it demonstrates the collective-capture architecture at **municipal scale**, for an **infrastructure commons** (not a resource commons), **over opposition from private incumbents and state-level political pressure** — and has survived to flourish. If the Alaska case closes the "it could never work here" objection at state scale on resource rents, the Chattanooga case closes it at municipal scale on infrastructure.

## §2. Historical anchor

EPB's municipal status dates to 1935. In the 2000s, EPB began planning a fiber-optic smart grid — primarily for electricity-grid modernization, not internet service. The fiber infrastructure, once built, could carry broadband service. Chattanooga's leadership recognized this as an opportunity: a municipal broadband service could fund smart-grid costs, reduce digital-divide issues in the city, and bring competitive pressure to the regional broadband market (then dominated by Comcast and AT&T).

EPB applied for federal stimulus funding in 2009 and received $111M in American Recovery and Reinvestment Act grants. Combined with bonded municipal financing, the build-out began. Gigabit service became available city-wide by 2010 — three years before Google Fiber's first city deployment (Kansas City, 2012), and seven years before major private providers began offering gigabit residential service in most markets.

The private-sector response was hostile. Comcast filed litigation. The Tennessee state legislature passed laws restricting EPB's expansion beyond its electric-service territory (a geographic constraint Comcast does not face). The FCC, under Chairman Tom Wheeler in 2015, attempted to preempt state restrictions on municipal broadband — but was overruled by the Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals in 2016 on federalism grounds.

Chattanooga's broadband service continued to expand within its permitted territory and flourish financially. The legal restrictions affected expansion scope but did not erode the existing service.

## §3. Framework application

### 3.1 What Chattanooga solved

- **Municipal-scale collective capture of infrastructure commons.** The fiber network is publicly-owned; revenues support municipal services + smart-grid operations + fund expansion. Revenue that private broadband providers would have captured as rent now funds city services.
- **Cross-subsidization with electric-grid modernization.** The fiber network was built for electric-grid smart-metering and then leveraged for broadband service. The infrastructure serves two commons functions from one investment.
- **Digital-divide reduction in the service area.** Low-income households receive subsidized access; gigabit service at $58/month is a fraction of major private-sector pricing in comparable markets.
- **Survival against private-sector litigation and state-legislative pressure.** Institutional-durability evidence for municipal-commons against concentrated private opposition.

### 3.2 What Chattanooga does not solve

- **Expansion blocked at state level.** TN legislation prevents EPB from extending service beyond its electric territory, even to neighboring communities that want the service. The private-incumbent-protection architecture is maintained at state scale.
- **Federal preemption case failed.** The FCC could not, under current federalism doctrine, override state restrictions on municipal broadband. Other states (22 states had similar restrictions as of 2016) can continue to constrain municipal broadband deployment.
- **Not scalable to private-sector-equivalent deployment.** Chattanooga's success does not automatically translate to every municipality. Municipal broadband works best where a municipal electric utility exists (providing fiber infrastructure as a byproduct of grid modernization) and where local political will can survive the private-incumbent response.
- **Does not price permanent foreclosure or externality tail.** Like Alaska, Chattanooga demonstrates partial-architecture for collective capture. It does not implement the Commons Bonds framework's full proposal.

### 3.3 What it proves for Ch 9

**Collective-capture architectures operate successfully at municipal scale in the US, in red-state territory (Tennessee), over organized private-sector opposition.** The "political economy is too hostile for this kind of thing in America" objection has specific US-domestic counter-evidence in Chattanooga's case. The case is smaller-scale than Alaska but equally clear as a reachable-horizon demonstration.
