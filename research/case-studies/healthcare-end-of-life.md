## The case in brief

The American healthcare system has, over the past two decades, evolved a set of externalization patterns that route cost away from the system's beneficiaries and onto individuals, families, and the un-compensated caregiving labor of household members. Three visible signatures document the mechanism:

1. **The GoFundMe crowdfunding economy** — over one-third of all GoFundMe campaigns are for medical expenses; only 12% of medical campaigns reach their funding goal. The replacement for a social safety net has itself become unreliable.

2. **The racial-disparity layer within crowdfunding** — campaigns organized by individuals with distinctively African American or Hispanic surnames raise significantly less and are less likely to meet their goals than those with names perceived as white, even after controlling for location and time period.

3. **End-of-life extraction** (Butler/pacemaker case) — the Medicare reimbursement structure pays $54 for a long conversation about treatment alternatives and $461 (plus ~$12,000 hospital flat fee) for pacemaker implantation. The liability architecture makes the procedure defensive; declining creates malpractice exposure. Both incentives point toward the procedure. Cost severance operating on the dying body.

**The framework implication:** the healthcare-commons case is cost severance operating on individual medical and end-of-life vulnerability. Value captured by providers, hospitals, and device manufacturers. Cost externalized onto patients, families, unpaid caregivers (the 80-hour-week elderly-wife pattern the Butler case documents), and the public-health systems that absorb the downstream disability and distress.

## The GoFundMe economy as structural indicator

- Over one-third of all GoFundMe campaigns are for medical expenses
- Between 2016 and 2020: 438,000 US medical campaigns raised more than $2 billion from 21.7 million donations
- Only 12% of campaigns met their fundraising goals
- 16% received no donations at all
- Annual US medical campaigns reached ~200,000 in 2020 — **25 times the number in 2011**
- GoFundMe's own CEO acknowledged the platform was never designed to function as healthcare financing but has become the default

The growth curve is the load-bearing fact. A 25× increase in medical crowdfunding over a decade indicates that this is not a boutique phenomenon. It is infrastructure. The crowdfunding platform has become the de-facto safety net for American households facing medical expenses the formal healthcare system does not cover — and it functions as a safety net with a 12% success rate.

Call this what it is: the American healthcare system's cost-severance architecture outsources a fraction of its deficit to charitable peer-to-peer giving, which itself has been measured and found to fail for 88% of those who use it.

## The racial-disparity layer

Campaigns organized by individuals with distinctively African American or Hispanic surnames raise significantly less and are less likely to meet their goals than those with names perceived as white, even after controlling for location and time period (Ly & Soman, 2020, *Journal of Marketing Research*).

**The replacement for a social safety net replicates racial inequality in the act of charity.**

This is cost severance operating through a second layer: the system that should have absorbed the medical cost did not; the charitable backup that emerged to catch what the system dropped does not catch equally by race. The severed cost doesn't land evenly — it concentrates on populations whose informal-network fundraising capacity is already constrained by prior wealth-and-network disparities. This is the same racial-wealth-gap pattern operating in extraction-community geography, now operating in healthcare-cost distribution.

**The framework implication:** the case demonstrates that cost severance is not only about who captures value — it is also about which populations bear concentrated residual cost when the formal systems fail. Any rebuild of the accountability architecture that does not address the concentration of residual cost on specific populations will reproduce the disparity in different form.

## The end-of-life extraction case (Butler/pacemaker)

This is the individual-scale illustration the sociology-paper source identifies as Tier 1 material. Reconstruction from the source, citing Katy Butler's 2010 reporting on her father's care:

**The reimbursement architecture:**
- Medicare reimbursement for a long office visit to discuss alternatives: **$54**
- Surgeon fee for pacemaker implant: **$461**
- Hospital flat fee for implant: **~$12,000** (nearly half to the device manufacturer)

**The liability architecture:** performing the procedure protects from malpractice exposure; declining the procedure creates malpractice exposure. Both incentives point toward the procedure.

**The outcome:** Butler's father spent 5+ years in profound cognitive decline with the pacemaker still functioning. His 80-year-old wife provided 80-hour weeks of unpaid caregiving — labor for which no compensation exists and no market instrument captures. The device manufacturer received approximately $6,000. The conversation that might have prevented the procedure was reimbursed at $54.

From Butler's own account:

> My parents had lost control of their lives to a set of perverse financial incentives — for cardiologists, hospitals, and especially the manufacturers of advanced medical devices — skewed to promote maximum treatment.

**The structural argument:** The extraction does not stop at the grave. The system is organized around reaching into it. This is cost severance operating on the dying body: profit captured by the cardiologist, hospital, and device manufacturer; cost externalized onto the family as unpaid caregiving labor that appears on no ledger, onto the patient as prolonged decline, and onto the 80-year-old spouse as 80-hour caregiving weeks.

**The Quill distinction (1991, *NEJM*).** Timothy Quill's 1991 article articulates the distinction the financial system suppresses: the difference between prolonging *life* and prolonging *dying*. The reason this conversation remains structurally marginalized is not ethical — polling shows majority public support for end-of-life autonomy. The reason is financial: discussing withdrawal of treatment means discussing not billing for something the system is organized to bill for.

This is the medical-commons equivalent of the coal-dust-regulation resistance documented in the Appalachian coal case. The industry knows. The regulatory architecture permits the knowledge to not translate into practice. The extraction continues.

## Framework application

### Three tiers visible in the case set

**Tier 1 (Lifetime Survival)** — visible in the end-of-life extraction case. Butler's father's 5+ years of cognitive decline under an unnecessary pacemaker are Tier 1 at the final phase of a lifetime rather than the extractive phase.

**Tier 3 (Dynastic Labor) at the caregiver scale** — Butler's mother's 80-hour caregiving weeks are Tier 3 operating in reverse-direction across a generation. The pattern the Appalachian coal case documented at the workforce-formation level (children paying the dynastic cost of parents' extraction-community collapse) operates here in the opposite direction: an elderly spouse absorbing the care cost of a medical decision that served the providers and the device manufacturer.

**Tier 5 (Community Transition Reserve)** — operating via the absence of formal elder care infrastructure. Butler's mother is the community-transition reserve by default, because no formal alternative exists for a family of modest means. The unpaid caregiving that the American system depends on is a trillion-dollar annual externality; when you convert unpaid hours into wage-equivalent values, the scale exceeds the entire formal healthcare budget of several OECD nations.

**Tier 8 (Political Capture)** operating at multiple layers — the reimbursement-code structure (CPT codes, RVU weighting) is a regulatory artifact that systematically underprices deliberation and overprices procedures; the malpractice-exposure architecture is a civil-litigation artifact that reinforces the procedure bias; the device-manufacturer lobbying presence in Medicare coverage decisions is a visible political-capture instance.

### The shield typology operating

**Information asymmetry** (patient and family do not have evaluated information about how the reimbursement architecture shapes the physician's recommendation).

**Temporal distance** (the full consequence of the procedure decision unfolds over years, long past the moment of decision when consent is given).

**Consent-normalization** (v1.2.1 rigor-protocol shield type) — the surrounding medical, legal, and insurance framework treats the consent-to-procedure as closing the accounting, even when the consent was given under acute conditions with structurally-incomplete information.

**Economic precarity** (decline-of-procedure exposes families to different medical-bankruptcy risks than acceptance; the economic structure shapes the consent).

### The accountability gap: GoFundMe as measurement

The GoFundMe figures are the most honest quantitative measure currently available of the accountability gap in American healthcare. $2B raised from 21.7M donations over 2016–2020 represents roughly $400M/year in peer-to-peer medical-cost absorption. That is the order-of-magnitude of the informal backup. The formal healthcare system's annual cost-shifting to patients — via high-deductible plans, surprise billing, out-of-network exposure, pharmaceutical cost-sharing — exceeds that by at least an order of magnitude.

The 12% success rate means ~88% of medical crowdfunding campaigns fail. The cost that campaign was supposed to absorb does not disappear. It lands somewhere — on credit-card debt, on unpaid bills transferred to collections, on medical-debt bankruptcy filings (still the single largest cause of US personal bankruptcy), on foregone-care decisions that later produce more expensive acute-care events.

## Counterargument — "people choose their healthcare spending"

The individual-choice framing is the healthcare version of the "miners chose that work" counterargument in the Appalachian coal case. It has real content: patients do have agency in accepting or declining procedures; families do make choices about caregiving arrangements.

But the free-choice frame has the same two structural problems:

**First — constrained choice.** The choice to decline a pacemaker or similar procedure, in the face of acute symptoms, with a physician recommending it, with family members anxious about the alternative, and with the liability architecture that structures the provider's recommendation — is not the unconstrained choice that the framing assumes. Constrained choices are still choices, but they are not the same thing as free choices, and the reimbursement structure that fairly compensates a free choice would be different from the reimbursement structure that fairly compensates a constrained one.

**Second — information.** Patients and families do not have evaluated information about how the reimbursement architecture shapes the physician's recommendation. The physician may not have evaluated information about it either, in the sense that the incentives are embedded in routine practice rather than consciously weighed. This is consent-without-full-information, which is partial consent, which is what the framework identifies as the information-shielding condition.

## Expansion notes

**1. The three-case arrangement is deliberate.** The sociology-paper source §2 presents the GoFundMe case, the racial-disparity case, and the Butler case as a tight structural unit: the formal system fails, the informal backup fails unevenly, and the individual-scale extraction mechanism that produces the failures is documented in a specific reported case. The three sub-cases support each other — removing any one weakens the argument. Integration into chapter drafts should preserve the arrangement.

**2. Medical bankruptcy data.** Medical debt remains the single largest cause of US personal bankruptcy, at rates that far exceed those in other developed economies. Specific data sources: Himmelstein et al. studies (various years), American Journal of Public Health. Integration opportunity: quantify the bankruptcy-as-accountability-escape pattern at household scale — the same mechanism the Libby, Purdue, and 2008 cases show at corporate scale.

**3. Surprise billing and out-of-network exposure.** The 2022 No Surprises Act addressed a fraction of surprise billing but left substantial gaps. Worth developing as a specific policy-response case — what accountability-bond architecture would the framework propose that the existing mechanism doesn't achieve?

**4. Pharmaceutical cost-sharing as second-layer extraction.** The $100K-range annual cost of specialty medications (oncology, autoimmune, hematology) routes through patient cost-sharing structures that result in millions of "financial toxicity" diagnoses — patients forgoing medication because of cost. This is cost severance at the pharmacy-counter scale. The opioid case file covers the targeted-extraction pattern; this would cover the general high-cost-medication pattern.

**5. Unpaid caregiving as a commons.** AARP and similar sources estimate unpaid family caregiving in the US at $470B+/year in wage-equivalent value. This is a massive hidden externality — the system's ability to function at current spending levels depends on the absorption of this labor by family members, predominantly women, often at the expense of their own careers and retirement savings (which then contributes to the next generation's 2008-style risk-shift exposure). Integration opportunity: unpaid caregiving is a commons — labor contributed by individuals to a shared-need system without institutional accounting. A case-study treatment of this as a commons may warrant its own file.

**6. Butler's original reporting.** Katy Butler's full account in her 2010 NYT Magazine piece ("What Broke My Father's Heart") and her subsequent 2013 book (*Knocking on Heaven's Door*) develops the case far beyond the sociology-paper source's summary. Full integration into chapter prose should source directly from her reporting. Permission / attribution consideration: this is the kind of source that may want a brief quoted passage and clear citation rather than heavy paraphrase.

**7. Ch. 1 personal narrative connection.** The sociology-paper source explicitly suggests that Ch. 1's personal narrative anchor (the author's wife's illness, the plane, the paternal leave running out) creates a natural bridge into the end-of-life case — the same healthcare externalization pattern operating on the author's own family, 30 years earlier, in its upstream form (the knowledge-worker / healthcare-IT case) would bridge into the Butler case as its downstream form. Ch 1 currently does not make this bridge explicit. Integration opportunity during the guidance-revision sweep.
