## Why this case matters

The blood-diamonds case is not primarily a cost-severance illustration (though it is that — miners bear coerced-labor cost while diamonds capture Western retail markup). Its unique analytical contribution is that the **Kimberley Process certification and "ethical diamond" retail premium** represent a performance of accountability reattachment that *doesn't actually reattach anything*. The consumer pays more, the miner gets nothing, and the retailer/brand captures the additional surplus as premium margin.

This is the **system's immune response to the framework's critique**: when cost severance becomes publicly illegible and consumers demand better, the extraction architecture can manufacture a performance of reattachment that satisfies demand without altering the underlying severance. Certification becomes theater; the bond remains severed; the extra money goes to the severing parties, not the cost-bearers.

## Implication for the framework

If simulated-severance / performance-reattachment is a generalizable pattern (and there's no reason it wouldn't be — the pattern exists in fair-trade coffee, carbon offsets, ESG certification, etc.), then the framework must:

1. **Distinguish real reattachment from simulated reattachment** — the distinction is whether the additional consumer payment reaches the cost-bearing party or stays captured by the extraction-side.
2. **Build verifiability into any proposed remedy** — ethical-premium schemes need direct-transfer mechanisms to cost-bearers, not intermediated certification that extraction-side parties can co-opt.
3. **Recognize this as structural, not incidental** — the system reliably produces simulated-reattachment wherever genuine reattachment becomes politically salient. Commons Bonds proposals need to anticipate this response, not be surprised by it.

## Adjacent cases / pattern extensions

The "simulated reattachment" pattern plausibly applies to:

- **Fair-trade coffee** certification vs. actual producer margins
- **Carbon offsets** that reduce marketing cost of emissions without reducing emissions
- **ESG investment screening** that satisfies fiduciary-appearance without altering capital-flow to problematic industries
- **Corporate social responsibility** programs that generate brand equity without changing operational cost-bearing

A cross-case treatment of the simulated-severance pattern could anchor a Ch. 10 passage on "why the system absorbs its critics" or feed a Book Two chapter on policy architecture that prevents simulation.
