### §2.3 Treaty record + tribal status (verified facts)

#### 1646 Treaty of Necotowance (Treaty Ending the Third Anglo-Powhatan War)

- **Date:** October 1646.
- **Signatories:** Necotowance (Opechancanough's successor; signed on behalf of the Powhatan tribes) + the Virginia General Assembly.
- **Reserved lands:** All area north of the York River reserved as Indian land, officially establishing what became the Pamunkey Reservation.
- **Tribute provision:** 20 beaver skins annually to the King's Governor "att the goeing away of Geese yearely" (the autumnal "going-away-of-geese" timing is part of the canonical treaty text).
- **Source for treaty text:** Encyclopedia Virginia primary documents collection.
- **Status:** In force.

**Sources:**

- [Powhatan Treaty October 1646 — Native Heritage Project](https://nativeheritageproject.com/2014/05/16/powhatan-treaty-october-1646/)

#### 1677 Treaty of Middle Plantation ("Articles of Peace")

- **Date:** May 29, 1677.
- **Signatories:** Charles II of England (via colonial commissioners) + Cockacoeske (weroansqua of the Pamunkey; signed on behalf of several tribes including the Mattaponi) + the King of the Notowayes + King Peracuta of the Apoomattux + the Queen of Wayonaoake + others.
- **Provisions:** Virginia tribes retained land rights + received state protection + paid tribute in lieu of taxes. 1677 compact set the Pamunkey rent at "twentie beaver skinns" annually, later amended to "Fin, Fur, or Feather." 18,000-acre exclusive Native American zone established by prohibiting colonists from living within three miles of the Pamunkey Neck settlement.
- **Continuing obligation:** Mattaponi + Pamunkey representatives bring gifts (turkey, deer) to the Governor of Virginia annually (typically around Thanksgiving) in symbolic fulfillment of the treaty's tribute obligations. 2021 was the 344th consecutive annual tribute ceremony.
- **Status:** In force (continuously honored 1677 → 2026, a 349-year continuous-treaty record).

### §2.4 Chesapeake etymology — CORRECTED

**CRITICAL CORRECTION of prior-spawn candidate substrate.** Pass 3.3 §4 #10 ("What flags" #1) and the spawn-instruction candidate-citation list BOTH carried the "great shellfish bay" gloss for "Chesapeake." This gloss is **factually incorrect** per public-record linguistic scholarship.

**Verified etymology:** Per Blair Rudes (Algonquian linguist; published linguistic scholarship 2005), the name comes from the Algonquian word *Chesepiooc*, referring to a village "at a big river" or — in the more often-cited rendering — "country on a great river" (from *K'che-sepi-ack*).

**Year-2005 disambiguation:** Rudes specifically published work dispelling the widely-held "great shellfish bay" folk etymology. The "great shellfish bay" reading was a 20th-century mistranslation popularized in tourist material; the actual Algonquian linguistic record points to the "at a big river" / "great river country" sense.

**Why this matters:** Shipping the "great shellfish bay" gloss in publisher-facing prose at the cultural-resonance-load-bearing audience moment would have been an invented factual claim that any Algonquian-linguistics-fluent reader (a non-trivial subset of the Berggruen Institute editorial brain map's "cultural-engagement seriousness" target) would catch — and would be exactly the propagation-from-essay-to-book reputational asymmetry that `feedback_no_invented_factual_claims_in_publisher_facing_prose.md` is designed to prevent.

**First English written form:** "Chesepiook," 1585 or 1586 (explorers heading north from the Roanoke Colony into a Chesapeake tributary). Seventh-oldest surviving English place-name in the U.S.

**Citation form:** If insertion uses the etymology, attribute to "the Algonquian linguist Blair Rudes" OR cite via Rountree 1989 or 2005 (Rountree includes the corrected etymology in *Pocahontas: Pocahontas's People*; Rudes is also cited in subsequent linguistic literature). A safe sentence-level integration: *"The Bay's name comes from Chesepiooc — an Algonquian word for 'country on a great river,' as the linguist Blair Rudes documented in 2005, correcting a century of mistranslations."* OR simpler: *"The Bay's name is Algonquian. Chesepiooc — 'country on a great river.'"*

- In 1918, the Pamunkey Indian Tribe established a shad hatchery on their reservation along the Pamunkey River.

### §2.6 Kecoughtan / Hampton geographic anchor (SECONDARY DISCOVERY)

**Direct geographic-anchor verification:** The Kecoughtan (also Kikotan / Kiccowtan / Kikowtan) tribe was an Algonquian-speaking member of the Powhatan paramountcy whose main village stood at the mouth of the Hampton River — the SAME geographic area that is now Fox Hill in Hampton, Virginia (where the V-D-prime essay's father grew up).

**Verified facts:**
- Kecoughtan village stood "amid a few thousand cleared acres on the east side of the mouth of the Hampton River" at the time of John Smith's first English contact in 1607.
- Powhatan paramount chief Powhatan had slain the Kecoughtan weroance in 1597 + appointed his own young son Pochins as successor; some Kecoughtan were resettled at the Piankatank River.
- Pre-contact population estimated up to 1,000; by 1608 ~60 (post-Powhatan-overlord-replacement).
- Kecoughtan was where Captain John Smith first received English settler welcome in 1607.
- The Kikotan/Kecoughtan were one of the constituent groups within the Powhatan paramountcy that the 1646 Treaty of Necotowance encompassed.

**Why this is geographically load-bearing:** The V-D-prime essay's §VI Fox Hill geographic anchor at line 177 reads:

> *"He grew up on Fox Hill, an island fishing community on the Virginia side of the Bay, less than ten miles from the wind-tunnel hangars at Langley where his own father was machining test parts."*

Fox Hill is in Hampton, Virginia. NASA Langley Research Center is in Hampton, Virginia. The Hampton River — at whose mouth the Kecoughtan village stood — is the SAME geographic system. The father's people grew up on land that was an Algonquian fishing community four centuries before it was an English-settler-descendant fishing community.

The chronological lineage at the §VI title-earning moment ("older than the regulations, older than the federal disaster declarations, older than the interstate fisheries commissions") is FAR older than the essay currently names. The Algonquian fishing-community-on-this-land predates the federal disaster declarations by approximately 600 years (Kecoughtan village confirmed at the Hampton River mouth at least back to 1607; earlier Algonquian settlement patterns documented archaeologically several centuries before that).

**Substrate-safety:** The Kecoughtan presence at the Hampton River mouth is unambiguously public-record (John Smith's own writings; subsequent archaeological + ethnohistorical scholarship including Rountree 1989 + Potter 1993). Any prose-level integration should cite either Rountree 1989 (canonical) OR the William & Mary Center for Archaeological Research's documented Kecoughtan archaeological record.
