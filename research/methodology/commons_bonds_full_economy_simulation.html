The Full-Economy Simulation

What happens to each resource category under full RCV pricing:

Resource Repricing Under Full RCV Model

  • Coal: $500-700/ton, immediately uncompetitive with renewables
  • Oil: $250-400/barrel, gasoline roughly triples
  • Natural gas: $8-15/MMBtu, heat pump transition accelerates
  • Rare earths: 2-3x current prices, modest consumer electronics impact
  • Lithium: 2-3x, accelerates alternative battery chemistry research
  • Iron ore: 20-40% increase, modest
  • Copper: 50-100% increase, recycling becomes economically dominant
  • Water from depleting aquifers: dramatically more expensive, agricultural restructuring
  • Timber: old-growth priced out, plantation timber dominates
  • Fisheries: overfishing becomes economically impossible
  • Agriculture: industrial farming loses cost advantage over regenerative

Aggregate Cost-of-Living Impact

40-50% increase for a representative household.

Household Budget Impact Calculation

The math:

  • Energy (7% of budget × 3x = up 14 points)
  • Food (12% × 1.4x = up 4.8 points)
  • Transport (16% × 2.5x = up 24 points)
  • Housing (30% × 1.2x = up 6 points)

Total ≈ 49 points

The Distributional Crisis

Bottom 40% currently spending 80-95% of income on essentials. A 40-50% increase puts them at 112-142% of income. Mathematically impossible without additional income or subsidized essentials.

This is cost severance operating in a new dimension. The transition to honest pricing creates its own severed costs — and those costs fall disproportionately on the people least responsible for the extraction economy and least equipped to bear the transition.

The model predicts this. It's not a bug. It's the model telling you that the transition to honest pricing is itself an extraction event unless it's accompanied by redistribution of the pricing surplus.

The Pricing Surplus

The difference between current prices and full-cost prices. Trillions annually, globally. This is not new money — it's the monetization of costs currently absorbed as silent suffering.

Global Pricing Surplus Calculation

Order-of-magnitude: The global fossil fuel industry alone generates on the order of 4-5 trillion dollars in annual revenue. If full-cost pricing would raise those revenues by a factor of 2-3x, the annual global severed cost from fossil extraction is in the range of $8-10 trillion annually.

Add non-fossil extraction sectors (mining, logging, fisheries, aquifer drawdown, agricultural topsoil loss), and the global figure lands somewhere between $10-15 trillion annually.

The Structural Discovery

Full pricing without redistribution is mathematically incompatible with a society that has no floor under the cost of living. This isn't ideology. It's arithmetic. The floor is a structural requirement of the pricing system.

Where does the money go? Under full RCV pricing, the price of resources rises by trillions of dollars per year globally. That money doesn't disappear. It goes somewhere.

Three Options for the Pricing Surplus

Option A: It goes to the extractors (higher profits) - Worst outcome, devastates cost-of-living

Option B: It goes to governments (through taxation/fees/bonds) - Requires recycling to population

Option C: It goes directly to affected communities and future-generation funds - Norway model, community benefit agreements

The Key Insight: The surplus must be recycled to the population, particularly to the bottom 40% whose cost of living has risen most.

Market Reaction Timeline

Short-Term Reaction (Years 1-5)

  • Capital flight toward renewables - Investment case becomes overwhelming
  • Recycling and circular economy explode - Virgin materials carry full RCV pricing
  • Geographic restructuring of extraction - Extraction shifts to lower-impact sources
  • Employment disruption - Coal mining, oil/gas extraction shed jobs rapidly
  • Demand destruction and efficiency gains - Energy consumption optimization becomes urgent

Medium-Term Adjustment (Years 5-15)

Energy prices actually fall. Massive renewable investment produces enormous new capacity. Solar/wind have near-zero marginal cost once installed.

  • Food prices stabilize at new level, then decline due to regenerative practices
  • Materials become more expensive but more efficiently used
  • Essential services are publicly funded - Pricing surplus funds robust floor

Long-Term Equilibrium (Years 15-50)

  • Energy is cheaper than today - Renewable energy with zero fuel costs
  • Food is moderately more expensive but more stable - Less volatile, sustainable
  • Extraction communities are compensated - Spatial cost severance pricing
  • Strategic reserve exists - High-CSG resources preserved
  • The economy is still capitalist - Markets set prices, but prices reflect actual costs

Universal Services Mathematical Necessity

Full externality pricing without redistribution of the pricing surplus is mathematically impossible for the bottom half of the income distribution. They can't pay. The surplus must flow back to them in some form — cash or services. The only question is the mechanism.

This isn't a left-wing conclusion. It's an accounting identity. If you raise the cost of essentials by 40% and the bottom half already spends 65% of income on essentials, you need to either give them more money or provide the essentials outside the market. There is no third option. The math forecloses it.

Two Redistribution Mechanisms

Cash transfers (universal basic income, dividend checks, carbon dividends): Give every citizen a proportional share of the pricing surplus. Preserves market mechanisms and consumer choice but requires massive, permanent transfer system.

Universal provision of essentials (guaranteed healthcare, housing, food security, education): Provide essentials directly, funded by pricing surplus. Stabilizes essentials that full pricing would make unaffordable.

What the Model Actually Reveals

Lesson 1: Full externality pricing is not socialism. It's completed capitalism.

The current system is not a free market. It's a market where certain costs are hidden — pushed onto communities, ecosystems, and future generations who didn't consent to bearing them.

Lesson 2: But completed capitalism requires a universal floor.

The model doesn't start from "universal services are morally good" and argue backward. It starts from "price everything honestly" and discovers that honest pricing is mathematically incompatible with a society that has no floor under the cost of living.

Lesson 3: The current system socializes costs and privatizes benefits. Full pricing reverses this.

Currently, the pricing surplus is captured as private profit, and the severed costs are absorbed as public suffering. Full pricing flips this: the costs are borne by whoever creates them, and the surplus is available for public benefit.

Lesson 4: The economy that emerges is more resilient, not less productive.

Higher resource prices drive efficiency. Circular material flows reduce waste. Renewable energy produces cheaper long-run electricity. The transition is painful. The destination is better.

Lesson 5: This is what Earth's economy looks like when it operates like a Mars colony would have to.

A Mars colony can't externalize costs. There's no "away" to throw waste. The model says Earth should build the same economy.

The Unknown Truth the Model Reveals

The current economy is not a market economy. It's a subsidy economy.

Every barrel of oil at $80 instead of $300 is a subsidy — paid not by government but by the communities and ecosystems and future generations absorbing the severed costs.

The total global subsidy — the sum of all severed costs across all resource extraction — is on the order of $10-15 trillion per year. This dwarfs all government subsidies, all foreign aid, all charitable giving combined. It is the largest wealth transfer in human history, and it flows in one direction: from the powerless to the powerful, from the future to the present, from extraction communities to consuming communities.

The model's deepest lesson: There is no cheap energy. There is only energy whose costs are visible and energy whose costs are hidden. We have been choosing hidden costs for two centuries. The model makes them visible. What happens next is a choice.

For The Book

This entire analysis — the full-economy simulation, the distributional crisis, the structural necessity of a universal floor, the "completed capitalism" insight — represents the chapter where the reader realizes that Commons Bonds isn't just about coal in Appalachia or rare earths in Mongolia. It's about the fundamental structure of the economy they live in every day.

Every purchase they make is priced dishonestly. Every cheap thing is cheap because someone somewhere is absorbing a cost they didn't choose. The model doesn't tell them to feel guilty. It tells them what things actually cost — and then shows that the economy that emerges from honest pricing is not a catastrophe but a better, more stable, more resilient economy with a floor that prevents the transition itself from becoming another form of extraction.

That's the chapter that makes Commons Bonds a book people argue about at dinner tables. Not just "extraction is bad" — but "here's what your electricity actually costs, here's who's currently paying the difference, and here's what happens when we stop pretending."
