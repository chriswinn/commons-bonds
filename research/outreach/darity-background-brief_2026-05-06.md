## 1. Sandy's reparations program — what he advocates concretely

Darity's program, developed with co-author A. Kirsten Mullen in *From Here to Equality* (UNC Press 2020; second edition with new foreword on state-and-local developments), is the most fully specified reparations framework in contemporary American economics. Four non-negotiables structure it ([NPR interview](https://www.npr.org/2020/06/17/879041052/william-darity-jr-discusses-reparations-racial-equality-in-his-new-book); [Roosevelt Institute](https://rooseveltinstitute.org/publications/resurrecting-the-promise-of-40-acres-the-imperative-of-reparations-for-black-americans/)):

1. **Eligibility is lineage-based, not race-based.** Two criteria: (a) at least one ancestor enslaved in the United States, and (b) self-identification as Black/Negro/African American on a public document for at least 12 years prior to enactment or a study commission. This aligns Darity with — but not identical to — the **ADOS** (American Descendants of Slavery) framing. He uses "Black American descendants of US slavery" to avoid the political baggage of the ADOS movement while preserving the lineage criterion.
2. **The federal government must pay.** State, municipal, and university programs are categorically insufficient.
3. **The target is the Black-white per-household wealth gap**, not enumerated atrocities. The gap *is* the summary statistic of cumulative injustice.
4. **The preponderance of disbursement must be direct payments to individuals**, not vouchers, scholarships, or programmatic spending ([Brookings 2020](https://www.brookings.edu/blog/up-front/2020/06/15/black-reparations-and-the-racial-wealth-gap/)).

**The number.** Darity and Mullen converge on **$14 trillion** as the working figure (updated from the $10-12 trillion range in the 2020 first edition). Methodologically, they reject atrocity-enumeration and use the wealth gap as the summary measure: ~$840,000 per-household gap × ~40 million eligible recipients yields roughly $350,000 per person ([CNBC Jan 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/01/28/reparations-owed-to-black-americans-william-darity-kirsten-mullen.html)). They also cross-validate via the present value of unredeemed 40-acres-and-a-mule promises: $3.1T at 6% historic capital returns; $16.5T at 9% (S&P-equivalent) ([AEA *JEP* 2022, "Cumulative Costs of Racism"](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Fjep.36.2.99)).

**HR 40.** Sandy is **not a fan**. He calls it "a badly written piece of legislation" and has argued it should be replaced or leapfrogged. His specific objections: appointment structure is incoherent for a Congressional commission; lacks substantive directives, leaving the commission free to recommend an apology, vouchers, or scholarships and call it reparations; doesn't mandate the wealth-gap-closure target ([Moguldom](https://moguldom.com/313051/top-reparations-scholar-dr-sandy-darity-what-hr40-gets-wrong-and-why/); [INDY Week](https://indyweek.com/news/durham/duke-economist-and-reparations-scholar-is-not-a-fan-of-hb-40/)).

**California Task Force.** Darity served as a paid consultant but became one of its sharpest critics. His core objection: California's annual budget (~$300B) cannot support a per-eligible-Californian gap of ~$1.2M × ~2M people = ~$2.4T. He argues that state programs *create new inequities* because every state's program will differ, and that they may **politically substitute for** rather than catalyze federal action ([CalMatters](https://calmatters.org/california-divide/2023/03/california-reparations-2/)).

**Universities (Georgetown, Harvard).** Same logic: institutional reparations are misframed because they reproduce a "we owned them, we own the solution" mentality and undermine the federal project. Universities' obligation is to use their clout to *push for* national reparations, not write checks themselves ([Quartz](https://qz.com/2010943/georgetown-and-the-jesuits-slavery-reparations-plan-falls-short)).

**Recurrent slogan (Aug 2025, Freedmen Fridays):** **"Stop calling everything reparations."** Wealth-building programs, baby bonds, and HBCU funding are not reparations unless they meet the lineage + federal + wealth-gap-closing + direct-payment criteria ([YouTube Aug 2025](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f18ZBRki1TI)). The author should expect this distinction to police the conversation throughout the interview.

## 2. *Umbrellas Don't Make It Rain* — the methodological foundation

The 2015 Insight Center / New School / Duke report (Hamilton, Darity, Price, Sridharan, Tippett) is the empirical anchor for the wealth-as-structural-variable claim ([Insight Center PDF](https://www.insightcced.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/Umbrellas_Dont_Make_It_Rain_Final.pdf)).

**Headline empirical finding:** The median Black household *headed by a college graduate* holds **less wealth** than the median white household *headed by someone who dropped out of high school*. Education does not produce wealth; the umbrella is correlated with rain because it is brought out when rain is anticipated, but it does not cause it. **Wealth precedes and predicts educational attainment, not the reverse.**

A complementary finding: when Black and white families are *similarly resourced*, Black parents make *larger* per-child financial transfers for education — undercutting any "cultural deficit" or "savings rate" explanation. The conclusion: the racial wealth gap is **not** explained by individual choices, financial literacy, or human-capital accumulation; it is a structural inheritance reproduced across generations through *intergenerational transfer asymmetries* ([St. Louis Fed reframing](https://www.stlouisfed.org/open-vault/2019/february/education-wealth-catch-22)).

This is the methodological taproot for the framework's *wealth-as-structural-variable* claim. It is also load-bearing in **stratification economics** (see §3).

## 3. Related instruments: Baby Bonds, Job Guarantee, stratification economics, climate

**Baby Bonds.** Co-authored with Darrick Hamilton, *Review of Black Political Economy* 2010. A federally-funded, means-tested birthright trust ($500-$60,000 depending on parental wealth), released at 18 for asset-building uses. Projected to compress the young-adult racial wealth gap from ~16x to ~1.4x ([Hamilton-Darity 2010](https://socialequity.duke.edu/wp-content/uploads/2019/10/Can-Baby-Bonds-Eliminate-the-Racial-Wealth-Gap.pdf); [Russell Sage](https://www.russellsage.org/news/recent-%E2%80%9Cbaby-bonds%E2%80%9D-proposal-uses-research-rsf-grantees-sandy-darity-and-darrick-hamilton); CT enacted state version 2021).

**Crucial distinction the author must walk in knowing:** **Baby Bonds are NOT reparations.** They are race-blind and forward-looking; reparations are lineage-specific and backward-looking. Sandy treats them as **complementary, not substitutive** — one addresses the inheritance gap going forward, the other repairs the historical debt. He has explicitly rejected efforts to repackage Baby Bonds as a reparations workaround ([A Thousand Natural Shocks podcast Aug 2025 on "MAGA bonds"](https://streetfins.com/ep-8-simplifying-racial-economic-inequality-with-william-sandy-darity-jr-of-duke-university/)).

**Federal Job Guarantee.** With Hamilton and Mark Paul, Darity proposes a National Investment Employment Corps offering all adults non-poverty-wage public employment ($15/hr indexed, federal benefits, ~$543B/yr) ([CBPP](https://www.cbpp.org/research/economy/the-federal-job-guarantee-a-policy-to-achieve-permanent-full-employment); [Data for Progress memo](https://www.dataforprogress.org/memos/case-for-a-federal-job-guarantee-program)). He frames it as labor-market structural reform — again, complementary to but not a substitute for reparations.

**Stratification Economics.** Darity (with Hamilton and James Stewart) is the field's principal architect ([Cook Center primer PDF](https://socialequity.duke.edu/wp-content/uploads/2019/10/An-Introduction-to-Stratification-Economics.pdf); [EPI](https://www.epi.org/publication/stratification-economics/)). Its core moves:
- Inequality is *intergroup* and *relational*, not the residual of individual optimization
- Dominant groups have material interests in maintaining hierarchy
- Disparities in *intergenerational resource transfer capacity* are the engine of persistence
- Cultural / behavioral explanations are explicitly rejected as ideological cover

This is the framework the author should expect Sandy to use to interpret McDowell County, Libby, Niger Delta, opioid-Appalachia. Stratification economics applies wherever a dominant coalition extracts from a subordinate group across generations — but Sandy is *fastidious* about not collapsing those cases into the Black-American reparations case, which he holds as singular.

**Climate / Environmental.** TBD via further research — Darity has not, to public knowledge, written a major treatment of climate reparations or Pigouvian instruments. The Cook Center has work in this adjacency (Bentley-Edwards on health burden), but Darity himself approaches environmental harm through the racial-wealth-gap and stratification lens rather than as an independent damage class. **This is a real opening for the author** on the *Foreclosure Bond* side.

- **Ta-Nehisi Coates** — Sandy is broadly aligned with Coates's 2014 *Atlantic* essay but has been clear that Coates's framing left the *number* and the *delivery mechanism* unspecified; Sandy and Mullen explicitly fill that gap ([Demos](https://www.demos.org/blog/expert-responds-ta-nehisi-coates-reparations); [New Republic](https://newrepublic.com/article/117856/academic-evidence-reparations-costs-are-limited)).
- **Mehrsa Baradaran** (*The Color of Money*) — adjacent and complementary; Baradaran on banking/credit, Darity on wealth-gap closure.
- **William Spriggs** (d. 2023) — close intellectual ally on racial bias in economics methodology.

**Contested terrain:**
- Against **race-based** (vs. lineage-based) eligibility — distinguishes himself from some progressive proposals.
- Against **state and university** reparations as substitutes for federal action.
- Against **HR 40** in its current drafting.
- Against **conflation** of Baby Bonds, Job Guarantee, and HBCU funding *with* reparations.

## 7. Where the *Commons Bonds* two-instrument decomposition intersects his program

**Restitution Bond (backward-looking).** Direct lineage to FHTE. Sandy's program *is* a financial-instrument-style commitment device for an intergenerational debt. Productive interview moves: ask whether instrumentalizing reparations as a *bond* (with explicit maturity, coupon, recipient ledger) addresses, exacerbates, or sidesteps his concerns about disbursement integrity. **Likely friction:** Sandy will police whether the Restitution Bond satisfies his four non-negotiables (federal payer, lineage eligibility, wealth-gap target, direct payment). If the framework allows non-federal issuers or non-direct disbursement, expect pushback.

**Foreclosure Bond (forward-looking).** This is **outside Sandy's published terrain.** Stratification economics gives him a vocabulary for extraction-community analysis (McDowell County, Libby, Niger Delta), but he has not built a forward-looking instrument analogous to the Foreclosure Bond. The interview can productively position the Foreclosure Bond as a *generalization* of stratification logic to non-racial extraction contexts — but Sandy will likely insist that the **Black-American case is sui generis** and should not be flattened into a universal extraction frame. Handle with care.

## 8. Things to NOT walk in unaware of

- **"Stop calling everything reparations"** is currently his most insistent public position. Do not refer to Baby Bonds, Job Guarantee, HBCU funding, or environmental remediation as reparations.
- **Lineage, not race.** Do not say "reparations to Black Americans" without qualifier; say "Black American descendants of US slavery."
- **$14 trillion / ~$350,000 per person** are the live numbers; the older $10-12T figure is superseded.
- The **California task force** is a sore spot — he was paid consultant *and* loud critic. Acknowledge both.
- He **just retired** (2025) and Tyson Brown now runs the Cook Center; refer to him as Professor Emeritus.
- His **Jan 2026 paper on Jim Crow violence-as-economic-control** is the freshest thing on his desk; reading it signals seriousness.

## Key takeaways for the live interview

- **Open with the lineage distinction**, not with "reparations" as a generic category. Signal that you understand "stop calling everything reparations" before he has to say it.
- **Frame the Restitution Bond as *operationalizing* FHTE's debt**, not as an alternative to it. Ask him explicitly whether instrumentalization (bond form, maturity, ledger) helps or hurts the four non-negotiables.
- **Don't generalize the Foreclosure Bond to the Black-American case.** Position it as a *separate* instrument addressing forward-looking extraction (Appalachia, Libby, Niger Delta) and ask whether stratification economics travels — let him decide how far.
- **Be precise on $14T and ~$350K/person**, and on the wealth-gap-as-summary-measure methodology versus atrocity enumeration. Citing *Umbrellas* by name will land.
- **Acknowledge Mullen as co-architect**, not as a junior co-author. The work is genuinely co-authored.
- **Have a question ready about the Jim Crow violence-as-economic-control paper** (Jan 2026, *Explorations in Economic History*) — it shows you've read past the canonical works.
