## What this guide does

The Phat companion document is a *consent guide* — Phat is alive and we are asking for his signature. This guide is different. Biggie has been dead for more than thirty years; there is no consent to obtain. What there is, and what the discipline asks for, is a good-faith effort to find any reachable surviving family member and let them know in advance that their relative is being named and portrayed in a book. The notification is the right thing to do. It is not a request for permission.

Three things follow from that:

1. **The conversation is one-way by default,** with the door open for the family to respond. The author goes in to share, not to ask.
2. **If the family responds with concerns,** those concerns matter — but they are not vetoes. The discipline is *courtesy*, and that means hearing the family out, considering edits or omissions if they request them, and making a judgment call.
3. **If the family is unreachable after a good-faith effort,** the author proceeds with naming. The discipline does not block publication on a closed door.

Concrete steps follow.

## 1. Locate reachable family

The author's father is the relationship-holder and the obvious first source. He knew Biggie from boyhood; the connection has run through the family for a generation. Start there.

### What to ask your father

- **Biggie's full given name.** The chapter currently uses only the working nickname. The notification letter and any later printing will benefit from the real name — and the family will recognize the courtesy of being addressed accurately.
- **Year of death** (approximate is fine). The chapter currently writes *"Biggie has been dead for more than thirty years."* If your father can pin the year, the chapter can be tightened ("died in ~~the late 1980s~~ [year]"), and the family will know the timing in the book matches their memory.
- **Surviving family the father knows of.** Spouse, children, grandchildren, siblings. Names, ages, last known locations.
- **Where the family lives now.** If they are still in Fox Hill / Poquoson / wider Hampton, in-person notification may be possible. If they have moved away (Norfolk, Newport News, elsewhere on the Peninsula, or further), letter/phone is the path.
- **Who else might know.** If your father has lost touch, who in his generation of Fox Hill would still have the connection? A single name and a phone call may unblock the entire pathway.

### If your father can't help

Fallback channels, in rough order of effort:

- **Obituary search** — the *Daily Press* (Hampton/Newport News) archive is the most likely source for a Fox Hill waterman who died in the late 1980s or 1990s. Many libraries hold print or microfilm archives; the *Daily Press* also has an online searchable archive (subscription or via newspapers.com). The obituary will usually list surviving family by name.
- **Find a Grave / BillionGraves** — if Biggie was buried locally (likely; Fox Hill watermen of his generation typically were), the gravesite record may list family. The Hampton-area cemeteries to check include St. John's Episcopal (Fox Hill), Greenlawn, and the Hampton National Cemetery if he served.
- **Fox Hill community contacts** — the working watermen who remain in the area (Phat among them) almost certainly know the family or can name someone who does. A single phone call to the right person closes this fast.
- **Church records** — if the family was churched in Fox Hill (most were), the church's current pastor or longest-tenured member may know the family.
- **Virginia public records** — death certificates are public after a certain interval; the Virginia Department of Health vital-records office can be queried for a fee.

Most of these can run in parallel; one will usually surface a reachable family member within a week or two.

## 2. What to send / show the family

A notification packet is small. Bigger is worse, not better. The family has not asked for a book; they are receiving a courtesy from someone who is writing about their relative. The packet should fit in a manila envelope and be readable in fifteen minutes.

### What goes in the packet

1. **A short cover letter** — see §3 below. One page. Warm, direct, plain.
2. **The Ch 3 Biggie portrait** — printed as a standalone four-page excerpt, exactly as it appears in the chapter. The relevant block runs lines ~46–54 of `Chapter__3_TheWaterman__Draft.md` and consists of four paragraphs:
   - The opening portrait — oak-tree build, generational oysterman, one-oar-between-the-beds.
   - The six-pack-of-Coca-Cola / oyster-shucking scene with your father watching as a small boy.
   - The "Biggie has been dead for more than thirty years" paragraph with the shad-fishery date.
   - The closing four-line stanza on the dying motion of the wrist.
   Print these four paragraphs only; nothing else from the chapter. Print on standard letter paper, single-sided, generous margins. About four pages.
3. **A one-paragraph project framing** — what the book is and why Biggie is in it. Two to four sentences. Sample:

   > *Commons Bonds* is a book I have been working on for the last few years. It is about the way the cost of certain kinds of work — the work that holds places together — ends up getting carried by the people doing the work, while the value those places produce flows somewhere else. The book has chapters on coal miners, on workers in Norway and Spain who built different arrangements, on the watermen of the Chesapeake. Biggie is in the book because Biggie was, in the way my father has carried him forward to me, the watermen — an example, in one man's life and motion, of what the work and the community looked like at their best.

   Adapt to how you would actually say this in person. The above is a starting point.

4. **Your contact information** — full name, mailing address, phone, email — on the cover letter and on a separate slip in the packet. If the family wants to respond, you want it to be easy.

### What stays out of the packet

- The rest of Ch 3 (the analytical material on rockfish, blue crabs, menhaden, watersheds, the Norway comparison, the cost-severance argument). The family is being notified about Biggie. The chapter's surrounding argument is not what they are being shown.
- Other chapters. None of them mention Biggie.
- The book's framework, theorems, methodology, anything technical. Not relevant to this conversation.
- The story-draft source file or any working notes from the project. Those are author-facing.

### Total length

About 6–8 pages, including the cover letter. A single thin envelope. The family can read it in fifteen minutes at a kitchen table.

## 3. Cover letter draft

Adapt freely. The author will know how to talk to the family better than this template will. The point is to be warm, clear, brief, and to leave the door open without making the door the subject.

> Dear \[family member name\],
>
> My name is Chris Winn. I am writing because I am the son of \[father's name\] — \[father's name\] grew up in Fox Hill, and your \[father / grandfather / husband / brother — whichever is right\] was someone he knew well as a boy. I have heard about \[Biggie / Biggie's real name\] my whole life, and I am writing now because I have written about him in a book.
>
> The book is called *Commons Bonds*. It will come out from a publisher \[in 2026 / on a date I'll know once the contract is signed\]. The book is about the way certain kinds of work — the kind \[Biggie\] did — hold communities and places together, and what happens when that work stops being possible. There is a chapter in it about the watermen of the Chesapeake, and a portrait in that chapter about \[Biggie\]. He is named, and he is described from things my father told me about him growing up.
>
> I wanted you to see it before it goes to print. I have enclosed the part of the chapter that is about \[Biggie\] — about four pages — so you can read exactly what is being said. The portrait is, I hope, what you would want a portrait of him to be. My father remembers him with enormous respect, and the chapter tries to carry that forward.
>
> I am not writing to ask permission. He has been gone a long time, and it is not the family's job to give permission for what people who knew him remember. I am writing because it would not feel right to put him in a book without letting his family know first. If you read the chapter and something feels off — a detail wrong, a name spelled the way he did not spell it, a beat that misses what he was actually like — please tell me. The book is not yet locked. I would rather hear from you than not.
>
> My contact information is at the bottom of this letter, and on a separate slip in case you want to keep one and pass the other to someone else in the family. I am reachable by phone, email, or letter, whichever is easiest. If you do not want to respond, you do not have to; the chapter will run with him in it either way, but it will run a little warmer if I know you saw it.
>
> Thank you for the time. Please pass my regards to anyone else in the family I have not been able to reach.
>
> With respect,
> Chris Winn
>
> \[address\]
> \[phone\]
> \[email\]

### Tone notes for the letter

- **Use his real name** in the letter and the printed chapter excerpt if you have it by then. If you do not yet have the real name, use the working nickname in both, and consider including a separate sentence asking the family which form they would prefer in the printed book.
- **Address the closest reachable relative** — spouse if living, otherwise eldest surviving child, otherwise eldest surviving sibling, otherwise any reachable family member. If you find more than one and they are not in close contact, send a separate copy to each rather than asking one to forward to the others.
- **Sign by hand if you can.** Especially if mailed. The act of signing communicates more than the words.
- **Do not apologize** for writing about him. The letter is a courtesy and an honor; framing it as a hesitation reads as if you were not sure you should have done the work. The work is right. The letter is the closing gesture, not a request to be forgiven for the writing.

## 4. Delivery options

In rough order of preference:

### In-person

If a reachable family member is local (Fox Hill, Poquoson, broader Hampton) and willing, in-person delivery is best. Bring the packet. Sit at a kitchen table or porch. Hand them the packet. Let them read. Be quiet while they read. Answer questions if they ask. Leave the packet with them.

This is the warmest version of the gesture. It is also the most demanding for both parties. Use judgment about whether it is welcome. If your father can broker the introduction, that is the easiest route.

### Mail (letter + packet)

If in-person is not possible — distance, scheduling, family preference — mail the packet. USPS Priority or First Class with tracking. The packet is small enough to fit a standard business envelope or a manila envelope for the unfolded version.

Allow at least 14 days for the family to receive, read, and respond before any follow-up.

### Phone

If you have a phone number but no mailing address, call first to introduce yourself and ask for an address you can mail the packet to. Do not deliver the substance of the notification on the phone alone — the family deserves to read the actual passage on paper, not to have it described to them.

### Email

Lowest preference. If you only have an email address, email the cover letter and attach the four-page excerpt as a PDF. Email is fine when it is the only channel; it is not the channel of choice for a notification of this kind.

## 5. What to do with the response

Five scenarios cover almost everything that happens.

### A. The family responds warmly, no changes requested

Most common outcome. The portrait is overwhelmingly positive; families of named subjects in similar situations typically receive this kind of letter as an honor.

**Action:** thank them. Send a copy of the book when it is published. File the correspondence at `research/outreach/subjects/biggie/courtesy-notification-record_<YYYY-MM-DD>.md` (see §7).

### B. The family responds with factual corrections

Common. Family will often know the exact year of death, the spelling of his name, a detail that differs slightly from your father's memory, a story-detail that landed slightly off.

**Action:** thank them. Make the corrections in Ch 3 if they hold up against the source material your father provided. Consider sending the corrected page back to the family so they can confirm the fix lands. Update [Chapter__3_TheWaterman__Draft.md](manuscript/chapters/Chapter__3_TheWaterman__Draft.md) and re-ratify the chapter per the Stage-3 discipline.

### C. The family responds with a sensitivity or framing concern

Less common but worth thinking through in advance. Possible asks: do not describe the daily six-pack-of-Coca-Cola (worry it implies a vice the family does not want associated with him); do not give his real name (preserve the nickname only); do not include the eating-oysters-all-day anecdote; do not mention X.

**Action:** take the ask seriously. The discipline is courtesy; courtesy means weighing the family's stated preferences against the chapter's purposes. In most cases, accommodating a sensitivity ask is the right call — the chapter does not lose anything important by adjusting a detail the family is uncomfortable with, and the portrait gains the family's blessing. If an ask would gut the portrait's load-bearing material (which would be unusual — the portrait is admiring throughout), have a follow-up conversation rather than an immediate yes/no.

### D. The family asks you not to use his name at all

Rare for the kind of portrait this is, but possible. The discipline says courtesy-notify, not consent-required, so technically you can proceed with naming. But the spirit of the discipline is respect; if the family genuinely does not want him named, you should consider honoring that.

**Action:** ask once for the reason. If the reason is one that landing the portrait might address (privacy concern, fear of misuse), see whether an adjustment satisfies it. If the family is firm, consider anonymizing — refer to him by an invented short name (the Phat-and-Biggie-and-others pattern), preserving the substance of the portrait. Document the decision in the record file (§7).

### E. No response after good-faith effort

The exit ramp. Sometimes the family is unreachable, has scattered, has its own reasons not to engage, or simply does not see the letter.

**Action:** proceed with naming. Document the good-faith effort in the record file (§7). The discipline is satisfied.

## 6. The good-faith-effort threshold

For the "no response" exit ramp to be discipline-compliant, the author's effort needs to clear a defensible bar. The bar is not high, but it is real.

**Minimum effort:**

- **Two attempts via the best-available primary channel** (letter + follow-up letter, or letter + phone follow-up), spaced at least 14 days apart.
- **At least one alternative channel tried** if available (e.g., a different family member by a different channel; or a letter to one and a phone call to another).
- **Total window: 30 days from first attempt to disposition.** If 30 days pass with no response, the discipline is satisfied.

**If the effort surfaces no reachable family at all** (no obituary, no family found in records, no Fox Hill contact who knows), document what you tried and proceed. The discipline does not require contacting people you have no way to reach.

**What the bar is not:**

- Not a requirement to track down distant relatives the family itself has lost touch with.
- Not a requirement to wait indefinitely.
- Not a requirement to escalate through legal channels, paid search services, or professional genealogists. Common-sense effort is the standard.

## 7. Documentation

After the notification process resolves one way or another, file the record at:

`research/outreach/subjects/biggie/courtesy-notification-record_<YYYY-MM-DD>.md`

The record file is short — half a page is plenty — and captures:

- **Family contacts identified** (names, relationships, channels used).
- **Attempts made** (date, channel, sent-to, follow-up).
- **Responses received** (verbatim if substantive; paraphrase if brief).
- **Disposition** — one of: *named with notification, family receptive*; *named with notification, corrections incorporated*; *named with notification, no response after good-faith effort*; *anonymized at family request*.
- **Any commitments made to the family** — e.g., sending a copy when published, an inscribed thank-you, a follow-up at publication.
- **Notes for future similar workstreams** — what worked, what to do differently.

The record file is the closing artifact of the notification workstream. Keep it brief and concrete.

## 9. What if the chapter cites a date the family corrects?

The Ch 3 portrait carries one specific date — *"He died before Virginia stopped its commercial shad fishery in 1994"* — and one approximate timing — *"Biggie has been dead for more than thirty years."* The shad date is verified (VMRC, ratified via commit `b5692f1`); it is the death-date framing that the family is the source of truth on.

If the family confirms a death year, replace the "more than thirty years" framing with the exact year ("Biggie died in [year]") and let the prose flow from there. The chapter becomes more specific, more rooted, and more accurate. This is a clean win.

If the family corrects something about the shad-fishery-as-temporal-marker (e.g., they say Biggie died well before 1994 in a way that makes the "died before" framing read as understated), tighten accordingly. The marker is doing time-placement work; the family's input on whether it does it well is welcome.

## 10. What this guide does NOT do

For symmetry with the Phat consent guide and the Colden citation-verification packet:

- It is **not** a request for permission. Permission is not the discipline for deceased subjects.
- It is **not** an invitation for the family to rewrite the chapter's framing. The chapter's argument about the Bay, the watermen, and the cost-severance pattern is the author's; the family is being shown the portrait, not asked to endorse the surrounding analytical work.
- It is **not** a legal-permissions process. If a publisher's contracts team needs formal written permission for a deceased subject's portrayal (rare but possible), that is a separate workstream, typically handled at the manuscript-handed-to-publisher stage and routed through whatever permissions counsel the publisher uses. This guide covers the relational, not the legal, gesture.
- It is **not** time-limited to pre-Aeon. The notification can fire any time before the book ships to publisher. There is no submission window pressure; the discipline only requires that notification happen before the printed book exists.
