**To:** william.darity@duke.edu
**Subject:** Putting a few things in writing from our May 13 call

Sandy,

I should have sent this note within a few days of our conversation, and I let it slip for longer than I meant to. I'm sending it now because the substance of what's in it shouldn't sit on my side any longer than it already has — these are commitments that belong in your inbox in writing, not just in mine.

I want to put four things from our May 13 call into writing — partly so you have a record of what we agreed to, partly because the trade publisher I'll eventually contract with will appreciate the documentation when their permissions team takes a look down the road.

**On attribution.** When I asked at the start of the call how you'd like your words handled in print, your answer had two parts. On your published written work, you said:

> *"Anything that I have in writing is essentially fair game. Even if the quotation used is not in the context that's consistent with my views, that's fair game."*

On material from our conversation, you said:

> *"My only constraint would be — please let me see what you're going to use before you [use it]."*

I'll abide by both, across the book *Commons Bonds* and any derivative essays (a Noema essay is in preparation; one or two more if the publishing arc lands the way I'm aiming). Specifically, that means I'll treat your published written work as freely quotable with attribution, and I'll send you any direct quotes or substantive paraphrases I want to draw from our conversation — book and essays both — for your review before they're published. I'll batch those into per-essay or per-chapter packets to keep the request count low and the context clear. The first such packet will come separately, soon — it ratifies the citation form I've converted to and raises a small set of close-paraphrase paragraphs I want to be sure you're comfortable having attributed.

If anything about the two-tier framing above isn't quite what you meant, please correct me and I'll defer to your refinement.

**On the recording.** I should be straight with you: I had the call on Otter.ai for my own working transcript, but I didn't separately ask your verbal yes to that at the top of the call — I went straight to the attribution question. The recording is legal on my own consent in both Virginia (my end) and North Carolina (your end), and the audio is used only to produce an accurate transcript for the materials I'll be sending you for review per the attribution preference above. The audio file sits on my own working devices, not a cloud service.

**On deletion.** If you'd prefer I delete the recording now and work from notes plus my memory of the call, say the word and I'll do it today — no questions, no negotiation. If you don't ask me to delete it now, I'll keep it until your attribution reviews are complete and the relevant manuscript work has shipped, then delete it and let you know when it's done. The default, in other words, is: deletion on demand at any point, and deletion once the recording has served its purpose. You hold the switch.

**On publication scope.** The materials drawing on our conversation will likely include:

- **The book** *Commons Bonds* — trade publisher TBD; target publication 2026–2027.
- **A magazine essay in Noema** — currently in preparation.
- **One or two further magazine essays** — Aeon and/or others, if the pitches land; different angles; would likely draw on different portions of our conversation if at all.
- **Op-eds + talks** drawing on the same material from time to time.

If a future use surfaces that's substantially outside this scope, I'll loop back to you before publishing.

**On where we left things.** I sent the packet I promised — Chapter 6, the Technical Appendix, and Chapter 5 (added beyond what I'd committed because of a specific attribution it carries) — on May 14. No reply needed on any particular timeline; whenever a read fits, fits. The proactive citation note I mentioned above will follow as a separate email so you have all three close-paraphrase paragraphs in front of you in one place rather than surfacing them as you read.

Thank you again for the time. I'm still sitting with the question you stopped me to ask — whether the common bond is a metaphor or something literal — and the answer is genuinely reframing how I think about the framework's center of gravity. The conversation has already shifted real things in the manuscript.

Best,
