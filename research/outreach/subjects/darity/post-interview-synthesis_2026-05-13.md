## 10. Framework-revision implications

### MUST-INCORPORATE

**MI-1. "Restitution" sits inside Sandy's reparations definition, not alongside it.**

Sandy's direct correction: *"In our definition of reparations, the second component is redress... two types — acknowledgement, atonement, and restitution — and we declare in favor of restitution. So I don't know that restitution is a concept that's really independent of reparations from the way in which we've tried to define it."*

**Framework consequence.** The current positioning of "Restitution Bond" as the backward-looking instrument *distinct from* reparations *flattens this distinction* from Sandy's vantage. Three resolution options the framework should now choose between:

1. **Keep "Restitution Bond" but explicitly position it as "the instrument that operationalizes the restitution component of Darity-Mullen's redress in their reparations typology."** Adds nuance; preserves the framework's existing vocabulary; requires a half-page Ch 5 §"Restitution and Foreclosure" insertion that defers to Sandy's typology before extending.
2. **Rename the backward-looking instrument** (candidates: Repair Bond, Redress Bond, Legacy-Effect Bond, Atonement Bond). Sharper but invalidates a year of vocabulary buildup.
3. **Embrace Sandy's permission to call the framework's calculations "reparations" in non-Black-American cases** (per his Q3 response — see MI-2) and reorient: the instrument is *the* reparations instrument; the bond decomposition (B₁ = Restitution Bond for legacy-effect pricing; B₂ = Foreclosure Bond for forward-harm pricing) is internal to "reparations" rather than alongside it.

**Recommendation:** Option 1 is the safest near-term — keeps existing vocabulary; explicitly defers to Darity-Mullen's typology; positions framework as extension rather than competitor. Option 3 is intellectually cleaner but requires reworking Ch 5 + Ch 6 + Tech Appendix vocabulary and may invite the "you're flattening 'reparations'" critique from voices other than Sandy. Author decision; flag in a PM session pass.

**MI-2. Coercion vector is unpriced; slavery is sui generis to the framework's information-asymmetry analysis.**

Sandy: *"what's distinctive about slavery, in contrast with the other cases you're talking about, is I don't think that there's a denial of information, per se. I mean, there's just outright coercion. So I don't know that we've ever really come up with a satisfactory way of assigning a damage to the coercive dimension."*

**Framework consequence.** The framework's cost-severance analysis depends substantially on information-asymmetry vectors (NFL/concussions, cigarette industry, opioid-Appalachia, etc.) — workers and consumers participating in extraction don't know the true costs. **This analytical move doesn't reach the slavery case**, where participation is forced, not informed-or-uninformed. Two consequences:

1. **The framework needs an explicit scope-acknowledgment** for the coercion vector. Right now Ch 5 §"Restitution and Foreclosure" and Ch 6 methodology implicitly suggest the framework's pricing extends to slavery; Sandy's correction implies that any framework instrument applied to slavery is pricing **legacy effects** (the racial wealth gap; the longevity gap), NOT direct compensation for the coerced labor itself. This matches FHTE's own position.
2. **The framework could attempt to extend its analytical vocabulary to the coercion vector** — but this is heavy lift. Sandy's field hasn't done it satisfactorily; cost-severance vocabulary isn't obviously the right tool. Recommend: in Ch 5 + Ch 6, explicitly demarcate the framework's scope as legacy-effect pricing (which extends across all cases including slavery's wealth-gap and longevity-gap pricing) and explicitly note the unpriced coercion vector as outside the framework's scope. The Ostrom-path discipline (drop "canonical"; lists are illustrative) applies — don't claim universal coverage.

**MI-3. Ostrom's homogeneity-of-interest assumption is an additional break-point.**

Sandy's add-on critique: *"another issue with Ostrom is, I think she's assuming homogeneity of interest in using the commons across what she's calling the community. But suppose the community is divided on ethnic grounds or racial grounds, and have conflicting views about what should be done with the commons, then I don't think that her view provides a solution to the problem."*

**Framework consequence.** The framework's existing Ostrom-positioning (canonical articulation in `alignment/commons_bonds_framework_positioning_disciplines_v1.0.0.md`) identifies non-sustained-yield commons as the place where Ostrom's design principles break down. Sandy adds a second break-point: **heterogeneous-stakeholder commons**, where ethnic / racial / class divisions produce conflicting use-preferences. This is a substantive add to the Ostrom-positioning that strengthens the framework's claim of necessity.

**Where it goes:** Ch 6 (Three Ways of Counting) Ostrom-extension section + the canonical Ostrom-pivot articulation in the FPD doc. Tech Appendix §E.3 may want a paragraph on heterogeneous-stakeholder commons. Update the live-call companion glossary if it's reused.
