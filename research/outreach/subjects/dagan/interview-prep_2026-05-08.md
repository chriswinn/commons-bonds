## What this interview is for

Per `alignment/commons_bonds_framework_positioning_disciplines_v1.0.0.md` §"Goal alignment": the project's success criterion is **vocabulary adoption at scale** — the framework's terms (*cost severance*, *RCV*, *Restitution Bond*, *Foreclosure Bond*, *CSD*) becoming naturalized in legal, regulatory, advocacy, and academic contexts. This interview exists primarily to **road-test the framework's vocabulary** in the applied-implementation register where Dagan's day-to-day operates — a Doughnut-organized civic-network layer trying to translate framework articulation into operational discipline.

What we want from this conversation, ordered by load-bearingness:

1. **Vocabulary failure-mode surfacing.** Where does *cost severance* misfire when applied alongside Coalition vocabulary (*donutstad*, *binnen de donut*, *donutpioniers*, *droom and doorbraak*, *change-makers*, *living labs*)? Where does *Foreclosure Bond* compete with vocabulary the implementation already has?
2. **Adjacency mapping.** What vocabulary does the Coalition already use for the action-level binding territory the framework's terms are trying to name — particularly anything *regulatory or financial* that the public-facing aspirational register doesn't surface?
3. **Forwardability stress-test.** Can Dagan articulate the framework's vocabulary back to us cleanly? Given that his job is *literally* vocabulary-translation work, his ability or struggle to re-narrate the framework is itself diagnostic.
4. **Direct quotable substrate** for Ch 9 *Pricing Honestly* (entering-wedges section) + Noema essay §VI.
5. **Scope-claim validation.** Does the framework's *complementarity-at-different-scales* claim — Doughnut sets the goal-state; Commons Bonds operates one layer in — hold up when Dagan engages it from inside the implementation work?

**Subject pushback is the highest-value outcome.** Confirmation moments are good; pushback moments that reveal vocabulary failure-modes we can fix before publication are *better* — that's what shifts the success criterion's probability.

**Tone calibration (key — see background brief §10).** Dagan is **aspirational/inclusive in register; absorbs critique generously rather than counter-arguing**. Closer to a Mondragon-implementer interview than a Darity contest. **Frame questions as invitations to extend his methodology into adjacent territory** rather than pushback-extraction. The contingency probability (FPD-11) is correspondingly *lower* than for Darity — but contingency still gets captured, because his generous absorption of critique does not guarantee he'll commit to specific framework-vocabulary positions on the record.

### Q1. Narrative-translation methodology — your work in your own words (SHOULD-ASK · opener)

> *"Your career has three clear chapters in the public record — visual art and Supperclub at the Rietveld Academy, twenty-plus years in advertising creative direction at Saatchi and Lowe, the WDCD years running the Climate Action and Make it Circular challenges, and now ADC plus CHANGENCY. ### Q2. The four-lens asymmetry — naming vs. acting on Global-Social cost severance (MUST-ASK)

> *"The Amsterdam City Doughnut report's signature methodological contribution is the four-lens framework — Local-Social, Local-Ecological, Global-Social, Global-Ecological. The Global-Social lens explicitly names the impact of Amsterdam's consumption patterns on people elsewhere: labour conditions in textile factories, mining for metals in electronics, the imported costs that aren't visible in Amsterdam's local foundation/ceiling. But reading the published Coalition material, the Global-Social lens's what-to-do vocabulary feels thinner than the work on the local lenses. Which is exactly the framework I'm developing's strongest opening: McDowell County, West Virginia is the textbook failure of direct cost severance — coal extracted, costs stayed locally. Amsterdam's work is the opposite at the local level. But the cost severance Amsterdam imports — rare earths from Niger Delta, battery materials from extraction zones, cobalt for the energy transition — is the same structural mechanism, displaced in space. Where in the Coalition's actual work is that imported / upstream cost severance most visible to you? And what's the gap between naming it through the Global-Social lens and acting on it at the city scale?"*

### Q3. Coalition vs. City — what does the Coalition do when Amsterdam faces a foreclosure-pattern deal? (MUST-ASK)

> *"A clarifying question first: the Coalition is the network layer — citizens, businesses, knowledge institutions, neighborhoods — and the City is the municipal-policy layer where formal deal-evaluation happens. Marieke van Doorninck championed the April 2020 adoption from the deputy-mayor seat; René Koop now leads the city-side circular economy work. Suppose the City of Amsterdam were approached tomorrow with a deal that would leverage water-management infrastructure in a way that could affect crop-growing capacity — a hypothetical pulled from the framework's pricing mechanics. Doughnut would flag the affected social foundation (food security) and ecological ceiling. But evaluating it at the action level — pricing what's being foreclosed, binding the counterparty, ensuring obligations survive corporate dissolution — sits one layer past the framework's named goal-state. From your network-layer vantage: what tools does the Coalition (vs. the City) actually use or push for in moments like that? Where does the implementation currently have those tools, and where is there a gap something like a Foreclosure Bond instrument might fill?"*

### Q4. Framework-fit calibration — does this duplicate vocabulary you already have, or fill a gap? (MUST-ASK · load-bearing for vocabulary calibration)

> *"The framework I'm developing — cost severance as the structural mechanism, Residual Commons Value as what gets foreclosed by extraction, the Foreclosure Bond as the action-level pricing instrument — sits at a different scale than the donut. Doughnut articulates the goal-state (the safe and just space between social foundation and ecological ceiling); my framework focuses on action-level accountability when extraction would impose un-priced costs. The Coalition vocabulary I've read in the public record — donutstad, binnen de donut, donutpioniers, droom and doorbraak, change-makers, living labs — operates in a register that's aspirational and experiential. From your vantage as the person translating the donut into Amsterdam's civic vocabulary, does the framework I'm developing fill an action-level accountability gap that your implementation has been working around? Or does it duplicate vocabulary you already have under different names — particularly anything regulatory or financial that the public-facing register doesn't surface?"*
