# Worked example — Foreclosure Bond as forward-looking deal-evaluation tool

**The Amsterdam water-management hypothetical**

**Created:** 2026-05-07 by Claude per author direction during Dagan-conversation prep work.
**Status:** Working note. Captured for two purposes: (1) as a candidate question / framing for the Dagan interview if he accepts; (2) as a candidate worked example for Ch 9 *Pricing Honestly* entering-wedges material.
**Origin:** Author asked whether Commons Bonds could be used by Amsterdam to evaluate a deal *prior to signing* to ensure honest pricing. The hypothetical was constructed to test the framework's forward-looking action-level diagnostic function. The answer (with caveats) was yes, and the walkthrough below demonstrates the mechanism step by step.

---

## The hypothetical

The City of Amsterdam is approached by an external organization that wants to leverage Amsterdam's water-management infrastructure (levees, polders, dyke system) for some commercial purpose — for instance, monetizing capacity in a way that could impair crop-growing capacity downstream. The organization offers a price; Amsterdam must decide whether to accept.

This hypothetical is structurally generic — the specifics of "what kind of water deal" matter less than the structure: a proposed action that has both **value extracted** (some party benefits financially) and **cost severable** (Amsterdam's crop-growing capacity is potentially impaired). The framework should be able to evaluate any deal of this shape.

---

## The framework's step-by-step evaluation

### Step 1: Identify the parties + the proposed action

- **Party A:** External organization (the proposer)
- **Party B:** City of Amsterdam (and its citizens, future generations, ecosystems)
- **Action:** Some monetization of water infrastructure that could affect crop-growing capacity

### Step 2: Identify what's being extracted

What value would the organization capture?

- The headline payment / yield / financial return from the deal
- This is what Amsterdam would normally evaluate against "fair price"

### Step 3: Identify what costs would be severed

The framework's core diagnostic question: what costs would be transferred from the value-capturer to Amsterdam (and its citizens, ecosystems, futures)? These are costs that *would not appear* on the deal sheet under normal accounting:

- **Reduced crop yields** → economic loss to farmers; food security loss to citizens
- **Long-term soil degradation** → reduced future agricultural capacity (intergenerational)
- **Ecosystem effects on wetlands and biodiversity** → ecological cost
- **Cascading water-quality effects** → public health cost
- **Climate-resilience reduction** → exposure to future flood / drought events
- **Infrastructure flexibility foreclosed** → other future uses of the water system that become impossible

### Step 4: Calculate RCV — the worth permanently foreclosed

If the deal is approved, what is permanently foreclosed?

- If water levies are leveraged past the system's regenerative capacity, agricultural capacity transitions from "renewable" to "effectively non-renewable" — that's the canonical Commons Bonds threshold-crossing case (per FPD-8 scope-explicitness; same structural pattern as Chesapeake fisheries past regen).
- Multi-generational food security
- Ecosystem services that can't easily be restored
- Future infrastructure flexibility (alternative uses foreclosed)

**RCV calculation methods** (per the framework's "Three Ways of Counting" — Ch 6 methodology):

1. Discount-rate-based intergenerational pricing (option-value-loss treatment)
2. Replacement-cost analysis (what would replacement of foreclosed capacity cost?)
3. Triangulation across methods

### Step 5: Calculate B — the Foreclosure Bond required

For Amsterdam to accept this deal *honestly*, the organization would need to post a Foreclosure Bond (B₂) sized to RCV. The bond would:

- Price the full foreclosed commons value
- Be posted as collateral, drawable on if costs materialize
- Include monitoring requirements + draw-down triggers
- **Survive corporate dissolution** (per the framework's reform design principles — closes the "form a shell company, extract, dissolve, leave costs behind" loophole)
- Be assessed by **independent third-party evaluators** (not by the organization itself; not by the city's negotiating team — independent capacity)
- Allow **community legal standing** to challenge if costs materialize beyond what's been bonded

### Step 6: Compare deal value to bond requirement

The framework's diagnostic equation: **Cost Severance = RCV − B**.

| Outcome | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Deal price ≥ RCV (Bond fully covers foreclosed value)** | Deal is honestly priced; cost severance = 0; can proceed. |
| **Deal price < RCV (Bond would have to be partial or absent)** | Deal would impose cost severance equal to (RCV − B). Either renegotiate price upward, or reject the deal. The shortfall *is* what would be severed onto Amsterdam. |

The diagnostic is what makes the cost-severance visible. **Without the framework, the deal could close at the headline price; the gap between price and full RCV would silently transfer to Amsterdam.** With the framework, the gap is named and quantified — the city can see exactly what they're agreeing to absorb.

### Step 7: The decision

Amsterdam decides whether to accept. Three branches:

1. **Accept at the bond-included price.** Deal proceeds; bond is posted; cost severance is bound to zero (or near-zero, modulo enforcement risk).
2. **Renegotiate.** Push the organization to increase the price or accept a larger bond, or modify the deal to reduce RCV (narrower scope, shorter duration, more reversible mechanisms).
3. **Reject.** The organization isn't willing to post the bond at full RCV — which reveals they were proposing a cost-severed deal. Walk away.

**The third branch is the framework's most powerful diagnostic.** An organization unwilling to post B = RCV is, by definition, asking for a cost-severed deal. This makes the negotiation leverage transparent.

## What this demonstrates about the framework

The hypothetical confirms three things about Commons Bonds as a forward-looking action-level evaluation tool:

1. **The framework operates as designed in this mode.** The Foreclosure Bond instrument is built precisely for this scenario — pricing what would be foreclosed by a proposed action, binding the counterparty to that price, ensuring the obligation persists past corporate dissolution.

2. **Reclamation bonds in mining are the real-world precedent.** Same structural pattern. Commons Bonds generalizes that pattern beyond mining.

3. **Operational maturity is a real caveat.** The framework provides the *design specification* — RCV calculation methodology, bond-architecture requirements, independent-assessment + community-standing + dissolution-survival requirements. The *tool* itself (specific pricing methodology for a specific context; specific implementation infrastructure) needs to be developed per-context. A city using this framework would still need to (a) adopt the framework, (b) develop the specific pricing methodology for their context, (c) implement the bond posting + enforcement mechanism.

## Why this matters for the Doughnut / Commons Bonds complementarity

The hypothetical also confirms the complementarity-at-different-scales framing captured in FPD §"Canonical framework articulations" → Raworth / Doughnut Economics:

- **Doughnut Economics** sets the goal-state — "stay in the safe and just space between social foundation and ecological ceiling"
- **Commons Bonds** provides the action-level pricing instrument that operates within the goal-state — "evaluate this specific proposed action; price what would be foreclosed; bind the counterparty"

A Doughnut-pursuing city *needs* something like Commons Bonds at the action level. Doughnut articulates that the deal in the hypothetical might push Amsterdam out of the safe and just space (food security threatened; ecological ceiling violated). But Doughnut alone doesn't tell the city how to *evaluate the specific deal* in financial terms, *price what's being foreclosed*, *bind the counterparty to that price*, or *ensure the bond survives corporate dissolution*. Commons Bonds is one of the action-level tools that fills that operational gap.

The "imported / upstream cost severance" challenge is particularly important here: the proposed deal might affect Amsterdam's crop-growing capacity directly (local cost severance), but Amsterdam also imports cost severance through its supply chains (rare earths from Niger Delta; battery materials from extraction zones; construction inputs from globally-extracted resources). Commons Bonds is in principle applicable to both — though the imported version requires international coordination on enforcement (a real operational challenge worth flagging in any framework-positioning conversation).
