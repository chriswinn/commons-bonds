## The framework, in one paragraph

*Commons Bonds* names a structural mechanism — *cost severance* — that separates extracted value from the costs of producing it, transferring those costs to people, ecosystems, and futures who didn't capture the upside. The framework's interest is structural rather than ideological: the same mechanism operates across capitalism, feudalism, colonialism, and command economies, which means the question isn't "what economic system" but "what makes accountability bind." Two contributions. **Cost severance** names what the mechanism does. **Residual commons value (RCV)** names what it costs — the worth permanently foreclosed when a non-renewable resource, or a renewable resource pushed past regenerative capacity, is drawn down. The book's applied cases — including Amsterdam's adoption of the Doughnut framework — are studied as **accountability-instrument architectures**: institutional structures where the binding of cost to actor has gone past values-statement and into operational discipline. Doughnut sets the goal-state; my framework operates one layer in — pricing what would be foreclosed by a specific proposed action and binding the counterparty to that price.

## Questions I'd most value your view on

*Pushback on framework terms is more valuable than confirmation. "This language doesn't quite fit because…" is the highest-leverage thing you can give us — that's what the manuscript can revise before publication.*

1. **Methodology in your own words.** Public-record search surfaced no long-form essay laying out your narrative-translation methodology directly. Could you walk me through how you actually translate an economic framework into something Amsterdammers can imagine themselves inside — and how does **ReFan: Reshaping the Fashion Narrative** compare as a parallel-domain test of the same approach?
2. **Four-lens asymmetry.** The Amsterdam City Doughnut report's Global-Social lens *names* the imported / upstream impact of Amsterdam's consumption (textile labour, mined metals, battery materials). The Coalition's published *what-to-do* vocabulary on that lens reads thinner than the local-side work. Where in your day-to-day is that imported cost severance most visible — and what's the gap between *naming it* and *acting on it*?
3. **Coalition vs. City — when a foreclosure-pattern deal lands.** Suppose Amsterdam were approached with a deal that would leverage water-management infrastructure in a way that could affect crop-growing capacity. Doughnut would flag the threatened social foundation and ecological ceiling. From your network-layer vantage (vs. the City's policy seat held by van Doorninck-then / Koop-now), what tools does the Coalition actually *use* or *push for* at the action level — pricing what's being foreclosed, binding the counterparty, ensuring obligations survive corporate dissolution?
4. **Framework-fit calibration.** Does the framework I'm developing — cost severance + RCV + Foreclosure Bond — fill an action-level accountability gap your implementation has been working around? Or does it duplicate vocabulary you already have under different names — particularly anything regulatory or financial that the public-facing *donutstad / donutpioniers / droom & doorbraak* register doesn't surface?
5. **Reading recommendations.** Beyond DEAL's Doughnut Unrolled, Goodwin's *Five Cities*, and the AUAS executive report — what would you add, particularly on the imported / upstream cost severance challenge, the relationship between narrative-translation and operational policy adoption, and design-fiction methodology?
