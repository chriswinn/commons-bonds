## Section 1 — The community on the water

The community on this water has a name, in the place I am writing from. The neighborhood of Hampton I am closest to is called Fox Hill, and Fox Hill is, or was, an island fishing community — a place where, for most of the twentieth century, almost every family in every house had at least one person on the water. My grandfather worked at NASA Langley — twelve miles or so up the road — building pieces of a different kind of America in a different kind of building, and he was an exception. The neighbors on either side of him were on the water. The men my father grew up with as boys were the sons of watermen. My father, who is from Fox Hill, did not become a waterman, but the men who taught him how to handle a small boat and where the fish were running and what the smell of the channel meant in late August were men whose lives were the water. My family is one generation removed from the working life. We are zero generations removed from the community.

Most days at most hours, the Chesapeake looks like a postcard — flat water, soft light, the kind of bay you would put a child on. Some days it is not. If you have never worked a small boat in the Chesapeake you should know one thing about the water that the watermen know in the way they know their own hands. The Bay is not the ocean. The waves on the Bay are different from the waves on the ocean in a specific way that matters. In the open ocean, a five-foot swell is almost not worth a comment, because the wavelength — the distance and the time between one peak and the next — is long. Twenty seconds between waves on the Atlantic feels like a slow rocking. The Bay does not give you twenty seconds. The Bay's wavelength runs three or four seconds, sometimes less, because the body of water producing the waves is shallow and finite, and the energy in those waves stacks differently than open-ocean energy. A three-foot swell on the Chesapeake can be steeper than a much larger swell on the Atlantic. A Bay chop on a bad afternoon will roll a small boat in ways an oceangoing yacht in much larger seas does not get rolled. The men who have worked this water for forty years know which days are the days you do not go out and which days are the days you can go out but you stay close to home and which days the sky is lying about what the water is going to do in three hours, and they know all of it without having to consult anything external to themselves. The water grants them this knowledge in exchange for the obligation never to mistake which day they are looking at.

What the watermen know is not what someone learns by going out on weekends. It is what someone learns by going out every day for forty years — the sweltering summer days when the heat takes the air; the winter mornings when the gale wind comes off the cape and blows through whatever you are wearing and shakes your very soul. The men go out anyway, because the houses are not paid for and the families do not eat when there is nothing to bring home. You go out early. You stay out until you bring food back.

The community that grows up around that obligation has a particular shape. It is not a metaphor when the watermen of a place like Fox Hill say that they look out for each other. It is a structural condition of the work. A waterman whose nets need rebuilding before the next season is a waterman who needs help rebuilding them, because the nets cannot be done by one man in one season's worth of available time, and there is no industry of net-rebuilders to hire. So a morning is set, and at three or four in the morning, when everyone in this community would normally already be on the water or on the way to it, the boats stay tied up and the men come instead to whoever's place needs the nets. They come in pickups. They bring their own coffee. They work until it is done. The understanding behind the morning is not the understanding of a favor done. It is the understanding that, as surely as the sun rises and the sun sets, there will be a morning when the man who needs the help is you or your son or your father, and the community that fails to come on the day you need it is a community that has already failed before you needed it. That is the bargain, and the bargain holds for as long as the community holds.

The most precise thing the bargain produces, the thing that is hardest to render to anyone who has not been around it, is what happens when one of the men who said he would be there is not. Everyone stops. The nets get put down. Somebody picks up a phone and calls somebody else. Within ten minutes the whole morning has reorganized itself around the question of where the missing man is, because everyone there has done this work for long enough to know that the only thing that keeps a waterman from being where he said he would be is something serious enough that he cannot tell anyone about it. It might be his truck. It might be his health. It might be — in the older men's bodies, which carry this possibility the way a cook's body carries the possibility of burns — that he was on the water at four-thirty and did not come back, and the water is the part of the morning the rest of the men have to go check on first.

This is the community Fox Hill was for most of my father's life, and the community I have watched, in my own life, slowly thin.

## Section 2 — The man called Biggie

One of the oystermen my father knew, growing up on Fox Hill, was a man called Biggie. Biggie had the build of an oak — not tall, in the way some big men are not tall, but solid through the shoulders and the back in a way that registered as the build of a man who had been doing one specific kind of work for most of his adult life. He worked oysters. He had been working oysters since he was old enough to be useful in a boat with whoever brought him into one. The bars he worked were bars he had worked his entire life. The boat he ran was a boat suited to the work. He went out before the day. He came back when the bushels were what the bushels were going to be that day. You could watch Biggie work his bars with one oar between the beds, the kind of effortlessness that has no wasted motion in it, and you could see immediately that his family had been doing this for generations.

Biggie carried, every morning he went out, a six-pack of Coca-Cola onto the boat. He did not carry a lunch. The first time my father asked him about the lunch — my father was a small boy then, still young enough to be honestly puzzled about it — Biggie told him that his lunch was waiting for him in the water. Once the sun was high enough in the day and the work had hit its rhythm, Biggie would settle into eating oysters as he worked. He had a particular spot on the boat, near his body, where he would set the oyster he was about to shuck. He did not have to reach. He did not have to lift his arm. The motion he used to shuck was the motion of a man who had reduced the work to its smallest possible movement after a working life of doing it — a quarter turn of the wrist, the shell parting, the meat coming up clean. He ate as he worked. My father watched him as a boy, more than once, and tried to count how many oysters Biggie put away in a single day on the water. My father told me, when I was old enough to ask, that he had never managed to keep count. Not because there were too many to count, although there may have been. Because the eating was so woven into the work that there was never a single moment when an oyster started or stopped being eaten — it was simply something Biggie was doing alongside everything else he was doing, the way breathing is something a person is doing alongside everything else.

Biggie has been dead for more than thirty years. He died before Virginia stopped its commercial shad fishery in 1994, which is a date the men still on the water sometimes use as a marker for when things were still working. He went out with what an earlier generation of men had gone out with. He came back with what they had come back with. The bars sounded under his tongs the way the bars had sounded under the tongs of the men before him.

The bars do not sound that way anymore. The motion of the wrist that Biggie's body had reduced to its smallest possible movement is a motion that no younger man at Fox Hill is being shown how to make, because no younger man at Fox Hill is going out to bars that would let him learn it. The motion is dying with the men who hold it.

## Section 3 — Phat [ABOUT YOU]

There is a man I will call Phat, which is the short form of his given name, and I have not yet asked him whether he wants to be called something else in a book. Phat is alive. He is a large man, not tall, who has been on the Bay since he was a boy. He is from the same Fox Hill world Biggie was from, and his work and his life have unfolded in the next stretch of the same arc.

Phat is a crabber and a fisherman, and he has been one all his working life. The water near Fox Hill and Poquoson is the water he has always known. What he has watched, across his decades on it, is that the math for a man like him has changed. The catches have come back thinner. The costs of fuel and gear and the slip have moved in the other direction. The species he came up working are not all of them still running. None of these are observations particular to Phat; they are the conditions every man still working this water has had to absorb in his own way. What is particular to Phat is what he has done with the absorption.

He still gets up at two or three in the morning. He still goes out and pulls his crab pots. He runs them the way he ran them when he was younger, except the math at the end of the day is different now than it was then. So he comes back to the dock by mid-morning. He hoses down the boat. He goes to the mechanic shop he owns, on the Peninsula, where his name is on the sign and his crab pots are stacked behind the locked fence in the back lot, behind the cars he is working on for customers. He works the shop until five or six in the evening. What he calls this is between him and the people who know him. What it does, in any case, is the work that keeps the family math balancing and the boat in the slip and the license current and the presence on the water continuing.

Phat did not quit the water. He could not. He has spent decades building, on his own time and his own dime, the second economy that has allowed him to not quit it. The accounting that made the older men's lives possible — the accounting in which the crab and the boat and the long days were enough — does not work for him. He has filled the gap with the only thing he had left to fill it with: more of himself.

I have not asked Phat what he would say about all this, and the chapter does not have his answer to put on this page. What I can guess, from watching him and from knowing the men around him, is that if asked over a beer at the end of a long day he would say he is glad to still be on the water and that he is grateful for the shop. The accounting this book is trying to make visible is not, necessarily, the accounting Phat would put on himself. It is what the surrounding economy has been accepting from Phat, and from many men like Phat up and down this Bay, in exchange for a continued supply of the seafood the surrounding economy has not stopped wanting.
