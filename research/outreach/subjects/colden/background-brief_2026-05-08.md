## 1. Allison Colden — role, bio, tenure

**Current title.** Maryland Executive Director, Chesapeake Bay Foundation (CBF), since **April 2023**. Announced via CBF press release ([CBF newsroom 2023](https://www.cbf.org/news-media/newsroom/2023/maryland/allison-colden-named-chesapeake-bay-foundations-maryland-executive-director.html); see also [Baltimore Fishbowl coverage](https://baltimorefishbowl.com/stories/chesapeake-bay-foundation-names-fisheries-scientist-allison-colden-to-be-its-next-maryland-executive-director/); [Southern Maryland Chronicle 2023-04-25](https://southernmarylandchronicle.com/2023/04/25/chesapeake-bay-foundation-names-dr-allison-colden-as-maryland-executive-director/); [Eye on Annapolis](https://www.eyeonannapolis.net/2023/05/allison-colden-named-cbfs-maryland-executive-director/); [Talbot Spy](https://talbotspy.org/allison-colden-named-chesapeake-bay-foundations-maryland-executive-director/)). Reports up through CBF SVP for Programs Alison Prost to President & CEO Hilary Harp Falk.

**Prior CBF role.** Maryland Senior Fisheries Scientist, **2017–2023** (six years). In that role she oversaw CBF's Maryland oyster restoration program (tens of millions of oysters per year added to sanctuary reefs) and built the body of testimony catalogued below.

**Pre-CBF career path.**
- **Senior Manager of External Affairs, Restore America's Estuaries** (the immediate pre-CBF post; ~2016–2017).
- **John A. Knauss Marine Policy Fellow, U.S. House of Representatives** (2015–2016 cohort; placed in the office of California Congressman **Mike Thompson**, where she covered climate, ocean policy, fisheries, natural resources, public lands, and crude-by-rail) ([Virginia Sea Grant: Knauss Fellow profile](https://vaseagrant.org/knauss-fellow-habitat-restoration-water-allison-colden/)).
- **Ph.D. in Marine Sciences, Virginia Institute of Marine Science (VIMS) / William & Mary, 2015** (advised by Rom Lipcius). Dissertation on oyster reef restoration — the substantive empirical work later anchoring her policy authority on oysters ([VIMS top story 2017 on her tipping-point paper](https://www.vims.edu/newsandevents/topstories/2017/oyster_tip.php)).
- **B.S. in Biology, University of Virginia, 2009** ([Virginia Sea Grant Knauss profile](https://vaseagrant.org/knauss-fellow-habitat-restoration-water-allison-colden/)).

**Geographic / institutional positioning within CBF.** Annapolis-based (CBF Maryland HQ at the Philip Merrill Environmental Center). Maryland office is the largest of CBF's regional offices and historically the one closest to the legislative-and-fisheries-management center of gravity for the Bay (MD General Assembly, MD DNR, ASMFC seat). Colden is one of three regional EDs (Maryland, Virginia, Pennsylvania) plus a Federal Director and Hampton Roads sub-regional director. See `research/outreach/subjects/cbf/organizational-brief_2026-05-06.md` §1 for the broader org map.

**Personal-narrative texture.** Limited public-record voice on personal background. The [Chestertown Spy interview (2023-05-29)](https://chestertownspy.org/2023/05/29/meet-the-chesapeake-bay-foundations-new-maryland-director-allison-colden/) (also republished by [Talbot Spy](https://talbotspy.org/meet-the-chesapeake-bay-foundations-new-maryland-director-allison-colden/); video on [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CxlJgvK3uCQ)) is the closest thing to a profile interview, and it focuses on her Bay-policy substance (invasive blue catfish, science-into-policy translation) rather than personal narrative.

## 2. Signature work + methodological orientation

### 2a. Doctoral / academic publications (peer-reviewed)

Three peer-reviewed papers carry the science-credentials weight:

1. **Colden AM, Lipcius RN (2015).** *Lethal and sublethal effects of sediment burial on the eastern oyster Crassostrea virginica.* Marine Ecology Progress Series 527: 105–117. ([Inter-Research abstract](https://www.int-res.com/abstracts/meps/v527/meps11244)).
2. **Colden AM, Fall KA, Cartwright GM et al. (2016).** *Sediment Suspension and Deposition Across Restored Oyster Reefs of Varying Orientation to Flow: Implications for Restoration.* Estuaries and Coasts 39: 1435–1448. ([Springer link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12237-016-0096-y)).
3. **Colden AM, Latour RJ, Lipcius RN (2017).** *Reef height drives threshold dynamics of restored oyster reefs.* Marine Ecology Progress Series 582: 1–13. ([Inter-Research abstract](https://www.int-res.com/abstracts/meps/v582/meps12362); [W&M ScholarWorks PDF link](https://scholarworks.wm.edu/vimsarticles/543/); [VIMS news writeup](https://www.vims.edu/newsandevents/topstories/2017/oyster_tip.php); [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2017/11/171113144209.htm); [Phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2017-11-oyster.html)). **Most-cited / featured paper** — establishes a 0.3 m (≈1 ft) tipping-point threshold for restored oyster reef survival; reefs built taller develop into self-sustaining ecosystems with oyster densities 3.5x greater than lower reefs, while lower reefs are buried by sediment.

The 2017 *MEPS* paper is **load-bearing for her authority on oyster restoration policy** — the empirical case for the Maryland tributary-scale sanctuaries rests in part on threshold-dynamics work she co-authored. When she defends sanctuaries in legislative testimony, she is in part defending the operationalization of her own science.

[Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com/citations?hl=en&user=wICeOs0AAAAJ); [ResearchGate profile](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Allison-Colden); also a co-author on at least one earlier conference contribution on reef structure–flow interactions ([ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/261323477_INTERACTIONS_OF_REEF_STRUCTURE_WITH_LOCAL_FLOW_ENVIRONMENTS_IMPLICATIONS_FOR_RESTORATION)).

### 2e. Methodological orientation — fisheries-scientist register vs. policy-advocate register

Colden's signature is the **fisheries-scientist-as-policy-advocate** register: she presents science findings as the load-bearing premise, then derives policy positions from them. Her CBF press statements, op-eds, and legislative testimony all anchor first in stock-assessment numbers, juvenile-recruitment surveys, or empirical reef-threshold dynamics, then move to "what science indicates is warranted."

A characteristic articulation, on menhaden: *"Just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should, we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that that's warranted"* ([WMRA 2025-10-29](https://www.wmra.org/2025-10-29/menhaden-board-cuts-amount-harvesters-can-catch-along-the-atlantic-next-year)). The grammar — *the science indicates that that's warranted* — is the signature: she positions herself as a translator-of-science-into-action rather than as an interest-group advocate. **This is the register the framework's vocabulary needs to land inside.** "Cost severance" needs to register as describing what her science is already telling her; not as an external philosophical lens being imposed.

She is not a polemicist. Colden's public voice is **measured, technical, precautionary**, with the precautionary principle ("err on the side of conservation") functioning as her default fallback when the science is incomplete. She does not throw rhetorical rocks at watermen, regulators, or industry — even when she opposes their positions — and she preserves the institutional civility CBF as an organization needs to keep working with everyone in the Bay management ecosystem. **Plan for an interview register that mirrors hers**: declarative, science-first, careful with attributions of motive.

## 3. Stance on key Bay issues — striped bass, oysters, menhaden, climate

### 3a. Striped bass — "looming disaster" / "looming crisis"

**Position.** Maryland's striped bass population is in active collapse and ASMFC's regulatory architecture has consistently failed to act on the warning signs. CBF urged ASMFC to adopt a 14% coastwide reduction for the 2025 season; was disappointed by ASMFC's October 2025 deferral; the November 2025 ASMFC vote ultimately *stood pat* on stripers while making modest menhaden cuts ([Southern Maryland Chronicle 2025-11-03](https://southernmarylandchronicle.com/2025/11/03/asmfc-approves-modest-menhaden-cuts-stands-pat-on-striped-bass/)).

**Recommended actions:** Coastwide harvest reductions (14%); Maryland-side emergency action (cancel spring trophy season, increase spawning-population protections — Colden was a vocal supporter of the November 2023 MD DNR emergency regulations); ASMFC Amendment 7-style regulation that more aggressively responds to recruitment failure.

**Anchor quote — "looming disaster".** *"Maryland's striped bass surveys have made it clear — we're facing a looming disaster. Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future. Additional regulations will have real impacts, but they pale in comparison to the threat of continued striped bass decline. ASMFC must err on the side of conservation."* (Captured in `research/literature/bibliography.md` §19.5; aligns with [CBF press 2025 "Striped Bass Fishery Plan Aims to Address Looming Crisis"](https://www.cbf.org/news/striped-bass-fishery-plan-aims-to-address-looming-crisis/) and the August 2025 statement reiterated in [Outdoor News](https://www.outdoornews.com/2025/08/25/striped-bass-fishery-plan-to-address-looming-crisis/).)

**On the 2024 juvenile recruitment survey** (Maryland: 2.0 fish/sample vs. long-term avg of 11.0; Virginia: 3.43 vs. 7.77): *"Many people in Maryland rely on striped bass for their food, their businesses, and their futures. Without action, that will all be at risk"* ([Southern Maryland Chronicle 2024-10-18](https://southernmarylandchronicle.com/2024/10/18/striped-bass-numbers-in-chesapeake-bay-continue-to-decline-in-2024/)). She also flagged: *"The annual survey of juvenile striped bass in Chesapeake Bay gives us a sense of what the future will look like. With these latest results, that picture is bleak"* ([same source](https://southernmarylandchronicle.com/2024/10/18/striped-bass-numbers-in-chesapeake-bay-continue-to-decline-in-2024/)).

**Bluntness register from Bay Journal:** *"there are very few levers left to pull"* — quoted in Bay Journal coverage of ASMFC striper deferrals ([Bay Journal 2025-08](https://www.bayjournal.com/news/fisheries/more-catch-restrictions-due-in-2026-to-help-struggling-striped-bass/article_6ddce63f-3e89-48bd-a805-4a0bf7e436dc.html); cross-confirmed via `research/literature/bibliography.md` §19.5). Pre-2024-stock-assessment anticipation: *"A lot of people are really holding their breath for this 2024 stock assessment"* (search-summary attribution; verbatim flagged provisional pending direct source).

**Climate-fisheries interplay surfaced in striper context.** Colden has been on record that *"rising water temperatures and low oxygen levels — both exacerbated by climate change — are pummeling striped bass in the Bay during summer"* (search-summary attribution from the [CBF Save-the-Bay blog "What Comes Next for Striped Bass" Nov 2024](https://www.cbf.org/blogs/save-the-bay/2024/11/what-comes-next-for-striped-bass.html); 403-blocked from direct fetch).

### 3b. Oysters — sanctuaries, aquaculture-vs-wild-harvest, restoration

**Position cluster.** (1) The five tributary-scale oyster restoration sanctuaries (Harris Creek, Little Choptank, Tred Avon, St. Mary's, Manokin) are *the* model of what works in the Bay; defending them legislatively and in OAC proceedings is load-bearing for her work. (2) Aquaculture is part of the solution, not the problem; proposals that pit watermen against aquaculturists are typically structured to shift restoration ground back into the wild-harvest column. (3) The recent record harvests are *evidence the sanctuaries are working*, not evidence the sanctuaries should be opened.

**Anchor quote — sanctuaries are working.** *"Large-scale oyster restoration is working. In Maryland, the oyster population has tripled since 2005."* ([Maryland DNR press 2025-08-26](https://news.maryland.gov/dnr/2025/08/26/governor-wes-moore-announces-completion-of-marylands-five-tributary-scale-oyster-restoration-sanctuaries/); [Southern Maryland Chronicle 2025-08-27](https://southernmarylandchronicle.com/2025/08/27/maryland-meets-oyster-restoration-goal-with-manokin-river-completion/)). *"Oysters are the bedrock of our ecosystem and are key to adapting to climate change, building resilient shorelines, improving water quality, and supporting local economies. An investment in oyster restoration is an investment in a healthier Chesapeake Bay and more vibrant communities."* ([CBF press release on additional large-scale restoration commitment](https://www.cbf.org/news-media/newsroom/2024/maryland/maryland-commits-to-additional-large-scale-oyster-restoration.html), 403-blocked from direct fetch).

**Anchor quote — sanctuaries are not yet finished work.** *"Our effort to bring oysters back from the brink in Chesapeake Bay is working, but continued recovery is not guaranteed. Oyster sanctuaries are likely significantly contributing to the overall abundance of oysters in Maryland."* ([CBF Maryland Commits to Additional Large-Scale Oyster Restoration](https://www.cbf.org/news/maryland-commits-to-additional-large-scale-oyster-restoration/)). Closing the Manokin marks *"the beginning of the next phase of oyster restoration"* (Aug 2025).

**Anchor quote — aquaculture-vs-wild-harvest, on the 2020 DNR PSFA proposal.** *"This proposal undermines the process put in place by the legislature to implement actions to increase the oyster population and end overfishing."* ([Chesapeake Bay Magazine, Nov 2020](https://www.chesapeakebaymagazine.com/md-oyster-fishery-proposal-stirs-up-controversy/); [CBF press release Nov 2020](https://www.cbf.org/news-media/newsroom/2020/maryland/cbf-opposes-maryland-proposal-to-limit-areas-for-oyster-restoration-and-aquaculture.html); [Delmarva Fisheries Association mirror](https://delmarvafisheries.org/cbf-opposes-maryland-proposal-to-limit-areas-for-oyster-restoration/)). Characterized the PSFA proposal as *"a one-sided proposal to increase the oyster harvest at the expense of restoration and aquaculture efforts that are helping to bring Maryland's oysters back"* (same sources). The framing — *the spirit of the law*, *undermines the process* — is a distinctive Colden signature: she frames bad policy not as morally wrong but as **subverting institutional commitments already made**.

**Anchor quote — Seafood Watch/aquaculture.** *"We have long recommended that consumers choose farmed oysters from the Chesapeake Bay, because that eliminates any possibility of concerns about sustainability or about oyster recovery."* ([Maryland Matters May 2023](https://marylandmatters.org/2023/05/27/consumer-guide-criticized-for-saying-avoid-chesapeake-oysters/); [Bay Journal](https://www.bayjournal.com/news/fisheries/consumer-guide-criticized-for-saying-avoid-chesapeake-oysters/article_ee8b2426-f99d-11ed-b5d5-07b35f3a2b81.html)). Note the politically interesting move: she **pushed back on Seafood Watch's "avoid" rating for Chesapeake wild oysters** (alongside scientists, watermen, and other groups), while simultaneously aligning with Seafood Watch's *farmed* oyster recommendation. This is consistent with her aquaculture-and-sanctuaries-yes, wild-harvest-needs-better-management overall position.

**SB 922 (2024) — sanctuary boundary protection.** SB 922 would have repealed the prohibition on DNR altering existing oyster sanctuary boundaries until DNR adopts an updated oyster management plan. Colden submitted unfavorable testimony (Feb 27, 2024). The full text of her UNF testimony is 403-blocked but is referenced in `research/literature/bibliography.md` §19.5. Note: per the original outreach response, the "five large-scale oyster restoration sanctuaries" framing combines her **2019** advocacy work (the 2019 General Assembly bills permanently protecting the five sanctuaries — referenced in the [CBF appointment press release](https://www.cbf.org/news-media/newsroom/2023/maryland/allison-colden-named-chesapeake-bay-foundations-maryland-executive-director.html)) with her **2024** SB 922 UNF testimony defending those same sanctuaries against attempts to reopen the boundaries question. **The "permanently protected" claim is grounded in 2019; the "still defending the protections" claim is grounded in 2024.**

**OysterFutures / Little Choptank — the nuanced position.** Colden participated in a stakeholder consensus process (OysterFutures) that included permitting some hand-tonging in formerly off-limits Little Choptank sanctuary areas, but countered that DNR's management plan *"cherry-picks"* the OysterFutures recommendations most favored by watermen while *"ignoring or glossing over"* others (notably stronger anti-poaching enforcement). She agreed to opening that sanctuary section *only* if combined with completion of the full large-scale restoration originally planned in the Little Choptank ([Bay Journal](https://www.bayjournal.com/article/comments_sought_as_dnr_lawmakers_weigh_options_for_managing_marylands_oyste); also referenced in [Maryland Matters Jan 2022](https://marylandmatters.org/2022/01/18/after-two-years-consensus-on-oyster-policy-still-elusive-in-maryland/)). **The "cherry-picks" framing is a distinctive Colden quotable** — it's her sharpest public characterization of regulatory selective implementation.

**On record harvest concerns (the Bay Journal 2023 coverage).** *Without adequate controls on fishing effort, increased harvests could contribute to declines in the population when conditions in the Bay affect oyster reproduction.* ([Bay Journal "Amid oyster bounty, Maryland worries about overfishing"](https://www.bayjournal.com/news/fisheries/amid-oyster-bounty-maryland-worries-about-overfishing-eyes-harvest-limits/article_e3b6d802-0bb7-11ee-bf2e-8bae333da9e9.html)). Paraphrase, not direct quote.

### 3c. Menhaden — Bay cap, industrial reduction fishery, ASMFC seat

**Position cluster.** (1) The 51,000 metric tons Atlantic Menhaden Fishery Management Plan **Bay cap** — established by ASMFC in November 2017 on Colden's original motion — is the floor for menhaden management inside the Bay, not the ceiling. (2) Industrial reduction fishing inside the Bay should be paused until science is complete enough to ensure sustainability. (3) Maryland-side advocacy is calibrated on the precautionary principle, not on industry capacity.

**The 2017 motion — historical anchor.** Colden, then CBF MD Senior Fisheries Scientist, made the original motion that became the 51,000 MT Chesapeake Bay cap at the November 2017 ASMFC Menhaden Management Board meeting (CBF and third-party reporting, captured at `research/literature/bibliography.md` §19.5; cross-confirmed via search-result summaries of [CBF "Atlantic Menhaden Are in Jeopardy Again"](https://www.cbf.org/blogs/save-the-bay/2018/01/atlantic-menhaden-are-in-jeopardy-again-what-does-it-mean-for-stripers.html) and [CBF "Celebrating a Menhaden Milestone"](https://www.cbf.org/blogs/save-the-bay/2020/05/celebrating-a-menhaden-milestone.html)).

**Anchor quote — the science-symmetry framing.** *"Just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should, we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that that's warranted."* ([WMRA 2025-10-29 / WVTF coverage of ASMFC Oct 2025 board action](https://www.wmra.org/2025-10-29/menhaden-board-cuts-amount-harvesters-can-catch-along-the-atlantic-next-year)). **This is a methodologically precise framing of CBF's position on menhaden TAC** — the symmetry-of-deference move is what distinguishes Colden's voice from a generic environmental-NGO voice.

**On the substitute motion at the ASMFC board (Oct 2025).** Colden offered *"to establish a board work group to consider and evaluate options for further precautionary management of Chesapeake Bay menhaden fisheries, including time and area closures, to be protective of piscivorous birds and fish during critical points of their life cycle"* ([ASMFC press release on the November 2025 board action](https://asmfc.org/news/press-releases/asmfc-atlantic-menhaden-board-reduces-2026-tac-by-20-and-initiates-addendum-for-chesapeake-bay-cap/)). The Nov 2025 ASMFC board ultimately reduced 2026 TAC by 20% and initiated an addendum on the Chesapeake Bay cap.

**Sept 2025 Bay-pause call.** CBF formally called for a pause on industrial reduction fishing inside the Bay until the science is complete enough to ensure sustainability ([CBF "Time to Pause Menhaden Fishing in the Chesapeake Bay" — Sept 2025 blog post, 403-blocked direct fetch](https://www.cbf.org/blogs/save-the-bay/2025/09/time-to-pause-menhaden-fishing-in-the-chesapeake-bay.html); also [CBF "Virginia Could Push Pause on Industrial Menhaden Fishing"](https://www.cbf.org/news/virginia-could-push-pause-on-industrial-menhaden-fishing-in-chesapeake-bay/)). VA-side legislative bills (Sen. Marsden's SB474 menhaden science fund; Del. Carr's HB1048 pause; HB1049 observer/seasonal limits) all blocked in the 2025 and 2026 VA General Assembly sessions per `research/outreach/subjects/cbf/organizational-brief_2026-05-06.md` §5.

### 3d. Climate-fisheries intersection

**Position.** Climate change is **an across-the-board pressure multiplier**, not a separate issue category. Colden frames climate as compounding existing fisheries-management failures — temperature increases hit striped bass during summer; sea-level rise / shoreline erosion intersect with oyster restoration as both a problem (loss) and an asset (oyster reefs as living shoreline protection); blue-crab declines are linked to "changing conditions in the Bay."

**Blue crab anchor quote.** *"The red flags are flying for blue crabs."* And: *"With more than five years of below average crab numbers, it is clear that changing conditions in the Bay are undermining the current management of this important species."* ([CBF "Chesapeake Bay Blue Crab Population Drops to Distressing Low"](https://www.cbf.org/news/chesapeake-bay-blue-crab-population-drops-to-distressing-low/); [Maryland Matters May 2025](https://marylandmatters.org/2025/05/23/blue-crab-population-second-lowest-2025/)). ## 7. Where the framework intersects Colden's work

### 7a. Renewable-past-regeneration

**Where Colden is on record about regenerative-capacity threshold crossings.** Two anchor points:

1. **Striped bass — "looming disaster" framing.** *Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future.* This is, in framework vocabulary, a *species-specific RCV claim* — that the regeneration capacity has been crossed (or is being crossed) and the foreclosed future value is the loss of a fishery generations have depended on. Colden has not used the word "regeneration" or "threshold" in public-record statements (though her academic work on oyster reef *thresholds* shows she is fluent in the concept); the threshold idea is **implicit** in her policy framing.
2. **Blue crab — "changing conditions are undermining current management."** This is the closest she gets in public-record language to **the framework's claim that the regulatory architecture is structurally lagging the science**. The science (stock assessments, juvenile recruitment indices) keeps surfacing red flags; the regulatory architecture (ASMFC, DNR) keeps not matching the response to the signal. Colden names this gap in operational terms; the framework names it structurally.

**Where the framework's vocabulary may travel.** The 0.3 m oyster-reef threshold from her 2017 paper is a clean species-level analog of the framework's *renewable-past-regeneration threshold* construct. There is room to ask her whether the threshold construct generalizes from reef structure to species-level fishery outcomes — and whether she'd accept that some Bay species (oysters historically; arguably stripers now) have crossed the threshold and others (blue crabs) are being tested at it.

### 7b. Cost severance

**The Bay-and-watermen-as-cost-bearers framing.** Colden's clearest articulation of cost-bearing language is in the oyster disaster context — she has been on record that *watermen* are absorbing the cost of *changing conditions* and *market competition* even as the oyster *population* recovers. The 2026 oyster paradox (growing oyster population alongside watermen suffering) is the cleanest Colden-articulated Bay analog of cost severance: **the upstream conditions that produced the situation are not paying the downstream price; watermen are.** This is structurally the framework's claim, but Colden frames it in livelihood-economics terms rather than in extraction-regime terms.

**Where divergence may be productive.** The framework's *cost severance* construct names the *systematic* gap between who profits and who pays. Colden's framing names *distributional* outcomes (watermen suffering; populations recovering) but typically does not point fingers at specific upstream cost-creators. Her professional position (CBF must work with everyone in the Bay management ecosystem) means she will likely be cautious about the framework's language if it reads as locating *blame* rather than *structural cost-flow*. **The interview opportunity is to test whether the framework's vocabulary lands as describing what she is already saying, or as imposing a register she would not adopt.**

### 7c. Foreclosure Bond / forward-looking accountability

**Where Colden is on record about accountability mechanisms.** She has not, in public record, used the language of *bonds*, *escrows*, *foreclosure*, or *accountability instruments* in the framework's sense. Her accountability vocabulary is *regulatory* — quotas, sanctuaries, science-based TACs, precautionary-management work groups. The closest framework-adjacent move in her record is the **menhaden science fund** advocacy (SB474) — a *funding* instrument explicitly tied to the *information* gap that the regulatory architecture currently has. **This is the closest analog in her current operational toolkit to a framework-style instrument that prices in the cost of decision-making under science gaps.**

The Foreclosure Bond as a *generalization* of the science-fund logic — the entity benefiting from the activity posts a bond against the cost of decision-making under uncertainty — may have purchase, but would need careful translation. **High-value interview question.**

### 7d. Generational accountability

**Where she is on record about generational cost-bearing.** Three direct hits:
1. *"Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future."* (Striped bass.)
2. *"...so that the next generation of blue crabs, watermen, and Virginians can enjoy blue crabs in the future."* (Blue crab.) **The grouping — blue crabs, watermen, Virginians, future generations — is itself a framework-aligned articulation; it ties species, livelihoods, polity, and time horizon into one accountability frame.**

The vocabulary is mostly *future-tense forward-looking* ("won't have...in the future") rather than *backward-and-forward-looking* (what's already been lost; what is still being foreclosed). **The interview can productively probe both edges.**

## 10. Substitution-analysis judgment

**How rich is the public record on Colden's positions?** **Rich.** The 2026-05-08 sweep surfaced 26 distinct quotable items spanning striped bass, oysters, menhaden, blue crabs, climate, regulatory architecture, generational framing, and bipartisan-policy register. **Could Ch 3 be drafted from public-record material alone?** **Yes — but with two important caveats.**
