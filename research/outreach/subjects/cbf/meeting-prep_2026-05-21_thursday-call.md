## §3. How the framework aligns with CBF's work (talking points)

**Pre-articulated for vocabulary calibration moments. Lead with whichever lands most naturally; don't recite serially.**

### Alignment Point 1 — "Cost severance" names what CBF documents constantly

CBF has been telling this story for decades, in different words, case by case:
- Omega Protein extracts menhaden → costs (osprey, stripers, blue crab, watermen) borne by Bay communities + ecosystem
- Upstream agriculture pollutes → costs borne by Bay-side communities downstream
- Historic over-harvesting → costs borne by present-day watermen who can't make a living
- 1980s striped-bass collapse → cost borne by 1980s-1990s watermen; recovery shared; current contemporary crisis is its own iteration

"Cost severance" provides a **single structural vocabulary** for what CBF currently names case-by-case. If it catches on in advocacy, CBF policy staff have a clean shorthand for the structural argument across testimony + media.

### Alignment Point 2 — "RCV" / renewable-past-regeneration captures Moore's "structurally different now" thesis

Moore on the public record (Feb 2026 VA Mercury): *"It is obvious that conditions have changed and striped bass will not have the same recovery they had in the 1980s."*

The framework's *residual commons value* names what's foreclosed when the threshold is crossed — the prior recovery dynamic is not coming back. **This is the analytic vocabulary for what Moore is already saying.** The chapter's thesis is that the Bay has crossed renewable-past-regeneration in some species (Moore's stripers claim is the load-bearing case); the framework gives CBF a way to talk about that structurally rather than as one-off observation.

### Alignment Point 3 — "Regulatory architecture lagging the science" = Moore's "Bay cap from historic landings" critique

Moore on the public record: *"We have a Bay cap that's based on historic landings."*

The framework names this as a **structural feature of accountability instruments built before the biology was understood** — not accidental, not fixable by good intentions, requires architectural rebuild.

Colden's parallel critique on stripers + oyster sanctuaries + ASMFC deferrals shows the same pattern.

**Framework value:** CBF can keep making the case case-by-case; the framework provides an architecture for arguing the *pattern* is structural, not species-specific.

### Alignment Point 4 — Two-instrument bond decomposition mirrors CBF's own advocacy tools

| CBF tool | Framework analog |
|---|---|
| Oyster sanctuaries (SB 922; 2019 legislation; five large-scale tributaries) | **Foreclosure Bond** — protected against extraction; symmetry binding |
| Watershed Agreement 2040 targets | **Restitution Bond** — accountability toward future state |
| Bay cap (when properly bio-grounded) | Structural accountability instrument (currently mis-built per Moore's critique) |
| Restoration targets across species | Restitution architecture |

Sanctuaries-as-regulatory-failure-insurance is one of the chapter's threads — Colden may push back on this read; that pushback is welcome. (See §4 on the chapter's openness to her revision.)

## §4. Why CBF has vested interest in working with us (talking points)

**Don't deliver this serially — pull on these if/when momentum stalls or when they need explicit reasons to invest staff time.**

1. **External vocabulary amplification.** A trade-press book + national essays (Noema / Aeon / Atlantic / Boston Review / Phenomenal World — all in flight) lift CBF's structural argument out of regional press and into national audiences CBF can't reach directly.

2. **Their voices get cited with attribution they're comfortable with.** The Q0 attribution menu gives Colden + Moore (and any program staff who interview) full control: freely-quotable / paraphrase-with-review / transcript-review-before-quoting. Author abides. No surprises.

3. **The framework respects their institutional discipline.** No Soviet / cross-system framing in CBF-facing material. Bay-specific terms. Calibrated political register (industry-blocks-science, not industry-villain; erosion-and-shoreline, not climate-rhetoric-forward). CBF's working relationships across the politically heterogeneous watershed are not put at risk.

4. **Working-waterfront communities are protagonists, not bystanders.** Watermen voice is structurally load-bearing for the chapter. This matches CBF's Port Isobel + Tangier + Smith Island commitments. The chapter doesn't pose restoration work against watermen; both bear cost-severance externalities.

5. **Public-record positioning is explicit and honoring.** The chapter is already ~70-80% draftable from Colden's + Moore's public record alone (per the substitution-analysis in the prep stack). Author has been honest about that. Live conversation is calibration, not extraction. CBF leadership is not asked to carry the chapter on their backs.

6. **Vocabulary calibration is bi-directional.** The framework expects pushback. Where it overreaches on Bay terrain, the manuscript revises before publication. CBF input shapes the published version, not just the cited material.

7. **Long-term ally cultivation.** If the chapter + derivative essays land well, CBF gains an articulate external advocate who has been disciplined into their calibration register. Future research, advocacy, joint work all become easier.

8. **The book's success criterion is vocabulary adoption.** If *cost severance* / *RCV* enter the wider advocacy vocabulary, CBF has a more legible structural framing for the work they're already doing. That's a small operational win at low cost.
