> Dear Val and David,

> **The framework, in one paragraph**
>
> *Commons Bonds* names a structural mechanism operating across the full extraction spectrum — from non-renewable resources (coal, oil, minerals) elsewhere in the book to renewable resources pushed past regenerative capacity (Chesapeake fisheries, oyster reefs, forest systems). In each case, value is extracted while the costs are deliberately severed from the entity that profited and transferred to others — communities, ecosystems, future generations. Two contributions. **Cost severance** is the structural act that cuts profit from the cost of producing it. **Residual commons value (RCV)** is the worth permanently foreclosed when a renewable resource is pushed past regenerative capacity, or a non-renewable resource is drawn down. The book's renewable-past-regeneration chapter — Chapter 3 — argues the Bay is a regulatory architecture that has consistently lagged the science, with the menhaden Bay-cap question and the contemporary striped-bass crisis as the load-bearing cases.
>
> ---
>
> **Chapter 3 — brief outline**
>
> 1. **Hampton-harbor opening.** Daily-visible watermen from the sailboat; dockmasters as mentors; the harbor-at-dawn texture that grounds the chapter in personal geography rather than visiting-academic register.
> 2. **One or two watermen at depth.** Named (with consent) or anonymized (with care) — multi-generational ties to the Bay; the chapter follows the people before it follows the structure. (This is the load-bearing voice need; CBF-facilitated introductions to working or retired watermen would carry significant weight here.)
> 3. **The generational arc.** What grandfathers fished, what fathers fished, what children fish (if they fish at all) — the fishery across three generations as the chapter's temporal spine.
> 4. **The specific moment the water changed.** The remembered year, the named event, a species' last good season — institutional acknowledgment vs. lived knowledge.
> 5. **Renewable-past-regeneration as undertone.** Oyster collapse, blue crab cycles, striped bass, the menhaden Bay-cap question — woven through the watermen stories without framework vocabulary in-prose; the reader recognizes the structural pattern without being told.
> 6. **Working-waterfront collapse and what was asked vs. paid back.** Tangier, Smith Island, the Hampton working waterfront — specific places where the working-waterfront economy thinned; what watermen and the Bay's ecology absorbed.
> 7. **The closing tie-back.** A single line that names the structural mechanism without theorizing it — *different resource, different geography, same pattern* as the miner case the prior chapter develops.
>
> Target length 5,000–6,000 words. Pure storytelling register; framework vocabulary lands briefly only at the closing tie-back.
>
> ---
>
> **Questions I'd most value Dr. Colden's view on**
>
> Calibrated to her public record (striped bass "looming disaster" framing; defense of the five large-scale oyster sanctuaries / SB 922 / 2019 legislation; menhaden Bay cap; ASMFC and Maryland Oyster Advisory Commission vantage).
>
> 1. The framework's central vocabulary — *cost severance* and *residual commons value* — names the gap between what fisheries science said the Bay could sustain and what regulatory and political processes actually permitted. Where does that vocabulary do work your existing fisheries-policy vocabulary doesn't, and where does it overreach or underreach on Bay-specific terrain?
>
> 2. CBF has been on record repeatedly that ASMFC and DNR have failed to act on red flags — striped bass, oyster sanctuaries, menhaden. Is there a structural reason, outside individual decisions, why those institutions consistently produce that failure pattern? What would you point a reader at if they wanted to understand the architecture rather than the politics?
>
> 3. The chapter argues that a renewable resource pushed past regenerative capacity becomes effectively non-renewable, and that the Bay's renewable fisheries crossed that threshold decades ago in some cases. Which species or which moments would you point to as the clearest examples of that transition having already happened — versus being still reversible?
>
> 4. Sanctuary protection has been load-bearing in your testimony — SB 922, the 2019 legislation, the five large-scale sanctuaries. The framework reads sanctuaries as a regulatory-failure-insurance instrument — an admission that the harvest regime can't be trusted to self-correct. Does that read as fair, or is the role of sanctuaries different from that?
>
> 5. The chapter treats watermen as carrying the Bay's institutional accountability gap on their bodies and their families' futures. Does that read as an accurate description of what's happened on the Bay, or does it miss something important about how watermen themselves describe their position?
>
> 6. The Maryland-Virginia divide on Bay management has been a recurring theme in your testimony and CBF's reporting. Where has that divide been most costly, and where (if anywhere) has it produced something the unified jurisdiction wouldn't have?
>
> 7. What's the framework most likely to get *wrong* about the Bay if a reader who hasn't done the science encounters it?
>

> Calibrated to his public record (the Bay-cap-from-historic-landings critique in February 2026; the striped-bass "will not have the same recovery they had in the 1980s" framing in the 2024 stock-assessment response; the September 2025 pause-the-Bay-fishery articulation; the menhaden advocacy work generally).

>
> 2. **Bay cap as structural critique.** Your "Bay cap that's based on historic landings" framing names the regulatory *unit of analysis* as the problem. What would an architecture grounded in current Bay-specific biology actually look like — and what's the institutional gap between here and there?
