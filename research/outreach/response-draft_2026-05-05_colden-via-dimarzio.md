## Draft response

**To:** vdimarzio@cbf.org
**Subject:** Re: Interview request — your fisheries work at CBF Maryland + a forthcoming book on the Chesapeake fishery and cost severance

> Dear Ms. DiMarzio,
>
> Thanks for the warm reply, and please thank Dr. Colden for routing my note to you. Quick answers to your four questions:
>
> **1. Scientific or economic perspective?** Both — but specifically where they meet. Dr. Colden's combination of fisheries-scientist training and her current vantage on ASMFC and the Maryland Oyster Advisory Commission sits exactly where the chapter's argument lands. Her public record — the "looming disaster" framing on striped bass, the defense of the five large-scale oyster sanctuaries, and the work on the menhaden Bay cap — is closer to the chapter's structural claim than almost anything else in the literature: that the gap between what fisheries science said the Bay could sustain and what regulatory and political processes actually permitted has been opening for decades, and that watermen and the Bay itself have absorbed the cost. The chapter doesn't need her to say something new on the empirical question; it needs her structural read on whether the cost-severance framework names something the existing fisheries-policy vocabulary doesn't, and where it overreaches or underreaches.
>
> **2. Connecting Bay fisheries to Soviet industrial planning.** The connection is structural, not ideological — and worth me being clear up front because the framing sounds stranger than it is. The book identifies a pattern I call *cost severance*: extracting value from a commons while transferring the costs onto people, ecosystems, and futures who didn't capture the upside. My claim is that this mechanism is *not* specific to capitalism — feudal lords did it, colonial empires did it, Soviet planners did it (the Aral Sea is the textbook case), and market economies do it now. That cross-system framing is what keeps the book non-partisan; it forecloses both the "this is just an anti-capitalist critique" dismissal and its mirror image. The Chesapeake fishery enters the book as the *renewable-resource* case — distinct from the coal, oil, and Aral cases developed earlier — demonstrating that a renewable resource pushed past regenerative capacity becomes effectively non-renewable, with the costs absorbed by watermen and the Bay itself. So Soviet planning isn't a parallel to the Bay; it's one comparison case in a longer sequence showing the pattern travels across ownership structures. The comparison cases live in earlier chapters where the framework is built; the Bay chapter is the framework's renewable-resource demonstration on its own terms — what's happened to the Bay's fisheries, the watermen who depend on them, and the institutions that govern both. The Bay chapter is about the Bay.
>
> **3. Watermen and other Bay voices.** Yes, in motion. Parallel outreach this week to Rom Lipcius and Roger Mann at VIMS (blue crab and oyster ecology) and to Chris Moore at CBF's Virginia office. The watermen voice itself is the one the chapter most needs on the page, and it cannot land without it. If Dr. Colden — or you — were open to introductions to working or retired Maryland watermen (Tangier and Smith Island in particular), that would be enormously valuable, with or without an interview with Dr. Colden directly. I recognize watermen relationships may sit with CBF's community-engagement or working-waterfront staff rather than with Dr. Colden personally; happy to be routed there if that's where the connection lives.
>
> **4. Timeframe.** Flexible. The related essay is on a tighter cycle (next 2-3 months); the book is on a 2026-2027 publication arc. Anything from the next few weeks through midsummer would land well. Phone, Zoom, or in-person — I'm in Hampton, but Annapolis or another Maryland location is straightforward if she'd prefer it.
>
> One thing worth being clear about, in case it shapes the decision. What I'm asking for is closer to a calibration conversation than a quote-mining one. The chapter's empirical claim — that the gap between fisheries science and what regulatory and political processes actually permitted has been opening for decades, and that watermen and the Bay itself have absorbed the cost — is something Dr. Colden's public record already states more sharply than the framework does. What I'd most want from her is the harder layer: whether the *cost severance* vocabulary names something her existing fisheries-policy vocabulary doesn't, and where the framework overreaches or underreaches on Bay-specific terrain. That's an interview that gives her room to push back on framework-level claims that misfire, and to shape how a forthcoming book represents her territory.
>
> Happy to send any of the following in advance, whichever would be most useful for her decision: a one-page framework summary, the Ch 3 outline, or the specific 5–8 questions I'd plan to ask. Whichever is easiest.
>
> Thanks again for engaging — I'm grateful for your time.
>
> Warm regards,
> Chris Winn

### Generalizable lessons surfaced this session

1. **Public-record screen before any politically-sensitive cold outreach.** If the recipient isn't on record about the territory we're asking them to engage, asking them to be first is a guaranteed decline. Apply to Lipcius, Mann, Moore before tomorrow's send window — particularly Moore (CBF Virginia ED, same advocacy register as Colden; checkable) and Lipcius/Mann (academics, scientific-record check rather than political-record).
2. **Framework-internal structural parallels are not always safe to put in front of subjects whose institutional position is constrained by them.** The structural parallel can be true *and* operationally toxic for the person you're asking to engage with it. The menhaden-as-within-Bay-Soviet-analog rewrite was the canonical example: defensible internally, lethal externally. Worth tracking as a discipline for outreach correspondence going forward.
3. **For coordinators / gatekeepers, the most useful response is one that makes their recommendation easy.** Val's job is to hand Allison a yes/no recommendation. Reducing her decision uncertainty (offering advance questions, naming the interview register, demonstrating we've read the public record) is strategically stronger than expanding our own framing. The audience for the response is Val and Allison together, not Allison alone.
4. **Formality discipline — keep it formal until the recipient shifts first.** Default to "Dear [Title] [Surname]" register in correspondence; downshift to first-name only when the recipient explicitly invites it (signs off with first name + asks to be called by it; uses informal opener themselves on a subsequent message; etc.). Val signing off as "Val" in her own message is *self-presentation*, not an invitation for us to mirror — the author's register doesn't downshift until she explicitly says so.
