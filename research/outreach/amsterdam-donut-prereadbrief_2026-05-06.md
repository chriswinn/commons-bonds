## The framework, in one paragraph

*Commons Bonds* names a structural mechanism — *cost severance* — that separates extracted value from the costs of producing it, transferring those costs to people, ecosystems, and futures who didn't capture the upside. The framework's interest is structural rather than ideological: the same mechanism operates across capitalism, feudalism, colonialism, and Soviet industrial planning, which means the question isn't "what economic system" but "what makes accountability bind." Two contributions. **Cost severance** names what the mechanism does. **Residual commons value (RCV)** names what it costs — the worth permanently foreclosed when a non-renewable resource is liquidated. The book's applied cases — including Amsterdam's adoption of the Doughnut framework — are studied as **accountability-instrument architectures**: institutional structures where the binding of cost to actor has gone past values-statement and into operational discipline.

1. **Operational calibration.** The framework distinguishes accountability-instrument architectures (where cost-binding is institutionally enforced) from values-statement commitments (where framework adoption is symbolic). Where does Amsterdam's Doughnut implementation sit on that spectrum, and where has it shifted *because of* the framework's operational discipline rather than despite it?
2. **Decisions changed.** What's a specific decision Amsterdam made that wouldn't have been made the same way without the Doughnut framework? Conversely, what's a decision the framework hasn't been able to change?
3. **Friction with traditional municipal vocabulary.** Where does the Doughnut framework's vocabulary translate cleanly into the existing accountability languages of municipal governance (budgeting, regulatory authority, climate planning), and where does it not?
4. **Cost-severance vocabulary applied.** Does the *cost severance* terminology name something Amsterdam's accountability work has already been doing under different vocabulary, or does it sit alongside without adding analytical work?
5. **The strongest skeptic.** What's the strongest critique you encounter from skeptics — within Amsterdam, from peer cities, or from the broader policy community — and where does it land hardest?
