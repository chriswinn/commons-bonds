### MUST-ASK (deploy across any interview length)

#### Q2. McDowell-vs-Amsterdam contrast + imported cost severance

> *"In the framework I'm developing, McDowell County, West Virginia is the textbook case of direct cost severance — coal extracted, value flowed out, costs stayed (black lung, environmental devastation, generational poverty). Amsterdam under the Doughnut is on the opposite end — explicitly aiming to prevent cost severance via the framework's adoption. In your implementation experience, where does the Doughnut framework actually catch cost-severance moments at the action level? Where does it miss them — particularly the **imported / upstream** kind, where Amsterdam's local foundation/ceiling stays inside the donut but the city imports cost severance through its supply chains (rare earths from extraction zones, battery materials, construction inputs from globally-extracted resources)?"*

**Why ask:** Tests the complementarity hypothesis directly. The imported / upstream cost severance challenge is the framework's strongest analytical contribution beyond what Doughnut alone supplies. Dagan's narrative-translation vantage is well-positioned to say where the Coalition's vocabulary already addresses this and where it leaves a gap.

#### Q3. The water-deal hypothetical (Foreclosure Bond as deal-evaluation tool)

> *"Suppose Amsterdam is approached with a proposed deal that would leverage water-management infrastructure in a way that could impair crop-growing capacity. Doughnut would flag that this affects the social foundation (food security) and potentially the ecological ceiling. What tools does Doughnut give you to actually evaluate the deal at the action level — to price what's being foreclosed, to bind the counterparty to that price, to ensure obligations survive corporate dissolution? Where does the implementation currently have those tools, and where does it have a gap that something like a Foreclosure Bond instrument might fill?"*

**Why ask:** Substantive forward-looking deal-evaluation question that engages Dagan at the level his implementation work operates. The hypothetical is structurally generic (any extraction-pattern deal would test the same framework moves); the water/food-security framing is recognizable to anyone in the Amsterdam policy ecosystem. See `dagan-water-deal-hypothetical_2026-05-07.md` for the full step-by-step framework walkthrough — that's our internal scaffolding; don't show it to Dagan, but be prepared to walk through any of the 7 steps if he wants to interrogate the apparatus during the conversation.

#### Q4. Framework-fit calibration

> *"The framework I'm developing — cost severance as the structural mechanism, Residual Commons Value as what gets foreclosed by extraction, the Foreclosure Bond as the action-level pricing instrument — sits at a different scale than the Doughnut. Doughnut articulates the goal-state (the safe and just space between social foundation and ecological ceiling); my framework focuses on action-level accountability when extraction would impose un-priced costs. From your vantage as the person translating the Doughnut into Amsterdam's civic vocabulary, does this fill an action-level accountability gap that your implementation has been working around? Or does it duplicate vocabulary you already have under different names?"*

**Why ask:** The directly-falsifying question. If Dagan says "we already have action-level instruments under names X, Y, Z," that's critical input — either we re-position the framework against existing vocabulary or we discover redundant work. If he says "yes, that gap is real," that's substantial confirmation from someone whose ground truth is the implementation work.

#### Q5. Reading recommendations

> *"What in your work — or others' — would most help us position the framework well in the literature? Particularly anything addressing the imported / upstream cost severance challenge, the relationship between narrative-translation work (your specialty) and operational policy adoption, or critical work on the Doughnut implementation that's been useful to you?"*

**Why ask:** Reading-recs fallback per FPD design — even if no other question lands well, value extraction is preserved. Dagan's narrative-translation specialty likely surfaces literature outside the standard Doughnut canon (creative-agency / civic-imagination / behavioral-economics-of-vocabulary-adoption material) that the framework needs.

### SHOULD-ASK (deploy if 45–60 min available)

#### Q6. Wished-for action-level vocabulary

> *"Are there places in your implementation work where the Doughnut articulates the goal but doesn't supply the action-level vocabulary you need? Specific decision-moments where you've had to improvise — borrowed vocabulary from other frameworks, invented language, or just left a gap and operated by intuition — because the framework's design principles didn't translate to a specific binding move?"*

**Why ask:** Surfaces the precise edges where Doughnut's vocabulary runs out and where Commons Bonds (or some other action-level instrument) might supply what's missing. Dagan's narrative-translation specialty makes him uniquely well-positioned to articulate this; he has lived experience with vocabulary gaps that purely policy-side practitioners might not register.

#### Q7. Cross-Doughnut-city comparative

> *"As you've engaged with other Doughnut-adopting cities (Brussels, Copenhagen, Tokyo, Nanaimo, etc.), where has the framework translated cleanly, and where has it gotten stuck? Particularly around action-level accountability — where do other cities have moves Amsterdam doesn't, or vice versa? Are there cross-city implementation patterns you'd point to as more or less successful at catching cost-severance moments?"*

**Why ask:** Comparative texture for the book's Pattern 2 demonstration material. Multi-city implementation experience is unique to Coalition-network roles like Dagan's; primary-source comparative observations are very hard to assemble from secondary literature alone.

#### Q8. Citizen / non-expert reception

> *"How have Amsterdam's regular residents responded to 'donut city' framing? Has the vocabulary actually adopted at street level, or has it stayed primarily in policy circles? What's the lived experience of a vocabulary adoption project — when does the language stick and when does it bounce off?"*

**Why ask:** Goes to the heart of Dagan's narrative-translation work, and directly informs Commons Bonds' own vocabulary-adoption strategy. The framework's success depends on whether terms like *cost severance* and *Foreclosure Bond* will adopt at scale; Dagan has years of pattern-recognition on what makes new economic vocabulary stick (or fail to) in civic contexts.
