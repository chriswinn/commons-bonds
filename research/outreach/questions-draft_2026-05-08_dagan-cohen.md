### MUST-ASK (deploy across any interview length)

#### Q2. The four-lens asymmetry — naming vs. acting on Global-Social cost severance

> *"The Amsterdam City Doughnut report's signature methodological contribution is the four-lens framework — Local-Social, Local-Ecological, Global-Social, Global-Ecological. The Global-Social lens explicitly *names* the impact of Amsterdam's consumption patterns on people elsewhere: labour conditions in textile factories, mining for metals in electronics, the imported costs that aren't visible in Amsterdam's local foundation/ceiling. But reading the published Coalition material, the Global-Social lens's *what-to-do* vocabulary feels thinner than the work on the local lenses. Which is exactly the framework I'm developing's strongest opening: McDowell County, West Virginia is the textbook failure of *direct* cost severance — coal extracted, costs stayed locally. Amsterdam's work is the opposite at the local level. But the cost severance Amsterdam *imports* — rare earths from Niger Delta, battery materials from extraction zones, cobalt for the energy transition — is the same structural mechanism, displaced in space. Where in the Coalition's actual work is that imported / upstream cost severance most visible to you? And what's the gap between *naming it through the Global-Social lens* and *acting on it at the city scale*?"*

**Why ask:** The brief surfaced (a) the four-lens methodology as the canonical analytical apparatus and (b) the visible asymmetry between local-lens implementation depth and Global-lens action vocabulary. Naming the asymmetry specifically demonstrates we've read the Amsterdam City Doughnut report; it positions Commons Bonds as *acting on* what Doughnut already names — complementarity, not competition. The framework's clearest opening with Dagan because no public-record piece makes the argument as cleanly as Commons Bonds frames it.

#### Q3. Coalition vs. City — what does the Coalition do (vs. the municipality) when Amsterdam faces a foreclosure-pattern deal?

> *"A clarifying question first: the Coalition is the network layer — citizens, businesses, knowledge institutions, neighborhoods — and the City is the municipal-policy layer where formal deal-evaluation happens. Marieke van Doorninck championed the April 2020 adoption from the deputy-mayor seat; René Koop now leads the city-side circular economy work. Suppose the City of Amsterdam were approached tomorrow with a deal that would leverage water-management infrastructure in a way that could affect crop-growing capacity — a hypothetical pulled from the framework's pricing mechanics. Doughnut would flag the affected social foundation (food security) and ecological ceiling. But evaluating it at the action level — pricing what's being foreclosed, binding the counterparty, ensuring obligations survive corporate dissolution — sits one layer past the framework's named goal-state. From your network-layer vantage: what tools does the Coalition (vs. the City) actually *use* or *push for* in moments like that? Where does the implementation currently have those tools, and where is there a gap something like a Foreclosure Bond instrument might fill?"*

**Why ask:** Coalition ≠ City. Asking Dagan to comment on municipal deal-evaluation directly is off-axis (FPD-10 violation); his actual ground truth is the network layer — what the Coalition does, what advocacy it carries, what pressure it surfaces. Naming van Doorninck and Koop demonstrates we've mapped the city-side actors. The framework-fit dimension is preserved; the credibility-axis is corrected. See `dagan-water-deal-hypothetical_2026-05-07.md` for the full step-by-step framework walkthrough — internal scaffolding; don't show Dagan, but be ready to walk through any of the 7 steps if he wants to interrogate the apparatus.

#### Q4. Framework-fit calibration — does this duplicate vocabulary you already have, or fill a gap?

> *"The framework I'm developing — cost severance as the structural mechanism, Residual Commons Value as what gets foreclosed by extraction, the Foreclosure Bond as the action-level pricing instrument — sits at a different scale than the donut. Doughnut articulates the goal-state (the safe and just space between social foundation and ecological ceiling); my framework focuses on action-level accountability when extraction would impose un-priced costs. The Coalition vocabulary I've read in the public record — *donutstad, binnen de donut, donutpioniers, droom and doorbraak,* change-makers, living labs — operates in a register that's aspirational and experiential. From your vantage as the person translating the donut into Amsterdam's civic vocabulary, does the framework I'm developing fill an action-level accountability gap that your implementation has been working around? Or does it duplicate vocabulary you already have under different names — particularly anything regulatory or financial that the public-facing register doesn't surface?"*

**Why ask:** The directly-falsifying question. Sharpened with the actual Coalition vocabulary the brief surfaced. If Dagan says "we already have action-level instruments under names X, Y, Z," that's critical input — either we re-position against existing vocabulary or we discover redundant work. If he says "yes, that gap is real," that's substantial confirmation from someone whose ground truth is the implementation work. Naming the Coalition register specifically (and asking whether anything regulatory/financial sits *underneath* the public-facing vocabulary) earns standing for the question.

#### Q5. Reading recommendations — beyond what's already published

> *"DEAL has published Doughnut Unrolled (the four-lens methodology guides), Creating City Portraits, Doughnut Data Portraits, and the Designing the Doughnut Five Cities essay; the Coalition's public material includes the AUAS executive report (March 2023), the Donut Night five-year retrospective, and the annual Donut & Friends Festival. Beyond what's already on those public reading lists, what would you add — particularly on three things: the imported / upstream cost severance challenge; the relationship between narrative-translation work and operational policy adoption; and critical work on the Amsterdam donut implementation that you've found genuinely useful. CHANGENCY's about-page mentions design fiction as a methodological anchor — anything in that literature would be valuable too."*

**Why ask:** Reading-recs sharpened. By naming the specific DEAL-published materials we already know about, we earn standing AND signal the question is asking for incremental adds, not the canon. Three named topics (imported/upstream; narrative-translation-to-policy; Amsterdam-implementation criticism) make the question productive even if his answer is brief. The design fiction prompt acknowledges his CHANGENCY methodology and his Feb 2026 *De Klerenpartij* design-fiction project.

#### Q7. Cross-Doughnut-city comparative — what's missing from the published accounts?

> *"The published comparative material covers a lot. Goodwin's *Designing the Doughnut: A Story of Five Cities* covered Sydney, Melbourne, Berlin, Brussels, and Amsterdam through March 2021; **BrusselsDonut** is led by Confluences with a 'deeply participatory' register; **Copenhagen's** economic department was tasked with implementation within six weeks of Amsterdam's announcement; **Nanaimo** voted to adopt in December 2020; **Cornwall** adapted the method on a two-month timeline; **Berlin** ran a Doughnut Pioneers Programme in 2025; **Tokyo's Setagaya** ward engaged DEAL tools; **Cardiff and Glasgow** carried Welsh and Scottish council moves; the **Vermont Climate Equity Advisory Committee** is a US instance. From your network-layer vantage — what's *missing* from those published accounts, particularly around action-level accountability? Which cities have moves Amsterdam doesn't? Which cities are doing the imported-cost-severance work that the four-lens Global-Social material under-articulates?"*

**Why ask:** Naming the published comparative material specifically demonstrates we've done the cross-city homework. The question then probes for the unpublished texture — what's missing from those accounts, particularly around the action-level accountability gap the framework targets. Multi-city comparative observations are unique to Coalition-network roles like Dagan's; primary-source comparative texture is hard to assemble from secondary literature alone.

#### Q8. Citizen reception — when does the donut language stick at street level?

> *"Sophia Manolis (Stanford, hosted by Willem van Winden at HvA) has empirical findings on the citizen-reach gap — that the 'donut city' framing has high recognition in Amsterdam policy circles and active-citizen networks but limited penetration into ordinary residents' daily vocabulary. Van Winden has made that critique repeatedly in his published reflections. From your narrative-translation vantage — when does the language *actually* stick at street level, and when does it bounce off? What's the unwritten texture, beyond what Manolis and van Winden have surfaced? And how do you think about the *next* layer of citizen reach for the Coalition's narrative work — what's the unfinished piece of the vocabulary-adoption project?"*

**Why ask:** Names the existing critique sources (Manolis, van Winden) so Dagan doesn't have to ground-truth the citizen-reach gap from scratch. Positions the question to start where their published critiques end. Three layers of probe: (a) when does it stick / bounce; (b) the unwritten texture; (c) the next layer of citizen reach. Each layer is incremental — Dagan can pick where to spend depth. This goes to the heart of his narrative-translation work and directly informs Commons Bonds' own vocabulary-adoption strategy.
