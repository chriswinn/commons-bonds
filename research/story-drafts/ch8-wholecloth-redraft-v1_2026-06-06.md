# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 8: What Things Actually Cost

---

What if everything cost what it actually costs?

Ask the question with no ideology attached, no politics riding on the answer, and the framework these chapters have been building starts answering before the argument even begins. Not from preference. From arithmetic. Take one extraction, any extraction, though the one we have been following since Chapter 2 will do, and walk it through the full accounting the earlier chapters made legible. Price the lungs. Price the poisoned creek. Price the community that bore the weight. Price the foreclosure carried forward to every generation downstream of the families that scattered. Price the knowledge that left with them, the political work that kept the costs invisible, the speed that compressed centuries of a resource into a few decades. Add it up. Look at the number. Then look at the price on the invoice. Notice the gap.

This chapter does that exercise for one ton of Appalachian coal. I want the reader to see what the gap looks like when it is not aggregated, not abstracted, not dissolved in policy language, but priced, one cost component at a time, against the single extraction this book has been following from its opening.

The result is not degrowth. It is not anti-capitalism. It is not, in this book, a policy recommendation. It is an accounting exercise. What anyone chooses to do with the accounting is a separate question, a political one, and one this book is deliberately not trying to answer. The book's job is to make the numbers visible. What gets built from that visibility is the work of the policy-makers, communities, and political coalitions who carry the framework's vocabulary into the specific places where it has to be implemented.

What follows walks each cost component the framework admits, every cost that passes the discipline laid out in the earlier chapters: the inversion that surfaces it, the units that hold consistent, the bound that keeps it finite, the independence that keeps it from being double-counted. The eight components below are the ones McDowell County coal surfaces. A different extraction surfaces a different set. The method is universal. The components are case-specific.

---

### The coal, again

Return to McDowell County. Return to the county Kennedy drove through in 1960, the county that produced more coal than any other in the United States while its residents queued in the hollows for surplus food. A ton of coal sold at the mine mouth that year for just under five dollars. The Energy Information Administration's nominal series puts the 1960 mine-mouth price of bituminous coal at roughly four dollars and seventy cents, a little higher for all grades combined, so call it four dollars and seventy to eighty-five cents and round, in your head, to just under five. By the time the rail car cleared the last tunnel, the accounting was complete as far as the market was concerned. A buyer paid. A seller was paid. The transaction cleared. The coal was gone.

What the market did not price, what Chapter 6 spent its pages showing every serious pricing methodology agrees it did not price, was not a rounding error. It was the majority of the actual cost.

What follows is conservative the whole way through. Where an estimate has a range, I use the low figure. Where an externality is contested, I leave it out. The goal is not a ceiling. It is a floor, the smallest honest number the framework can defend, and the point is to show that even the floor sits orders of magnitude above the price that cleared the market.

---

### Direct health cost

Begin with the lungs. Black lung is the most completely documented externality coal has ever produced. It is federally tracked, specifically attributable, and shifted onto the public ledger every time a coal company files for bankruptcy and the original liability dissolves with the corporate shell. Chapter 2 walked the federal Black Lung Disability Trust Fund in detail, its funding gap, its debt, the mechanism by which the cost gets severed from the party that caused it. For this chapter, the only number we need is the per-ton headline. Allocated against the national coal tonnage produced since the program was established in 1969, the trust fund alone runs between one and one and a half dollars per ton.

That is before the rest of the direct health cost. The excess cardiovascular deaths from particulate exposure. The respiratory illness in mining communities, including the children who were never in a mine. The cancers near the coal-processing facilities. The medical bills of a population whose life expectancy diverges from the national figure by more than a decade.

Add the Medicare and Medicaid costs shifted to the public ledger, the uncompensated care, and the private bills left behind by miners whose employers went bankrupt before the obligations matured, and a conservative figure lands between two and four dollars per ton at the national level.

Two to four dollars. Against a market price of just under five in 1960. Direct health alone, the single most documented component, already returns most of what the coal sold for.

---

### Environmental degradation cost

Walk outside the mine. The creek running orange with acid mine drainage is not a metaphor. It is a specific chemical process. Sulfide minerals, exposed by mining and left in contact with water and air, oxidize into sulfuric acid, and the acid mobilizes heavy metals into the watershed for a century or longer. Treatment is perpetual. There is no version of acid mine drainage that ends when the mine closes.

Across Appalachia, the reclamation bonds coal companies were required to post under the 1977 Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act fall billions short of the documented remediation need. Hundreds of thousands of acres across the region wait for cleanup, close to a million nationwide. The available bonds total a few billion dollars against a cleanup cost estimated in the high single-digit billions. The shortfall, several billion dollars, is a severed cost sitting in the federal ledger in plain sight, waiting on a funding source the law assumed would exist and that does not.

Add to that the Appalachian cancer cases linked to mountaintop-removal mining in the Hendryx epidemiology, the long-tail loss of ecosystem services from streams buried under valley fill, the soil contamination downstream of the processing plants, and the mercury from coal combustion that now turns up in fish as far away as the North Atlantic.

Allocated per ton, conservatively, one to three dollars. Added to direct health, that brings the national severed cost to somewhere between three and seven dollars a ton, already comparable to the whole 1960 price, and we have not priced a single molecule of carbon yet.

---

### Community disruption cost

The county peaked near one hundred thousand residents in 1950. By 2020, just over nineteen thousand remained, under twenty thousand in a place that had once approached six figures. Mechanization cut the workforce by more than half within a decade of Kennedy's visit. The closures ran across the 1980s, the U.S. Steel mine at Gary among them, and each one dropped paychecks the local economy could not replace. In 2016 the last Walmart left. In 2022 the county sat near the bottom of the national income distribution, a median household income around twenty-eight thousand dollars and a poverty rate near thirty-eight percent, in a country whose median runs over sixty-seven thousand. In 2015 McDowell recorded the highest drug-induced death rate of any county in America, about one hundred forty-one per hundred thousand, close to ten times the national rate of roughly sixteen that same year. This is the human shape of community disruption cost. A county the size of a single high-school district, losing its people to overdose at a rate an actuary would flag as a disaster zone.

A community does not fall to a fifth of its population without carrying costs. There is the infrastructure: schools, fire departments, water systems, hospitals, all of which have to be maintained at some minimum no matter the headcount, and whose per-person burden rises as the headcount falls. There is the multiplier loss: every local business that closed, every institution that could not sustain itself, every dollar that no longer circulated because the person who would have spent it had left. There is the public-program cost: food assistance, disability, Medicaid, absorbed by federal and state government in a county that had once generated billions in coal revenue, almost none of which stayed.

Economists who study regional collapse have ways of pricing this, and the figures they produce for sustained decline run from five to fifteen dollars per ton of the coal that drove the collapse. I will take the low end. Five dollars a ton.

Running total across the first three components, roughly eight dollars a ton, already approaching twice the 1960 price. And we still have not priced the carbon.

---

### Foreclosure cost

This is the component Chapter 2 named when it walked the cost out past every order of magnitude the coal had sold for, and the one Chapter 6 formalized as the residual-commons-value integral. For a fossil fuel, it is also the component that dominates all the others combined.

One ton of coal, burned, releases roughly 2.6 metric tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. The number is not a guess. The EPA's AP-42 §1.1 emission factor for bituminous combustion is 93.28 kilograms of CO₂ per million British thermal units of heat released. McDowell County's coal comes from the Pocahontas seams, whose heat content the U.S. Geological Survey and the EIA put at roughly 28 million British thermal units per short ton, near the high end of the bituminous range. Multiply the two and one short ton of McDowell coal yields 2.61 metric tons of CO₂. (This is the McDowell-specific version of the framework figure Chapter 6 introduced. Chapter 6 used the national-average bituminous heat content, which gives 2.32 tons of CO₂ per ton of coal. The seam McDowell sat on simply ran hotter, so its number is higher. I flag the heat-content source on purpose, because the whole carbon term turns on it, and a reader who wants to check the arithmetic should be able to find the same 28 mmBtu I used.)

The EPA's 2023 estimate of the social cost of carbon, the economic damage caused by each ton of CO₂ released, is one hundred ninety dollars per metric ton. The EPA built that figure on the integrated climate-damage modeling published by Rennert and colleagues in *Nature* in 2022, whose own central estimate landed slightly lower, near one hundred eighty-five. I cite the figure to the EPA, because the EPA is the body that adopted one hundred ninety as its central number. The arithmetic from there is not subtle. The climate cost of burning one ton of McDowell County coal, at the 2023 estimate, is about four hundred ninety-six dollars. That is 2.61 times one hundred ninety.

Just under five hundred dollars. Against a market price that has swung between roughly four dollars and one hundred forty over the whole life of the industry. The carbon term alone, before any of the other seven components are added, exceeds every era's market price for a ton of coal, by a factor that runs from about three against today's market peak to more than a hundred against the 1960 mine mouth.

And that is still only the part of the foreclosure the carbon number captures. Set beside it the option value it does not: every future metallurgical use that no substitute can serve, every chemical feedstock application, every use we cannot yet imagine for a carbon resource whose existing stock is being burned for electricity in a world that can now make electricity from sources that do not require combustion at all.

So the foreclosure component, for coal burned in any era after viable renewable substitutes existed, is a minimum of four hundred ninety-six dollars a ton. That is before option value, before the ecosystem-service losses that come with a warming atmosphere, before the climate-driven mortality and migration that the social cost of carbon is known to understate.

Conservative floor across these four components, the four that carry hard numbers, runs from five hundred four to five hundred eighteen dollars a ton.

### Lineage labor cost

Now the harder components, the ones the market has no instrument for at all.

Lineage labor cost is the one the inversion surfaces most plainly. It is the cost borne not by the worker but by the worker's family, and by the generations downstream of that family's scattering.

A mining community that collapses by eighty percent does not take its children with it evenly. The ones who stay grow up in a food desert where the nearest grocery store is an hour off and the local school's graduation rate is among the lowest in the country. The ones who leave were often raised by parents whose work was destructive enough that many did not live to meet grandchildren. Intergenerational wealth transmission, which in most American communities is the single largest determinant of which lives are possible and which are not, becomes impossible when the family's entire asset base is a house in a hollow that cannot be sold.

This is not a rhetorical claim. It is in the data. A child born in McDowell County in the bottom income quintile has among the lowest odds of reaching the top quintile of anywhere in the United States. The mobility data the Opportunity Insights project has made public puts the county at the floor of national outcomes. The extraction did not only take the coal. It took the children's odds of a different life.

In naming this, the framework is naming something Black American writing named more than a century ago and named better. Paul Laurence Dunbar published *We Wear the Mask* in 1896. *We wear the mask that grins and lies, it hides our cheeks and shades our eyes, this debt we pay to human guile.* The miner who has been told the dust is better now is wearing the same mask. The framework's claim is that the mask is not his alone to wear. It belongs to the system that produces black lung at industrial scale and then asks the man producing it to grin while it does.

Dunbar named the survival strategy. W. E. B. Du Bois, in *The Souls of Black Folk* in 1903, named the structural condition that made the strategy necessary, the veil and the doubled consciousness of living a life the surrounding economy refuses to see whole. Frantz Fanon, in *Black Skin, White Masks* in 1952, carried the analysis into the colonial and economic register that the framework's extraction-community cases sit closest to. Ralph Ellison, in *Invisible Man* the same year, gave the long-form account of a life lived under the mask, seen by everyone and witnessed by no one. What the framework adds to that lineage is the accounting mechanism, the specific reason the mask stays structurally necessary: the costs the mask hides are real, they are large, and the ledger was built so they would never appear.

I should be honest about how I arrived at the word I have been using for this. *Masking*, in the sense of a cost made invisible by abundance, is a term I first met in my own working life, in information-security engineering, where a masked failure is one the system's surface health hides until it has already done its damage. That is the door I came in through. But the older and deeper account of masking, the account of what it costs a person to wear one, belongs to Dunbar and Du Bois and the tradition that runs through Fanon and Ellison, and I want it credited there, because that tradition saw the human cost of the hidden ledger long before I had a spreadsheet for it. The two meanings converge on the same thing. A cost you cannot see is a cost no one is paying down.

The lineage labor cost is real, but the data attaching a specific dollar figure to it per ton is thin. Development economists estimating intergenerational mobility losses have produced regional figures in the single-digit billions for counties the size of McDowell. Allocated per ton, imprecisely, that sits somewhere between one and five dollars. The number is small next to the carbon term. It is enormous next to the life of a child whose median lifetime income was fixed, at birth, by the zip code printed on the certificate.

I price this component at two dollars a ton, a conservative placeholder. The reader can decide whether two dollars a ton, run across sixty years of extraction, comes anywhere near a fair accounting for what it cost a generation to be born in a county that had already been priced at nothing.

### Knowledge and cultural cost

This component is the hardest to see while it is happening, because it is invisible right up until it is already gone. Every mining community carried practical knowledge: how to read the seams, how to manage risk on one specific geological formation, how to keep one particular valley's water system running, how the weather moved through that hollow over a century. The knowledge existed because specific people stayed in specific places long enough to accumulate it.

When the community dispersed, the knowledge went with it. Not to anywhere it could be preserved. To places where it had no use. The retired miner who knew exactly how the West Fork ran in February did not move somewhere that needed to know how the West Fork ran in February.

And that is only the ecological and occupational knowledge. Add the cultural continuity, the congregations and the civic clubs and the webs of mutual aid that could absorb a personal crisis because everyone knew everyone, and the loss compounds. The replacement cost for the civic fabric of a community of a hundred thousand people is not cleanly priced, but it is certainly not zero.

I price this component at one dollar a ton. It is not the point of the chapter. But it belongs in the accounting, because the inversion surfaces it, and because a reader thinking carefully about what the coal cost cannot simply drop the part of the cost that fell outside the ledger for the sole reason that no one had denominated it in dollars.

### Political capture cost

The reason the first six components are obvious in hindsight and were invisible in the moment is not that the information was unavailable. It is that the industry whose invoices did not reflect the costs had a large financial interest in keeping the costs unpriced, and acted on that interest, for decades, through documented lobbying, regulatory capture, and the strategic suppression of research.

Black lung was identifiable as an occupational disease by the early twentieth century. It was not recognized as a basis for federal compensation until 1969, and then only after a miners' strike and the West Virginia Black Lung Association forced the question onto the legislative floor. The United Mine Workers under John L. Lewis had declined to press the issue at several points in the preceding decades, reportedly on the grounds that raising it would interfere with the mechanization that was driving wages up. The mechanism that drove the wages up was, of course, the same mechanism that produced the disease. The rank and file paid the difference.

The social cost of carbon has itself been an object of capture. Under the Obama administration it was set near forty-two dollars a ton, anchored to a global-scope calculation of the externality. Under the Trump administration it was cut to somewhere between one and seven dollars, by narrowing the calculation's geographic scope to the United States rather than the globe, a change that the cost-benefit literature flagged as ungrounded in standard practice. Under the Biden administration it returned to fifty-one dollars on a return to the global scope, and the EPA's 2023 update raised it to the one hundred ninety used in this chapter. The number is not uncertain because the carbon physics is uncertain. The number is uncertain because how much of the cost gets priced is a political fight, and one side of that fight has far more resources than the other.

The cost of the fight is itself a severed cost. Democracies spend real money, through lobbying, through captured regulatory agencies, through think tanks funded to reach the conclusions their funders need, through the civic attention pulled away from other questions, to keep the honest accounting from happening. The fossil fuel industry's visible annual lobbying spend alone runs into the hundreds of millions of dollars, and the visible fraction is the smaller part.

The architecture that produced McDowell County's particular form of collapse was not impersonal. It was shaped, generation by generation, by specific actors: operators, absentee mineral-rights holders, industry trade associations, whose lobbying at the West Virginia statehouse and at one federal regulatory body after another is a matter of public record going back decades. The cost-bearing analysis the framework runs, who absorbed what, over what span of time, against what counterfactual, operates on top of an architecture that identifiable actors built. The reparations-economics tradition supplies the vocabulary for naming the actors and coalitions behind a wealth gap. The public-choice tradition supplies the vocabulary for the extractor's own reasoning, the rent-seeking logic that shaped the record on the books. The framework's contribution is different in kind. It is the ledger that counts what those traditions described: the magnitudes, the names attached to the dollars, McDowell County's thirteen-year life-expectancy gap stated as a number rather than left as an attribution problem. The rent-seeking vocabulary survives many readings of its originators' broader political projects, and this book draws on the vocabulary without endorsing any of those projects. Chapter 9 develops the framework's relationship to public-choice analysis at greater length.

I assign this component one dollar a ton, a floor so low it is almost certainly an undercount, set there to keep the running total conservative. A reader who has followed the argument this far can revise it upward.

### Temporal displacement cost

This component, which the inversion surfaces most starkly of all, is the cost of *when*.

The coal in McDowell County had sat in the ground for hundreds of millions of years. Its extraction was compressed into roughly a single century, with the bulk of the tonnage moved in the forty years after mechanization. This was not because the country needed coal at that exact rate. It was because the rate at which coal could be pulled out had been raised by machinery, and because the financial structure of the industry rewarded fast extraction over measured extraction.

The temporal displacement cost is the cost of foregone optionality, the value that would have come from extracting more slowly, over a longer span, with time to develop alternatives, time for communities to diversify, time for the externality tail to be absorbed at the rate it could have been absorbed instead of all at once. The oil shocks of the 1970s produced a fifty-percent improvement in U.S. energy intensity over the following two decades, because the price signaled that efficiency mattered. Had coal been priced anywhere near its honest cost in 1960, the same signal would have driven the same kind of adaptation, at a pace communities could have survived.

Instead, the mechanization compressed the timeline, the price suppressed the signal, and the adaptation did not arrive until the damage had piled up to the point where the pile itself forced it.

I assign this component two dollars a ton. Again, almost certainly an undercount.

### The sum

The framework sums each admitted component across the relevant time horizon. The per-ton arithmetic this chapter has been doing is the snapshot version of that sum, applied to one extraction. The Technical Appendix carries the formal integral for any reader who wants it.

Run the total at the low end of every component:

- Direct health: $2

- Environmental degradation: $1

- Community disruption: $5

- Foreclosure: $496

- Lineage labor: $2

- Knowledge and cultural: $1

- Political capture: $1

- Temporal displacement: $2

Total: five hundred ten dollars a ton.

Against a 1960 market price of just under five dollars. Against forty to one hundred forty dollars today, depending on grade. Against every price coal has ever sold for.

The Intergenerational Pricing Gap, the ratio of the honest price to the market price, for McDowell County coal sits somewhere between thirty-three and one hundred twenty-two times the 1960 sale price, depending on which cost categories the accounting admits and which social-cost-of-carbon anchor it uses. Even at today's higher prices, the carbon term alone exceeds the market price by a factor of at least three. The finding holds across every era the industry has operated in. The honest cost of a ton of McDowell County coal has always exceeded what the market paid for it, often by more than two orders of magnitude.

### The pattern made legible

If this were an isolated case, a reader would be right to ask whether McDowell County is a special exception, whether the arithmetic that works here falls apart somewhere else. Chapter 5 answered that with case after case. The ratios were not identical. The mechanism was. Deepwater Horizon's documented costs against its projected well revenue produce a gap of fifteen to seventeen times, and that is before the framework adds the foreclosure layer the documented-costs accounting leaves out: the Macondo well's projected lifetime production of forty to sixty million barrels would release seventeen to twenty-six million tons of CO₂ when burned, which at this chapter's one-hundred-ninety-dollar carbon anchor is another three to five billion dollars of carbon-tail externality. The addition barely shifts the ratio, because Deepwater's documented direct costs already run an order of magnitude past its revenue, but the consistency is the point: the framework that prices coal's carbon tail prices oil's the same way, and the answer comes back the same in shape if not in scale. The W. R. Grace vermiculite mine in Libby, Montana produced a gap of forty to eighty times against its lifetime revenue. The Baotou rare-earth extractions, priced against the documented environmental and health costs to the villagers living beside the tailings lake, land in the same band. The Exxon Valdez spill, measured against the market value of the oil spilled into the sound, produced a gap that resists clean calculation only because the recovery was never finished.

Across different industries, geographies, decades, and resources, the same mechanism produces the same structural gap between what the invoice said and what the extraction actually cost.

The pattern does not stop at what conventionally counts as extraction. The housing-market pattern Chapter 2 touched, nearly half of American renters cost-burdened while an asset-owning class captures the surplus that would otherwise have funded mobility, produces the same shape at the scale of a single household. Run the cost-component arithmetic against a rent-burdened family and the components populate: a foreclosure-of-mobility cost, what is no longer possible as a life once housing eats the savings that would have funded a move; a lineage labor cost, what cannot be inherited because the parents could never accumulate; a political capture cost, the zoning and tenant-law architecture that keeps the revenue flowing in one direction. The value lands with asset owners who are not present at the scale of the local community. The costs land with the household whose savings do not grow. The arithmetic differs from McDowell's. The structure is the same.

There is a serious objection to that housing reading, and it comes from a specific direction: that high housing costs are driven by zoning restrictions on supply rather than by any capture of rent, so that the mechanism this paragraph names is misdiagnosed, the symptom mistaken for the cause. The objection is not weak, and it deserves more than a sentence. Chapter 9, which takes up the question of what an alternative architecture would actually be built from, makes the full supply-side case and answers it there, because the answer belongs with the design discussion and not in the middle of a coal ledger. For the purposes of this chapter, one distinction carries the weight: a supply story explains why housing units have not kept pace with demand in the high-cost metros, but it does not explain why, where units do exist, the rent they command has captured a structurally larger share of household income across markets with very different supply elasticities at once. Something is pressing on the rent-share of income across the whole spectrum, and supply restriction is not it. That something is the framework's subject. Both mechanisms are real, both are operating, and the policy response has to address both. The point here is only that the household-scale finding survives the objection, and that the next chapter is where the objection gets the full hearing it is owed.

The same pattern scaled, in 2008, all the way up to the architecture of the response itself. The housing-bubble collapse destroyed roughly eleven trillion dollars of U.S. household wealth and set off about ten million foreclosure filings over the following five years, roughly five million of them completed. The federal response, the Troubled Asset Relief Program plus the Federal Reserve's emergency lending, deployed about seven hundred billion dollars in direct authorization and trillions more in liquidity and guarantees, nearly all of it aimed at recapitalizing bank balance sheets rather than restructuring household debt. The choice was contested at the time and has been documented since by economists including Mian and Sufi, whose 2014 *House of Debt* weighed the welfare cost of the architecture that was chosen against the household-debt-restructuring architecture that was reachable but not selected. What the framework's accountability logic adds to that retrospective is the structural reading: the 2008 response was not a market outcome, it was a policy choice, and the choice fixed a particular distribution of the crisis's costs between bank balance sheets and household balance sheets. Chapter 9 returns to what the alternative would have looked like under honest pricing.

### What honest pricing does *not* mean

Three misreadings have followed this argument every time I have offered it, and I want to name them before the chapter ends.

The first is that honest pricing claims the extraction should never have happened. It does not. Cheap energy, even severely mispriced energy, lifted billions of human beings out of poverty, and no honest reader of this book should accept an account that pretends otherwise. The claim here is narrower. Whoever bore the cost of the extraction should have been compensated at a rate that reflected what they actually gave up. The miners who developed black lung. The communities that collapsed when the coal ran out. The children born into a county whose economic future had been extracted before they drew breath. The generations who will absorb the climate cost. These are not hypothetical entities. They are people. They paid. They are still paying. The question the framework asks is not whether the extraction happened. It is who paid for it, and whether they ever agreed to.

The second misreading is that honest pricing claims the market is fake. It does not. Prices cleared transactions. Money changed hands. Real coal left McDowell County on real rail cars. The framework's claim is that the price cleared the transaction the market could see and left untouched the transaction that was actually occurring. The market priced the extraction of the coal. The severed costs, the ruined lungs, the poisoned creek, the collapsed town, the carbon in the air, the scattered families, the political fight needed to keep the books closed, were a second transaction running alongside the first, unpriced, unwitnessed, and binding on parties who were never asked.

The market is not fake. The market is incomplete. The framework's job is to name the part that got left out.

The third misreading is that honest pricing is a ladder the already-industrialized nations pull up behind the developing ones. This is the objection that has to be taken seriously, because if it were true it would be a real moral defeater. It is not true, and the answer is structural rather than rhetorical. Whoever created the historical cost owes its repayment, and differentiated transition timelines are a built-in feature of honest accounting, not an afterthought bolted on for fairness. The developed nations built their wealth by severing costs onto the global commons for two centuries. The atmospheric carbon, the disrupted climate, the industrial base that benefited from free disposal of its externalities, all of it was built by specific countries whose present wealth reflects costs borne by people who were never compensated. Honest pricing generates the revenue to fund clean industrialization for developing nations, not as charity but as the repayment of a historical severance. Solar electricity is now cheaper than coal across most of the developing world. The technology exists. The transition timeline should be asymmetric. The framework requires exactly that.

### The brief extrapolation

If coal is mispriced locally by somewhere between thirty-three and one hundred twenty-two times its 1960 sale price, and if the same pattern shows up across every extraction case this book has examined, then the aggregate global severed cost across all resource extraction sits in a range the framework can bound but cannot fully pin down from here.

Order of magnitude: the global fossil fuel industry alone generates something on the order of four to five trillion dollars in annual revenue. If full-cost pricing would raise those revenues by a factor of two to three, which is conservative given the carbon term alone, the annual global severed cost from fossil extraction lands in the range of eight to ten trillion dollars. Add the non-fossil sectors, mining, logging, fisheries harvested past regeneration, aquifer drawdown, the loss of agricultural topsoil, and the global figure lands somewhere between ten and fifteen trillion dollars a year.

Ten to fifteen trillion. Annually. To put that in a scale a reader can hold: it is larger than every government subsidy program on Earth combined, larger than all international development assistance, larger than all charitable giving in every country, all of it added together. It is, as far as I can tell, the largest continuous wealth transfer in human history. And it runs in one direction. From the communities closest to the extraction to the consumers farthest from it. From the future to the present. From the people with no voice in the market to the ones who have all of it.

That number is the argument's civilizational-scale implication. It is enormous. It is also, in this book, a gestured claim, one sentence of supporting arithmetic against an assertion that would, taken seriously as a policy agenda, demand years of sectoral simulation and political-economic analysis and implementation work that cannot honestly be compressed into the closing pages of a framework book.

One reading the figure invites is that the chapter is doing degrowth in a different vocabulary. It is not. GDP measures market transactions, not welfare. Every dollar spent cleaning up an oil spill counts as production. Every medical bill from black lung counts as output. GDP rises when the children of McDowell County develop addiction disorders and pay for treatment. GDP does not fall when they die at fifty-eight. Honest pricing lowers GDP in the narrow technical sense while raising welfare in every sense the measurement was ever meant to proxy. It changes the composition of economic activity, not the total. Leaded gasoline was banned and the refineries reformulated, and the industry did not collapse, and children's cognitive outcomes improved. That is the pattern honest pricing repeats at a larger scale.

So I am not going to compress it. I will name the figure and leave it there.

### The closing line

What happens if an entire global economy begins to reprice at honest rates? Resource by resource, sector by sector, country by country? What happens to energy costs in the short term, in the medium term, in the new equilibrium? Who absorbs the transition? What happens to a household in the bottom forty percent of the income distribution when essentials rise by forty percent? Where does the pricing surplus go, and who decides? What does the economy look like on the far side, and is that economy still recognizably capitalist?

These questions are real. The framework answers them. The mathematics produces specific results, the simulations run in recognizable directions, and the distributional crisis and the structural necessity of a universal floor beneath it emerge with a clarity that is neither ideological nor optional. They are, all of them, consequences of the pricing exercise this chapter has just run on a single ton of coal.

They are also outside the scope of this book.

This book's job is to make the invisible visible. Chapters 1 through 8 have done that. Chapter 9 sketches the direction a civilization committed to honest accounting would move, and builds the alternative architecture this chapter kept pointing toward. Chapter 10 closes with the miner, the waterman, and the off-world administrator recognizing each other across the distance the mechanism was built to maintain.

These questions presuppose the framework this book establishes. Their pursuit belongs to the policy-makers, scholars, communities, and political coalitions who carry the framework's vocabulary into the specific places where it has to be implemented. The framework cannot do that work itself. What honest accounting at civilizational scale would reveal, when it is performed by the people who do that work, in time and on terms not legislated by these pages, is not a catastrophe. It is a better, more stable, more resilient economy, with a floor beneath it.

For now, what this chapter gives the reader is the one worked example. A ton of coal, priced honestly, at a floor of roughly five hundred ten dollars. The gap between that number and the just-under-five-dollar invoice is the severed cost. The invoice is what McDowell County was paid. The severed cost is what McDowell County bore.

The next two chapters return to what that means. For McDowell County. For the waterman. For the reader's own life. And for the century ahead.
