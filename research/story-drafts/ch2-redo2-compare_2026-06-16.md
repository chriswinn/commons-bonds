## Three verbatim side-by-sides

### 1. THE OPEN

**NEW-A (L12, L18–20):**
> "In the spring of 1960 a senator drove into the poorest county in one of the richest seams of coal on earth, and what he saw there did not match anything in his briefing books. […] Later that year, in September, in Canton, Ohio, John F. Kennedy stood up and named what he had seen. He said the situation was an indictment of the country that allowed it, and he was right, and that is the trouble. He had the indictment. He delivered it from the heart. He just had the wrong defendant. That sentence is going to organize this whole chapter, so let me set it down plainly. He had the wrong defendant."

**NEW-B (L7, L15–19):**
> "In May of 1960 a young senator drove through McDowell County, West Virginia, and what he saw there did not leave him. […] Four months later, in September, he stood at a podium in Canton, Ohio, and named the thing he had seen. He spoke as a man builds a case. He named the want, and the idleness, and the children, and he laid the blame where a man laying blame from a podium lays it, on an opponent, on an administration, on a policy that had failed. He had the want right. He had the suffering right. […] He had the wrong defendant. The men on the porches had not been robbed by a party or a policy. They had been emptied out by an arrangement so ordinary that it had no name…"

*Judge note:* Both opens are strong and seam-free. A's "that sentence is going to organize this whole chapter, so let me set it down plainly" is a mild structural self-narration; B lets "He had the wrong defendant" land as its own paragraph and then turns straight into the argument ("an arrangement so ordinary that it had no name") without captioning. B's open is the cleaner execution of the same controlling image. (Note: A withholds Kennedy's name to L18 for a reveal; B names "a young senator" then "John F. Kennedy" later too. Both defensible.)

### 2. THE P-5 SECOND-CYCLE ATTRIBUTION (the discipline the brief most fears getting wrong)

**NEW-A (L126):**
> "Put the two records together and you do not have a coincidence. You have a pattern, written partly by the company in its own marketing and reconstructed partly by the people who came afterward to count the dead, and the pattern points at distress as the thing the second extraction sought out."

**NEW-B (L163):**
> "This is the part where the temptation is to say that the company drew a map of the broken places and aimed for them, and I am not going to say that, because the honest account is more careful and more damning. There are two records here. […] Put the two records together and a pattern stands out that no single document confesses to…"

*Judge note:* Both are P-5-compliant (neither asserts the forbidden self-drawn-map claim). B is sharper: it surfaces the forbidden claim explicitly and refuses it, converting the no-fabrication discipline into a rhetorical strength ("more careful and more damning"). This is the single best execution of P-5 in either draft.

### 3. THE CLOSE (Chesapeake bridge)

**NEW-A (L140, L142):**
> "The bay is not like that. A bay, left alone, keeps making what is taken from it, the oyster bed reseeds, the crab returns, the water cleans itself. So the severance out there is conditional. It arrives only when the rate of taking runs past the rate of making, and it does not have to arrive at all. That is the hope hiding in the harder case… This is the miner I meant, back in the last chapter, when I spoke of options a coal miner does not have. Now you have met him. Now we go to the water."

**NEW-B (L177, L181):**
> "The bay is different. A bay can keep making what is taken from it, oyster after oyster, crab after crab, generation into generation, if the rate of taking stays under the rate of making. The severance there is not built into the resource the way it is built into the coal. It is conditional… It is the difference between an elegy and a warning. […] The senator saw the suffering and named the wrong defendant. The right defendant is an instrument that does not exist. The rest of this book is the work of building it."

*Judge note:* Both nail the renewable/finite distinction and the conditional-severance point. A closes on the "miner I meant" Ch1 callback ("Now you have met him. Now we go to the water") — warmer, more bridge-forward. B closes on "elegy and a warning" then a hard return to the wrong-defendant frame — tighter, more thesis-final. A's close is the better *bridge to Ch3*; B's is the better *chapter button*. **Fold-in candidate:** graft A's "This is the miner I meant… Now you have met him. Now we go to the water" forward-bridge onto B's close, after B's "the work of building it." (B already carries the "miner I meant" callback at L31, so this is an echo-and-bridge, not a duplication.)
