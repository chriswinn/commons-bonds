# Chapter 8: What Things Actually Cost

---

What would the world look like if everything sold for what it actually costs?

The question has the sound of a slogan. It arrives wearing a political jersey. Strip the jersey off and underneath there is nothing but arithmetic: a list of costs, a column of figures, a sum. Nobody needs a worldview to add a column of figures, only an agreement not to leave anything out.

So take one ton of coal and do the addition yourself.

Price the lungs: the breath a man loses across thirty years underground, the X-rays that come back wrong, the benefits paid out for decades after the company that owed the money dissolved in a bankruptcy court. Price the water: a creek running orange below an abandoned portal, acid pulling metal out of broken rock long after the last shift went home. Price the community that absorbed the loss when the work left, the county built for a hundred thousand people carrying its roads and pipes and schools on the backs of the few who stayed. Price the foreclosure handed to every generation that comes after the burning: the carbon already in the air, the options already gone. Price the families dispersed down the interstates, and the knowledge that died in the dispersal, the things one man knew about one creek in February that no one left in the valley will ever need to know again. Price the political effort spent keeping all of it off the books, decade after decade, purchased at market rates by people who understood exactly what they were buying. Price the compression: a few hundred million years of stored sunlight, drawn down in a single working century.

Add the items. Write the total at the bottom of the column. Then find the invoice, the real one, the price a buyer actually paid at the mine mouth, and set the two numbers side by side.

Notice the distance.

---

The exercise runs once, on a single ton of coal from McDowell County, West Virginia, the county these pages have kept returning to since the miner first walked into them. One ton, so the gap can be seen whole: unaggregated, undissolved in policy language, priced item by item.

Be clear at the outset about what the exercise is not. It is not degrowth. It is not anti-capitalism. It is not a policy recommendation. It is an accounting exercise, and the framework behind it does not legislate: what a society does with an honest ledger is a separate question, a political one, and this book deliberately does not answer it. The cost components priced below are the ones this particular extraction surfaces. A different extraction surfaces a different set; the method runs anywhere, but the list is always local. Nor does that universality mean every act of extraction carries a hidden cost. Consumption that stays within what a living system can restore severs nothing: a forest cut slower than it regrows, a fishery harvested under its replacement rate, carries a clean invoice. The hidden cost appears when taking outruns restoring, which for coal, a thing the earth restores on no human calendar, is always.

A cost earns its place in the ledger by surviving four plain checks. First comes the inversion: ask who actually bears a cost when the party who should have been billed cannot be found or cannot pay, and follow the cost to the bodies and places where it landed. Second, the units must stay consistent, dollars per ton, so the items can be added. Third, every figure must be bounded, anchored low, kept finite. Fourth, no item may overlap another, so that nothing is counted twice. The Technical Appendix carries the formal machinery; none of it is needed to follow the addition.

---

Stand at the mouth of a tunnel in southern West Virginia in 1960 and watch a loaded rail car come through. The car rides low on its springs. The coal in it is Pocahontas-seam bituminous, some of the best metallurgical coal ever taken out of the ground in North America, and the car rolls across a scale, and a number is recorded, and the train takes the grade out of the county toward the coke ovens and the power plants and the open sea.

McDowell County out-produced every coal county in the nation in those years while its own people stood in lines for federal surplus food. The contradiction was famous enough that a presidential candidate named John Kennedy came through during the 1960 campaign to look at it. The candidate left. The coal kept leaving too.

At the mine mouth, that coal sold for $4.71 a ton, just under five dollars, in the federal price series the Energy Information Administration keeps. And the moment the car cleared the scale, as far as the market was concerned, the accounting was complete. A buyer had paid. A seller had been paid. The transaction cleared, the coal was gone, and every ledger anyone kept agreed that the matter was settled.

What the market did not price was not a rounding error at the edge of that transaction but the majority of the actual cost. The measurement chapter built the instruments for saying so with care: two independent ways of pricing what was lost land in the same range, and what polities have actually paid back, where they have paid anything back at all, sets a floor beneath both. Here those instruments go to work on one ton. The rule throughout is conservatism: the low end of every range, contested externalities left out entirely, every figure either anchored to a named source or owned explicitly as the framework's own estimate and labeled as such. The goal is a floor a skeptic can stand on. Even the floor sits far above the cleared price.

---

Begin with the lungs.

Coal dust does its work slowly and from the inside. It settles in the small airways, and the tissue that meets it scars, and the scarring spreads across years until the lung that remains moves less air with more effort. Men who have it describe the same progression: first the hills get steeper, then the stairs do, then the distance from the truck to the porch. The clinical name is coal workers' pneumoconiosis. The men who carry it call it black lung, and the federal occupational-health surveillance that tracks their deaths (NIOSH) puts the loss at 8 to 13 years of potential life per man it kills.

Black lung is the most completely documented externality coal has ever produced: federally tracked, specifically attributable, and paid for, when it is paid for at all, out of a public program. Congress established the federal Black Lung Benefits Program in 1969, and an earlier chapter walked its history and its trust-fund shortfall in detail. Two numbers from that history matter here. Cumulative federal black-lung benefits ran to $44 billion through 2009 (GAO and the Congressional Research Service), and when coal companies pass through bankruptcy, their black-lung liabilities are routinely discharged onto that public ledger; the program's trust fund itself stood $5.1 billion in debt as of September 2024 (Labor Department and GAO figures), a debt distinct from, and on top of, the benefits already paid. Allocate the documented benefits against every ton of coal the nation has mined since the program's 1969 establishment and the result is between one and one and a half dollars per ton.

That is the documented core of the component. Around it stand the costs that are just as real and harder to fence. Excess cardiovascular deaths from particulates downwind of mining and processing, which Paul Epstein and his colleagues priced across coal's full life cycle in 2011. Elevated respiratory illness among mining-community children who never set foot in a mine, in the county-level mortality work of Michael Hendryx and Melissa Ahern (2009). Cancers clustered near processing facilities. The medical cost of a population whose life expectancy has come apart from the national average by more than a decade, in the county-level series the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation maintains; for men in McDowell County the divergence reaches thirteen years.

On a per-ton basis, the framework's conservative allocation for direct health, built on the named record above rather than lifted from any single study, is between two and four dollars per ton. Take the low end: $2. But pause on the top of the range before moving on. Four dollars against a $4.71 invoice means that the single most thoroughly documented cost coal produces returns, by itself, most of what the coal ever sold for.

---

Below an abandoned mine portal, the creek runs orange.

The color is iron, and the iron is in the water because of a chemical reaction no one has to maintain. When mining breaks rock and exposes sulfide minerals to water and air, the sulfides oxidize. The oxidation produces sulfuric acid. The acid dissolves heavy metals out of the rock and carries them into the watershed, and the reaction does not stop when the company does. It runs for a century or more, and the treatment that holds it back is perpetual: lime dosing, settling ponds, pumps, maintenance, forever. Acid mine drainage is a specific hydrochemical process with a flow rate, not a figure of speech.

The law that was supposed to pay for the cleanup has never held the money the cleanup requires. The Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977 requires mining companies to post reclamation bonds before they dig. Across Appalachia, roughly $3.8 billion in bonds stands against cleanup work estimated at $7.5 to 9.8 billion, a gap of roughly $4 to 6 billion, with about 633,000 acres of mined land awaiting cleanup, per the Appalachian Voices analysis *Repairing the Damage* (2021); the federal abandoned-mine inventory (OSMRE's e-AMLIS database) puts the national figure close to a million acres. The bond was the instrument that was supposed to keep this cost on the company's books. The bond was set too low, and the difference quietly became public debt and private damage.

The damage is not confined to the water. In communities below mountaintop-removal operations, Michael Hendryx and colleagues reported in the *Journal of Community Health* in 2012 that the odds of a cancer diagnosis roughly double: an odds ratio of 2.03, from a self-reported survey design whose limits the paper acknowledges. Valley fills bury the headwater streams a watershed grows from. Soils downstream of processing carry the residue. The mercury released when the coal burns settles in fish as far away as the North Atlantic (Lee et al., 2016).

Per ton, the framework's allocation for environmental degradation, again owned as its own conservative estimate on the named record: one to three dollars. Take $1. Set it beside direct health and the severed cost is already in the neighborhood of the 1960 market price, and nothing has yet been said about carbon.

---

In 1950 the census counted 98,887 people in McDowell County. By 2020 it counted just over nineteen thousand.

The collapse began as productivity. Within a decade of Kennedy's visit, mechanization had cut the county's mining workforce by more than half: the same coal, fewer men, and no other work waiting. U.S. Steel, the county's anchor employer at Gary, wound its operations down across the 1980s, and the families followed the work out of the hollows, to Charlotte, to Columbus, to wherever the interstates ran. A county does not lose four out of every five people evenly. It loses the young first, then the businesses the young supported, then the institutions the businesses anchored, until what remains is a physical plant built for a hundred thousand carried by the people who stayed.

What stayed behind is measurable. McDowell County is the sixteenth-poorest county in the United States as of 2022, with a median household income of $28,235 against a national median near $67,521 and a poverty rate of 37.6 percent (Census Bureau small-area estimates). In 2015 the county's drug-induced death rate ran 141 per 100,000 residents against a national rate near 16 (CDC mortality data; the county figure is a crude rate and the national figure age-adjusted, a basis difference that narrows the gap without closing it). The number is what community-disruption cost looks like once it reaches human scale: not an abstraction about decline but a count of funerals.

And the carrying costs continue whether or not anyone prices them. Roads must be maintained whether two hundred people drive them or forty. Water systems serve the houses that remain at whatever it costs to keep pressure in pipes laid out for five times the customers. Every public dollar stretches across fewer taxpayers; every closed business takes its multiplier with it; the public programs that hold the county together are paid by state and federal taxpayers, in a county whose ground produced billions of dollars in coal revenue that did not stay.

No external study prices regional collapse by the ton, so the per-ton band here is the framework's own allocation, resting on the named series above: the census collapse, the income gap, the mortality record. Conservatively, five to fifteen dollars per ton. Take the low end: $5.

Stop and add. Three components in, the running total stands at roughly eight dollars per ton, nearly twice what the coal sold for in 1960, and carbon has not yet entered the ledger.

---

Now burn the ton.

In the firebox of a power plant, the carbon that spent a few hundred million years underground combines with oxygen pulled from the open air, and the product goes up the stack. The arithmetic of that moment is precise. Pocahontas-seam coal is dense with energy, about 28 million Btu per short ton in the federal heat-content records (USGS and EIA data for the seam). Apply the EPA's combustion factor for bituminous coal, 93.28 kilograms of carbon dioxide per million Btu (AP-42, section 1.1), and one short ton of this coal releases approximately 2.61 metric tons of carbon dioxide. The gas weighs more than the coal did, because the burning borrows its oxygen from everyone's air. The measurement chapter ran this arithmetic on the national bituminous average, 2.32 metric tons per ton of coal; McDowell's seam burns hotter than average, and 2.61 is the local calibration. A reviewer who swapped the national factor back in would land near $441 a ton in what follows, the national-basis figure; the seam's own recorded heat content is the honest basis here, and the difference is worth being precise about.

The price on the carbon comes from outside the framework: $190 per metric ton of carbon dioxide, the EPA's 2023 central estimate of the social cost of carbon. Multiply. The carbon term on one ton of McDowell coal is 2.61 times $190: $496. Just under five hundred dollars.

Set that one term beside every price this coal has ever cleared. Coal today sells for $40 to $140 a ton depending on grade. The carbon term alone, before any of the other seven components, exceeds every era's market price, by a factor running from roughly three against today's market peak to more than a hundred against the 1960 mine mouth.

And the $496 is only the part of the foreclosure that damage arithmetic can reach. A ton burned is also a ton gone. Whatever future metallurgical applications the seam's particular chemistry might have served, whatever chemical feedstocks, whatever uses no one has imagined yet for a carbon resource of that quality: all of it was foreclosed in the firebox, in a world where electricity, the thing the burning bought, can now be generated without combustion. For coal burned in any era after viable substitutes existed, the carbon term prices the floor of what was destroyed, with the foreclosed options stacked unpriced above it.

The same reasoning shows where the number goes to zero, and an honest instrument has to be able to return zero. If a perfect substitute exists for what is consumed, the honest cost of consuming it is zero, because nothing is actually foreclosed; the next user simply uses the substitute. And consumption within what a commons can restore severs nothing: harvest within regeneration leaves the future exactly as it found it. The number is enormous here because this ton fails both tests, a non-renewable resource burned into a saturating atmosphere. Where either test passes, the ledger writes zero, and the framework has nothing to add.

Add the carbon term to the first three components. Four components in, the conservative floor stands at $504 to 518 per ton.

---

In much of McDowell County, a family's entire asset base is a house in a hollow that cannot be sold: roof sound, mortgage paid, worthless on any market, because the buyers left with the work.

A community that collapses by eighty percent does not take its children with it uniformly. The ones who stay grow up in a food desert, the nearest full grocery an hour away, with one of the country's lowest graduation rates waiting at the end of the bus route. The ones who leave were raised by parents whose work was destructive enough of the body that many did not live to see grandchildren. Either way the inheritance fails, because intergenerational wealth transmission requires something transmittable, and the house in the hollow transmits nothing but the obligation to keep its roof on.

None of this is rhetoric. It is visible in public data. The Opportunity Atlas, built by Raj Chetty and his colleagues from anonymized tax records, traces what happens to children born at the bottom of the income distribution in every county in America. A child born into the bottom fifth in McDowell County has among the lowest odds in the United States of ever reaching the top fifth. The mobility map has a floor, and the county sits on it.

This is the component the inversion surfaces last and least visibly: the cost borne not by the worker but by the worker's family, and by the generations downstream of the family's dispersal. The wage priced the shift. Nothing priced what the shift did to the people who depended on the man who worked it.

There is a literary tradition that priced this cost long before any economist tried, and it deserves credit at length, because the whole move performed in this chapter, finding the true value hidden behind the presented one, is that tradition's move. Black American writing carried it for over a century.

Paul Laurence Dunbar named the strategy in 1896: "We wear the mask that grins and lies, / It hides our cheeks and shades our eyes." The mask in Dunbar's poem is a debt paid to human guile, the face a person presents because the world has made the true face too expensive to show. The miner who has been told the dust is better now, the regulations tighter, the technology improved, and who half believes it while the other half reads the X-rays, wears the same mask. The addition the framework makes is small and ledger-shaped: the mask is not the wearer's alone. It belongs to the system that produces the disease at industrial scale and asks the wearer to grin while it does.

W. E. B. Du Bois named the condition beneath the strategy in 1903, in *The Souls of Black Folk*: the veil, and the doubled consciousness of always seeing oneself through the eyes of a world that looks on in contempt. Frantz Fanon, in *Black Skin, White Masks* (1952), carried the analysis into the colonial and economic register, the register closest to the extraction towns in these pages: the mask as something an economic order requires of the people it draws value from. Ralph Ellison, the same year, gave the condition its long narrative form in *Invisible Man*: a man seen by everyone and witnessed by no one. What the tradition described, decade after decade, was a hidden ledger, the cost of appearing acceptable to a system that was extracting from you, carried privately, never invoiced.

I want to say where my own version of the move came from, because the provenance matters to me. I spent years in information security, where masking is a term of art: hiding a true value behind a presented one, so that what the world reads is not what is there. When I started following costs behind the prices that present them, I thought I was importing a trick from my old trade. The tradition above had done the work generations earlier, without the jargon and at far higher stakes, and it saw the human cost of the hidden ledger long before I had a spreadsheet for it. I would rather the move be credited to the people who paid to learn it than to anyone who arrived later with a column of numbers.

The dollar figure the framework assigns to lineage labor is honest about its own smallness: between one and five dollars per ton, the framework's own allocation anchored on the named mobility data, priced at $2 as the conservative placeholder. Two dollars a ton, next to the carbon term, is almost nothing. Two dollars a ton, multiplied across sixty years of extraction, set against the life of a child whose income possibilities were fixed by the zip code of birth: judge for yourself whether it is anything like a fair accounting.

---

Somewhere in the diaspora of the county there was a retired miner who knew exactly how the West Fork ran in February. He knew it as fluency, accumulated across a life lived in one watershed: which stretches iced first, what the water meant when it changed color after a thaw, what the creek was telling you about the slopes above it. When the county emptied, he did not move somewhere that needed to know it.

Every mining community carried knowledge of this kind. How to read the seams. How to manage risk on one specific geology that punished generic rules. How to keep a particular valley's water system working. How the weather behaved in that hollow, observed across a century of mornings. The knowledge existed because specific people stayed in specific places long enough to accumulate it, and it had no container except those people. When the community dispersed, the knowledge went where the people went, to places where it had no application, and it ended there, unrecorded, with no one to receive it.

The cultural infrastructure went the same way. Congregations. Civic organizations. The mutual-aid networks that could absorb a personal catastrophe, a fire, a death, a bad winter, because everyone knew everyone and help did not require an application form. The replacement cost of the civic infrastructure of a community of a hundred thousand is not straightforwardly priced, and it is certainly not zero.

The ledger takes $1 a ton for knowledge and cultural cost. The item appears at all because the inversion surfaces it, and a careful reader cannot omit a real cost merely because it was never denominated in dollars.

---

None of the costs above were secrets.

Black lung was identifiable as an occupational disease by the early twentieth century. It did not become a basis for federal compensation until 1969, and then only after the Farmington mine disaster of 1968 and a miners' strike, with the West Virginia Black Lung Association forcing the issue; the sequence is in the federal mine-safety record and in Alan Derickson's 1998 history of the disease. Through stretches of the intervening decades, the mine workers' union under its mid-century leadership reportedly declined to press the issue, on the grounds that it would interfere with the mechanization that was delivering higher wages. The same mechanization was producing the dust.

The pattern is the component. The earlier costs in this chapter stayed invisible for decades not because the information was unavailable but because the industry whose invoices omitted them had a financial incentive to keep them unpriced, and spent accordingly: documented lobbying, regulatory capture, suppression and starvation of inconvenient research.

Watch the social cost of carbon itself, the $190 doing the heaviest work in this ledger, move through the same machinery. Under one administration the federal government priced a ton of carbon dioxide at roughly $42, on a global-scope calculation set out in the interagency working group's technical support documents. The next administration cut the figure to between $1 and $7 by the simple expedient of counting only damages inside the United States, a scope change that academic commentary (Gernot Wagner, David Anthoff, Maureen Cropper, and colleagues, 2021) flagged as ungrounded in standard cost-benefit practice. The administration after that restored the global scope and an interim value of roughly $51, and the EPA's 2023 update raised the central estimate to the $190 used here. Carbon physics did not change across those administrations. What changed was how much of the cost would be allowed onto the books, and that is a political fight, and one side of it has more resources than the other.

The fight itself is a severed cost. Oil and gas interests spent roughly $124 to 150 million on federal lobbying in the 2024 cycle, with the broader energy sector near $435 million (OpenSecrets tallies for that cycle), and lobbying is only the visible line item; captured agencies, funded think tanks, and decades of diverted civic attention were all purchased so that numbers like the ones in this chapter would stay off invoices.

Two traditions deserve credit here. The vocabulary for naming the actors and coalitions who keep a cost unpriced, and the people who keep paying while it stays unpriced, comes from the reparations economists and essayists: Ta-Nehisi Coates, William Darity and Kirsten Mullen, Darrick Hamilton, Dalton Conley. The extractor's side of the reasoning, the cold logic of spending a million on politics to protect a billion in unpriced costs, was worked out by the public-choice economists James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock under the name rent-seeking. This book borrows the rent-seeking vocabulary without endorsing the broader political project it traveled with, and the next chapter takes the relationship up at length.

The ledger takes $1 per ton for political capture, a floor chosen deliberately low to keep the total conservative, and almost certainly an undercount.

---

The Pocahontas seam was laid down across hundreds of millions of years: forests growing, falling, compressing under their own accumulating weight, a stock of stored sunlight assembled on a timescale that has no human analogue. Its extraction was compressed into roughly one century, and the bulk of it moved in the few decades after the machines arrived.

The compression was not driven by need. The country did not require coal at that rate. Machinery had raised the rate at which coal could be extracted, and the industry's financial structure rewarded speed over measure, so speed is what happened. The component this surfaces is the cost of when: the foregone option of having moved slower, with time to develop alternatives, time for communities to diversify, time for the externality tail to be absorbed at a survivable rate rather than delivered all at once to a county that had no say in the schedule.

There is a documented glimpse of what the alternative looks like. After the oil shocks of the 1970s, when prices finally said that energy mattered, the energy used per dollar of real output in the United States fell steadily from 1973 through the 1990s; the Energy Information Administration's energy-intensity series carries the record. Price signaled, and the economy adapted. Had coal carried a price closer to its honest cost in 1960, the same signal would have driven the same adaptation, at a pace the hollows could have survived. Instead the suppressed price killed the signal, the machinery compressed the timeline, and the adaptation waited until the damage forced it.

Per ton: $2.

---

Here is the column, complete.

- Direct health: $2
- Environmental degradation: $1
- Community disruption: $5
- Foreclosure, the carbon term: $496
- Lineage labor: $2
- Knowledge and culture: $1
- Political capture: $1
- Temporal displacement: $2

**Total: $510 per ton.**

The progression bears restating once, so no reader has to reconstruct it: the first three components reached roughly $8 a ton, already nearly twice the invoice; the carbon term carried the conservative floor to $504-518; the remaining four close the account at $510. Every component took the low end of its range. Every contested externality stayed out.

Against the $4.71 the county was paid in 1960, just under five dollars, the honest total runs more than a hundred times the invoice. That multiple belongs to a specific lens applied against a specific price: it is the damage arithmetic of this chapter, set against the 1960 mine mouth, and stated that way it is roughly two orders of magnitude. The Technical Appendix names this distance the Intergenerational Pricing Gap and prices it lens by lens: the damage lens returns the hundredfold figure against the 1960 price, while the framework's most conservative lens, the premium of what was foreclosed over what the market paid, a ratio in which the era's market price cancels out entirely, still returns roughly 8.5 to 26 times, centering near 15. The appendix's full multi-generational integral, the most demanding lens it runs, returns roughly 61 to 115 times against the same prices, and the appendix carries the decomposition.

Against today's prices, $40 to $140 a ton depending on grade, the carbon term alone still exceeds the market by a factor of at least three.

However the gap is priced, the structural finding does not move. The honest cost of a ton of this coal has exceeded what the market paid in every era of its extraction. The county sold the ton for less than five dollars and bore costs around five hundred and ten.

---

If McDowell County were an exception, the arithmetic would be a curiosity. Run the same accounting across the other extractions examined earlier in this book and the same shape returns every time.

Deepwater Horizon. The Macondo well was projected to produce 40 to 60 million barrels over its lifetime; that is the reservoir estimate, the well's planned output, not the volume spilled, which a federal court later fixed at about 3.19 million barrels released into the Gulf. The documented costs of the disaster, cleanup, penalties, settlements, ran to roughly 15 to 16 times the revenue the well was projected to earn. And the documented accounting still omits the layer this chapter prices: burned, the well's projected production would have released roughly 17 to 26 million tons of carbon dioxide, an additional $3 to 5 billion in carbon externality at the $190 anchor. Adding it shifts the multiple only slightly, because the documented costs already exceed the revenue by an order of magnitude. The consistency is the point: the framework that prices coal's carbon tail prices oil's the same way.

Libby, Montana. The vermiculite mine W. R. Grace operated above the town shed asbestos-laced dust over it for decades, into the air, the gardens, the ballfields, the insulation of the houses. Priced on the damage lens, the costs run to roughly sixty-five to a hundred times the mine's lifetime revenue. A town's lungs, against one mineral deposit's receipts.

Baotou, Inner Mongolia. The rare-earth processing that anchors the global electronics supply chain maintains a tailings lake beside villages whose documented environmental and health costs follow the same structure: value leaving for the world's factories, costs pooling within walking distance of the lake. The documented record there does not support a computed multiple, and honest accounting says so rather than inventing one; documented remediation estimates run in the range of $5 to 15 billion. The shape is unmistakable even where the arithmetic cannot be finished.

The Exxon Valdez put eleven million gallons of crude into Prince William Sound, against which the market value of the cargo is a gap that defies simple calculation only because the recovery was never completed. Case after case, the ledger comes back lopsided the same way.

Nor does the structure confine itself to mines and wells.

Run the components against a cost-burdened renter in any expensive metropolitan area and the same headings populate. About half of American renters now pay more than 30 percent of income for housing (Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies, *America's Rental Housing 2024*). Above that line, the squeeze prices out the things mobility is made of: the move closer to the better job, the training course, the savings cushion, the children's continuity in one school. Foreclosed mobility is a lineage cost by another name, because what cannot be saved cannot be transmitted. And the rules that hold the squeeze in place, the zoning maps and tenant law and the politics of who may build what where, are written in processes where asset owners reliably appear and renters mostly cannot, which is capture operating at municipal scale. Value flows to the owners of the asset; costs accumulate in the households. The arithmetic differs from McDowell's in every line item. The structure is identical. A serious objection waits here, and it deserves naming rather than waving past: a substantial body of research holds that housing costs are driven mainly by restrictions on supply rather than by what asset owners capture, and that the remedy is building. The objection is not weak, and the next chapter makes its case in full and answers it; the claim here is only that the costs are real and that they land where the rent burden lands.

In northern Alberta, bitumen from the tar sands is priced against heavy-crude benchmarks while the costs fall elsewhere: on the boreal forest and peatlands stripped to reach it; on the Athabasca Chipewyan, Mikisew Cree, and Fort McKay First Nations, whose territories host the operations under Treaty 8 of 1899; and on whoever inherits the carbon in the barrels, wherever they are burned. Elevated cancer rates and contamination downstream of the operations have been reported and disputed since a physician, John O'Connor, raised the alarm in 2006; the Alberta Cancer Board's 2009 review and a later study commissioned by the Mikisew Cree found patterns that industry and province contest, and causation remains alleged rather than settled. Run the framework's accounting on a barrel of bitumen and the gap that returns is the same order of magnitude coal returns.

The structure scales past extraction into finance. The housing collapse of 2008 destroyed roughly $11 trillion in American household wealth (Federal Reserve financial accounts) and produced roughly 4.1 million completed foreclosures through 2012 (CoreLogic's count; filings ran far higher). The federal response deployed roughly $700 billion in direct authorization plus trillions more in liquidity and guarantees, nearly all of it directed at recapitalizing bank balance sheets rather than restructuring household debt, a scope choice contested at the time and documented since (Atif Mian and Amir Sufi's *House of Debt* carries the analysis). The next chapter returns to what the alternative architecture would have looked like under honest pricing.

Different industries, different geographies, different decades, different resources. The same mechanism produces the same gap between what the invoice said and what the extraction actually cost, because the accounting system was built to track only one side of the ledger.

---

Three misreadings of the $510 need answering, because each is natural and each is wrong in an instructive way.

The first misreading: that honest pricing claims the extraction should never have happened. It claims no such thing. Cheap energy, even severely mispriced energy, lifted billions of people out of poverty, and no responsible reading of the last century can pretend otherwise. The claim is narrower and harder. Whoever bore the cost should have been compensated at rates reflecting what they actually sacrificed. The miners, the collapsed communities, the children born into a foreclosed county, the generations absorbing the climate cost: real people paid, and are still paying. The question was never whether the lights should have come on. The question is who paid for them, and whether anyone asked.

The second misreading: that honest pricing claims the market was fake. Prices cleared transactions. Money changed hands. Real coal left on real rail cars, and every party to the visible bargain received what they bargained for. The claim is that the visible bargain was not the only one being struck. Alongside the transaction the market could see ran a second transaction, unpriced, unwitnessed, binding on parties never consulted: the miner's lungs, the creek, the county's future, the chemistry of everyone's air. The price was not false. It was incomplete.

The third misreading is the most serious: that honest pricing is a ladder pulled up by nations that industrialized on cheap extraction against nations still climbing. If that were what honest pricing required, it would be a compelling moral defeater, so take it at full strength. The answer is structural. Whoever created the historical cost owes its repayment, and two centuries of severed costs sit overwhelmingly on the ledgers of the developed world; honest pricing therefore generates, in the same motion, the revenue that funds clean-industrialization pathways for developing economies, owed as repayment of historical severance rather than offered as charity. Differentiated transition timelines are a structural feature of honest accounting, not an afterthought bolted on to soften it: the parties that severed longest owe most and move first. And the economics has shifted beneath the objection while it aged. Solar electricity is now cheaper than coal across most of the developing world, so the ladder the objection imagines being pulled up is, increasingly, no longer the cheapest way to climb.

---

Scale it, briefly, and with the brakes on.

The finding that survives every lens has a consistent shape. The most conservative lens the framework runs, the foreclosure premium that does not care which era's price you use, still returns a multiple in the lower double digits, centering near fifteenfold. The carbon term alone runs more than a hundredfold against the 1960 invoice. The same shape returned in every case swept above. So suppose, very conservatively, that full-cost pricing would multiply fossil revenues by only two to three. The global fossil-fuel industry turns over roughly $4 to 5 trillion in annual revenue, on the accounting of the International Energy Agency and the industry's own statistical reviews. Two to three times that revenue puts the annual severed cost of fossil extraction on the order of $8 to 10 trillion. Add the non-fossil forms, mining, logging, fisheries harvested above regeneration, aquifers drawn down faster than they recharge, topsoil lost faster than it forms, and the global figure lands somewhere between $10 and $15 trillion a year.

Put that at human scale. It is larger than every government subsidy program on Earth combined. It is larger than all international development assistance. It is larger than all charitable giving on the planet combined. As far as I can tell, it is the largest continuous wealth transfer in human history, and it flows in one direction: from the communities closest to extraction to the consumers farthest from it, from the future to the present, from those with no voice in the market to those who have one.

Be clear about the register of that claim. One sentence of supporting arithmetic stands behind an assertion that, taken seriously as a policy agenda, would require years of sectoral simulation and implementation design, work that cannot honestly be compressed into the closing pages of a framework book. The figure is a gesture at scale, named as such. I will name it and leave it there.

One reflex deserves disarming before the close, because it arrives on schedule whenever a number that size appears: the objection that repricing on this scale would crash the economy, with GDP as the proof. GDP measures market transactions, not welfare. Cleanup spending counts as production. Medical bills count as production. Addiction treatment counts as production. GDP does not fall when people die young. Honest pricing would reduce GDP in the narrow technical sense while increasing welfare in nearly every sense the measurement was ever meant to proxy, because it shifts the composition of activity rather than destroying the total. There is precedent at smaller scale. Leaded gasoline was banned; the refineries reformulated; the industry did not collapse; and the blood-lead levels of American children fell in step with the phaseout, with the cognitive gains documented across a generation (the EPA's phaseout record and the CDC's national blood-lead surveillance carry the curve). Honest pricing repeats that pattern at larger scale.

---

A repricing of everything raises questions that deserve better than a closing page. What happens to energy costs in the first years, the first decade, the new equilibrium. Who absorbs the transition, and in what order. What happens to a household in the bottom 40 percent when essentials rise sharply. Where the pricing surplus goes, and who decides. Whether the economy on the far side is still recognizably capitalist.

Every one of those questions is real. The framework's mathematics, its simulations, and the structural necessity of a universal floor produce specific answers to them, and those answers sit outside the scope of this book, with the policy-makers, scholars, communities, and coalitions who will fight over them, which is where such answers have always belonged. What honest accounting at civilizational scale would reveal is not a catastrophe but a more stable, more resilient economy than the one we have: an economy with a floor beneath it.

One worked example stands at the bottom of the column. A ton of coal, priced honestly, lands at a floor around $510. The invoice said just under five dollars. The distance between those two numbers is the severed cost. The invoice is what the county was paid. The severed cost is what the county bore.

The next chapter sketches the direction a civilization committed to honest accounting would move. The last returns to the people who have carried these pages, the miner and the waterman among them, and lets them recognize one another across the distance the mechanism was built to maintain.
