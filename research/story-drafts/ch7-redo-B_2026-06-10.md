# Chapter 7: On Other Worlds

A fair reader could grant every page of this book so far and still hold one reservation. The coal was mispriced, yes. The oyster bars were drawn down, the costs landed on people who had no say, the ledgers did not tell the truth. But every case so far happened on one planet, inside one political economy, under arrangements of lobbies and regulators and balance sheets that Earth's extraction industries spent two centuries building. Perhaps the underpricing is a fact about those arrangements rather than a fact about pricing. Perhaps the framework is a political critique of one civilization's bookkeeping, dressed up as a general instrument. If that is what it is, it deserves to be read as that, and its claims shrink to fit.

There is one clean way to find out. Take the instrument somewhere Earth's political economy cannot follow. Run it where there are no lobbies, no regulators, no industry with a stake in the answer, and see whether it still returns the answers a careful planner would independently recognize as correct. If it does, the mispricing of the earlier chapters stops looking like a local scandal and starts looking like the ordinary consequence of a general law, operating on a planet that has learned not to notice it.

So: Mars. Not for romance. Mars is the off-world body humanity has actually been sending instruments toward for half a century, which means the parameter ranges for extracting from its ground are measured rather than invented. But nothing that follows is Mars-specific. The same logic holds on the Moon, on a free-flying station, on an asteroid platform, on habitats no one has imagined yet. What matters is the shape: a small bounded system, far from help, whose people depend for survival on a few finite local resources. The conclusions are conclusions about bounded systems with the distances compressed.

One thing should be said flatly before anything else is said. What follows is a thought experiment, offered as one. The habitat does not exist. The administrator does not exist. The consortium does not exist, and no decision is pending in any year a reader could mark on a calendar. The device is a test bed for the framework's logic, not advocacy for settling other worlds; whether humanity should pursue off-world settlement is a separate question, with serious arguments on both sides, and it returns near the end of this argument on its own terms. Everything inside the habitat is constructed and announced as constructed. Everything outside it, the launch economics, the labor systems of dead empires, the lifetime of the Sun, is sourced and can be checked.

The test will also turn up something the earlier chapters have been using without naming: a variable that explains why cost severance works the way it works on Earth. It becomes visible only in the place where it disappears.

## The Administrator's Problem

Picture a habitat in its established middle age. Roughly 10,000 people, born there or long since committed to it, living under pressure domes that have held for decades. The settlement is self-sustaining in the ways that matter and dependent in one way that matters more: water. Surveys have confirmed 3 subsurface water-ice deposits within reach. At current consumption, drinking, agriculture, electrolysis for oxygen, industrial use, the deposits hold roughly a 50-year supply.

A trading consortium comes to the habitat's resource administrator with a proposal. Accelerated extraction: profitable in the near term, the consortium's return delivered inside 5 years, and the remaining water horizon cut from roughly 50 years to roughly 20. A 60 percent cut in her habitat's future, in exchange for a return that will be captured and banked before the first shortfall registers. She must decide.

It is worth being precise about what she is not deciding. She is not adjudicating discount-rate philosophy. She is deciding whether to shorten her habitat's water by 60 percent for one consortium's near-term gain, knowing that the people who will live with the consequence include her own descendants; that the school dome and the agricultural domes sit a short walk from her office; that the consortium, once its return is captured, will operate under incentives that no longer include her habitat's far horizon. The consortium is not villainous. It is following the incentives in front of it. That is what makes the case useful.

Now walk her problem through the framework, one term at a time. The residual commons value of Chapter 6, the RCV, prices what is foreclosed when a unit of a commons is consumed, and it is built from four terms.

Substitutability asks the probability that an adequate substitute exists at a given future time. For Martian water in its survival-critical uses, drinking, agriculture, electrolysis to oxygen, that probability sits near zero. The chemistry has no substitute at the relevant scale. The speculative alternatives, harvesting the thin atmosphere, importing cometary ice, synthesis from rare feedstocks, are neither operational nor on any guaranteed trajectory inside the extraction horizon. Hope is not a supply line.

Stock-dependent utility asks what each remaining unit is worth as the stock falls. Water is abundant to an empty planet and scarce to 10,000 people with 3 deposits. Each remaining unit grows more valuable as the stock shrinks; the utility curve steepens toward a survival threshold and then stops being a curve. Past that point it is a cliff.

The externality tail asks what damage continues after extraction ends: contamination of the remaining stock with drilling chemicals; destabilization of adjacent formations; vented volatiles the habitat's air systems must scrub for years. And at the outer edge, one possibility that does not fit in any column: if Martian subsurface microbial life exists in those deposits, acceleration could end it. That loss has no denomination. The value at stake is scientific and philosophical, and no currency carries it.

Discount weighting asks how heavily the future counts against the present. Here the future is the same population's own near future; the people of year 40 are the children in the school dome. The administrator weights them almost in full: a discount rate near zero, which is to say a discount factor near one. Discounting a 50-year horizon at terrestrial cost-benefit rates would be a slow way of voting against her own grandchildren.

Every term sits at an extreme. Substitutability near zero; utility at life-or-death; the stock small and fixed; the tail potentially lethal, possibly beyond pricing; the future weighted essentially in full. The RCV comes out extraordinarily high, and the recommendation that falls out of it is unsubtle. Reserve the deposits. Ration. Price each unit on a scale that rises as the stock declines. Invest in closed-loop recycling. Refuse the consortium. The near-term return is trivial against a foreclosure cost the inhabitants would bear themselves, in person, on a date many of them will live to see.

No one needs a framework to reach that answer. Any thoughtful administrator of any inhabited place on any world would call it obviously correct. That is exactly what the test requires: a case where the right answer is unambiguous, so that the instrument's agreement with it counts as evidence about the instrument.

## One Parameter at a Time

Hold the model where it stood for her decision and start moving the dials.

Increase the stock. The pressure from stock-dependent utility falls; the recommendation loosens. Increase substitutability, give the habitat a second source or a working synthesis pathway, and the foreclosure component falls; it loosens further. Shrink the externality tail, make the extraction clean, and it loosens again. Weight the future less, suppose the population is transient and the habitat is scheduled for decommissioning, and it loosens further still.

Run the dials far enough and the parameter space resolves into two regimes. At one combination, abundant, substitutable, clean, reasonably discountable, the recommendation converges on market pricing: the honest price approximately equals the market price, cost severance is negligible, extract freely. At the other, scarce, substitution uncertain, a long externality tail, the future weighted heavily for some structural ecological or intergenerational or existential reason, the recommendation diverges sharply: the honest price vastly exceeds the market price, cost severance is large, extract only under extreme accountability or not at all. Between the two runs of the dial, nothing in the model changed. Only the parameters did.

And Earth is not categorically different from the habitat. It is the same kind of system with different parameter values. Coal's stock is enormously larger than 3 ice deposits, and it is being drawn down with zero restoration; coal does not grow back. Coal's substitutability for generic electricity is high today, solar, wind, nuclear, hydro can carry the load, but for specific hydrocarbon chemistries in specific industrial uses it is lower than a casual reader expects. Coal's externality tail, the scarred lungs, the acid drainage in the watersheds, the carbon in the air, persists long after the mine closes. And the weight Earth should put on intergenerational harm is not a settled number; it is the precise subject of one of the longest-running arguments in economics.

That argument is worth naming, because it shows the question is unavoidable rather than settled. In 2006 the Stern Review argued for a near-zero pure rate of time preference, on the ethical premise that future generations cannot be discounted merely for arriving later. William Nordhaus, in the DICE tradition, answered with a market-calibrated rate, on the methodological premise that climate policy must be judged against what the same capital would have earned elsewhere. The original exchange ran at roughly 1.4 percent for Stern against a Nordhaus rate of around 4 to 5 percent, and that gap, compounded over a century, translates into a vast difference in how much mitigation counts as correct today. Beside them both stands John Hartwick's 1977 rule: invest the rents from exhaustible-resource extraction into reproducible capital sufficient to leave future generations no worse off. Hartwick's rule operationalizes the intergenerational-equity premise Stern's framing depends on, and it is the kind of discipline the framework's accountability-bond logic instantiates: if you must draw the stock down, bind yourself to replace what the drawdown forecloses.

None of this settles the discount rate, and nothing here will try. The point of the lineage is narrower. The weight-of-future-people question cannot be waved off; the best economists of two generations have stood on opposite sides of it. Mars makes the answer easy because the future people are the habitat's own next generation and cannot be pretended away. Earth makes it hard because the future people are strangers, and strangers are easy to discount.

So the universality claim, stated plainly. Every parameter that drove the administrator's decision exists on Earth. The values differ; the structure is identical. And the same instrument that told her to preserve the Martian deposits says, applied honestly to Appalachian coal, that the resource has been severely mispriced for a century.

## The Sweep

One scenario proves nothing. A model tuned to make Mars water come out sacred could be a model rigged for sanctity. Universality needs range: cases where the right answer is preservation, cases where it is extraction, cases where it is neither, and an instrument that finds each one for the right reasons. So run the sweep.

Iron from an uninhabited asteroid. Iron is abundant across thousands of accessible bodies. Substitutability is high: other asteroids, Earth's own ore, recycled orbital structure. The externality tail is minimal; there is no atmosphere to foul, no ecosystem to break, no community to displace. Stock-dependent utility is low. The RCV falls to approximately the market price of the extracted metal, and cost severance, under any reasonable orbital-debris and energy-accounting bond, is near zero. Recommendation: extract freely. This is the lowest-RCV case the model recognizes, and it should be.

Helium-3 in the lunar regolith. Here the model meets a resource with essentially no current industrial use. Its potential value hinges on whether a particular fusion cycle, deuterium and helium-3, ever achieves net energy gain, a technology horizon no one can date. For that hypothetical use it has no substitute by definition. The stock is large, roughly 1 million tons in the regolith by the estimate of Wittenberg, Santarius, and Kulcinski in 1986, though that figure is an estimate and should be held as one. Extraction is energy-intensive and hard to reverse. The model does not pretend to know the probability of fusion. It observes instead that preserving lunar helium-3 costs almost nothing, and the Asymmetric Regret Rule applies cleanly. Preserve, and if fusion never arrives, nothing is lost. Extract, and if fusion later proves viable, what may be the most important fuel in the solar system is gone. Recommendation: preserve, wait, reassess as the technology matures. The insurance is free.

Common regolith on the Moon, for habitat construction. Same body, different resource. The regolith is abundant beyond any depletion horizon, highly substitutable for construction, and carries a modest externality tail of dust and operational contamination. Stock-dependent utility is negligible. Recommendation: extract freely, manage the dust, build. The same location has now yielded opposite recommendations for different resources, because the parameters differ by resource. A model that said preserve everything on the Moon, or extract everything, would be useless to anyone actually deciding.

Volatiles from a long-period comet during flyby. Now the situation inverts toward extraction. The resource is reachable only during a short window, weeks to months, before the comet leaves for millennia. The externality tail is near zero; nothing lives there, nothing downstream is disturbed. Substitutability is moderate; other comets exist. Asymmetric regret flips. Not extracting permanently forecloses access to this body's material; extracting costs little. Recommendation: extract aggressively while the window is open. The model is not conservation-biased. It is parameter-sensitive, and when the parameters favor extraction it says so.

The subsurface ocean of Europa. Here the model meets its boundary, and the boundary deserves to be stated carefully because it is one of the framework's most important behaviors. If an independent biosphere exists in Europa's ocean, the scientific and moral value of possibly destroying it is genuinely not priceable in dollars. The externality term cannot be filled with a number, and the model does not manufacture one. It says, honestly, that it cannot price this externality; that the Asymmetric Regret Rule therefore dominates, because losing the only other known biosphere would be absolute and irreversible while the cost of waiting is only time; and that the extraction decision should be deferred until the science can answer the question. This is the model behaving correctly, not failing: naming a limit where a limit exists rather than faking completeness. Counting what can be counted has never required pretending everything can be. The same refusal returns at the end of the sweep, on Earth, in a smaller key.

Rare earth elements near an inhabited Mars habitat. This case carries the sweep's deepest structural insight. Suppose the habitat's people mine rare earths locally to make their own electronics rather than importing from Earth. Substitutability depends on the supply chain: moderate if Earth imports are reliable, near zero if they are not. Utility is high; strategic independence is worth a great deal to people 200 million kilometers from the nearest factory. But the crucial feature is where the externality tail lands. The dust from the mining contaminates their own air filtration. The chemical waste must be managed inside a closed system that has no downstream, no somewhere-else for anything to go. Worker injuries draw on the habitat's own limited medical stores. The inhabitants breathe their own emissions. And so cost severance collapses toward zero, not because the costs are small but because the extractor and the affected community are the same people. There is no one to sever the cost to. If extraction happens, it gets priced at full internal cost, because the inhabitants will pay that full internal cost whether or not the ledger admits it. What they pay and what extraction costs converge automatically. That convergence extends to Earth in a way the off-world cases have not yet shown, and the sweep's last case will show it.

Rare earths on a living exoplanet. One more constructed case, offered in the same announced register as the habitat, because it combines the two pressures the prior cases kept separate. Imagine rare earth deposits on a distant world that also bears, or plausibly bears, a biosphere of its own. The resource pulls hard toward extraction with care: rare earths' magnetic, catalytic, and electronic properties have no general substitute for the long-horizon applications a technological civilization keeps finding, so the gap between what current industry could replace and what the far future might need is wide. The living world pulls the other way. Unlike Europa, the externality here is not wholly beyond denomination; a biosphere's contamination risk is real and partly priceable even while its destruction would not be. So the case sits in tension rather than at a clean boundary, and the model's behavior in the tension is the instructive part. It does not refuse to price, as it did for Europa. It does not wave the resource through, as it did for asteroid iron. It recommends extraction only under extreme accountability and a minimized footprint: RCV high, the cost severance reducible by a large accountability bond, the B of Chapter 6, sized honestly enough to internalize the biosphere-damage risk and fund the footprint's minimization. This is the model in the hard middle, the regime where a large and honestly sized bond is what makes responsible extraction defensible at all.

And then the sweep comes home, to a case with no off-world body in it and, more important, no extracting counterparty at all. A person in suburban Maryland signs a lease. The house is cheaper out there; the office is in Washington, D.C.; the commute will run on the order of 5 hours a day, round trip. No corporation extracts anything. No community is displaced. The cost-bearer chose the arrangement freely, with the destination and the travel time knowable in advance.

Run the same arithmetic the model ran on asteroid iron and will run on McDowell coal. Roughly 5 hours a day, across roughly 250 working days a year, multiplied by the years of the lease: thousands of hours of life. Against any defensible valuation of those hours, lost wages, lost family time, lost rest, depleted attention, the slow accumulation of chronic stress, the true price of the lease runs to many multiples of the roughly $20,000 a year saved on housing. The cost was severed from the decision as surely as any cost in this book. But the mechanism that severed it was not a corporation. It was a past self underpricing a future self's time. Temporal asymmetry: the person who signed was deciding for the person who would drive. Informational asymmetry: the lived reality of hour 4 on a Tuesday in February was not internalized at signing, though the data sat in plain view. Normative routinization: long commutes are an accepted shape of working life, so the cost arrived pre-approved. Abundance masking: hours of life feel unlimited until the arithmetic collapses the illusion. Four of the concealment patterns this chapter is about to name, operating inside one person, across time, with no bad actor anywhere in the story. The Commons Inversion Test, applied at the kitchen table before signing, would have surfaced the lease's real price while it was still a decision rather than a routine.

One discipline, stated here because this is where a reader will first want it. Pricing those hours is not a claim that hours of a life reduce to dollars. The figure is a lower-bound accounting of what was given up, the part of the cost that can be counted, exactly as the model's silence at Europa marked the part that cannot. The two cases are one discipline seen from its two ends: price the severance, always; price the worth of a life, a biosphere, a sacred thing, never. The model defers the question of ultimate worth and counts what was severed. That is the whole of its ambition, and the limit is load-bearing.

From iron on an uninhabited rock, through water under a town of 10,000, through fuel for a technology that may never exist, through a window closing on a comet, through an ocean that must not be touched yet, through miners breathing their own dust, through a bond sized to a living world, to a lease signed in Maryland: the same structure, priced the same way, recommending differently every time the parameters differ. Costs systematically underpriced get found whether or not any counterparty is doing the underpricing on purpose.

## What Severance Requires

Cost severance, named in Chapter 2 and developed since: the act of cutting the cost of extraction loose from the profit of extraction and transferring it to people who had no say in the transaction. A transfer needs a transferee, and it needs a structural arrangement that lets value flow one way while cost stays behind. The cases in this book keep surfacing the same small set of arrangements, alone or in combination. Six recur often enough to deserve names.

Geographic distance. Coal leaves McDowell County and becomes electricity somewhere far away; the value gathers to shareholders and consumers separated by distance and regulatory layers from the people who pay in lungs and watersheds.

Temporal asymmetry. The same arrangement stretched across time instead of space: the extractor is far away in years, the cost-bearer is unborn and cannot bid.

Informational asymmetry. The 2008 housing collapse is the clearest exemplar. The bankers and the underwater homeowners shared a country, a legal system, often a city. What severed cost from value was what one side knew, and when. A cost-bearer cannot refuse what they cannot see in time.

Power asymmetry. Coerced labor severs cost from value through coercion alone. Enslaver and enslaved often shared the same ground; the conscripted laborers of the pyramids worked within sight of the monuments' beneficiaries. Distance was zero and information was symmetric. What severed the cost was that the bearer could not refuse.

Normative routinization. When cost-bearing has gone on long enough to become an accepted baseline, the cost of doing business, severance operates with full information and no formal coercion, because the culture has done the work that distance and force did earlier. The miner accepts the lung damage. The town accepts the closure. The consumer accepts cheap power as honestly priced because the cost never appeared on an invoice.

Abundance masking. When a resource looks unlimited, no one prices it, and the costs flow continuously, uncounted. Air is the paradigm. The same dynamic ran through the ocean fisheries in the decades before their collapses, when harvest outran regeneration; through the Ogallala aquifer as center-pivot irrigation, arriving in the mid-20th century, let drawdown outrun recharge; through the atmosphere's carbon-absorbing capacity as emissions outran absorption; through soil wherever depletion outruns its regenerative pace. In each case the abundance was real, and what it hid was a rate: a commons being drawn down faster than it could restore itself. "Masking" is the right word because the hiding is structural rather than perceptual, built into industrial and agricultural architectures, and the closed habitat below will show the mask collapsing by physical construction, which no mere error of perception could do. The formal treatment is Theorem 10.3 of the Technical Appendix, with the Commons Inversion Test as its detection apparatus.

These six, geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry, power asymmetry, normative routinization, abundance masking, are illustrative, not exhaustive: patterns observed in this book's cases, offered in the spirit of Ostrom's eight design principles, Nussbaum's ten central capabilities, Rawls's primary goods. The operational test is the arrangement itself, value flowing one way while cost stays the other, not whether any one named pattern is present, and readers applying the method to their own cases may surface patterns this list does not carry. One warning attaches to the list. An extraction practice that addresses each named pattern with surface-level interventions has not addressed cost severance; it has simulated addressing it. The chapter returns to that simulation later, because the architecture of extraction has learned to perform accountability, and the performance has a name.

## A Place Without Cover

Now put the six patterns back inside the habitat and watch what happens to them.

Geographic distance collapses by construction; in a pressurized settlement, there is no far away. Informational asymmetry collapses: the system is small and closed, the feedback is direct, the water meters are public infrastructure, and no party to the consortium's proposal knows less than any other. Power asymmetry collapses: the administrator's own family stands among the cost-bearers, and the habitat has no coercive apparatus to make anyone absorb a cost they can see. Normative routinization has not had time to form; nothing about rationed water has been background long enough to disappear into it. Temporal asymmetry remains technically available, a present self can still underprice a future self, but the closed loop returns the consequence in months rather than deferring it for generations, so the asymmetry has nowhere to hide. And abundance masking fails by construction, because every liter is enumerated, and every breath and every drink visibly counts against a finite stock. All six of the arrangements, the full set Chapter 2 promised would someday be seen failing together, fail together here, at once, in one place.

Which exposes the variable the framework has been relying on all along. Severance is not impossible on Mars; it is impossible to hide. Call the variable concealment availability. An administrator who underprices the water still produces severance, against her future self, against the future population. What she cannot do is keep the cost off the books, because the closed loop puts it on the books by physics, and a cost that is visible gets priced. Earth's mispricing has never depended on Earth being different in structure. It has depended on Earth being a place where the cost can be hidden.

Widen the lens one step and the habitat exposes something larger than three ice deposits. A resident of that habitat, visiting Earth, would not see what we see. Where we see ordinary ground and ordinary sky, the visitor would see free air, in any quantity, unmetered. Free water falling on a usable schedule. Free temperature regulation within a survivable band. Free continuous solar input, free gravitational and tidal stability, free magnetic shielding, free ultraviolet filtering. Every line item a closed habitat budgets and maintains, Earth supplies ambiently, for nothing, and has for so long that the ledger does not carry a column for any of it. Earth's ambient abundance is the dominant cost-hiding apparatus on the planet. The Commons Inversion Test exists to ask, of each part of that abundance, the only question that matters: is it being drawn down faster than it restores itself?

On Earth the compression of a habitat is not available, so cost severance hides behind some combination of the six patterns, and the combination matters, because it determines which interventions can reach a given case. McDowell coal severed mainly through geographic distance and informational asymmetry. The 2008 collapse ran mainly on informational asymmetry. The pyramids ran on power asymmetry. Modern industrial subsidy runs on normative routinization. The atmosphere's carbon-absorbing capacity was spent under abundance masking. The policy half of this book builds on that mapping: collapse distance with place-based and spatial cost-severance pricing and border carbon adjustments; collapse temporal asymmetry with intergenerational accounting; collapse informational asymmetry with transparent supply-chain disclosure; collapse power asymmetry with institutional accountability requirements; collapse routinization by making the hidden baseline legible as a price; collapse abundance masking with honest stock-and-flow accounting. No instrument on that list addresses the underlying structural test by itself, which is why a policy regime can hit each named pattern with a surface intervention and leave cost severance fully operating underneath.

## Not All Resources Matter Equally

The sweep leaves a residue that deserves its own section: the model does not treat all resources the same, because its parameters do not. For some resources the RCV collapses to near the market price, and market pricing is approximately right. For others it runs orders of magnitude higher, and a century of market pricing has been a century of systematic underpricing. The difference is a property of the resource, not a choice by the analyst, and the property has a name.

Call it the existential substitutability gap: the difference between a resource's substitutability for current industrial applications and its substitutability for the long-term civilizational applications, known and unknown, that a species with a long planning horizon would want to keep open. In the framework's Substitutability Function S, the gap is S_max(industrial) − S_max(existential). The concept borrows its horizon from the existential-risk literature (Bostrom 2002; Ord's *The Precipice*, 2020; MacAskill's *What We Owe the Future*, 2022) as pricing apparatus for resources critical to civilizational continuity, while adopting none of that literature's population-ethics or total-utilitarian commitments.

Ranked by the gap, the familiar resources sort themselves. Coal sits at the bottom, near zero: its one product, heat for electricity, is already substitutable by solar, wind, nuclear, hydro, geothermal, and gas, and no foreseeable civilizational application uniquely needs coal. Sand, common iron ore, and common stone sit beside it. Conventional oil, copper, and zinc sit a step up, low but not zero: substitutable for most current uses, with no clear future application that uniquely requires them. Uranium, lithium, and cobalt occupy the middle, where the hedges live: thorium and eventual fusion stand behind uranium baseload, sodium-ion and solid-state chemistries are advancing alongside lithium, iron-phosphate cathodes already relieve cobalt, yet each plays a strategic role now, and future applications plausibly, though not certainly, depend on the specific material. The rare earth elements and the platinum-group metals stand near the top. Their specific magnetic, catalytic, and electronic properties are what technologies not yet invented will be built from: neodymium and dysprosium for permanent magnets at usable energy densities, platinum and palladium for fuel-cell and electrolyzer chemistries, gallium, germanium, and indium for the next generations of semiconductors. Substitutes exist for some of their current uses; there is no clear path to substitutes for all of them. And helium-3 sits in a category of its own, conditionally high: one of the most strategically important resources in the solar system if deuterium-helium-3 fusion ever works, a curiosity if it never does, its whole classification contingent on an undemonstrated technology.

The ranking is not a complete reserve-or-extract map. It is one input, which combines with each resource class's stock estimate and externality tail to produce the reservation policies of the later chapters. The claim being made for it is modest: the ranking returns answers a careful reader will recognize as intuitively right, and the resources worth worrying about reserving for descendants are the ones whose gap is high, because those are the things descendants might need and cannot replace.

Chapter 9 returns to this ranking at the moment it matters most. The renewable-energy transition runs on motors, magnets, electrolyzers, fuel cells, and battery cathodes, which is to say it runs on rare earths, platinum-group metals, cobalt, and nickel, the high-gap names on this list. Getting the transition wrong on precisely these resources would foreclose the civilizational capabilities the transition exists to deliver. The pricing logic of this chapter and the policy architecture of that one meet on this list.

## Two Objections

The first: Mars is a false analogy, because Mars is simple and Earth is not. The habitat has 10,000 people, 3 deposits, transparent feedback, and a mispricing that kills within a decade. Earth has unclear boundaries, vast and partly understood stocks, continuous new discovery, innovation that shifts substitutability year by year, and political and economic feedback so slow that a mispricing can outlive everyone who committed it. On Mars the administrator gets the answer right because she cannot afford to get it wrong. The terrestrial policymaker, extractor, and consumer operate under no comparable constraint. What works in the clean little system, the objection runs, says nothing about the sprawling dirty one.

The objection is right about the complexity and wrong about what the complexity touches. Earth's complications change the parameters: stocks less certain, substitutability curves contested, externality tails harder to bound, the discount rate an open argument between Stern and Nordhaus. None of them changes the structure of the pricing problem, which is the same in both places: a commons is being drawn down faster than it can restore itself, the costs and the benefits are distributed across space and time, and the question is how to price the drawdown so that the price reflects those distributions. Complexity makes the parameters harder to estimate, and leaves the problem the same problem.

That is also why the thought experiment helps rather than cheats. Mars strips away the layers of uncertainty under which Earth's cost severance has operated for a century; the parameters are stark enough that the right answer is unmistakable. On Earth the parameters are moderate, reasonable people can disagree about each one, and disagreement is exactly the condition under which severance persists at scale. Physics teaches with frictionless planes and thermodynamics with perfect insulators, not because the world contains either, but because the limiting case exposes the law, which then applies in the messy world with corrections for friction and leakage. The administrator's water problem is the frictionless plane of resource economics. The principles it exposes apply, imperfectly and noisily, to every real extraction decision on this planet.

Settling that objection invites its opposite. Mars is a thought experiment; there is no habitat, no administrator, no consortium, no pending decision. Importing science fiction into a serious resource-economics argument, the objection runs, is a category error.

The methodological answer was settled long before this book. A thought experiment is not a prediction; it is a stress test of a logical apparatus. The frictionless plane is fictional. So is the ideal gas, the perfectly rational agent, the frictionless market, the electron at rest in an inertial frame. Each has been more useful to its discipline than a thousand empirical cases, and each is foundational to a field nobody now calls science fiction. The habitat does not need to be real to do its work: the framework's structure produces outputs that can be checked against intuitions thoughtful readers actually hold, and the checking is the test. Had the model told the administrator to accept the consortium's proposal, or declared the self-bearing rare-earths case indistinguishable from asteroid iron, readers would have noticed, and the framework would have been falsified by an exercise that costs nothing but careful reading. That is what the thought experiment buys that the empirical record cannot: scenarios where the right answer is unambiguous, run against the instrument, with every reader free to score the result. An instrument that passes at the extremes is the one worth trusting in the noisy middle.

There is also a longer answer, on which the experiment stops being fiction at all. The Sun's luminosity is rising. Mainstream solar-physics models project that the increase will render Earth uninhabitable for biological civilizations on the order of 500 million to 1 billion years from now (Caldeira and Kasting, 1992), long before the red-giant expansion that engulfs the planet itself roughly 7.5 billion years out (Schröder and Smith, 2008). On those horizons, whether humanity or its descendants attempt to live on other worlds is a question of when rather than whether, and the behavior of a pricing instrument on bounded off-world habitats is a coherent forward-looking concern even though no present-day decision turns on it.

None of which is advocacy. The habitat here is a test case for a pricing instrument, not an argument that humanity must accelerate Mars settlement or treat off-world living as a present priority. The existential-risk literature frames off-world inhabitation as one long-horizon mitigation among several; a skeptical academic lineage answers that off-world expansionism is geopolitically destabilizing and a distraction from near-term stewardship of Earth. Both sides have serious arguments, and the framework holds whichever way that question resolves.

One last version of the objection comes from the opposite direction: for asteroid iron, the model just reproduces market pricing, so what has it added? Exactly what it should have. A well-calibrated instrument agrees with existing instruments where they are correct and diverges only where they are wrong. Market pricing is approximately right for asteroid iron, no externalities, no displaced community, no disrupted ecosystem, no foreclosed future access, so the novel terms collapse to zero, RCV collapses to market price, and the recommendation is the market's own. Where the market misses something, the divergence appears, sized to what was missed; where the market misses nothing, nothing is added. A framework that always disagreed with the market would be as useless as one that always agreed. The one worth having is the one that identifies when the market errs, and by how much.

## The Long Record, and the Immune Response

Two more pressure points bear on the universality claim, and both deserve direct answers rather than gestures.

The first is temporal: is all of this just a critique of capitalism wearing a longer coat? The clearest answer is the pattern operating in civilizations that had no contact with each other, no shared cosmology, and no inheritance from which a common ideology could have come.

Stand at Giza around 2550 BCE. Over roughly 70 years, the span of the whole necropolis program rather than any single monument, Khufu's pyramid alone took about 20 years, the Old Kingdom raised the pyramids with skilled craftsmen and conscripted rotational laborers drawn from the agricultural population. The revised archaeological consensus, built from Mark Lehner's Giza Plateau Mapping Project and Zahi Hawass's excavation of the workers' cemetery, is careful on both edges: these were not chattel slaves in the plantation sense. They were paid in rations, given medical care, buried properly within sight of the monument. They were also not free in any sense a modern reader would recognize. The centralized pharaonic economy left no alternative employment, no mobility beyond the Nile Valley, no structure within which refusal was possible. The pharaoh captured the value: political legitimacy, divine kingship, monumental architecture, provision for the afterlife. The laborers bore the cost in their backs and their lifespans, and the monument still standing on the plateau is permanent evidence that the mechanism operated 4 and a half millennia before anyone had a word for capitalism.

Roughly 23 centuries later, on the other side of the world, Qin Shi Huang unified the warring states and commissioned the first unified Great Wall, spanning roughly 5,000 kilometers of northern frontier, the Qin wall, not the later and longer Ming wall a tourist walks today, together with a mausoleum complex near modern Xi'an of which the Terracotta Army, found by well-diggers in 1974, is one fragment. Sima Qian, writing in the *Records of the Grand Historian* about a century after the dynasty fell, reports the Wall built by 300,000 to 500,000 concurrent conscripts, peasants, prisoners, convicts, with mass mortality he records without apology, and a mausoleum workforce he puts upward of 700,000. All those figures are his reports and should be held as his reports. What is not in doubt is the organizing philosophy, which was explicit: legalism, with strict penalties for missed conscription quotas, merit-graded administration, efficient production regardless of who bore the cost. The dynasty collapsed within 4 years of the First Emperor's death, under peasant rebellions. The pattern, value extracted by the imperial state, cost borne by labor whose exit was foreclosed, is legible on nearly every page Sima Qian gives the period.

Egypt and Qin China had no contact. Their cosmologies, religions, political systems, and philosophies of rule were incommensurable. The mechanism operated in both. Bring the record closer and it keeps operating: feudal lords extracting agricultural surplus from serfs working land they did not own, the serfs bearing the cost in their bodies, their families, and a poverty whose exit was foreclosed by birth. Each version arrived with a narrative explaining why the cost-bearer's condition was natural: divine order, the Great Chain of Being, the serf's God-ordained station. The lord called it protection. The company town called it paternalism. Today's extraction apologists call it culture. The vocabulary updates; the move is the same move, framing the cost-bearer's condition as a property of the cost-bearer rather than a product of the extraction.

So: cost severance is not a feature of capitalism; it is a pattern that uses whatever economic system is available as a delivery vehicle. Capitalism is the current vehicle and the most efficient one yet built. Feudal agriculture ran a version. Pharaonic divine kingship ran a version. Qin legalism ran a version, and Soviet industrial planning ran its own. The mechanism requires no particular ownership structure. It requires the conditions the cases in Chapter 5 exhibit, and those conditions are not ideologically parochial. The pyramids and the Great Wall have stood longer than any political vocabulary we still use.

The second pressure point is contemporary, and it is the one the simulated-addressing warning earlier in this chapter pointed toward. When cost severance becomes publicly visible and consumers begin demanding accountability, the extraction architecture does not simply yield. It can mount what is best understood as an immune response: manufacturing a performance of accountability that does not reattach the costs it claims to.

The textbook case is the Kimberley Process certification of "conflict-free" diamonds. A consumer pays an ethical premium. The diamond carries a certificate. And the structural pattern is that the premium stays with the retailer and the certification infrastructure rather than reaching the miners in whose conditions the cost was born; the conditions of artisanal miners are not substantively improved by the certificate's existence. The bond between payment and cost-bearer is severed, and the certificate is a performance of non-severance. The pattern generalizes. Fair-trade certification that does not substantively raise farmer income. Carbon offsets that lower the marketing cost of emissions without lowering emissions. ESG screens that satisfy fiduciary appearance without altering where capital actually flows. In each case the architecture has adapted to frameworks like this one by building a simulated-reattachment layer: an instrument-shaped object that performs accountability without delivering it. A framework that ignores this adaptation will be captured by it, certified, screened, offset, and fully severed.

The discipline this imposes on the rest of the book is exact: distinguish accountability bonds that actually reach the cost-bearing party from mechanisms that merely appear to. The design problem belongs to Chapter 9, and it is hard, because verification structures must resist the same adaptive pressure that produced the simulation in the first place, a problem that runs straight into the Public Choice tradition's analysis of rent-seeking architecture. Chapter 9 also weighs what the existing partial implementations, the Norwegian sovereign-fund discipline, the early-twentieth-century Nordic policy architectures, the reachable-horizon institutions it catalogs, already get right and where each remains open to capture.

The two pressure points together locate the mechanism in time. It is older than its most visible contemporary forms and more sophisticated than its most visible contemporary critics, which is why the testing had to run to the limits. What survives the limits is an instrument that holds where the parameters change and names its boundaries where it cannot reach.

## One Ton

What the test established can be said in a few plain sentences. The framework produces different answers for different situations. It agrees with market pricing where the market is right, for asteroid iron and lunar regolith, and diverges where the market is wrong, by an amount sized to what the market missed. It names its own limits where an externality is not priceable, at Europa and at every Earthly analogue of Europa. And along the way it exposed the quiet variable underneath the whole book: cost severance at scale requires arrangements that hide the cost from honest accounting, which means the policy response on Earth is partly a project of collapsing the hiding, pattern by pattern, in whatever combination is carrying the load in a given case.

One accounting remains before the policy chapters can begin, and it is owed to the resource and the community this book opened with. One ton of Appalachian coal, from a specific mine, in a specific county, in a specific year, walked through the framework tier by tier: every cost category named, every number conservative and defensible, and the sum set against the price the transaction actually cleared at. In 1960, at the mine mouth in McDowell County, by the United States Energy Information Administration's historical series, that price was just under five dollars a ton.

The next chapter computes what the honest invoice would have said. Hold the two numbers side by side as you turn the page: the small one the market wrote, and the one it should have written.

## A Note on the Numbers Behind the Thought Experiment

The off-world scenarios are constructed, but the economics underneath them is not, and a reader is owed the parameter ranges.

Launch costs to low Earth orbit fell by roughly an order of magnitude in about 15 years. The Space Shuttle era's fully loaded cost ran near $54,500 per kilogram (Jones, NASA ICES-2018-81); the current commercial baseline runs roughly $2,000-5,000 per kilogram, with Falcon 9 at about $2,720 per kilogram as the clean anchor in the same analysis. Newer heavy vehicles, Vulcan Centaur and New Glenn, have reached orbit. A further order-of-magnitude reduction, to roughly $200-1,000 per kilogram in the next decade, is plausible but unvalidated, contingent on Starship-class vehicles reaching operational maturity.

Mars remains far costlier than orbit. Recent science missions delivered roughly a kilogram of operational hardware to the Martian surface for on the order of $2.5 million, a constructed ratio, total mission cost over delivered rover mass, dominated by interplanetary cruise and entry, descent, and landing rather than launch. A habitat at the scale imagined here would carry, under current economics, delivery costs in the tens to hundreds of billions of dollars before in-situ resource use or next-generation transport changed the arithmetic.

Asteroid-mining economics exist mainly as proposals. The Keck Institute's asteroid-retrieval study of 2012 and the pre-acquisition projections of now-defunct mining ventures targeted per-kilogram costs orders of magnitude below current science-mission figures. Those are actor-stated targets, not validated operational numbers, and they are quoted here as targets only.

None of this is a delivery-cost forecast. The asteroid-iron conclusion, that RCV approaches market price under any reasonable accountability bond, does not depend on which cost per kilogram eventually settles, because the structural argument turns on substitutability and externality, both near zero for asteroid iron regardless of delivery economics. The numbers are here for one purpose: to show that the parameter ranges the thought experiment runs on are grounded in measured engineering rather than in fiction, even where the habitat itself is offered, beginning to end, as the fiction it announced itself to be.
