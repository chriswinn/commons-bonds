## (2) ORDERED BEAT / SECTION INVENTORY

Eleven sections. Each is described by what it must accomplish and what it must contain, not by any wording from a prior draft.

### Beat 1, Opening: the pattern you cannot stop seeing
- Open on the mechanism as something that, once seen, recurs with mechanical regularity: an entity extracts value from a resource, community, or system; the costs of that extraction are detached from the entity that benefited and shifted onto a party with less power, less mobility, less ability to refuse; and when the reckoning arrives, the original beneficiary is no longer reachable to pay.
- State the through-line as a short aphorism: the reckoning always arrives; the only open question is who is still standing where it lands.
- Preview the cases as a single sentence-list keyed to escalating scale (a vermiculite mine in Montana; an oil platform in the Gulf; a tanker on a reef in Alaska; a rare-earth complex in Inner Mongolia; a national financial system in 2008; the retirement compact between generations; the healthcare system that routes its shortfalls through crowdfunding and unpaid family labor).
- Land the thesis: each case has different arithmetic; the mechanism is identical.

### Beat 2, Libby, Montana (the town; spatial severance + concealment + legal-architecture failure)
- W.R. Grace operated a vermiculite mine at Libby, 1963-1990 (~27 years); the mine produced a large majority of the world's vermiculite; Zonolite insulation reached tens of millions of American homes; the operation was profitable.
- The vermiculite was contaminated with a lethal form of asbestos. Internal documents released in later litigation show the company identified contamination early and measured asbestos exposure in workers throughout operations, while continuing to mine. Worker families exposed via dust carried home, and children near waste piles, developed disease.
- Human toll: hundreds of residents dead of asbestos-related disease; thousands diagnosed with lung abnormalities; EPA declared the town a public-health emergency (the first time it made such a declaration for a man-made disaster).
- The financial reckoning: Grace's revenue against the documented downstream costs (medical care, remediation, settlements, lost property value, health monitoring) is the severance made legible. **See §6 for the canonical figure treatment, the old "$4B / 40:1" is NOT clean; use the disclosed framework-estimate treatment.**
- Grace declared bankruptcy in 2001; assets reorganized; shareholders and executives moved on; the town remained.
- **Legal-architecture failure beat (load-bearing, places Pistor; see §5):** the 2005 federal indictment of Grace and senior executives (the largest environmental criminal prosecution of its time, naming executives by role) ended in a 2009 acquittal after a long trial. The acquittal is not evidence the harm did not occur; it is evidence that the criminal-law architecture, built to prosecute identifiable acts with identifiable knowledge at identifiable moments, is not structurally suited to a mechanism that operates across decades through accumulated decisions. This is the Pistor point: the bankruptcy discharge and the acquittal are design features of the legal modules that encode and protect capital, not aberrations.

### Beat 3, Deepwater Horizon (the ecosystem; spatial + temporal + ecological severance; cross-border asymmetry)
- The 2010 blowout killed eleven workers and released roughly 4.9 million barrels over 87 days; the operator had drilled through formations its own engineers flagged as extreme-risk geology.
- BP had extracted substantial value from the Gulf; name the lifetime/blowout-well value bracket (see §6).
- Severance in every form: spatial (oil refined and sold far away poisons waters where local fishermen had worked for generations); temporal (damage persists long after quarterly reports move on); ecological (keystone species absorb toxins affecting reproduction for decades).
- Penalties/settlements vs total losses: BP's penalties and compensation fund against the aggregate economic loss yields a recovery ratio far below 100 percent, name the bracket and the ratio per §6 (do NOT state the old "$150B total" or "40 percent" as documented fact; use the corrected/ranged treatment).
- Cross-border asymmetry beat: the same spill reached Mexican Gulf waters; the BP-Mexico settlement was orders of magnitude smaller per comparable coastline damage than the U.S. settlements. The asymmetry tracks the legal architecture available to each jurisdiction's claimants (U.S. Oil Pollution Act 1990 + state tort/environmental law + class-action aggregation vs. a weaker cross-border architecture for claims against a foreign-domiciled corporation), not the physical distribution of the slick.
- Close on the unmonetizable costs that never appeared on any ledger BP had to balance: lost generational fishing knowledge, cultural disruption, severed ecological relationships.

### Beat 6, 2008 and the double risk transfer (the whole economy; the scale-hinge; full four-objection engagement; Mian-Sufi macro consolidated IN)
This is the chapter's longest and most load-bearing case. It must be present at full substance, the full four-objection engagement lives here and NOWHERE else in the book (see §8, D1). It also absorbs the 2008-macro material consolidated in from Chapter 9.

- **The first transfer:** the 1978 creation of the 401(k) structure and the subsequent replacement of defined-benefit pensions with individual accounts, presented to workers as freedom/ownership. The undisclosed reality: the freedom was chiefly the corporation's freedom from liability. Market, longevity, and behavioral risk moved from institutional balance sheets to individuals with no capacity to pool or hedge. This ran quietly for ~30 years.
- **The second transfer:** the compressed 2001-2008 creation of mortgage-backed securities and CDOs, instruments whose complexity was structural (only complex instruments could be sold to investors who could not evaluate them), packaged into the same retirement accounts. When they collapsed in 2008, the losses landed in those accounts.
- **Mian-Sufi macro (consolidated IN from Ch9; RATIFIED, see §9):** the household side of the crisis. Reference *House of Debt* (Mian & Sufi). The ~$11 trillion household net-worth drop; the leverage math of why low-net-worth, high-leverage households lost a multiple of what wealthier households lost (the levered-losses mechanism: a highly leveraged homeowner's equity is wiped out by a price decline that only dents an unleveraged balance sheet). **Precision (see §7): Mian-Sufi support a roughly 3x MPC differential, NOT "3-4x"; the brackets are income/leverage deciles and top/bottom quintiles, not "bottom 40% / top quintile", state it as an MPC differential, ~3x, cited to the QJE 2013 work + House of Debt, not as a published "welfare benefit ratio."**
- **The asymmetric rescue:** TARP's $700B; the Federal Reserve's cumulative emergency lending exceeding $16 trillion (per the GAO 2011 audit, this is the one well-sourced 2008 figure, see §7); executive bonuses resuming within ~18 months; against roughly ten million foreclosure filings 2008-2012 (~five million completed). **Reconcile to "ten million" filings / ~five million completed, do NOT drift to "eleven million" (see §8).** The system mobilized trillions in days for institutions and organized nothing comparable for the households whose accounts had absorbed the losses and whose homes were foreclosed.
- The Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission's 2011 conclusion that the crisis resulted from human action and inaction, not forces beyond control.

- **The four objections, each steelmanned then answered (FULL, this is the ratified must-include, D1):**
  1. **Borrower agency.** Many defaulting mortgages went to borrowers who could not service them under any reasonable model, and the borrowers signed. Answer: agency requires information; the information architecture was structurally adversarial (origination commissions tied to volume not default rates; disclosed terms differing from contract terms; adjustable-rate products marketed to borrowers whose grasp of rate-reset mechanics was expected to be incomplete; the CFPB post-crisis enforcement record documents the pattern). The agency was real; the information asymmetry was the lever.
  2. **Global, not U.S.-policy.** The European exposure, Asian export contagion, cross-border MBS holdings, the Basel framework's role are real. Answer: the global character amplifies rather than dissolves the U.S.-mechanism finding, the propagating instruments were predominantly U.S.-originated MBS/CDO; the originator-fee architecture, the issuer-pays rating-agency conflict, and the scaling exemptions were U.S. policy choices.
  3. **TARP was repaid.** Treasury accounting records substantial recovery; some accounts show a modest profit (GAO/CBO retrospectives). Answer: TARP is the wrong accounting unit. The cost the chapter prices is the unrecovered welfare loss to the foreclosed households, the workers whose account balances reset to a lower compounding base, the regional economies that contracted, and the political-trust commons depleted by the asymmetric-rescue pattern. None is recovered by TARP repayment; none appears on the federal-balance-sheet flow accounts the recovery argument cites.
  4. **Dodd-Frank fixed it.** Dodd-Frank created the CFPB and the FSOC, restricted proprietary trading (Volcker Rule), and imposed enhanced capital/liquidity requirements, real changes. Answer: it is a partial implementation of accountability-bond architecture, but reform that is partial and reversible (the Volcker Rule's later relaxation; the CFPB's later institutional weakening; contested systemic-importance designations) has not closed the gap and has not been consolidated to the point where its persistence is structurally guaranteed. **Forward-promise discipline (see §8, B-4): if you state "Chapter 9 will name Dodd-Frank as an entering wedge," that promise must be kept by Ch9; the seam decision is that Ch9 names it, so the promise may stand.**

- **Sweden 1930s contingency comparison (consolidated, see §9):** Sweden in the 1930s faced banking-crisis pressure structurally analogous to the U.S. in 1929-33 and 2008-09, and combined bank-rescue WITH substantial household relief: the 1931 krona devaluation / gold-standard exit restoring export competitiveness and household-debt purchasing power; coordinated bank-rescue paired with mortgage modification; mid-1930s public-works employment programs restoring household income; the 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement institutionalizing the welfare-state architecture. **Date-verify the program years against §7 (Swedish-1930s dates are a flagged item).** The comparison is cited in the comparative-banking-crisis literature (Kindleberger; Eichengreen) as demonstrating bank-rescue and household-relief are not either-or, a society can choose both. The U.S. chose otherwise in 2008-09 and again in 2020. The choice is contingent. Note Chapter 9 develops the broader cross-country comparison.

### Beat 7, Social Security (intergenerational scale; TIME as distance; the $108T figure handled per the ratified NET treatment)
**This section contains the most consequential figure discipline in the chapter. Read §6 and §9 carefully before drafting it.**

- Set the frame: the most devastating example hides inside the system Americans treat as the opposite of extraction, the promise that each generation cares for the one before it.
- Mechanics: designed 1935 as pay-as-you-go; current workers fund current retirees; for decades the system ran surpluses held in special-issue Treasury obligations in the trust fund; the baby-boom retirement wave was foreseeable for forty years; by the early 2020s outflows exceeded payroll-tax inflows; under current law and projections the trust fund depletes around 2033, after which payroll-tax revenue funds roughly three-quarters of promised benefits.

- **THE $108T FIGURE, RATIFIED HANDLING (banned-gross / disclosed-net; see §6 + §9):**
  - The gross "$108 trillion foregone compounding" figure is **BANNED** as a cost-severance number. It fails reconstruction from its own inputs (it is roughly two orders of magnitude above the nearest authoritative counterfactual and exceeds the trust-fund peak, cumulative program income, and equity-vs-bond differential ceilings). It must NOT appear as a real cost-severance magnitude, and the phrase "largest cost severance in human history" must NOT be attached to it.
  - The gross figure may appear **ONLY** as the named-and-debunked "dishonest version", stated, then dismantled in the prose.
  - Use the **disclosed NET treatment**: cumulative contributions over the program's life (~$26T) approximately equal cumulative benefits paid (~$26T); only the running surplus (the accumulated reserve, on the order of ~$2.7T) was ever actually investable. The honest counterfactual foregone-return on that investable surplus is on the order of a **few trillion dollars, not a hundred**. Use the qualitative floor: the severed cost **runs into the trillions**. Disclose the method (which base compounds, at what assumed real return, netting pay-as-you-go payouts) inline or in a brief methods note rather than asserting a fragile headline number.
  - Resolve the internal inconsistency: the qualitative "tens of trillions in unfunded obligations" framing elsewhere in the section refers to the Trustees' actuarial shortfall (~$22.6T, 75-year), a DIFFERENT quantity from the foregone-investment counterfactual, keep them distinct and labeled.

- **The severance is in obligation-generation, not portfolio choice (the structural core, keep this; it is the chapter's strongest move on this case):** the cost severance is not located in how the surpluses were invested (holding reserves in Treasuries was administratively prudent). It is located in how the obligations were generated. Benefit expansions across the 1960s-1980s were enacted by Congresses whose members would, in nearly every case, leave office before the demographic-transition obligations matured; they were funded by the favorable working-age-to-retiree ratio of exactly those decades; and every actuarial report of the period openly projected the deficit window the expansions would eventually hit. The political incentive to expand during the demographic-surplus window without funding the deficit window the actuaries identified is what created the gap. Time is the distance: value-extraction (the electoral value of the promises) was severed from cost-bearing (the future taxpayers who fund the deficit).
- Contrast with Norway (callback to Ch4, see §8): the 1935 U.S. architecture (pay-as-you-go, surpluses as Treasury obligations) was a contingent choice; Norway's GPFG channels resource rents into a compounding sovereign fund that accumulates capital for future generations. Same structural problem, different architectural choice, different outcome, not a function of national wealth or demography but of architecture chosen at specific moments.
- **Two objections steelmanned then answered (keep both):**
  1. **Public-finance prudence.** Holding reserves in Treasuries is exactly what a sound fund manager does; equities would have exposed retirees to market and political-economy risk. Answer (concede fully): the bond-holding is prudent at the portfolio level; the chapter's claim operates upstream, at the obligation-generation level, a portfolio-level defense does not address it.
  2. **Timeline / tractability.** The gap is solvable through phased adjustments (payroll-rate increases, taxable-wage-cap changes, benefit-formula refinements at the high end, eligibility-age changes tracking longevity) per the Trustees and various commissions. Answer (concede the arithmetic): the gap is mathematically closeable, but every closure transfers the obligation onto the cost-bearing party (the demographic-deficit-window cohort who pay more or receive less), not onto the cohorts who extracted the electoral value of generating the obligation. Tractability is not the absence of severance; it is severance unfolding on schedule.
- Hold the line that Social Security is not a Ponzi scheme, it is a genuine, essential intergenerational transfer; the critique is of the specific funding architecture, not the program.

### Beat 10, "The Same Object, Named Honestly": Polanyi / Fraser / Sandel (THE SIGNATURE ADDITION)

- **Set up the structural-commons identity:** a commons is defined structurally, not by whether it is ecological, anything depended-on and shared but not produced as a commodity. ### Beat 12, Restitution and Foreclosure: the two-instrument response + lineage (naming + academic/legal grounding)
This is the diagnosis→prescription pivot: the cases produce a question (what would honest accounting of these severances require?), and the framework's two-instrument response is the answer the remaining chapters elaborate. Name the apparatus at the level of STRUCTURE only, Chapter 6 formalizes it, Chapter 9 handles political implementation. (Lineage-roll-call discipline: keep it from burying the reader; see §8 + §10. Break the lineage across the cases it grounds rather than listing authors back-to-back.)

- **The bidirectional structure:** the accountability gap has two temporal faces, harm already done (the realized extraction-portfolio: lives, communities, ecosystems already harmed) and harm not yet done (future-generation costs current extraction is foreclosing). An architecture addressing one without the other addresses half the problem.
- **The two instruments:** the Restitution Bond discharges harm already realized (the backward/person-affecting instrument); the Foreclosure Bond discharges potential future-generation costs current extraction forecloses (the forward/impersonal instrument). Together they form the aggregate accountability bond the framework's central relation requires (cost severance = residual commons value minus bond posting).
- **Why "bond":** the umbrella term is deliberate against the rejected alternatives (tax: paid at transaction against general revenue, no architecture matching a posted amount to a future obligation; fee: licenses activity rather than discharges harm; fund: aggregates without discharging). A bond is posted before the full cost is borne, carries forward across the cost's long tail, and discharges observably by a specific figure against a specific liability. Place the real-world bond lineages: reclamation bonds under the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act; oil-and-gas decommissioning bonds against well closure; Norway's sovereign fund at national scale; reparations economics treating the obligation as a documented historical debt (bond-like in structure); the civic-republican standing obligation against future generations for which bond posting is the natural accounting form.
- **Restitution vs Reparations (the named-asymmetry explanation):** the instrument is "Restitution," the grounding tradition is "Reparations", intentional. Restitution (restitutio in integrum) is the older, broader legal-philosophical term for the obligation to restore what was taken; the framework needs an umbrella broad enough for the full field of cost-severance contexts (environmental, occupational-disease, community, ecosystem). The Coates-Darity-Mullen-Hamilton-Conley reparations tradition is the primary informing source, its methodology is how the framework knows what it knows about backward-looking accountability, but "Reparations Bond" would narrow the umbrella to one historically specific application and make the methodology's portability read as analogical extension rather than direct application. Note why the narrower alternatives fail (Restoration presupposes physical restorability many harms do not allow; Indemnification routes through an insurance register the framework does not use; Damages collapses the bond into the measurement instrument).
