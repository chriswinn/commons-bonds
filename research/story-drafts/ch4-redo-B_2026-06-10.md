# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*

## Chapter 4: The Existence Proof

---

Three days before Christmas, 1969, on a concession block of the Norwegian North Sea numbered 2/4, a drill found oil. The sea above it was doing what the North Sea does in late December. The well became Ekofisk. Under and around it lay one of the largest petroleum reservoirs the industrialized world had ever found, and it belonged, by the lines on the concession map, to a nation of fewer than 4 million people, a fishing economy strung along the cold eastern edge of the Atlantic, a country that had spent its history pulling protein out of the sea and was now, abruptly, sitting over a sea floor of hydrocarbons.

What a country does next, in that position, is among the most consequential decisions it will ever make. It is a decision, though almost nobody standing at the fork experiences it as one. The oil does not come with instructions. Someone decides who captures the value coming up out of the seabed, and someone decides who carries the costs of its coming up, and the two decisions are usually made in different rooms, on different timescales, by people who never meet. Every resource-rich jurisdiction on earth has stood at this fork. Most never noticed it was there.

Most spend the windfall. Some spend it quickly and badly. Some spend it carefully, on real things, schools and roads and tax relief, and still spend it, inside the consumption horizon of a single generation. Alberta built a heritage trust fund to capture a slice of its oil royalties and then reached into it, again and again, whenever prices fell, so that the fund never grew to anything like the scale of the resource it was named for. That is the careful version of the common path. Norway, almost uniquely, chose otherwise. The choice was argued and debated like any other public question, and then it was enforced, structurally, across decades, by rules built for one purpose: to remove from every future government the discretion to undo it.

Out of those two paths comes the claim this book has been moving toward since the coal counties of Chapter 2. Cost severance is a policy choice. The same barrel, from the same seabed, sold into the same market, produces radically different outcomes depending on the institutional architecture that decides who captures the value and who bears the cost. The architecture is engineered. It is not handed down by geology, or culture, or markets, or luck. And what has been engineered once can be engineered again, somewhere else, by someone else.

Norway is the existence proof.

---

Every Norwegian citizen alive today, the newest newborn included, is co-owner of a fund whose per-person share comes to roughly $390,000 (NBIM; Statistics Norway). No Norwegian can spend their share. That is the design.

The machinery behind the number is concrete and almost deliberately dull. In 1990 the Storting, Norway's parliament, established the Government Petroleum Fund. The first deposit went in six years later, in 1996. In 2006 the fund was renamed the Government Pension Fund Global, though no pension draws on it. Money enters three ways: taxation of the private operators on the shelf, at a marginal rate near 78 percent; state production licenses; and the state's direct ownership stake through Equinor, the national oil company called Statoil until 2018. Counted together, the Norwegian state captures roughly 70-80 cents of every dollar of net petroleum wealth that comes out of its sea floor (Norwegian Petroleum Directorate; Ministry of Finance).

The fund invests none of it in Norway. The money goes outward, deliberately, into global equities, bonds, and real estate spread across thousands of companies in dozens of countries, so that the oil wealth compounds on the productive output of the wider world rather than churning through the small economy it came from. At the end of 2025 the fund held more than $2 trillion (NBIM Annual Report 2025), built from roughly 55 billion barrels of oil equivalent produced through 2024, about 57 percent of what the Norwegian Offshore Directorate estimates the shelf will ever give up. In recent years the fund's investment returns have exceeded the state's revenue from extraction itself (NBIM); in those years, the savings out-earned the oil.

The withdrawal rule is the keel. In 2001 the Storting capped what any government may take in a year at the fund's expected real return, judged then to be 4 percent. In 2017, with the fund vastly larger and the temptation vastly larger with it, the rule was revised downward, to 3 percent. The direction of that revision is the tell: as the money grew, the discipline tightened. The principal is never drawn down. The fund is designed to outlive the oil, and everyone now living.

Around the keel, the rest of the rigging. The fund is operated by Norges Bank Investment Management at arm's length from political direction. A public ethics council reviews holdings and forces divestments in the open. Quarterly reports put the balance where every voter can see it, so that any breach would be politically visible within months. Structural change requires a supermajority of the Storting. None of this machinery is secret or clever. All of it is aimed at one failure mode: the future government that wants the money now.

A Norwegian born the year of the first deposit turned 30 this year. For the whole of that life the fund has been there, growing, public, untouched at its core, through changes of government, through oil-price crashes, through every populist season with a use for the money and every industry season with a case for pumping faster. Every administration that followed, of every party, has held the rule. A consensus running from center-left to center-right treats the fund's integrity as something closer to a constitutional fact than a budget line. Each generation has inherited capital sufficient to transform its standard of living and has declined, explicitly, year after year, to consume it.

The discipline was designed in from the beginning, by people who had done their reading. In 1971, two years after Ekofisk, the Storting set down ten principles for the new resource, still known in Norway as the ten oil commandments, and the ministry white papers of that decade show the planners studying the countries oil had found first. What they saw resolved into two failure modes. In the first, the windfall is consumed within a generation: a boom, a swollen state, a bust. In the second, the wealth is captured by elites or foreign operators, and the population over the resource is left holding the costs. Norway's architecture was built to close both doors at once.

---

Hold one barrel of Norwegian crude still for a moment and look at what is in it. Part of it is fuel, burned and gone within weeks of landfall. Part of it is feedstock for things that have no scalable, cost-competitive substitute at industrial volume yet: the medical-grade plastics of a surgical suite, its tubing and seals and sterile film; jet fuel for the aircraft fleet that actually exists; asphalt; lubricants; the specialty chemistries behind carbon fiber. A barrel is energy, and it is also material options, and the two deplete together.

Now ask about Norway the question this book asks about every extraction: what would honest pricing have counted, and what was actually paid? Norway looks like the case where the question has no purchase, which is exactly why it has to be asked here.

Begin with what Norway solved. Call the value a polity actually internalizes against a commons it draws down the accountability bond; the Technical Appendix gives the instrument its formal definition, and later chapters put it to work. Norway's bond is enormous: 70 to 80 cents of every dollar of net petroleum wealth, captured and held in permanent public form. No extraction community on earth has matched it.

What would honest pricing have counted? The full intergenerational value of the thing drawn down. Access foreclosed for everyone who comes later, weighted by how substitutable the resource turns out to be. The tail of damage that extraction leaves running after the wells are capped. The value of keeping options open for uses no one has imagined yet, which the Technical Appendix and the later chapters treat under the name intergenerational option value. Call that whole quantity the residual commons value. Severance is what is left of it once the bond is subtracted: the residual commons value, minus what was actually internalized. The remainder lands, as it always lands, on whoever cannot leave.

Here is what the fund does that the capture rate alone does not explain. Because the wealth is held rather than consumed, held as a permanent reserve that no annual budget can draw below its principal, Norway keeps open the option to repair what can be repaired, to fund substitutes as they become reachable, to compensate whom compensation can still reach. An architecture that spends the proceeds as they arrive forecloses twice, once at the seabed and once in the spending. Norway's forecloses once. The framework reads the fund as an attack on the irreversibility of the loss itself, the foreclosure premium shrinking because the reserve exists; the formal version of that claim is in the Technical Appendix. The fund makes the loss less final, not only better paid for.

Then the honest bound, because an existence proof is only worth having if it is honestly bounded. The barrel is still gone. Compound interest does not reconstitute a physical resource. The applications with no scalable substitute at industrial volume do not regain access because the revenue was invested well; if some future use turns out to require petroleum's specific chemistry, no rate of return restores it. The externality tail is still running: the disturbed ecology of the North Sea, and above all the carbon, because Norwegian petroleum burns all over the world, and no fund extracts carbon back out of the sky. As for the price: spread the fund across every barrel the shelf has produced and you get what the polity actually internalized, per barrel. The Technical Appendix derives the figure. Its meaning is the discipline that matters here: a revealed lower bound on what the extraction was worth, a floor under the truth. It says nothing about whether the price was right.

John Hartwick showed in 1977, in the American Economic Review, that an economy which invests its resource rents in reproducible capital can sustain its consumption indefinitely. The fund is Hartwick's rule made real at the scale of a nation. The rule assumes the world will always offer substitutes; where substitution fails, no investment policy fully compensates the foreclosure, and the generalization of Hartwick to that harder world is worked out in the Technical Appendix. Norway's architecture goes one step past the rule even so. It does not only invest the rent. It preserves the optionality.

So the verdict, with both margins counted: Norway's severance is dramatically smaller than Appalachia's, and it is still positive. The framework's accounting of the remainder is in the Technical Appendix; the short version is that the gap between honest pricing and actual pricing can be narrowed by orders of magnitude when the architecture is built to narrow it, on the bond side and on the finality side at once, and that no architecture yet built closes it. Norway proves the gap can be narrowed. Nothing about Norway proves extraction has been priced correctly anywhere, Norway included.

One boundary, before the mirror case. Petroleum is the limiting case of the problem: a non-renewable, where consumption forecloses future access faster than any restoration can run, by definition, so that unpriced extraction always severs something. Renewable commons obey a different clock. A reef or a fishery severs only when the taking outruns the regeneration, and within restoration the severance is zero. The distinction will matter again, on home water.

---

McDowell County, West Virginia, was Chapter 2's ground: a county that sat over wealth for a century, watched the value leave, and keeps the costs still. Fewer than 20,000 people remain.

The objection arrives on schedule. Norway had advantages McDowell never saw: strong institutions, low corruption, a long tradition of competent public administration, a culture of consensus, high social trust. The Niger Delta had less still. If the existence proof requires Norwegian conditions, it proves something about Norway and nothing about anywhere that needs it.

Concede every word of the premise. The conclusion runs the other way. Severance is the operation of a mechanism: value extracted, costs transferred onto communities with limited capacity to resist the transfer. A mechanism like that does its most efficient work exactly where Norway's conditions are absent, where institutions are thin and the people bearing the costs have the least standing to contest them. The mechanism selects for weakness. Expecting to find it interrupted only where communities were already strong is expecting exactly what the selection predicts.

None of which makes McDowell a story about bad men. The people there were not less deserving than Norwegians. The operators were not unusually malicious; they operated where standard accountability was absent, and absent accountability does what absent accountability does. Every structural condition for severance was present. Severance occurred.

One more absence comes into focus beside the fund. McDowell lacked the bond, and it lacked something the bond's absence hides: the optionality. Where proceeds are consumed as they arrive, by the operator, by the county, by whoever reaches them first, nothing is held back that could buy down the finality of the loss. The coal is as gone as Norway's oil, and no reserve keeps any door open behind it. The foreclosure runs at full force. That is the American baseline.

So the objection, followed honestly, lands as instruction. The communities most exposed to the mechanism are the ones with the weakest institutional defenses, which is a reason to build the defenses deliberately, the way Norway built them: in advance, in law, against the preferences of whoever holds the value at the moment of building.

On 10 November 1995, the Nigerian state hanged Ken Saro-Wiwa and eight other Ogoni leaders. The nine are remembered together as the Ogoni Nine. On 11 November, the day after, Nigeria was suspended from the Commonwealth. In 1999 it was reinstated. Four years of formal disgrace, and then the world's business resumed. Through all of it, before the hangings and during the suspension and after the reinstatement, the structural arrangement in the Niger Delta did not change. The oil came up. The communities bore what they had been bearing. The hangings cost Nigeria four years of Commonwealth membership; they cost the arrangement nothing.

Nigeria has produced oil since the 1950s. The first commercial find came in at Oloibiri in 1958, eleven years before the strike at Block 2/4. Over roughly six decades, the reserves under the Niger Delta have generated hundreds of billions of dollars of petroleum revenue for the Nigerian government, about $583 billion by the count of the country's own extractive-industries audit (NEITI), and that is the government's receipts alone, not the total value of the oil.

Where did it go. Much of the value moved through international oil companies into financial systems far from Nigeria. Transfer pricing carried some of it: a company trading with its own affiliates across borders sets the internal prices so that the profit comes to rest where taxes and disclosure are thinnest. Financial secrecy carried more: a secrecy jurisdiction is a place whose laws are written so that ownership cannot be traced from outside. The terms of extraction were old before the country was new: concessions inherited from pre-independence arrangements, negotiated before there was a Nigerian state to bargain on Nigerians' behalf. And much of what did reach the state was captured by elites, a record documented in the corruption prosecutions of the decades since. Nigerian institutional weakness is real, and it is one component in an international architecture that works this way wherever it is permitted to. The failure does not stay inside Nigeria's borders, and neither did the money.

In the Delta itself, the crude that never reached a tanker reached the mangroves. Decades of spills have contaminated mangrove channels and farmland across the producing region. When the United Nations Environment Programme assessed Ogoniland, one territory within the wider Delta, in 2011, it found contamination severe enough that the initial restoration funding it called for ran on the order of $1 billion, with full restoration projected to take 25-30 years of sustained remediation (UNEP 2011). The years since have not kept that schedule; monitoring shows the cleanup running well short of it. Around the contamination, the rest of the tail: chronic illness tracking the spilled crude, the flare gas, the heavy metals; fishing and farming economies collapsed into dependence on compensatory payments often unpaid, or captured before they arrived; political violence organized around the extraction itself.

Run the framework's question against the Delta and the arithmetic is short. The residual commons value drawn down is enormous. What reached the communities over the oil approaches zero. No bond was posted. Nothing was held in reserve, so nothing buys back any part of the finality; the loss arrived total and has stayed total. Nigeria's severance is vast because the architecture did neither of the two things Norway's did. It captured nothing for the people bearing the costs, and it kept nothing that could make the loss less final.

A framework that said extraction is always a disaster would describe Norway and Nigeria identically, prescribe identically, and be of no use to anyone deciding anything. Run this one and the verdicts split: a small severance for Norway, an enormous one for Nigeria, the geology held constant, and the entire difference driven by the one variable a polity can actually change, the institutional architecture of accountability. That is what makes it an instrument, not a moral gesture.

---

The remaining objection is the practical one, and it is the one raised in every room where policy gets made. Norway is far away, small, homogeneous, rich, high in trust; whatever it proves, it proves about Norway. The answer is that the pattern repeats at every scale the objection cares about, in places with none of Norway's reputed advantages.

In 1976, by a margin of roughly 75 to 25, Alaskans voted to amend their state constitution, under Governor Jay Hammond, dedicating at least 25 percent of the state's oil and mineral royalties to a permanent fund whose principal the constitution itself protects (Alaska Constitution, Article IX, Section 15). The corpus stands near $89-90 billion in 2026 (Alaska Permanent Fund Corporation). Since 1982 the fund has paid a dividend to every Alaska resident, every year, for more than 40 years; the 2024 check was $1,702. None of this happened in Scandinavia. It happened inside the United States Constitution, in a state whose politics nobody has ever called consensual, and it has survived every administration since. The objection now has to explain Alaska first.

The crews that strung fiber across Chattanooga worked for the electric company the city has owned since 1935. EPB, the municipal utility, had gigabit internet running city-wide by 2010, three years before Google Fiber lit its first city in Kansas City; the build was financed with about $111 million in federal stimulus money from 2009 plus bonded municipal financing, it serves roughly 125,000 customers, and the service has run profitably since about 2015 (EPB; FCC 2015 preemption record). The incumbent cable company sued to stop the network in 2008, before the first customer was ever connected. The suit failed; state law still caps the network's footprint at the city's edge. Households there can buy service of up to 10 gigabits from a utility their grandparents' city built. The objection now has to explain Tennessee.

In 1956, in the Basque country of Spain, a priest named José María Arizmendiarrieta organized the first cooperative of what became Mondragon. Today it is the largest worker-cooperative federation in the world: roughly 80,000 worker-owners across some 95 cooperatives, competing in ordinary markets at ordinary prices. Pay inside is capped by rule, historically around 3 to 1 between top and bottom, stretching to roughly 6 to 9 to 1 in the largest industrial cooperatives, against ratios of 200 to 1 and beyond at large American firms. When Fagor, a flagship cooperative, went bankrupt in 2013, the federation absorbed most of its workers across the other cooperatives rather than discharging them into the labor market. Worker ownership was supposed to work only small and only niche. Mondragon has been disproving that at industrial scale for 70 years.

The municipal apartment blocks Vienna began building in 1919 are organized around courtyards, and an address in one carries no stigma. About 6 in 10 of Vienna's 1.9 million residents live in the city's roughly 220,000 municipal apartments or in non-profit cooperative housing, together well over 400,000 homes (City of Vienna). The program dates to the Red Vienna years of 1919-1923, financed in part by a dedicated housing-promotion contribution of 1 percent on payrolls alongside progressive taxation and subsidized federal loans, and it sustains itself now. It has survived every Austrian government since, the country's accession to the European Union in 1995, and the crisis years of 2008-2013. Collective provision was supposed to mean poverty housing. A century in, it is simply how the city houses itself.

A nation, a state, a city, a federation of firms. A resource, an infrastructure, labor, housing. Four scales and four kinds of commons, each with value captured collectively and held across generations, each built and defended through ordinary politics. None of the four is a fluke of national character, and together they take the geography excuse off the table.

---

In 1935, in the sixth year of the Depression, the United States built an intergenerational architecture of its own. The problem in front of the designers was immediate: an elderly generation destitute in the present tense. The reachable instrument was a transfer. Tax the wages of those working now, pay those who are old now, and promise the workers their own turn later. Social Security has run on that pay-as-you-go logic ever since. When the system ran surpluses, the surpluses were lent to the Treasury and booked as special-issue bonds, debt from one pocket of the government to another rather than capital compounding in the outside world. The trustees currently project the reserves depleted around 2034, after which the payroll tax covers roughly 81 percent of scheduled benefits (SSA Trustees).

Set it beside the fund in Oslo and the temptation is to grade them. Resist it. Norway captures a finite rent and compounds it in the productive economies of other nations. The United States captures an ongoing tax and moves it across the generations in nearly real time. The difference is historical contingency, not virtue. Norway designed in the 1970s and 1980s, with a windfall to capitalize and a mature global financial system to hold it. The United States designed in 1935, when the windfall did not exist and the financial system was the disaster being survived. The funded form was a real alternative in the world even then: Bismarck's old-age and disability insurance of 1889 was partially funded and stayed that way until the German hyperinflation of the early 1920s consumed the fund. Architectures are chosen under constraint, and the constraints of 1935 chose a transfer.

The general claim follows. Every architecture that distributes value and cost across generations is engineered. There are no naturally occurring distributional outcomes. There is the architecture somebody chose, and there is the architecture nobody noticed themselves choosing, and there is nothing else. The framework does not legislate; nothing in this comparison says the United States should hold a Norwegian fund, or that a pay-as-you-go pension is a failure. The claim is narrower: the architecture is a choice, and it becomes visible as one the moment a different architecture is held up beside it.

There is a household version of the point. The honest architecture looks expensive from inside the dishonest one; that is the optics of every reform. But you are already paying for the severed costs. The payments are collected late and badly: in the taxes that fund remediation after the operator is gone, and in the public systems that absorb what the ledger refused to carry. Honest accounting does not conjure a new bill. It moves the payment to the front of the transaction, where it can buy prevention and capital instead of cleanup, and worked through the household arithmetic, the margin between the architecture you live under and an honestly accounted one is far smaller than the politics assumes. <!-- structure-note: eleven-cent precise figure is verify-before-ship; the prior figure rested on a Tax Foundation 2024 table not canonically supplied; structure carries without the number (Prose Rule 5, Path A) -->

Chapter 3 ended on a question pointed at this one: whether the instrument built in Oslo could, in some adapted form, be built for the oyster. I do not come to that question from a neutral distance. My father grew up on Fox Hill, among men who worked the bars of the lower Chesapeake, and the Hampton seafood trade those bars fed was the economy of his boyhood. The standing I bring is theirs.

A number now exists for part of the answer. Chapter 3 put a price on the backward half, restitution for the reef the dredges ground down: a computed floor of $20-37 million and a central estimate near $211 million (Chapter 3; Technical Appendix, §11.12). <!-- structure-note: confirm the Ch 3 landed figure and match its phrasing; this worktree's Chapter__3_TheWaterman.md predates the 2026-06-09/10 merge --> What remains open is the forward half. An oyster is not a barrel of crude. The reef renews, and severance arises only when the harvest outruns the regeneration, which the Chesapeake record shows it did, decade after decade, until the structure that produced the renewal was itself taken; within restoration, the severance is zero. The forward question is Norwegian in form: whether the rents of a living commons can be captured and held the way Norway holds the oil's, so that the next century of the fishery is not priced at zero. What the Norwegian case settles is the existence question. Such a thing can be built. Built, it can be kept.

---

An existence proof is a narrow instrument, and Norway's leaves four gaps standing. The chapters ahead are built against them.

First, the permanent foreclosure is not fully priced. The barrel is gone, and if a use someday emerges for which petroleum's specific chemistry is structurally required, no rate of compounding reconstitutes it. The reserve holds open the options of repair and substitution, which is exactly why the architecture works as well as it does; even so, the loss is not made whole. The fund buys back some of the option. It never buys back the barrel.

Second, the externality tail stays open. The North Sea's ecosystems recover slowly where they recover at all. And the carbon from Norwegian petroleum burns into a sky everyone lives under, with the costs coming down hardest on people who receive none of the fund's revenue: the delta country of Bangladesh, where the flood line moves a little further inland with the years; the herding and farming belt of the Sahel, where the rains have grown unreliable; the low-lying Pacific island states whose whole national territory sits within a few meters of the tide. No quarterly report from Oslo reaches them.

Third, the solution does not scale by copying. The fund compounds because it owns a slice of the productive output of the rest of the world's economy. If every country built one, there would not be enough rest-of-the-world to absorb them. Norway found a local optimum; the global version of the problem is still unsolved, and later chapters take it up.

Fourth, the option value is preserved but never priced. The fund monetizes present use, and it keeps the option to restore alive as a side effect of its permanence. It does not hold, as a priced instrument, the value of the resource in situ for uses not yet imagined. The Technical Appendix and the later chapters build toward that instrument; Norway's architecture does not contain it.

None of the four cancels the proof. They schedule the work. Norway demonstrates the partial solution under the most favorable conditions on offer. What remains is the remainder: in places that have Norway's foundations, and, harder, in places where the foundations must be built against the resistance of the very extraction economy whose revenue would pay for them.

---

The drill at Block 2/4 went into the same kind of rock that drills went into all over the world in those decades, for the same market, under the same weather. Everything that made Norway different happened afterward, on land, in legislation, in the patient and unglamorous work of writing rules that future people would be unable to quietly break. Cost severance is a policy choice. The same extraction, run through one architecture, becomes a permanent public fortune and a loss made less final; run through another, it becomes the Niger Delta. An architecture that posts a permanent, rate-limited bond and holds the wealth in a form that keeps restoration open interrupts the mechanism the first three chapters watched run. An architecture that does neither lets the mechanism run to completion. Neither outcome was inevitable. Both were engineered.

What follows is the harder geography. Chapter 5 carries the question into the industrial catastrophes, Libby, the Deepwater Horizon, the Exxon Valdez, Baotou, where the costs have already landed and the architecture failed in advance. The fund in Oslo will publish another quarterly report this year. The capital will be intact, and every Norwegian will still be unable to spend a krone of the principal, which is the entire achievement. Somewhere, this year, a country is standing over something it has just found, deciding.
