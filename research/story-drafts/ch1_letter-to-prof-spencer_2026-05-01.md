Hello Prof. Spencer,

Sorry for the late reply. I could make excuses and say the nursing program revised the application process and it's been a shifting process. But really your email had me doing some soul searching and I wanted to give you a real and complete answer to your question. It felt like it was important to put my feelings into a response... into words that I could look back on whenever my path goes through days that aren't so easy, and those days find all of us.

Please find my response below, and the instructions for submitting your recommendation attached to this email as a .pdf file.


Why I Chose This Path
You know how when you're a little kid, someone always asks what you want to be when you grow up? Astronaut. Lawyer. Doctor. We parrot back whatever the world around us has decided sounds impressive. It's funny, and maybe a little beautiful, how differently we end up from where we thought we were headed, and how the path we actually leave on turns out to be exactly the right one to bring us home.
I have always wanted to help people. I have always loved solving problems. And I have always believed, somewhere deep in my bones, that there are an infinite number of ways to solve any given problem. As a consultant, I would typically open an engagement with exactly that statement, then pivot to the real work: defining the root problem before we even discuss solutions, and identifying what parameters matter most to the client cost, timeline, integration with existing resources. It turns out I am, by most measures, a pretty exceptional consultant.

But my definition of success has shifted in ways I didn't anticipate.

I no longer believe that an extraordinary paycheck justifies uprooting my life. My parents are visibly moving slower these days, and I've done the quiet math that we all eventually do: if I disappear for another few years, surfacing only for weddings and Thanksgivings, the odds are growing that the next flight home will be for something I can't bear to name. These are the years I have with them, and I intend to be present for them to laugh with them... And to be here for them when there is no laughter left.
So I found myself wanting something more grounded, a stable career with lasting relevance, one that keeps me here, connected to the people and the community that made me who I am. That reflection led me back through the chapters of my professional life, looking for the work that had actually felt like meaning.
Working at NASA felt like coming home. My grandfather was a model maker in the wind tunnels at Langley Research Center, and there was not a single morning I drove through those gates that I wasn't proud to be there. But it was the work I did in healthcare technology that has stayed with me longest. As a co-founder of MDLogix, I built patient registries and clinical trial systems for Johns Hopkins. Earlier in my career, I rewrote the front end of the NIH Children's Center website, and with it came a question that stopped me cold: How do you explain to a child with cancer that the research happening around them might mean future children won't have to be sick? I have never felt more humbled. I have never felt more useful.

That is what led me here.

Not a single dramatic moment, but a life examined, one that kept returning me to the same conclusion. Healthcare is where my skills create the most meaningful impact. Nursing offers me the rare combination of scientific rigor, human connection, and the kind of daily problem-solving that I find genuinely energizing. It also offers something increasingly rare in my professional history: stability, portability, and the ability to remain rooted in a community I love rather than chasing opportunity across the country.
Why I Am a Stronger Candidate

I come to this program having already lived several careers. I have operated at the intersection of technology and healthcare at institutions like NASA, Johns Hopkins, and NIH. I currently maintain a 4.0 GPA while serving as Vice President of the Student Government Association at VPCC, and more committees and projects than I probably have any business doing. I don't care if my name is mentioned. I just want to leave this place better than I found it. And there is so much that could be improved in this world.
 I bring not just academic preparation, but executive function, stakeholder communication, and the kind of composure under pressure that only comes from having genuinely high-stakes responsibilities. I am also not someone who chose nursing because I couldn't think of anything else. I chose it after a long life of meaningful work that kept pointing me back toward healthcare, and after being honest enough with myself to follow where that pointed.
Something Interesting

I write poetry. It tends to explore consciousness, impermanence, and the edges of what language can actually hold. It is probably the least résumé-appropriate thing about me, and also one of the truest.
Warm regards,

Chris Winn
