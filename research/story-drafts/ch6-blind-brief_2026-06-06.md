## 1. ROLE / PURPOSE OF THE CHAPTER

Chapter 6 is the book's **measurement chapter** and the formal hinge of the whole argument. The chapters before it have shown, case by case (Appalachian coal, Gulf oil, Libby asbestos/vermiculite, a degraded Chesapeake), that markets understate the true cost of drawing down a commons. Those are illustrations of a mechanism. Chapter 6 answers the question a serious skeptical reader is entitled to ask next: **how do we know the cases are representative and not cherry-picked disasters, and how large is the gap, computed rather than asserted?**

The chapter's job is to **compute** — to take the same historical cases and price them by three methods built on different intellectual foundations, show that the methods converge in direction and roughly in magnitude, and then name honestly what the computation does NOT resolve. Convergence-across-independent-methods is the chapter's epistemic engine: a mechanism shown once is an anecdote; shown across cases is a pattern; priced by multiple independent methods that never reverse direction is a structural claim about how the extraction economy works.

The chapter is the book's most number-dense and most contestable. Its register is analytical, not narrative; its human-texture density is low by design (the bookend chapters carry the emotional arc across this analytical stretch). It formalizes the framework's apparatus — the RCV integral, the triangulation, the Four Gates, the Commons Inversion Test — that earlier chapters only gestured at, and it hands forward named tools (Asymmetric Regret Rule, the two-instrument architecture) that later chapters operationalize.

Within the ten-chapter arc: Ch5 names the two instruments; **Ch6 formalizes and measures**; Ch7 stress-tests universality off-world; Ch8 walks one worked ton of coal; Ch9 operationalizes into policy. Ch6 is the "formalize/measure" station.

**B6 — Approach 2: The Real Options Model.**
The method that speaks Wall Street's language. A unit of resource in the ground is an **option** — the right but not obligation to extract later; options have value; premature exercise destroys the information value waiting would have produced. This is standard finance — how oil majors already value undeveloped reserves. What the standard model omits is the **social** side of the ledger: the externality costs avoided by extracting later, the substitutability that develops while you wait, and the information about resource criticality that accumulates over decades. When the full social option value of leaving a unit unextracted exceeds today's net extraction revenue, extraction is socially **premature** (not merely suboptimal). Apply to McDowell coal in 1960: net revenue per ton well under the mine-mouth price; social option value (later-cheaper renewables, avoided externalities, information about community collapse) on the order of tens to hundreds of dollars. Conclude 1960 coal was socially premature — a failure of pricing (the social side was never a line item), not only of information. Note this method reaches a reader the damage-function approach does not: a CFO who dismisses an externality list will recognize option value as real because she values her own reserves that way. Apply across cases: every extraction event examined produced net revenue below the social option value of leaving the resource in place — **the direction does not flip.**

**B7 — Approach 3: The RCV Model (plain-English first, then formula).**
The approach the book has been building toward; its architecture differs from the first two. Bottom-up enumerates costs; real-options prices early-exercise loss; **RCV prices what every future generation loses by not having the resource, weighted by the probability no substitute exists when they need it.** Give the plain-English version BEFORE the formula (the argument must survive for readers who skip the integral). Two components the market does not price:
- **Foreclosure cost:** extracting a unit denies it to every future generation; the cost is real only to the degree no adequate substitute exists. Substitutability varies by resource (high and rising for coal-in-electricity; low for rare earths in advanced electronics; near zero for helium in cryogenics, for fossil-aquifer water).
- **Externality tail:** harm that continues after the mine closes — black lung decades later, acid mine drainage for generations, carbon in the atmosphere for centuries. Runs independently of substitutability (a substitute for coal does not clean the mine; a substitute for oil does not pull carbon from the sky).
RCV combines the two, integrated across the full affected time horizon. When RCV exceeds market price — which it does in every tested case — the difference is the severed cost.

Then present the formula as a SUMMARY of the plain-English argument just made (formal articulation lives in the Technical Appendix §3; link it). Translate the integrand term by term: the no-substitute probability factor [1 - S]; the stock-dependent utility U (rises as stock depletes); the externality flow E (independent of substitutability and of depletion); and the discount factor D following **Weitzman's declining-rate framework** (under long-horizon discount-rate uncertainty, the certainty-equivalent rate declines toward the lowest plausible rate — converting an unresolvable philosophical question into a treatable empirical one). Then give the two derived definitions: **cost severance CS = RCV − B** (what remains after subtracting whatever accountability bond B has been posted) and **IPG = RCV / market price**. **B9 — The Substitutability Function.**
The model's most novel and most vulnerable move; introduce it before the convergence result. Prior intergenerational-cost approaches leaned almost entirely on the **discount rate** ("how much do future generations matter?") — a philosophical question economists have argued since Ramsey 1928 without converging (Stern's low rate, Nordhaus's higher rate, Weitzman's declining rate, philosophers' objections to any positive rate). The substitutability function **sidesteps** this by asking instead "how quickly can we develop alternatives?" — an **empirical** question engineers, energy economists, and hydrologists can estimate, and empirical questions admit progress. Four natural properties: starts near zero at extraction (if a cheaper substitute existed we would not be extracting); rises monotonically (substitutes, once invented, do not un-invent); approaches an asymptote ≤ 1 (complete substitution may be physically impossible for some resources); depends on investment (the timeline responds to price signals an honest regime would generate). Concede the critic's fair objection — we have moved the hard problem from choosing a discount rate to choosing a substitutability curve — and answer: it is a **better** hard problem, because substitutability error bars shrink with research while the discount rate's have not shrunk in a century. (Link Technical Appendix §13.)

**B11 — The three follow-on objections (the chapter owes the reader answers).**
1. **Choose-your-own-adventure / parameter-arbitrariness:** partly correct, but the convergence is itself evidence against arbitrariness — three methods on different foundations would not converge if the output were a pure artifact of arbitrary choices.
2. **Popperian falsifiability:** the model is falsifiable — it has simply not yet met a failing case. From CS = RCV − B, CS can in principle be zero or negative; two case-classes approach CS ≈ 0 (a sustainably managed renewable with full ecosystem accountability; a highly abundant non-renewable under stringent accountability) and both predictions are testable and would be falsified by clean counterexamples. The structural reason CS > 0 in every fossil-fuel case is that no existing accountability regime prices substitutability-weighted intergenerational foreclosure — the instruments do not yet exist; the prediction is falsified the day a jurisdiction implements full intergenerational foreclosure pricing and shows CS ≤ 0 for a non-renewable (the sound-barrier analogy is available — reinvent it). Distinguish the two falsifiability operations: the **Commons Inversion Test** is a thought-experiment filter (conceptual; thought-experiments are not Popper-falsifiable and the framework does not claim they are); the **empirical** falsifiability operates one layer up, at the level of specific CS > 0 predictions testable through the case record.
3. **Reproducibility:** two analysts will not produce identical numbers (different substitutability curves, SCC estimates, discount factors, tail assumptions); their outputs overlap but differ. This is how all applied cost-accounting works (benefit-cost analysis, health-technology assessment, nuclear-decommissioning estimates, actuarial forecasts); none treats the presence of a range as failure — failure is undisclosed sensitivity or an invented central case. The framework guarantees not uniform outputs but **transparent disagreement** that resolves to specific empirical questions further research can answer.

**B14 — What Is Owed (Parfit non-identity grounding).**
The framework prices what extraction severs from future generations — a strong claim needing a strong philosophical anchor against the question "how can extraction wrong people who do not yet exist?" Give Derek Parfit's **non-identity problem** (different policy choices determine which specific future people exist at all; on a strict person-affecting account the aggressive-extraction children cannot be wronged because their alternative is non-existence). State this is the deepest objection to any future-cost framework, not an abstruse puzzle. Give Parfit's response, which the framework adopts: operate at the level of **outcomes** rather than specific persons (is the future-generation outcome worse than under different policy? if yes, the loss is real regardless of whether the bearers could have been the same persons) — an **impersonal** moral framework. RCV operates in exactly this register: it prices what extraction severs from whatever future generations arise. Connect to the two-instrument architecture: the Foreclosure Bond (forward-looking impersonal-outcomes evaluation) and the Restitution Bond (backward-looking person-affecting evaluation) inherit Parfit's distinction; both are legitimate moral domains the framework distinguishes rather than collapses. Note the differing methodological work each requires (reparations economics for already-realized person-affecting harm; environmental-bond economics for prospective impersonal-outcomes loss) and that a single undifferentiated Cost-Severance object would fail under stress. **B15 — Sen capabilities (complementary grounding).**
A second philosophical grounding complementary to Parfit: **Amartya Sen's** capability-and-social-welfare framework. Where Parfit grounds *whether* future generations have standing, Sen grounds *how* their standing is valued — in terms of capabilities the accounting can preserve or extinguish. RCV is, in Sen's vocabulary, a measurement of the capability set extraction forecloses on whoever-the-future-people-turn-out-to-be. The Parfit-grounds-standing / Sen-grounds-valuation pairing is what makes the intergenerational-pricing claim defensible across moral-philosophy AND welfare-economics traditions simultaneously.

**B16 — Two Kinds of Commons (Ostrom boundary).**
Answer the Ostrom-tradition reader's held question: is the Commons Inversion Test filtering the same kind of commons Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* describes, and does the framework compete with or extend Ostrom-tradition commons economics? Distinguish two structurally different classes:
- **Coordination commons** (what Ostrom's eight design principles address): a finite resource multiple users draw from, where the risk is uncoordinated drawing producing collective ruin (Hardin's "tragedy"), and Ostrom's contribution was demonstrating empirically across hundreds of cases that shared-stakeholder communities self-govern through specific institutional architectures. The cost here is coordination breakdown — already priced well by the existing Ostrom apparatus where the institutional architecture is in place. This is NOT what this framework prices. (This is the book's one clean contradiction of Hardin, via siding with Ostrom's empirical refutation.)
- **Extraction commons** (what THIS framework addresses): atmospheric carbon-stability, mountain habitat, ancestral land, collective cognitive bandwidth. Multiple users still draw, but the actors capturing value through extraction are NOT shared-stakeholders with the actors bearing cost (the mining company is not a member of the community; the carbon emitter is not a member of the foreclosed future generation). The asymmetry between value-capture and cost-bearing is structural, not contingent; the cost the framework prices is the severance that asymmetry enables.
Make the distinction operational via the CIT's two sub-forms (Commons-Absence Inversion / Commons-Consumption Inversion — see B17): a coordination commons fails the consumption test (an inexhaustible fishery would still need governance); an extraction commons passes it. Ostrom and this framework are adjacent and complementary, not competing; where a real case carries both elements (a forest that is both shared pastoral commons and target of external timber extraction) the analyses run in parallel. Add the **heterogeneous-stakeholder break-point**: Ostrom's design principles presume substantially-aligned stakeholders; where stakeholders are divided on ethnic/racial/class lines with conflicting use-preferences, the design principles do not solve it because the problem is contestation over acceptable use, not coordination of mutually-acceptable use. This framework reaches such commons because the CIT operates on the commons-extraction relationship, not the stakeholder-coordination relationship — connecting to **stratification economics** (Darity, Hamilton, collaborators). **NOTE (seam R-5):** consolidate the coordination-vs-extraction distinction to a single clean statement; the prior draft made it three times.

**B17 — The Four Gates (CIT + three more).**
Name the admission discipline directly now that the formula is specified: any cost admitted into the RCV computation must pass four gates; fail any one and it is excluded.
- **Gate 1 — The Commons Inversion Test.** Ask whether a candidate cost depends on a commons by imagining the commons inverted (absent altogether, or unconsumed). Two sub-forms: **Commons-Absence Inversion** (imagine the commons not existing — surfaces replacement cost: what it would take to recreate or compensate for what was destroyed; McDowell's stripped habitability commons generates a Commons-Absence cost — the life-expectancy loss, communities that don't refill, un-priced remediation) and **Commons-Consumption Inversion** (imagine the commons not being consumed — surfaces opportunity cost: what the commons would have done if not drawn down; the lifetime hours a long commute consumes; the forest that could have stayed a carbon sink). Most resource-extraction cases are Commons-Absence; most personal-scale extractions are Commons-Consumption; some exercise both. The two sub-forms run different filters: Commons-Absence distinguishes commons-grounded claims from private-property externalities; Commons-Consumption distinguishes consumption-of-finite-commons claims from coordination-of-mutually-acceptable-use claims (the Ostrom routing). Include the CIT naming-justification — but BRIEFLY (this is part of the ~1,960-word naming-thesaurus cut target; make the case that "inversion" names the specific premise-flip — holding the world fixed except inverting the resource-scarcity dimension; if the cost survives non-scarcity it is intrinsic to the activity and excluded, if it vanishes it is commons-grounded and admitted — and that the method is the David Lewis counterfactual-reasoning tradition specialized to one premise-flip; do NOT run the full rejected-names catalogue). State that the framework declined **revealed preference** as the discrimination gate (it reveals what existing institutions have captured, not what the commons costs to extract from; the gate must be independent of the architecture the framework is assessing) while retaining revealed preference as one estimation input in Approach 2 / Method 2 — the two roles are different and specified separately.
- **Gate 2 — Units Consistency.** A candidate must be expressible as dollars per unit of resource per unit of time; excludes ordinal-only costs (quality-of-life scores, dignity indices) until converted to dollar-equivalents with transparent methodology (hedonic pricing, contingent valuation, revealed preference all admissible, but explicit before entry). A DALY fails until paired with a published willingness-to-pay valuation.
- **Gate 3 — Boundedness.** The candidate's contribution to the integral must converge to a finite value under the discount assumptions; excludes unbounded theoretical constructions (a claim of infinite intergenerational damage fails even if it survives Commons Inversion).
- **Gate 4 — Independence.** No double-counting what an existing term captures; resolve overlaps by redefining, merging, or rejecting (a community-disruption cost priced once as Cohesion and again as Habitability fails until resolved).
State the gates are jointly necessary (CIT alone is necessary but not sufficient) and link the Technical Appendix Four-Gates apparatus + the formula generalization (RCV as a discounted integral of a sum of cost terms; the two-term form is the n=2 special case; the generalization is what makes the framework extensible). The two-term expression remains the formula that produces the convergence-table numbers.

**B18 — The Contribution.**
State plainly what the RCV framework ADDS. It extends two parent traditions — **Pigouvian externality theory** and **Ostrom-lineage commons governance** — into a setting neither addressed: extraction contexts where commons are drawn down rather than managed-in-place. From Pigou: internalize externalities through pricing instruments. From Ostrom: commons can be governed sustainably under specific design-principle conditions. The framework adds what neither developed: a measurement methodology (Commons Inversion Test, two sub-forms) for the commons-extraction relationship; a quantification framework (Residual Commons Value); a pricing instrument (Accountability Bond) sized to RCV rather than to a single externality.
Give the **RCV naming justification** (the three loaded sub-choices, each honoring a tradition — but keep it tighter than the prior draft): *Residual* in John Hartwick's 1977 sense (the rents owed forward as residual obligations to future generations; what remains to be priced after market revenue and standard externality accounting); *Commons* in Elinor Ostrom's specific institutional-form sense (rejecting "resource"/"public good"/"natural capital" for stated reasons); *Value* in Mariana Mazzucato's value-created-vs-value-extracted sense (the value the commons creates that extraction takes without paying for; rejecting "cost"/"wealth"/"worth"). The term is the convergence of the three.
Then the three additions, each consequential:
1. **Substitutability-weighting.** Standard externality analysis prices damage at time-of-damage; it has no category for foreclosure cost (the cost of extracting early, weighted by the probability no substitute exists at each future time). Name the formal precedent: **Hotelling's 1931** exhaustible-resource economics prices the private extraction rent but not the option-value of leaving resources in situ for future generations; Pigou prices third-party harm but not that option-value either. The **Hotelling Identity** is the framework's bridge: per-unit RCV minus per-unit standard Hotelling rent equals per-unit Cost Severance — commons-extraction cost is precisely the residual value beyond standard Hotelling rent under honest accounting. It does not replace either tradition; it formalizes what each leaves out.
2. **Integrated architecture.** Standard practice treats foreclosure, externalities, community disruption, intergenerational equity, and epistemic loss as separate concerns with separate methods, literatures, reviewers. No single existing method delivers one formula integrating them. The RCV integrand unites foreclosure and externality terms; the general form extends to any additional gate-passing cost term. A synthesis, not a replacement — it lets analysts produce a single number when a decision requires one (a reclamation bond, a remediation fund, a severance levy) and show transparently how components contribute.
3. **The Commons Inversion Test as discovery method.** Standard externality analysis prices what the analyst already decided to price (the category is inherited; only the magnitude is in question). The CIT is a discovery method: it asks whether the same physical extraction would impose a given cost if the resource were non-scarce; if yes it is intrinsic to the activity and excluded, if no it is a scarcity-grounded intergenerational claim belonging in the integrand. This makes the framework generative — new variables added as the test reveals them, each with its own worked reasoning rather than by assertion.
Apply the three additions to McDowell coal, Deepwater, and Libby (same case, same historical data, larger and more complete number — the RCV figure is larger because it integrates more of what was severed; "standard Pigouvian analysis has no principled reason to exclude these costs, it simply has no method for including them — RCV has a method"). Close: the three additions are real; the framework is non-trivial relative to the tradition it grew from; it is an extension of externality economics, not a rejection.

## 5. LINEAGE PLACEMENTS + THE CLAIM EACH CARRIES

Place each thinker/tradition with the SPECIFIC relationship-claim below. The framework's spine is "extend-into-a-setting-they-could-not-reach" against the formal-economics canon, "add-to" against the diagnostic/critical traditions, "parallel" for compatible-but-distinct frames, "contradict" only for Hardin.

- **A.C. Pigou (externality theory, 1920)** — EXTEND. Pigou prices third-party costs via pricing instruments; the framework treats Pigouvian instruments as PARTIAL — externality components of bond B are strictly less than RCV, forcing aggregate B < RCV. RCV adds substitutability-weighted foreclosure + community + epistemic terms Pigou has no method to include. One of the two named parent traditions. Strongest line: standard Pigouvian analysis has no principled reason to exclude these costs; it simply has no method for including them; RCV has a method.
- **Harold Hotelling (Economics of Exhaustible Resources, 1931)** — EXTEND. The signature formal claim: the **Hotelling Identity**, RCV − Hotelling rent = CS per unit. Hotelling prices the private owner's scarcity rent but structurally excludes intergenerational foreclosure (future utility is not in the owner's objective function). Formalizes what each tradition leaves out; does not replace either.
- **John Hartwick (Hartwick's Rule, 1977)** — EXTEND / source of the word "Residual." Invest resource rents in reproducible capital for non-declining consumption (Norway GPFG = cleanest application), assuming strong substitutability (S_max = 1). RCV generalizes to S_max ≤ 1: under imperfect substitution no investment policy fully compensates for foreclosure.
- **Herman Daly (ecological economics / strong sustainability)** — ADD-TO / operationalize. Daly argues normatively that strong sustainability requires maintaining critical natural-capital stock; RCV operationalizes this — where Daly argues S_max MUST equal 1, RCV asks empirically what S_max IS per resource class and prices the cost when S_max < 1. ("Daly gives the warning; he does not give the invoice.")
- **Elinor Ostrom (Governing the Commons, 1990)** — PARALLEL / EXTEND. The most sustained boundary-drawing in the book. Ostrom governs renewable coordination-commons (harvest-rate management); the framework prices non-renewable extraction-commons that are drawn down, plus the heterogeneous/structurally-divided-stakeholder commons Ostrom's homogeneity-of-interest condition excludes. The CIT's Commons-Consumption sub-form routes coordination-commons OUT to Ostrom. Named with Pigou as the two parent traditions; also illustrative-list precedent (eight design principles).
- **Garrett Hardin (Tragedy of the Commons)** — CONTRADICT (the only clean contradiction). Invoked through Ostrom: side with Ostrom's empirical refutation (communities self-govern across hundreds of cases) against Hardin's tragedy.
- **Nordhaus vs Stern (discount-rate debate; Ramsey 1928)** — CONTRADICT-WITHIN-SCOPE / refuse-to-settle. The framework declines to settle the rate dispute; via Weitzman declining rates + sensitivity analysis it shows CS > 0 across the entire plausible range — only magnitude varies. The substitutability function sidesteps the impasse (not "how much do future generations matter?" but "how quickly can we develop alternatives?").
- **Martin Weitzman (Gamma Discounting 2001; fat-tail damages 2009)** — ADOPT-DIRECTLY. The framework adopts Weitzman declining discount rates as its D(t,t0) factor — the most significant methodological choice; biases RCV DOWNWARD, making results conservative-by-construction. The "externality tail" usage joins the fat-tail / tipping-point lineage.
- **Dixit & Pindyck (1994); real options / irreversibility** — ADD-TO / EXTEND. The lineage for RCV's forward-looking irreversibility pricing — specifically **Method 3 (Scarcity-Adjusted Option Value)**. Standard real options is what oil majors already use; the framework adds the social terms.
- **Savage 1951 (minimax-regret)** — EXTEND / specialize. The **Asymmetric Regret Rule** specializes Savage minimax-regret for collective commons-extraction where one direction of regret is commons-extinction. Rejected alternative is standard CBA-under-risk (fails reversibility, expected-value reliability, symmetry).
- **David Lewis (Counterfactuals, 1973)** — EXTEND / specialize. Counterfactual reasoning is the lineage for the **Commons Inversion Test** — specialized into two sub-forms, inverting only the resource-scarcity dimension. Chosen over revealed-preference-as-discrimination-gate because revealed preference is institutionally captured.
- **Costanza et al. (ecosystem services) + revealed-preference economics (Samuelson, Hicks) + Campbell & Fiske 1959** — ADD-TO / estimation input. Replacement-cost valuation (Costanza) = **Method 1** (floor; cannot price irreplaceability). Revealed preference (Norway calibration) = **Method 2** (retained for estimation, rejected as discrimination gate). The **triangulation** is grounded in the **Campbell-Fiske multitrait-multimethod matrix** — convergence across three independent foundations as evidence the measured thing is real, not a parameter artifact.
- **Mariana Mazzucato (The Value of Everything, 2018)** — ADD-TO / specialize. Supplies the "Value" in Residual Commons Value (value created vs value extracted); Cost Severance specializes it for the value-capture-vs-cost-bearing separation in resource extraction. ("Mazzucato names the extraction; the framework adds the accounting.")
- **Derek Parfit (Reasons and Persons, 1984)** — ADD-TO / moral grounding. Intergenerational moral grounding for the forward Foreclosure Bond (non-identity territory); the framework operates at the level of outcomes rather than specific persons; the two-instrument architecture inherits Parfit's forward-impersonal / backward-person-affecting distinction. Does NOT claim to solve the non-identity problem (conceded-unsolved — flag as a named limit, not a defended win).
- **Sen (Development as Freedom) + Nussbaum (capabilities)** — ADD-TO / parallel. Paired with Parfit ("Parfit grounding the standing, Sen grounding the valuation"); RCV is, in Sen's vocabulary, a measurement of the capability set extraction forecloses. Ten central capabilities = precedent for the illustrative-not-exhaustive commons list.
- **Civic-republican tradition — Pettit (1997), Skinner (1998), Anderson (Private Government 2017)** — PARALLEL / EXTEND. Freedom-as-non-domination extended intergenerationally; one of the divergent commitment-spaces the framework accommodates without legislating over (the autonomy-commons reading).
- **Darity & Mullen (From Here to Equality, 2020) + stratification economics (Darity, Hamilton); Conley 1999** — ADD-TO / EXTEND. The "primary informing source." The backward Restitution Bond uses their language inside their typology, not against it; extends it beyond US-slavery scope to the general cost-severance class without renaming/absorbing the historical reparations claim. Stratification economics = the bridge to the heterogeneous-stakeholder commons Ostrom cannot reach. Source of the longevity-gap legacy-effect methodology (see §6/§7 figure flag).
