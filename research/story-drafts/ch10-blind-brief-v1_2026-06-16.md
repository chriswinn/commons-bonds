### FILL 1 — THE FINANCIALIZATION / LEGAL-CODING OBJECTION (highest priority in the book)

**What it is.** The strongest hostile-reader line the book has, and the one the
canonical Ch10 leaves unanswered: *by pricing and bonding the commons, you extend
the very Code-of-Capital / fictitious-commodity logic that turned land and nature
into commodities in the first place. The accountability bond is not a refusal of
commodification; it is commodification's next instrument — the commons repackaged
as a new tradable asset class.*

**Why it lands hardest at Ch10.** Ch10 carries the bond apparatus and the
title-polysemy ("the bond is the account that keeps the debt visible"). A hostile
reader who has followed the book to its bond instruments asks, at exactly this
chapter, whether the instrument is the disease. The matrix routes the objection's
handle to "Ch9/Ch10"; Ch9 plants the rebuttal-adjacent material (Pistor,
Christophers) and Ch5 fully rebuts it — **but Ch10, where the objection is
sharpest, currently has no answer.** This is why it is the book's #1 fill.

**It is a DIFFERENT objection from the existing ESG-capture one (§2.6). Both
belong.** The ESG objection (Marxist/left-ally) says: *the framework is too
reformist; capital will swallow it and continue.* The financialization objection
(Polanyi/Pistor/Christophers) says something deeper: *the act of pricing-and-
bonding the commons is itself the commodifying move; you are doing the harm you
diagnose.* The redraft must keep them distinct — one is about capture-in-use, the
other is about the instrument's own nature.

**The objectors, named as objectors (not allies):**
- **Karl Polanyi** — *The Great Transformation* (1944); the **fictitious
  commodity**: labor, land, and money are treated as if produced for sale when
  none was. Pricing the commons looks, to a Polanyian, like manufacturing one more
  fictitious commodity.
- **Katharina Pistor** — *The Code of Capital* (2019); capital is a **legal
  artifact** assembled from legal modules (property, collateral, trust, the
  corporate veil, bankruptcy, choice of jurisdiction). A bond is exactly such a
  module. To a Pistorian, coding the commons as a bond is coding it as capital.
  (Pistor is already met at Libby in Ch5 as a diagnostic ally; here she is turned
  into the objector — the redraft should signal that the same diagnosis can be
  aimed back at the book.)
- **Brett Christophers** — the **asset-economy** critique (the rentier turn in
  which everything depended-upon becomes an asset to be owned and charged for).
  Bonding the commons, to Christophers, risks creating one more asset class for
  the rentier economy to capture.

**The answer (must be CONSISTENT with Ch5 L150-166; this is the consistency
anchor — do not contradict it, but do NOT re-expose Ch5's full treatment).**
The distinction the answer turns on:

- **Pricing-cost-severance-to-force-honest-accounting-and-restitution is the
  opposite operation from financializing-the-commons-as-a-new-tradable-asset-
  class.** "Accounting a cost is not coding an asset." Pistor's modules **code
  assets**; this book **accounts costs.** The bond does not create a thing to be
  owned and traded; it records a debt that must be paid and kept visible until it
  is. (Ch5's exact distinction: "Pricing-to-sell turns a forest into board feet.
  Pricing-to-reserve names what the forest is worth so that the destruction can no
  longer be booked as free. They are opposite operations that happen to share the
  word price.")
- **The lineage IS the defense.** To stand in the tradition of **Polanyi and
  Fraser** — the very thinkers who diagnosed commodification as a harm — is to mark
  the difference. Polanyi's **double movement** (a society re-embedding in social
  obligation a thing the market stripped out) is precisely what the bond apparatus
  is: the double movement made into an accounting tool. "The whole point of
  standing in the Polanyi-Fraser tradition is that the tradition is the one that
  refused the asset frame, and a framework standing inside it is using their
  refusal, not betraying it."
- **The honest residue.** Concede, as the ESG answer concedes, that the
  instrument CAN be perverted in use — a "commons bond" could in principle be
  rebranded into a tradable instrument by an institution that wanted to, the same
  way carbon credits were. The defense is not that misuse is impossible; it is
  that the framework's DESIGN polarity is reserve, not sell, and its lineage is
  the refusal of the asset frame. The answer to "any instrument can be captured"
  is the same answer the ESG objection got: the design and the lineage are the
  guard, and the politics is the work.

**Register instruction (load-bearing).** This must NOT read as a citation
roll-call or a white-paper rebuttal. It is the man on the deck, at that hour,
turning over the hardest thing a reader could say to him. Name Polanyi, Pistor,
Christophers economically — a clause each, the way the canonical names Nordhaus
and Stern and Weitzman in one breath — not a literature-review paragraph. The
distinction (account a cost vs. code an asset) should land as a **plainly-stated
conviction**, the way "the choice is between two numbers" lands in Ch5, not as
apparatus. **Forward-reference, do not duplicate:** Ch5 carries the full Polanyi/
Fraser exposition; Ch10's job is to state the answer at the point of maximum
pressure (the bond apparatus, the close) and let it carry the weight of having
been earned across the book, not to re-teach Polanyi. One disciplined paragraph,
maybe two, in-register.

**Fact-discipline:** Polanyi *The Great Transformation* (1944) and Pistor *The
Code of Capital* (2019) are correctly dated above. Christophers' asset-economy /

### FILL 2 — THE SANDEL COMMODIFICATION STEELMAN (Ch10 is the home)

**The Ch6-or-Ch10 decision resolves to Ch10.** The matrix instructed "build a
dedicated steelman-and-answer in Ch6 OR Ch10"; this brief places it in Ch10

**The steelman (Sandel as OBJECTOR, not ally).** Everywhere else in the book
Sandel is used as an ally on the moral limits of markets. Here, steelmanned
against the book: *to monetize the priceless — to put a number on a sacred
landscape, a reef, a community, a generation — is already to concede the
extractor's frame. Once a thing has a price it can be traded against a quarter's
earnings, and the act of pricing it, however well-intentioned, has performed the
reduction the extractor wanted. The framework's number is the extractor's victory
in moral disguise.* (This is the "pricing the priceless concedes the extractor's
frame" objection — give it its full force; it is a serious objection from a
serious thinker.)

**The answer (consistent with Ch5 L158/L166 and with the canonical's own
non-denominable limit).** Two moves:

- **The zero-is-already-a-number move** (from Ch5, re-rendered in-register).
  "The choice is not between a sacred thing left unpriced and a sacred thing
  reduced to a number. The choice is between two numbers, because the thing
  already has a number, and that number is zero." Zero is the number the
  extractive accounting already assigned — the number that made the extraction
  profitable. The framework refuses that zero. Pricing-to-reserve is the opposite
  of pricing-to-sell. Sandel's worry is answered from the same Polanyi-Fraser
  position as FILL 1: naming what a thing is worth so the destruction can no

  not denominated in any currency" (the Europa ocean; the Black Hills). So the
  Sandel answer is not "everything can be priced" — it is "the framework prices
  what can be priced TO RESERVE it, is honest about the genuinely non-denominable,
  and never invents a number to maintain the appearance of completeness." The
  Sandel objection and the framework's own non-denominable limit are the same
  boundary seen from two sides; the redraft should make them rhyme rather than

### FILL 3 — THE CONSERVATIVE TOO-STATIST OBJECTION (symmetric steelman-and-answer)

**The asymmetry to fix.** The canonical NAMES the conservative reaction ("parents
whose conservative friends will read it and find me too generous to the
institutional reform the framework implies," §2.6) but does NOT rebut it the way
the Marxist/ESG objection is rebutted. The left objection gets a full steelman and
a full answer; the right objection gets a name and a wave. **Give the conservative
objection a SYMMETRIC steelman-and-answer — matching the care the ESG objection
gets.**

**The steelman (conservative reader, full force).** *The framework is a Trojan
horse for statism. Accountability bonds, strategic reserves, sovereign wealth
funds, border adjustments, universal floors — this is a vast new apparatus of
state control over private extraction, a central planner's dream dressed up as
accounting. Pricing every externality is a license for unlimited government reach;
"honest accounting" is the thin end of a wedge that ends in the state owning the
commons it claims only to be measuring. The framework is too statist, too
generous to the regulatory state, and it will be captured by the same agencies
whose capture the book elsewhere documents.*

**The answer (symmetric to the ESG answer — same structure, opposite valence).**
- **The framework legislates nothing.** As the ESG answer says the model does not
  argue for or against capitalism, the conservative answer says the model does not
  argue for or against the state. The model **measures what extraction costs.**
  What a society does with the measurement — a tax, a bond posted to a private
  trustee, a market in tradable reserve-credits, a contractual obligation between
  private parties, a sovereign fund, or nothing at all — is a political question
  the framework does not legislate. An honest number is not a statist number; a
  conservative can use the same number to argue for a market instrument that a
  progressive uses to argue for a public fund.
- **Honest accounting is a conservative value too.** Pricing a cost honestly is
  what a sound balance sheet requires; a hidden liability is a fraud whether the
  entity is public or private. The framework gives the center-right reader an
  instrument that is, on its own terms, a demand for honest books — exactly the
  discipline conservatives ask of government and firms alike. (This connects to
  the book's ratified non-partisan-disarm posture and the center-right-dispositive
  audience: name that the instrument is available to a market-based answer, not
  only a state-based one.)
- **The capture point cuts both ways and is conceded.** Yes, the apparatus can be
  captured by the regulatory state, just as it can be captured into an ESG slogan.
  The conflict-free-diamond certificate is the left's capture example; the captured
  Minerals Management Service is the right's. The answer to both is the same: the
  framework is an instrument, capture is a political risk run by every instrument,
  and the guard is the design (account costs, do not code assets) plus the
  politics, which the framework does not legislate.
- **The symmetric close (preserve and complete the canonical's move).** The
  canonical already has the symmetry sentence ("friends on the left… too modest…
  conservative friends… too generous… both readings are misreadings of the same
  mistake"). The redraft must EARN that sentence by giving the conservative side a
  real steelman-and-answer first, so the symmetry is demonstrated, not merely
  asserted.

**Register instruction.** Same grounded register. The conservative steelman is
voiced with the same generosity as the left one — the man on the deck has parents
in Hampton whose conservative friends are real people whose objection he respects.
No strawman. The answer is the same plainly-stated conviction as the ESG answer:
the framework is an instrument; the politics is the work.

### FILL 4 — RESTORE THE NORDHAUS/STERN ADJUDICATE + WEITZMAN ADOPT-DIRECTLY RELATIONS

**The drafting-miss to fix.** The canonical names Nordhaus, Stern, and Weitzman
at the discount-rate limit (beat 7) but renders only the **honest-limit half**:
"the model is honest about this… does not pretend to settle a question the
discipline has not settled." The matrix-assigned RELATIONS were dropped:

- **Nordhaus / Stern — adjudicate / contradict-within-scope.** The redraft must
  do more than say "they have not converged." It must show the framework's actual
  move: the result (cost severance > 0; reserve a fraction of a high-substitution-
  gap resource) is **robust across the entire Nordhaus-to-Stern range** — the
  framework does not need to win the discount-rate fight because its conclusion
  holds whether you discount the future at Nordhaus's higher rate or Stern's lower
  one. That is the adjudicate move: not picking a side, but showing the
  conclusion survives both sides. (This is the conservative-by-construction
  posture from the meta-claims: "CS > 0 robust across the Nordhaus-to-Stern
  range.")
- **Weitzman — adopt-directly.** Weitzman proposed a **declining discount rate for
  the longest horizons** (gamma discounting). The framework does not merely "name a
  useful direction"; it **adopts Weitzman's declining-rate approach directly**,
  and doing so **biases the framework's estimates downward** — i.e., the framework
  takes the most conservative defensible discounting and still finds the cost. The
  redraft must render this adopt-directly relation, not just credit Weitzman with a
  gesture.

**Why it matters.** This restoration is what disarms the "pick Nordhaus 5.5% and
the multiples collapse" objection (a separate hostile-reader line the matrix
tracks). Showing the result is robust across the range, and that the framework
adopts the conservative end, is the answer to that objection — and it strengthens
the honesty posture rather than weakening it.

**Register instruction.** Keep the honest-limit half (it is doing real work and is
in the right register). ADD the adjudicate and adopt-directly relations in the

economical breath, as the canonical already does. Do NOT turn the discount-rate
passage into a technical exposition; the conviction is "the model is honest about

**Fact-discipline (figure ledger):** the discount-rate passage is qualitative and
