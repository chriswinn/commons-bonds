# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*

## Chapter 9: Pricing Honestly

---

A ton of coal left a McDowell County mine mouth in 1960 for $4.71. The price is the Energy Information Administration's, from its historical series on bituminous coal. The honest cost of the same ton, counted in the previous chapter component by component, ran to $510: $496 of it the carbon alone, at the Environmental Protection Agency's 2023 central estimate of $190 per metric ton of carbon dioxide, the remainder spread across the smaller severed costs the ledger named one at a time. Just under five dollars collected; more than five hundred owed. The gap is arithmetic. It does not soften with rechecking. I have tried.

The same gap opens wherever extraction outruns restoration. For coal, oil, and ore it is always open, because the restoration rate of a non-renewable is zero. For an oyster bar or a forest it opens only when the taking outpaces the regrowing, and it closes to zero when harvest stays inside what the system rebuilds. That is the whole scope condition: a statement about rates, descriptive, no more.

A reader who accepts the number usually asks the next question in plain words. Then what do we do? What follows is a sequence of four practical moves, none of which requires an institution that does not already exist somewhere in the world: Classify, Reserve, Invest, Reassess. Expectations should be set plainly at the start. There is no manifesto here and no model statute. There are four moves, in order, and then the evidence that every working part of them is already running somewhere.

---

Classify comes first.

In the Texas Panhandle a farmer is watching the irrigation pivots in the next county come off the fields one at a time as the wells stop drawing what they used to draw. The water under his land accumulated over millennia and recharges on timescales measured against geologic time. The iron in his pivots is among the most abundant elements in the crust, substitutable in nearly every use, recyclable at scale. His operation depends on both. Only one of them can foreclose his grandchildren.

Not every resource carries the same foreclosure risk, and a pricing instrument that treats them alike will spend its force on the abundant while leaving the irreplaceable exposed. The sorting variable is the existential substitutability gap: the distance between how badly a resource will be needed and how readily anything else can stand in for it. In running prose, the gap. A rough sort into three bins, low, medium, high, is enough to drive every decision that follows.

Low-gap resources are abundant and substitutable in principle: iron, coal as fuel. The renewable commons sit in this bin by construction, sunlight, wind, atmospheric oxygen, regional rainfall, because they are themselves the renewable substrate that the substitution investments of step three rest on, and because use within regeneration prices to zero. For the low bin the move is familiar: price the externality tail, require reclamation bonding adequate to the real cost of restoration, and let a corrected market allocate.

Medium-gap resources warrant progressive extraction rates and sovereign capture of the rents, so that depletion at least converts into a permanent asset held in common.

High-gap resources are geologically concentrated, chemically hard to substitute, and required for technologies not yet invented but very likely needed: the rare earth elements, the platinum-group metals, helium, phosphorus for agriculture, fossil groundwater on millennial recharge. Phosphorus makes the bin's logic plain. It is an element, not a design choice; no food system runs without it, and no laboratory will ever synthesize a substitute for it, because there is nothing to substitute with. These resources get the move the whole instrument is organized around, reservation, which is step two.

None of the sorting is mysterious. Materials scientists, energy economists, agronomists, aerospace engineers, and hydrologists already hold most of the needed information, and pieces of the apparatus already operate in public: the European Union maintains a statutory list of critical raw materials, and the United States Geological Survey publishes mineral assessments every year. What does not exist is a single legible ranking that integrates substitutability, stock dynamics, externality tail, and criticality into one score. Producing one is a matter of months, not decades.

One tradition objects at the threshold. Julian Simon's substitution optimism, vindicated in his famous commodity bet with Paul Ehrlich, holds that scarcity summons substitutes, so there is no missing price to find. Concede most of it: substitution usually wins, which is exactly why step three invests in substitution and the reserve holds only the residual that substitution may not reach. The formal treatment is in the Technical Appendix.

---

Reserve comes second, and it is the idea that sounds strange on first hearing and obvious on the second.

The ground has been holding these deposits for hundreds of millions of years without a guard, a roof, or an insurance premium. For high-gap resources, designate a fraction of the known economic deposits as a strategic reserve and leave it where it has always been. Not extracted and warehoused. Left in the ground, which is free, permanent storage requiring no refrigeration, no security detail, and no actuarial table.

The cost of holding the reserve is the revenue foregone on the reserved fraction, and the cost is small, because extraction is constrained by current demand and mining throughput, not by the size of the deposit. If, say, a tenth of a deposit class were set aside, and the industry was never going to work through more than a fraction of that class this century anyway, the revenue actually given up is modest and arrives spread across decades.

Held this way, the reserve behaves like a free option. It costs little to carry. It appreciates as the unreserved stock depletes. It pays its full substitution value if the scarcity ever arrives, and it can be reclassified and released if a substitute matures and the resource stops mattering. Step four exists for exactly that release.

No new international body is required. Strategic petroleum reserves and mineral stockpiles already exist in most industrial countries. What is missing is the principle that decides what to reserve and how much, and the principle is the step-one ranking, with reserved fractions sized to the asymmetric regret of the decision. The Asymmetric Regret Rule, formalized earlier in the book, does its work here without reintroduction. If the reserve proves unnecessary, the cost is modest foregone revenue spread across a century and a global economy. If the reserve proves necessary and the deposit has already been dug up and burned through, the cost is civilizational, and there is no one left to invoice. When the insurance is cheap and the downside is unbounded, the rational move is to buy the insurance.

A market-oriented reader will hear industrial policy in this, winner-picking, a distortion that invites capture. Reservation subsidizes no producer; it declines to foreclose an option, sizes the holding by regret, and leaves the corrected market to allocate everything not reserved.

---

Invest comes third. Reservation buys time; investment decides what the time is for.

On the Bay where my father grew up, the oyster bars no longer sound under the tongs the way they sounded when he was a boy watching the old oystermen work. Nobody reserved the bars, and nobody invested in what would have taken the pressure off them while there was still structure left to spare. The two failures were one failure. Step three is that failure inverted and run forward, with two simultaneous priorities.

The first is substitution, and it means more than renewable energy. Alternative battery chemistries to relieve lithium and cobalt. Recycled and synthesized flows to displace virgin rare earths. Regenerative agriculture to lift pressure off fossil aquifers and mined phosphate. Alternative proteins to lift it off collapsing fisheries. Each line of that list maps onto a cost-bearer this book has already met, which is what distinguishes a substitution agenda from a research wish list: the spending is aimed at the places where the severed costs are landing now. Every dollar of substitution research does three things at once: it lowers the residual commons value of the high-gap resources by widening the paths around them; it preserves the optionality the reserve is holding; and it shrinks the externality tail of whatever extraction continues in the meantime. Few expenditures reduce present harm, future harm, and future need simultaneously. This one does.

The second priority is alternative supply itself, which eventually means supply from off this planet, and the case is arithmetic rather than science fiction. A lifeless body has no community bearing spatial costs, no biosphere to cascade, an externality tail measured against empty rock. Its gap is very low by construction. As launch costs fall, material from such a body begins to compete on total honest cost with terrestrial deposits whose full residual commons value has finally been priced. The arithmetic says nothing about timing. It says only where the bulk extraction of the most abundant materials eventually goes once the books are honest, and the answer is off this world.

The governing principle for both priorities fits in one sentence: extract the lowest-residual-commons-value source first. For most materials over the next century that is the substitute. For a narrow set, eventually, it is rock that was never alive. Neither conclusion is political. Both fall out of the same arithmetic that priced the ton of coal, and the appendix develops the option-value method underneath it.

---

Reassess comes fourth, because nothing in the first three steps is static.

For most of a century the United States government sold helium so cheaply it was effectively giving the gas away; the same element that fills a party balloon cools the magnets in hospital imaging machines, and only one of those uses can tolerate losing it. Helium moved from afterthought to critical inside living memory. Cobalt ran the same direction: it looked abundant a generation ago and is now treated as strategically critical by most industrial nations. The traffic also runs the other way. A high-gap resource can become low-gap inside thirty years if a substitution breakthrough matures: magnet chemistries that need no rare earths, sodium-ion storage at grid parity, industrial processes that design helium out. When that happens, reserved fractions reclassify, some released to the corrected market, some held permanently as a hedge against the substitute's failure modes.

So the classification is a living document, maintained on a five-to-ten-year cadence by the same scientific bodies that produce it. The cadence matters less than the standing commitment: a ranking revisited on schedule cannot calcify into a subsidy, and a reserve that can be released is much harder to attack as a land grab than one that pretends to be permanent. The machinery mostly exists: the United States Geological Survey, the European Union's science apparatus, the International Energy Agency, the national stockpile programs. What is missing is the structure inside which reassessment matters, and the gap-driven reserve is that structure. One exposure belongs in the open rather than in a footnote: the choice of discount rate beneath any long-horizon valuation is contested, Stern against Nordhaus, and the quantitative treatment belongs to the Technical Appendix, not to this sequence.

---

Near Baotou, in Inner Mongolia, a woman's kitchen window looks out across a narrow band of houses at a tailings pond roughly 4 square miles across, about 10 square kilometers of processing waste from the rare earth refining the world's electronics depend on. In the cobalt supply chain of the Democratic Republic of the Congo, UNICEF estimated in 2014, in a figure Amnesty International carried forward in 2016, that some 40,000 children worked in the mines; until very recently, most of the world's cobalt passed through that country. Both burdens sit at the far end of supply chains that terminate in jurisdictions that will never price them.

That is the border problem. Cost severance crosses borders; borders are among the mechanisms by which it operates. A country that prices its own extraction honestly while importing unpriced foreign extraction has merely moved the severance to the port. The import gap becomes the new gap.

The response is a principle rather than a drafted treaty: apply the pricing to imports, mechanically a border adjustment, scaled to the residual commons value the exporting country left unpriced. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, CBAM, is a partial, real, imperfectly working version of exactly this, for carbon alone, and its existence is the proof that matters: the move is legally, administratively, and diplomatically feasible at scale. It faces honest non-discrimination questions at the World Trade Organization and a live risk of retaliation, and nothing here should overstate its ease. What it does not require is unanimity. A border adjustment spreads by market access, beginning wherever one country or bloc decides to begin, the way carbon pricing itself spread from a handful of jurisdictions to many.

---

The first check most households write each month is the rent, and the logic of the four steps does not stop at the mineral commons.

The strongest account of why American housing costs what it costs is supply restriction, and it deserves to be heard at full strength. Edward Glaeser and Joseph Gyourko's supply-elasticity work showed prices pulling away from construction costs precisely where regulation makes building hardest; Jenny Schuetz's land-use research traces the zoning rules and permitting gauntlets that do the restricting, metro by metro. Where building can answer demand, prices stay near the cost of building. Where it cannot, prices climb until they ration. As far as it goes, the account is right, and the remedy it implies, permit more homes where people want to live, is right as far as it goes too.

The claim this book adds operates at a second level of the same case, and it should be flagged for what it is: an interpretive argument of this book, contested by the supply-side scholars, and not a settled finding of the housing literature. The argument is that a meaningful share of the cost burden is rent in the economist's sense, captured through consolidation and coordinated pricing, stacked on top of the supply constraint. What is documented either way is the burden itself: roughly half of American renters pay more than 30 percent of their income for housing, in supply-restricted and supply-expanded metros alike, on the accounting of Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies in *America's Rental Housing 2024*.

The stakes of the distinction are not academic. If the burden were supply restriction alone, building would dissolve it; if a meaningful share of it is captured rent, building is necessary and insufficient, and the household keeps writing the first check of the month into someone else's ledger no matter how many permits the city issues.

The coordination half of the claim has one concrete exhibit on the public record. RealPage, whose software recommended rents to landlords holding large shares of particular markets, drew a Department of Justice antitrust investigation in 2022; the government filed its formal complaint in August 2024; a proposed settlement was lodged on November 24, 2025, and awaits court approval. The complaint described an algorithm pooling nonpublic competitor data and recommending prices in concert. Whatever the courts conclude, the case put a name and a docket number on the kind of coordination the supply story alone cannot see.

Housing can also be held differently altogether. One European capital has been demonstrating that for a century, and the count of existing pieces below returns to it.

The older shape underneath all of it is familiar from the coal camps. Work for the company, rent from the company, owe the company store. The arrangement has come back in distributed form: households cost-burdened to an asset class whose upside they will never hold a share of, paying the first check of every month into the gap.

---

One way to see that none of this is utopian is to count the pieces already in place.

Start with a check in a mailbox. Since 1976 the State of Alaska has captured a share of its mineral royalty income into a constitutional trust, roughly $89 billion as of 2026, and since 1982 the trust has paid every resident an annual dividend, about $1,702 in 2024. That is rent capture made ordinary, settled enough that repealing it is politically unthinkable in a state not otherwise known for collectivism.

Norway is the deeper proof, and the book has already built it; only the reading needs restating. What the Government Pension Fund Global buys, in this book's terms, is reversibility. A nation holding roughly $2 trillion against the day the oil is gone, about $390,000 per citizen, retains options that a nation that spent the royalties does not, and preserved options are precisely what collapse the foreclosure premium on extraction. Read off the realized record, Norway internalized about $48 per barrel of oil equivalent, a revealed floor under what a polity will actually pay to keep its extraction from foreclosing its future. The parameters live in the Technical Appendix.

Carbon pricing operates across many jurisdictions, covering a substantial and growing share of global emissions on the World Bank's running count. Almost everywhere it prices below the social cost of carbon. It is nonetheless real, legally functional, politically defended, and expanding, which is what an entering wedge looks like in the wild.

Strategic reservation is already practiced wherever dependency has made it visible. Most industrial countries hold petroleum reserves and mineral stockpiles. The United States watched its one significant rare earth mine, at Mountain Pass, close in 2002, pass through its prior operator's bankruptcy in 2015, change hands in 2017, and restart in 2018, a supply-chain repair conducted entirely in the existing vocabulary of national security. The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act entered into force in May 2024 with its lists of 34 critical and 17 strategic materials. Japan answered its 2010 rare earth shock by building alternative sourcing rather than waiting. None of these moves was designed in this book's vocabulary. Each shows gap-driven reservation becoming reachable the moment the classification problem acquires visibility.

Finance carries a wedge of its own. The Dodd-Frank Act is a partial implementation of accountability-bond logic: institutions that capture the upside of risk made to hold more of its downside. The crisis it answered is also the clearest modern record of the alternative not taken. The 2008 response recapitalized bank balance sheets rather than restructuring household debt, while roughly $11 trillion in household wealth, on the Federal Reserve's flow-of-funds accounting, disappeared, and some 4.1 million completed foreclosures followed. Under honest pricing the alternative was visible in advance; Chapter 5 carries the full case, including Atif Mian and Amir Sufi's analysis of the response, and it stays there.

The surface-mining reclamation statute requires bonds against the cost of restoring mined land, and the mechanism is sound even where the practice is not. The bond pool covers a fraction of the true cost; the gaps have been papered over through bankruptcy after bankruptcy and transferred to the public, as the early case chapters documented; the federal black lung program alone had paid out roughly $44 billion through 2009, on Government Accountability Office and Congressional Research Service figures, for a cost that never appeared in any mine-mouth price. The repair is specifiable in one line: bond to the real remediation cost, index it, refresh it as the liability tail lengthens.

Environmental impact assessment, under the National Environmental Policy Act and its analogs abroad, prices nothing in dollars and has survived five decades of litigation anyway: a procedural, durable, partial implementation of pricing the externality tail. The border adjustment already met above is another standing piece. So, in its own register, is the community-reinvestment statute, which encodes the spatial principle that capital extracted from a place owes something to the place.

Three further pieces prove the adjacent claim, that collective capture works at scale and survives hostile weather. Chattanooga's municipally owned electric utility, EPB, lit a citywide fiber network in 2010 and has run it since at multi-gigabit speeds and prices below the private incumbents, profitably, cross-subsidizing the smart grid, through incumbent litigation and state-level restriction. Mondragon, the Basque worker-cooperative federation operating since 1956 and the largest worker-cooperative system in the world, has crossed the end of a dictatorship, European integration, the 2008 crisis, and the bankruptcy of its flagship appliance maker, Fagor, while holding an internal discipline on the ratio between its highest and lowest pay. And Vienna has housed its people through municipally owned and non-profit cooperative housing since 1919, roughly 60 percent of the city living in it today, across a century of regime change and financing regimes, at rents well below private comparables and without stigma. The closing chapter gathers what these places feel like from inside. Their work here is narrower: three running existence proofs that capture by the commons is neither small-scale nor fragile.

The hardest live test of the four steps is underway right now, on the seabed. The Clarion-Clipperton Zone, a stretch of Pacific floor of about 4.5 million square kilometers, holds vast fields of polymetallic nodules rich in exactly the metals the battery transition wants. The International Seabed Authority, the ISA, established in 1994 under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, has issued exploration contracts since 2001 and has been negotiating a commercial mining code under timeline pressure since Nauru triggered a deadline provision in 2021. Run the steps in order. Classify: the metals are high-gap, and the seabed's recovery timescales are unknown at the scale of decades to centuries, which places its externality tail in the unbounded category Chapter 7 established in its ocean-world treatment. Reserve: with the downside unbounded and the insurance cheap, asymmetric regret points to preserve and study. Invest: the same metals are reachable through substitution chemistry and terrestrial recycling carrying lower residual commons value. Reassess: any mining code written now can carry reclassification provisions, the living-document clause built in from the start. The pro-mining counter-case is honest and should be said plainly: terrestrial cobalt and nickel extraction carries severe human and ecological costs of its own, the children in the cobalt chain among them, so the regret math is contested in both directions. Whether the ISA's verification machinery can resist the capture pattern Chapter 7 stress-tested under the name of the immune response, the simulated reattachment of a cost-bearer that changes no actual flows, is the live question, and the parties are answering it in real time. The reading is the contribution. The ruling belongs to the parties.

Each of these pieces is partial. Each evolved piecemeal, under its own crisis, carried by its own coalition, none of them with this book's vocabulary in mind. Together they put every component of honest pricing on the ground somewhere in the world, already past proof of concept, already survived into institutional furniture. What remains is connection: strengthening, aligning, extending what exists, ordinary institutional work made strategic by the principle that organizes it.

---

Walk a managed timber forest and you can read the owner's horizon in the spacing of the trees. The most serious objection to everything above comes from the tradition that planted those rows.

Property rights, the tradition runs, already solve the commons problem where it can be solved. Ronald Coase's 1960 essay on social cost showed that when rights are well defined and bargaining is cheap, the parties price externalities among themselves, no central instrument required. The record backs the tradition further than its critics like to concede. Fisheries stabilize under quota shares. Forests regrow when owners internalize the timber's future value. For rivalrous, excludable, present-tense goods, ownership is often the best preservation technology anyone has found, and nothing in this book quarrels with that finding.

Three things sit structurally beyond the reach of any deed. First, intergenerational foreclosure: future generations hold no deeds and cannot bid, so the Coasean table is missing the party with the largest stake, and the bargain struck there is complete only in form. Second, transboundary externality: drainage, atmospheric carbon, aquifer drawdown, tailings dust respect no fence line and no title registry. Third, civilizational information asymmetry: markets price what their participants currently know, and the value of a reserve held against uses not yet invented is exactly the information that does not exist at the moment of pricing. The foundational chapters carry the arguments and the Technical Appendix carries the formalism. The relationship to Coase is extension. Where his conditions hold, let bargaining work; where they cannot hold even in principle, the pricing has to be built.

Then why does the gap stay open when the arithmetic is this legible? Public Choice supplies the cleanest answer on offer, and it costs the book nothing to take it. Mancur Olson's *The Logic of Collective Action* explained in 1965 why concentrated beneficiaries reliably out-organize diffuse cost-bearers. Gordon Tullock identified in 1967 the social cost of contesting for privilege, the dynamic the profession later named rent-seeking. James Buchanan and Tullock built the school that reads political actors as interested parties rather than benevolent planners. The borrowing here is vocabulary; the broader political project that often travels with the school stays where it is. Public Choice names who shaped the rent-seeking arrangements and why the severance persists, with no villain required. This book supplies the ledger of what the arrangement cost, and to whom. The two accounts fit precisely because neither needs the other to be wrong.

Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* supplies the legal half of the same answer. Capital, in her account, is coded out of ordinary legal modules, property, collateral, trust, corporate, and bankruptcy law, and the coding systematically hardens the asset side of the ledger while shedding the cost side. That is the diagnosis the previous chapter reached from the accounting side, arriving independently from the legal literature, and it is why well-defined property rights, left to themselves, leave the costs exactly where the code put them.

The nearest neighbor in the current literature argues from the opposite direction and deserves the longest engagement. Brett Christophers' *The Price Is Wrong* (Verso, 2024) holds that markets are failing the energy transition not because prices are wrong but because investment follows profit; renewable projects, whatever their falling costs, do not clear private profit thresholds without state support. His stronger claim is the one worth taking seriously: that the entire pricing approach, carbon taxes included, mistakes the disease, and that decommodified public provision is what the transition actually requires. On the mechanism he is right, and the concession matters: an honest price that no investor can profit by answering will not, by itself, build a single turbine. Where this book differs is on what the pricing is for. Pricing the residual commons value of fossil extraction does not redirect capital on its own; it makes the subsidy-and-rent arrangement visible, line by line, and visibility is what makes the political work of redirecting capital possible. Christophers explains why capital will not move on price signals alone. The ledger shows what the standing arrangement costs and who carries it. The two arguments converge on the same ground: public investment, and public-purpose criteria for allocating capital, are what honest accounting arrives at once the gap between price and profit is named as the structural problem.

The deepest lineage here is older. Karl Polanyi argued in *The Great Transformation* (1944) that land, labor, and money are fictitious commodities, traded as if produced for sale though no one ever produced them, and that a society pricing them as ordinary goods calls up a countermovement that tries to re-embed the market in social life before the market consumes its own substrate. Nancy Fraser has carried the diagnosis forward: behind every wage relation stand expropriated nature and unwaged social reproduction, taken rather than paid for, the hidden subsidy the visible economy runs on. What those accounts diagnosed, this book prices. Cost severance is the fictitious-commodity problem restated as arithmetic, and the reserve and the bond are the double movement built as an accounting instrument rather than awaited as an upheaval. The debt to that lineage is a real debt; what the book adds is a unit of account.

An honest reader of Pistor will press one more question: whether pricing and bonding the commons simply extends the coding she warned about, one more legal module converting nature into an asset class. The distinction is worth a careful sentence. Pricing a cost to force the books to tell the truth, and reserving a deposit against commodification, runs the code in reverse; the bond fixes the liability on the extractor instead of fixing a ticker on the commons. Accounting the cost is not coding the asset.

None of this leaves the market tradition behind. A price that omits most of the cost is a distortion in Adam Smith's own terms, and correcting it completes the market rather than replacing it.

---

Anyone who has spent a decade trying to move a bill through a legislature has a fair question waiting at the end of all this. Carbon pricing has been argued for since before the Kyoto Protocol; the most ambitious schemes ever enacted are riddled with exemptions; the politics have consumed careers. If the world cannot hold a simple carbon tax together after three decades of near-consensus among economists, what are the odds of comprehensive residual-commons-value pricing across every resource and every jurisdiction? Show the pathway, the coalition, the treaty draft. Otherwise this is one more book that diagnoses what it cannot cure.

The honest answer concedes the premise. No single book can produce the pathway, and pretending otherwise would misrepresent what a book is. This one offers no model statute and no whip count, and the instrument it builds does not legislate. The stakes of the work it leaves to others are the previous chapter's aggregate: severed costs running on the order of $10 to 15 trillion a year worldwide, by the derivation built there, a sum that does not wait politely while coalitions form.

What a framework can do is change what legislation becomes conceivable, and the record on that point is better than the legislator's question allows. David Harvey named accumulation by dispossession, and the conversation about enclosure and gentrification has not run the same since. Elinor Ostrom named the design principles that long-lived local institutions had already evolved, and a generation of commons governance gained standing against the twin defaults of privatization and state management. Kate Raworth drew a doughnut, and a city and a number of public bodies adopted it as a guiding frame because it gave coherent shape to what they were already trying to do. Matthew Desmond's *Evicted* helped shift how a country talks about eviction and housing instability. None of them passed a statute. Each changed what a statute could say.

I spent twenty years inside institutions whose ledgers managed costs by passing them along, and I never once watched a cost die because it went unrecorded. It moved, every time, to whoever had the least power to refuse it. Classify, Reserve, Invest, Reassess is the instrument I wanted in those rooms and did not have.

What *Commons Bonds* offers sits in the same genre as the work of those four: a vocabulary, cost severance, residual commons value, the severed cost, the accountability bond, the substitutability gap; a measurement tool, two independent estimators and a reading of what polities have actually internalized that sets a floor beneath them, testable against any extraction in any jurisdiction; and a way of thinking that a coal-country resident, a municipal council, a state legislature, or an international body can apply at its own scale. The entering wedges are where the applying starts: strengthen, connect, align, extend what already exists. The book gives the people who will do that work a common language and a common instrument. Whether the work gets done is the question every generation answers for the one that follows.

---

The miner in McDowell County is underground this morning, his lungs sounding the way his father's lungs sounded. The waterman is on the Lower Bay, running pots he will mend in the afternoon at the shop, because the morning's math no longer carries a family alone. Near Baotou the kitchen window still frames the tailings pond. In the Panhandle the farmer is deciding which field comes out of irrigation next. And somewhere ahead of all of them, on a world with no margin for severance, an administrator will sign or refuse an extraction order on which a habitat's water and ten thousand lives depend.

Five figures, one problem in five sets of parameters. The mechanism that severed cost from value in a coal seam runs by the same structural logic on an oyster bar, in a tailings pond, down a depleting well, and under the ice of another world.

What extraction costs, what an honest price would say, and how existing institutions can be bent toward saying it: those questions now have answers, within the limits of one volume. The question that remains is of a different kind, less a question of policy than a fact about ourselves and the generations we belong to and owe, and no ledger can settle it for us. The closing chapter goes there, to the present-day moments where it is being decided, by people who are alive now, in the decades while the deciding is still ours to do.
