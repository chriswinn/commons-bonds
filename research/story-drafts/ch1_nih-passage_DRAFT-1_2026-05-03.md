I started my career at the National Institutes of Health, twenty-two years old, on a contract that was supposed to last three months. The Human Genome Project would complete during my time there. I cannot lay claim to any of that work — that work belonged to researchers I still consider some of the greatest minds in the world. My job was downstream of theirs. I built the websites that took the proven work of those researchers, arranged the peer review, and then took the insights from the proven work and translated them so they were useful to different audiences.

That sounds simple. It was deceptively difficult. The same paper on a marker for a rare genetic issue had to be technically rigorous enough for the researchers in the field to take seriously, and accessible enough that, on the same web page, a child living with that genetic issue could understand what the scientists had found and what it might mean. There is a particular look on a child's face when they realize that maybe, one day, children won't have to be sick the way they have been sick. I saw that look. It changes you. It humbles you. It makes the work feel like the kind of work worth not sleeping for.

Which I didn't, much. The first contract was supposed to be three months, but the project I was brought in to look at had been running for two years already. It had been planned for a team of forty-five people, one year, one million dollars. By the time I arrived it was at two-and-a-half million and there was no end in sight, and the team's plan was to ask for another year, another two million dollars, more people. I looked at it. I cut everything that wasn't load-bearing on the original intent. Three months later I had a working proof-of-concept. The contract got renewed. They had another project they wanted me to look at, one that another team had given up on. Then another. The renewals kept coming.

I want to be honest about both halves of what those years were. The work was meaningful, and the recognition for the work was the work itself. That is a real thing. There is a satisfaction that comes from looking at a finished piece of work and knowing it was good — even when no one else will ever see it the way you see it. I learned that satisfaction at the NIH. I learned it from my father long before that, but the NIH is where I first learned it could carry an adult through weeks of grinding a problem into a shape it would yield. I carried that lesson through the rest of my career.

I also learned, at the NIH, the pattern that would, twenty years later, cost me my marriage and the early years with my son. I billed over a hundred and twenty hours a week for stretches, sometimes for months at a time. There were three weeks back-to-back, right before a project deadline, where I billed close to a hundred and thirty-eight hours every seven days. I delivered the project early. There was a stack of paper paychecks on my desk that I had not opened. My hair grew long because I did not have time for haircuts; eventually I started shaving my own head in the bathroom on mute during conference calls. I ordered food at drive-throughs on mute during conference calls. I have a memory of waking up one morning at my desk, the laptop still open in front of me, and realizing I had fallen asleep mid-sentence typing an email — to whom, I don't remember; about what, I don't remember; the thing I remember is cussing at myself for not having clicked send before sleep took me, because someone had needed it before they woke up.

Sleep took me. I had stopped choosing.

Both halves of what those years were are inseparable. I did not notice this until decades later. The skill that made me good at the work — the willingness to refocus on what actually mattered, to ask the question no one else was willing to ask, to grind through the hard parts because the work was worth doing — was the same skill that taught me to absorb cost into my own body without registering it as cost. The recognition came, when it came, much later.

The contracts at the NIH ended naturally and on good terms. The main project contact who had been bringing me into project after project was on a golf course one weekend talking to friends at the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank. Both of them had projects their teams were stuck on. He called me on Monday morning. That was how I left the NIH. That was how the next decade began.
