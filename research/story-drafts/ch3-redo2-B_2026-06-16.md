# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 3: The Waterman

---

The harbor wakes before the city does.

At Old Point Comfort the water is still the color of slate when the first engines turn over, a sound that carries a long way across flat water in the cold, so that you hear a boat before you see it and you see it before the sun is up to light it. The air smells of diesel and salt and the low tide. A man's voice crosses the harbor, calling something to another man, and the words arrive clean and unhurried, the way speech does over water when there is nothing yet awake to compete with it. A channel marker blinks out past the point, green, then dark, then green. The sky goes from black to a gray that is not yet morning. And then the watermen go out, the way the watermen have gone out of this harbor for longer than anyone living can remember, to bring back what the water gives.

I grew up on this water. I want you to love it before I tell you what was taken from it.

---

The Chesapeake is not a gentle bay, and the people who work it are the first to tell you so. Out on the open ocean a wave that stands five feet high will roll under a boat across thirty or forty feet of wavelength, and the boat lifts and settles, lifts and settles, a long slow breathing you can sleep through. The Bay does not do this. The Bay can stack a five-foot wave onto a wavelength of barely more than its own height, a near-square wall of water that does not roll under you but hits you, again and again, with no rhythm a body can learn. Watermen call it the chop. It will turn the hardiest blue-water sailor green inside an hour. The men who work the Chesapeake have it in their knees and their hips before they have it in any book, and they do not think of it as hardship. They think of it as the water. It is simply the character of the thing they love.

There is a boat for this water. They call it the deadrise, a low working boat built flat enough to come up onto an oyster bar in shallows and sharp enough at the bow to take the chop without snapping a man's spine. It is not a beautiful boat the way a yacht is beautiful. The yachts are somewhere else, anyway, in deeper and warmer and easier water, in harbors where the work is done by other people in other places and the costs of it are nowhere in sight. The deadrise is beautiful the way a good tool is beautiful, which is to say it is exactly shaped to its job and nothing about it is for show. You can read a man's whole working life in the wear of his deadrise.

And the morning gives you the sun. On the water at that hour, before the heat, before the day has decided what it will be, the light comes up over the far shore and lays a road of it straight across the chop toward your boat, and for a while the day holds every possibility it will ever hold. A waterman watches that light come up more mornings of his life than most people watch anything, and he will tell you, if you ask him and he trusts you enough to answer honestly, that it does not get old. I have watched it come up out there. It does not get old.

That is the water, loved. Now the men.

---

To work an oyster bed with a single oar is one of the most intimate skills I have ever watched a human being perform.

The boat sits over the bed, and the waterman moves it not with an engine, which would foul the water and waste the fuel and announce him to every other boat for a mile, but with one oar, worked over the stern in motions so small you would not see them if you did not know to look. A turn of the wrist. A feathering of the blade. The boat slides six inches sideways and holds, and he tongs up the oysters from directly beneath him, and then another turn of the wrist and the boat slides six inches more. He is reading the bed by feel, through the oar, the way a blind man reads a face with his fingers. Nobody teaches this in a year. It is a conversation between a man and a piece of bottom that takes a lifetime to learn to have, and the men who have learned it carry it lightly, the way my grandfather carried the things he was best at, as if it were nothing at all.

The water that gives them this also wants to kill them, and they know it, and they go anyway.

In the cold months they wear rain slicks over thick base layers and heavy sweaters, and the slicks keep them dry but give back no warmth of their own, so the warmth has to come from the wool underneath, layer on layer until a man is heavy as a diver before he has touched the water. Every waterman who works the cold knows the arithmetic of those clothes. He knows that if he goes over the side into water near freezing, the same weight that keeps him warm on deck will hold him down and will not come off in time, that wet wool and a soaked slick are a thing a drowning man cannot fight his way out of with the minutes the cold allows him. They know this the way you know the road you drive every day. And they put the clothes on, and they go out onto the chop in the dark, because that is where the work is and the work is how the family eats. There is no bravado in it. It is just the price of the morning, paid the way you pay a thing you have stopped noticing you pay.

The master of all of it, in the stories I grew up inside, was a man my father knew as a boy. People called him Biggie. He has been gone more than thirty years now, an oysterman of Fox Hill from a generation that is mostly gone with him, and what I know of him I know the way you know a thing told to you so many times that you can finish it before the teller does. My father is my source for Biggie. I am giving you my father's story, worn smooth by the telling.

Biggie worked with an economy of motion that the other men talked about the way men talk about a thing they cannot quite do themselves. No wasted movement. No hurry that was not the hurry of the work. He could put up more oysters in a morning than men half his age and come in looking like he had been for a walk. And the story my father tells, the one I have heard most and believe most, is small.

It was a long morning's work ahead, and my father, young then, noticed that Biggie had brought no lunch. There was a wire carrier on the deck with a six-pack of Coca-Cola in it, no ice, no cooler, the bottles just sitting in the wire in the open air. My father, thinking the man would go hungry, asked him about it. What was he going to eat. And Biggie smiled and told him not to worry. His lunch, he said, was still in the water.

His lunch was still in the water. When the hour came he would reach over the side and bring it up, an oyster or a crab or whatever the bed was giving that day, as fresh as a thing can possibly be, because it had been alive a minute before he ate it. The water was the larder. You did not pack a lunch when you worked on top of the most abundant table in the world. You brought something to drink, and the rest you took up as you needed it, and there was no end of it that anyone alive then could imagine.

Hold that. Hold the six-pack and the lunch still in the water, because I am going to need you to remember it.

---

The water fed them, and they governed it.

I want to be careful here, because there is a famous idea that says they could not have. In 1968 the biologist Garrett Hardin published an essay called "The Tragedy of the Commons," and its argument has shaped how educated people think about shared resources ever since. A pasture open to all, Hardin said, is doomed. Each herdsman has every reason to add one more animal, because he keeps all the gain from that animal and shares the cost of the overgrazing with everyone, and so every rational herdsman adds animals until the pasture is destroyed. A resource that belongs to everyone, in this telling, belongs in practice to no one, and is fated to be used up, because no single user has any reason to be the one who holds back.

The watermen of Fox Hill are the thing Hardin's essay cannot explain.

Consider one morning my father carried out of his boyhood. A man's nets had been torn up, by a storm or by accident, in the way that working gear is always being torn up, and a torn net is a man's income stopped until it is mended. And one morning the other watermen did not go out to their own beds. They set their own work aside and they spent the morning rebuilding the one man's nets, all of them together, and then they went back to their own work the next day. Nobody organized it from above. There was no company, no union hall, no foreman assigning the labor. There was a community that had worked out, across generations, a bargain that no one had ever written down: you give up a morning for the man whose gear is wrecked, because the morning is coming, as sure as the tide, when the man whose gear is wrecked is you. A community governing its own labor, with no industry above it to do the governing, each man paying a cost now against a cost he knows is coming for him later.

That is not the tragedy Hardin predicted. That is its opposite, and it was the ordinary water-knowledge of a place.

The oyster bar itself taught the same lesson, and this is the part worth slowing down for, because it is the most sustained version in this whole book of a boundary I am going to draw again and again. An oyster reef is not like a coal seam. A coal seam is finite from the day it forms. Every ton you bring up is a ton that will never be there again, and when you have taken the last of it you have a hole and nothing else. A reef is the other kind of thing entirely. A reef is an instrument that makes more oysters every year, on its own, for free, as long as you do not damage the instrument. The oysters filter the water and build the structure that the next generation of oysters settles onto, so that a healthy reef left mostly alone will hand you a harvest this year and a harvest next year and a harvest for your grandchildren, the same harvest, undiminished, forever, on one condition. The condition is that you take less than it makes. Take some and leave the rest to spawn, and come back next year, and the bed is still there, still working, still building itself out of the water. This is the renewable commons. It is the textbook case of the kind of self-renewing resource that a community living on top of it can sustain across generations, which is exactly the case the political scientist Elinor Ostrom spent her life's work describing, the renewing common-pool resource whose abundance lasts precisely as long as the people who depend on it hold the rate of taking below the rate of making. The Fox Hill watermen held it for a long time. The reef-as-instrument is the renewable half of the boundary. The coal seam is the other half. The whole argument of this book lives along the line between them.

The watermen knew which kind of thing they were standing on. They governed the rate of taking because they understood, without anyone teaching them a theory of it, that the bed they did not exhaust this year was the bed that would feed their sons. That is the before. That is the commons so abundant it fed a man his lunch straight out of the water, worked by a community that governed its own hand.

It did not last. I watched it not last, across my own life.

---

The loss did not come all at once, which is part of why it was so hard to see while it was happening.

It came the way the sunset comes out there, on what the islanders call island time. You watch the sun go down over the water and you would swear it takes forever, the light reddening and spreading and holding, the whole sky given over to it, an hour that feels like it will never close. And then you look away for a moment, to coil a line or answer a man, and when you look back it is gone, and the dark is already on the water, and you cannot say when the change happened because there was no moment when it happened. It was slow and then it was sudden and you missed it. That is exactly how the abundance went. Slowly, slowly, across years a man could tell himself were just a bad season or two, and then it was simply gone, and looking back you could not name the year it left.

The fish I loved to eat as a boy thinned out and some of them vanished. The striped bass above all, the rockfish, the great anchor species of the Bay, the fish around which an entire way of life had organized itself. There were years they were everywhere and years you could not find them, and the watermen and the scientists argued about why, and the arguing has not stopped.

I am not going to pretend the watermen had clean hands, because they did not, and neither did I, and neither did anyone who ever ate a Chesapeake oyster or a Chesapeake crab. There came a season, late in the abundance, that turned out to be the last meaningful season of a thing, though nobody knew it was the last while they were in it. And in that season, knowing the bottom was falling out, knowing the catch was thinner than it should have been, we still took as much as we could carry to the shore. Everyone did. There was no villain in a boat that year. There was a whole community of decent people, each one making the only choice the situation seemed to offer, which was to take what was there while it was still there, and the sum of all those reasonable choices was the thing none of them wanted. The severance does not need a villain. It never has.

The people who study the Bay for a living have been saying the plainest version of this for years, and they say it carefully, because their careers ride on saying only what the data supports. Chris Moore, who directs the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's Virginia office, put it about as flatly as a scientist will, responding to a recent stock assessment of the striped bass: *Despite recent management actions, striped bass continue to struggle. It is obvious that conditions have changed and striped bass will not have the same recovery they had in the 1980s. Warmer water from climate change, habitat loss, and invasive species are all making it very hard for striped bass to bounce back.* Not the same recovery they had in the 1980s. That is a scientist telling you that the water you remember is not coming back in the form you remember it.

And the squeeze reaches the men even where the news looks good. Allison Colden, who runs the Foundation's Maryland office, will tell you that the oyster population has tripled since 2005, which is a real and hard-won recovery, and in nearly the same breath she will tell you the watermen are still feeling the pinch, that there are a lot fewer eyes on the water than there used to be, that for the striped bass the looming disaster is real and for the blue crab the red flags are flying. A tripled oyster population and watermen feeling the pinch are not a contradiction. They are what it looks like when a fishery comes partway back and the people are already mostly gone. There is also the menhaden, the little oily fish at the bottom of the whole food web that everything else in the Bay eats, and the people who watch it most closely watch the harvest of it with concern. I am naming that concern because Moore and Colden and others have named it on the record. I am not pricing it, and I am not asserting the science past what they have said. The menhaden is named, and left named.

This is the after, and it has a face.

There is a waterman I know of Fox Hill whom I am not going to name, because he is alive and private and the naming would cost him something and gain you nothing. He works the same water Biggie worked. He is good at it the way the old men were good at it. And he cannot feed his family with the water alone anymore. So he runs a mechanic shop, his name on the sign out front, crab pots stacked behind a locked fence, and he splits his life between an engine on a bench and a boat on the chop, working two trades to make the one living his grandfather's generation made from the water by itself. He is not a failure. He is a master of a thing the water no longer pays for. Put him next to Biggie and you have the whole story in two men, one generation apart, on the same Fox Hill water: the man whose lunch was still in the water, and the man who has to fix engines to keep his boat in it.

And here is the thing that breaks the Hardin story for good, because you might expect, watching the fishery fall, that these were the doomed herdsmen after all, each one taking his last while he could. But watch what they actually did when the rockfish collapsed. In the mid-1980s the striped bass had fallen so far that the states closed the fishery, and the watermen, the charter captains, the recreational anglers, the whole community whose income and pleasure depended on that fish, agreed to stop. For five years. They agreed to take nothing, to forgo the thing they depended on, so that the fish could come back, and the fish did come back, enough that the moratorium was lifted and is remembered as one of the few clean victories in the Bay's long decline. People whose livelihoods sat on a resource chose, collectively, to give it up for five years to save it. That is not a tragedy of the commons. That is a commons governing itself, paying a cost now for a resource later, doing the precise thing Hardin's model says is impossible. What broke the Chesapeake was never the absence of self-governance among the watermen. It was a severance that happened somewhere else, and settled here.

To show you that, I have to take you back to a pile of shells.

---

There was a time when the wealth of this water was so large it was stacked in the open air four stories high, and you could stand on the Hampton waterfront and look at it.

The man who built that was J.S. Darling, and the operation he ran out of Hampton in the decades around the turn of the last century was, by the accounts that survive, the largest oyster business in the world. At dawn the log canoes came in off the bars and unloaded onto his docks. Inside, a workforce that ran to something like a hundred and sixty shuckers opened oysters all day, and the meat, the valuable part, went into iced tubs and onto trains, two hundred thousand bushels of it in a good year, bound north for the restaurant tables of Baltimore and New York and the cities up the coast. By the late years the operation was packing something on the order of fifty thousand gallons of shucked oyster meat a year. The meat left on ice. What stayed in Hampton was the shell.

The empty shells came out of the shucking houses on conveyor belts and went onto piles, and the piles grew, year over year, into mountains more than four stories high on the Hampton waterfront. A child could have climbed them. They were the single most visible fact about the place, white hills of discarded shell, the leavings of a feast that was being eaten three hundred miles away.

I want you to see what that picture is, because it is the same picture as the last chapter, only wet. The value, the meat, went up and out, onto the ice, onto the trains, into accounts and onto tables in cities that never saw the water it came from. What stayed in Hampton was the cost, and the cost wore the form of a mountain of the thing the Bay most needed back. Because here is what nobody at the time had any reason to know: those discarded shells were not waste. They were the instrument. Oyster shell is what baby oysters settle onto. Pile the shell back on the bottom and you have built the next reef, for free, out of the leavings. The shell was the seed of the recovery, and it was sitting in mountains on the waterfront because the part that could be sold had already left.

Most of those mountains did not go back into the water. The shell was cheap and it was useful and it was everywhere, so they crushed it for fill. It went into roads and driveways and the foundations of wharves all over the region, a whole landscape paved, quietly, in the ground-up substrate of its own future. Some of it, in time, did get returned to Back River and other beds to seed reefs, and that mattered. But most of it was crushed into the ground people drove on, and it is still under there, and it is not coming back.

I know that detail in the most personal way a person can know a thing, because it ran through my own family.

One college summer my father, who could pour concrete as well as he could do most things, helped pour the boardwalk at Buckroe Beach. He poured it in concrete and rock, the way you build a thing meant to last, and it has lasted. The Buckroe boardwalk is still there. I have walked on it. There is no oyster shell in it, and I am telling you that plainly because the easy version of this story, the one that would land harder, is the one where the boardwalk is paved in the Bay's own discarded shell, and that version is not true, and I will not tell you a thing that is not true to make a point land harder.

But the true version is in the family too, a few feet away. The driveway of the family home was paved in oyster shell. Not for any sentiment about the Bay. Because oyster shell was cheaper than rock. That is the whole of it. The same calculation that crushed the region's reefs into its roads was running in my own family's yard, a driveway of ground oyster shell because the shell cost less than stone, the future of the water spread out to be parked on because in the accounting that anyone ran at the time it was simply the cheaper fill.

I was born into the after. The Darling operation shut down in 1979. I was born in 1980. I never saw the four-story piles. I never stood on the white mountains of shell. The abundance that built them was already a thing my father talked about in the past tense by the time I was old enough to listen, and so the only way I ever possessed it was the way I possessed Biggie, as a story, handed to me already finished. I inherited the abundance the way you inherit a photograph of a house that has already burned down.

And now, in my own lifetime, the public is paying to put the reef back. The shell that was crushed into the driveways is gone, so where the reef has to be rebuilt, it is being rebuilt out of concrete. There is a crew at the Navy's installation at Hampton Roads that stacks concrete blocks into structures they call oyster castles, on the federal dime, to do for the water exactly what the discarded shells would have done for free if anyone had thought to put them back. We ground up the regenerative substrate and paved our driveways with it, and now we cast its replacement in concrete and pay for it out of public money. That is the full circle. That is the whole severance in a single image, and once you have seen it you cannot unsee it.

So let me do for the oyster what this book refused to leave undone for the miner. Let me add up the column.

---

The instrument that adds up this particular column is the one I am going to call the Restitution Bond, and this is the first time in the book I am going to take an instrument out and run it on a real commons, so let me say plainly what it is.

The Restitution Bond is the backward-looking instrument. It looks back at a commons that was drawn down, prices the physical cost of making that commons whole again, and names who should hold that bill, instead of letting the bill drift, one more time, onto the public that did not capture the value. It is the backward-looking partner to a forward-looking instrument I will develop later, the one that catches a cost before it lands and keeps the money until the bill comes due. The Restitution Bond is for damage already done.

The discipline of it is not mine. It belongs to a body of work I want to credit before I run it on a reef. The economists William Darity and Kirsten Mullen, in *From Here to Equality*, and the writer Ta-Nehisi Coates, in "The Case for Reparations," built the architecture of exactly this move: you take a drawn-down thing, you price the injury by the documented, physical cost of restoring it, and you assign that bill to the party that captured the value and to its successors, rather than handing it, yet again, to the people who already bore the cost. That is their discipline, and I am using their language inside their typology, applied here to a reef. I want to be careful about the boundary of the borrowing, because it matters. The reef's restitution number is a substrate-restoration cost, the price of physical reef bottom. It is not, and I am not going to let it pretend to be, a price on a human injury. The reparations economists priced a human wrong. I am crediting their instrument and pricing a reef with it. The lineage is theirs. The reef is mine to count.

Now the counting, and the first discipline of the counting is restraint.

I am not going to price the lost fishery. I am not going to price the menhaden, or the foregone oysters of all the years the reefs were down, or the livelihoods of the watermen who left the water, or the long human weight of a community thinned out the way Fox Hill has thinned out. Every one of those is a real cost. Every one of them is also a number a critic could argue with for a week, and each one invites an argument I do not need to have to make this case. So I am going to leave them off the books, on purpose, named and unpriced, and I am going to price exactly one thing: the reef bottom itself. Physical. Documented. There.

And I am going to refuse a second temptation, which is to build the number out of those four-story mountains of shell. It would be easy. The mountains are the most dramatic image in the chapter and the arithmetic would look enormous. It would also be wrong, and a critic would take it apart in an afternoon, because raw shell is cheap, and multiplying a cheap thing by a century of volume gives you a big number built on the wrong measuring stick. The mountains are the image of the severance. They are not the price of it. The price of putting a reef back is not measured in shell. It is measured in acres.

So here is the floor, and I am building it from acres.

In the lower Bay, the best record we have of how much reef was lost is for the James River, which I am naming as the proxy for the Hampton bars rather than hiding it. The James is the documented case nearest the water I am writing about, and I am using it as the stand-in for the broader lower-Bay loss because it is the figure I can defend. The high-quality oyster rock of the James River fell from about 7,047 acres to about 4,309 acres between 1910 and 1981, a loss of roughly 2,738 acres, a 38.9 percent decline in the physical extent of the reef. That is the area I am going to make whole on paper.

Restoring an acre of reef is a thing people actually do now, and it has a price. The cheapest realized number, what it actually cost at the Great Wicomico project, runs about $7,300 an acre. A forward-engineering estimate runs higher, about $13,500 an acre. Take the 2,738 lost acres and put them against those two figures, and you get a restoration cost of roughly $20 to $37 million.

That is the floor. Twenty to thirty-seven million dollars. I am calling it the floor and I mean it as the deliberately smallest defensible figure, the number I have built to survive any honest attack, the conservative end of the conservative estimate.

There is a robust center to the estimate that runs higher, and I will name it so you know I am not hiding it, but I am not going to lead with it. The bay-wide average cost of completed restoration programs runs closer to $77,000 an acre, and 2,738 acres at that rate comes to about $211 million. That is the robust center of the estimate. It is not the floor, and I am not leading with it, because the floor is the part nobody can take from me.

Now set the floor next to what the reef was worth while it stood, and hold three numbers in your hand at once.

The watermen who brought the oysters up were paid the dockside price, which at the Maryland average runs about $35 a bushel, and across a strong year of the historic harvest that comes to something like $7 million. Call that what it was: a labor fee for shipping the wealth somewhere else. Because the same oysters, sold by the plate in the restaurants of the cities, are worth on the order of $120 million a year, a couple hundred oysters to a bushel and a few dollars to an oyster, realized at tables hundreds of miles from the water. I am fencing that figure deliberately. It is a single peak year at today's prices. I am not multiplying it across a century, I am not claiming Darling-era table prices, and I am not building any total on it, because the moment you stack those multiplications a critic is right to knock them down. One year. Today's prices. Hold the three numbers together: about $7 million to the men who pulled it up, about $120 million realized at the tables it was carried to, and a restoration floor of $20 to $37 million for the reef the whole thing was taken from. The value went up and out. The cost stayed and settled down.

This is the move, and I want to be honest about whose move it is. Mariana Mazzucato, in *The Value of Everything*, drew the hard line between value created and value captured, between the making of worth and the mere taking of it. She named that difference. What I am doing here is adding the accounting to it, giving the difference a number on this one reef. And David Harvey gave a name to the mechanism underneath, accumulation by dispossession, the process by which value is pulled out and the cost is dispossessed onto the commons and the people who live on it. He diagnosed that mechanism. What I am doing is measuring its magnitude, putting the dockside fee and the table value and the restoration bill in a row so the size of the dispossession is a figure and not a feeling. I am not claiming to have discovered the mechanism. Mazzucato named the extraction and Harvey diagnosed the dispossession. The book's whole contribution is the column of numbers, the accounting added to the diagnosis, the magnitude given to the mechanism others described.

And then comes the fact that makes the case almost impossible to argue with, which is that the reef is already being rebuilt, on the public dime, right now.

In Maryland the state has put more than $92 million into building back five oyster sanctuaries covering about 1,300 acres, a number the governor's office posted itself. Across the whole Bay the public restoration bill, itemized, comes to about $115 million: roughly $93.52 million in Maryland and roughly $21.77 million in Virginia, funded by the federal government and the Army Corps and the state natural-resource agencies. There is a rounder figure of about $108 million that sometimes gets quoted at the program level, and I will mention it only to say that it corroborates the magnitude and nothing more. The itemized $115 million is the figure that matters. And the oyster castles the Navy crew is stacking at Hampton Roads, built from spat raised through the Chesapeake Bay Program, I am not going to put a dollar figure on at all, because I do not have one I can defend and I am not going to invent a number to make a point. I will tell you the part I can defend, which is who paid and who did not. The public paid. The federal government, the state, the Army Corps, the Navy. And the oyster industry, the successor to the operation that shipped the meat north and stacked the shell four stories high, the industry's line in this entire column is zero. Every funder is public. The finding is not that the bill is enormous, though it is real. The finding is that the bill has been mis-assigned.

You have read this paragraph before, in the last chapter, written dry instead of wet. In McDowell County the coal came up, the value dispersed across a continent, and when the bill for the cleanup came due the company that captured the value was gone or bankrupt and the public paid for the reclamation. Here the oysters came up, the value dispersed to the cities, and the public is paying to rebuild the reef. Same severance. Same defendant, still not in the room, because the defendant in both places is the same instrument that does not exist. I am not going to re-derive McDowell County. I am only going to point out that it is the same sentence, written once in coal and once in water.

So understand what the Restitution Bond actually does, because it is more modest than the size of these numbers makes it sound. It does not invent a new bill. The bill already exists. It is already being paid, right now, in Maryland and Virginia, out of public money. All the Restitution Bond does is name who should have been holding that bill in the first place, and move the cost back onto the value that was extracted, and direct the money where it belongs, which is into the reef and not into anyone's pocket. It does not write a check to a waterman. It funds the commons. It pays to rebuild the thing, not to compensate a person, because the thing is what was drawn down and the thing is what can be made whole.

And it closes on the floor, on purpose.

Twenty to thirty-seven million dollars. That is the floor. The real figure is higher, and I know it is higher, because I left the lost fishery off the books, and the menhaden off the books, and the livelihoods and the foregone harvests and the thinned-out community off the books, every one of them, deliberately, because each one of them is an argument I do not need and the floor is not. I left all of it off the page on purpose. The floor is the part nobody can take away from me. It is the smallest true thing I can say, and it is enough.

What was done for the oil could be done for the oyster, and a country once did exactly that with the wealth under its own water, which is where this book goes next.
