# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*

## Chapter 6: Three Ways of Counting

---

In 1960 a ton of bituminous coal at the mouth of a West Virginia mine sold for $4.71. The number is not folklore. It sits in a federal table, the Energy Information Administration's Total Energy series, Table 7.9, the long ledger of what American coal fetched year by year. Just under five dollars. McDowell County was near its peak then, close to 100,000 people living on coal money. By the 2020 census the county held fewer than 20,000. United States Steel, the county's great employer, drew down and was gone across the 1980s. A man born in McDowell today can expect 63.5 years of life against a national figure of 76.5, by the county-level estimates Dwyer-Lindgren and colleagues published in *JAMA Internal Medicine* in 2017, working from data centered around 2013. Thirteen years, in the county that loaded the trains.

Chapter 2 stood in that county and followed the costs that stayed behind when the value left. Chapter 3 watched the same arithmetic work the Chesapeake, where the waterman is paid for the crab and not for the Bay. Chapter 4 went to Norway, the one place that built an instrument against it. Chapter 5 named the two instruments an honest system would carry, a bond posted forward and a restitution paid backward, and showed how rarely either exists.

Case by case, the gap was visible. What case-by-case work cannot answer is whether the cases are exceptions. One county is an anecdote. A shelf of cases is a pattern. A gap that can be computed by independent methods, built on different foundations, with the direction never once flipping, is a structural property of the pricing itself. That is the claim now on the table, and it has to be earned in numbers.

Here is the claim in full, stated once. The honest cost of the extractions this book has followed exceeds what the market paid for them by an order of magnitude and more. The gap can be computed three independent ways: by bottom-up damage accounting, by a real-options model of the kind oil companies already run, and by the framework's own residual commons valuation. The approaches agree in direction and overlap in magnitude, case after case. And the third way of counting, read correctly, shows something the first two cannot: what has actually been paid so far, and by whom.

What the computation cannot settle should be said just as plainly. The largest single term is carbon, and the official price of carbon has swung by two orders of magnitude with the occupant of the White House. The substitutability schedules that drive part of the apparatus carry honest uncertainty. The computation structures that uncertainty; it does not remove it. A reader who wants certainty will not find it here. A reader who wants to know whether the uncertainty is large enough to change the answer will find that it is not, and will find the work shown.

---

The miner who loaded the 1960 ton was paid for the coal. The dust settled its own account later, on an X-ray. Begin there, with one ton, and price what the $4.71 never carried. Enumerate, price, add. This is the first approach: bottom-up damage accounting, the oldest and plainest of the three.

Start with the lungs. The federal black-lung compensation system paid out roughly $44 billion through 2009, the last year for which the Government Accountability Office and the Congressional Research Service compiled a cumulative figure. The date travels with the number on purpose: no authoritative cumulative exists past 2009, the program reports annual disbursements thereafter, and so the figure is a conservative floor for a program that has run since 1969. The allocation is straightforward and worth seeing once. Divide the cumulative payments across the national tonnage mined in the compensable era and the program alone contributes a dollar or two per ton; the per-ton figure looks small precisely because the tonnage was so enormous, which is the arithmetic of a cost diffused until nobody can see it. Add what the program never reached: the costs Medicare and Medicaid absorbed when claims were denied or never filed, and the downwind cardiac and respiratory burden that hospital admissions data carries for communities near mining and coal combustion. The health head comes to $2-4 per ton. One more figure belongs here because it is routinely confused with the first. The fund that pays the miners is itself $5.1 billion in debt as of September 2024, by Department of Labor and GAO accounting. The debt is not the cumulative paid; it is a measure of how the program has been financed, a record of excise rates set too low and borrowing rolled forward, and the two numbers should never be blurred. Chapter 2 carries the Trust Fund's mechanics, including who has been servicing that debt.

Then the water and the ground. Acid mine drainage that runs orange for generations. Tailings impoundments whose liners fail on schedules longer than the companies that built them. Headwater streams buried under valley fill. The reclamation bonds that were supposed to pay for repair are short by $3.7-6 billion across Appalachia, a derivation Chapter 2 owns. Allocated per ton, the environmental head runs $1-3.

Then the community, and here the accounting has to be careful about what it is pricing. Not the jobs; wages were paid, and counting them as a cost would double-count the bargain. What gets priced is what remained after the bargain closed: the outmigration of four out of five residents, the schools consolidated and closed, the costs of a drug epidemic that moved into the vacancy, and the 13-year life-expectancy gap, male 63.5 against 76.5. These are legacy effects, harms that persist and compound after the harming activity stops, and there is a methodology for pricing them with a lineage worth naming. William Darity Jr. and his collaborators built it for the legacy effects of slavery and Jim Crow, anchored on the 6-to-7-year Black-white longevity gap that the national vital statistics carried across the historical record. The method converts a persistent mortality differential into a priced harm by the same life-valuation conventions federal agencies already use, which is what makes it auditable rather than rhetorical. McDowell's 13-year gap is the coal-calibrated analogue, computed the same way and kept strictly separate from the racial gap it borrows the method from; the two gaps measure different histories and neither stands in for the other. Priced on that method, the community head runs $5-15 per ton.

Add the three heads and the non-carbon floor comes to $8-22 per ton. Set that against the price. It is roughly twice to nearly 5 times the $4.71 the ton sold for in 1960, and a visible slice of the $40-140 a ton fetches at contemporary mine mouths, depending on region and grade, in EIA and USGS price data. Before a single molecule of carbon enters the ledger, the unpriced costs already rival the price.

---

Combustion is addition. The carbon in a ton of coal leaves the ground weighing what it weighs and comes out of the stack bound to oxygen, at more than twice the starting mass. For an average American bituminous ton, the figure is 2.32 metric tons of carbon dioxide per short ton burned. McDowell's coal runs hotter than average: the Pocahontas seam carries about 28 million Btu per short ton in USGS and EIA seam data, and the EPA's AP-42 compilation (section 1.1) puts bituminous combustion at 93.28 kilograms of CO2 per million Btu. Multiply, and the McDowell-specific factor is 2.61 metric tons of CO2 per short ton, a property of the seam's heat content, not an anthracite assumption. Both bases matter and both will be carried: the national 2.32 as the conservative default, the seam-specific 2.61 where Chapter 8 and the Technical Appendix price the McDowell ton itself.

Why carry two emission bases instead of choosing one? Because each is honest about a different thing. The national 2.32 keeps the arithmetic conservative and comparable across the country's coal; the seam-specific 2.61 prices the actual ton McDowell shipped, and pretending McDowell mined average coal would understate the very county the book is about. The calibration between them is documented where Chapter 8 does its walking, and a reader who meets a McDowell figure built on 2.61 should know it was a measurement choice made in the open, not a thumb on the scale.

Each of those tons of CO2 has a price. The EPA's 2023 central estimate of the social cost of carbon is $190 per metric ton, an agency figure drawing on the work Rennert and colleagues published in *Nature* in 2022, whose own central estimate was about $185. The attribution matters: $190 belongs to the EPA's report, not to the paper alone.

The multiplication is short. On the national basis, 2.32 tons of CO2 times $190 is a carbon term of $441 per ton of coal. On the McDowell basis, 2.61 times $190 is $496. Keep the label fixed to that second number, because it is easy to misread: $496 is the carbon term only, the foreclosure component of one ton's honest cost, never the total. The total, across all 8 components the Technical Appendix prices for the McDowell ton (§11.6), is $510. The narrower 4-component figure, the carbon term plus the three non-carbon heads, runs $504-518, which is the strictly Pigouvian floor. On the national-bituminous basis the same arithmetic gives $449-463, and that is labeled arithmetic, not a McDowell figure.

Two things follow from the addition. First, carbon dominates: the $496 carbon term is an order of magnitude larger than the $8-22 of all other heads combined, which is why the politics of the carbon number will need a section of its own. Second, the capture fraction: a ton that cost $510 sold for $4.71. The price carried about 0.9 percent of the honest cost. A little under a penny on the dollar.

---

On the night of April 20, 2010, the Macondo well blew out under the Deepwater Horizon, killed 11 men, and opened the worst marine oil spill in American history. Run the same bottom-up accounting there. The oil itself was worth about $4 billion: roughly 50 million barrels at the roughly $80 a barrel crude fetched in 2010, a $3.2-4.8 billion band. That figure is a derivation of this framework from EIA price data, labeled as such, because no audited revenue number exists for a reservoir that was mostly never produced. Against it stands BP's own final accounting: an all-in pre-tax charge of $61.6 billion, the company's June 2016 estimate, risen past $65 billion by 2018, with ongoing Gulf ecosystem and fisheries externalities beyond BP's charges running another $8-12 billion through 2025. The gap is a factor of roughly 15-16.

Libby, Montana, is smaller and worse. W.R. Grace's vermiculite mine there laced the town with asbestos-contaminated dust; company documents show Grace knew of the contamination from 1963 and concealed it for 27 years. Naik and colleagues documented 694 deaths from asbestos-related disease through 2011, and the dying is not finished. Through the entire operating period the accountability payment was effectively nothing: no bond that priced the dust, no settlement until the concealment broke, every dollar of the eventual payments arriving after the harm was already in the town's lungs. The mine's lifetime revenue is usually put near $100 million, an industry estimate no primary source confirms, so the qualifier travels with it. The documented cash already spent or committed runs about $2.6 billion: roughly $1.35 billion in EPA Superfund commitments and settlements, plus about $1.25 billion in accrued health monitoring and care from 2000 to 2025 at more than $50 million a year. That is a 26-to-1 ratio against the revenue, and at least 13-to-1 even if the true revenue were twice the estimate. Price the deaths the way federal agencies price mortality, at the EPA's value of a statistical life of $7.4-11.0 million, and the mortality-inclusive total reaches $6.5-10.2 billion, a damage-function gap of roughly 65-100 times revenue. (The range moves with the vintage of the life-value used: 65-77x on 2006 dollars, 78-102x updated. The direction does not move.)

And then the case this chapter prices in full, because no other chapter does. Just after midnight on March 24, 1989, the tanker Exxon Valdez ran onto Bligh Reef in Prince William Sound and spilled some 11 million gallons of Alaska crude, by the Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Trustee Council's accounting. The cargo that went into the water was worth about $5.5 million as product. Hold onto the qualifier, because the ratio is useless without it: $5.5 million is the product value of the spilled oil, not the lifetime revenue of an extraction. Valdez is one event, not a county. What the event cost is on the public record. Exxon's cleanup ran $2.2 billion. The 1991 settlement with the United States and the State of Alaska added $900 million in civil recoveries and $25 million in criminal fines. Private claims took roughly $507 million more, and the punitive award, after the Supreme Court reduced it in 2008, paid out another $507 million. The Trustee Council's restoration science kept finding damage after the checks cleared; decades later, its status reports still list injured resources as not recovered. Sum the cleanup, the litigation, and the ecological damage the public record supports and the event's costs sit at the $7-10 billion scale, against $5.5 million of product. The bottom-up ratio is roughly 1,200-1,900 to 1.

Those ratios need a name and a discipline, and both arrive here. The name: the Intergenerational Pricing Gap, the IPG, true cost divided by market price. The discipline: every IPG quoted in this book names its lens, meaning which approach produced the numerator, and its price basis, meaning which price and which era sit in the denominator. The reason is not pedantry. Divide the McDowell ton's honest cost by the 1960 price and the multiple lands above 100; divide the same cost by a contemporary mine-mouth price and it lands an order of magnitude lower; both divisions are correct, and a multiple quoted without its basis lets a careless reader, or a motivated one, swap denominators mid-argument. The three gaps just computed are all damage-function-lens figures, and even among themselves they divide by different things: a 1960 mine-mouth price, a derived reservoir revenue, the product value of a spilled cargo. A single bare multiple, quoted across mixed lenses and mixed price eras, misleads, and the Technical Appendix (§11.11) decomposes exactly how. One-event disasters carry a second caution: their totals are bounded by what has been counted, the counting is still running, and a bounded numerator means the true ratio sits above the printed one, not below it.

---

A bottom-up inventory has a structural modesty that should be named before leaning on it. It prices what someone thought to count. Walk a warehouse at night with a flashlight and you can list, with perfect accuracy, everything the beam crosses. The list is true. The warehouse is bigger than the beam.

The largest item the beam has found is carbon, and the carbon number has been political weather. The same ton of CO2 was priced at about $42 by the Obama administration's interagency process under Executive Order 12866, at $1-7 by the first Trump administration, which counted only domestic damages at high discount rates, at about $51 by the Biden administration's interim figure, and at $190 by the EPA's 2023 central estimate. The molecule did not change. The fight over its price is a proxy war over how much cost severance the polity is prepared to tolerate, conducted in the language of discount rates.

What survives the weather is the direction. At $1-7 a ton, the McDowell carbon term shrinks to a few dollars and the gap narrows; it does not close, because the $8-22 non-carbon floor stands on its own data and any positive carbon price adds to it. The qualitative finding is robust for any social cost of carbon above zero. And zero is not a measurement. The zero-externality default is itself an assumption about damages, the one assumption in the whole argument that nobody defends out loud.

The honest posture toward all of this is the forecaster's. A weather model does not promise the storm's track; it bounds where the storm can go and attaches probabilities to the corridor. The apparatus in this chapter does the same with cost. The corridor has been narrowing, and Rennert and colleagues' 2022 work, which the current federal estimate draws on, tightened the largest structural pieces. That sentence of comfort is left at one sentence, because the history of resource forecasting punishes anyone who leans on it harder.

---

On a lava plain in Iceland, Climeworks runs the first commercial plants that pull carbon dioxide directly out of the air with banks of fans and a filter chemistry, and the price of doing it is the other half of the carbon question. The social cost of carbon prices what a ton does once it is in the air: the damage side. Direct air capture prices what it costs to take the ton back out: the replacement side. They answer different questions, both belong in honest carbon accounting, and neither substitutes for the other.

The replacement price is honestly a moving target, so the honest statement is four horizons, not one number. Climeworks' first-of-a-kind Orca plant ran above $1,000 per ton; its larger Mammoth plant came online in 2024, and the company's third-generation trajectory, disclosed at the Zurich Carbon Removal Summit in June 2024, points toward $400-600 per ton of net removal by 2030, with a $250-350 capture-only target. Carbon Engineering's Stratos plant in Texas, commissioning now and ramping through mid-2026, is projected at $300-600. The IEA's *Direct Air Capture 2022* study puts the band at $230-600. The IPCC's Sixth Assessment (Working Group III) holds out an optimistic $100-300 by mid-century. Anyone who quotes one number has chosen a horizon; the choice should be visible.

Keep the two sides of the carbon ledger on separate lines. The damage side belongs to the first approach, where it has just done its work. The replacement side belongs to the third approach's first method, where it is about to do different work: a damage price says what the ton costs the world, a replacement price says what undoing the ton would cost an engineer, and the gap between the two is itself information about how far the harm outruns the repair. Keeping them separated is part of the framework's reporting discipline, because a reader who catches an analyst swapping damage prices for replacement prices mid-argument is right to stop trusting the analyst.

---

The second approach starts on an oil company's balance sheet, with barrels nobody has drilled. An undeveloped reserve is not valued at today's price times the barrels; it is valued as an option, because the right to extract later, when prices or technology or information have moved, is worth real money, and exercising it early destroys that value. The valuation machinery for this is standard finance, built out in Dixit and Pindyck's 1994 treatment of investment under uncertainty, and the majors run it as a matter of course. Waiting has value because information arrives.

The standard model counts only the operator's information: prices, costs, geology. What it omits is the social side of the same logic, and the omission has three parts. Externality science accumulates; the social cost of carbon did not exist as a number in 1960, black-lung epidemiology was a generation away from forcing federal legislation, and the option to wait was, among other things, the option to learn what the extraction would actually cost. Substitutes mature; a unit extracted the year before its substitute arrives is a unit wasted at the margin, and the substitution curves for coal in electricity were always going to bend, because the underlying technologies were always going to improve. Criticality information accumulates; a society learns, slowly and expensively, which resources its future actually needs and which it only thought it needed. Restore those three terms and the model produces a social option value of leaving the unit in the ground, and a clean decision rule with it: when the social option value exceeds today's net extraction revenue, the extraction is socially premature. It destroys value for the society even while it makes money for the operator, and the destruction is invisible at the time because the information that would have revealed it is precisely what the premature exercise forecloses.

Run it on the 1960 McDowell ton. The price was $4.71. The model, with the social terms restored, puts the social option value of that ton in the tens to hundreds of dollars; the Technical Appendix's three-model table (§9.5) carries the full computation, and the option-lens gap for McDowell comes out at 45-89 times the price. Could anyone have computed that in 1960? A fair question, and the answer the record supports is narrow: nobody did, and the price carried none of it. A failure of pricing, whatever else it may also have been.

What recommends this approach is who can read it. The vocabulary is already on the books. A board that prices the option to delay a development for shareholder reasons can read the same computation run with society's terms in it; nothing about the math is foreign, only the scope of whose information counts. And the direction holds across cases: 45-89x for McDowell, 12-21x for Deepwater, 57-93x for Libby, all from the same appendix table. Different blind spots than the damage inventory, same sign, overlapping size.

---

The dust files its claim years after the last shift; the acid keeps running long after the portal is sealed. The third approach begins from that observation and builds the price the other two approximate. It is the framework's own, and it goes by the name residual commons value, RCV.

In plain English first. When a unit of a commons is consumed, two costs go unpriced, and they are different in kind, which is why the formula carries them as separate terms. The first is foreclosure: whatever future access the consumption removes, weighted by how substitutable the unit is. A ton of coal burned in 1960 is a ton no future generation can burn, or leave banked against a need nobody in 1960 could foresee; what that foreclosure costs depends entirely on whether the future has something else that does the job. Substitutability is an empirical variable, not a slogan. For coal in electricity it is high and rising; wind, solar, and storage do the same job at falling cost. For rare-earth elements it is low. For helium in cryogenics, and for water in fossil aquifers that recharge on geologic time, it is close to zero; nothing else does what they do. The better the substitutes, the smaller the foreclosure term, and for some commons the term is large and does not shrink. The second unpriced cost is the externality tail, which does not care about substitutability at all: black lung surfacing decades after the seam closed, acid drainage running for generations, carbon working the atmosphere for centuries. A perfect substitute for coal would not shorten the tail of the coal already burned. The tail is named in the statistician's sense, a distribution with no end date the data can certify, which is why the bond against it must carry forward indefinitely and why its lineage sits beside Weitzman's 2009 work on fat-tailed climate damage; the Technical Appendix (§3) carries the formal definition and that one sentence is the whole defense the name gets here.

The formula is a summary of the argument, not a replacement for it. The residual commons value of a unit is the discounted sum, over future time, of the unsubstitutable share of the foreclosed value plus the externality flow:

RCV = the integral over time of D(t) x ( [1 - S(t)] x U(t) + E(t) )

where S is the substitutability schedule, U the foreclosed commons value, E the externality flow, and D the discount factor, taken at Weitzman's declining rates for long horizons. Two definitions fall out and the book uses both. The severed cost is what was never paid: CS = RCV - B, where B is the bond actually posted, in whatever form a society posted it. And the Intergenerational Pricing Gap already defined is RCV divided by market price. The proofs and the parameterizations live in the Technical Appendix (§3); the argument stands without them.

One scope statement, and it is load-bearing. The price RCV computes is positive exactly when consumption forecloses future access faster than the commons can restore itself. For non-renewables that is always, because restoration is zero. For renewables it is true only when harvest outruns regeneration; the Chesapeake oyster crossed that line, a well-managed fishery does not, and within the regeneration rate the correct price is zero. For commons that are not resources at all, the test is whether degradation outruns recovery. The floor under the framework is scarcity dynamics, not physical finiteness. The fossil-fuel cases this book computes all come out strictly positive, and that is a property of the cases, not a definition smuggled in at the start.

---

Every trading day, the markets run a vast distributed computation on the future profitability of extraction: discounted cash flows, aggregated and corrected through equity and debt prices, updated hourly. There is no counterpart computation for future harms. No exchange aggregates estimates of what the tail will cost; no price corrects when the estimate is wrong. The machinery inside the third approach exists to stand in for that missing half, and it has its own internal structure, which is the most important structure in this chapter. The naming is nested on purpose: Approaches 1, 2, and 3 are the outer triangulation; Methods 1, 2, and 3 are the inner machinery of Approach 3 alone.

Method 1 prices replacement. Suppose the commons function had to be supplied by engineering: what would it cost? For the carbon dimension of the McDowell ton, the engineering is direct air capture, and the answer spans the DAC horizons just walked: $290-875 per ton if the optimistic 2050 cost curves arrive, $812-1,658 on the at-scale trajectory, $1,595-2,702 on the conservative horizon, with the at-scale center near $1,235 (Technical Appendix §11.6). Method 1 is the floor, built from engineering data in the lineage of Costanza's ecosystem-services accounting, and it systematically underprices whatever cannot be rebuilt: no capture plant restores a foreclosed hydrocarbon, and none restores a town. The formal treatment is at Technical Appendix §3.3.

Method 3 prices option-bearing scarcity. It is the quasi-option value of preservation that Arrow and Fisher, and independently Henry, formalized in 1974: the value of keeping a decision reversible while information arrives. The computation starts from the resource's own market value and carries it up by two weights, one for scarcity, one for irreversibility. For the McDowell ton it yields $340-3,670, geometric center near $1,115; for a Norwegian barrel of oil equivalent, $96-610, central estimate $281 (Technical Appendix §11.6 and §11.5). The width of those ranges is parameter uncertainty honestly carried, not imprecision; the appendix publishes every sensitivity. The irreversibility weight is the heavier of the two, and it is weighted that way because the empirical record says it must be: the IPCC's Sixth Assessment Summary for Policymakers (B.5) on losses already locked in, and Bernhardt and Palmer's 2011 accounting of Appalachian headwater streams that valley fills buried for good. The formal treatment is at Technical Appendix §3.5.

Methods 1 and 3 are the two estimators of RCV, and they are genuinely independent: one is built from engineering cost curves, the other from market prices carried through scarcity and irreversibility weights. Where they converge from those separate foundations, the estimate is well supported. For McDowell they overlap across roughly $340-2,700 with centers near $1,000-1,500; for Norway the bands overlap around the $281 center. Where the estimators diverge, the divergence is data: it says which assumption is doing the work, and the reporting discipline requires saying so. RCV is the convergence of Method 1 and Method 3. Nothing else in the apparatus estimates it.

Method 2 does a different job, which is why it is taken out of numerical order. Method 2 is revealed preference, the Samuelson-Hicks instrument, and it reads not what the commons was worth but what society actually paid: the realized bond, the B in CS = RCV - B. The point deserves a slow sentence, because the recast it forces is the most consequential piece of bookkeeping in the framework. The same observable cannot serve as an estimate of the value and as the payment subtracted from it; treating revealed payments as a third estimate of RCV would be entering a figure on one side of a ledger because it appeared on the other, and the subtraction CS = RCV - B would quietly subtract a number from itself. Revealed preference measures behavior, and behavior reveals what was paid. What was paid and what was owed are the two quantities this whole chapter exists to hold apart.

Read correctly, the Method 2 reading delivers two findings. The first is a lower bound: a rational polity does not internalize more than it values, so the residual commons value is at least what was actually paid, and every realized bond the apparatus can find becomes a free consistency check on the estimators. Norway anchors the forward reading. The Government Pension Fund Global stood at 21,268 billion kroner at the end of 2025, about $2.0 trillion; at the roughly 75 percent capture rate the appendix documents, that implies about $2.67 trillion of net petroleum wealth, and dividing by the roughly 55 billion barrels of oil equivalent Norway has cumulatively produced, by Norwegian Petroleum Directorate figures, gives a realized bond of about $48 per barrel (Technical Appendix §11.5). The estimators put the barrel's residual value near $281. The bound holds, with room to spare, and a polity that has voluntarily set aside $48 a barrel has told you something no model could: the value is at least that, by the testimony of the one government that actually paid.

The second finding is the one this book did not have before the apparatus was built, and it answers a question the estimators cannot: who paid. For the McDowell ton, the realized bond reads $50-88 per ton paid by the public, counting the Black Lung program's federally financed share, against $8-15 per ton paid by the industry through reclamation bonds, roughly $5-8 billion of them, and private medical settlements (Technical Appendix §11.6, which documents the Trust Fund's general-fund share as the worked instance). An accountability instrument for Appalachian coal exists. It operates at scale. It is charged to the wrong party. That is the mis-assignment finding, and it generalizes: wherever the apparatus has been able to read a realized bond, the reading shows the bill already being paid, partially, quietly, and mostly by the people the price was supposed to protect. The policy debate has spent decades arguing about whether to create accountability instruments. The reading says the instruments exist and the argument is actually about the addressee.

The methodology runs backward as well as forward; only the calibration changes. Backward, Method 1 specializes to remediation cost, what repair actually requires, which is why the Superfund commitments at Libby and the restoration spending in the Gulf slot directly into the backward ledger. Method 2 reads the realized restitution, what was actually paid for harms already done, and the historical roster of such payments is the empirical base: German reparations to Holocaust survivors, the Civil Liberties Act of 1988 that paid redress to interned Japanese Americans, the Black Lung Trust Fund's payouts, the reparations program attached to South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission, and the reparations-economics methodology Darity and Mullen set out in 2020. The roster is short, which is itself a finding; across the full history of extraction and atrocity, the list of restitutions actually paid fits in a sentence. And the backward reading carries the same diagnostic the forward one does: it matters who posted the payment. Where repair is publicly funded while the extractive beneficiary contributes nothing, the bond has been mis-assigned a second time, once when the harm was done and once when the public paid to mend it, and the reading says so in public numbers. Method 3 backward becomes the option value extinguished at the moment of extraction; the appendix exposes, without settling, the fork between valuing that loss as it looked then and as it looks now (Technical Appendix §5.5).

The backward direction also forces a discipline about empty columns, and the appendix states it as three distinct conditions a gate-admitted cost can be in. A slot can be Zero, because under genuine abundance the cost truly is nothing. It can be Filled, with a value entered and the derivation shown. Or it can be Open: real, admitted by every gate, and deliberately left empty. Open is not Zero. The coercion column of America's backward ledger, the one reparations scholarship addresses, is Open in this book, and I want to say why in the first person, because the reason is mine. I am not part of the community to whom that debt is owed, and I am not going to put a number in that column. The framework offers the ledger; I decline to fill that line of it. The field that has done the work has its own figure, $14 trillion in Darity and Mullen's accounting, in the second edition of *From Here to Equality* and their 2022 *Journal of Economic Perspectives* treatment, and that figure is the field's, never this framework's. Chapter 5 engages their work at the depth it deserves.

One reporting rule gathers all of this, and it travels with every estimate the framework publishes: both estimators individually, the convergence range, the realized-bond reading with who paid it, and the identified sources of divergence. No single-method claim of severed cost has standing in this book.

---

Frank Ramsey, who died in 1930 at 26, wrote in 1928 that discounting later enjoyments against earlier ones is "a practice which is ethically indefensible." The discipline he helped found has spent a century not resolving the question. Stern's review priced the future at a near-zero rate and was attacked for sentimentality; Nordhaus used market-anchored rates and was attacked for callousness; Weitzman proposed rates that decline with horizon, which this framework adopts for its discounting; the philosophers, by and large, side with Ramsey. The impasse is real, a moral parameter wearing the costume of a number.

The substitutability schedule is the framework's way of stepping around the impasse rather than through it. Instead of asking how much the future's welfare is worth, an ethical question with no laboratory, it asks how quickly alternatives can be developed, an empirical question with several: cost-curve histories, deployment data, patent records, the learning rates that energy economists have measured for half a century. The schedule has four natural properties. It starts near zero at the moment of extraction, because a substitute that does not exist yet substitutes for nothing, and most of the harm in the historical cases happened in exactly that early window. It rises monotonically, because technology does not unlearn what it has learned. It approaches but never exceeds 1, because no substitute is more than a substitute; the last increment of any commons function resists replacement, which is why Method 1 is a floor and not a ceiling. And it moves with investment, because substitution timelines are bought, not awaited, which converts the schedule from a forecast into a policy lever.

A fair critic will say the hard problem has been moved, not solved, and the critic is right. It has been moved to a better neighborhood. A century of argument has not narrowed the discount rate's error bars by much, because the disagreement is about values. Substitution timelines are a research program, and the error bars of research programs shrink when they are funded. The Technical Appendix (§13) develops the schedule in full.

A surveyor who cannot walk to a point measures it from 3 stations and trusts the answer where the sight lines cross. Campbell and Fiske made the same move a formal method in 1959: when no single instrument can be trusted alone, run instruments with different failure modes and credit what they agree on. That is the standard scientific response to a quantity nobody can measure directly, and it is the chapter's response too, twice over: once across the three approaches, once inside the third.

Across the approaches first, and the reason it takes all three is that each one's weakness is another one's strength. Bottom-up damage accounting sees only what the beam has crossed, but what it sees is documented to the dollar. The real-options model imports the market's own horizons and the market's own discount habits, but it speaks in a vocabulary extraction executives already trust, and its blind spot, the uncounted tail, is exactly what the damage inventory has counted. The residual-commons apparatus depends on parameter choices it can only publish, not escape, but it reaches the foreclosure and tail costs the other two structurally cannot, and its parameters are disciplined by having to land where the other two land. Three blind spots, three data foundations: counted damages, exercised options, residual value. Run all three on the cases the appendix can compute (§9.5) and the gaps come out as follows. McDowell: 107-110x by the damage lens, 45-89x by the options lens, 61-115x by the RCV-integral lens. Deepwater: 15-16x, 12-21x, 17-18x. Libby: 65-100x, 57-93x, 72-111x. The ranges overlap within each case. The magnitudes differ across cases by two orders, as they should, because the cases differ; an apparatus that produced the same multiple for a contained spill and a century of strip mining would be measuring its own assumptions. And the direction never flips. Not once, in any case, under any lens.

Two ways of counting agree on the size of the debt. The third shows who has been making the payments, and the answer, across every row of the table, is mostly the wrong party or nobody.

Three objections deserve their own paragraphs, because each is the kind a careful reader should make.

The first: you chose the parameters, so you chose the answer. The reply is the convergence itself, and it is worth being concrete about why convergence answers it. Method 1's inputs are direct-air-capture cost curves, Superfund engineering estimates, restoration budgets; they come from project filings and agency records, and nobody building them was thinking about this framework. Method 3's inputs are market prices, depletion data, and the irreversibility record; a different literature, different authors, different decades. An analyst who wanted to rig their agreement would have to rig two unrelated bodies of evidence in the same direction by the same amount, and then do it again for the damage inventory and the options model, which were built by different schools for different purposes and land in the same ranges anyway. Parameter mischief produces divergence, and divergence is exactly what the reporting rule forces into print. The choice of parameters moves the centers; it has not been able to move the sign, and the sign is the claim.

The second: can the framework be wrong? Yes, checkably. The severed cost CS = RCV - B can come out at or below zero, and the framework names the two classes of cases where it should: a sustainably harvested renewable under full accountability, where the price within the regeneration rate is zero, and an abundant non-renewable under stringent bonding, where the posted bond covers the residual value. Both classes are observable. Find a fishery managed inside its regeneration rate with full cost accountability and the framework predicts a severed cost of zero; measure, and if the measurement comes back large, the framework is wrong. Find an extraction where the framework predicts a large gap, measure the realized payments, and if they cover the residual value, the framework is wrong again. A theory that tells you where the wall is can be tested by flying at the wall. The Commons Inversion Test that screens cases is a thought experiment, and thought experiments are not falsifiable; the computed estimates that follow it are, one layer up, where falsifiability belongs in any applied science. And the recast inner structure strengthens the test rather than weakening it: the B side of the equation is now directly observed in settlements, trust funds, and sovereign funds, public numbers no model can massage, which converts half of every severed-cost claim from estimate to record.

The third: could anyone reproduce this? The answer is the reporting rule. Both estimators, the overlap, the bond reading, the divergence sources, published every time. Ranges are how applied cost accounting actually works; every actuarial table and every engineering estimate carries them. The failure mode in this field has never been the disclosed range. It is the undisclosed sensitivity, the single confident number whose load-bearing assumption appears nowhere on the page.

At the end of 2025 the fund held 21,268 billion kroner. The figure is the Norges Bank Investment Management number, about $2.0 trillion at the year-end exchange rate, briefly above $2.2 trillion during the year, about $390,000 for every Norwegian citizen. Norway is the best-managed extraction case the modern record offers, which is precisely why the apparatus has to be run on it: a framework that could not tell Norway from Libby would be a sermon, not an instrument.

The mechanics, once, and Chapter 4 carries the full story. The fund was established by statute in 1990 and took its first deposit in 1996. A fiscal rule caps withdrawals at the fund's expected real return, 3 percent under the 2017 revision of the rule first set at 4 percent in 2001. A carbon tax has applied to the shelf since 1991. An ethics council governs holdings and has forced divestments. It is Hartwick's 1977 rule made real: extraction rents converted into productive capital for the generations who will not see the oil.

Now the apparatus. The fund is the realized forward bond, and Method 2 reads it at about $48 per barrel of oil equivalent, by the derivation already shown. The estimators put the barrel's residual commons value near $281, Method 1 at $161-422, Method 3 at $96-610 with its center at $281. The severed cost is therefore about $233 per barrel, roughly $12.8 trillion cumulative across 55 billion barrels. Positive, and small relative to every other case in the table.

Why it is small is the instructive part, and the mechanism is not the obvious one. The intuitive story says Norway captured the rent, and the intuitive story is wrong in a way that matters for anyone trying to copy it. The fund does not buy back the barrel; nothing manufactures subsurface petroleum a second time, and no sum in any account un-burns the carbon. What the fund changes is the irreversibility of the loss. Recall what made Method 3's premium large in the other cases: an extraction that leaves the polity with no capacity to respond is an extraction whose losses are locked, and the option-value machinery prices locked losses brutally. A polity holding $2 trillion in diversified claims on the world economy retains the capacity to restore, to compensate, to adapt, to fund whatever the externality tail eventually demands; the option the extraction would otherwise have extinguished is partly preserved in a different form. In the model's terms, the effective irreversibility falls from nearly total toward something like a half to three quarters, and that collapse pulls Method 3's premium out of the divergent regime down to a modest 2.4-5.1 times market value. The mechanism is irreversibility-reduction. What Norway bought with the fund was not absolution and not a refund; it was the preservation of its own future capacity to make things right, and the model turns out to price that capacity as the most valuable thing a resource state can hold.

The result reads exactly as a working instrument should. The severed cost is positive: the carbon in Norway's exported oil lands on people who hold no claim on the fund, the atmosphere over Bangladesh does not draw a withdrawal under anyone's fiscal rule, and the $233 per barrel is mostly that, an externality the fund's beneficiaries do not bear. The severed cost is small: one country, under real political pressure for three decades, through changes of government in both directions, narrowed the gap by an order of magnitude relative to the unbonded cases, and did it with instruments any resource state could copy. The framework differentiates, and the differentiation is the credential. Norway is the closest thing the world has to honest extraction pricing, and it is still not all the way there, and the apparatus can say both things with numbers, which is more than either Norway's admirers or its critics usually manage.

What counts as a commons? The morning Chapter 3 described, boats staying tied up so a neighbor's nets get rebuilt, is one answer; the atmosphere is another; they do not obviously belong to the same category until the counting starts. The book works with ten, offered as illustration, not legislation.

Habitability: breathable air, drinkable water, a climate band a body can live in. Most of this chapter's arithmetic has been habitability arithmetic; the carbon term is a habitability price, the acid drainage head is a habitability price, and the reason the category leads the list is that it is the one the apparatus can already price end to end, from emission factor to damage function to replacement cost, with named federal sources at every step. The others are earlier in their measurement history, which is a statement about the state of the data, never about the reality of the cost. Spatial: the places themselves, the ground a community stands on and the coherence of what stands there. Temporal: the hours of a life, rest, time not owed to anyone. Institutional: courts that function, schools that teach, administration that answers. Kindred: the web of family and care across generations, the commons McDowell's outmigration drew down. Ecosystem: living systems doing unpriced work, the oyster reef that was also the next reef's foundation. Political: the capacity for self-rule, consumed when an extractive interest buys a statehouse. Cohesion: the trust between neighbors that the net-mending morning runs on. Epistemic: the shared stock of knowledge and the reliability of the record, consumed when a company knows for 27 years and says nothing. Autonomy: the standing to refuse.

Autonomy deserves a moment, because three centuries of political philosophy converge on it from different directions and the framework needs none of them to lose. Pettit reads freedom as non-domination, the condition of not living at another's mercy. Skinner traces the older neo-Roman view, on which a person dependent on another's goodwill is unfree even if never interfered with. Anderson reads equality as a relation between people rather than a quantity of goods, and finds the modern workplace one of its main sites of failure. On any of the three readings, the company town consumed an autonomy commons: where wages, housing, credit, and the doctor all ran through one set of books, the standing to refuse was extracted along with the coal. Chapter 2 walked that ground; Chapter 1 ran the most-favorable-conditions version, the 120-hour week at full pay, and found the temporal and autonomy ledgers debited even when every other column was generous.

The list is illustrative on principle, and there is precedent for lists that illuminate without closing: Ostrom's eight design principles, offered as regularities rather than statute; Sen and Nussbaum's capability lists, revised openly across decades; Rawls's primary goods. Traditions will divide the territory differently. A classical liberal reads commons as the background conditions of free contract; a civic republican, as what a free people must hold together; a socialist, as the social product; a Marxist, as the conditions of production; the traditions born from slavery and colonization, as what was never permitted to be held at all. The framework prices foreclosure within any of these vocabularies, and it does not legislate which one a society must speak. Wherever a tradition locates the shared thing, the question the framework asks is the same: is it being consumed faster than it can restore itself, and who is paying.

---

The deepest objection to everything above comes from philosophy, and it deserves to be stated at full strength. Decisions large enough to change history change who exists. Shift energy policy in 1960 and different people meet, different children are born; the great-grandchild who would stand in court to claim the foreclosed seam exists only because the seam was mined. To whom, then, was the duty owed? This is Parfit's non-identity problem, set out in *Reasons and Persons* in 1984, and no honest framework for intergenerational pricing gets to walk past it.

Parfit's own response is the one the framework adopts. Wrongness need not be person-affecting; outcomes can be impersonally worse, worse for the world even if no identifiable individual can claim to have been made worse off than they would otherwise have been. A depleted future with different inhabitants is worse than a conserved future with different inhabitants, and the comparison between the two futures is meaningful even though no single person appears in both. The residual commons value operates in exactly that register: it prices states of the commons, not injuries to named persons, which is why it can run forward into a future whose inhabitants are not yet determinate, and why the non-identity objection, fatal to a compensation claim, leaves a foreclosure price standing.

The two-instrument architecture Chapter 5 named inherits the distinction cleanly. The forward bond is impersonal, posted against foreclosures whose victims cannot be identified because identity itself is downstream of the decision. The backward restitution is person-affecting, owed for identified harms to identifiable people and their descendants, which is why backward work belongs to reparations economics and its methods while forward work belongs to environmental-bond economics and its methods. Different registers, different mathematics, one ledger. The concession is owed and made: the framework operates within Parfit's vocabulary, it does not solve his problem, and a reader who concludes that no one has solved it is reading the literature correctly.

What is owed, then, on the framework's most conservative lens, the price-independent Method-3 foreclosure premium: 8.5 to 26 times what was paid, centered near 15. The other lenses say more. No lens says less.

---

Parfit grounds who can be wronged; Sen grounds what the wrong consists of. The capability approach reads a person's well-being not as income or utility but as the set of lives they are actually able to lead, and Nussbaum gave the idea its working lists. In that vocabulary, the residual commons value is a measurement of the capability set that extraction forecloses: the breathable air, the standing community, the hours, the option to stay. The McDowell ton priced at $510 is, in Sen's terms, a partial price on the lives the county's children could no longer choose, and the partiality is admitted; capabilities that resist denomination stay outside the number and are named instead. The pairing is deliberate. With Parfit, the framework's standing survives moral philosophy's hardest objection; with Sen, its unit of account means something in welfare economics. An apparatus that can be read in both rooms can be argued with in both rooms, which is what an apparatus is for.

---

Elinor Ostrom spent a career on commons that did not collapse: the huerta irrigators of Valencia dividing water for half a millennium, the alpine pastures of Törbel grazed in common since the Middle Ages. *Governing the Commons*, in 1990, distilled the record into eight design principles, clear boundaries, rules fitted to local conditions, the users themselves making and monitoring the rules, graduated sanctions, cheap conflict resolution, recognized rights to organize, nested layers for larger systems, and put an empirical end to Hardin's 1968 fable, which had predicted ruin for every commons not privatized or nationalized. The record sided with Ostrom. This book contradicts exactly one thinker by name, and Hardin is the one, by her evidence rather than by argument.

But Ostrom's commons share a structure worth naming precisely, once. In a coordination commons, the people who capture the value and the people who bear the costs are the same people, facing each other across seasons in a game that repeats; her eight principles are the engineering of that repetition. In an extraction commons, the capturers and the bearers are different parties, separated by distance, time, or power, and no repeated game disciplines an absentee; the shareholder who profits from the seam will never stand in the checkout line of the county it emptied. The asymmetry is structural, and it is why Ostrom's principles, which assume the parties must keep living with each other, do not transfer to cases where one party has already left. The two kinds are adjacent and complementary, and where both elements are present they get parallel analyses: a fishery can be locally well governed in Ostrom's sense and still sit at the bottom of a watershed whose runoff it cannot refuse, which is the Chesapeake's exact condition. The Commons Inversion Test's two forms, described next, are what make the sorting operational. And the moment stakeholders go heterogeneous, the analysis connects to stratification economics, where Darity and Hamilton have shown that who bears costs is patterned by group position, not distributed by chance; the extraction commons is where that patterning does its quietest work.

---

Not every claimed commons cost belongs in the ledger, and the framework's credibility hangs on saying no correctly. Four gates stand between a proposed cost and the book's arithmetic, and a candidate must pass all four.

Gate 1 is the Commons Inversion Test, which takes Lewis's 1973 counterfactual machinery and runs it with a single premise flipped, reading what the flip does to price; the Technical Appendix (§6) gives the formal statement, and that sentence is the name's whole defense. The test has two forms that run different filters, and the difference is operational, not decorative. The absence form asks: suppose the commons had never existed; what would it cost to supply its function? Run it on the atmosphere's carbon-absorption service and the answer is the direct-air-capture industry, priced earlier; run it on a fashionable amenity and the answer is nothing anyone would pay, which is the gate doing its filtering. A positive answer routes to replacement cost, Method 1's territory, and the same form sorts coordination commons, whose value lives in arrangement rather than stock, toward Ostrom's apparatus instead of this one. The consumption form asks: suppose this unit were not consumed; what value persists or accrues? Its answer routes to opportunity cost, the ground Method 3 prices. Revealed preference was a candidate for this gate and was declined, and the inner recast shows why the declination was right: revealed preference reads what was paid, never what is owed, a blind spot fatal in a gate and exactly correct in the instrument that reads the bond. The instrument was not discarded; it was reassigned to the one job its blind spot fits.

Gate 2 is units consistency. A disability-adjusted life year is a sound unit and a dollar is a sound unit; adding them raw is not arithmetic. Every component arrives in common units with the conversion shown, the way the EPA's value of a statistical life is shown wherever this chapter used it.

Gate 3 is boundedness. Every estimate the framework produces must be bounded above. An infinite price is an unfalsifiable price, and it forecloses the count-not-stop posture the framework needs; where something is beyond pricing, the honest move is the one this book makes elsewhere, recording the refusal rather than printing an infinity.

Gate 4 is independence. Components must not double-count: the collapse of a coal town's civic fabric enters once, under Cohesion or inside the community tier of Habitability's accounting, never both. One harm, one line.

The gates are jointly necessary; a candidate that passes three and fails one stays out. The Technical Appendix carries the gates in full, together with the generalization of the formula they discipline.

Arthur Pigou put the factory chimney into economics in 1920: the smoke that dirties the town's laundry is a cost of production that the producer does not pay, and a corrective tax can make the price tell the truth. A century later his instrument runs the carbon term of this chapter. Ostrom mapped the commons that govern themselves. The framework extends both into ground neither reached: extraction cases where the capturers and the bearers are different parties and the costs arrive across generations.

Three additions do the extending. The first is substitutability weighting, closed by an identity Hotelling deserves a century's credit for making possible: markets already capitalize the scarcity rent of an exhaustible resource, the rent Hotelling priced in 1931, and that rent functions as a bond the price itself posts. Subtract the Hotelling rent from the residual commons value and what remains is the severed cost per unit. The second is integration: one gate-disciplined formula carrying foreclosure and externality tail together, rather than a different ad hoc instrument per cost. The third is the Commons Inversion Test as a discovery method; inherited category lists can only confirm what a tradition already counts, while the inversion generates candidates the analyst was not looking for, which is how the epistemic and autonomy ledgers earned their places.

Run the additions back across the cases and the extension is visible in the numbers. Standard Pigouvian analysis prices McDowell's smoke: $496 of carbon term on the McDowell basis, $441 on the national basis, and a well-built carbon tax would have collected either. Nothing in Pigou argues for excluding the buried headwaters, the foreclosed seam's option value, or the town that emptied; Pigou simply had no method for including them, and a century of welfare economics inherited the silence. The additions are the method. They are what carries the McDowell ton from the $496 carbon term to the $504-518 Pigouvian floor and the $510 honest total; what gives Deepwater's uncounted long tail a line to be entered on instead of a footnote to vanish into; what lets Libby's 27 concealed years register as the consumption of an epistemic commons rather than as mere fraud, priced and gated like every other cost in the book. The name residual commons value compresses the lineage into three words, Hartwick's residual, Ostrom's commons, Mazzucato's value, and the Technical Appendix (§3) records the derivation. One boundary stone, set here deliberately: the apparatus accounts a cost; it does not code a new asset for finance to trade, and Chapter 9 takes up why that distinction has to be guarded.

---

Two tests wait, and the apparatus goes to both. Chapter 7 takes it to a place with no atmosphere to hide in, where cost severance is visible by tomorrow morning, and asks whether the framework is universal or merely terrestrial; if the apparatus only works where the costs are already obvious, it is a description, and the Mars case is where that gets settled. Chapter 8 walks a single McDowell ton through every line of the accounting, end to end, so that a reader who distrusts summaries can audit the whole chain at the scale of one ton. And one rule has been earning its keep throughout this chapter without being named: the Asymmetric Regret Rule, Savage's 1951 minimax-regret criterion specialized to decisions where one direction of regret is the extinction of a commons, regret rather than risk, asymmetric rather than one-sided, deferred to Chapter 9 and stated formally in the Technical Appendix (§8). Its weighting of irreversibility is grounded where Method 3's was: in the empirical record of what does not come back, the IPCC's Summary for Policymakers (B.5) on losses already locked in, Bernhardt and Palmer's buried headwaters. The rule does not replace cost-benefit analysis where cost-benefit analysis works, which is most places. It governs the narrower class of decisions where being wrong once is being wrong forever.

---

Three ways of counting, then.

Two of them price what the commons was worth, from separate foundations, engineering on one side and priced scarcity on the other, and they meet: near $1,100 for the ton that sold for $4.71, near $281 for the barrel that sold for $80. The third reads what has actually been paid, and finds the payment real, small, and posted by the wrong party: $50-88 a ton from the public against $8-15 from the industry that took the coal. Across three approaches, five cases, six decades of price data, and methods built by economists who never met, the direction never flips. The honest cost sits above the price by an order of magnitude and more, and the difference has been paid all along, in lungs and water and emptied towns, by the people nearest the hole.

The gap is not an accident that keeps happening. It is how the extraction economy keeps its books, and now it has a number, a method behind the number, and a name on the unpaid bill.
