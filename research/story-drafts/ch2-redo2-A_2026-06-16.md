# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 2: The Miner

---

In the spring of 1960 a senator drove into the poorest county in one of the richest seams of coal on earth, and what he saw there did not match anything in his briefing books.

He was campaigning in West Virginia, which meant he was driving through McDowell County, and McDowell County had spent the previous half century shipping out the metallurgical coal that built the steel that built the country. The best coking coal in the world came out of that ground. It went into the furnaces at Pittsburgh and Gary and Birmingham, and it came back out as the rails and the bridges and the skyscrapers and the ships, and the men who dug it were proud of it, and they had every right to be. You could stand in McDowell and know, with no exaggeration in it, that the modern American century had been assembled out of what lay under your feet.

And the senator drove past children with the swollen bellies of the malnourished. He saw families living on surplus government commodities handed out in the hollows because there was not enough work and not enough food. He saw men who had armed and supplied a war sitting idle on porches, the strength still in them and nowhere to spend it. He had not expected this. Almost no one outside the place expected it. The wealth went out by the trainload, decade after decade, and the county it came from was hungry.

Later that year, in September, in Canton, Ohio, John F. Kennedy stood up and named what he had seen. He said the situation was an indictment of the country that allowed it, and he was right, and that is the trouble. He had the indictment. He delivered it from the heart. He just had the wrong defendant.

That sentence is going to organize this whole chapter, so let me set it down plainly. He had the wrong defendant. Not the wrong crime. The crime was exactly as he described it: enormous value generated in a place, and the place left destitute. But the thing he was reaching to put in the dock, whatever it was, the operators, the indifference, the system, was not the thing actually responsible, and the reason he could not name the right defendant is that the right defendant did not exist. It still does not. The argument of this book is that we have to build it.

The last chapter ended with a ledger and a set of empty columns. A cost that lands on a life and stays there, that no one can bill back, that appears on no invoice. This chapter fills in the first column. It takes one place and asks a single stubborn question, and it answers that question one entry at a time until the shape of the answer is impossible to mistake.

The question is not what a ton of that coal sold for. The question is what a ton of that coal actually cost.

---

Start with a man named Robert Bailey, because the first column in the ledger is written on a human body, and a body is not a statistic.

Bailey was a coal miner in West Virginia, and by the end of his life he could not breathe. He had black lung, the disease the dust gives you, coal dust packed into the tissue of the lung until the lung can no longer do the one thing a lung is for. He described what it felt like, and the description is five words long. It is almost like drowning. Hold those five words. People who have not had the disease reach for metaphors. Bailey was not reaching for a metaphor. He was telling you, as exactly as he could, what it is to suffocate slowly inside your own chest with air all around you that your lungs can no longer use.

He got a double lung transplant. Two new lungs, somebody else's, to replace the ones the work had filled in. That is the scale of what the dust does, that the repair for it is to take the lungs out and put different ones in. The transplant gave him time. It did not give him the rest of his life back, and it brought its own bill, because the body fights a transplanted organ, and the only way to stop the body from killing the new lungs is to suppress the immune system that would otherwise protect him from everything else, including cancer.

Here is the part that matters for the ledger. The company Bailey had worked for had gone bankrupt. When a coal company goes bankrupt the corporate shell dissolves, and the obligations the shell was carrying dissolve with it, and one of those obligations was the black-lung benefit owed to the man whose lungs it had filled. So the obligation did not vanish. Obligations do not vanish. It moved. It moved onto a federal fund, the Black Lung Disability Trust Fund, which is to say it moved onto the rest of us, the ordinary taxpayer, who never sold a ton of coal and never booked a dollar of the profit and now quietly carries the cost of the lungs.

Bailey testified before Congress about all of this, twice, about the backlog of claims, about men dying while they waited for benefits the law already promised them. He said that most laws have been written with blood. He knew the lineage he was standing in. He died twenty-eight days after his last interview, in February of 2019, of a cancer brought on in part by the very drugs that kept the transplanted lungs alive. The disease the work gave him required a transplant; the transplant required the drugs; the drugs opened the door to the thing that finally killed him. Follow that chain from the first breath of dust to the last, and ask the question this chapter asks. What did the coal cost? Part of what it cost was Robert Bailey, and the bill for it landed on his body and then, the part that could be moved, on everybody but the people who sold the coal.

He is not a special case. He is a clear one. Across the central Appalachian coalfields, one in five long-tenured miners now carries black lung, twenty percent in Kentucky, Virginia, and West Virginia among men with twenty-five years or more underground, against a national figure that even at its worst was reckoned above ten. And the disease is not receding into the past with the old timers. It is rising again, and it is rising fastest in younger miners, men with less than twenty years in, the ones who were supposed to be safe because we were supposed to have solved this. Each death from it costs, by the public-health accounting, somewhere between eight and thirteen years of life that the man would otherwise have had. Eight to thirteen years, per body, multiplied out across the coalfields. That is one column.

And the part of the bill that moves does not stop moving. By September of 2024 the Black Lung Disability Trust Fund, the fund that catches the obligations the bankrupt companies shed, was carrying about five point one billion dollars in debt. It has had to borrow almost every year since 1979 to pay benefits the coal companies were supposed to fund and did not. Going back to the program's start, the federal black-lung benefits paid out come to roughly forty-four billion dollars through 2009, the most recent honest cumulative figure anyone has published, which means the true total since is larger and no one has totaled it. Three coal-company bankruptcies alone, Alpha Natural Resources and Patriot Coal and James River Coal, between 2014 and 2016, shed about eight hundred and sixty-five million dollars in black-lung benefit obligations straight onto that public fund. The companies kept the coal money. The public kept the lungs.

---

The second column is written on the water and the rock.

Go to McDowell and look at the creeks. Some of them run orange, the color of rust, the color of iron sulfide, and others run gray and dead with sediment, and the reason is acid mine drainage. When you mine coal you disturb rock that holds sulfur, and the sulfur, exposed to air and water, makes acid, and the acid leaches metals out of the ground and carries them into the streams. It begins when the mining begins. It does not end when the mining ends. It runs for decades after the last mine closes, water turning the rock to acid with no miner anywhere in sight, a cost that keeps arriving long after the revenue has stopped.

Cleaning it up is not free, and the bill is enormous, and the bill is not covered. Across Appalachia the abandoned mine lands run to more than six hundred and thirty thousand acres. The cost of cleaning up the acid drainage and the rest of the damage has been estimated, as of 2021, at somewhere between seven and a half and nine point eight billion dollars. The money set aside in reclamation bonds to pay for it comes to about three point eight billion. Do the subtraction. There is a gap of four to six billion dollars between what the damage costs to fix and what was ever reserved to fix it, and that gap is a cost too, sitting out there in the water and the rock, waiting for someone to pay it or for no one to, which only means the creek stays orange.

That is the second column, and it has the same shape as the first. Value went out. A cost stayed behind. Nobody wrote it on the invoice.

---

The third column emptied a county.

In 1950 McDowell County held nearly a hundred thousand people. The census counted ninety-eight thousand, eight hundred and eighty-seven. By 2020 it held just over nineteen thousand, nineteen thousand one hundred and eleven. The county lost four out of every five people it had.

You can read that as a number and feel nothing. So do not read it as a number. Drive through Welch, the county seat, or through Keystone or Kimball or War, towns whose names tell you something about the era that built them, and look at what is there now and what the foundations tell you used to be. Department stores. Movie theaters. Hotels. Newspapers. A downtown that on a Saturday in 1950 was packed shoulder to shoulder with people spending coal wages. McDowell was where the first food stamps under the modern program were issued, a pilot that began in Welch when a man named Alderson Muncy received the first allotment on the twenty-ninth of May, 1961. There were enough people, and enough money moving, for this to have been a real place with real downtowns. Now there are empty lots and empty storefronts where the stores were.

The departure was not a single cliff. That is worth saying plainly because the easy version of this story is wrong. The income of the county did not fall by two-thirds in one year. It bled out over forty years. There was a worst year, a drop of about thirteen percent from 1986 to 1987, and roughly fifteen percent cumulatively off the 1985 peak over three years, and then the slow grinding continuation of it, decade after decade, as the mechanization of the mines meant fewer men, and the closures meant fewer still. The U.S. Steel operation at Gary, in the county, wound down across the 1980s, more than twelve hundred jobs gone by the late part of the decade. People left because the work left, and the income left with them, not in a single catastrophe you could point to and name, but in a long draining-out that is harder to see precisely because it was slow.

---

The fourth column is the one that is hardest to put a figure on, and I want to put it where you can feel it.

A family in a coal town raises a child. They cook the breakfast at seven before the shift. They buy the Little League uniform. They pay, somehow, for the piano lessons. They pour into that child the years and the money and the care that raising a child takes, which is to say they make an investment, the most serious investment most people ever make, and they make it in a place. And then the child grows up and the work is gone, so the child leaves, and goes to a city, and produces value there for the rest of a life. The city gets the engineer, the nurse, the teacher, the carpenter, fully formed, the whole cost of making that person already paid. By somewhere else. By a coal town that fronted the entire bill for raising a worker and never collected a dollar of the return.

This is the lineage cost, the brain-drain cost, and it is real even though no ledger in the country records it, because the place that pays to raise a person and the place that harvests that person's working life are two different places, and the value flows from the first to the second, uphill against every intuition about who earned what. The children who did not come back are not a tragedy of sentiment. They are a transfer of wealth, paid by the poor county to the rich one, and never invoiced, and never repaid.

---

Now the numbers, plainly, because they are real and you should see them.

In McDowell County a man's life expectancy runs around sixty-three and a half years, against a national figure of about seventy-six and a half. That is a gap of thirteen years. The drug-overdose death rate has run as high as a hundred and forty-one per hundred thousand, against a national rate, in the year of comparison, of about sixteen. The poverty rate sits at thirty-seven and a half percent, the median household income at about twenty-eight thousand dollars as of 2022, and on the standard ranking of health outcomes across the fifty-five counties of West Virginia, McDowell ranks fifty-fifth. Out of fifty-five. Last.

These numbers are real. But the numbers are not the argument. The numbers are what the argument explains. You can find places with bad numbers anywhere. What makes McDowell different is the juxtaposition, the wealth and the destitution sharing one piece of ground, the richest coal and the poorest county, and the numbers are simply the wealth's receipt, the cost side of a transaction whose value side went out by rail a long time ago.

There is one more column, and it does not belong here. It belongs in a later chapter, because it is the largest of all and it cannot be honestly priced in a paragraph. When that coal was burned, it put carbon into an atmosphere that everyone alive after us has to live in, and that deferred bill, the cost of the combustion itself, lands on people who have not been born to object. We will return to that. For now I only mark the column and move on, because the honest computation of it requires machinery this chapter does not yet have, and I would rather defer the number than fake it.

---

I have just done something, and I want to name it before I go on, because it is the move the whole book turns on.

I asked what a ton of coal cost, and I answered with a list, and every item on the list was real, and not one of them appeared in the price of the coal. The mine-mouth price of McDowell coal in 1960 was just under five dollars a ton. That price was real and honest. It covered the wages and the leases and the operator's margin, the things the market knew how to count. It did not cover the lung, because no one billed the lung. It did not cover the creek, because no one billed the creek. It did not cover the county that emptied or the children who left or the carbon that has not yet finished arriving. Those costs were not erased. They were severed, cut loose from the transaction that caused them and set adrift to land on whoever happened to be standing where they came down.

That is the mechanism, and it is time to give it a name, because we are going to need the name in every chapter that follows.

Call it cost severance. A cost is severed when it is separated from the transaction that produced it and made to land somewhere else, on someone who was not party to the deal and never agreed to carry it. The thing that lands is a severed cost, the lung and the creek and the emptied county, each of them a cost cut loose from the price that should have held it. And the act of generating value while severing the cost of generating it, of keeping the upside and shedding the downside onto others, is value extraction. The coal sold for what coal sells for. The value extracted was the difference between that price and the true cost, the gap that the lung and the creek and the children quietly filled.

These three words, cost severance, severed cost, value extraction, are the load-bearing vocabulary of this book, and they will be used in every chapter that follows. So it is worth being careful about what they do and do not claim.

The market already has a word for some of this. The word is externality, and it is a good word, more than a century old. The economists who developed it, Arthur Pigou foremost among them, saw clearly that a transaction can throw off costs onto third parties, smoke onto the neighbors, and that the price will not reflect them unless something is done. Externality is technically accurate. It is also practically insufficient, and the insufficiency is the whole story. To call the lung an externality is correct and changes nothing. The word names the leak without naming a way to stop it. What that century-old tradition was missing was not the diagnosis. It was an instrument, and the building of one is taken up later in this book.

There is a deeper lineage here too, worth a single nod. The economist Mariana Mazzucato has spent her career insisting on the difference between making value and merely taking it, between the activity that creates wealth and the activity that captures wealth others created, and arguing that our accounts have lost the ability to tell the two apart. Cost severance is value-taking of a particular kind, taking by not bearing the cost, and it echoes what David Harvey called accumulation by dispossession, the older name for wealth amassed by stripping it out of a place and the people in it. The pattern is not new. The naming of it is old. What this book attempts to add is the application of it to physical-resource extraction, to the lung and the creek and the carbon, priced honestly enough that the severed cost can be seen while there is still time to act on it.

---

There is one more thing the place itself teaches, and it is the dimension you cannot get from a single column, because it is about where the columns point.

The value of McDowell coal did not stay in McDowell. It went up and out, to Pittsburgh and Gary, into the steel, into the country, and the cost stayed home, in the lung and the creek and the county. The two things separated in space. The value traveled. The cost did not. And the people who eventually held the value, the ones who drove the cars and crossed the bridges and worked in the buildings the steel made, were nowhere near the place that paid for it, and never saw the place, and had no way to.

In the last chapter, the way to see a thing truly was to get above it, to climb to the high point until the shape of the place showed itself, the river bend and the old roads and where the money flowed and where it flowed past. Here is the inverse. The people who held the value of that coal never got above the thing to see its shape. They could not, because the cost was a hundred miles away in a hollow they would never drive into, severed not just from the price but from the line of sight. Distance is part of how the severance works. Put enough miles between the value and the cost and the people who enjoy the one will never have to look at the other. Call that spatial cost severance, the geographic version of the same cut, and notice that it is built to keep the cost out of view of the people best positioned to object to it.

---

Now I have to be careful, because a chapter like this one invites three reflexive responses, and each of them, if you let it, lets the real defendant walk.

The first response is that the coal companies were evil. That the operators were greedy men who knew what they were doing to those lungs and did it anyway. Maybe some were. It does not matter, and it is not the argument. Cost severance does not require a villain. It requires only a price that does not include a cost, and a market that rewards whoever sheds the most cost from their price, which it does automatically, because the company that pays for the lung is undersold by the company that does not. There was no villain at the leasing office in the last chapter, and there is no villain required here. The mechanism runs on incentives, not on malice. Point at the motive and you will miss the mechanism, and the mechanism is the thing that is still running.

The second response is that industrialization was a mistake, that we should never have dug the coal, that the whole enterprise stands condemned. But the country was built out of that steel. The coal did real work. The point is not that the value was fake. The value was enormously real, which is exactly why the failure to price the cost was so consequential, because the bigger the value, the bigger the severed cost riding alongside it. The question is not whether we should have done it. The question is whether the people who bore the cost were ever compensated for bearing it, and the answer, column after column, is no.

The third response is that the miners were passive, that they let this happen, that a people who organized could have stopped it. But they did organize. The miners of these coalfields built one of the most militant labor movements in the country's history. The Black Lung Association formed, and in 1968 the miners walked out in a wildcat strike that shut the West Virginia fields and forced a state legislature to recognize the disease and compensate it, and out of that same ferment came the Miners for Democracy movement that took back their own union. They were not passive. They had agency, and they used it, hard, and they won real things with it. And it was not enough, because agency operating inside a structure can change the terms of the deal but cannot reach the thing that severed the cost in the first place. A constrained choice is still a choice. It is not a free one. The miners chose, and fought, and bled, within a set of options that did not include the one that would have mattered, which was an instrument to make the price tell the truth.

And so the indictment Kennedy delivered in Canton still stands. Every word of it. The value generated, the place destroyed, the wrong of it. Only the name of the defendant changes. It is not the operators, though some of them behaved badly. It is not industry, which did real work. It is not the miners, who fought. What belongs in the dock is an instrument that does not exist, a way of pricing that would have held the cost to the transaction instead of letting it sever and drift, and the argument of this book is that we have to build it. Kennedy could point at the crime. He could not point at the defendant, because the defendant had not been invented. It still has not.

None of this is a verdict on the men. The pride was earned. The work was real and hard and skilled and it built the country, and nothing in this chapter is a verdict on the people who did it. It is a verdict on an arithmetic.

---

There is a place where someone will say the mechanism does not apply, and I want to take it head on, because answering it is the freshest thing this chapter has to offer.

Someone will say that what happened to McDowell was the coal, the industry, the structure, but that the county's worst recent affliction, the drug epidemic that drove the overdose rate to a hundred and forty-one per hundred thousand, was something else. Culture, despair, weakness. A second wound the place inflicted on itself. The numbers from the first cycle, they will grant. The second cycle, they will say, was not extraction. It was decay.

It was extraction. It was a second cycle of exactly the same mechanism, run by different operators on the wreckage the first had left, and we can show it, because there are two records to read.

There is the record the companies wrote down themselves, in the ordinary course of business, the litigation record, what came out in the lawsuits. Purdue Pharma, which made OxyContin, marketed it in writing to what one state's litigation quotes the company describing as vulnerable, underserved, and stigmatized populations. The consulting work done for the company spoke of evolving the franchise to excellence and turbocharging the sales engine. The prescriber data the company bought let it find, county by county, doctor by doctor, where the prescriptions could be pushed. And there is the second record, the one researchers and prosecutors had to reconstruct after the fact, in the public-health literature and the economic geography, which found that the places hit hardest were the places already hollowed out, the distressed coal and factory counties, the ones where bodies were already broken from the work and the social fabric already thinned by the leaving. Put the two records together and you do not have a coincidence. You have a pattern, written partly by the company in its own marketing and reconstructed partly by the people who came afterward to count the dead, and the pattern points at distress as the thing the second extraction sought out.

Because that is the tell. The Purdue campaign did not employ McDowell. It did not dig anything or build anything or pay a single wage there. It found the wreckage the wage economy had left behind, the broken bodies and the emptied towns and the people in pain with nothing to do, and it made a market out of that wreckage. This is what Nancy Fraser calls expropriation, accumulation by means other than the wage contract, taking that does not even bother with the form of employment, and it is the purest instance of it this chapter has, the value pulled out of a place through no exchange the place was party to at all. The first cycle at least paid the miner a wage before severing the cost of his lung. The second cycle skipped the wage and went straight for the severance.

The numbers on that second cycle are their own ledger. The Sackler family extracted somewhere around eleven billion dollars from the enterprise. The three big distributors that moved the pills, McKesson and Cardinal Health and AmerisourceBergen, settled for twenty-one billion dollars in 2022. The company pleaded to a federal felony for misbranding back in 2007 and paid six hundred million dollars, and three executives, Friedman, Udell, and Goldenheim, pleaded to misdemeanor counts. It pleaded again in 2020, three federal felony counts that time. The settlements name the harm and put a price on it after the fact, which is the system working backward, paying the cost only once it has already landed, only once the bodies are already counted, which is the opposite of the instrument that would have held the cost to the transaction before it severed.

Culture did not do this to McDowell. The same mechanism did it twice.

---

Everything in this chapter has been one place. But the structure is not one place, and to make that plain I want to end by pointing east, to a different kind of ground and a different kind of water, where the same thing is happening in a register that looks nothing like a coal town.

I grew up on the Chesapeake Bay, among watermen, men who go out before light and come back with what the water gives. And the water gives less than it did, because costs have been put into it that no one ever wrote on an invoice, costs that settle onto the oyster and the crab and the men who chase them, while the value that caused those costs floated off upstream and inland and out of reach. Same structure. Different details. The defendant is the same defendant, an instrument that does not exist, a price that will not hold the cost to the transaction that caused it.

There is one difference worth naming, because it changes the arithmetic in a way the next chapter will need. The coal is finite. What came out of McDowell is gone, and the severed cost of it is a debt with no possibility of repayment, because the seam cannot make more of itself. The bay is not like that. A bay, left alone, keeps making what is taken from it, the oyster bed reseeds, the crab returns, the water cleans itself. So the severance out there is conditional. It arrives only when the rate of taking runs past the rate of making, and it does not have to arrive at all. That is the hope hiding in the harder case, and it is where we go next, to the man who works the renewable thing, and to what it means that his ruin, unlike the miner's, was never inevitable.

This is the miner I meant, back in the last chapter, when I spoke of options a coal miner does not have. Now you have met him. Now we go to the water.
