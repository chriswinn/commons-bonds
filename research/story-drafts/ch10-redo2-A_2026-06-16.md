# Chapter 10. Common Bonds

A long time before the sun, the first thing that arrives is not light. It is the sound of men going to work. A diesel coughs somewhere down the line of slips, catches, and settles into itself. A line that has been slapping a mast all night stops slapping. The sound travels across the water as if the water were carrying it, the way it carries everything here, the way it gives the harbor its own acoustics at this hour. Behind the slips the Fort is dark. The Bay is one shade of gray opening, very slowly, into a slightly different gray. The channel marker blinks red on its own slow schedule, indifferent to whether anyone is watching. North of Newport News a tug is working, and you hear it long before you see it, if you see it at all.

I am awake because the work boats are starting and there is no sleeping through them, and because I would not want to. I am on the deck of a sailboat in a slip on the harbor side at Old Point Comfort, twelve miles down the road from where my grandfather built models for a living, and I came here, finally, to write the end of this. The chapter is what I have to say. The morning is what I came here to listen to.

What I have to say is one sentence. Value leaves the place it comes from, and the costs of its leaving stay behind. That is the whole of it. The nine chapters before this one are the unfolding of that sentence, but the unfolding is not the framework. The sentence is. The framework is one sentence, and everything else is the labor of making it count.

The cases were the unfolding. The coal of West Virginia. The oysters of the Chesapeake, an hour from where I am sitting. The rare earths of Inner Mongolia. The oil of the North Sea and the oil of the Niger Delta. The water of the Ogallala under the High Plains, and the water ice locked under the surface of Mars that nobody has touched yet, that I asked you to imagine before anyone had. Different resources, different centuries, different continents, one sentence. What I have wanted to do, across all of it, is make the sentence count.

I came to the harbor for four reasons, and I might as well say them plainly, because they are why the book exists and not some thesis I went looking for. The first was the ledgers. I spent a working life reading financial statements that were honest about everything except the thing that mattered, ledgers that balanced perfectly while the cost of balancing them sat in some column that had been left off the page. The second was my parents, who are getting old in Hampton, who pick up the phone on the first ring or the second, and whose aging made the arithmetic of time stop being abstract. The third was my wife, who nearly died, and whose nearly dying taught me what a cost looks like when it cannot be transferred to anyone, when it has to be borne by the person it lands on. And the fourth was the architecture itself, the shape of the thing, which I had been living inside without looking at, until one day I turned around and looked at it, and could not stop.

---

If you put them all in one room, the people this book is about, you would not at first see what they share.

The miner in southern West Virginia, pulling on his boots in the dark. The waterman on the Lower Bay who has been crabbing since he was twelve and runs a mechanic shop in the afternoons because the water alone no longer pays. The woman in Baotou whose kitchen window looks out on a tailings pond. The fisherman in the Niger Delta whose son will not fish the creek his father fished. The farmer in the Texas Panhandle watching the irrigation pivots come off the field because there is no longer enough under the ground to justify running them. The rural nurse. The third-shift warehouse worker. The long-haul driver who is thirty nights a year from his own bed.

They do not know each other. They would not, most of them, recognize anything of themselves in the others. The miner does not think of the woman in Baotou as kin, and she has never heard of McDowell County, and the driver thirty nights from home would be surprised to learn he has anything in common with a Niger Delta fisherman. But the mechanism that extracts from all of them is the same mechanism. The value each of them helped produce went somewhere else, and the cost of its going stayed with them, and nobody ever told them they were standing in the same line.

Put a Mars administrator in that room too, the one I asked you to imagine, running a ten-thousand-person habitat, asked by the people back home to accelerate the extraction of the subsurface water everyone depends on to breathe and drink and grow food. On Mars the cost severance is not impossible. Nothing about human nature changes on the way up. It is impossible to hide. There is no downwind. There is no later generation in another country who will absorb the loss out of sight. Everyone who would bear the cost is in the room, breathing the same recycled air, and so the severance has to be argued for in front of the people it would be severed onto. On Earth, the same severance happens all the time, and the only difference is that Earth's room is larger and the lighting in it is poor. The difference is the cover.

There are a handful of covers, and the book's cases surfaced them often enough that they earned names. There is distance, the gap between the place the value goes and the place the cost stays. There is time, the present generation deciding and the later generations absorbing. There is information, the gap between what one side knew and what the other side did not know in time to do anything with the knowing. There is power, the difference between the parties who could have refused and the parties whose refusal was foreclosed before they were ever asked. There is routine, the slow normalization by which a cost stops being counted as a cost at all and becomes simply how things are done. And there is abundance, the working illusion that a resource is unlimited, that holds right up until the math turns and the illusion becomes the most expensive assumption anyone ever made.

These six are not a complete list. I do not believe there is a complete list, and I have tried not to write as if there were. They are the six this book's cases surfaced often enough to name, and another set of cases would surface others. McDowell County's coal moved out under distance and information, mostly, the value shipped north and east while the cost stayed in the hollows, and the men who signed away the mineral rights not knowing what they were worth. The crash of 2008 ran almost entirely on information, the side that structured the loan against the side that signed it. The pyramids were built under power. The whole vocabulary of treating a community's ruin as the cost of doing business is routine, dignified into an accounting line. And the atmosphere absorbed two centuries of carbon under abundance, under the assumption that the sky was so large it could not fill.

You can map the instruments onto the covers, roughly. Carbon pricing collapses part of the abundance gap, by making the sky no longer free. A border adjustment collapses part of the distance gap, by following the cost across the line it was shipped over. A sovereign wealth fund collapses part of the time gap, by holding value for the generations that would otherwise inherit only the depletion. Disclosure requirements collapse part of the information gap, by forcing into the light what one side knew and the other did not. Each instrument reaches a piece of it. None of them reaches all of it. And the closer you can bring the cost-bearer and the value-capturer to standing in the same room, the harder the severance becomes to maintain, which is the whole lesson of the habitat on Mars brought back down to the harbor.

---

My grandfather was a model maker. His name was LaVern Winn, and he worked at NASA Langley, twelve miles up the road from this slip, in the wind tunnels. He built scale models, aircraft and spacecraft components, out of wood and metal and resin, so that a tunnel could tell the truth about a full-size design that did not yet exist and could not yet be flown. The whole discipline rests on a single demand. Build the model faithfully enough that reality will not lie when it is run against your work. His patented work extended the discipline past the tunnel itself. And the domain he was building toward was spaceflight, where reality does not negotiate, where the cost of being wrong is borne by people who cannot recover from the error.

I grew up around the small repairs in the workshop behind his house, around the patience that kind of work demands, the refusal to fudge a measurement because the measurement would be checked by something that could not be argued with.

The framework is a model in that lineage. I am the grandson of a model maker, working in a different shop, with different materials, on a different scale. The inheritance is the thing I have used to build it. And like every honest model, it is defined as much by what it cannot do as by what it can, and a model maker who will not tell you the limits of his model is not making models, he is selling them.

So here are the limits, the ones I know.

The model cannot settle the discount rate. When you price a cost that lands a century from now, you have to decide how much a harm to people not yet born weighs against a benefit to people alive today, and that decision is the discount rate, and economists have argued about it for decades without converging. Nordhaus discounts the future more steeply and Stern discounts it far less, and the gap between them is the gap between two moral pictures of what we owe the people who come after us. The model does not pretend to settle that. It is honest that the discipline has not settled it. But here is the move that matters, the one I cannot leave out, because the honesty is only half the answer. The framework does not need to win that fight. The result holds across the whole range. Reserve a fraction of a resource whose loss could not be substituted for, and the cost severance comes out greater than zero whether you discount the future at Nordhaus's higher rate or Stern's lower one. The conclusion survives both pictures. I do not have to pick a side in an argument I am not equipped to end, because my answer does not depend on the side. And where Weitzman argued that the discount rate should itself decline over the longest horizons, the framework does not merely nod at that as a useful direction. It adopts the declining rate directly, which biases its own estimates downward, toward the most conservative defensible accounting, and finds the cost there anyway. The model is honest about what it cannot settle, and its conclusion does not depend on settling it. Both halves are true, and I have learned not to say only the first.

The model cannot price the things that are not denominated in any currency. There are losses that have no number, not because the number is hard to find but because the thing was never the kind of thing a number describes. I will come to one of them shortly, the hardest one, and I will not pretend the framework reaches it.

The model cannot predict the timeline of substitution. It can tell you that a resource's existential substitutability gap is wide, that nothing can replace it on any horizon we can see, and it can tell you that another resource will likely be substituted before the loss becomes irreversible. It cannot tell you the year. The model prices the gap; it does not own a calendar.

And the model cannot resolve the tension between what people need now and what has to be preserved for later. It can make the trade-off visible. It cannot make the trade-off for you. There are real present needs, and there are real future losses, and a society has to choose between them with its eyes open, and the most the model can do is keep both eyes open while it chooses.

What the model can do is smaller than what people sometimes hope and larger than what they sometimes fear. It can name the second transaction, the unpriced one, the one that happens in the same instant as the priced one and never appears on the ledger. It can structure an honest accounting in a vocabulary a person can actually use. And it can show, again and again, that the cost of the insurance is small against the cost of having no insurance at all. Reserving a fraction of a resource whose loss could not be undone costs only the revenue foregone on the reserved fraction. Capturing the rents in a sovereign fund costs the discipline to maintain the fund, which Norway has demonstrated, across decades, is a discipline a society can actually keep. A border adjustment costs administrative complexity, and that is most of what it costs. None of these is large against the foreclosure it prevents. The framework's job is to make the work conceivable. The rest belongs to whoever picks up the instrument.

The framework runs backward as well as forward. This is not an afterthought; it was there from Chapter 5, and the technical appendix formalizes it. What was extracted from a community in 1860 or 1960 or 1995 can be priced by the same instrument that prices what will be extracted in 2027. The accounting does not expire. Whether the pricing then produces compensation, restitution, structural reform, or some different shape of acknowledgment is a question the framework does not answer, and is not mine to answer for harms I have no standing to price. The framework names the column. It does not fill it. It only insists that the accounting has to be honest if any answer that follows is going to be honest, and that is not nothing, but it is not the answer either.

---

There is one account the framework does not reach, and I want to stand at the edge of it carefully, because it is the account that holds the framework honest.

The Lakota Sioux were guaranteed the Black Hills, Ȟe Sápa, by the Treaty of Fort Laramie in 1868. The treaty drew the Great Sioux Reservation and named the Hills inside it. Six years later the Custer expedition entered the Hills and found gold. Three years after that, in February of 1877, an Act of Congress stripped the Hills away, the formal severance of a sacred landscape. In 1980 the Supreme Court ruled, in *United States v. Sioux Nation of Indians*, that the 1877 taking had been unconstitutional. The Court awarded approximately seventeen and a half million dollars, the land's value in 1877, plus interest at five percent running from 1877 forward. By 1980 the award had grown to roughly a hundred and six million dollars. The Lakota refused it. The award sat in escrow, and went on accruing interest, and the trust account now holds more than one and a half billion dollars. It has been refused for more than four decades and counting.

The framework cannot reach this. Not because the number is too small. Because the land is not substitutable by money. Not at any amount. Not for any purpose. The framework prices what can be priced and is honest about what it cannot price, and the Black Hills falls squarely in the category it cannot price. The money in that escrow account is a number the framework can compute. The thing the number is supposed to stand for is not.

I am a settler. The book is a book written by a settler. The framework operates inside a settlement whose original wound it cannot heal and does not pretend to. The miner pulling on his boots and the waterman running his mechanic shop are not in the same position as the descendants of the people who held Ȟe Sápa under treaty before the gold was found, and I am not going to flatten that difference to make my framework look more complete than it is. I do not assign a dollar figure to that taking. It is not mine to assign, and I will not pretend otherwise. Nor will I tell anyone else what is or is not theirs to do with it. The framework offers the ledger. It does not fill that column, and I am the wrong person to fill it.

A reader who wants to understand what the framework cannot reach should put this book down and read other ones. Read Robin Wall Kimmerer, who is Citizen Potawatomi. Read Leanne Betasamosake Simpson, who is Michi Saagiig Nishnaabeg. Read Glen Coulthard, who is Yellowknives Dene. Read Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz. Read Vine Deloria Jr., who was Standing Rock Sioux. They are asking a different shape of the question this book asks, and a deeper one, and the framework is not a substitute for it and was never meant to be.

There is an objection I have to meet head on here, because the meeting of it is the honest thing. To treat the dispossession of indigenous peoples as one case among many, filed alongside coal and oysters and rare earths, risks flattening it, risks making the gravest coercion in the hemisphere's history sound like a line item. I take the risk seriously. But the framework's claim is structural, not a claim of moral equivalence. It says these cases share a mechanism, not that they share a weight. And naming the mechanism's reach clarifies rather than reduces, because what was done to indigenous peoples in North America was not an aberration of the American system. It was the American system operating exactly as designed, visible at its most severe. The Black Hills account is the account that holds the framework honest, the one that stands at the edge of everything the framework can do and marks the line.

---

I have left the hardest objections for the harbor, because the harbor is where I can hear myself think them.

The first objection I take most seriously, and the people who make it are not strangers to me. They are, most of them, my allies. They say the book does not go far enough. Honest pricing, strategic reserves, accountability bonds, universal floors, none of it will save us, they say, from a system whose internal logic drives it to extract until there is nothing left. The whole apparatus is reformist, and a reform is exactly the kind of thing a capitalism can swallow and keep running. A carbon-neutral multinational could adopt every instrument in the book and use it to justify continuing to extract, only slightly more slowly, with better disclosures. What is needed, they say, is not a better price but a different relationship to the resource altogether.

They are not wrong about what they see. The framework can be captured. It can be softened into a slogan on a quarterly report, an ESG line that signals virtue and changes nothing. Carbon pricing has been captured that way. Sovereign wealth funds have been captured. Reclamation bonds have been hollowed out in almost every place they exist. The conflict-free certificate that delivers a premium to the retailer and nothing at all to the miner who dug the thing out of the ground is the pattern in miniature. I have watched good instruments turned into marketing.

But the book is not an argument for capitalism, and it is not an argument against it. The model measures what extraction costs. What a society does with the measurement is a political question the framework does not legislate. And here is the part the objection misses. State-owned mines that sever costs are still severing costs. Worker cooperatives that sever costs are still severing costs. The pharaohs severed costs, and the Qin emperors severed costs, and the feudal lords severed costs, none of them capitalists, all of them running the same mechanism. The mechanism is not a feature of any ownership structure. Capitalism is the current delivery vehicle, the most efficient one ever built, but it is not the cause, and treating it as the cause has exactly the same practical effect as treating it as the cure. Both pull the whole conversation into political vocabulary, into the argument about systems, while the mechanism keeps running, undisturbed, underneath the argument.

That is one objection. There is a second one, and it cuts deeper, and it is the hardest thing a reader could say to me, because it does not say I went too easy on capital. It says I have done the very harm I claim to diagnose.

The objection is this. By pricing the commons, by bonding it, by building an instrument that puts a number on a reef or a watershed or a generation, I have extended the exact logic I set out to refuse. I have taken something that was not a commodity and turned it into one. The accountability bond is not a refusal of commodification. It is commodification's next instrument, the commons repackaged as a tradable asset class, and I am the one doing the repackaging. Polanyi named the fictitious commodity, the way the market treats labor and land and money as if they were produced for sale when none of them was, and a Polanyian looks at my bond and sees one more fictitious commodity being manufactured. Pistor showed that capital is a legal artifact, assembled out of modules, property, collateral, trust, the corporate veil, choice of jurisdiction, and a bond is exactly such a module, so a Pistorian looks at my instrument and sees the commons being coded as capital. Christophers showed how the rentier economy turns everything depended-upon into an asset to be owned and charged for, and a reader of Christophers looks at a bonded commons and sees one more asset class waiting to be captured. I met Pistor earlier in the book as a friend, a diagnostician on my side. The honest thing is to admit that the same diagnosis can be turned around and aimed at me.

I have sat with this one on this deck more than any other, because it is the worry closest to my own.

And the answer is a distinction, and the distinction is everything. Accounting a cost is not coding an asset. They are opposite operations that happen to pass near the same word. Pistor's modules code assets, things to be owned and traded and charged for. This book accounts costs, debts that must be paid and kept visible until they are. The bond does not create a thing to be owned. It records a thing that is owed. Pricing a forest in order to sell it turns the forest into board feet. Pricing a forest in order to reserve it names what the forest is worth so that its destruction can no longer be booked as free. Same word, opposite operation, and the whole framework lives on the far side of that distinction from the thing the objection accuses it of being.

And the lineage is the proof, not the alibi. The tradition I am standing in is Polanyi's and Fraser's, and that tradition is the one that refused the asset frame in the first place. Polanyi's double movement, the way a society pushes back to re-embed in social obligation a thing the market had stripped out of it, is precisely what the bond is. The double movement made into an accounting tool. To stand in the Polanyi-Fraser tradition is to use their refusal, not to betray it, and you cannot manufacture a fictitious commodity out of the very thinkers whose entire project was naming the fiction. The deeper exposition of all this lives back in Chapter 5, where the same object is named honestly and the argument is built rather than asserted. Here, at the close, with the bond apparatus laid out in full, I only need to state the conviction, because it has been earned across nine chapters and it does not need to be re-taught.

There is an honest residue, and I owe it the same candor I owed the first objection. The instrument can be perverted in use. Someone determined to do it could rebrand a commons bond into a tradable instrument, the way carbon credits were turned into a market to be gamed. I do not claim the misuse is impossible. I claim that the design polarity is reserve, not sell, and that the lineage is the refusal of the asset frame, and that those two facts are the guard. The answer to "any instrument can be captured" is the same answer the first objection got. The design and the lineage are the guard, and the politics is the work, and no clever instrument was ever going to do the politics for us.

There is a third objection, and I am giving it the same care as the other two, because it is made by people I love and it deserves better than the wave it has sometimes gotten. My parents are in Hampton, and their friends are conservatives, and these are not abstractions to me. They read a book like this and they see a Trojan horse for statism. Accountability bonds, strategic reserves, sovereign wealth funds, border adjustments, universal floors, the whole apparatus, looks to them like a vast new machinery of state control over private extraction, a central planner's wish list wearing the costume of accounting. Pricing every externality, they say, is a license for unlimited government reach. Honest accounting is the thin end of a wedge whose thick end is the state owning the commons it claimed only to be measuring. And the framework, they say, will be captured by the same agencies whose capture the book itself documents.

This objection is right that capture is a risk, and I will come back to that, but it is wrong about what the framework legislates, which is nothing. Just as the model does not argue for or against capitalism, it does not argue for or against the state. The model measures what extraction costs. What a society does with the measurement, whether it builds a tax or a bond posted to a private trustee, a market in tradable reserve-credits or a contractual obligation between two private parties, a sovereign fund or nothing at all, is a political question the framework does not decide. An honest number is not a statist number. A conservative can pick up the same number and argue for a market instrument that a progressive would use to argue for a public fund, and the number does not care which of them is holding it.

And honest accounting is a conservative value, maybe the conservative value. A sound balance sheet requires that a cost be priced honestly. A hidden liability is a fraud whether the entity hiding it is a government or a firm, and the demand that the books be honest is exactly the discipline conservatives have always asked of the state and of corporations alike. The framework hands the center-right reader an instrument that is, on its own terms, a demand for honest books. It is available to a market-based answer at least as readily as to a state-based one, and I would rather it were used for both than captured by either.

Which brings me to the capture point, and the capture point cuts both ways, and I concede it in both directions. Yes, the apparatus can be captured by the regulatory state. It can also be captured into an ESG slogan. The conflict-free diamond certificate is the left's example of capture, the premium going to the retailer and not the miner. A regulatory agency captured by the industry it was built to police, its inspectors taking the industry's travel and gifts, is the right's example, and the book documents one of those too. The answer to both is identical. The framework is an instrument. Capture is a political risk that every instrument runs. The guard is the design, account costs, do not code assets, plus the politics, which the framework does not legislate and never could.

So I have friends on the left who will read this and find me too modest about how much the institutions have to change, and my parents have conservative friends who will read the same pages and find me too generous to the institutional reform the framework implies. Both readings are misreadings, and they are the same misreading, the mistake of asking the framework to take a political side it was not built to take. I have tried to earn that sentence by giving both objections a real steelman and a real answer, rather than naming one and waving at the other, because a symmetry asserted is worth nothing and a symmetry demonstrated is the point.

There is one more objection, gentler than the others and closer to the bone, and Sandel has stated it better than anyone. To put a number on the priceless, on a sacred landscape, a reef, a community, a generation not yet born, is already to concede the extractor's frame. Once a thing has a price it can be set against a quarter's earnings, weighed, traded off, and the act of pricing it, however well meant, has already performed the reduction the extractor wanted. The framework's number, on this reading, is the extractor's victory wearing a moral disguise. I take this seriously because it is the worry closest to my own about the whole project, the one that kept me from writing the book for years.

The answer is the same one I gave back in Chapter 5, and it has not changed at the harbor. The choice is not between a sacred thing left unpriced and a sacred thing reduced to a number. The choice is between two numbers, because the thing already has a number, and the number is zero. Zero is the number the extractive accounting already assigned. Zero is what made the extraction profitable in the first place, the reef priced at zero, the community priced at zero, the future priced at zero. The framework refuses that zero. Naming what a thing is worth, so its destruction can no longer be booked as free, is not the commodification Sandel fears. It is the refusal of the commodification the zero already performed. And where the thing genuinely has no number, where it is not denominated in any currency, the Black Hills, the ocean under the ice of Europa, the framework says so and stops. It never invents a number to keep up the appearance of completeness. Sandel's worry and the framework's own honest limit are the same boundary seen from two sides, and the framework lives along that boundary, pricing what can be priced in order to reserve it, and falling silent where the silence is the honest thing.

---

This is our generation's chapter, and I do not think that is grandiose to say. We are the first generation to know, with the numbers in front of us, that the resources are finite. We are the first to have, in hand, the technology for substitutes. We are the first with the institutional capacity, scattered and partial and underfunded as it is, to do something about it. And we are, in all likelihood, the last generation with meaningful leverage on the window, the last who can act while acting still changes the outcome.

The carbon clock is the most legible version of the constraint, the one with a countdown anyone can read, but it is not the only one. There is the drawdown of the aquifers, the Ogallala emptying under the farmer's pivots. There is the collapse of the fisheries, the waterman's afternoons at the mechanic shop. There is the loss of the soil. There is the depletion of the very resources the renewable transition itself depends on, the ones whose substitutability gap is widest. The decision points are not in the next century. They are in this one, most of them in the next two decades, and that is what it means to be at the hinge. We are at the hinge, and a hinge is not a thing you get to be at twice.

The title has two readings, and they turn out to be one reading.

*Commons Bonds.* The obligations a participant owes to the commons. A bond, in that reading, is the account that keeps a debt visible until it is paid, the instrument that will not let the cost be quietly written off. And *common bonds*, the other reading, the bonds among the people who have been extracted from, the miner and the waterman and the woman in Baotou and the driver thirty nights from home, who share a structural position they never chose and were never told they shared. The bonds of a class position you did not choose are still bonds. The two readings are one reading. The obligation owed to the commons and the kinship among the extracted-from are the same fact looked at from two sides, and the title is the whole framework compressed into two words that reverse into each other.

---

The first gray is coming up now over the water, that thin line that is not yet light but is the promise of it. In a little while I will put the kettle on. The dog will come up from where she has been sleeping at the foot of the companionway and put her chin on the deck and wait to be told it is morning.

Today is made of small attentions, and I have come to think the small attentions are most of what a life is. There is nursing coursework I am doing, late in a working life, which is its own kind of admission that I have things left to learn. There are hours at the hospital, learning the bedside work, the actual care of an actual body in a bed, which the framework has nothing whatever to teach me about, and which I would not trade for any framework. There is desk work on the book. There is the call to my parents, who pick up on the first ring or the second. There is the tutoring office and the first-generation college students who remind me what it is to stand at the edge of something and not yet know you are allowed in. There is an hour on the foredeck with a book that is not this one. The dog. The line between the work and the life shortened, deliberately, over years, until it is short enough that the attentions stay attached to the people they were meant for.

Camus closed *Le Mythe de Sisyphe* in 1942 with a sentence I have carried a long time. *Il faut imaginer Sisyphe heureux.* One must imagine Sisyphus happy. Not because the work is ever finished. The work is the rock and the rock always rolls back down. He is happy because the work is his, because he has chosen it, because he walks back down the hill seeing each stone on the slope, knowing exactly what the boulder weighs, and puts his shoulder to it again anyway.

The framework is not the boulder, and it is not the hill. It does not flatten the slope or lighten the load or grant anyone rest. What it does is tell the walker where north is. A compass is not the journey. It is the small instrument by which a person who has to keep walking can know whether the direction is the right one. The framework's honesty does not lift the work. It tells the walker that the work, when it is finally done, will have been the work in the right direction, and for a man pushing a rock that does not stay pushed, that turns out to be most of what he needs to know.

The watermen already have their compasses. Out on the water before me, the men whose diesels woke me know honest weather from weather that lies, carry the knowing in their hands when they need it and in their bodies when they do not. They did not need the framework before the framework existed, and they will not need it after it is gone. The framework was never for them. What it offers the rest of us is the chance to see what the men on the dock have been seeing all along, the second transaction underneath the first one, the cost underneath the price, and then the chance to do something about it.

Naming the thing is the beginning. It is not the end. The end is institutional and generational, decades of patient work in legislatures and treaties and balance sheets and rooms where the cost-bearers finally sit across from the value-capturers, and most of it will be done by people I will never meet, after I am gone. But it begins with the naming, with seeing the second transaction and refusing to let it stay invisible, and that part is available now, this morning, to anyone willing to look.

The first work boat is well out into the channel now. The kettle is about to go on. The dog has come up to wait.

Let us begin.
