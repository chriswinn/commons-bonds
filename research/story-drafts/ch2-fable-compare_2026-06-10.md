## 3. Side-by-side excerpts (verbatim)

### Beat 1 — Mechanism-section close (the chapter's thesis landing)

**Canonical:**
> Cost severance is not a story about villains. It is a story about an instrument that does not exist. The argument of this book is that we have to build it.

**Variant A:**
> So the indictment Kennedy delivered in Canton stands; only the name of the defendant changes. There was never a villain to put in the dock. What belongs in the dock is an instrument that does not exist, and the argument of this book is that we have to build it.

**Variant B:**
> No villain is required for any of it. What is required is an absence, the instrument that does not exist, and the argument of this book is that we have to build it.

*Judge note: A is the only version that returns to Canton and the dock — the frame the chapter opens on and explicitly says it turns on. Canonical never closes that loop. This is A's single clearest win.*

### Beat 2 — the numbers-paragraph close

**Canonical:**
> These numbers are real. But the numbers are not the argument. The numbers are what the argument explains.

**Variant A:**
> These numbers are real, and they are still not the argument; they are what the argument has to explain. The explanation begins with a price, and with the question the price never answered: what did a ton of that coal actually cost?

**Variant B:**
> The numbers are real, but they are not the argument; they are what the argument has to explain.

*Judge note: canonical's anaphoric triplet is the stronger music; A's forward bridge into the price section is the stronger architecture. Fold-in 4 takes both.*

### Beat 3 — the empty lots

**Canonical:**
> The towns that had department stores and theaters now have empty lots where the buildings used to be. Not ruins, in many cases. Lots. The buildings were pulled down because no one was coming to use them, and grass grew over where the little New York of the coalfields had stood.

**Variant A:**
> The towns that had the department stores and the theaters now have empty lots where the buildings used to be, and many of those lots are not even ruins; the buildings were pulled down because no one was coming to use them, and grass grew over the ground where the little New York of the coalfields had stood.

**Variant B:**
> The towns that had department stores and theaters now have empty lots where the buildings used to be, and in many cases they are lots rather than ruins, because the buildings were pulled down when no one was coming to use them, and grass grew over where the little New York of the coalfields had stood.

*Judge note: "Not ruins, in many cases. Lots." is the best two-beat in the chapter. Both variants smooth it away. Fold-in 3.*
