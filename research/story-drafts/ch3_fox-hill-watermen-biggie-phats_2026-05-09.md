## Raw dump (preserved verbatim, paragraph breaks added for readability)

Ok first my father is from an island community of fisherman known as Fox Hill. His father worked at NASA as has been previously discussed, but most every other family worked on the water for their income or at least a large portion of their food. You have to understand how dangerous and difficult it is to work the water day in day out to make your living regardless if the weather is perfect or not. Some days the Chesapeake Bay is as inviting as any beautiful postcard. But some days it just isn't.

The thing the Chesapeake Bay is most famous for beyond the beautiful waters and plentiful seafood is the chop. Now when I say chop I mean the type of waves that are in the bay. To explain I'll first talk about waves in the ocean. In the ocean a 5 foot wave feels almost not worth commenting on as the 5 foot wave may have 20-30 feet distance in between the each waves peak, and that distance is known as the wavelength. In the ocean you might have 10-20+ seconds in between waves which just gives you a gentle rocking motion. And certainly the waves in the ocean can get massive especially during storms and getting caught in a storm that turns an unexpected direction is when it famously gets very very dangerous on the ocean.

That isn't how the waves are in the Chesapeake Bay, in the Bay the wavelength is very very short. Most of the time the waves in the Chesapeake Bay are less than 2 meters high (about 6.5 feet) but the wavelength is also very very short at around 3-4 seconds so even a 3-5ft swell (wave) can be very steep which will expose smaller boats to capsizing, and has tested the stomaches of many aspiring boaters in the Chesapeake Bay.

Now the water is something you quickly learn to respect growing up on the Chesapeake Bay because you know it can support life with one wave and it can take it away with the next. So when a fisherman in the community needs help with fixing their nexts it's such a big job that all of the other fishermen know that morning 3-4am when everyone would normally already be on the water or heading to it they will be coming to your place to help you with your nets as we are a community and we don't let anyone fall or go without help as surely as the sun rises and sets there will be a day that we or our families will be the ones that needs help. So the areas of Fox Hill, Poquoson, and the countless others along with the various live-aboard communities around the Bay are the sorts of communities that when someone says they need help everyone comes. And if on that day of reworking the nets someone isn't there at 3am or so when everyone said they would arrive everyone doesn't just shrug and assume they are running late. Everyone stops what they were doing with the nets and goes to look for the missing person because you know something is wrong and they might be in dire need of a rescue.

Which brings us to the stories of Phat's and Biggie. Biggie was an oak tree of a man who was an oysterman for his entire adult life. And Phat's, a crabber and fisherman, who is also a large mam by anyone's estimation but was never very tall. You have to understand my father knew Biggie & Phat's since my father was a small child. And both Biggie & Phat's are guys that make their living on the water like so many others.

And there is a certain level of familiarity that you get with the water that is only earned over lifetime on it. I'm not talking about just going fishing on the sunny spring and fall days that are just picture perfect where anyone would enjoy a day on the water. But the real work on the sweltering hot summer days, and even being out their on the rainy winter days with a chilled gale wind that blows right through your clothes and starts shaking your very soul. The watermen are out there every day and as long as they can be out there as there houses aren't paid for and their families don't eat when they don't bring home food. Which means you go out early and you stay out until you bring in food.

Now even a passing glance watching Biggie or Phat's maneuvering their boats effortlessly between and around their oyster beds or crab pots will tell you a story. Watching them maneuver their boats with one oar and zero wasted motions will tell you that he and his family have done this for generations.

Watching Biggie go to work you will be sure that he forgot his lunch as he only ever gets on the boat with a 6 pack of Coca-Cola and nothing else. However Biggie would simply smile and say his lunch is waiting for him in the water. And once the sun was higher in the sky Biggie would start eating oysters. My father often talked about how Biggie would place an oyster in a particular spot on the boat very close to his body and then without having to barely reach out or lift his arm the effortless move and minimal twist of Biggies wrist that he would make as Biggie shucked oyster after oyster. As Biggie worked his oyster beds all day he never stopped eating oysters. And despite trying my father trying, my father said he never did keep count of how many oysters Biggie put away in a single day.

Now Phat's is a different person altogether. Where as Biggie passed long before Virginia stopped Shad fishing in 1990, Phat's lived through it. Sadly there really aren't shad in the Bay anymore. As even though they live in the ocean most of their lives they come into the Bay to mate, and they would swim up the rivers to lay their eggs. So when there aren't shad in the Bay that means there aren't shad anywhere in this region anymore. As time went on Phat's slowly had to find other work and even though he still wakes up between 2-3 every morning and goes out on the water to pickup his crab pots he comes back to land and works his day job of being a car mechanic until his shop closes around 5-6 every day.
