## [transcript msg #82]

Hmm what is the water for me? water is life for me. It doesn't matter if you are rich or poor you can go to the water and play, eat, live. You can enjoy the limitless possibilities of a sunrise on a new day or the tranquility of a sunset over the water. It's said the Chesapeake Bay is known for Blue Crabs, and it's tributaries are known for Clam. Additionally to those familiar with its waters the Chesapeake Bay is known for its 'chop'. Now chop is an odd word so I'll define it. If you were on the ocean in most mid 30' - to - mid 40' boats and there were 5' waves it would be a gentle rocking motion that would make for a create nap as the tops of those waves would generally be 30' apart or more from each other which is known as the wavelength. Yet for reasons I don't really understand the Chesapeake Bays waves just aren't shaped like that. The wavelength in the bay is very short so a 5' wave might have a 5' wavelength which is almost square and can make even the hardiest of seamen go green under the right circumstances. When I think of the Chesapeake Bay boats I don't think of super yachts because those are somewhere else in the world. I think of the gentle tinging sound of sailboats, and the  gentle rise on the bow on a style of boat called the Deadrise. The deadrise is not a fancy boat it is a waterman's boat, and for some reason they are known to be more stable than most in the Chesapeake Bay's famous chop. Now when you think of powering a boat most people think of motors or sails and surely both are everywhere on the bay, but when I think of a waterman I think of people so familiar with their boat they get where they are going with the power of their motors then shift around their oyster beds tending to them and maneuvering around with just one oar in the water making the most subtle of movements to shift the boat from here to there. I think of watermen that wear clothing known as rain slicks which are absolutely water proof and keep them mostly dry even in the cold winter but offer no real warmth which means that of course you have to wear very thick full body base layers and sweaters to deal with the brutal cold rainy winter mornings because unlike the tourists they are on the water year round regardless of the weather. And they go out knowing full well the weight of their clothes means if they go into the freezing water they won't be able to get the clothes off to swim to the surface before the cold waters become their permanent home.

A great example of that are my three favorite fish to eat from the Chesapeak Bay: Striped Bass, Red Snapper, and Grouper. When I was a child in the 1980's you could find those but like any waterman we know that you only really fish them based on their spawning season as you aren't supposed to fish them off season or you will throw the gentle balance of the world off. Yet as I grew up there seemed to be one bad season after another and at first it was just not noticing yourself catch them very often anymore, and maybe a passing thought that I haven't noticed one or more of them on any restaraunt menus in the last couple of years. Yet now I know the difference that was waved away as just a bad season or two was actually the last of the seasons that they were available in any meaningful quantity, and yet we still took as much as we could carry to the shore. Now their populations are so low that I don't know if the  generation being born as I write this will even encounter one in their lifetime.; The reef and the shells. Oyster shells are everywhere in this area we had so many at one point that a quick search of oyster piles in Hampton, VA will show you pictures from around the year 1900 that oyster shells were piled 4 stories high. Interestingly we didn't really know what to do with them so they were used in concrete mix to pave sidewalks. When my father was in college he had to work every summer to be able to  pay his way through college and one of his summer jobs was pouring the oyster shell packed concrete that makes up the boardwalk in Hampton, VA that still stands at the time of this writing. I recall the first time I saw the old pictures of the oyster shells piled 3-4 stories tall and being told what at the time felt like an old wives tale that we just didn't know you needed to put them back in the water to help the water. Years later I heard that there is actually some scientific basis that supports oyster shells being regenerative substrate that can be used to restore reefs. In a strange complete circle I have heard of recent efforts to try to use old concrete to help rebuild oyster reefs via creating concrete oyster castles for the baby oysters to cling to. "Just off the mouth of Hampton River is an extensive shoal known as Hampton Bar. There, a major industry that helped rebuild Hampton after the Civil War was literally spawned. James Sands Darling, an industrious New Yorker who arrived to make his fortune in 1866, was advised by old-timers that the bar was not a good place for planting and harvesting oysters, but he knew they were wrong. Beginning in 1882, J.S. Darling and Son Oyster Planters and Packers made Hampton Bar oysters famous. Darling and his son, Frank, built the company into the largest such operation in the world.
Everyday, a fleet of log canoes, some built here on the Hampton waterfront, sailed out to the bar where watermen tonged for oysters. Back at the plant, 160 shuckers scooped the meat from 200,000 bushels of oysters each year, packed them in metal quart and gallon containers, and shipped them off in iced tubs for dinner tables and restaurants in Baltimore, New York, and other cities. By 1938, the company was selling 50,000 gallons of oysters annually.

The empty shells were carried out of the plant on conveyor bets and dumped on giant piles that dominated
