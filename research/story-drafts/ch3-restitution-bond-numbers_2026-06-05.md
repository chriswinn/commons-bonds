# COMMONS BONDS — Ch3 RESTITUTION BOND CONSERVATIVE-FLOOR REPORT

**For author review BEFORE Ch3 drafting fires.** Methodology-locked: price the physical reef substrate only; bond funds restoration (paid into the reef, not cash to people); killer fact = restoration is already happening on the public dime; number is the smallest *defensible* floor.

**Headline, honest up front:** The cleanest defensible floor is **differently-shaped than the substrate-volume framing implies, and the most-conservative version is *smaller* than you might expect — roughly $20M–$37M, not hundreds of millions.** And there is a genuine fork: the *volume-of-shell* construction (severed-substrate strand) collapses to an almost trivially small number (~$0.8M–$1M/peak-year of raw shell) because raw shell is cheap; the *area-of-reef* construction is the one that holds, because restoration cost-per-acre embeds the actual installed reef the bond is meant to fund. **I recommend the area construction.** Details below.

## (1) THE CONSERVATIVE-FLOOR NUMBER

**Construction (recommended): foreclosed reef AREA × realized installed restoration cost per acre.**

This is the methodology-aligned construction because the bond "funds the restoration paid into the reef" — so the unit price must be what it actually costs to *install* an acre of reef, not the spot price of raw shell.

| Input | Most-conservative value chosen | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Foreclosed reef extent | **2,738 acres** (James River high-quality oyster-rock bottom lost 1910→1981: 7,047 ac → 4,309 ac, −38.9%) | Schulte 2017, *Frontiers in Marine Science* 4:127, citing Moore 1910 / Haven et al. 1981 |
| Installed restoration cost/acre | **$7,300/acre** (Great Wicomico realized: $907,000 ÷ 124 ac) | NOAA Fisheries per-tributary table, as of Dec 31 2024 |

**FLOOR = 2,738 ac × $7,300/ac ≈ $20 million** (precisely $19,987,400; round to **$20M**).

**Two defensible step-ups** (offered so the author can pick the posture):

- **Forward-engineering floor — $37M.** 2,738 ac × **$13,500/ac** (documented light-shell/stone construction on existing shell bottom, natural recruitment; Great Wicomico Tributary Plan §5). This is the better *headline* floor: the $7,300 realized figure spans 2003–2024 un-inflated work and reads suspiciously low; $13,500 is method-explicit, primary-sourced, and still excludes seeding (honest only where natural recruitment is reliable — i.e. lower-Bay Virginia, which is exactly the Hampton/James subject geography).
- **Cherry-pick-proof central — $211M.** 2,738 ac × **$77,000/ac** (bay-wide completed-program average, construction + seeding). Use this if a reviewer says "you picked the cheapest river." It is *not* the floor; it is the robust center.

**Recommended single line for the chapter:** *"a conservative floor of roughly $20–37 million — and a fully-defensible central figure near $200 million — to physically rebuild the reef bottom foreclosed in the James alone."*

**Why NOT the substrate-volume construction.** The severed-substrate strand (Darling, 200,000 bu/peak-year) is vivid and worth keeping as *narrative*, but as a *price* it fails the methodology: 200,000 bushels × the most-defensible shell prices ($4–5/bu, USACE/MD-DNR) = only **$0.8M–$1.0M per peak year of raw shell** — and that is *uninstalled* material. Even multiplying the full ~98-year run at peak rate lands near $80M–$100M of raw shell, but that full-run multiplication is the single weakest link in all four strands (see stress-test #1). Raw-shell pricing also undersells what the bond funds (installed reef ≫ pile of shell). **Keep Darling as the human/physical anchor of severance; do not derive the dollar figure from bushels.**

## (2) THE PUBLIC-PAYS ANCHOR (the killer fact)

The reef-rebuild is *already* underway and is *entirely publicly funded* — the bond formalizes a cost the public is otherwise silently absorbing.

- **Maryland, rock-solid single source:** **>$92 million** in combined Maryland-state + federal funds to restore ~1,300 acres across five sanctuaries (MD DNR / Office of the Governor, Aug 26 2025). Manokin alone $21.6M.
- **Bi-state program:** **~$108 million over ten years** (program-level, reported) / **~$115M** summing NOAA's itemized per-tributary table (MD $93.52M + VA $21.77M) across ~2,400 acres. Funders: NOAA, USACE, MD DNR, Virginia Marine Resources Commission — **all public.**
- **Virginia / Hampton-local anchor:** the **NSA Hampton Roads** oyster-castle installation (Lafayette River Annex, July 16–17 2020) — Navy Public Works, NAVFAC Atlantic, DoD Chesapeake Bay Program, USACE, Virginia DEQ/VMRC; 500+ castles over ~90 ft; **CBP supplied the spat; no oyster-industry money.** No dollar figure published — its value is *qualitative* (public/military pays; industry absent). The Lafayette River itself shows **$716K** of public restoration cost (NOAA table).

**Recommended line:** *"The public is already paying. More than $92 million in Maryland alone, on the order of $110 million bay-wide, has gone into rebuilding reef the harvest economy took down — and at the Navy's own Hampton Roads shoreline, sailors and federal agencies are stacking oyster castles while the industry that profited from the reef pays nothing into its return."*

**Internal consistency check (use it — it strengthens the floor):** $108M ÷ 1,900 actively-restored acres ≈ **$57,000/acre** of *actual public spending*, which sits squarely between the $13,500 floor and the $77,000 average. The public-pays evidence independently corroborates the cost-per-acre band the floor is built on.

## (3) ADVERSARIAL STRESS-TEST

**Attack 1 — "You picked the cheapest river ($7,300) and the gentlest loss figure (39%). Cherry-picking."**
*Survives, by design.* Cherry-picking the *cheap* unit price and the *conservative* loss figure both push the number DOWN — that is the whole point of a floor. The honest framing is: "Even using the cheapest documented river and the smallest defensible areal loss, the bill is $20M; the realistic figure is multiples higher ($211M at the bay-wide average)." A floor that errs low is unimpeachable. *Retreat only if* a reviewer insists the floor must use a *realized multi-acre fully-audited* cost — then hold at $7,300 (Great Wicomico is realized and multi-acre) with the natural-recruitment caveat stated.

**Attack 2 — "Apples-to-oranges: $7,300 Great Wicomico relies on free natural recruitment; that's not a generic restoration cost."**
*Partial retreat, then survives.* True — the ~10× VA-vs-MD spread ($7,300→$94,800) is *method* (seeding), not noise. **Defense:** the chapter's subject is the lower Bay / James / Hampton, where natural recruitment *is* reliable and substrate-only restoration *is* the standard method (Great Wicomico, Piankatank, James all restore this way). So the natural-recruitment unit price is the *correct* one for this geography, not a cherry-pick — but it must be stated explicitly. If forced off it, fall to the $13,500 forward estimate (still natural-recruitment, but engineering-grade and method-explicit).

**Attack 3 — "The '99% of reefs are gone' you'll be tempted to cite is biomass/structure, not area. Your area only fell ~39%."**
*Survives only if you keep the measures clean.* Three distinct things: (a) **abundance** ~99.7% gone (Wilberg 2011, Maryland upper-Bay) — biomass; (b) **areal** oyster-rock extent ~39% gone in the James (Schulte/Haven) — the floor uses this; (c) **emergent 3-D structure** ~99% gone (Woods 2005, James: ~30 ac remain of >2,471). The floor deliberately uses the *most conservative* measure (b). **Do not** let the chapter multiply the dramatic 99% by an acre price — that conflates structure-loss with area-loss and a reviewer will catch it. If the author wants the "near-total loss" emotional punch, cite the emergent-structure 99% *as structure* and note the floor is built on the more conservative areal 39%.

**Attack 4 — "These per-acre figures exclude surveys, planning, permitting, monitoring — so they understate true replacement cost."**
*Survives; helps you.* Every government figure excludes those (NOAA/CBP state it). Loading them in pushes the number UP, so it can never threaten a *floor* — disclose it as "true social cost is higher than this floor." Free credibility.

**Attack 5 (only if you use the Darling/volume number) — "200,000 bushels every year for 98 years is fiction."**
*Indefensible; do not publish the full-run product.* 200,000 bu is a *single representative/peak year* (LVA: "In one year…"); the same source documents catastrophic pollution losses 1926–1933 and industry deterioration after. Multiplying ×98 assumes peak output across the entire run, which the record contradicts. **This is the single biggest vulnerability in the corpus** and is the main reason I route the dollar figure through *area* (strand 2), not *volume* (strand 3). Keep Darling's 200,000 bu / 50,000 gallons / four-story shell-pile / 160 shuckers / "crushed for roads and driveways" as the *physical-severance narrative*; anchor any number to a single documented year if you must quantify it.

## (5) PLAIN-ENGLISH STATEMENT OF THE FLOOR (for author reaction)

> The Restitution Bond prices one thing only: the physical reef bottom the oyster trade stripped out and never put back. Take the most cautious numbers available — the smallest documented loss of reef (about 2,700 acres of high-quality oyster bottom gone from the James River between 1910 and 1981, a peer-reviewed figure) multiplied by the cheapest price anyone has actually paid to rebuild an acre of it (about $7,000–$13,500 an acre on Virginia bottom where oysters still set on their own). That puts the floor at **roughly $20 to $37 million** to physically restore what was foreclosed in the James alone — and a fully mainstream figure, using the bay-wide average rebuild cost, lands near **$200 million**. The bond doesn't pay anyone; it pays *into the reef*. And here is the part that makes the case unanswerable: that reef is **already being rebuilt right now, entirely with public money** — more than $92 million in Maryland, on the order of $110 million bay-wide, with Navy crews and federal agencies stacking oyster castles on the Hampton Roads shoreline while the industry that profited from the reef contributes nothing. The bond doesn't invent a new bill. It names who should have been paying it all along.

**Honest bottom line for you:** the defensible floor is *smaller and area-shaped*, not volume-shaped. The Darling shell-mountain is your best *image* of severance but your *worst* source of a number — keep it as narrative, build the dollar figure from foreclosed acres × realized restoration cost, and lead with the killer fact that the public is already paying.

### public-funding-confirmation
**Best estimate:** The reef-rebuild cost in the Chesapeake is borne by the public, not the historical oyster industry. The single most-defensible, conservative figure is the Maryland program total: more than $92 million in combined Maryland-state and federal money to restore ~1,300 acres across five sanctuaries (Maryland DNR / Office of the Governor, Aug 2025) — a clean, on-the-record state+federal attribution. For the full bi-state 10-tributary program, the most-defensible conservative whole-program figure is $108 million over ten years from public agencies (NOAA, USACE, Maryland DNR, Virginia Marine Resources Commission); NOAA's own itemized per-tributary table sums slightly higher to ~$115M (MD $93.52M + VA $21.77M). The NSA Hampton Roads oyster-castle installation (July 2020, Lafayette River Annex) is confirmed public/military-led — Navy Public Works, NAVFAC Atlantic, DoD Chesapeake Bay Program, USACE, and Virginia state agencies — with no oyster-industry funding, though that specific installation carries no published dollar figure. Use '$92 million+ in state and federal funds for Maryland alone' or 'roughly $108 million in public money over a decade' as the hostile-reviewer-proof floors.
