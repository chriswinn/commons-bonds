# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 2: The Miner

---

In May of 1960, John F. Kennedy drove through McDowell County, West Virginia, and what he saw changed the direction of his campaign. He had come to win a primary in a state he was expected to lose. What he found did not fit the story the country was telling about itself that year, the story of postwar plenty, of a nation that had beaten the Depression and then the Axis and was now growing its way toward something permanent and good.

He saw children with the swollen bellies of hunger. He saw families eating surplus commodities handed out right there in the hollows, because there was nothing else to eat. He saw men who had mined the coal that powered the cities and forged the steel that won the war sitting idle on porches, because the machines had come and the companies had no further use for them.

Kennedy was not a man given to sentiment in his public remarks, but four months later, in Canton, Ohio, he came back to what he had seen. McDowell County, he said, mines more coal than it ever has in its history, and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than in any county in the United States.

He meant it as an indictment of neglect, and an indictment was the right instrument. He had the wrong defendant. A man stood in the richest coal county in the world, looked at the poorest people in it, and named the crime correctly while pointing at the wrong party in the dock. He was not careless. He was looking at a real injury and reaching, as everyone reaches, for the nearest available explanation: somebody, somewhere, not doing their job. But the injury in front of him was produced not by anyone failing at a job but by the job working exactly as designed. The defendant he needed has never been summoned by any court, because it is neither a company nor a culture but an instrument that does not exist.

---

The last chapter ended with a ledger whose columns were empty. I said then that I could only point the columns out, that the numbers were yours to enter. This chapter enters the first of them, and it gives the thing I have been pointing at a name.

To enter the first number, you have to know what McDowell County was before it was a place Kennedy drove through.

At its peak, in 1950, the county was home to nearly a hundred thousand people. The towns had names: Welch, Keystone, Kimball, War. They had what towns are supposed to have, department stores and theaters, hotels and newspapers, a civic life thick enough that Welch called itself the little New York of the coalfields and was not entirely lying. The county sat on top of some of the richest metallurgical coal seams in the world, the kind of coal that makes steel hard enough to carry the load of the Golden Gate Bridge, the frame of the Empire State Building, the hulls of the aircraft carriers that turned the war in the Pacific. During the Second World War, McDowell County coal did more than heat homes. It armed a civilization.

The men who mined it were proud of that, and the pride was earned. The work was skilled and dangerous and it mattered, and the men who did it knew it mattered. Everything that follows is heavy enough to be mistaken for a verdict on those men, and it is a verdict on an arithmetic instead: who paid for what, who did not, and what the ledger looks like when all the columns are finally added.

The value that came out of McDowell County's mountains dispersed across a continent, to the steel mills and the streetcars and the apartment buildings, and settled into accounts a long way from the hollows. In the last chapter a man generated value that went up and out and settled into accounts that were not his. McDowell County is the same sentence written at the scale of a place and a century. The cost stayed home.

---

Robert Bailey spent thirty-six years underground. He started in McDowell County and finished in Boone County, after McDowell's own mines closed and there was nowhere nearer to go. He described what the work had done to his lungs in five words. It is almost like drowning, he said.

By the end he needed a double lung transplant. By then his employer had declared bankruptcy, and the legal obligation to pay for his care had dissolved along with the corporate shell, the way a debt dissolves when the thing that owed it stops existing on paper. The federal Black Lung Disability Trust Fund covered the cost instead, which is to say the rest of us covered it, a thread this chapter picks up shortly. In February of 2019, twenty-eight days after his last interview with West Virginia Public Broadcasting, Robert Bailey died of a cancer brought on, in part, by the anti-rejection drugs that were keeping the transplanted lungs alive. The medicine that let him breathe was helping to kill him. No invoice has a column for that.

He had gone to Washington and testified before Congress, twice, about the backlog of black lung claims still waiting to be heard, men suffocating in line. What he wanted from those trips was not complicated. Most laws across the nation, most laws have been written with blood, he told Jessica Lilly, who hosts Inside Appalachia. I don't want the things I went through just to drop and no knowledge to be gained, no people to be helped.

He could not bill the drowning back to anyone. There was no invoice to send, and no court that would recognize the debt once the company that owed it had ceased to be. He had spent thirty-six years turning his lungs into the price of everyone else's cheap steel, and at the end, the party that had collected the value was legally gone, and the bill came to a man lying flat in a chair so he could breathe.

---

The mechanization came in waves, and each wave took more men than the one before it. There was no single catastrophe, only a long, grinding subtraction that ran from the 1950s into the early 1990s, mine after mine, machine after machine, until there was almost nothing left to mechanize. When the U.S. Steel mine at Gary closed in 1986, it took more than twelve hundred jobs with it, and it was one closure among many across that decade. The bottom never fell out in any single year. It bled.

The income figures show the bleed if you look. After the Gary closure the county's personal income fell by about thirteen percent in a single year, and by roughly fifteen percent across the stretch from its mid-decade peak, as the layoffs spread and the paychecks stopped. But the deeper story is in the leaving. When the work goes, people go to find work, if they can, and the ones who can go first are usually the ones with the most to offer somewhere else. Each person who leaves takes the small weight they added to the local economy, the rent they paid, the groceries they bought, the taxes that kept a school open, and the next person finds it a little easier to justify leaving too. It compounds. It becomes a current.

In 1950 McDowell County held 98,887 people. By 2020 it held 19,111. The income bled out across forty years right alongside them. The towns that had department stores and theaters now have empty lots where the buildings used to be, and in many cases they are lots rather than ruins, because the buildings were pulled down when no one was coming to use them, and grass grew over where the little New York of the coalfields had stood.

What remains reads like this. A male life expectancy of about 63.5 years, against a national figure for men of about 76.5. Thirteen years is not a gap you close with a better attitude or smarter personal choices. Thirteen years is what happens to bodies when costs accumulate across generations and are never relieved. The drug death rate in McDowell County runs around 141 per hundred thousand, close to ten times the national rate of roughly sixteen per hundred thousand from the same period. The poverty rate is about 37.6 percent. Median household income is about $28,235. The county ranks fifty-fifth of West Virginia's fifty-five counties in health outcomes, which is to say last, which is to say there is no further down to go. The first food stamps issued under the modern program's 1961 pilot were handed out here, in this county. The current numbers suggest it has not stopped needing them since.

The numbers are real, but they are not the argument; they are what the argument has to explain.

### What a Ton of Coal Actually Cost

What was a ton of McDowell County coal actually worth? The question sounds simple and is not, because worth has two readings: what the coal sold for, and what it cost.

The price was real. At the mine mouth around 1960 it ran just under five dollars a ton, and that price reflected what a buyer would pay and a seller would accept at the moment of the sale. The transaction was real and voluntary and, in its own terms, honest. There was no fraud in the price, only everything the price left out: what happened before the coal came up out of the ground, what happened to the men who brought it up, and what would go on happening for decades after the coal was burned and gone. The honesty is exactly the problem. A price can be perfectly honest about the present transaction and silent about the future one, and the people who would have done the future's bidding, the ones not yet born who would inherit the burned air, cannot raise a hand at the auction. That a transaction can be voluntary on its face and still bind parties who never consented, because they did not yet exist to consent, is a contradiction Chapter 5 takes up squarely. This chapter only adds up the columns, one at a time.

This is the ledger Kennedy could not read.

---

**The man who developed black lung.**

Black lung disease, coal workers' pneumoconiosis, is what happens when coal dust collects in the lungs across years of underground work. It arrives the way a debt arrives, slowly and invisibly, until the day comes when you can no longer pretend not to owe it.

In the central Appalachian coalfields, in Kentucky and Virginia and West Virginia, roughly one in five miners with twenty-five or more years underground carries black lung. Nationally the rate among long-tenured miners exceeds one in ten. The disease costs each man it kills somewhere between eight and thirteen years of life he would otherwise have lived. The rates are no accident of luck; they are the predictable, statistically regular consequence of bringing coal up by the methods used in McDowell County for a hundred years. You could have written the number down in advance. People did. The pathology was documented for decades while the industry disputed it.

And the disease is coming back. After decades of dust regulation it should be in retreat. Instead it is rising, fastest among younger miners, men with less than twenty years in, some of them developing the most severe, rapidly progressive form. It is reaching the generation that was supposed to have been protected from it.

The federal Black Lung Benefits Program was set up in 1969 to compensate sick miners and their survivors. It has paid out around forty-four billion dollars in benefits, carried a debt of several billion in its trust fund, borrowed from the federal general fund almost every year since 1979, and is projected to owe roughly fifteen billion by 2050. Forty-four billion dollars, paid to men whose lungs turned to stone, to the widows who watched them suffocate, to the families who spent the last years learning to breathe around grief, and it was not enough. The fund built to make the accounting honest has run a deficit for more than forty years. That same fund paid for Robert Bailey's transplant after his employer's obligation dissolved. The liability did not disappear when the company did; it moved. Around 2014 to 2016, three large coal companies went bankrupt, and roughly $865 million in their black lung benefit obligations moved, in a single stroke of corporate law, onto the public.

Someone is paying the difference, and it is not the coal companies.

---

**The creek running orange.**

Drive through McDowell County and you will pass creeks that run colors a creek should not run: orange from iron sulfide, gray from sediment, some of them simply dead. This is acid mine drainage. It began when mining cut into sulfur-bearing rock and exposed it to air and water, and it will keep running for decades after the last mine has closed, because the chemistry does not stop when the digging stops. The mountain goes on bleeding rust into the water long after anyone is left to profit from the mountain.

The Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977 was supposed to handle this. It required companies to post reclamation bonds, money set aside up front to restore the land once the extraction was done. The theory was clean and correct: whoever creates the liability funds the cleanup. Price it in. Make the accounting honest.

The practice did not match the theory. Across Appalachian coal country, more than 633,000 acres still need environmental remediation. The estimated cost of the cleanup runs somewhere between $7.5 billion and $9.8 billion. The bond money actually set aside totals around $3.8 billion. The gap, somewhere between four and six billion dollars, is neither a rounding error nor a paperwork problem; it is the difference between what the industry promised to clean up and what it actually funded, and it sits in the orange water, waiting.

---

**The community that emptied out.**

One cost shows up in no trust fund and on no reclamation bond: the cost of a community ceasing to function. Schools go unfunded when the tax base collapses; roads go unmaintained; hospitals close because too few patients can pay; the municipal services degrade year by year as the people who would have paid for them leave. A town does not die in an afternoon. It thins, and then it thins faster, and then one year the last grocery store closes and there is no replacing it.

None of this is damage in the legal sense, and that matters. No single company is liable for McDowell County's collapse; the causal chain is too long and too distributed for ordinary liability to attach anywhere. The mine closure that started the income slide was a legal business decision, not a tort. The company had no legal obligation to fund the community's transition to whatever came next, because there was no next, and nobody had ever promised there would be.

That is precisely the point.

When the coal came up out of the ground it carried an implicit promise that nobody wrote down: that when the coal ran out or stopped paying, the community that had organized its whole existence around bringing the coal up would have something to organize itself around instead. The promise was never priced and never bonded. There was no contract to write it into, because the future of a place was not the kind of thing the system knew how to put a price on.

Costs that are not priced are still costs. They still get paid, and someone still pays them. What went unpriced in McDowell County was the future of the place itself, the bodies of the men, the institutions of the towns, the odds of the children, the soil and the water and the air. The plain accounting name for this is subsidy. The community subsidized the coal industry; the miners subsidized their employers; the future subsidized the present. The whole cost of the transition was absorbed by the community, alone, and it is absorbing it still.

---

**The children who did not come back.**

The people who left McDowell County did not leave because they wanted to. Most left because there was nothing left to stay for, and they took with them the education their parents had funded, the skills they had built, the years of forming a person that had been paid for by families who hoped, more than anything, that those children would have a future close to home.

This is a real cost, and there is nothing sentimental in calling it one. Every dollar a family puts into a child is an investment in a person who will one day produce value somewhere: every year of school, every piano lesson, every Little League uniform, every meal cooked at seven in the morning before a shift so a child can eat before the bus comes. When that grown child leaves to find work, the formed value leaves too and goes to work in some other county's economy, and the place that paid for the forming gets no return on it. The morning meals were cooked in McDowell County. The value they grew into is being spent in Charlotte and Columbus and a hundred other places that never bought a single one of those breakfasts.

This is the lineage dimension of the cost: not only what was taken from the people who stayed, but what was taken from the generations who would have built whatever came next, if anything had been left to build it on.

**The one sentence we will return to.**

None of these columns includes what happens when the coal is burned. We will return to that.

### The Vocabulary

Chapter 1 made you feel a thing this book had not yet named. Every chapter after this one will use the name, so it gets set down here, against the place that shows it plainly.

**Cost severance** is the mechanism by which costs are cut loose from the transactions that create them and land on parties who took no part in the value extraction. The coal company captured the value when it sold the coal. The miners and their families and their towns bore the cost: the black lung, the orange water, the empty lots, the children who left. The severance is the structural gap between those two parties, between who got the money and who got the bill.

The noun is **severed cost**: a cost that has been detached from its source and set down on a third party. The four-to-six-billion-dollar reclamation gap is a severed cost. The black lung trust fund deficit is a severed cost. The thirteen-year life expectancy gap is the human body absorbing a severed cost, one breath at a time.

The paired motion is **value extraction**: the act of separating worth from its source while declining to carry the full cost of the separation. Value extraction is neither theft nor fraud, and in most cases it is perfectly legal; it is simply what happens when the accounting system does not require honest pricing, when a price can stay silent about everything it leaves out and still clear the market.

These three terms, cost severance and severed cost and value extraction, run through this entire book. They are not decoration; they name a mechanism that works the same way whether we are talking about coal mines in West Virginia, oil wells in the Niger Delta, copper in Zambia, or oysters in the Chesapeake Bay. The phrase value extraction carries a lineage worth naming once. Mariana Mazzucato, in The Value of Everything, draws a hard line between making, which adds to what exists, and taking, which only moves existing value into new pockets. Her cases live mostly in finance and pharmaceuticals. What this book tries to add is the extension of that distinction into physical extraction, into coal and water and air, where the taking lands on specific bodies in specific places, and where you can build an instrument to price it.

That instrument is the work of later chapters. The names had to come first. You cannot fix a thing you cannot say, and Kennedy could not say this one.

### Distance

The value that came out of those mountains was not used in those mountains. It powered Pittsburgh's steel. It lit Chicago's streetcars. It heated New York apartments. It built the ships launched out of Norfolk. And the people who used it never saw McDowell County. They never met a miner or drove past an orange creek. They did not know, in any concrete way, that the cheap energy running through their ordinary days was being paid for in a body and a place four hundred miles off.

In the last chapter there was a man who climbed to the highest point in every city he visited, because you cannot see the shape of a place from inside it. The consumers of McDowell County coal were that man's opposite. They never climbed high enough to see the place their warmth came from, and the transaction was built so that they would never have to. This is spatial cost severance, the geographic gap between where the value is taken and where the cost is borne. Distance made ignorance possible, and ignorance made the accounting invisible. The consumers were not villains. Most of them would have been sick at the sight of McDowell County if they had ever been brought to see it. They were not brought to see it.

Distance is one of several ways a cost stays hidden from honest accounting. Chapter 7 walks through the others, and through what happens when all of them collapse at once. McDowell County shows the plainest one: a cost can disappear without a hundred years of delay, on distance alone, so long as the people who benefit never have to look.

Ted Latusek worked for Consol Energy in northern West Virginia, and he was proud that his crew outmined the others two to one. A lot of times, you couldn't see your hand in front of your face, he told Chris Hamby of the Center for Public Integrity in 2013. It felt like somebody had put a bag over my face.

By the time he said it, he had been fighting for his federal black lung benefits for nineteen years. Each time he won at an administrative stage, the company's lawyers appealed. My goal in life was to outlive Consol, he told Hamby. I thought that I had. And then it goes to the Fourth Circuit Court, and they take it all away from me.

The disease was almost the part he had made his peace with. What he never made peace with was the discovery that the loyalty had only ever run one direction, and that the system had a name for what he was owed and no intention of paying it without a war.

### The Mechanism, Not the Motive

McDowell County does not prove that the coal companies were evil. Some of the men who ran them were callous, and some were genuinely trying to do right by their workers inside the constraints they faced, and the difference between those two kinds of men matters enormously to the men themselves and almost not at all to the ledger. Cost severance runs on accounting, not on cruelty. A kind operator and a cruel one, working the same un-pricing system, produce the same severed cost, because the cost is severed by the structure of the transaction, not by the temperament of the party on the profitable end. In the last chapter there was no villain at the leasing office. There is no villain here either, or at least the villainy, where it exists, is not the engine. The engine is the missing price.

Neither does the county prove that industrialization was a mistake. Cheap coal was essential to industrialization, and industrialization pulled an enormous share of humanity up out of poverty, and McDowell County coal was part of the system that did it. The men who brought it up had something to be proud of, and I will not take that from them. The question is not whether the extraction should have happened but whether the people who bore its costs should have been compensated for bearing them. Those are different questions, and folding the second into the first is the exact move that hides the severance. You can believe industrialization was worth doing, as I do, and believe at the same time that the man who drowned slowly in his own chest and the town that emptied out should have been made whole. If anything, believing industrialization was worth doing makes the case for compensation stronger, because then the people of McDowell County subsidized a benefit the whole world collected, and making them whole is not charity but the settling of a debt.

What the cheap-energy argument passes over is that the cost-bearing of specific, named, identifiable communities was treated as an acceptable price for a benefit spread thin across everyone, and nobody asked those communities, and nobody compensated them, and there was no mechanism by which they could have declined. The miner who developed black lung did not sign up to carry the cost of industrialization on behalf of the planet. He signed a wage contract with a coal company, and the transition costs that came when the mine closed were not in it. Nobody told him what the dust would do, either, though the industry knew; the pathology had been documented for decades while companies disputed the science with the same playbook the tobacco companies would later make famous. Consent means something only when it is informed.

And the county does not prove that its mining communities were helpless or passive. They organized and they struck; they fought for wages and safety and benefits, and they won many of those fights. The United Mine Workers built that fight across a century. The Black Lung Association's wildcat strike across West Virginia in 1968 won the state its pneumoconiosis statute and helped push the federal Black Lung Benefits Program into existence the next year. Miners for Democracy took the union back after the Yablonski murders. The entire regulatory architecture this chapter has been describing exists because miners built it with their own hands and their own bodies and, in more than one case, their own funerals. The agency was real, and honoring it is not optional, because a framework that treated working people as passive victims would miss the actual mechanism. The mechanism operated on people who were fighting it the whole time.

But the agency had structural limits. At the industry's peak in McDowell County the coal company was not one employer among many that a man could choose between. It owned the land, the store, the house the miner rented. When the alternative to staying was leaving behind everything and everyone you had ever known, the choice to stay was not the free, unconstrained choice that market theory assumes when it pronounces a wage fair. In the last chapter I wrote that I had options a coal miner does not have. This is the miner I meant. I had rare skills, several possible employers, the freedom to move, significant pay, and even with all of that I still got pulled where the pattern pulled me. The miner had none of it. A constrained choice is still a choice, but it is not a free one, and the wage that fairly compensates a free choice is not the wage that fairly compensates a constrained one.

What McDowell County does prove is that the accounting system those transactions ran through was structurally incapable of pricing what was actually being exchanged. The price captured the value of the coal and nothing else: not the town's long-term life, not the men's biological futures, not the transition costs that would fall on the next generation, not the cleanup that would transfer to taxpayers. Those costs were real. They were foreseeable, not in every detail but in aggregate, their existence plain. They went unpriced because the system had no instrument for pricing them.

No villain is required for any of it. What is required is an absence, the instrument that does not exist, and the argument of this book is that we have to build it.

### The Second Cycle

If the story of McDowell County ended with the mines closing, the accounting would already be wrong, but it would at least be closed: the coal extracted, the costs borne, the community left to recover or not. It did not end there.

In the 1990s, when Purdue Pharma built a national sales campaign around OxyContin, the promotion was not aimed at random. We know this from two different kinds of record, and the truth is strong enough that keeping them straight costs nothing. The litigation record, the unsealed complaints and the internal documents pried loose in lawsuits like Massachusetts against Purdue, shows the company deliberately targeting high-prescribing physicians and marketing into what one of its own documents called a large unmet need for a vulnerable, underserved, and stigmatized population. The consulting firm McKinsey, in documents that surfaced through the settlements, advised Purdue on how to, in its words, turbocharge sales and evolve the prescriber base to higher volumes. That is the part the companies wrote down themselves.

The other record is the one researchers and prosecutors reconstructed after the fact, and it is just as damning. A 2009 study by Art Van Zee in the American Journal of Public Health documented that Purdue's 1996 sales push moved hard into economically distressed areas in which mining and logging had already taken their toll in injuries, using prescriber data to find them. Economists later traced the same geography with hard causal methods. Lay the two records over each other and a pattern emerges, county by county: high rates of physical-labor injury, thin healthcare infrastructure, dense concentrations of high-prescribing physicians, deep economic precarity. Nobody at Purdue drew that four-factor map and filed it in a drawer. The pattern is what the evidence shows when the sales data is laid over the distress data, and it reads as a near-perfect overlay of the communities whose coal the country had already burned and whose coal economy had already collapsed. The injury rates, the disability caseloads, the thin medical alternatives, the desperation: these were the predictable leavings of the first extraction. The second wave found them and made a market of them.

This is what Nancy Fraser calls expropriation, accumulation by means other than the wage contract. The Sackler family pulled roughly eleven billion dollars out of the company before it went into bankruptcy. The three large pharmaceutical distributors, McKesson and Cardinal Health and AmerisourceBergen, settled in 2022 for twenty-one billion dollars over their role in shipping pill volumes no legitimate medical demand could explain. Purdue itself pleaded guilty to federal crimes twice, a felony misbranding plea in 2007 and three more federal felony counts in 2020. And the communities the campaign targeted are still in crisis. The figure from earlier in this chapter, 141 overdose deaths per hundred thousand in McDowell County, is the signature not of a culture that gave up but of a vulnerability that was found, mapped by inference from prescriber data and injury data, and supplied with a product engineered to create dependency.

There is a familiar story told about places like McDowell County, a story that reads the overdose numbers as cultural pathology, a failure of Appalachian character, a learned helplessness, a deficit in the people themselves. The record of the second cycle is the most direct refutation of that story available. The pattern blamed on culture follows, with precision, the strategy of an industry that identified the wreckage left by the first extraction and built a business on top of it. The culture did not choose the opioid crisis; an industry identified the crisis as an opportunity and supplied it. That is a different story, and making it visible is what the vocabulary of this chapter is for.

Cost severance is specific to no industry. The pattern appears wherever its conditions appear: information asymmetry, a vulnerable target community, weak regulation, an architecture that lets accountability slip away. The product varies; the structural function is identical. What McDowell County has lived through is not one extraction but a sequence of them, each one going to work on what the last one left behind.

### What McDowell County and the Chesapeake Have in Common

About two hundred and fifty miles from the McDowell County courthouse, there are men who go out before light onto the Chesapeake Bay to bring up oysters and blue crabs from water their fathers and grandfathers worked before them. Most of them are independent operators who own their boats and their licenses. They are proud of the work in exactly the way the miners were proud of theirs, because it is skilled and physical and necessary, because it has run in their families across generations, because knowing where the crabs run and how the currents turn in late October is knowledge that takes a lifetime to earn.

The mechanism working on them differs in its details: a renewable resource instead of a finite one, ecological cost instead of occupational, water instead of tunnels. The structure is the same. The value disperses, to the restaurants in Richmond and Washington and Baltimore, to the people who eat the crab, to the supply chains that move it. The cost concentrates, on the water, on the communities that live off the water, on the Bay itself. The accounting that prices the crab cake does not price what it takes to keep the Bay that makes the crabs.

The miner and the waterman do not know each other. The thing connecting them had no name in ordinary speech, which is most of why it has been able to run so long. It has one now: cost severance, the value going up and out, the cost settling down and staying. The next chapter goes out onto the water to watch it work there, in a place I grew up on, against people I can name. The details change. The defendant does not, and it is still not in the room, because it is still an instrument that does not exist.
