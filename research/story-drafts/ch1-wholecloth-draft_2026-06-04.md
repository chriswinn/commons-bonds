# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 1: The Quiet Math

---

The most credentialed person at a company I once worked for made about half of what I made.

I want you to sit with that the way I had to sit with it, because the sentence is easy to read and slow to understand. He was not the most credentialed person in his department. He was the most credentialed person in the building. The work the company won, the contracts that kept the lights on and kept the rest of us employed, leaned on his name and his credentials the way a roof leans on a single load-bearing wall. Take him out and the structure does not sag. It comes down. Everyone who worked there understood this, in the way you understand the weather without checking it. He was the reason we could promise what we promised. And he was paid a fraction of what he was worth, pennies on the dollar, and there was nothing he could do about it.

I do not mean he chose not to do anything. I mean there was nothing he could do.

He was not a citizen. His permission to remain in the country was tied to his job, the way a tether is tied to a stake. If he left, the tether came with him only so long as it did not break, and leaving, or pressing too hard for what he was owed, or making himself inconvenient, risked breaking it. His family was here. His children were here. The whole life he had built was on this side of a line he could be sent back across if the company decided it no longer wished to hold the other end of the tether. So he came to work. He did the work, and he did it superbly, and the company collected the difference between what his work was worth and what it paid him, and that difference was large, and it went somewhere, and it did not go to him.

I did not have a word for what I was looking at. I want to be precise about this, because it is the whole reason I am telling you any of this at all. I did not lack the word because I was slow, or because I had not been paying attention. I lacked the word the way everyone around me lacked it. We could all see him. We all knew, more or less, the shape of his situation. It was not a secret. It was simply that there was no place in our ordinary way of talking about work, and pay, and fairness, and the market, where the thing I was looking at could be set down and named. A voluntary employee, freely working, freely paid, freely able to quit. That was the language available. And the language was a lie, not because anyone was lying, but because the language had no room in it for a tether.

What I felt, standing near him, was something older than language. I felt that something was being taken. Not stolen in the way a thief takes, with a hand in a pocket. Taken in a quieter way, a way the system was built to perform smoothly and without anyone's fingerprints on it. The value he generated went up and out and settled, as a kind of weather, into accounts that were not his. The cost of that arrangement, the difference between his worth and his pay, the years of a constrained life, the knowledge that he could not leave, all of that settled down onto him and stayed there. He carried it. He could not bill it back to anyone. There was no invoice he could send. There was no court that recognized the debt. The arrangement was legal, and ordinary, and it sat in my chest like a stone.

I recognized it on sight. That is the part I did not understand at the time. I looked at this man, a stranger to me really, separated from me by language and origin and circumstance, and I felt something move in me that was not pity and was not guilt. It was recognition. I knew this. I had no idea, then, how I knew it.

---

I would learn how I knew it, eventually, but the knowing came in pieces, and the first piece had nothing to do with him.

It had to do with a lease.

When I moved to the area around Washington, D.C., the region people who live there call the DMV, I did what every new arrival does. I looked for somewhere to live, and I did the math that everyone does, which is the math of rent against square footage against distance from work. Living farther out was cheaper. Everyone knew this. I knew it. And so I found a place farther out, more room, an extra bedroom, three hundred dollars a month cheaper than anything I could find near the office. I drove the route once before I signed, late at night, when the roads were empty, and it took forty-eight minutes end to end, and I thought I understood what I was agreeing to.

I did not understand what I was agreeing to.

The first day I drove it during working hours it took two and a half hours each way. Not the drive alone. The drive to the train, the parking, the train into the city, the walk. Two and a half hours in the morning and two and a half hours at night, and forty dollars to park, until I learned that parking in the alleys and eating the occasional ticket came out cheaper than the lot. I got home that first night completely emptied of whatever optimism I had carried into the lease. I worked five days a week, six when we were busy.

Do the arithmetic with me, because the arithmetic is the thing. Five hours a day. Five days a week. Fifty-two weeks. That is thirteen hundred hours. In one year, on the cheaper apartment, I spent thirteen hundred hours sitting in a car and a train and a station, going nowhere, generating nothing, becoming no one. Thirteen hundred hours is enough time to learn a language. It is enough time to learn an instrument well enough to play for people. It is enough time to fall in love, and, if you are not careful, it is exactly enough time to fall out of it.

I had saved three hundred dollars a month. Thirty-six hundred dollars in a year. And against that thirty-six hundred dollars I had spent thirteen hundred hours of my one life, and I had not put a single one of those hours on the ledger when I signed the lease. They did not appear anywhere. They were not on the listing. They were not in the rent. They were not in the calculation that everyone does, the calculation I had done, the calculation that told me, correctly, that the apartment was cheaper. The apartment was cheaper. It was also the most expensive place I have ever lived, and it would be years before I had any way to say both of those things at once and have them both be true.

That is the moment I want to mark, because it is small and it is the hinge of everything that follows. Nobody did this to me. There was no villain at the leasing office. There was no scheme. I did it to myself, freely, with full information about everything except the one thing that turned out to matter most, which was the cost no one had put on the invoice. And once I had felt that cost in my own life, once I had spent the thirteen hundred hours and understood, too late, what they had been worth, I could not unsee the shape of it. I started to look for the missing number everywhere. Not as a theory. As a flinch. The way you favor a joint you have hurt.

Seeing became a method. Quietly, without my deciding it, I had started doing a kind of math nobody had taught me, the math of what a thing actually costs, including the part of the cost that nobody writes down, including the part that lands on someone who never agreed to carry it. I did not name it. I just started running it, under everything, all the time.

---

And then I saw it everywhere.

I saw it in the watermen of the water I grew up on. The men who work the Chesapeake, who go out before light and come back with what the water gives and increasingly with less of what it used to give, because the water has been asked to carry costs that were never put on anyone's invoice either, costs that settled onto the oyster and the crab and the men who chase them while the value floated off somewhere upstream and inland and out of reach. I will tell you about them later, properly, because they deserve a chapter and not a sentence. For now I only want you to feel the recognition arrive, the way it arrived in me, fast and without permission.

I saw it in the deepest version, the version that is the floor under all the others, the version I cannot speak honestly about any of this without naming. People whose labor was taken from them entirely, by force, for someone else's fortune. Not only their labor. Their freedom, and underneath their freedom, the thing that is even harder to name and even worse to lose, which is the freedom to choose how to live a life at all. I am the child of a place that sits on that history. I think of Harriet Tubman, who escaped slavery and became free and then went back, again and again, into the country that would have re-enslaved her, to carry others north along the secret road. The wrongness there is not a missing number on a ledger. It is the ledger torn up and the person who kept it sold. I name it here, and I will be careful for the rest of this book to never let it sit on the same line as a commute or a lease, because it does not belong on the same line, and the entire honesty of what I am trying to do depends on my keeping the weights straight. The shape rhymes. The shape is the same vector, value generated by one person and captured by another. But the weights are not the same, and they will never be the same, and I will not flatten them. Naming the rhyme is not the same as making them equal. I need you to trust me on that, here at the start, because the book cannot work if you do not.

I saw it in the worker, any worker, who generates a value that does not stay near the worker, that flows past and settles, like the weather settles, as someone else's fortune. The person who builds the thing and is paid for an hour of building while the thing goes on producing value for years into accounts that are not theirs.

I am going fast on purpose. I am going fast because that is how it actually happened, once the lens had formed. The examples did not arrive one at a time, politely, in order, asking to be considered. They arrived all at once, faster and faster, each one calling up the next, until I could not walk through an ordinary day without three or four of them surfacing unbidden. And I suspect that as you read this, as the pace of it picks up, your own examples have started to arrive too. The job you took or left. The person you watched carry something that was never theirs to carry. The thing you bought cheap that someone, somewhere down the chain you could not see, paid the real price for. You have witnessed this your whole life. So had I. The seeing is not new. What was new, for me, was that I had finally been handed a way to see it on purpose, instead of feeling it as a stone in the chest and reaching, every time, for a word that was not there.

---

I said I recognized the colleague on sight without understanding how. I understand it now. I had been handed the feeling long before I had any of the words.

The man my own son would never meet was my grandfather. The family called him Pooh, after the bear in the children's story, a thing my father did not tell me until I was already grown. He spent his whole working life at NASA Langley, in Hampton, Virginia, from the years when the agency was still called NACA, through the change to NASA in 1958, until he retired not long before he died. He was a model maker in the wind tunnels, and an inventor, and his name is in the patent record.

I want to tell you what it means to be a model maker in a wind tunnel, because it turns out to be the quiet center of this whole book, and I did not understand that for most of my life either.

A wind tunnel tells the truth about an aircraft that does not yet exist. You cannot fly the real thing to find out whether it will fly, because if the answer is no, the cost of the answer is borne by people who cannot recover from it. So instead you build the world in miniature. Wood, metal, resin, a scale model accurate to a thousandth of an inch, and you put the model in the moving air and you let the air tell you the truth. But, and this is the whole thing, the air only tells you the truth if the model was built faithfully enough for the truth to come through. A careless model gives you a confident, beautifully measured, completely false answer, and the false answer kills people. So the model maker's discipline is a specific and unforgiving one. Build it true. Build it true enough that the truth can be seen before it is too late to do anything about it. My grandfather's work had to function where being wrong is paid for by someone who cannot survive the error.

He built faithful models so the truth could be seen in time. I am, in my own way, trying to do the same thing in this book, with the same discipline, and I did not plan that. It was handed to me.

He generated value his entire career. He solved things other people gave up on. My father liked to tell about the air compressor: how he stopped by the workshop and noticed the compressor moved out of its assigned place, and asked to borrow a drill bit, and was told the bits were worn and not ready, and was told almost in passing that the compressor's engine had given out. And how, a week or two later, the compressor was running again, and my grandfather, asked what had happened, shrugged without turning his head and said he had machined new pistons and new rings and rebuilt it, and that the drill bits were good to go now too, because he had sharpened them, and he did not think anything of it. That was the shape of the man. The value he generated was real and large, and the fame and the fortune of the enterprise he served went, as it does in the patient ordinary machinery of salaried invention, to the enterprise. That is how it works. It is how it is built to work. I am not telling you anyone reached into his pocket. I am telling you the same thing I told you about the colleague, which is that the value went up and out and settled somewhere that was not the person who made it, and the person who made it carried the difference and could not bill it back, and did not think anything of it.

My father carried it forward. He was a naval architect, a civil servant who worked for the Navy, who held a high clearance and traveled on a diplomatic passport, and who was, all my childhood, tight-lipped about what he actually did. He sat in rooms with captains and admirals. What I learned from him was not subject matter, which I never heard, but a way of being in the world. He told me, when I was a boy, that even if I grew up to dig ditches for a living, I should be able to look at my work at the end of the day and know in my heart that no one had ever done it better. He said pride in your own work makes the hard days a little easier, and that knowing you did well is often enough, even when no one else will ever see what you did. He had a way of asking, before he came to inspect something I had done, whether I would be proud of what he was about to see, and never forcing me to answer, because the question did the work without an answer. He passed all of it to me quietly, the way his father had passed it to him, the way three generations of men in my family pulled long hours and generated value and watched the fortune of it settle into someone else's accounts, and did not, on the whole, think very much of it, because the not-thinking-much-of-it was itself the inheritance.

So when I stood near the colleague and felt the stone settle in my chest, I was not feeling something new. I was feeling something I had been handed before I had words for anything. I knew the shape of value flowing away from the person who made it because it was the shape of my own family, three generations deep. That is why I recognized him on sight. The bone-sense was inherited. I had been given the feeling long before I was given the words.

---

I started my own working life downstream of greatness, which is a useful place to learn humility. I was twenty-two, on a contract at the National Institutes of Health that was supposed to last three months. The Human Genome Project was racing toward completion while I was there. I make no claim on that work. It belonged to researchers I still consider among the finest minds alive. My job sat downstream of theirs. I built the things that took their research, ran it through peer review, and translated it for the people who needed it.

That sounds simple and it was deceptively hard. The same finding about a marker for some rare genetic condition had to be rigorous enough that the specialists in the field would take it seriously, and clear enough that a child living with that very condition could understand, on the same page, what had been found and what it might mean for her. There were, at a baseline, around fifteen of these versions for around fifteen different audiences, sometimes rising into the mid-twenties, running from the researchers who would take the finding and build on it all the way down to the children themselves. We were not promising those children a cure. We were telling them that people were working, and that the work might one day mean a different kind of life for children like them. There is a particular look on a child's face when she understands that someday a child might not have to be sick the way she has been sick. That look is part of why I stopped sleeping properly for years.

The project I had been hired to look at for three months had already been running for two. It had been planned for a team of around forty people, one year, about a million dollars. I am rounding down on the team, by the way. It could have been thirty-six. It could have been closer to fifty. I was aiming low even then, in my own head, reaching for the smallest honest number I could stand behind, which is a habit I did not know I had until much later, when I found I had built an entire way of thinking on top of it. By the time I arrived the project was at about a million and a half and a year past due, and the plan was to ask for another year and another million and more people. I noticed that even while people were explaining the project to me, most of their explaining was actually a defense of all the work that had been added to it. So I cut everything that had drifted away from the original scope, and three months later there was a working proof of concept, and the contract was renewed, and they handed me another project a different team had given up on, and then another, and the renewals kept coming.

I was good at it. The thing that made me good at it was not special knowledge. It was the same thing my grandfather used when he sharpened the drill bits nobody had asked him to sharpen. It was a willingness to ask the question the role I had been hired into did not include, to work at the seams the documentation had never covered, to refocus on what the actual problem was instead of the problem everyone had agreed to keep having. Years later, as a CEO, I drove up to a cable television station on the East Coast at night, the lot empty under the sodium lights, the building lit from inside, the broadcast running on a queue that would run dry in three days and then play dead air on every commercial break for the rest of the week, which would have meant refunding the revenue and likely losing the clients. The system that played the commercials worked perfectly. The system that loaded new ones into it was dark, and nobody in the building knew where the server even was. I asked the only question I have ever really known how to ask, which is whether anything had changed in the last week, and a new guy pointed at a dusty machine in the corner and said that a few days back there had been a storm and the little red light on that one had stopped blinking. He did not know what it did. Nobody there did. They were not careless. They were competent people who had inherited systems other people installed, following the startup and shutdown sequences they had been handed, doing exactly the work their roles defined. The question I walked in willing to ask was simply not in any of their job descriptions. It could not be. That is how institutions are built. I slept on the production suite floor under the mixing board in stretches, three hours and then half an hour at a time, while a part flew in from a warehouse in Chicago in the hands of a worker who said he would come if I bought him a round-trip ticket and put cash in his hand, and we were back up in three days, and when I gave the station my number for a retainer they thanked me and declined.

I tell you this not to praise the skill but to be honest about its price, because the skill and the price are the same thing seen from two sides. I billed over a hundred and twenty hours in a week for stretches that ran into months, and there were three weeks back to back, before a deadline, where I billed close to a hundred and thirty-eight hours every seven days. There are only a hundred and sixty-eight hours in a week. I delivered early. There was a stack of unopened paper paychecks on my desk. My hair grew long because there was no time for a haircut, so I started shaving my own head in the bathroom with the phone on mute during conference calls, and ate, when I remembered to eat, from a drive-through window on mute during conference calls. I woke up one morning at my desk with the laptop still open and realized I had fallen asleep mid-sentence in an email, and the thing I remember is cursing at myself, not for falling asleep, but for not having clicked send before sleep took me, because someone needed it before they woke up so they could keep the work moving. Sleep took me. I had stopped choosing it.

That pattern, the one I learned at the NIH and carried the rest of my career, the inherited willingness to absorb the whole cost of the work into my own body without thinking much of it, would one day cost me my marriage and the early years with my son.

---

I want to bring this all the way home now, to the place the ledger reaches its limit and cannot go any further.

By the time I was deep into my best years, the company I worked for told me, correctly and without cruelty, that the leave had run out, that the protected time was over, that it was time to do what I was good at again. There was no villain in this. There almost never is. The company did precisely what its protocols told it to do. My colleagues were not indifferent. The system did exactly what it was designed to do, which is the entire problem, and the entire reason I needed, eventually, to build a way of seeing it. A free-market economist would tell me, correctly, that I had made voluntary trade-offs, and I had. I had options a coal miner does not have. I had rare skills, several possible employers, the freedom to move, and significant pay. And with all of that, with every advantage the language of voluntary choice could grant me, I still ended up doing the thing the pattern had always pulled me toward. The work pulled me away from my family at the worst possible moment of my life, and I went, and I did not understand until much later that this was the cost I had spent my whole life learning not to count.

Here is the math I could not run at the time, and can run now.

I had bought a beautiful house. The beautiful house had a mortgage, and the mortgage had to be paid, and the only way to pay it was to keep doing the thing that pulled me away. I have thought, many times since, about the smaller life I did not choose. A tiny house on the coast. A cabin in the woods. A boat. Any of them would have cost so little to keep running that I could have stopped working when it mattered most and stayed afloat for a while, long enough, maybe, to be where I needed to be instead of on a plane. The beautiful house had a cost that never appeared on the listing, the same way the cheaper apartment had, and the cost was the same kind of cost, the kind nobody writes down, the kind that lands on a life and stays there. The house bought me a cage I had to keep feeding, and feeding it took the one thing it was supposed to shelter.

And here is the harder number, the one that closes the door on the comforting idea that I simply earned too little, that more money would have fixed it. In my best years, the peak of them, salary and commission together touched around four hundred thousand dollars. That was not a steady annual figure. It was the ceiling, the highest the earning ever reached. And even at that ceiling, even at the very top of everything my skill and my inheritance and my refusal to sleep could generate, it would not have been enough to cover the year and more of surgery and rehabilitation that life eventually required. Not at the peak. Not at the absolute top of what I could earn. The system that priced my labor so well, that rewarded the inherited pattern so handsomely, still could not, at its most generous to me, cover the thing a life will eventually need covered. If it could not cover me, at the ceiling, I will let you sit with what that means for everyone earning beneath the ceiling, which is to say almost everyone.

But money is not the deepest entry on this ledger, and I would be breaking my own discipline if I let you think it was.

The deepest entry is that it cost me my marriage.

I am only going to tell you my side of that, because the rest of it is not mine to tell, and I am going to hold that boundary as carefully as I held the boundary around the weights of slavery, because the same honesty is at stake. The accumulated cost, the house and the hours and the absence at the moment that most needed presence, did not stop at money and it did not stop at time. It reached all the way into the one relationship that was supposed to be the point of all the rest, and it ended it. The marriage is the truest measure of the uncounted cost, because it is the entry that no salary, at any ceiling, could ever have billed back. There is no four hundred thousand dollars that buys it back. There is no proof of concept I could deliver early. There is no question I could walk in willing to ask. It is simply gone, and it is gone because, year after year, I ran every calculation except the one that mattered, the calculation no one had taught me, the calculation that counts what a thing actually costs, including the cost that lands on the people who never agreed to carry it, including the people closest to you, including yourself.

---

I started running the numbers because I thought, somewhere underneath, that if I got the math right I would finally be able to put a figure on what all of it had cost me. I thought there would be a number at the bottom of the page.

What the math gave me instead was not a number. It was a way of seeing. It gave me the words for the thing I had felt in my chest standing next to the colleague, and the thing I had felt too late about the lease, and the thing my grandfather and my father had carried and handed down without ever quite saying. It let me see the architecture plainly enough to ask, at last, after the fact, whether the people absorbing the cost of an arrangement were the people who had ever agreed to absorb it. And it taught me the one thing I had spent my whole life refusing to learn, which is that the deepest costs are exactly the ones that cannot be billed back.

The way of seeing in this book hands you a ledger. The numbers are yours to enter and yours to calculate, because they are your costs and your life and no one else can enter them for you. I can show you where the missing lines go. I can show you the column that nobody writes down. I can show you how to count the hours, and the lives, and the freedom, and the value that floats away and settles as someone else's weather. But the entering is yours.

And some entries, fully and honestly made, still cannot be repaid.

This is not a redemption mechanism. It does not undo anything. It did not bring back my marriage and it has not, so far, brought back my son, who is somewhere in the world now, and I do not know where, and I wish I knew, and we have no contact. The work of naming what hides behind the mask is older than this book. Black American writing has carried that work for more than a century, the mask and the veil, the long discipline of seeing clearly what a system is built to keep unseen. What is new here is only the pricing of the commons. The pricing is new. The seeing is old.

It is an honest accounting. It does not undo it. It is for the ones who come after, for the costs that are still ahead of us, the ones we still have a chance to see in time, while there is still a faithful model in the moving air, telling us the truth before it is too late to do anything about it.

— End of Chapter 1 —
