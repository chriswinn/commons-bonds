# Chapter 7: On Other Worlds

Grant the objection its full weight, because it is the strongest one available. Six chapters of cases, from a coal seam in southern West Virginia to the oyster bars of the Chesapeake, have pointed at one finding: extraction is systematically underpriced, because no standard pricing framework counts the full intergenerational cost of foreclosing a commons. A fair skeptic can accept every case and still hold the conclusion at arm's length. Perhaps the finding describes Earth's particular political economy of extraction: the lobbies that write the depletion schedules, the regulators who answer to the industries they oversee, the century of accounting convention built up around keeping certain costs off the books. Perhaps the mispricing is a fact about one planet's arrangements at one moment in its history, and a different politics would dissolve it. If that reading is right, the framework is a local political critique, useful in its place and generalizable nowhere, and it deserves to be read as exactly that.

There is one clean way to test which it is. Take the instrument somewhere Earth's political economy cannot reach. Run it where there are no lobbies, no regulators, no inherited conventions, no history at all, and see whether it still returns the answers a careful planner, working from nothing but the situation in front of her, would independently call correct. If it fails out there, the skeptic is right, and the framework belongs on the shelf with the political critiques. If it holds out there, at parameter extremes where the correct answer is unambiguous, then what it says about a ton of Appalachian coal deserves a different kind of attention.

---

Mars, for a practical reason. Mars is the off-world body humanity has actually been sending instruments toward for half a century, since the Soviet Mars 3 lander made the first soft landing in 1971, and so the quantities that matter for taking anything out of its ground, the energy budgets, the water chemistry, the delivery costs, are measured rather than guessed. Nothing in what follows is Mars-specific. The conclusions belong to any small bounded system where survival depends on a few finite local resources and the distance between cost and consequence has been compressed nearly to zero: a lunar base, a free-flying station, an asteroid platform, some habitat type not yet imagined. Mars simply supplies the best-instrumented stage.

And what follows is a thought experiment, offered openly as one. The habitat in this chapter does not exist. The administrator does not exist. The consortium does not exist, and no decision like hers is pending in any year a reader could mark on a calendar. The device is a test rig for the logic of the framework, a place to watch the machinery run where every moving part is visible. Whether humanity should pursue the settlement of other worlds is a separate question, taken up later in this chapter on its own terms; the test rig takes no position on it.

One more thing before the airlock cycles. The test will end up exposing something it was not designed to find: a quiet variable the framework has been relying on since the first chapter, visible only at the moment it disappears. It gets a name once it shows itself.

---

Imagine a settlement some decades old. Ten thousand people under a line of connected pressure domes along a canyon wall: agricultural vaults glowing through the dust season, a school, a clinic, machine shops, a council chamber that doubles as a cinema two nights a week. The settlement runs on water the way a body runs on blood, and drinking is the smallest part of it. Water feeds the crop vaults. Water splits into oxygen in the electrolysis plant. Water cools the machinery, carries the waste, scrubs the air. Every liter is metered, recycled, and metered again, and the recycling loops are good, but no loop is perfect. The settlement draws down its margin a little every year, and the margin comes from three known deposits of subsurface ice, mapped a generation ago, drilled, tested, and fenced behind procedure. At the current rate of consumption across every use, the three deposits hold roughly a 50-year supply.

Give the deposits an administrator, and give her a morning. She comes through the agricultural vault at the start of first shift, where the air is warmer and wetter than anywhere else under the domes and condensate beads on the intake gratings like sweat on a glass. She walks the rows under the lamps, the lettuce and the dwarf wheat and the tomato lines that took a previous administrator a decade of seasons to get right. She passes the school corridor as the youngest class files in, her granddaughter somewhere in the middle of the line, indistinguishable at this distance from the other five-year-olds. By the time she reaches her office the daily ledger is on her screen: extraction, recycling losses, agricultural draw, electrolysis draw, the margin. She has read that ledger every working morning for eleven years. There has never been a morning when it did not balance.

On this morning there is also a proposal waiting. A trading consortium, capitalized from Earth and incorporated under the settlement's own commercial code, wants permission to accelerate extraction from the three deposits. The terms are straightforward and honestly stated. Acceleration is profitable in the near term; the consortium's investors see their return inside roughly 5 years; and the settlement's remaining water horizon falls from roughly 50 years to roughly 20. A 60 percent cut in the time the settlement has to solve its next water problem, in exchange for a near-term return that lands mostly with the consortium. Nothing about the consortium is sinister. Its officers follow the incentives in front of them, the profit is real, and the paperwork is in order. She has to answer it.

Notice what she is actually weighing. She is deciding whether to cut her settlement's water horizon by 60 percent for a near-term financial return to one consortium, knowing that the people who will live with the consequence include her own descendants, that the children she passed this morning and the crop vaults she walked through stand a few hundred meters from her office, and that the consortium will operate under different incentives once its return is captured.

The framework this book has been building gives her decision a shape. Residual commons value (RCV), formalized in the last chapter, is built from four terms, and her case lets each one be read directly off the world outside her window.

Substitutability first: the probability that an adequate substitute for the resource exists at a given future time. For the survival-critical uses of water, drinking, agriculture, electrolysis into breathable oxygen, the answer is near zero. The chemistry has no substitute at the relevant scale. The speculative alternatives in her files, atmospheric water harvesting, cometary ice imports, synthesis from rare feedstocks, are neither operational nor on any guaranteed trajectory inside the extraction horizon she is being asked to approve. She has read those proposals. They are interesting. None of them carries a date.

Stock-dependent utility: the value of each remaining unit as the stock falls. Water is abundant to an empty planet and scarce to 10,000 people with three deposits. Each remaining unit grows more valuable as the stock shrinks, and the curve does not rise politely. It steepens toward a survival threshold and then becomes a cliff. She has run the rationing drills. She knows where the cliff is.

The externality tail: the damage that continues after extraction ends. Accelerated drilling means drilling chemicals in the remaining stock, destabilization of the formations around the deposits, vented volatiles her air systems will be absorbing for years after the consortium's equipment goes quiet. And at the tail's outer edge sits a possibility her science staff will not let her dismiss: if Martian subsurface microbial life exists anywhere, it exists in places like these deposits, and extraction at speed could end it. That loss is not denominable in any currency, because the value at stake is scientific and philosophical rather than monetary.

Discount weighting: the weight on future value relative to present value. Her future people are her own population's near-future selves, the five-year-olds in the corridor, the operators who will be running the recycling plant when the consortium's investors have long since been paid. The discount rate she applies sits near zero, which is the same as saying the discount factor sits near one: the future counts at nearly full weight. Discounting a 50-year survival horizon at terrestrial cost-benefit rates would be self-destructive, and her own settlement would bear the destruction.

Every term sits at an extreme. Substitutability near zero; utility life-or-death; the stock small and fixed; the externalities potentially lethal or civilizationally significant; the future weighted essentially in full. RCV here is extraordinarily high, and the model's recommendation is unsubtle: reserve the deposits, ration consumption, price each unit on a scale that rises as the stock declines, invest in closed-loop recycling, and refuse the consortium's acceleration. The near-term return is trivial against the foreclosure cost the settlement's own people would bear.

She refuses, of course. Almost any reader, handed her ledger, refuses with her. That is worth pausing on, because it is the demonstration the test needs: a case where the right answer is unambiguous, where any thoughtful administrator of any inhabited place on any world would call refusal obviously correct, and where the instrument, run honestly, returns it.

---

Keep her at her desk and start turning dials.

Hold every parameter where it sat for her water and raise the stock: utility pressure falls, and the recommendation loosens. Raise substitutability, suppose two of the alternatives in her files carried dates and budgets: the foreclosure-cost component falls, and the recommendation loosens further. Shrink the externality tail, suppose the deposits sat in sterile rock far from the settlement's air loops: looser again. Weight the future less, suppose the settlement were a temporary research camp scheduled for evacuation within a decade: looser still.

Turn the dials long enough and the parameter space resolves into two regimes. At one combination, abundant, substitutable, clean, reasonably discountable, the recommendation converges on ordinary market pricing: the honest price approximately equals the market price, cost severance is negligible, extract freely. At the other combination, scarce, substitution uncertain, a long externality tail, the future weighted heavily for some structural ecological, intergenerational, or existential reason, the recommendation diverges sharply: the honest price runs far above the market price, cost severance is large, extract only under extreme accountability or not at all. Through all of it the formula never changes. The parameters move; the formula sits still.

Now bring the dials home. Earth is not a categorically different system from the habitat; it is the same kind of system with different settings. Coal's stock is enormously larger than the settlement's ice, and it is being drawn down with zero restoration; nothing rebuilds a seam on any timescale that matters. Coal's substitutability for generic electricity is high today, and lower than a casual reader expects for specific hydrocarbon chemistries in specific industrial uses. Coal's externality tail, the lungs, the acid mine drainage, the carbon in the air, runs on long after a mine closes. And the weight owed to intergenerational carbon harm is the precise subject of one of the longest-running arguments in modern economics: whether distant-future people count as much as near-future shareholders.

That argument has names attached. In 2006 the Stern Review argued for a pure rate of time preference near zero, on the ethical premise that future generations cannot be discounted merely for being further away in time. William Nordhaus, in the modeling tradition built around his DICE work, used a higher, market-calibrated rate, around 4 to 5 percent, on the methodological premise that climate policy must be judged against what the same capital would have earned elsewhere; his 2007 review in the *Journal of Economic Literature* is the clearest statement of the position. Stern's figure in the original exchange was roughly 1.4 percent. The gap looks small written down. Compounded across a century, it becomes a vast difference in how much mitigation counts as correct today. Beside both sits John Hartwick's rule, published in 1977: invest the rents from exhaustible-resource extraction into reproducible capital sufficient to leave future generations no worse off. Hartwick's rule operationalizes the intergenerational-equity premise Stern's framing depends on, and it is the kind of discipline the framework's accountability-bond logic instantiates. None of this lineage settles the discount-rate argument, and nothing here will pretend to. The lineage is cited to show that the question, how much do future people weigh, is unavoidable. The habitat makes the answer easy, because the future people are the settlement's own next generation, visible in the school corridor, impossible to pretend away. Earth makes it hard, because the future people are strangers, and strangers are easy to discount.

Every parameter that drove the administrator's refusal exists on Earth. The values differ; the structure is identical. The same instrument that told her to preserve the ice under her settlement says, applied honestly to Appalachian coal, that the resource has been severely mispriced for a century. That is the universality claim, stated plainly. A single case at a single extreme cannot carry it, because a single case could flatter any model built with that case in mind. Universality needs range: scenarios that differ in every dimension the model claims to read, with answers a careful planner could check independently against judgment. So run the sweep.

---

Start with iron from an uninhabited asteroid. Picture the place: a nickel-iron body a kilometer across, tumbling slowly against the stars, no atmosphere, no ecosystem, no community downwind because there is no wind and no one. Thousands of accessible bodies carry the same material. Earth iron and recycled orbital structure substitute freely for it. The externality tail is minimal, at most a debris-management obligation; stock-dependent utility is low, because nobody's survival rides on any particular rock. RCV approaches the market price of the delivered metal, and cost severance, under any reasonable orbital-debris and energy-accounting bond, is near zero. Extract freely. This is the lowest-RCV case the framework recognizes, and the model has no quarrel with the market here.

Helium-3 in lunar regolith is a stranger case. Helium-3 has essentially no current industrial use; its potential value hangs on whether a specific deuterium-helium-3 fusion cycle ever achieves net energy gain, a technology horizon nobody can date. For that hypothetical use it has no substitute, by definition. The stock is large, roughly 1 million tons spread through the regolith by the solar wind, on the estimate Wittenberg, Santarius, and Kulcinski published in 1986, but harvesting it means processing enormous volumes of regolith, energy-intensive work that is hard to reverse. The model does not pretend to know the probability of fusion. It observes instead that preserving lunar helium-3 costs almost nothing, and the Asymmetric Regret Rule from the last chapter cuts cleanly: preserve, and if fusion never arrives, nothing is lost; extract, and if fusion later proves viable, what may be the most important fuel in the solar system is gone. Preserve, wait, and reassess as the technology matures.

Common regolith on the Moon, the loose ground a construction crew would sinter into habitat walls, is the same body with the opposite answer. The material is abundant beyond any depletion horizon, substitutable by nearly any other stretch of the same surface, carries a modest externality tail of dust and operational contamination, and has negligible stock-dependent utility. Extract freely; manage the dust; build. The same location yields opposite recommendations for different resources, because the parameters differ by resource. A framework that said preserve everything on the Moon, or extract everything, would be useless to anyone actually standing there.

Volatiles from a long-period comet invert the situation toward urgency. A comet on a millennial orbit swings through the inner system, and for a window of weeks to months its water and other volatiles are within reach; then it is gone beyond anyone's planning horizon. The externality tail is near zero, no life, no community, no atmosphere to disturb. Substitutability is moderate; other comets exist. Asymmetric regret flips its sign: declining to extract permanently forecloses access to this body's material, while extracting costs little. Extract aggressively while the window is open. The framework carries no conservation bias; it reads parameters, and when the parameters favor extraction, it says so.

The ocean under Europa's ice is where the model meets its boundary. Europa's subsurface ocean may host an independent biosphere; nobody knows. If it does, the scientific and moral value of possibly destroying it is genuinely not priceable in dollars. The externality term cannot be filled with a number, and the model does not manufacture one. It reports, honestly, that it cannot price this externality; that asymmetric regret therefore dominates, because losing the only other known biosphere would be absolute and irreversible while the cost of waiting is only time; and that the extraction decision should be deferred until the science can answer the question. This is the instrument behaving correctly, naming a limit where a limit exists rather than faking completeness. Count what can be counted, and where counting fails, say so. Pricing what can be priced was never a claim that everything has a price; for the things whose worth no currency denominates, the framework defers the question of worth entirely and prices only what the structure of the decision forecloses. That discipline returns before the sweep is over, closer to home.

Rare earth elements beside an inhabited habitat carry the sweep's deepest structural insight. Suppose the settlement mines rare earths locally to make its own electronics rather than importing them from Earth. Substitutability is supply-chain-dependent: moderate if Earth imports are reliable, near zero if they are not. Utility is high; strategic independence is worth a great deal to 10,000 people on a six-month resupply line. The crucial feature is where the externality tail lands: on the miners themselves. The dust goes into their own air filtration. The chemical waste has to be managed inside a closed system with no downstream somewhere-else. Worker health draws on the settlement's own small clinic. The inhabitants breathe their own emissions. And so cost severance collapses toward zero, not because the costs are small but because the extractor and the affected community are the same people. There is no one to sever the cost to. If extraction happens, it gets priced at full internal cost, because the inhabitants will pay that full cost whether or not anyone writes it down; what they pay and what the extraction costs converge automatically. Hold that thought. It reaches Earth in a form the off-world cases have not yet shown.

One more off-world case, built deliberately to combine the pressures the others isolated, and offered in the same openly hypothetical register: rare earths on a distant exoplanet that also bears, or plausibly bears, a living biosphere of its own. The resource side pulls hard. Rare earths sit at a wide gap between what current industry can substitute for them and what a long-horizon civilization could, their magnetic, catalytic, and electronic properties standing behind applications taken up later in this chapter, so the case leans toward reserve, or extract only with extreme care. But the externality side, unlike Europa's, is not wholly beyond pricing: a living world raises a real and partly pricable foreclosure-and-contamination cost, while the deposits carry genuine civilizational value. The case sits in tension rather than at a clean boundary. The model neither refuses to price, as at Europa, nor waves the resource through, as for asteroid iron. It recommends extraction only with extreme accountability and a minimized footprint: RCV high, the cost severance reducible by a large accountability bond, B, sized honestly enough to internalize the biosphere-damage risk and to fund the footprint minimization. This is the framework in the hard middle, the regime where a large and honestly sized bond is what makes responsible extraction defensible at all.

Then bring the sweep home, to a case with no rocket in it and, more important, no extracting counterparty at all. A person in suburban Maryland signs a lease. The house is bigger and cheaper than anything near the office in Washington, D.C., and the trade is a daily commute on the order of 5 hours, round trip, which for much of the year means leaving in the dark and returning in it. No corporation extracts anything. No community is displaced. The cost-bearer chose the arrangement freely, with the destination and the travel time knowable in advance. The framework computes this case the same way it computes asteroid iron and McDowell coal: roughly 5 hours a day across roughly 250 working days a year, multiplied by the years committed, comes to thousands of hours of life. Against any defensible valuation, lost wages, lost family time, lost rest, depleted attention, accumulated chronic stress, the true price of the lease runs to many multiples of the roughly $20,000 a year saved on cheaper housing.

The mechanism that severed cost from decision was not a corporation. It was past-self underpricing future-self's time. Four of the concealment patterns this chapter is about to name were operating intra-personally, across time: temporal asymmetry, the past self deciding for the present and future selves; informational asymmetry, the lived reality never fully internalized at decision time even though every datum was available; normative routinization, the long commute so culturally normalized in American life that nobody prices it; and abundance masking, hours of life feeling unlimited until the arithmetic collapses the illusion. The Commons Inversion Test, applied at decision time, would have surfaced the lease's real price before the signature. Same instrument; no bad actor required.

And note what the arithmetic does and does not do, because the discipline is the same one the model held at Europa. Thousands of hours, priced, is a lower-bound accounting of what the lease severed and gave away. The arithmetic sets a floor under what was given up; it makes no claim about what an hour of a life is ultimately worth, any more than the model at Europa claimed to know the worth of a biosphere. The framework prices the severance. The worth of the thing severed, where worth outruns denomination, it leaves where it found it. One discipline, both cases.

From a tumbling nickel-iron asteroid to a settlement's rare-earth pit, from a living exoplanet to a lease signed at a kitchen table in Maryland: the same formula reads the same structural severance across the whole range of cases where costs go systematically uncounted, whether or not any counterparty is doing the uncounting on purpose.

---

Every case in the sweep keeps arriving at the same act, so name it again with precision. Cost severance is the act of cutting the cost of extraction loose from the profit of extraction and transferring it to people who had no say in the transaction. A transfer needs a transferee, and it needs a structural arrangement that lets value flow one way while cost stays the other.

Across the book's cases, six patterns keep producing that arrangement, alone or in combination. The list is illustrative rather than exhaustive, in the spirit of Elinor Ostrom's eight design principles for governing commons, Martha Nussbaum's ten central capabilities, and John Rawls's primary goods: observed regularities, named because naming makes them workable, with no claim that the list is closed. The operational test is the arrangement itself, not the presence of any one named pattern, and a reader applying the method to a case of their own may surface patterns this book never met. One caution before the list: an extraction practice that answers each named pattern with a surface-level intervention can leave the severance itself fully intact, and that possibility returns near the end of this chapter under its own name.

The first pattern is geographic distance. Coal leaves McDowell County and becomes electricity somewhere far away; the value lands with shareholders and consumers separated by distance and regulatory layers from the people who pay in lungs and watersheds.

The second is temporal asymmetry, the same arrangement turned along a different axis. The extractor is far away in time rather than space, and the cost-bearer is unborn and cannot bid.

The third is informational asymmetry, and the 2008 housing collapse is its clearest exemplar. The bankers and the underwater homeowners shared a country and a legal system; what severed cost from value was what one side knew, and when. A cost-bearer cannot refuse what they cannot see in time.

The fourth is power asymmetry. Coerced labor severs cost from value through coercion alone. Enslaver and enslaved often shared the same ground; the conscripted laborers at Giza worked within sight of the monuments' beneficiaries. Distance was zero and information was symmetric. What severed the cost was that the bearer could not refuse.

The fifth is normative routinization. When cost-bearing has gone on long enough to become an accepted baseline, the cost of doing business, severance operates with full information and no formal coercion, because the culture has done the work that distance and force did earlier. The miner accepts the lung damage. The town accepts the closure. The consumer accepts cheap power as honestly priced, because the cost never appeared on an invoice.

The sixth is abundance masking. When a resource looks unlimited, no one prices it, and the costs it absorbs flow continuously, uncounted. Air is the paradigm. The same dynamic ran through the ocean fisheries before their collapses, as harvest outran reproduction; through the Ogallala aquifer, as drawdown outran recharge once center-pivot irrigation spread across the High Plains from the mid-20th century; through soils, as tillage outran their regenerative capacity; through the atmosphere itself, as carbon emissions outran what air and ocean absorb. In every one of those cases the abundance was real, and the hidden thing was a rate: drawdown outrunning restoration. The word mask is doing precise work here: the hiding is structural, built into industrial and agricultural architectures that meter the take but not the regeneration, so what disappears from view is the rate, not the stock. The Technical Appendix states the pattern formally as Theorem 10.3, Abundance Masking, and the closed habitat is about to show the mask collapsing by physical construction, which no merely perceptual error could do.

---

Walk the six patterns into the settlement and watch each one fail to find a foothold.

Geographic distance collapses by construction: the drill heads and the school corridor share an air system, and there is no away for anything to be sent to. Informational asymmetry collapses: in a small closed system with direct feedback, the consumption ledger is as public as the weather, and no party to any transaction knows less than the other in time to be exploited for it. Power asymmetry collapses: the administrator's own family stands among the cost-bearers, and the settlement has no coercive infrastructure for making anyone else bear them instead. Normative routinization has not had time to form; the settlement is too young for any cost to have faded into furniture. Temporal asymmetry remains technically available, a present self can still underprice a future self, but the closed feedback loop makes the consequence visible in months rather than generations: cut the recycling budget this quarter and the margin line moves before the next council meeting. And abundance masking collapses by construction, because every unit of air and water is enumerated, and every breath and every drink visibly counts against a finite stock. All six covers, gone at once, in one small pressurized place.

Say the reveal precisely, because it is the hinge of the chapter. Severance is not impossible on Mars; it is impossible to hide. An administrator who underprices the ice still produces severance, against her own future self and her settlement's future population. What she cannot do is conceal it. The closed loop puts the cost where everyone can see it, and the framework's logic requires that what is visible be priced. Here is the quiet variable from the start of the chapter, the one the framework has been relying on without naming it: concealment availability. On Earth, severance thrives because concealment is cheap. In the habitat, severance starves because concealment is unavailable.

Now widen the lens, because the habitat exposes more than the scarcity of particular resources. Put one of the settlement's residents on a transport and land her on Earth. She would not see what we see. Where we see ordinary background, she would see infrastructure running without a budget line: free air at the right pressure and mixture, free water falling out of the sky on a schedule, free temperature regulation inside a survivable band, free continuous solar input, free gravitational and tidal stability, free magnetic shielding against the solar wind, free ultraviolet filtering. Every one of those is a maintenance system her settlement budgets for, with names of responsible engineers attached. Earth's ambient abundance is the dominant cost-hiding apparatus on the planet, and the Commons Inversion Test is the tool for finding which parts of that abundance are being drawn down faster than they restore themselves.

On Earth, the habitat's compression is not available, and cost severance hides behind some combination of the six patterns. The combination matters, because it determines which interventions can reach a given case. McDowell coal severed mainly through geographic distance plus informational asymmetry. The 2008 collapse severed mainly through informational asymmetry. The pyramids severed through power asymmetry. Modern industrial subsidy severs through normative routinization. The atmosphere's carbon-absorbing capacity was spent under abundance masking. The policy direction the later chapters develop follows from the mapping: bringing cost-bearers and value-capturers closer together makes honest pricing more nearly automatic, collapsing distance through spatial cost-severance pricing, temporal asymmetry through intergenerational accounting, informational asymmetry through transparent supply-chain disclosure, power asymmetry through institutional accountability requirements, routinization through legible pricing of the hidden baseline, and abundance masking through honest stock-and-flow accounting. Candidate instruments exist for much of this: place-based transition-cost accounting, spatial cost-severance charges, border carbon adjustments, supply-chain disclosure regimes. None of them, alone, addresses the underlying structural test, which is why a policy regime can hit each named pattern with a surface intervention and leave cost severance running underneath at full capacity.

---

The administrator never had to ask which resources deserved her vigilance; the ice chose itself. A planner holding a whole planet's inventory has to ask, and the sweep supplies the answer's shape: the framework does not treat all resources the same, because its parameters do not. For some resources RCV collapses to near the market price, and market pricing is approximately right. For others it runs orders of magnitude higher, which is severe systematic underpricing. The difference is a property of the resource, not a choice by the analyst.

The property has a name: the existential substitutability gap, the difference between a resource's substitutability for current industrial applications and its substitutability for the possible long-term civilizational applications, known and unknown, that a species with a long planning horizon would want to keep options open for. In the notation of the framework's Substitutability Function S, the gap is S_max(industrial) - S_max(existential). The concept draws on the existential-risk literature, Nick Bostrom's early framing in 2002, Toby Ord's *The Precipice* in 2020, William MacAskill's *What We Owe the Future* in 2022, as pricing apparatus for resources critical to civilizational continuity, while adopting none of that literature's population-ethics or total-utilitarian commitments.

For coal, the gap sits near zero. Electricity is readily substitutable today, by solar, wind, nuclear, hydro, geothermal, and gas, and no foreseeable civilizational application uniquely needs coal; sand, common iron ore, and common stone sit beside it at the bottom of the scale. Conventional oil, copper, and zinc sit a step higher but still low: substitutable for most current uses, with no clear civilizational application that uniquely requires them. Uranium, lithium, and cobalt occupy the middle of the scale. Thorium and eventual fusion stand behind uranium baseload; sodium-ion and solid-state chemistries are advancing alongside lithium; iron-phosphate cathodes already relieve part of cobalt's burden; yet all three carry important strategic roles, and future applications plausibly, though not certainly, depend on these specific materials. The high end belongs to the rare earth elements and the platinum-group metals, whose specific magnetic, catalytic, and electronic properties stand behind technologies not yet invented that almost any advanced-manufacturing civilization will need: neodymium and dysprosium for permanent-magnet motors at usable energy densities, platinum and palladium for fuel-cell and electrolyzer chemistries, gallium, germanium, and indium for next-generation semiconductors. Substitutes exist for some of their current uses; no clear path leads to substitutes for all of them. And helium-3 sits in a category of its own, conditionally high: one of the most strategically important resources in the solar system if deuterium-helium-3 fusion becomes viable, a curiosity if it does not, its classification contingent on an undemonstrated technology.

The ranking is no complete reserve-or-extract map by itself. It is the input that, combined with each resource class's stock estimate and externality tail, produces the reservation policy the later chapters develop. The claim made for it here is modest: it returns answers a careful reader will recognize as intuitively right. The things worth worrying about reserving for descendants are the things whose gap runs high, because those are the things descendants might need and cannot replace.

Chapter 9 returns to this ranking when it asks what the renewable-energy transition's policy architecture must accomplish. The high gap of rare earths, platinum-group metals, cobalt, and nickel, the inputs to the motors, magnets, electrolyzers, fuel cells, and battery cathodes the transition runs on, is the reason getting the transition wrong on these specific resources would foreclose the very civilizational capabilities the transition is meant to deliver. The framework's pricing logic and its policy logic meet, in Chapter 9, on that list.
