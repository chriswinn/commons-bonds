## (1) Ratified fact ground + canonical figure table

### What this chapter is

Chapter 7 is the book's universality stress test. The book's nine other chapters argue that extraction is systematically underpriced because no standard pricing framework captures the full intergenerational cost of foreclosing a commons. A skeptic can grant every prior chapter and still object that the finding is a parochial fact about Earth's particular political economy of extraction (lobbies, regulators, and industries colluding to keep certain costs off the books) rather than a structural truth about how rational agents price scarce resources anywhere. Chapter 7 settles that objection by taking the framework off Earth and running it through a sweep of deliberately different extraction scenarios to see whether it keeps producing the answers a thoughtful planner would independently recognize as correct.

The chapter's claim, established by the sweep: the framework is not a political critique of one earthly arrangement. It is a general pricing instrument whose outputs differ across contexts precisely because the parameters differ, and it produces the right answer at parameter extremes (off-world, life-or-death, closed-system) where the correct answer is unambiguous, then applies on Earth where moderate parameters have allowed a century of mispricing to pass unnoticed. Along the way the chapter surfaces and names the variable the framework has quietly been relying on (concealment availability), shows why that variable collapses in a closed habitat, and ranks resources by how badly they must be reserved for descendants.

**The demonstration discipline (v2, load-bearing):** the chapter DEMONSTRATES instrument-hood through the scenarios; it never certifies itself. The v1-era verdict sentences ("that is what a calibrated instrument does," "this is what a calibrated instrument looks like," "instrument rather than an ideology") are DELETED as a class; see §2 Beat directives and §5 prose rules. The scenarios do the certifying.

### Beat 2: The habitat: the central thought experiment, term by term

Set the scene in the hypothetical register using the fixed contour from §1: an established, decades-old, self-sustaining habitat of roughly 10,000 people; 3 known subsurface water-ice deposits; at current consumption across drinking, agriculture, electrolysis for oxygen, and industrial use, roughly a 50-year supply. A trading consortium asks the resource administrator for permission to accelerate extraction: profitable in the near term, delivering the consortium's return within about 5 years, cutting the remaining water horizon to roughly 20 years. She must decide.

Make the stakes concrete and intimate without inventing documentary specifics: she is not adjudicating discount-rate philosophy; she is deciding whether to cut her habitat's water supply by 60 percent for a near-term financial return to one consortium, knowing the people who live with the consequence include her own descendants, that the habitat's children and agricultural domes are physically near her, and that the consortium will operate under different incentives once its return is captured. (This person-anchor opens the chapter's longest technical stretch; per prose rule 6 keep her physically present through the term-by-term walk.)

Walk the residual commons value (RCV) framework through her case, one term at a time. For each term, state what it measures and where it sits for Mars water:

- **Substitutability** (probability an adequate substitute exists at a given future time): near zero for the survival-critical uses (drinking, agriculture, electrolysis to oxygen); the chemistry has no substitute at the relevant scale; speculative long-run alternatives (atmospheric harvesting, cometary imports, synthesis from rare feedstocks) are neither operational nor on a guaranteed trajectory within the extraction horizon.
- **Stock-dependent utility** (the value of each remaining unit as the stock falls): water is abundant to an empty planet but scarce to 10,000 dependent people with 3 deposits; each remaining unit grows more valuable as the stock shrinks, the utility curve steepening toward a survival threshold and then becoming a cliff.
- **Externality tail** (ongoing damage after extraction ends): contamination of the remaining stock with drilling chemicals; destabilization of adjacent formations; venting of volatiles the habitat's air systems must absorb; and, at the outer edge, the possibility that Martian subsurface microbial life, if it exists, could be ended, a loss not denominable in any currency because the value at stake is scientific and philosophical, not monetary.
- **Discount weighting** (the weight on future value relative to present): the future is weighted almost as heavily as the present, because the future people are the same population's own near-future selves; discounting 50-year horizons at terrestrial cost-benefit rates would be self-destructive. **Direction guard (v2):** state this as the discount RATE near zero, equivalently the discount FACTOR near one. Do NOT write "the discount factor approaches zero" while meaning the future is fully weighted; in the TA's convention D(t,t₀) ∈ (0,1] and D near 1 means the future counts.

State that every term sits at an extreme: substitutability near zero, utility life-or-death, stock small and fixed, externalities potentially lethal or civilizationally significant, future weighted essentially in full. Conclude that RCV is extraordinarily high and the model's recommendation is unsubtle: reserve the deposits, ration, price each unit on a scale that rises as stock declines, invest in closed-loop recycling, and refuse the consortium's acceleration. The near-term return is trivial against the foreclosure cost the inhabitants would themselves bear. End on the point that any thoughtful administrator of any inhabited place on any world would call this obviously correct, which is exactly the demonstration the test needs.

### Beat 3: Earth is also a bounded system: one parameter at a time

Take the same model with every parameter where it sat for Mars water and change one number at a time, narrating the effect: increase the stock (utility pressure falls; recommendation loosens); increase substitutability (foreclosure-cost component falls; loosens further); decrease the externality tail (loosens again); weight the future less (loosens further still).

Show the two regimes the parameter space produces. At one combination (abundant, substitutable, clean, reasonably discountable) the recommendation converges on market pricing: honest price approximately equals market price, no meaningful cost severance, extract freely. At another (scarce, substitution uncertain, long externality tail, future weighted heavily for some structural ecological, intergenerational, or existential reason) the recommendation diverges sharply: honest price vastly exceeds market price, cost severance is large, extract only with extreme accountability or not at all. State the factual observation that the model does not change while the parameters do. **Do NOT close with the "that is what a calibrated pricing instrument does" verdict sentence (deleted refrain; §5 rule).** Let the paragraph end on the observation.

Then land the Earth point: Earth is not categorically different from Mars; it is the same kind of system with different parameters. Coal's stock is far larger than the habitat's water but is being drawn down with zero restoration; coal's substitutability for generic electricity is high today but lower for specific hydrocarbon chemistries in specific industrial uses than a casual reader expects; coal's externality tail (black lung, acid mine drainage, atmospheric carbon) persists long after the mine closes (qualitative; no dollar figures here); and the weight on intergenerational carbon harm is the precise subject of a decades-long economic debate about whether distant-future people count as much as near-future shareholders.

**Embedded discount-rate lineage (dual-audience; readable for a lay reader, precise for a specialist).** Name the debate's structure without settling it: the 2006 Stern Review argued for a near-zero pure rate of time preference on the ethical premise that future generations cannot be discounted merely for being further away in time; Nordhaus's DICE-style modeling used a higher market-calibrated rate on the methodological premise that climate policy must be judged against what the same capital would have earned elsewhere. The original exchange ran roughly 1.4 percent (Stern) against a Nordhaus rate stated as **around 4 to 5 percent, never a fixed 4 percent**. Note the gap translates, in present-value terms, into a vast difference in how much mitigation counts as correct today. Place Hartwick's 1977 rule adjacent to both: invest the rents from exhaustible-resource extraction into reproducible capital sufficient to leave future generations no worse off; it operationalizes the intergenerational-equity premise Stern's framing depends on and is the kind of discipline the framework's **accountability-bond logic** instantiates. **(v2 CORRECTION: write "accountability-bond logic," NOT "restitution-bond logic." Verified against the TA: B is the Accountability Bond; B₁ Restitution Bond is the BACKWARD sub-instrument; B₂ Foreclosure Bond is the FORWARD one. Hartwick's forward reinvestment aligns with the forward direction, so "restitution-bond" mislabels the sub-instrument; "accountability-bond" is the correct umbrella term.)** State the point of citing the lineage: not to resolve the discount-rate debate, but to show the weight-of-future-people question is unavoidable. Mars makes the answer easy because the future people are the same habitat's own next generation and cannot be pretended away; Earth makes it hard because the future people are strangers and therefore easy to discount.

Close the beat: every parameter that drove the administrator's decision exists on Earth; the values differ, the structure is identical; the same instrument that told her to preserve Martian water says, applied honestly to Appalachian coal, that the resource has been severely mispriced for a century. State the universality claim plainly, in the argument's own voice, without "this is the claim the chapter is establishing" self-narration.

### Beat 4: Running the test: the scenario sweep

Introduce the sweep's function inside the argument (one scenario proves nothing; universality needs range), not as chapter-self-narration. For each scenario, note the framework's recommendation and the parameters that drove it. Run them briskly, in this order:

- **Iron from an uninhabited asteroid.** Abundant across thousands of accessible bodies; high substitutability (other asteroids, Earth iron, recycled orbital structure); minimal externality tail (no atmosphere, ecosystem, or displaced community); low stock-dependent utility. RCV approaches the market price of the extracted material; cost severance under any reasonable orbital-debris and energy-accounting bond is near zero. Extract freely. This is the lowest-RCV case the framework recognizes. (TA coupling note for the drafting agent, not for the prose: this matches the TA §11.8 asteroid-iron calibration, where both M3 multipliers approach 1 and RCV approaches market price. The TA also notes that scarce asteroid-sourced resources such as helium-3 and rare earths do NOT price near market merely by being asteroid-sourced; do not generalize the iron result to them.)
- **Helium-3 in lunar regolith.** Essentially no current industrial use; potential value hinges on whether a specific deuterium-helium-3 fusion cycle ever achieves net energy gain (an undatable technology horizon); for that hypothetical use it has no substitute by definition; stock is very large (roughly 1 million tons in the regolith, hedged as an estimate) but extraction is energy-intensive and hard to reverse. The model does not pretend to know the probability of fusion. It observes that preserving lunar helium-3 costs almost nothing and that the Asymmetric Regret Rule (ARR) applies cleanly: preserve and fusion never arrives, nothing is lost; extract and fusion later proves viable, potentially the most important solar-system fuel is lost. Recommendation: preserve, wait, reassess as the technology matures.
- **Common regolith on the Moon, for habitat construction.** Same body, different resource. Abundant beyond any depletion horizon; high construction substitutability; modest externality tail (local dust, operational contamination); negligible stock-dependent utility. Recommendation: extract freely, manage dust, proceed. Make the teaching point in factual form: the same location yields opposite recommendations for different resources because the parameters differ by resource; a framework that said "preserve everything" or "extract everything" on the Moon would be useless. **Do NOT append the "instrument rather than an ideology" tag (deleted refrain).** The differentiation observation carries the point by itself.
- **Volatiles from a long-period comet during flyby.** The situation inverts toward extraction. The resource is reachable only during a short window (weeks to months) before the comet departs for millennia; the externality tail is near zero (no life, community, or atmosphere disturbed); substitutability is moderate (other comets exist). Asymmetric regret flips: not extracting permanently forecloses access to this specific body's material; extracting costs little. Recommendation: extract aggressively during the window. Point: the framework is not conservation-biased; it is parameter-sensitive, and recommends extraction when parameters favor it.
- **The subsurface ocean of Europa: the framework's named boundary.** Here the model meets a limit. If an independent biosphere exists in Europa's ocean, the scientific and moral value of possibly destroying it is genuinely not priceable in dollars; the externality term cannot be filled with a number, and the model does not manufacture one. It says honestly that it cannot price this externality, that ARR therefore dominates (the irreversibility of losing the only other known biosphere is absolute; the cost of waiting and studying is only time), and that the extraction decision should be deferred until the science can answer the question. Frame this NOT as a failure but as the framework behaving correctly: naming a limit where a limit exists rather than faking completeness. This is the chapter's clean statement of "count, not stop" / "cannot price the non-denominable." (Set up the Sandel-seam reconciliation here; see structural obligation 4.)
- **Rare earth elements near an inhabited Mars habitat: the deepest structural insight.** The inhabitants mine rare earths locally to make electronics rather than import from Earth. Substitutability is supply-chain-dependent (moderate if Earth imports are reliable, near zero if not); utility is high for strategic independence. The crucial feature: the externality tail falls on the inhabitants themselves. Dust contaminates their own air filtration; chemical waste must be managed in a closed system with no downstream somewhere-else; worker health draws on the habitat's own limited medical resources; the inhabitants breathe their own emissions. Therefore cost severance collapses toward zero, not because the costs are small but because the extractor and the affected community are the same people; there is no one to sever the cost to. Recommendation: extraction, if it happens, must be priced at full internal cost, because the inhabitants will bear that full internal cost whether or not it is priced honestly. What they pay and what extraction costs converge automatically. Note, briefly and without self-narration, that this insight extends to Earth in ways the off-world cases have not yet shown (sets up the commute lease and Beat 6).
- **Exoplanet rare earths on a living world.** Run in the hypothetical register: rare earth elements present on a distant exoplanet that also bears (or plausibly bears) a living biosphere of its own. This case deliberately combines the two pressures the prior cases isolated. The resource has a high gap between industrial and civilizational substitutability (rare earths' magnetic, catalytic, and electronic properties have no general substitute for the long-horizon applications Beat 7 discusses), pulling hard toward reserve-or-extract-only-with-extreme-care. But unlike Europa, the externality is not wholly non-denominable: the living world raises a real and partly pricable foreclosure-and-contamination cost while the rare earths carry genuine civilizational value, so the case sits in tension rather than at a clean boundary. The model's behavior: it does not refuse to price (as for Europa) and does not wave the resource through (as for asteroid iron); it recommends extraction only with extreme accountability and a minimized footprint, RCV high, the cost severance reducible by a large accountability bond (B) that internalizes the biosphere-damage risk and funds the footprint minimization. Teaching point: this is the framework in the hard middle, the regime where a large, honestly sized bond is what makes responsible extraction defensible. Must match the summary-table row exactly. Keep the register overtly hypothetical (no asserted exoplanet facts; the living world is a constructed test case).
- **A self-imposed commute lease on Earth (RETAINED CHAPTER STRENGTH; keep this pivot).** Bring the sweep home with a case that has no off-world body and, crucially, no extracting counterparty. A person in suburban Maryland signs a lease committing to a long daily commute (on the order of 5 hours per day round-trip) to an office in Washington, D.C. No corporation extracts; no community is displaced; the cost-bearer chose it, with the destination and travel time knowable in advance. The framework computes this the same way it computes asteroid iron and McDowell coal: roughly 5 hours per day across roughly 250 working days per year, multiplied by the years committed: thousands of hours of life. Against any defensible valuation (lost wages, lost family time, lost rest, depleted attention, accumulated chronic stress) the true price of the lease is many multiples of the roughly $20,000 a year saved on cheaper housing. The mechanism that severed cost from decision was not a corporation: it was past-self underpricing future-self's time, operating through temporal asymmetry (past-self deciding for present- and future-self), informational asymmetry (the lived reality not fully internalized at decision time even though the data was available), normative routinization (long American commutes culturally normalized), and abundance masking (hours of life feeling unlimited until the arithmetic collapses the illusion). Four of the concealment patterns operating intra-personally across time. Note that the Commons Inversion Test, applied at decision time, would have surfaced the lease's real price before signing; same instrument, no bad actor required. **Drift caution:** keep the figures to the canonical contour (§1 table); invent no new specifics; use numerals. **Sandel-seam:** apply the same-mechanism-not-same-worth deferral here (structural obligation 4): the hours-of-life figure is a lower-bound accounting of severed cost, not a claim that a life reduces to dollars.

Close the sweep with the cross-scenario synthesis: from asteroid iron through Mars rare earths and the exoplanet case to a Maryland commute, the framework prices the same structural severance across the whole range of cases where costs are systematically underpriced, whether or not any counterparty is doing the underpricing on purpose.

### Beat 5: What cost severance requires: the six-pattern concealment taxonomy

Define cost severance using the canonical noun: cost severance is the act of cutting the cost of extraction loose from the profit of extraction and transferring it to people who had no say in the transaction. (CANONICAL NOUN: write "cost severance"; never the gerund "the severing of cost.") The transfer requires a transferee and a structural arrangement that lets value flow one way while cost stays the other.

Name the six patterns through which that arrangement gets produced, alone or in combination. **Names and count must match Chapter 10's "six covers" exactly:** geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry, power asymmetry, normative routinization, abundance masking (Ch10's short forms: distance, time, information, power, routine, abundance; same set, same count, same order). Frame them as illustrative, not exhaustive: observed empirical examples drawn from the book's cases, in the same spirit as Ostrom's eight design principles, **Nussbaum's ten central capabilities (NUSSBAUM, not Sen; or drop the count)**, and Rawls's primary goods. State that the operational structural test is the arrangement itself, not whether any one named pattern is present, and that readers applying the method to their own cases may surface additional patterns. State that an extraction practice addressing each named pattern with surface-level interventions has not addressed cost severance, only simulated addressing it; forward-reference that the immune-response stress test later in the chapter returns to that simulated-reattachment dynamic.

The six patterns, each with one grounding example:

1. **Geographic distance.** Coal extracted from McDowell County and burned for electricity far away: value captured by shareholders and consumers separated by distance and regulatory layers from the people who pay in lungs and watersheds.
2. **Temporal asymmetry.** The intergenerational case: the same arrangement at a different scale; the extractor is far in time, not space; the cost-bearer is unborn and cannot bid.
