# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*

## Chapter 6: Three Ways of Counting

---

The Energy Information Administration keeps a table that runs back across the twentieth century. It sits in the agency's Total Energy series, Table 7.9, and it records what coal sold for at the mouth of the mine, year by year, in the plain dollars of each year. Find the row for 1960 and run your finger to the bituminous column. The number there is $4.71.

That is what a short ton of bituminous coal sold for at the tipple in the year this book keeps returning to. Two thousand pounds of it. Just under five dollars. The ton left the county, and the price went with it, and nearly everything else the ton would ever cost stayed behind.

Chapter 2 followed what stayed: the dust that settled in the lungs of the men who cut the coal, the trust fund built decades later to pay for those lungs, the reclamation bonds that were posted too small and forfeited too easily, the county that emptied. Chapter 3 watched the same shape on the water, a reef ground down faster than it could rebuild, until the taking consumed the instrument that made the taking possible. Chapter 4 found the one place that ran the experiment differently and built a fund where the rest of the world built a hole. Chapter 5 named the two instruments an honest ledger requires: a bond posted forward against costs not yet borne, and a restitution account run backward over costs already borne by people who can be named.

By now an honest reader has earned a suspicion, and the suspicion deserves to be said out loud. Perhaps these cases were chosen for their vividness. Perhaps McDowell County and the Chesapeake and Libby, Montana are the extraction economy's worst days, held up as if they were its ordinary ones. One demonstration is an anecdote. A dozen demonstrations are a pattern, and a pattern can still be an artifact of the picking. But a price, computed by independent methods that rest on different data and different decades of scholarship, coming out on the same side of the market price every single time the computation is run, is neither anecdote nor pattern. It is a structural claim about how the economy prices the things it takes, and it can be checked.

So the work ahead is a count, done three ways at the outer layer. The first way is bottom-up damage accounting, the oldest and most familiar instrument in the kit: enumerate the harms, price each one, add. The second is a real-options model, borrowed intact from corporate finance, the same mathematics oil majors use to value reserves they have not drilled. The third is the framework built across the preceding chapters, the residual commons value computation, run in full and shown with its gears exposed. <!-- structure-note: RCV expanded as "residual commons value" per the brief's B18 lineage hints (Hartwick residual / Ostrom commons / Mazzucato value); verify the expansion against TA canon at fact-check. -->

Honesty about what the count cannot settle belongs up front. Most of the number, in the coal cases, rides on the carbon term, and the official price of carbon has spent fifteen years as a political football; the chapter will show the kick. Substitution timelines, on which the framework's own method leans, are forecasts, and forecasts of engineering are humbling work. The product of the count is not certainty. It is structured uncertainty: ranges whose widths have named causes, and a direction that holds across every range.

Here, stated once, is what the count shows. Two independent ways of counting what a ton of the commons was worth converge, from data that share nothing, on a range an order of magnitude or more above what the market charged. The third way of counting reads what has actually been paid against that worth, and it returns two findings instead of one: the payment is a small fraction of the value, and most of it was charged to the wrong party.

### The Adding Machine

Begin where Chapter 2 left the men who cut the ton. Since 1969 the federal government has been writing checks to coal miners whose lungs stopped working, and to their widows. Between 1969 and 2009 those payments came to roughly $44 billion; the figure is the GAO's and the Congressional Research Service's, and it has to carry its date, because no authoritative cumulative total exists after 2009. The program reports annual disbursements now, so $44 billion through 2009 is a floor under a number that has kept growing for another decade and a half.

Spread that floor, and the costs that travel with it, across the national tonnage that produced it, and the health tier of a ton of Appalachian coal comes out at $2-4. The tier holds the black lung distributions; the costs shifted onto Medicare and Medicaid when miners' private coverage ran out or never existed; the cardio-respiratory harm carried downwind of the plants that burned the coal. Chapter 2 owns the mechanics of the Black Lung Disability Trust Fund and they are not rerun here, except for one figure that belongs in this ledger because it is so easy to blur with the first: the fund itself was $5.1 billion in debt to the United States Treasury as of September 2024, per the Labor Department and the GAO. The $44 billion is what has been paid out. The $5.1 billion is what the paying instrument still owes. They are different numbers about different things, and both will matter later in this chapter.

The environment tier prices what happened to the water and the ground: acid mine drainage that runs streams orange for generations after a mine closes; tailings impoundments whose liners fail on schedules their builders did not publish; headwater streams buried outright under valley fill. Priced across tonnage, the tier runs $1-3 a ton. The reclamation bonds that were supposed to prepay this tier fell short across Appalachia by $3.7-6 billion; that derivation is Chapter 2's and stands there.

The community tier is the one a damage accountant trained on smokestacks will miss, and it is the largest of the three. It does not price the jobs. The jobs were paid for; wages are a price, agreed and delivered. The tier prices what remained when the company left, and in McDowell County the company left across the 1980s, when US Steel wound down the operations that had organized the county's economy for most of a century. A county that held roughly 100,000 people in the boom years held fewer than 20,000 at the 2020 census. The schools consolidated and closed. The drug epidemic arrived into the vacancy and billed the public for it. And the bodies kept the longest books: male life expectancy in McDowell County stood at 63.5 years against a national 76.5 in the county-level estimates IHME published from the work of Dwyer-Lindgren and colleagues in *JAMA Internal Medicine* in 2017, figures vintage roughly 2013. Thirteen years, in the place the coal came from.

There is a method for pricing a gap like that, and it was not built for coal country. Applied economics learned to price legacy effects, harms that persist after the extraction that caused them has ended, in the work of William Darity and his collaborators on the Black-white longevity gap, the six-to-seven-year gap documented at the scale of the National Center for Health Statistics series across the twentieth century. That historical gap is its own subject with its own causes, and it is not McDowell's; the 13-year figure is the coal-calibrated case of the same arithmetic, a population's years of life read as a cost that someone's ledger never carried. Priced with the standard tools, the community tier runs $5-15 a ton.

Add the three tiers and the non-carbon floor under a ton of Appalachian coal comes to $8-22. Set that against the 1960 mine-mouth price of $4.71, just under five dollars, and the floor alone runs roughly two to four times the price of the ton. Set it against the contemporary mine-mouth band, which runs about $40-140 a ton by region and grade in EIA and USGS data, and it is smaller in proportion but not small. A factor, not a rounding error, and the count has not yet touched the largest term.

---

Now burn the ton, and watch the scale change.

The smoke outweighs the coal. That is not a figure of speech; it is stoichiometry. Each atom of carbon leaves the stack bound to two atoms of oxygen it picked up on the way out, so a short ton of bituminous coal, burned, becomes 2.32 tons of carbon dioxide on the national-average basis the EPA's emission factors imply. McDowell's coal runs hotter than the national average. The Pocahontas seam carries about 28 million Btu per short ton in USGS and EIA heat-content data, and the EPA's combustion factor of 93.28 kilograms of carbon dioxide per million Btu, from AP-42 section 1.1, works out to 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide per short ton of Pocahontas coal. The higher figure is a statement about heat content, not about a different rank of coal; it is bituminous coal with more energy in it, not anthracite smuggled into the arithmetic. The chapters ahead and the Technical Appendix price McDowell tons on the 2.61 basis; the national 2.32 basis is introduced here because both will appear in this book, and a reader should never have to guess which one a number is standing on.

Then price the smoke. In 2023 the EPA's *Report on the Social Cost of Greenhouse Gases* set the central estimate of carbon's damage at $190 per ton of carbon dioxide, drawing on the work of Rennert and colleagues published in *Nature* in 2022, whose own central figure was about $185. The $190 is the EPA's number, built on that scholarship; neither the agency nor the scholars should be made to wear the other's figure.

Multiply 2.32 tons by $190 and the carbon term on the national-bituminous basis is $441 per ton of coal. Multiply 2.61 by $190 and the carbon term on the McDowell basis is $496. Hold the label on that number firmly, because this book will use it many times: $496 is the carbon term alone, one component of the cost of a McDowell ton, and never the total.

The totals: on the national basis, carbon at $441 plus the non-carbon floor of $8-22 is arithmetic that lands at roughly $449-463 a ton, and it is labeled as exactly that, national-basis arithmetic, not a McDowell figure. For McDowell, the honest total across all eight component heads, assembled in full when Chapter 8 walks a single ton end to end, is $510 a ton. The narrower four-component Pigouvian floor, the components a conventional externality accounting would admit, runs about $504-518. So: $496 is the carbon term, $510 is the total, and the two numbers sit close enough together to be confused by anyone moving fast, which is why every appearance of either carries its label.

Two observations close the addition. First, carbon dominates: at $496 against $8-22, the carbon term is roughly twenty to sixty times every other tier combined, which means the honest total inherits both the size and the political volatility of the carbon price, a dependence the next section takes seriously. Second, the capture fraction: $4.71 against an honest $510 means the 1960 price captured less than one cent of each dollar of cost. More than 99 percent of what the ton cost was severed from what the ton charged, and it landed on people who were not party to the sale.

### The Same Arithmetic, Elsewhere

Just after midnight on March 24, 1989, the tanker Exxon Valdez ran onto Bligh Reef in Prince William Sound and opened her hull, and roughly 11 million gallons of North Slope crude went into some of the most productive cold water on the planet. <!-- structure-note: Valdez scene specifics (date, reef, gallons) are public record via the Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Trustee Council and DOJ; verify the gallons figure at fact-check (commonly reported ~10.8-11M). --> The fishing fleet out of Cordova watched the slick move toward the herring grounds. The cleanup crews who spent that summer on the beaches worked gravel shorelines a stone at a time, hot-water hoses against rock, while the Sound's seabird and otter carcasses came in by the boatload to be counted, because counting was suddenly the federal government's full-time work.

The market's number for what went into the water that night is knowable, and it is small: the spilled crude had a product value of about $5.5 million. Hold the qualifier, because the base matters as much as the multiple. That figure prices the cargo that spilled, at what crude fetched; it is not the lifetime revenue of an oil field, and any ratio built on it must say so.

What the spill cost is also knowable, in tiers of increasing completeness. Exxon's cleanup spending ran to about $2.2 billion, and the 1991 settlement added roughly $100 million in natural resource damage assessment, so the engineering-and-restoration count alone is about $2.3 billion. The fuller public-record accounting, cleanup plus litigation plus the ecological damages the Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Trustee Council has documented across three decades, runs to the $7-10 billion scale. Stack that against the $5.5 million product value of the oil that spilled and the bottom-up multiple is about 1,200-1,900 times, on the narrowest denominator on offer. What was actually paid is a different number again: about $1.9 billion, made of the $900 million civil settlement of 1991, roughly $507 million in private claims, punitive damages litigated for years before finishing at $507 million, and a $25 million criminal penalty. <!-- structure-note: the punitive-damages appellate history (Baker v. Exxon) is public record; verify the one-clause characterization at fact-check. --> Keep that gap in mind; the chapter returns to it with an instrument built for reading it.

Twenty-one years after Bligh Reef, in April 2010, the Macondo well let go beneath the Deepwater Horizon in the Gulf of Mexico. The revenue side of Macondo is a derivation, not a citation, and it is labeled as one: roughly 50 million barrels of reserve at the roughly $80 a barrel that crude fetched in 2010 gives about $4 billion, inside a band of $3.2-4.8 billion. The cost side comes from BP itself: the company's final estimate, published in June 2016, put its all-in pre-tax charge at $61.6 billion, and the figure had passed $65 billion by 2018, which is the number Chapter 5 carried. Beyond BP's own charges, ongoing Gulf ecosystem and fisheries externalities through 2025 add an estimated $8-12 billion that no settlement ever touched. On the damage-function lens, the gap multiple is about 15-16 times.

Libby, Montana is the smallest revenue line in this book and one of the largest human ones. The commodity was vermiculite, and W.R. Grace's mine above the town produced perhaps $100 million in revenue across its operating life; that figure is an industry estimate no primary source confirms, and it travels with that qualifier everywhere it appears. The mine's dust carried asbestos into the town's lungs. The documented dead numbered 694 through 2011, in the mortality study of Naik and colleagues published in 2017, and the dying did not stop in 2011. The documented cash floor of the response, about $1.35 billion in committed remediation plus roughly $1.25 billion in accrued monitoring and care from 2000 through 2025 at upwards of $50 million a year, comes to about $2.6 billion, roughly 26 dollars of documented cost for every revenue dollar, and at least 13 to 1 even if the true revenue were twice the estimate. Price the deaths the way federal agencies price mortality, the EPA's value of statistical life at $7.4-11.0 million, and the mortality-inclusive accounting reaches $6.5-10.2 billion, a damage-function multiple of about 65-100 times, with the spread between the 65-77 range on 2006 dollars and the 78-102 range on updated valuations available in the appendix for anyone who wants the vintage sensitivity. One more fact belongs in the open: W.R. Grace knew of the contamination from 1963 and concealed it for 27 years, and for the entire operating life of the mine the bond posted against any of this was approximately zero.

These ratios need a name and a discipline, in that order. The name: the Intergenerational Pricing Gap, the IPG, defined as true cost divided by market price. The discipline, adopted here at the definition and binding for the rest of this book: no IPG gets quoted without naming its lens and its price basis. The lens says which method produced the numerator, damage accounting or option valuation or the residual-commons-value integral, because the methods measure related but distinct quantities. The price basis says which market price sits in the denominator, because a 1960 mine-mouth dollar and a contemporary one differ by an order of magnitude. A bare multiple, floating free of both, is a fraction with an unstated numerator and an unstated denominator, and it misleads in proportion to how quotable it is. The Technical Appendix (§11.11) carries the full decomposition of how the lenses relate.

One more qualifier, owed to the disaster cases as a class: a one-event multiple is bounded by what has been counted. Valdez's herring fishery collapse, Libby's continuing mortality, the Gulf's long tail are all still accruing in the record. Disaster IPGs grow as the science does, which means the figures above are floors of a particular kind: the kind that rises.

### A Number That Would Not Hold Still

Bottom-up accounting has a structural modesty that its own practitioners name first: it prices only what someone thought to count. A flashlight in a dark warehouse tells the truth about everything in its beam and nothing about the warehouse. The method's totals are floors by construction, and the largest single beam in the coal accounting, the social cost of carbon, has spent fifteen years being grabbed by different hands and pointed at different walls.

Watch the number move. The Obama administration's interagency working group, operating under Executive Order 12866, priced a ton of carbon dioxide's damage at about $42. The first Trump administration recalculated, counting only damages inside US borders and raising the discount rate, and produced $1-7. The Biden administration's interim figure was about $51. The EPA's 2023 report set $190. The atmosphere did not change its chemistry between those administrations. The same ton of carbon dioxide, the same physics, carried a federal damage price that moved by more than an order of magnitude in each direction inside fifteen years, and every move tracked an election.

Call the fight what it is. The social cost of carbon is a proxy war over how much cost severance the polity will tolerate, conducted in the technical vocabulary of discount rates and damage functions because the plain vocabulary would be harder to defend. And precisely because the fight is real, the robustness result matters: run this chapter's arithmetic at the most hostile number any administration has produced and the direction does not flip. At $7 a ton, the carbon term on the national basis is about $16, the non-carbon floor still stands at $8-22, and a ton of coal that sold for $4.71 still cost a multiple of its price. The qualitative finding survives any social cost of carbon above zero. And zero is not the neutral default it pretends to be; pricing carbon's damage at zero is itself a precise empirical claim, the claim that unbounded emission costs nothing, and it is the one claim in the range that no damage study supports.

What an honest reader should take from the volatility is the shape of the uncertainty, not the absence of knowledge. A hurricane forecast does not say where the storm will land; it draws a cone, and the cone is the honest shape of what is known, and nobody calls the cone a failure of meteorology. The damage estimates have a cone. It has narrowed each decade as the science has matured, and it has not closed, and the ranges this chapter carries inherit both facts.

### The Cost of Putting It Back

On a volcanic plateau in Iceland, ranks of industrial fans pull ordinary air through chemical filters, strip out the carbon dioxide, and send it underground to mineralize in basalt. <!-- structure-note: Climeworks plant siting and mineralization texture are public record; verify at fact-check. --> The first commercial plant, Climeworks' Orca, did this at a cost upwards of $1,000 a ton, the honest price of a first-of-a-kind machine. Its successor, Mammoth, came online in 2024 an order of magnitude larger. The company's third-generation trajectory, disclosed at the Zurich Carbon Removal Summit in June 2024, targets $400-600 a ton for net removal by 2030, with a $250-350 capture-only target beneath it. In Texas, Carbon Engineering's Stratos plant has been commissioning toward full ramp through mid-2026 at an expected $300-600 a ton. The IEA's *Direct Air Capture 2022* report puts the technology's band at $230-600. The IPCC's Sixth Assessment, Working Group III, carries an optimistic $100-300 for mid-century.

Four horizons, four answers, and the honest statement is that the cost of pulling a ton of carbon back out of the air is horizon-dependent and will stay that way for a while. But notice what question these machines answer, because it is not the question the social cost of carbon answers. The $190 prices what a ton of carbon dioxide does once emitted: the damage side. The fans price what it costs to take the ton back out: the replacement side. Damage and replacement are different questions about the same molecule, both belong in honest carbon accounting, and a ledger that averages them together understands neither. The replacement-side question returns shortly as Method 1 inside the framework's own computation, and keeping the two sides separated, each labeled, is part of the framework's reporting discipline rather than a redundancy in it.

### The Option in the Ground

Inside every oil major there is a team whose job is to value reserves nobody intends to drill this year, and the team does not value them at zero. An undeveloped reserve is an option: the right, without the obligation, to extract later, when prices are better known, costs are better known, and the world has shown more of its hand. Options have value beyond today's extraction spread, exercising an option early forfeits that value, and the companies know it; undeveloped acreage trades and is carried on books at more than its drill-it-today worth. Dixit and Pindyck codified the mathematics in 1994, and it has been standard corporate finance ever since. This is the second of the chapter's outer approaches, and the point of borrowing it is that nobody can call it activist arithmetic. It is Wall Street's own.

The standard model counts the information that arrives while you wait: about prices, about extraction costs, about geology. What it omits, because its builders had no client who needed it, is the social side of the same waiting. Wait, and damage science clarifies; a polity that waited on a marginal coal seam learned, within decades, what the dust did to miners and what the carbon did to everyone. Wait, and substitutes mature; a unit of electricity that needed that seam in 1960 could be had from gas, then wind, then sun, at falling harm. Wait, and criticality information accumulates; some resources reveal themselves to be replaceable commodities and others reveal themselves to be the irreplaceable input of an industry not yet born. All three are option value, and all three are destroyed by premature exercise just as surely as the financial kind.

The social version of the model says: when the social option value of leaving a unit in the ground exceeds today's net extraction revenue, extraction is socially premature, even where it is privately profitable. Run it on the 1960 McDowell ton. Net revenue at the mine mouth: just under five dollars. The social option value of that same ton, in the appendix's Approach 2 model, lands in the tens to hundreds of dollars. The seam did not need to stay closed forever for the arithmetic to bind; it needed the option's value carried into the price, and nothing carried it.

Be precise about what kind of failure that was, because precision here is what keeps the claim defensible. Nothing in the argument requires anyone to have hidden anything in 1960. Even a decision-maker in full possession of what was then knowable had no instrument that could move option value into a mine-mouth price; the failure was a missing price, not only missing information, and a missing price is not repaired by better intentions or better science alone. That is also what makes the model reachable rather than utopian. The machinery is the same lattice any reserves team already runs; a chief financial officer who signs off on an undeveloped-acreage valuation could run the social-side version with the same staff and the same software, given a mandate. The barrier is not technical.

Across the cases this book can compute, the real-options lens points the same direction as the adding machine. On the appendix's three-model table (Technical Appendix §9.5), the real-options gap runs 45-89 times for McDowell coal against the $4.71 basis, 12-21 times for Deepwater, 57-93 times for Libby. Different method, different data, same side of the line.

### The Framework's Count

An MRI machine holds its magnet a few degrees above absolute zero with liquid helium, and there is no substitute for the helium; vent it and it rises through the atmosphere and leaves the planet, beyond recall at any price now imaginable. Water in the fossil aquifers under the High Plains fell as rain when mammoths drank from the recharge; pump it past the recharge rate and the refill clock is geological. Coal in a power plant's hopper, by contrast, has substitutes queueing up behind it. Any honest pricing of extraction has to be able to tell these cases apart, and telling them apart is what the framework's own approach was built to do.

The residual commons value of a unit prices the two things the market price omits. The first is foreclosure cost: what future access the unit's consumption forecloses, weighted by substitutability. For coal burned for electricity, substitutability is high and rising, so the foreclosure component is modest and shrinking. For rare earths it is low. For helium cryogenics and fossil-aquifer water it sits near zero, and the foreclosure component approaches the whole value of the function. The second is the externality tail: the harms that keep running after the transaction closes, and that do not care how substitutable the resource was. Black lung arrives decades after the seam closes. Acid drainage runs for generations. Carbon works on the centuries. The term externality tail is meant in the statistician's sense, the long thin end of a distribution that does not close on any date you can name, which is why the bond that answers it must carry forward indefinitely, and which places the term adjacent to Weitzman's 2009 work on fat-tailed climate risk; the Technical Appendix (§3.4) carries the definitional defense, and one sentence of it is enough here.

What the apparatus prices, in one phrase, is cost severance: the foreclosure of commons value. The price is positive whenever consumption forecloses future access faster than the commons can restore itself. For non-renewables that is always, because restoration is zero. For renewables it is true only when harvest outruns regeneration: Chapter 3's reefs carried a foreclosure price of zero for as long as the taking stayed inside the reef's rate of making, and began accruing one in the seasons when the dredges passed it. For commons that are not resources at all, institutional trust, civic cohesion, the price turns positive when degradation outruns recovery. Within restoration, the correct price is zero, and the apparatus prices it at zero. The floor under the whole construction is scarcity dynamics, not physical finiteness. Two sentences of discipline follow from that, and both are load-bearing. Nothing here claims that every act of extraction severs a cost; the strictly positive results in this book's fossil cases are findings about those cases, not a theorem about extraction as a category. And nothing here certifies renewable harvest as innocent by category; the ground-down reef is this book's own counterexample.

For the reader who wants the argument as a single object: at each future date, take the flow of commons value this unit's consumption forecloses, scale it down by however much of that function substitutes have by then matured to carry, add the externality flow still running, and discount the sum at rates that decline with the horizon, following Weitzman's declining-rate schedule; then integrate across the dates. The Technical Appendix (§3) builds the integral formally and proves what needs proving; the formula is a summary of the argument above, not a replacement for it, and the argument stands without it. Two definitions fall out, and the rest of the book uses both. Cost severance is residual commons value minus the bond actually posted: CS = RCV - B. And the Intergenerational Pricing Gap of the previous section gets its formal form: residual commons value over market price.

### Three Ways of Counting

Consider what the world already counts, and how hard it counts it. On every trading day, thousands of analysts revise their estimates of companies' future profits; the estimates flow through equity and debt markets, get discounted, get aggregated, and resolve into prices anyone can look up by lunchtime. The machinery that prices future profitability is vast, fast, disciplined, and one-sided. No symmetrical desk anywhere prices future harms. No screen updates when a headwater stream is buried or a county's school system starts to go. The apparatus this section lays out is the substitute for the missing desk, and because the outer frame of this chapter already uses a numbered triad, the nesting needs to be said plainly: Approaches 1 through 3 are the chapter's outer frame, damage accounting, real options, and the residual-commons-value framework. Inside Approach 3 runs an inner machinery of its own, and the book numbers it differently: Method 1, Method 2, Method 3. The inner triad is where the chapter's title lives. Three ways of counting: two that estimate what the commons was worth, and a third that reads what was actually paid for it.

Method 1 is replacement cost, and it asks the engineer's question: what would it cost to put the function back? It is the first of the two estimators, and it is the floor. For the carbon dimension of a McDowell ton, the replacement technology exists and was priced a few pages ago. Run the four direct-air-capture horizons against the 2.61-ton carbon load of a Pocahontas-seam ton and the replacement bill spans $290-2,702 per ton of coal: $290-875 if the optimistic 2050 cost curves arrive, $812-1,658 on the at-scale horizon with a center near $1,235, $1,595-2,702 on the conservative horizon (Technical Appendix §11.6). The reason Method 1 is a floor and not the value: replacement cost prices what can be replaced. The fans can pull the carbon back; they cannot un-foreclose the hydrocarbons or rebuild a county's civic core, and a method that prices the recoverable part of a loss systematically underprices the irreplaceable part. The method stands in the ecosystem-services replacement-cost tradition of Costanza and colleagues, and the appendix carries its construction (Technical Appendix §3.3).

Method 3 is scarcity-adjusted option value, the second estimator, and it asks the question Arrow and Fisher posed in 1974, the same year Claude Henry posed it independently: what is it worth to keep a decision open while information is still arriving? Economists call the answer the quasi-option value of preservation. The estimator starts from the resource's own market value and carries it upward by two context weights, how scarce the commons is and how irreversible its loss would be. For McDowell coal it returns $340-3,670 a ton with a geometric center near $1,115; for Norwegian petroleum, $96-610 per barrel of oil equivalent with a center near $281 (Technical Appendix §11.6 and §11.5). The ranges are wide because the parameters are honestly uncertain, and the appendix publishes every sensitivity; the width is candor about uncertainty, not imprecision in the method. Irreversibility carries the heavier of the two weights, and the warrant is empirical: the IPCC's synthesis catalogues losses already locked in on century scales (AR6, Summary for Policymakers, B.5), and Bernhardt and Palmer's 2011 synthesis of Appalachian valley fills documents buried headwater streams that do not come back. The Asymmetric Regret Rule, named at the end of this chapter, is the decision-theoretic form of the same warrant (Technical Appendix §3.5 for the estimator, §8 for the rule).

The residual commons value estimate is the convergence of the two. Where Method 1 and Method 3 land on overlapping ground, the estimate is well supported, and the support is of the strong kind, because the two estimators share no data: engineering cost curves on one side, market prices carried by scarcity-and-irreversibility context on the other. There is no common input through which an error could travel from one to the other. For McDowell the overlap runs roughly $340-2,700 a ton and the centers sit near $1,000-1,500. For Norway the bands overlap around a center near $281 a barrel. Where the two estimators diverge, the divergence is not an embarrassment to be averaged away; it is empirically informative, it points at the assumption doing the work, and the reporting discipline below requires publishing it.

Method 2 is revealed preference, and it is not a third estimator. The distinction carries the rest of the argument, so it gets stated carefully. Revealed preference, the instrument of the Samuelson and Hicks lineage, reads behavior: what did society actually pay? The answer to that question is the realized bond, the B in CS = RCV - B. And the same observable cannot stand on both sides of a subtraction. A number cannot serve as your estimate of what the commons was worth and simultaneously as the payment you subtract from that worth; an apparatus that let it do both would be grading its own homework. So the framework assigns revealed preference the job it can actually do. It reads the bill.

The reading returns two findings. The first is a floor on value: no rational polity pays out more in a resource's wake than it believes the resource's costs were worth, so the realized bond is a revealed lower bound, RCV is at least the Method 2 reading, and the estimators can be checked against it. The second finding is the one nothing else in the apparatus can see: who paid.

Add up the realized bond on a McDowell ton and it comes to $50-88 a ton: the black lung distributions, the public medical absorption, the remediation that actually happened. Now sort the same payments by payer. The industry-paid share, the reclamation bonds actually posted, about $5-8 billion nationally, plus private medical settlements, comes to $8-15 a ton. The balance, the large majority of the realized bond, was paid by society: by taxpayers, by federal programs, by the counties themselves. The Black Lung Disability Trust Fund is the documented analogue of the whole pattern (Technical Appendix §11.6): an instrument that exists, that operates at national scale, that has paid for decades, and whose financing has leaned on the federal general fund and Treasury borrowing deeply enough that the fund stood $5.1 billion in debt as of September 2024. The bond this book keeps asking for is not missing. It exists, at scale, and it is charged to the wrong party.

That is the third way of counting, and it is why the chapter's title survives contact with its own evidence. Two ways of counting agree on what the ton was worth; the third shows the bill already being paid, at roughly a twelfth to a twentieth of the converged value, mostly by the people the extraction was supposed to have enriched.

The methodology runs in both directions, and only the calibration changes. Forward, Method 2 reads the realized forward bond, the instrument posted against costs still coming; Norway's fund is the worked case, read later in this chapter at $48 a barrel. Backward, Method 1 specializes to remediation cost and Method 2 to restitution actually paid, and the backward record is not hypothetical. The twentieth century built a roster: the Holocaust reparations agreements; the Civil Liberties Act of 1988, under which the United States formally apologized for the wartime internment of Japanese Americans and paid restitution to each surviving internee <!-- structure-note: the Act's $20,000-per-survivor figure is statute text; omitted here pending fact-check preference, add if wanted -->; the Black Lung Trust Fund's payouts; the South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission; and the reparations-economics methodology Darity and Mullen set out in 2020. Backward, the who-paid reading is equally diagnostic: where repair is publicly funded while the extracting beneficiary contributes nothing, the realized bond is mis-assigned in exactly the McDowell pattern, and the reading says so in the same units. The backward form of Method 3 is the option value extinguished at the moment of extraction, and the appendix exposes, without settling, the fork between pricing it by what was knowable then and by what is known now (Technical Appendix §5.5).

A backward ledger slot, once the gates of this chapter's later section admit it, can stand in one of three states. Zero: under genuine abundance the cost genuinely is zero, and writing zero is honest work. Filled: a value entered, with its derivation shown. Open: a real, gate-admitted cost deliberately left unpriced, and Open is neither Zero nor a claim that no price could exist. One slot in the backward ledger I leave Open in my own voice. The costs severed by coercion in this country's history, from slavery forward, pass every gate this chapter will build. I am not part of the group that bore them, and I decline to be the one who writes a dollar figure into that column. That is a statement about me, not a ruling about anyone else's pen. The economics of repair is a developed field, built by Darity, Mullen, Hamilton, Coates, Conley, and others, and Chapter 5 engages it at the depth it deserves.

One rule binds everything from here through the end of the book, and it is the framework's own reporting discipline: a cost-severance estimate is published as both estimators separately, then the convergence range, then the Method 2 realized-bond reading with its payer decomposition, then the identified sources of divergence. A single-method cost-severance claim has no standing, in this book or in any application of it.

### The Substitution Question

In 1928 a Cambridge mathematician named Frank Ramsey, then in his mid-twenties, wrote down the equation economists still use to weigh the future against the present, and remarked that discounting the welfare of later generations seemed to him ethically indefensible. The profession has been arguing about the rate ever since, and the argument has not converged. Stern's review put the rate near zero and made the climate case urgent; Nordhaus anchored it to market returns and made the case gradual; decades of careful work by both, and the gap between them never closed. Weitzman proposed rates that decline as the horizon lengthens, which this book adopts for its integral, and a serious line of philosophers holds that no positive rate can be defended at all. The framework takes Weitzman's schedule and otherwise declines to referee a dispute the discipline itself has not settled.

What it does instead is move the load. The substitutability weight at the center of the integral shifts most of the work off the unanswerable ethical parameter and onto an empirical question: how quickly can we develop alternatives? That question has four natural properties. Substitutability sits at or near zero on the extraction date, when the function has no other carrier. It rises monotonically as engineering matures. It can approach but never exceed one, because a substitute can at most fully carry the function. And it is investment-dependent, which makes it a policy variable rather than a constant of nature; a society can buy itself substitutability, and the rate of purchase is a choice.

A fair critic will say the hard problem has been moved, not solved. Conceded, and the concession is the argument: it has been moved to a better hard problem. No quantity of research narrows the ethics of the discount rate; the positions are as far apart after fifty years as before. Substitution timelines are the kind of question on which error bars actually shrink, study by study, demonstration plant by demonstration plant. The Technical Appendix (§13) carries the substitutability function's full construction.

### The Convergence

A surveyor who needs the height of a mountain does not measure it once. She measures from two baselines that share no stakes, and if the two sightings agree, the agreement is the evidence; an error in one baseline has no way to reproduce itself in the other. Campbell and Fiske turned the same instinct into formal methodology in 1959 for constructs that cannot be grabbed directly, and triangulation has been the standard scientific response to hard-to-measure quantities ever since. The value of an unpriced commons is such a quantity. Hence three approaches rather than one, and hence the structure of this section: the outer convergence first, then the inner machinery run across the cases.

The outer result: three approaches with foundations decades apart, Pigou's damage accounting, Wall Street's option valuation, and the residual-commons-value framework, produce gap multiples far above one, with overlapping ranges, across every case this book can compute, and the direction never flips. For McDowell coal: about 107-110 times on the carbon damage-function lens against the $4.71 mine-mouth price; 45-89 times on the real-options lens; 61-115 times on the RCV-integral lens, which prices the ton at $525-540 by Weitzman declining-rate integral and spans its two price bases, the contemporary $8.66 basis and the 1960 $4.71 basis, with the calibration documented at Technical Appendix §11.1. For Deepwater: about 15-16, 12-21, and 17-18 times. For Libby: about 65-100, 57-93, and 72-111 times. The appendix's three-model table (Technical Appendix §9.5) carries all nine cells. The reason three approaches are worth the trouble is that each one's blind spot sits in another's beam. Bottom-up undercounts by construction, the flashlight problem. Real options imports the market's discipline and inherits the market's blind spots in its base prices. The RCV integral models the unpriced directly and pays for it in parameter uncertainty. When methods with disjoint failure modes agree, the agreement is doing real evidentiary work.

One housekeeping note before the table, because two nearby numbers must not blur: the appendix's $525-540 integral estimate for the McDowell ton is a model result, a separate construction from this chapter's $510 bottom-up honest total. They are not the same quantity and are never to be quoted as one. That two independent constructions land in the same neighborhood from different directions is part of the convergence story, not an identity.

So the count comes back, and it comes back the same way from every direction. Two estimators that share no data converge on what the ton and the barrel were worth. The realized-bond reading shows society already paying roughly a twelfth to a twentieth of that worth, with industry's share a small minority of even that. And across three approaches, five cases, and every lens, the direction never flips.

Three objections deserve their answers in the open.

The first: you chose the parameters, so the parameters chose the answer. The reply is structural rather than rhetorical. No claim in the apparatus rests on a single parameterization, because the two estimators draw on sources that cannot collude; an engineering cost curve has no channel through which to coordinate with a market price carried by context weights. Their overlap is the claim. And the inner result sits inside the outer one, three approaches with disjoint failure modes agreeing in direction across every computable case. Where parameter choices do move results, the sensitivity is published, and divergence between the estimators is reported as a finding rather than smoothed into a blend.

The second objection is Popper's: a framework that keeps finding positive cost severance sounds like a framework that cannot lose. But CS = RCV - B is an arithmetic claim, and arithmetic can come out at zero or below. The framework names the conditions where it should: a sustainably managed renewable harvest under full accountability, the bonded fishery living inside its regeneration rate, and an abundant non-renewable under stringent bonding. Both classes are testable; a materially positive cost severance found where the framework predicts zero would break it, the way any honest theory specifies what failure looks like before the test is flown. Two further points sharpen the falsifiability rather than soften it. The Commons Inversion Test, coming two sections below, is a thought-experiment filter at the gate, and gates are not where empirical claims live; the empirical claim lives one layer up, in the computed cost severance. And the recast of Method 2 strengthens the test: the B side of the subtraction is no longer a modeled quantity but a documentary one, read off settlements, statutes, and fund balances, so half the falsification surface now sits in the public record where anyone can audit it.

The third: hand two competent analysts the same case and they will return different numbers. They will, and they should, within disclosed ranges. That is how applied cost accounting works everywhere it is taken seriously, in insurance reserving, in pension actuarial practice, in natural-resource damage assessment; the discipline is transparent disagreement, the published sensitivity, the named contested parameter. The failure mode worth fearing is the undisclosed sensitivity, the confident point estimate quoted without its hinge. And where the inputs cannot support a computation at all, the honest output is no number: Baotou, whose documented inputs do not support the computation, is reported in the appendix as exactly that (Technical Appendix §11.4), a case the apparatus declines rather than invents.

### The Norway Backtest

On a public page maintained by Norges Bank Investment Management, a number updates with the markets. <!-- structure-note: NBIM publishes the fund's market value publicly on its site; verify the display detail at fact-check. --> At the end of 2025 it read 21,268 billion kroner: about $2.0 trillion at that date's exchange rate, the dollar figure moving with the krone, and the fund touched roughly $2.2 trillion at points earlier in the year. Divide by the population and every Norwegian citizen's share comes to roughly $390,000. It is the most visible single artifact of the best-managed extraction in the historical record, which makes it the apparatus's natural backtest: if the framework returns the same verdict for Norway that it returns for McDowell County, the framework is a slogan wearing equations.

Chapter 4 told the fund's story in full; the apparatus needs one paragraph of mechanics, stated here once. The fund was established by statute in 1990 and took its first deposit in 1996. A spending rule caps withdrawals at the fund's expected real return, 3 percent under the 2017 revision of the rule; the original 2001 rule said 4. A carbon tax has been on the books since 1991. An ethics council governs what the fund may own and has forced divestments. Hartwick wrote the underlying rule down in 1977, invest resource rents in reproducible capital and consume only the return, and Norway is Hartwick's rule with a balance sheet.

Run the three ways of counting. Method 2 reads the fund as the realized forward bond, and the arithmetic is short: about $2.0 trillion, representing roughly 75 percent of the net petroleum wealth Norway captured, implies about $2.67 trillion in total, spread across the roughly 55 billion barrels of oil equivalent of cumulative production in Norwegian Petroleum Directorate figures: about $48 per barrel, realized. The estimators: replacement cost puts the barrel at $161-422; option value at $96-610 with a center near $281 (Technical Appendix §11.5); the bands overlap around $281. Cost severance: roughly $233 a barrel, which across 55 billion barrels is on the order of $12.8 trillion, cumulative.

The instructive question is why Norway's option-value premium is modest, about 2.4-5.1 times on the Method 3 lens, where McDowell's foreclosure premium had no such brake. The answer is the fund's real mechanism, and it is worth getting exactly right because the obvious story is the wrong one. The fund does not buy back the barrel. No financial instrument manufactures subsurface petroleum, and the oil is as gone for Norway as the coal is for West Virginia. What the fund does is keep open the polity's capacity to restore and to compensate: to fund the transition, to build the substitutes, to pay for harms as they surface, on any timeline the future turns out to require. Keeping that capacity open shrinks the irreversible share of the loss; what cannot be recovered in kind becomes recoverable in function, and in the option-value arithmetic that pulls Norway's loss out of the worst regime entirely. The fund is an irreversibility-reduction machine, and irreversibility-reduction, not revenue capture, is what the backtest credits.

And still: $233 a barrel, on the order of $12.8 trillion. The carbon in Norway's exported petroleum burns in other countries' air, and that externality is not in the fund and never was. The verdict differentiates, which is the apparatus's credential as a measuring instrument rather than a polemic: the best-governed extraction in the record shows a cost severance that is small per barrel and still real, while the worst-governed cases show severances that dwarf their entire revenue streams. Norway is the closest thing the world has built to honest extraction pricing. The count says even Norway is not all the way there.

### What the Commons Includes

The ledger so far has priced lungs, streams, reefs, a county's hold on its own future, and a fjord state's petroleum, without ever stopping to say what, in general, a commons is. The omission was deliberate; definitions travel better after the cases that motivate them. But the apparatus needs a working inventory, and this book's runs to ten categories. Habitability: air to breathe, water to drink, a temperature band to live in, ground that holds. Spatial: shared place and passage, navigable water, open country, the routes between. Temporal: the open future, time not yet mortgaged by the present's commitments. Institutional: rules that can be relied on, courts, titles, standards, the machinery of recourse. Kindred: the networks of family and care that raise people and carry them. Ecosystem: the living systems that feed, filter, pollinate, and buffer. Political: the capacity for self-rule, a franchise that means something, a public square that works. Cohesion: the trust strangers extend one another because the place they share functions. Epistemic: the shared stock of reliable knowledge and the means of adding to it. Autonomy: a person's command of their own hours, movements, and choices.

The list is illustrative, and saying so is not a hedge; it is a posture with a pedigree. Ostrom offered eight design principles and called them principles rather than statute. Sen declined to close the capability list, and Nussbaum, who did write one down, wrote it as revisable. Rawls offered primary goods as a working inventory. The ten categories above are offered the same way, a scaffold for seeing, not a fence around what counts. Traditions disagree about what belongs on any such list, and the disagreement is old and respectable. A classical liberal reads the commons narrowly and watches the state's reach. A civic republican counts whatever the conditions of non-domination require. Socialist and communitarian traditions count the shared social product broadly. Marxist readings start from what production itself socializes. Traditions built from lived oppression count what was taken under coercion, which other lists were built without having to see. The accounting works inside any of them: a tradition nominates its commons, the gates of the next section discipline the nomination, and the integral prices what passes. The framework does not legislate the list.

Two categories get a longer look. Habitability first, because it is where McDowell's costs concentrated. Dust took the air; the black lung rolls are a habitability ledger kept in lungs. Acid drainage and buried headwaters took the water; Bernhardt and Palmer's valley-fill record is the same ledger kept in streams. Carbon is taking the temperature band, the one habitability entry every case in this book shares. And when the male life expectancy of a county runs 63.5 years against a nation's 76.5, the habitability account has already been read out loud, thirteen years at a time.

Autonomy earns the second look because three serious readings of it disagree, and the accounting can host all three without choosing. Freedom read as non-interference prices the hours and choices directly taken. Freedom read as non-domination, Pettit's formulation in the neo-Roman lineage Skinner recovered, prices something subtler: the standing lost when another party holds arbitrary power over your days, whether or not the power is exercised. Anderson's relational reading prices the asymmetry itself, the private government of the workplace and the company town. The three readings admit different entries into the autonomy column, and the gates treat all three the same way. Chapter 1 ran the autonomy question under the most favorable conditions on offer, the 120-hour week entered freely and paid well, and that result stands without being rerun here.

What the ten categories are not is a promise that each will someday be priced to the dollar. Some will resist denomination indefinitely, and the apparatus's three-state discipline, Zero, Filled, Open, exists precisely so that resistance can be recorded honestly rather than rounded to nothing.

### What Is Owed

Run a thought experiment, flagged as one. A child will be born in McDowell County in 2060. Whether she is born at all depends on everything between now and then: who leaves the county and who stays, who meets whom, which year, which month. Change the extraction policy of 1960 and you change the chain; under the changed policy, a different child is born to different parents. So when she inherits the emptied county and the thirteen-year gap, she cannot stand up and say the policy harmed her, because without the policy there is no her. Derek Parfit formalized that trap in 1984 and called it the non-identity problem, and it is the deepest objection to everything this book counts. An accounting of intergenerational harm seems to need victims, and the future supplies none who could not be argued out of standing.

Parfit's own response is the one this framework adopts: move the evaluation from persons to outcomes. Compare the states of the world, not the rosters of who inhabits them; a policy that leaves the future with foreclosed commons and running externality tails is worse than the alternative in the impersonal register, whoever turns out to exist in each. The residual commons value apparatus operates in exactly that register. It prices what the world loses, not what a nameable victim was deprived of, which is why it can run forward at all.

The two-instrument architecture inherits the distinction cleanly, and the inheritance explains why the two directions need different scholarship. The forward instrument, the bond, is impersonal by construction: posted against outcomes, held for whoever exists when the costs land. The backward instrument is person-affecting by construction: the bearers of past extraction are identifiable, named, often alive, and their descendants are not hypothetical. Forward work is therefore environmental-bond economics; backward work is reparations economics, the field whose methods Darity and Mullen built out and which Chapter 5 engaged at depth. One honest concession closes the philosophy: the framework operates within Parfit's vocabulary, it does not solve the non-identity problem, and a reader who concludes that the problem remains unsolved has concluded what Parfit concluded.

What is owed, then, on the gentlest arithmetic the apparatus offers? Take the most conservative lens it owns, the Method 3 foreclosure premium, the price-independent one, with no 1960 denominator to flatter it: 8.5-26 times, center near 15. Even counted that way, the commons consumed in the cases this book has computed was worth about fifteen times what anyone was ever charged for it.

Parfit answers who can be wronged. Amartya Sen answers what the wrong consists in. Sen's capability approach measures well-being as what people are able to be and do, and Nussbaum's catalogue gave the approach its working list. Read through that lens, residual commons value is a measurement of the capability set that extraction forecloses: the breathable air and drinkable water are capabilities in their plainest form, and McDowell's thirteen years are capability in its rawest denomination, years of being alive. The pairing is what makes the chapter's claim hard to dislodge: grounded in moral philosophy on the standing side and in welfare economics on the valuation side, it does not fall if either discipline shifts beneath it.

### Two Kinds of Commons

The mountain pastures and irrigation networks Elinor Ostrom documented had something in common that is easy to miss because it is structural rather than scenic: the people capturing the value were the people bearing the costs. The herder who overgrazed the alp wintered next to the neighbors he shorted. Out of decades of such cases Ostrom drew eight design principles for commons that govern themselves, and an empirical refutation of Garrett Hardin's tragedy, which had predicted ruin for every commons not privatized or nationalized. Ostrom documented commons that had survived for centuries under neither remedy. This book contradicts exactly one author in its canon, and it is Hardin, and the contradiction is not theoretical; it is Ostrom's, and it is empirical.

Call those coordination commons. The cases this book computes are a different kind. In an extraction commons, the value-capturers are not shared stakeholders with the cost-bearers: the shareholders of the company that mined McDowell County did not winter in McDowell County. Ostrom's principles assume the parties at the table are the parties in the harm's path, and the structural asymmetry of extraction breaks that assumption, not because the principles are wrong but because their preconditions are absent. The two kinds sit adjacent, and the analyses are complementary rather than rival; where a case carries both elements, a fishery locally coordinated and industrially extracted at once, both analyses run in parallel. The Commons Inversion Test in the next section is what makes the sorting operational rather than impressionistic. And the break-point generalizes beyond geography: wherever stakeholders stratify, by race, by class, by region, coordination assumptions fail along the strata, which is where this analysis meets the stratification economics of Darity and Hamilton.

### The Four Gates

Anyone can nominate a cost; an open list of commons categories guarantees it. Not every nomination may enter the sum, and the apparatus stands or falls on the checkpoints between nomination and addition. There are four, and a candidate cost clears all four or stays out.

Gate 1 is the Commons Inversion Test, which takes its name from its move, holding the world fixed and flipping a single premise in the counterfactual discipline David Lewis formalized in 1973; the Technical Appendix (§6) carries the construction and the name's defense, and that sentence is the whole defense this chapter gives it. The test runs in two sub-forms, and they answer different questions through different filters. The absence form asks: imagine the commons never existed; what would it cost to supply its function? Whatever survives that question is real, and its price is a replacement cost, which is the Method 1 filter. The consumption form asks: imagine this unit unconsumed; what value would still be available? That filter prices opportunity, and feeds the option-value side. The absence form also performs the routing of the previous section: invert a coordination commons and what goes missing is governance, which routes the case to Ostrom's machinery rather than to the extraction apparatus. One candidate gatekeeper was considered for Gate 1 and declined: revealed preference. As a gate it would admit only the costs society already acknowledged paying, which is precisely the circularity the apparatus exists to break. The recast of Method 2 makes that old decision cleaner than it first looked: revealed preference reads what was paid, never what is owed, and the blind spot that disqualifies it as a gate is the same feature that qualifies it as the reader of the realized bond.

Gate 2 is units consistency. A cost enters the sum in one denomination, and any conversion is explicit and sourced. A disability-adjusted life year does not add to a dollar until a named authority's valuation, the EPA's value of statistical life, for instance, carries it across, with the crossing shown. Sums that quietly mix denominations are not conservative or liberal; they are meaningless.

Gate 3 is boundedness. An admitted cost must arrive with a constructible bound, above and below. A harm that can only be asserted as unbounded may be real, and the honest treatment is to say so and hold it out of the arithmetic, the Open state again, rather than to invent a ceiling for the comfort of having a number. Baotou stands in the appendix as the worked example of the gate doing its job.

Gate 4 is independence. No cost enters twice in different clothes. The outmigration priced in the cohesion column does not get repriced in the habitability column because both columns can describe it; the partition comes before the addition, and the appendix's double-count audit walks the McDowell partition line by line.

The gates are jointly necessary; each catches a failure the others pass. Inversion without units consistency admits eloquent nonsense. Units without boundedness admits infinities. Boundedness without independence admits the same harm twice. The Technical Appendix carries the Four Gates in full, together with the generalization of the formula they discipline.

### What This Adds

In 1920 Arthur Pigou put a chimney in the economics curriculum: the factory's smoke dirtied the neighborhood's washing, the cost fell on people outside the transaction, and the remedy was the tax that has carried his name for a century. Everything in this chapter descends from that page, and it is worth being exact about where the descent extends the line into territory the line had not reached. The extension is threefold.

First, substitutability weighting, anchored by an identity the appendix proves. Hotelling priced exhaustion in 1931: the scarcity rent on a depleting resource, rising along the interest rate, is the part of the future the market does carry. Subtract the Hotelling rent the market already pays from residual commons value, and the remainder is the cost severance per unit. Hotelling gets full credit for the part of the problem he solved; the framework's subject is the remainder, and the identity keeps the two from ever being double-counted against each other.

Second, an integrated architecture: one gate-disciplined formula across resource types, coal and crude and vermiculite and helium, an aquifer and a reef. The practical alternative is what the field actually has, bespoke per-domain accountings that cannot be compared with each other or audited against a common standard.

Third, the inversion test as a discovery method. Inherited externality catalogues are closed lists; the test is generative. Invert the commons and read off what its absence would cost, and categories appear that no catalogue carried; the cohesion and autonomy columns of this chapter entered the inventory exactly that way. The name of the framework's central quantity wears its lineage in three words, residual commons value: Hartwick's residual, Ostrom's commons, Mazzucato's value, and the Technical Appendix walks the term's derivation.

Run the additions back across the cases with the chapter's pinned numbers, and the extension shows its work. McDowell: the four-component Pigouvian floor, about $504-518 a ton, already dwarfs the $4.71 price; the honest eight-component total of $510 adds the columns Pigou's method never had a way to admit; the carbon term inside both is $496 on the McDowell basis, $441 on the national basis. Standard Pigouvian analysis has no principled ground for excluding the community and cohesion costs. What it lacks is a method for admitting them, and the gates plus the inversion test are that method. Deepwater: a Pigouvian accounting prices the spill's flow damage cleanly, and the foreclosure and the decades-long tail need the integral. Libby: a Pigouvian tax on the dust would have priced the operating harm if anyone had counted it, and the 27 concealed years are the reason the apparatus pairs every price with a bond and a reading of who actually paid. One boundary, stated once: the apparatus accounts the cost of consuming a commons; it does not code the commons into a tradable asset, a distinction Katharina Pistor's work on the legal coding of capital sharpens, and Chapter 9 takes the financialization question up in full.

### What Comes Next

Two tests are queued, and one rule needs its name before they run. Chapter 7 takes the apparatus to a place where cost severance has nowhere to hide, a settled world with no distance to export across, no abundance illusion, and no one downwind who is not also upstream: if the framework is universal, it must survive there without modification. Chapter 8 walks a single McDowell ton through the full eight-component ledger to the $510, line by line, so that every number this chapter quoted in summary stands itemized. Chapter 9 builds the instruments.

The rule: the Asymmetric Regret Rule specializes Savage's 1951 minimax-regret criterion to decisions where the regret in one direction is commons-extinction while the regret in the other is foregone revenue, weighing regret rather than risk, and weighing it asymmetrically rather than one-sidedly, since foregone revenue is real regret too (Technical Appendix §8). The rule does not displace cost-benefit analysis where cost-benefit analysis works, which is wherever both directions of error are recoverable; it governs the regime where one direction is not. Its irreversibility weighting stands on the same empirical record this chapter has leaned on throughout, the IPCC's catalogue of losses already locked in (AR6, Summary for Policymakers, B.5) and the buried headwaters of the Appalachian record (Bernhardt and Palmer 2011). Chapter 9 states the rule formally and puts it to work; Chapter 7 will already be relying on it.

Go back to where this began: a federal table, a 1960 row, $4.71. The number was always exact. Exactness was never the problem.

Three ways of counting have now been run against that number. Two of them, built decades apart by people who never met, from data that share nothing, converge on what the ton was worth, and every lens the apparatus owns puts that worth an order of magnitude or more above the price, lens and basis named each time. The third way of counting found the bill. It is being paid now, every year, in McDowell County and Libby and on the Sound: in trust-fund checks and Treasury borrowing, in Medicaid absorptions and county budgets, by the same people who carried the harm the first time, while the parties the extraction enriched posted bonds a tenth the size of the public's share. The gap between the price and the truth is not a defect at the edge of the extraction economy; it is the operating margin at the center of it.

The commons kept honest books all along. It billed in lungs, in streams, in years of life, in counties emptied of their futures, and the bill was paid in full, every year, by somebody. The only entry the ledger never carried was who.
