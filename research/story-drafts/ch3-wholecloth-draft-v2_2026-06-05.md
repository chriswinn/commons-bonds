# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*

## Chapter 3: The Waterman

---

Water is the one rich thing in the world that does not check your pockets first.

You can be born with everything or born with nothing, and the water does not know the difference and does not care. You can walk down to it and put your feet in it. You can pull food out of it with your hands. You can live your whole life beside it on almost no money at all, and it will give you mornings no fortune can buy. That is the first thing to understand about the Chesapeake Bay, and it is the thing the rest of this chapter is going to take apart. Before I take it apart, I want you to love it the way I love it, because the argument I am building does not work unless you do. You have to feel the fullness of the thing before you can feel what it means to have it severed away.

I grew up on this water. Not beside it the way a tourist is beside it, for a week in August with an umbrella and a cooler. I mean the water of my family, the lower Bay around Fox Hill and Hampton, in Virginia, where my father's people knew the watermen and my father worked the summers and the names of the men who ran the oyster beds were spoken at our table the way other families speak the names of ballplayers. My family is one generation removed from the working life. We are zero generations removed from the community.

Fox Hill is, or was, an island fishing community, a place where, for most of the twentieth century, almost every family in every house had at least one person on the water. My grandfather worked at NASA Langley, just up the road, building pieces of a different kind of America, and he was the exception. The neighbors on either side of him were on the water. The men who taught my father how to handle a small boat and where the fish were running and what the smell of the channel meant in late August were men whose lives were the water.

The Bay is known for two things above all. Blue crabs, which belong to the open Bay. And the oyster, which built everything I am about to tell you about. There are clams in the tributaries too, the smaller waters that feed the big one, though if you want to know where the clams are you do not ask a book. You ask a waterman, and he will tell you, because the knowing of it lives in him and not on any chart.

Ask a waterman about the water itself and he will tell you the Bay is not the ocean and does not behave like the ocean, and that the difference can kill you if you forget it. Out on the open sea a five-foot wave under a boat is a gentle thing, a rocking, a good place for a nap, because the crests of those waves sit thirty and forty feet apart and the boat rides up one long slope and down the next. Twenty seconds between waves on the Atlantic feels like a slow rocking. The Bay does not give you twenty seconds. The Bay's water is shallow and close and impatient, and its wavelength runs three or four seconds, sometimes less, the crests stacked almost on top of each other, the wave nearly as tall as it is wide. Almost square. A three-foot chop on the Chesapeake can be steeper than a much larger swell on the Atlantic, and it will turn the hardiest seaman who ever lived a quiet shade of green. The waterman has a word for the whole of it. He calls it the chop, and the chop is the Bay's true face, the one that is nothing at all like the postcard.

He knows which days are the days you do not go out, and which days you can go out but you stay close to home, and which days the sky is lying about what the water is going to do in three hours. He knows all of it without consulting anything outside himself. The water grants him this knowledge in exchange for the obligation never to mistake which day he is looking at.

The boats out on it are not the boats you are picturing. There are no super-yachts on the Chesapeake. Those are somewhere else in the world, in the places the wealth went, and we will get to where the wealth went. What is on the water is the gentle tinging of rigging against the masts of small sailboats, and the workboats, and the one boat the Bay made for itself. The deadrise. A low wooden workboat with a sharp entry at the bow that softens back toward the stern, built by Chesapeake hands for Chesapeake water, and the watermen will tell you it rides the chop steadier than anything else that floats there. It is not fancy. It was never meant to be looked at. It was meant to come home.

And the man on it knows it the way you know your own hands in the dark. He runs the engine out to the beds, and then he cuts it, and from there he works the boat with a single oar over the side, the smallest movements, a push here and a draw there, sliding the hull from this bed to that one, holding it steady over the rock while he works. He is not fighting the boat. He is barely touching it. A lifetime is in that oar.

The cold is the part the tourists never see, because the tourists are gone by October. The waterman is not gone. He is on the water year round, in the rain and the wind and the hard gray mornings when the gale comes off the cape and blows through whatever you are wearing and shakes your very soul. His slicks keep the wet off him, but they give him no warmth at all, so under them he wears the heavy base layers and the sweaters, layer on layer, against a cold that does not let up from November to March.

And here is the thing he knows and goes out anyway. All that clothing, soaked, is a stone. If he goes over the side into that winter water, the weight of the wet wool and the soaked slicks will not come off in time. He will not get free of it and reach the surface before the cold takes him and the water becomes the place he stays. Every waterman on that Bay in February knows this about his own clothes. He puts them on in the dark and goes out anyway, because the houses are not paid for and the families do not eat when there is nothing to bring home. You go out early. You stay out until you bring food back. That is the bargain he was born into.

---

The community that grows up around that bargain has a particular shape, and it is not a metaphor when the watermen of a place like Fox Hill say they look out for each other. It is a structural condition of the work. A waterman whose nets need rebuilding before the next season cannot do it alone in one season's worth of time, and there is no industry of net-rebuilders to hire. So a morning is set, and at three or four in the morning, when every man in that community would normally already be on the water or on his way to it, the boats stay tied up and the men come instead to whoever's place needs the nets. They come in pickups. They bring their own coffee. They work until it is done.

The understanding behind the morning is not the understanding of a favor done. It is the understanding that, as surely as the sun rises and the sun sets, there will be a morning when the man who needs the help is you, or your son, or your father, and the community that fails to come on the day you need it is a community that has already failed before you needed it. That is the bargain, and the bargain holds for as long as the community holds.

The most precise thing the bargain produces is the hardest to render to anyone who has not been around it. It is what happens when one of the men who said he would be there is not. Everyone stops. The nets get put down. Somebody picks up a phone and calls somebody else, and within ten minutes the whole morning has reorganized itself around the question of where the missing man is, because everyone there has done this work long enough to know that the only thing that keeps a waterman from being where he said he would be is something serious enough that he cannot tell anyone about it. It might be his truck. It might be his health. It might be that he was on the water at four-thirty and did not come back, and the water is the part of the morning the rest of the men have to go check on first. The older men's bodies carry that possibility the way a cook's body carries the possibility of burns.

---

The waterman my father talked about most was a man everyone called Biggie.

Biggie was deceased long before I was old enough to understand who he had been, so I never worked beside him. What I have of him is what my father gave me, the way a family hands down the one story it cannot stop telling, the way my father's father came to me through the story of the air compressor. I have heard the Biggie stories so many times that I finish them now when my father starts them, and he lets me, because by now the telling belongs to both of us.

Biggie had the build of an oak. Not tall, in the way some big men are not tall, but solid through the shoulders and the back in a way that registered as a man who had been doing one specific kind of work for most of his adult life. He worked oysters, and he had been working oysters since he was old enough to be useful in a boat with whoever brought him into one. The bars he worked were bars he had worked his entire life.

Biggie was the master. My father always came back to how little he moved. Other men sweated and hauled and threw their whole bodies at the work. Biggie did not. He worked the oysters with an economy of motion that looked, my father said, like a man hardly working at all, the smallest turn of the wrist, the tongs coming up full, the boat sliding to the next spot on a single push of the oar, everything stripped down to exactly the motion the task required and not one motion more. You could watch Biggie work his bars with one oar between the beds and see immediately that his family had been doing this for generations. A lifetime of skill made to look like nothing. That is the mark of a master in any craft, and Biggie had it on the water.

There is one story my father tells about Biggie that holds the whole of the old Bay inside it.

In Biggie's day there were so many oysters that a man did not pack a lunch. Biggie went out in the morning with a six-pack of glass Coca-Cola bottles in a wire carrier, and that was all. No cooler. No ice. No sandwich. The first time my father went out with him, my father was a small boy then, still young enough to be honestly puzzled about it, he asked Biggie what he was going to eat for lunch, because a man works hard out there and a man gets hungry. Biggie just smiled. His lunch was still in the water, he said. Waiting for him. As fresh as it could be.

Once the sun was high enough and the work had hit its rhythm, Biggie would settle into eating oysters as he worked. He had a spot on the boat, near his body, where he would set the oyster he was about to shuck, so that he did not have to reach and did not have to lift his arm, and the motion he used to open it was the motion of a man who had reduced the work to its smallest possible movement after a working life of doing it. A quarter turn of the wrist, the shell parting, the meat coming up clean. He ate as he worked. My father tried more than once, as a boy, to count how many oysters Biggie put away in a single day, and he told me, when I was old enough to ask, that he had never managed to keep count. Not because there were too many, although there may have been. Because the eating was so woven into the work that there was never a single moment when an oyster started or stopped being eaten. It was simply something Biggie was doing alongside everything else, the way breathing is something a person does alongside everything else.

That is the Bay at its fullness. A commons so thick with life that it fed a man straight from the water at midday with nothing in his hands but a six-pack of Coca-Cola. You did not have to prepare for that abundance. You did not have to carry it with you or store it up against scarcity. It was simply there, every day, the way the sun is there. His lunch was still in the water.

I want you to hold that line, because I am going to need it again, and the second time I need it the water will have a different answer.

---

The Bay never feels like it is moving fast. It runs on a kind of island time, slow and wide, the days long and the seasons unhurried, and that is part of why what happened to it happened the way it did. It is like a sunset. You stand and watch a sunset and you feel that it is taking forever, that the light will hang there at the edge of the world as long as you want it to. And then you look away for a moment, and you look back, and it is gone, and you cannot say when. That is the shape of the loss. Slow, and slow, and slow, and then you blink and you have missed it.

There were three fish I loved to eat as a boy. Striped bass, which the watermen call rockfish, the one the Bay is famous for. Red snapper. Grouper. I am not going to dress those last two up as something they were not. They were fish I loved, fish that came to our table, fish a boy in the nineteen-eighties could count on the way you count on a thing that has always been there. The striped bass is the one that belongs to this Bay and to this story, and the striped bass is the one I can tell you the most honest thing about, because what happened to it I watched happen.

We took fish by the season, the way you are supposed to. You do not take them while they are spawning. You do not take more than the water can carry. That was not a regulation we resented. It was just how it was done, the gentle balance of the thing, the understanding that the water gives and you do not break the water to get more than your share.

Then there was a bad season. And then there was another one. At first it was nothing, just a thin year, the fish not running the way they used to, a thing you waved off the way you wave off bad weather. I remember noticing, almost idly, that the rockfish had quietly come off some of the restaurant menus, and thinking it was strange, and not thinking much past that. It was only later, much later, that I understood what those bad seasons had actually been. They were not a dip. They were the last of the real seasons, the final years of meaningful abundance, and we did not know it while we were inside them. And the part I cannot soften, the part that is the whole honesty of this book, is that even as the fish were vanishing, we still took as much as we could carry to the shore.

Nobody did this. There was no villain on the dock, no scheme, no cruelty. There were just a lot of people, my own family among them, doing what people had always done, taking what the water had always given, each of us certain that our own share was small and could not be the thing that tipped it. And the share was small. And it tipped anyway.

The striped bass is the species that taught Maryland and Virginia, in the 1980s, what intervention can do, and I am old enough to remember it. By the late 1970s the population had collapsed badly enough that the two states, working with the federal government, agreed to a moratorium. For five years on the Maryland side, beginning in 1985, no one fished commercially for striped bass at all. Not the watermen. Not the charter fleet. Not the recreational anglers. The species was given a generation off. It was a hard ask of the people whose income depended on the fishery. Many of them lost a livelihood for the duration, and some of them did not come back to the work. But the moratorium did the narrow thing it had been designed to do. The spawning population recovered. By 1995 the fishery was declared restored. The men who had given up five years saw, at the end of them, a thing brought back from a place very few fisheries get brought back from.

That recovery is part of why what is happening now is so hard for the men who remember it. Because what is happening now to the rockfish is what was happening to the rockfish before the moratorium. The juvenile numbers, year after year, are coming in below the long-term average. In 2024 the Maryland young-of-year index was 2.0 fish per sample, against a long-term average of 11.0. That is not a bad season. That is a structural signal, and it is the sixth such season in a row.

I do not write this with confidence that the fish is coming back now, and I am not the one you should take that from. Take it from the people whose job is to count.

Chris Moore, the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's Virginia executive director, has spent his career watching these numbers. He does not speak in the register of a man trying to frighten you. He speaks in the register of a man reading an instrument. *Despite recent management actions,* he said, after a recent stock assessment, *striped bass continue to struggle. It is obvious that conditions have changed and striped bass will not have the same recovery they had in the 1980s. Warmer water from climate change, habitat loss, and invasive species are all making it very hard for striped bass to bounce back.*

Read that again, because it is the quiet center of everything this book is about. The recovery they had in the 1980s. The Bay used to be able to come back. You could close the fishery, ease the pressure, wait, and the water would refill itself, because that is what a healthy commons does. That capacity is what Moore is telling you may be gone. Allison Colden, the foundation's Maryland executive director, who holds a doctorate in the science of all this, has said the same thing from the other side of the Bay. *Maryland's striped bass surveys have made it clear,* she said. *We're facing a looming disaster. Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future.*

The men on the water do not need to be told this. They felt it before the survey numbers came in. What the survey numbers do is force the institutions to acknowledge what the watermen have been carrying as a kind of private foreknowledge, the way miners know what the dust is doing to them years before the X-rays come back. The institutional acknowledgment, when it comes, is always late. Later than the data would have allowed. Later than the watermen had warned. The asymmetry is the point. When the offer was to increase the harvest, the committees acted with confidence. When the offer is to reduce it, the committees discover an unfamiliar caution. Bill Goldsborough, the foundation's fisheries director through the rockfish moratorium years, told *Smithsonian Magazine* how the institutional time-scale looks from inside the science. *We see that task taking place over a longer term,* he said of the work of restoring the Bay. *But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment.*

When I was a boy, the worst case was a bad season. Now the worst case, in the mouths of the people who count the fish, is a Bay that does not have the fish at all, and does not get them back.

So I do not know, honestly, whether the children being born in the year I am writing this will catch a wild striped bass in their lifetimes the way I caught one in mine. I cannot promise them the water I had. That is what it is to inherit a thing that is leaving while you hold it.

---

If you want to see the loss standing up in a single man, you do not need the numbers. You need to know two watermen.

Biggie was the first. Biggie's water fed him straight, his lunch waiting in it, no preparation required, abundance so total it needed no plan.

The second is a man I will not name, because he is alive and the choice to be named in a book like this should be his and not mine, and he has not made it. So I will tell you only what matters. He is a large man, not tall, who has been on the Bay since he was a boy, from the same Fox Hill world Biggie was from, and his life has unfolded in the next stretch of the same arc. He is a crabber and a fisherman, and he has been one all his working life. He knows the beds and the seasons and the currents the way you only know them if the water is in your blood. And he cannot feed his family on the water alone.

What he has watched, across his decades on it, is that the math for a man like him has changed. The catches have come back thinner. The cost of fuel and gear and the slip has moved in the other direction. The species he came up working are not all of them still running. He still gets up at two or three in the morning. He still goes out and pulls his crab pots, the way he ran them when he was younger, except the math at the end of the day is different now than it was then. So he comes back to the dock by mid-morning, hoses down the boat, and goes to the mechanic shop he owns, where his name is on the sign and his crab pots are stacked behind the locked fence in the back lot, behind the cars he is working on for customers. He works the shop until five or six in the evening.

The shop is not a hobby, and it is not a second love. It is the thing that lets him keep being a waterman at all, the land economy he has to carry on his back so that the water economy, the one he was born to, does not sink him. He did not quit the water. He could not. He has spent decades building, on his own time and his own dime, the second economy that has allowed him to not quit it. Two jobs to do the one thing his predecessors did with one, and the squeeze of it is the whole story of the Bay told in a single life. Biggie's water gave him lunch for free. This man's water cannot give him a living. Same Fox Hill. Same family-deep knowledge of the same beds. One generation between them, and the abundance that fed a man from the water at noon has become a scarcity that sends him to a second job on land.

I have not asked him what he would say about all this, and the chapter does not have his answer to put on this page. What I can guess, from watching him and from knowing the men around him, is that if asked over a beer at the end of a long day he would say he is glad to still be on the water and that he is grateful for the shop. The accounting this book is trying to make visible is not, necessarily, the accounting he would put on himself. It is what the surrounding economy has been accepting from him, and from many men like him up and down this Bay, in exchange for a continued supply of the seafood it has not stopped wanting.

This is the thing the framework of this book calls cost severance, and I named it in the last chapter so I would not have to re-explain it in this one. The value of the Bay went somewhere. The cost of the Bay's decline settled onto the men who work it, and onto the Bay itself, and there is no invoice either of them can send. The waterman cannot bill the collapse back to anyone. The Bay cannot bill it back at all.

And the strange, hard fact is that on paper the oyster, at least, is recovering. Colden will tell you so, and she is right to, because it is true and it is hard-won. *Large-scale oyster restoration is working,* she said. In Maryland the oyster population has roughly tripled since 2005. That is real, and it is the work of decades and a great deal of public money, which is a thread I am about to pull hard. But Mitch Tarnowski, who leads Maryland's oyster reef surveys, summed the paradox up in twelve words to *Maryland Matters*. *There are plenty of oysters out there,* he said. *They're just not being harvested.* In the same years the restoration was being celebrated as one of the largest successful ecosystem-restoration efforts in North America, the state of Maryland filed for a federal disaster declaration for the oyster fishery, because the harvest had collapsed in volume and value and the watermen who depend on the wild bars were in financial crisis. *Despite a growing oyster population,* Colden said, in support of the disaster request, *Maryland's watermen are feeling the pinch.*

A growing population, a federal disaster declaration, and watermen who cannot make the living their grandfathers made, all at once, on the same water. Jason Ruth, who owns an oyster house in Grasonville, Maryland, told the same paper how the longer arc looks from inside the industry. *While the demand for boutique aquaculture oysters has grown over the last decade,* he said, *the shucked oyster sector, the larger part of the industry, has seen a 60-year nationwide decline.* The resource line goes up and the human line goes down, at the same time, on the same water. That gap between the two lines is the severance, present tense, happening now, in the words of the Bay's own scientists. The cost has been cut loose from the value, and it has landed where it always lands, on the people nearest the water and least able to send it back.

There is one more loss the scientists name, and I am going to name it the way they do and no further than they do, because it is the kind of loss that invites a person to claim more than he can prove, and I would rather hold the honest line.

There is a fish you have probably never eaten. It is small. It is oily. It is silver. It runs in schools so dense their movement can flatten the surface of the water like a passing wind, and it is, by weight, one of the most important fish in the Atlantic. The menhaden. You do not eat it, because it is not pleasant to eat. What it is, instead, is the thing that almost everything else eats, the forage fish at the base of the Bay's food web, the species the striped bass and the bluefish and the ospreys all come for. And it is the one industry in the Bay that nets it by the millions of tons, out of Reedville, Virginia, to grind into meal and oil. The catch leaves the Bay in tank trucks and railcars and on ships, and it does not get eaten by people. It gets converted, by an industrial process, into inputs for industries somewhere else: the omega-3 capsule in Indianapolis, the salmon farm in Chile, the forty-pound bag of dog food in a Phoenix grocery. The value disperses across a continent and across industries. The cost concentrates here, on the rockfish, the ospreys, the watermen who depend on a Bay that depends on the menhaden.

J.S. Darling, the oysterman whose shells this chapter is about to climb, ran a menhaden factory at Factory Point in Fox Hill more than a hundred years ago, rendering thousands of tons of it a year into meal and oil, so the pressure is old. What is new is the worry. Moore puts it carefully, because he is careful. *It's a struggle because we don't have base science to make good decisions,* he said. *We have a Bay cap that's based on historic landings.* And he names the cost he can measure. *Our bait catch has been reduced by about 80% over the last five to six years.* The people who work the science name the same hole. Genny Nesslage, a research professor at the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science, told the public broadcaster WHRO, *We don't know the abundance of menhaden in the bay at any given time relative to the coast.* Rob Latour, a menhaden researcher at the Virginia Institute of Marine Science, put it more bluntly to the same outlet. *We have no data to really do this in a meaningful way, from a biological perspective.*

On the last day of 2025 a Virginia resident named Tanya O'Connor filed a 349-page petition with the Virginia Marine Resources Commission, asking the agency to declare a temporary moratorium on the Bay's reduction fishery until it produced Bay-specific science. The petition put the absence in regulatory language: the commission *did not have, and still does not have, a Bay-wide estimate of menhaden biomass, age structure, spatial distribution, recruitment trends, or localized depletion risk.* The legal grounding cited Virginia's public-trust doctrine, the old idea that the waters are held in trust for the people. The commission held a hearing in April of 2026 and received 1,266 public comments. It voted six to two against the petition, and the moratorium did not move forward.

I am not going to build a dollar figure on the menhaden. The science of exactly how that pressure ripples up the food web is genuinely contested, and a great deal of the research is paid for by the industry that does the netting. So I will do what Moore and Colden and the petition do. I will name it as a real concern, in the mouths of the people qualified to raise it, and I will leave it unpriced. The honest boundary of a case is part of the case.

---

Now I want to take you to the shells, because the shells are where the severed cost stops being a figure of speech and becomes a thing you could have touched, four stories high, in my own family's town.

In 1881, on a shoal called the Hampton Bar off the mouth of the Hampton River, a New Yorker named James Sands Darling started an oyster business. He had come south to make his fortune, and the old watermen told him the Hampton Bar was no good for oysters, and he knew they were wrong. He was right. By 1884 his son Frank had joined him and the firm took the name J.S. Darling and Son, when Frank joined his father's existing oyster business, and over the years that followed the two of them built it into the largest oyster operation in the world. Not the largest in Virginia. In the world. I am drawing this from the historical marker that still stands for it, and I am telling you the world part because it matters to what comes next.

Picture the work of it. At dawn a fleet of log canoes, some of them built right there on the Hampton waterfront, sailed out to the bar, and the watermen tonged the oysters up off the rock by hand the way watermen had done for centuries. The canoes brought them in. Inside the plant, a hundred and sixty shuckers stood at their stations and opened the oysters and scooped the meat out, working through something on the order of two hundred thousand bushels in a year. The meat went into metal quart and gallon containers, packed in iced tubs, and the iced tubs went onto the railcars and the boats. By 1938 the operation was shipping fifty thousand gallons of shucked oyster a year. Hampton was known up and down the coast as Crabtown, and the oyster was not the only thing that left it whole and came back as money somewhere else. In the 1870s a man named James McMenamin invented and patented a canning process that put cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in airtight tins and shipped it to tables far from the Bay.

Now follow the meat, because the meat is the value, and the value is the part of this story that left. It did not stay in Hampton. It went to the dinner tables and the restaurants of Baltimore and New York and cities beyond them, fifty thousand gallons a year of Chesapeake oyster eaten in dining rooms hundreds of miles from the water that grew it. The whole downstream economy of that oyster, the restaurants, the shuckers in the city kitchens, the distributors, the half-shell culture, the tax base it fed, all of it was built somewhere else, in the places that ate the oyster, not in the place that grew it. Hampton grew the wealth and shipped it out and kept the work. In the last chapter I watched the value of a man's labor go up and out and settle into accounts that were not his. Here it is again, in the historical record of my own community, written at the scale of an industry. The value went up and out, to Baltimore and New York and the world. What stayed in Hampton was the labor, and the bill, and the shells.

The shells. Follow the shells now, because the shells are the other half of the story and the half nobody followed at the time.

The empty shells came out of the plant on conveyor belts and were dumped onto piles on the Hampton waterfront, and the piles grew, and grew, until they stood more than four stories high. A mountain of oyster shell. For years it was a landmark, a thing that dominated the working waterfront in sight and in smell, so large and so permanent that the town came to take it for granted. And then those mountains went into the ground. Most of the shell was sold and crushed and used to build the region. It went into the roadbeds and the driveways and the wharves of the lower Chesapeake, ground up and packed down under the feet and the wheels of a whole region that never thought about what it was walking on. Some of it, a smaller portion, was hauled out to Back River and dumped back into the water, where new oysters could anchor on it and grow.

That last part matters, and I want to be honest about it, because the easy version of this story is wrong. The easy version is that nobody knew the shells should go back in the water, that it was all ignorance, an old wives' tale that turned out to be true too late. That is not what happened. Some of the shell did go back, to Back River, on purpose, to build reef. The knowledge was not entirely absent. But most of it did not go back. Most of it went under the roads. And that is the severance, made literal and physical and four stories tall. The oyster shell is not waste. The oyster shell is substrate, the hard surface a baby oyster has to land on and cement itself to in order to live, the foundation a reef is built from. An oyster needs something hard to set on. The shells are how the reef rebuilds itself. And the bulk of a world's-largest oyster operation's worth of that foundation, decade after decade, got crushed into the roads of the lower Bay instead of going back to the water that would have grown new oysters on it.

This is the difference between an oyster reef and a coal seam, and it is the difference the whole chapter turns on. A coal seam is finite from the day it forms. You can extract it faster or slower, but you cannot extract more of it than was laid down, and once it is burned it is gone. An oyster reef is something else. An oyster reef is, in principle, an instrument that makes more oysters every year, as long as you do not damage the instrument. Take some, leave the rest to spawn, come back next year, and the harvest does not diminish across the generations. You can do it, and your son can do it, and your son's son can do it. The reef makes more of itself. What happened in the Chesapeake is what happens when the rate of taking exceeds the rate of making, for long enough that the taking reaches the structure that produced the renewal. When you ground the reef down, you did not just take this year's oysters. You took the capacity of the bottom to make any oysters at all. From that point on, leaving the system alone is no longer the answer. You have to put something back, or accept that what was lost stays lost.

My father had his hands in that shell.

He worked the summers to pay his way through college, and one of those summers the work was concrete, the oyster-shell concrete they poured for the boardwalk in Hampton. The shell went into the mix, the way it had gone into a century of the region's roads and walks, and they poured it, and it set, and it is still there. You can walk on it. The boardwalk my father helped pour still stands at the time I am writing this, oyster shell packed into the concrete underfoot, the severed substrate of the Bay turned to pavement by my own father's hands in a college summer.

I never saw the four-story piles.

The Darling company closed in 1979. I was born in 1980. By the time I came into the world there was no longer enough oyster coming out of that bar to pile the shells that high, and the piles were gone, and the mountains my father grew up beside were already a story he told rather than a thing he could show me. I inherited the abundance of that water the way I inherited Biggie, secondhand, in stories and in old photographs of shell heaps taller than the buildings. My whole life has been lived in the after. I am the one who came after. And that is exactly why the line I keep coming back to, the one about writing for the ones who come after, lands so hard in my own chest, because I am one of them. I am the generation that got the depleted Bay and the photographs of the full one.

Here is the part that closes the circle, and once you see it you cannot unsee it.

They ground the oyster shell into the roads. The shell that rebuilds reefs. And now, up and down this Bay, people are spending real money to build reefs out of concrete instead. They make blocks called oyster castles, stacked structures of concrete with holes through them, and they sink them into the water so that baby oysters will have something hard to land on and cling to and grow, which is precisely, exactly, the thing the discarded shell would have done, for free, if it had gone back into the water instead of under the roads. At the Navy's own installation on the Hampton Roads shoreline, in the very waters this chapter has been standing in, sailors and federal agencies have stacked these concrete castles into the water to bring the oysters back. We crushed the substrate into pavement, and now we manufacture a substitute for it and pay to sink it where the substrate used to be.

That is the severed cost. It did not disappear when the shell went under the road. It waited. And now it has come due, and someone is paying it.

---

So let me do for the oyster what I refused to leave undone for the miner. Let me add up the column.

The instrument that does this adding is one I am going to name here and build properly in a later chapter. I will call it the Restitution Bond, and the idea of it is simple even though the machinery is not. It looks backward at a commons that was drawn down, prices the physical cost of making it whole, and names who should hold that bill. It is the backward-looking partner to an instrument that looks forward, and the forward one will keep until later. Right now I only want to run the backward one, once, on this reef, and take it to a floor I can defend against anyone who wants to fight me about it.

Here is the first discipline of the floor. I am not going to price the lost fishery, or the menhaden, or the livelihoods of the watermen, or the value of all the oysters that bar would have grown if it had been left whole. Every one of those is a real cost and every one of them invites an argument I do not need to have. I am going to price one thing only, the thing that is physical and documented and beyond dispute. The reef bottom itself. The hard foundation that was stripped out of the water and never put back. What does it cost to physically rebuild the reef that was foreclosed?

And I am not going to build that number out of Darling's shell mountains, tempting as it is, because the mountains are the wrong measuring stick. Raw shell is cheap, and the dramatic figure you would get by multiplying a peak year across a century is the kind of figure a careful critic takes apart in an afternoon, because no operation produces its peak every year for a hundred years. The four-story piles are the image of the severance. They are not the price of it. The price is built from acres.

So here is the floor. Scientists who study the James River, which is the river system the Hampton bars belong to, have measured how much high-quality oyster reef bottom was lost there across the twentieth century. Between 1910 and 1981 the James lost about two thousand seven hundred acres of it. I use the James figure and I tell you plainly that I am using it as the stand-in for the Hampton bars, because the careful historical acreage exists for the James and not for the Hampton Bar alone, and I would rather name my proxy than hide it. Two thousand seven hundred acres of foreclosed reef. Now multiply that by what it actually costs to rebuild an acre of reef on lower-Bay bottom where oysters still set on their own, which the restoration record puts somewhere between about seventy-three hundred and thirteen thousand five hundred dollars an acre.

That gives a floor of roughly twenty to thirty-seven million dollars.

That is the conservative number. It is deliberately the smallest defensible figure, built from the smallest documented loss and the cheapest documented rebuild cost, because a floor that errs low is a floor nobody can knock over. If you instead use the average cost of completed reef restoration across the whole Bay, the figure climbs toward two hundred million. I am not leading with that one. I am leading with the floor. Twenty to thirty-seven million dollars to physically rebuild the reef bottom foreclosed in this one river system. That is the column, added up, the way Chapter 1 left columns empty and Chapter 2 began to fill them.

Now set that number next to what the reef was worth while it stood, because the gap between the two is the whole argument.

The watermen who brought the oysters up were paid by the bushel at the dock, and the dockside price runs around thirty-five dollars a bushel. Two hundred thousand bushels in a peak year at thirty-five dollars is about seven million dollars, and I will not multiply that across the decades for the same reason I would not multiply the shells. Seven million dollars in a peak year, paid to the men who did the bringing-up. But that dockside seven million was never what the oyster was worth. It was the fee Hampton charged to ship its own wealth away. By the time those same oysters reached the tables they were sold to, at roughly two hundred oysters to a bushel and a few dollars an oyster on the plate, the same harvest was worth on the order of a hundred and twenty million dollars a year, realized in the dining rooms of Baltimore and New York and beyond. The watermen got the dockside fee. The cities got the oyster and the restaurants and the jobs and the culture and the tax base, the entire downstream economy of the Bay's own abundance, built everywhere except the Bay.

Hold the three numbers together, because together they are the indictment. The value the reef produced, around a hundred and twenty million a year, dispersed up and out to other regions' tables a century ago. The labor fee, around seven million in a peak year, stayed at the dock. And the cost, the twenty to thirty-seven million it takes to rebuild the foreclosed reef, stayed home and went unpaid, and is coming due now. Value goes up and out. Cost stays and settles down. It is the same sentence I wrote about a man in an office and a county under coal, written one more time in water.

And here is the fact that I think makes the case impossible to argue with.

The reef is already being rebuilt. Right now. And the public is paying for the whole thing.

In Maryland alone, more than ninety-two million dollars in combined state and federal money has gone into rebuilding oyster reef across the sanctuaries, roughly thirteen hundred acres of it, all of it public money. Across the whole Bay the public figure runs to something on the order of a hundred and ten million dollars, paid by federal agencies and the two states, by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration and the Army Corps of Engineers and the Maryland and Virginia resource agencies. Every dollar of it public. And out at the Navy's Hampton Roads shoreline, in the very waters this chapter has been standing in, sailors and federal agencies stacked the concrete oyster castles into the river themselves, the bill carried by the Navy and the public agencies, the oyster spat supplied by the public restoration program. I cannot give you the dollar figure for that one installation, because it was never published, and I am not going to invent a number to make a point. But I can tell you exactly who paid for it and exactly who did not. The Navy paid. The taxpayers paid. The industry that took a world's-largest oyster operation's worth of value out of that water and shipped it to other people's cities paid nothing into the reef's return.

This is the same shape as McDowell County, and I want you to see that it is the same shape, because the rhyme is the proof that we are looking at a mechanism and not an accident. In the coalfields, the value of the coal dispersed across a continent and the cost of the black lung and the orange creeks and the wrecked land stayed in the hollows, and when the bill came due it was the public trust fund and the public reclamation money that paid it, because the companies that had collected the value were gone or shielded or simply never on the hook. Here the value of the oyster dispersed to the cities and the cost of the foreclosed reef stayed in the water, and when the bill came due it was public money and Navy labor that paid it. The defendant is the same in both, and in both it is not in the room. Coal or oyster, dry land or open water, the instrument that should have priced the cost into the value at the moment of the transaction does not exist, so the cost gets severed loose and floats free until it lands, decades later, on whoever happens to be standing nearest when it comes down. Usually that is the public.

So understand what the Restitution Bond actually does, because it is more modest than it sounds and that modesty is its strength. It does not invent a new bill. The bill already exists. We are already paying it, ninety-two million in Maryland and a hundred and ten million across the Bay and a Navy crew on its knees in the Hampton Roads mud stacking concrete blocks. The Restitution Bond does one thing the present arrangement refuses to do. It names who should have been holding that bill all along, and it moves the cost back onto the value, instead of leaving it severed onto the public. It does not pay a single waterman a single dollar, because the moment you start cutting checks to individuals you start an argument about which ones and how much that drowns the whole idea. It pays into the reef. It funds the foundation. It rebuilds the commons that was drawn down, and it asks the parties who drew it down, and their successors, to be the ones who fund the rebuilding, rather than handing the invoice, one more time, to everyone.

Twenty to thirty-seven million dollars. That is the floor. The real figure is higher, and the true social cost, the fishery and the menhaden and the watermen's lost living and the hundred years of dispersed wealth, is higher still, and I have left all of it off the books on purpose, because the floor is the part nobody can take away from me. It is the smallest honest number in a very large injury, and I would rather stand on a number I can defend to the last decimal than reach for a bigger one and hand a critic the lever to dismiss the whole.

---

I started this chapter by asking you to love the water before I took it apart, and I have now taken it apart, so let me end where the love started and tell you the one thing I actually believe.

The reef can come back. That is the part the coal could never offer. A coal seam, once burned, is gone, and a drowned lung does not refill. But an oyster reef is a renewable thing, a living commons that rebuilds itself if you give it back its foundation, and the proof of it is that it is rebuilding right now, in the very water this chapter has walked, under public money and Navy hands. Moore, who does not deal in false comfort, will tell you what it looks like when it works. *With oysters,* he said, *we can adapt to climate change, support the Bay's ecological, economic, and social resilience, and build vibrant communities where people and nature thrive together.* The fullness is not gone for good. It is foreclosed, not destroyed, and a foreclosure can be answered.

What is missing is not the will and not the science and not even the money, because the money is already being spent. What is missing is the instrument, the one that would have put the cost on the value at the start, and would put it there now, so that the reef's return is funded by the wealth the reef produced and not by the public that never ate the oyster.

I keep thinking about Biggie's six-pack, and the lunch waiting in the water, and the man with the mechanic shop who works the same beds and cannot do what Biggie did. The distance between those two men is the whole of what this book is trying to price. And I think about my father's hands in the oyster-shell concrete of a boardwalk that still stands, the substrate of the reef poured into pavement in a summer when nobody yet understood what they were paving with.

There is an instrument that could carry this. We have built one before, for a different resource, in another country, out of the rents of something pulled from the ground, and it worked, and it is still working. What was done for the oil could be done for the oyster.

That is the next chapter.
