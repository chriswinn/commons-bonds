# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 3: The Waterman

---

The first work boat out of Old Point Comfort leaves in a light that has not yet decided to be light. A diesel coughs, catches, and settles into an idle you feel in the dock boards before you hear it. A man on the next pier says something to a man on the boat, and neither of them raises his voice, because at this hour sound carries across the water as if the water itself were an instrument. Behind them the Fort sits dark. Ahead, the mouth of the Bay opens out gray on gray, the channel marker blinking red at its own patient interval. Somewhere across the channel a tug is shoving supplies west toward Newport News, one more load for the HRBT expansion that has dragged on for years. In the slips, halyards ting against sailboat masts, a sound so constant here that you stop hearing it.

I live aboard a boat now, but I did not grow up on the water the way a waterman's son would have. When I went out as a kid, it was for a day of fun. Maybe the fish bit and maybe they didn't, and nothing in the house depended on which. A waterman is deep into his work before dawn, and he stays out until the hold holds enough to feed a family and put money in a pocket. He comes back in the afternoon, tired. Some afternoons the hold is full of crabs. Some afternoons it is full of nothing, and the men coming home with nothing are quieter than the men coming home with crabs, though neither set is talkative. They tie up and hose down and walk to a truck. At four the next morning they will be at the slip again, or they will not, and the Bay will not notice the difference one way or the other. The slip will notice. The dock will notice. The man two slips over, who has been doing this since before any of them were born, will notice, because noticing is how a working waterfront keeps its books.

This chapter is about what those books are not allowed to record.

---

I am writing from Hampton, which once held one of the largest oyster-packing operations in the country. I grew up on this bay. Off the mouth of the Hampton River lies an extensive shoal called Hampton Bar, and in 1866 a New Yorker named James Sands Darling arrived to make his fortune on it. The old-timers advised him that the bar was a poor place for planting and harvesting oysters. He decided they were wrong. The firm took the name J.S. Darling and Son in 1884, when Frank Darling joined his father's existing oyster business, and the historical marker that stands on the waterfront today calls what the two of them built the largest such operation in the world.

Every day a fleet of log canoes, some of them built right on the Hampton waterfront, sailed out to the bar, and watermen tonged oysters up out of the dark. Back at the plant, 160 shuckers scooped the meat from 200,000 bushels of Chesapeake oysters a year, packed it into metal quart and gallon containers, and shipped it out in iced tubs to dinner tables and restaurants in Baltimore and New York and other cities. By 1938 the company was selling 50,000 gallons of oysters annually. The meat left. The shells stayed. They rode out of the plant on conveyor belts and were dumped on piles that dominated the Hampton waterfront in sight and in odor, mountains of shell rising more than four stories high, a Hampton landmark for years.

What the region did with the mountains tells you most of what this book is about. Some of the shell was hauled out to the Back River and dumped overboard to form reefs, and new oysters anchored on the old shell and grew. Most of it was sold and crushed and spread into roads and driveways and industrial wharves across the lower Bay, because crushed shell was cheaper than rock and kept a dirt road out of the mud. People drove on it. People parked on it. The driveway at my father's family's place was paved with oyster shells, like driveways and lanes all over this part of Virginia. One college summer my father helped pour the boardwalk at Buckroe Beach, working his way toward the next year's tuition, and the boardwalk still stands. I had that story wrong for years; I believed the boardwalk's concrete was packed with oyster shell, and my father, who poured it, is clear on the point: concrete and rocks, nothing else. The shells were not under the boardwalk. They were under the family driveway, and under half the region besides.

When I was a boy, somebody showed me photographs from around 1900 of the shell mountains and told me, in the way you pass along an old wives' tale, that people back then were supposed to have put the shells back in the water to help the water. The truth turns out to be both better and worse than the tale. Better, because some men did know; the Back River reefs are the proof. Worse, because the knowledge was sitting right there while most of the substrate went into pavement. An oyster needs something hard to set on, and shell is what the Bay had always provided itself. That is what the mountains behind the Darling plant actually were: the next century of bottom, stacked four stories high, sold off by the truckload. In recent years the Navy has been stacking concrete blocks called oyster castles along the shoreline at Naval Support Activity Hampton Roads, structures for young oysters to cling to that help protect the shore. We paved with the thing that builds reefs, and a generation later we are casting concrete to do what the shell would have done.

Hampton's seafood reach ran wider than oysters. The Darlings also ran a menhaden factory at Factory Point in Fox Hill, rendering thousands of tons of menhaden a year into fish meal for fertilizer and oil for industrial uses. In the 1870s, James McMenamin invented and patented a canning process that put cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in airtight tins and shipped it to tables far from the Bay, and Hampton was known up and down the coast as Crabtown.

The Darling operation shut down in 1979. I was born in 1980. I have never seen the shell mountains except in photographs, and nobody will see them again, because there are not enough oysters left in the Bay to pile shell four stories high. The whole abundance reached me secondhand, as my father's stories and other people's pictures. A renewable resource turning non-renewable is supposed to be a paradox. I was born into the after of one, which may be why it has never once struck me as a paradox.

---

The community on this water has a name. The Hampton neighborhood I am closest to is called Fox Hill, and Fox Hill is, or was, an island fishing community, the kind of place where for most of the twentieth century nearly every house had at least one person on the water. My grandfather, who worked at NASA Langley just up the road, building pieces of a different kind of America in a different kind of building, was the exception on his street. The neighbors on either side of him were on the water. The boys my father grew up with were the sons of watermen. My father did not become a waterman, but the men who taught him how to handle a small boat, and where the fish were running, and what the smell of the channel meant in late August, were men whose whole lives were the water. My family is one generation removed from the working life. We are zero generations removed from the community.

Most days at most hours, the Chesapeake looks like a postcard: flat water, soft light, the kind of bay you would put a child on. Ask any waterman about the chop and you will hear about the other days. On the ocean, a five-foot wave is barely worth a comment. In a boat of thirty-five or forty feet it is a gentle rocking, the kind that puts a man to sleep, because the tops of those waves run thirty feet apart or more; the distance between crests is the wavelength, and on the Atlantic the wavelength is long. The Bay does not build waves that shape. The Bay's wavelength runs short, short enough that a five-foot wave can come at you on something close to a five-foot wavelength, a wave very nearly square, and a square wave will turn the hardiest seaman green. The men who have worked this water for forty years know which days you do not go out, which days you go out but stay close to home, and which days the sky is lying about what the water is going to do in three hours, and they know all of it without consulting anything external to themselves. The water grants them the knowledge in exchange for one obligation: never mistake which day you are looking at.

The boats match the water. When I think of Chesapeake boats I do not think of superyachts; those are somewhere else in the world, which is its own small fact about where the water's value went. I think of the deadrise, a waterman's boat with nothing fancy about it, known on this water to ride the famous chop more steadily than most hulls. And I think of a waterman so familiar with his boat that he runs the motor out to the oyster beds and then barely uses it again, shifting among the beds for hours with one oar in the water, the subtlest movements easing the boat from here to there while he tends what is under it.

The clothes tell you the rest. A waterman wears rain slicks, fully waterproof, which keep him mostly dry and give him no warmth at all, so under them go thick full-body base layers and sweaters against the brutal cold of a rainy winter morning, because unlike the tourists he is on the water year round, in whatever the weather turns out to be. And he steps aboard on those mornings knowing a thing he does not say out loud: the weight of all that clothing means that if he goes into the freezing water, he will not get it off in time to swim to the surface. He knows the water he works would keep him. He goes anyway, because the house is not paid for, and a family does not eat on what stays in the Bay.

The community that grows up around that kind of work has a particular shape. When the watermen of a place like Fox Hill say they look out for each other, they are not reaching for a figure of speech; the work is built so that they have to. A man whose nets need rebuilding before the next season cannot rebuild them alone in a season's worth of spare hours, and there is no industry of net-rebuilders to hire. So a morning is set. At three or four, an hour when every man in this community would normally be on the water or driving to it, the boats stay tied up and the pickups come up whoever's lane needs the nets. Every man brings his own coffee. They work until it is done. Nobody calls it a favor, because a favor implies a choice, and what is operating here is closer to certainty: as surely as the sun rises and the sun sets, a morning is coming when the man who needs the help is you, or your son, or your father, and a community that fails to come on the day you need it has already failed before you needed it. The bargain holds for as long as the community holds.

And the bargain has one clause that is nearly impossible to render for anyone who has not stood inside it: what happens when one of the men who said he would be there is not. Everyone stops. The nets go down. Somebody picks up a phone and calls somebody else, and within ten minutes the whole morning has reorganized itself around the question of where the missing man is, because every man there has done this work long enough to know that the only thing that keeps a waterman from being where he said he would be is something serious enough that he cannot tell anyone about it. Maybe his truck. Maybe his health. Maybe he was on the water at four-thirty and did not come back, and the water is the part of the morning the rest of the men go and check on first. The older men carry that possibility in their bodies the way a cook's body carries the possibility of burns.

That is the community Fox Hill was for most of my father's life, and the community I have watched, across my own, slowly thin.

---

To understand what is happening on the Chesapeake right now, start with what the Chesapeake was. Not the prehistoric Bay, not the Bay before the Powhatan or before the English came up the James in 1607, but the Bay in a register the people working the water still carry in their bodies. I have lived near this water long enough to have known three rough generations of the men who work it. The Bay the grandfathers fished was not the Bay the fathers fished, and the Bay the fathers fished is not the Bay the sons would have fished, had there been a Bay to fish. Across those three generations the resource has been visibly thinning, in every part of the Bay, in every season of every year.

The oyster is the place to begin, because the oyster is the species the historical record carries most clearly. In the late 1880s the Chesapeake produced something on the order of fifteen million bushels of oysters a year. By the 1990s, that number had fallen to under two hundred thousand. Two orders of magnitude in a century; a 99 percent decline. Few fisheries anywhere in the world have lost that much of themselves and remained recognizable as the same fishery, and there is a real argument that the Chesapeake oyster fishery did not. What the men were going out for in 1990 was a different thing from what their grandfathers had been going out for in 1890, even though the boats looked similar and the tongs looked similar and the dock at the end of the day looked similar.

No weather explains a 99 percent decline, and no run of bad luck does either. The mechanisms are now well documented and were largely understood at the time. The first wave was overharvesting: dredges scraping the reefs flat, season after season, faster than the reefs could rebuild. The second wave was disease, MSX arriving in the late 1950s and Dermo in the years that followed, parasites the weakened, low-density populations had no resilience against. The third wave was the land itself coming down the rivers: sediment from disturbed ground smothering the oyster spat where they tried to set, and nutrient loading feeding algal blooms that consumed the dissolved oxygen the rest of the system depended on. Each wave compounded the last. Reefs that had once been visible at low tide, that ships in the colonial period had to chart around, were ground down to flat, silt-buried bottom. An oyster needs something hard to set on, and by the late twentieth century much of the hard bottom the oysters had built across centuries had been ground into the same mud the runoff was laying over it.

Hold the oyster reef next to a coal seam for a moment. A coal seam is finite from the day it forms; you can extract it faster or slower, but you cannot extract more of it than was laid down. The reef plays by other rules. Left standing, it is an instrument that produces more oysters every year, for as long as you do not damage the instrument. Take some, leave the rest to spawn, come back next season. Done correctly, the harvest does not diminish across the generations. You can do it, and your son can do it, and your son's son can do it. The fishery makes more of itself.

What happened in the Chesapeake is what happens when the rate of taking exceeds the rate of making. For a long while, the difference can be invisible. The harvest is still good. The price still feels right. The boats come back full-ish, most days, and sure, there are good seasons and bad seasons. Decline on this water moves the way a sunset moves: you watch it and it seems to take forever, and then you look away for a moment, or you only blink, and it is gone. There comes a season when one more light year cannot be excused, and the difference turns visible and irreversible in the same instant, because the structure that produced the renewal was itself being taken all along, written off as the cost of doing business. The reef the oysters built was the bottom the next generation would have set on. Grind the reef down and you take more than this year's oysters. You take the capacity of the bottom to make any oysters at all. Past that point, leaving the system alone no longer heals it. You put something back, or you accept that what was lost stays lost.

Nobody marks the day this happens. The men going out the next morning go out the same way they went out the morning before. But something has been done that cannot be undone by any decision the next morning's men can make.

My father grew up on Fox Hill with the story I am about to tell, and he has told it so many times that I can finish it for him now, and do, whenever he starts it again. One of the oystermen of his boyhood was a man called Biggie. Biggie was built like an oak, not tall, in the way some big men are not tall, but solid through the shoulders and the back, with the kind of solidity that comes from doing one specific work for most of an adult life. The work was oysters. He had been at it since he was old enough to be useful in a boat with whoever first brought him into one. The bars he worked were bars he had worked his entire life, and the boat he ran was a boat suited to the work and nothing else. You could watch him shift around his beds with one oar in the water, no wasted motion anywhere in him, and know without being told that his family had been doing this for generations.

Every morning he went out, Biggie carried aboard a six-pack of Coca-Cola in a wire carrier, with no ice, no cooler, and no lunch. The first time my father went out with him, a small boy then and honestly puzzled, he asked Biggie what he was going to eat. And every time after that, he asked again, because the answer was worth hearing again. Biggie would smile and say that his lunch was still in the water, waiting for him, as fresh as it could be.

Once the sun stood high enough and the work had found its rhythm, Biggie would settle into eating oysters as he worked. He kept a particular spot on the boat, near his body, where he set the oyster he was about to shuck, no reaching, no lifting of the arm, and the motion he shucked with was the motion of a man who has spent a working life reducing the job to its smallest possible movement: a quarter turn of the wrist, the shell parting, the meat coming up clean. He ate as he worked. My father tried, more than once, to count how many oysters Biggie put away in a single day on the water, and he told me, when I was old enough to ask, that he never managed it. The trouble was not that there were too many to count, though there may have been. The trouble was that the eating was so woven into the work that there was never one moment when an oyster started or stopped being eaten. It was something Biggie did alongside everything else he was doing, the way breathing is something a person does alongside everything else.

Biggie has been dead for more than thirty years. He died before Virginia stopped its commercial shad fishery in 1994, a date the men still on the water sometimes use to mark when things were still working. He went out with what an earlier generation of men had gone out with, and he came back with what they had come back with, and the bars sounded under his tongs the way the bars had sounded under the tongs of the men before him.

The bars do not sound that way anymore. And the motion Biggie's body had pared down to a quarter turn of the wrist is not being shown to any younger man at Fox Hill, because no younger man at Fox Hill is going out to bars that would let him learn it. The motion is dying with the men who hold it.

---

The men who could tell this story best do not use my words for it. Sit with them on a dock late in a season that has not gone the way the season was supposed to go and you will not hear *renewable*; you will not hear *regeneration rate*. You will hear what they remember. A grandfather who went out of a place that no longer exists as a working dock. Boats their fathers ran, named after wives or daughters, some of the names still on the water and some not. Standing in the bow as a small boy, off the mouth of the Poquoson River on a calm summer day, looking down through the water at the bottom, actually seeing it, the grass and the crabs walking through the grass, a thing a child cannot do anymore in most parts of the Bay where it could once be done. The water greener than it used to be, in a way that does not feel like life. The smell at the dock gone slightly different in late August. A kind of catch their fathers came home with that does not come home anymore.

A man in his sixties who has worked the Lower Bay since he was twelve does not need a stock assessment to tell him that something has changed. He has been running the comparison every morning for fifty years.

This is the texture of generational witness on the water, and there is nothing nostalgic in it. In tone it sits closer to the way you might describe a relative's slow illness: small, precise observations from someone who has been paying attention long enough that the attention has become a part of how he sees. Around men like this, what you meet is not anger so much as a patient, unsentimental clarity. They are not asking to be pitied; most of them would refuse the offer. They are telling you what they have seen.

What they have seen is the disappearance of margins. Seasons that used to carry a year now carry only part of one. Species that used to fill in for each other when one ran short no longer fill in the same way. The crabs run smaller, on average, than the crabs they used to bring in. The striped bass runs their fathers had timed to the calendar do not run on the calendar anymore. The water itself seems to keep a different temper. None of this is anecdote in the dismissive sense. It is observation across forty and fifty years, one of the longest continuous attentional records any working person in America keeps, and the instrument is their bodies, and the data is their memory.

J.C. Hudgins, who runs the Virginia Watermen's Association and works the Piankatank River in the Middle Bay, has done the math out loud. *You're lucky if you can put $150 in your pocket per day,* he told *Maryland Matters*. *And it ain't no easy job. You're freezing your butt off. It's cold.* On the buying end: *People aren't going to spend $20 for a pint of oysters. It's pretty expensive eating. I think people are feeding their families in other ways.*

---

Striped bass, what they call rockfish on the Bay, was one of the three fish I loved most to eat as a boy. The other two were red snapper and grouper, warmer-water fish, and I make no claim for them as Chesapeake species; I only say that they were on our table in the 1980s, and then, somewhere along the way, they were not. A boy raised around watermen absorbs the rule early: you take a fish in its season and you leave it alone otherwise, because taking it out of season throws off a balance nobody around you needs to explain. Then came one bad season, and another. At first the change was just not catching them as often, a passing thought that one or another of them had slipped off the restaurant menus in the last couple of years. Only much later did I understand that the difference everyone waved away as a bad season or two had been the last of the seasons, the end of any meaningful quantity, and that we kept taking, all of us, as much as we could carry to the shore, the whole way down. The striped bass is the one of the three whose story the institutions wrote down as it happened, which is why it is the species that taught Maryland and Virginia, in the 1980s, what intervention can do.

I am old enough to remember it coming on. The watermen I knew as a boy, at the marinas where my family kept boats, had begun talking about something they had not had to talk about before. I did not understand the politics of it then. I understood, the way a child can understand without being told, that a thing had reached a point where the men whose work depended on it had agreed, with whatever reluctance, to stop the work for a long time so that the thing could come back.

By the late 1970s, the population had collapsed badly enough that Maryland and Virginia, working with the federal government, agreed to a moratorium. For five years on the Maryland side, beginning in 1985, no one fished commercially for striped bass at all: not the watermen, not the charter fleet, not the recreational anglers. The species was given a generation off. It was a hard ask of the people whose income depended on the fishery; many of them lost a livelihood for the duration, and some of them did not come back to the work. But the moratorium worked, in the narrow sense that the moratorium had been designed to do. The spawning population recovered, and by 1995 the fishery had been declared restored. The men who had given up five years saw, at the end of those five years, an instrument that had been brought back from a place very few fisheries get brought back from.

That recovery is part of why what is happening now is hard for the men who remember it, because what is happening now to the rockfish is what was happening to the rockfish before the moratorium. The juvenile recruitment numbers are coming in below the long-term average, year after year. In 2024 the Maryland young-of-year index was 2.0 fish per sample, against a long-term average of 11.0, and it was the sixth such season in a row. A bad season is a bad season. Six in a row is a structural signal.

Allison Colden, the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's Maryland executive director, has watched that signal accumulate across the years and has put the situation in terms the watermen would recognize. *Maryland's striped bass surveys have made it clear,* she has said in public statements; *we're facing a looming disaster. Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future.* In a different interview, more bluntly: *there are very few levers left to pull.* Many people in Maryland, she has said elsewhere, *rely on striped bass for their food, their businesses, and their futures. Without action, that will all be at risk.* The rising water temperatures and the low-oxygen periods of summer, both worsened by warming, are *pummeling* the species, in her word, during the months it can least afford to be pummeled.

The men on the water did not need the telling. They felt it before the survey numbers came in, and what the survey numbers do is force the institutions to acknowledge what the watermen have been carrying as a kind of private foreknowledge, the way miners know what the dust is doing to them years before the X-rays come back. The institutional acknowledgment, when it comes, comes late, later than the data would have allowed, later than the watermen had warned, and late in a way the moratorium of 1985 was not, because in 1985 there had been a political will that has thinned in the decades since, and the regulatory architecture that used to be capable of asking a fishery to take a generation off has lost some of the capacity to ask.

So when the watermen read the rockfish now, the thing they are actually reading is whether the institutional system that was built to manage the Bay is capable of acting on what its own science is telling it. So far in this round, the answer has been mostly no. Catch limits get tightened in increments smaller than the science has indicated are warranted. Emergency actions get debated longer than the species can spare. The committees meet, the substitute motions are offered, and the language of *err on the side of conservation* gets answered with the language of *we are not yet certain enough to act.* The asymmetry is the point: when the offer on the table was to increase the harvest, the committees acted with confidence; now that the offer is to reduce it, the committees have discovered an unfamiliar caution.

Bill Goldsborough, the fisheries director at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation through the rockfish moratorium years, told *Smithsonian Magazine* how the institutional time-scale looks from inside the science. *Our objective is to try to restore the health of bay and aquatic life so that it can support viable fisheries,* he has said. *We see that task taking place over a longer term [...]. But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment.*

A retired waterman who worked the Bay through the 1985 moratorium, into the recovery, and into the present decline can give you the whole arc in a single sentence from his own deck. He gave up five years of his life so that a fishery could come back; he watched it come back; he has watched it begin to go again; and he has been told, gently and not so gently, by people whose job is to manage the resource, that the political conditions for another moratorium do not exist. Nobody has told him what the political conditions for another collapse are.

---

The rockfish recovery is not the only intervention story this Bay has produced. In January 1994, a few years after the rockfish moratorium ended, Virginia closed its commercial shad fishery, the spring run that had been one of the Bay's reliable fisheries through most of the twentieth century. Shad live most of their lives in the open Atlantic and come into the Bay each spring to spawn, running up the tributary rivers to lay their eggs in fresh water, which makes the Bay the spawning ground for the shad of an entire region; when its rivers fail the shad, the shad fail in the ocean too. The men whose fathers had timed the shad run to the calendar were still on the water when the runs stopped coming, and they could tell you the spring the nets in late March began to come up empty. The closure was meant to do for the shad what the moratorium had done for the rockfish: give the species a generation off so the runs could rebuild. The runs did not come back after 1994. The science on why has not converged on a single explanation. What it has converged on is the absence of a recovery. There are not, in any working sense, shad in the Bay anymore. The same instrument that worked once for rockfish was tried for shad and arrived too late.

---

Then there is the blue crab.

A crab builds nothing. Its life cycle is short, a year and a half or sometimes two, which means in principle the population can rebound from a bad year quickly if the next year is good, and that resilience is one of the reasons the Chesapeake blue crab fishery was, for a long time, the Bay's most reliable seasonal income for working watermen. The crabs were here. They came back every spring. Through the warm months they were tonged and trotlined and potted, and sold to picking houses up and down the Bay, where mostly women picked the meat by hand into containers that ended up in restaurants in cities where most of the people eating them had never seen a crab pot in the water.

The blue crab fishery is now in a kind of trouble the oyster fishery already lived through and the rockfish fishery is living through. The 2025 winter dredge survey put the Bay-wide population at around 238 million crabs, the second-lowest level since the survey began in 1990, and the sixth consecutive year of below-average numbers. *The red flags are flying for blue crabs,* Colden has said. And, in the structural framing: *with more than five years of below average crab numbers, it is clear that changing conditions in the Bay are undermining the current management of this important species.*

That sentence carries more than it appears to. *Changing conditions* are *undermining* a management regime that was designed for an earlier Bay. The regulatory architecture is being outrun by the system it was built to govern, and the trouble is not that the rules are wrong; the rules were calibrated to a Bay that no longer exists. The committees that set the limits still meet on the schedule the previous Bay had. The crabs have left that schedule. The Bay has left that schedule.

The men on the water have been reading this from the deck for decades. They do not call it any of that. They say what they see. They aren't where they used to be. They aren't running when they used to run. The bigger ones are gone. We aren't catching what we used to catch.

---

There is a fish you have probably never eaten, small and oily and silver, that runs in schools so dense their movement can flatten the surface of the water like a passing wind, and by weight it is one of the most important fish in the Atlantic ecosystem. On the New Jersey side they call it bunker. In New England it is pogy. On the Bay, it is just menhaden.

You do not eat it because it is not pleasant to eat. Nearly everything else eats it. It is the forage fish at the base of the Bay's food web, the species the striped bass and the bluefish and the ospreys and the loons and the dolphins all come for. It is also the species harvested, in the largest single fishery on the East Coast, by an industrial reduction operation out of Reedville, Virginia, which catches it at scale and grinds it down into fish meal, fish oil, omega-3 supplements, pet food, and aquaculture feed. The catch leaves the Bay in tank trucks and railcars and on ships. No person eats it. An industrial process converts it into inputs for industries somewhere else.

Structurally, this is the interesting case, because the menhaden is the food web. Remove enough of it and the rockfish do not have enough to eat in summer, when they are heat-stressed and oxygen-starved and trying to put on weight. In some Bay tributaries the ospreys have begun to fail to fledge their young, and the suspicion among the people who watch ospreys is that the parent birds are not bringing in enough small oily fish to feed a growing brood. People who like the phrase describe the Bay-wide signal as *a hollowing*: the bigger species look fine until you ask whether they are reproducing and whether their young are surviving, and then the picture shifts.

How much menhaden to take out of the Bay is the longest-running fisheries-policy fight in the region. There is a Bay-specific cap, set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission in 2006 and reduced in 2017 to its current level of 51,000 metric tons, which the conservation side regards as a floor not a ceiling and the industry side regards as a hard-fought concession. The relevant scientific committee has, in recent years, been recommending coastwide reductions and Bay-specific precautions; the management board has, in recent years, been adopting reductions smaller than the committee recommended and on schedules slower than the committee proposed. *Just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should,* Colden has said, *we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that that's warranted.*

To the people working the science, the gap is no abstraction. Genny Nesslage, an associate research professor at the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science, said it plainly to WHRO: *We don't know the abundance of menhaden in the bay at any given time relative to the coast.* Rob Latour, a Virginia Institute of Marine Science menhaden researcher, said it more bluntly to the same outlet: *We have no data to really do this in a meaningful way, from a biological perspective.* In 2023, the Virginia Institute of Marine Science drafted a $3 million proposal for a Bay-specific menhaden study. The Virginia General Assembly declined to fund it.

Increases adopted at the speed of the appetite for them; reductions adopted at the speed of the resistance to them. The watermen know the asymmetry. The ospreys feel it.

More than any other Bay-management debate, the cap on menhaden makes visible who the system is built to serve. The fishery is not feeding people in any direct sense; it is supplying intermediate inputs to industries whose end customers have no idea that the small oily fish at the base of the Chesapeake food web are being moved through a Reedville plant and into their lives. Reedville sits at the mouth of the Great Wicomico River in Northumberland County, and the plant has employed several hundred people, in shifts that follow the seasons the menhaden run. The end customers are the consumer of an omega-3 capsule in Indianapolis, the owner of a salmon farm in Chile, the buyer of a forty-pound bag of dog food in a Phoenix grocery. The value disperses across a continent and across industries. The cost concentrates here, on the rockfish, on the ospreys, on the watermen who depend on a Bay that depends on the menhaden.

On the last day of 2025, a Virginia resident named Tanya O'Connor filed a 349-page petition with the Virginia Marine Resources Commission asking the agency to declare a temporary moratorium on the Bay's reduction fishery until it produced Bay-specific science. The petition's central finding put the absence in regulatory language: the commission *did not have*, it said, *and still does not have*, *a Bay-wide estimate of menhaden biomass, age structure, spatial distribution, recruitment trends, or localized depletion risk.* For legal grounding it reached back to Virginia's public-trust doctrine: *Commonwealth v. City of Newport News*, 158 Va. 521 (1932); *Taylor v. Commonwealth*, 102 Va. 759 (1904); *Bradford v. Nature Conservancy*, 224 Va. 181 (1982). The commission held a hearing on April 21, 2026, and received 1,266 public comments, and then voted six to two against the petition, and the moratorium did not move forward.

The shape of the whole sequence is familiar. Coal had the same shape.

---

The Chesapeake watershed covers some 64,000 square miles across six states and the District of Columbia, which means the water that makes the Bay starts as rain on a corn field in central Pennsylvania, or a soybean field on the Eastern Shore, or a parking lot in Northern Virginia, or a chicken house on the Delmarva, or a wastewater plant in Baltimore, or a suburban lawn in Loudoun County. By the time it reaches the Bay, it is carrying everything those landscapes have given it: nitrogen from fertilizer, phosphorus from manure and runoff, sediment from disturbed ground, pesticides, pharmaceuticals, whatever the upstream economy has not contained.

The science of this has been understood for forty years. The Chesapeake Bay Program, established in 1983, has been measuring nutrient loads, modeling them, allocating them, and asking the upstream parties to reduce them ever since, with partial successes and significant failures. Point-source pollution from wastewater plants and factory discharges has, in many places, been reduced. The recent review by the Program's own science advisory committee, evaluating the forty-year effort, was as plain as such reports are allowed to be: the cleanup is not on track to meet its goals. The agricultural sector has not been required to do what the science said the agricultural sector needed to do. The political will to require it has not formed.

The science was right, the targets were modeled, the schedule was set, the jurisdictions agreed, and the loads, in the places where the loads were largest, did not come down at the rate the agreement said they would, because the political weight of the upstream agricultural economy was greater than the political weight of the downstream fishery economy, and the decisions accumulated, year by year, in the direction of the heavier weight.

From the deck of a sailboat in summer, in the years I have been on it, I have watched the green of this water change in a way that does not feel like life. The grass beds I could see through the water in the early 2000s are not visible the same way anymore. The watermen know all of this in a more sustained register; they have been watching it for decades. They know that the runoff hitting the grass beds in Mobjack Bay started as fertilizer on a field they have never seen, owned by a farmer they will never meet, in a state none of their children have lived in. They know that the dead zones, the seasonal volumes of low-oxygen water that form in the deeper parts of the Bay every summer, suffocating the bottom-dwelling species that cannot move and stressing the swimming species that can, are the downstream signature of an agriculture-and-development pattern over which they have never had any voice.

They live at the bottom of a watershed whose accounting does not include them. Everything every upstream actor does has to come past their boats; nothing they do reaches the upstream actors at all. The asymmetry is the geography, and the geography does the work the accounting will not.

There is a man on Fox Hill I am leaving unnamed here. He is alive, still on the water, and the question of how he wants to appear in a book is not yet settled between us. He is a large man, not tall, who has been on the Bay since he was a boy. He is from the same Fox Hill world Biggie was from, and his work and his life have unfolded along the next stretch of the same arc.

He is a crabber and a fisherman, and he has been one all his working life, on the water near Fox Hill and Poquoson that he has always known. What he has watched, across his decades on it, is the math change for a man like him. The catches have come back thinner. The costs of fuel and gear and the slip have moved in the other direction. The species he came up working are not all of them still running. None of these are observations particular to him; they are the conditions every man still working this water has had to absorb in his own way. What is particular to him is what he has done with the absorption.

He still gets up at two or three in the morning. He still goes out and pulls his crab pots, and he runs them the way he ran them when he was younger, except that the math at the end of the day is different now than it was then. So he comes back to the dock by mid-morning, hoses down the boat, and drives to the mechanic shop he owns on the Peninsula, the one with his name on the sign and his crab pots stacked behind the locked fence in the back lot, behind the cars he is working on for customers, and he works the shop until five or six in the evening. What he calls this is between him and the people who know him. What it does, in any case, is keep the family math balancing, the boat in the slip, the license current, the presence on the water continuing.

He did not quit the water. He could not. But the water that fed Biggie's family by itself will not feed his by itself, and so he has spent decades building, on his own time and his own dime, the second economy that allows him to not quit it. The accounting that made the older men's lives possible, the accounting in which the crab and the boat and the long days were enough, does not work for him. He has filled the gap with the only thing he had left to fill it with: more of himself.

I have not asked him what he would say about all this, and the chapter does not have his answer to put on this page. What I can guess, from watching him and from knowing the men around him, is that if asked over a beer at the end of a long day he would say he is glad to still be on the water and that he is grateful for the shop. The accounting this book is trying to make visible is not, necessarily, the accounting he would put on himself. It is what the surrounding economy has been accepting from him, and from many men like him up and down this Bay, in exchange for a continued supply of the seafood the surrounding economy has not stopped wanting.

---

There is a particular kind of community that grew up on the working waterfronts of the Bay, and a particular kind of decision the people in those communities are now being asked to make.

Tangier Island, in the middle of the Lower Bay, is two square miles of low-elevation marsh and sand that has been continuously inhabited since the 1770s. By some accounts, the accent the people there speak is closer to a colonial-era English than to anything else still spoken in North America. The economy has been the water, crabs in the warm months and oysters in the cold ones, the rhythms and the vocabulary carried through eight generations of the island's families. The island is also losing about fifteen feet of shoreline a year to erosion, and the projections suggest most of it will be uninhabitable within the lifetime of children currently in the school. The mayor and the men on the docks have asked, repeatedly, for the engineering that would buy them another few decades. The asks have, so far, mostly not been answered at the scale that would matter.

James "Ooker" Eskridge, the mayor of Tangier and a working waterman for more than fifty years, told the Thomson Reuters Foundation what was at stake: *We're talking about saving a culture, the people, (a) way of life*, he said, and then, *the whole package.* Cook Cannon, a former Tangier waterman who has spent the years since at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation, told *Smithsonian Magazine* what the prospect looked like from inside the island, sitting in his island home on a cold January night. *I could cry to think about what's going to happen to Tangier,* he has said.

Smith Island, the Maryland counterpart twelve miles to the north, is in roughly the same shape. The picking houses there have been thinning; for generations, mostly older women picked the meat from soft-shell crabs that the men on the boats brought in, and the young people are leaving for the mainland. There is a saying, on Smith, that you get mud between your toes when you spend time there, and that the mud means the island never leaves you. The women who say it are mostly past the age where leaving was an option. The children who hear it have been on the mainland for a decade.

Up the Bay on Tilghman Island in Maryland, the same work continues. Pat Whewell, photographed culling oysters aboard Lonnie Gowe's boat in Broad Creek in 2023, is one of the named on-water presences in the Maryland working-waterfront record; Chumley Fisher and Chet Schwartz, photographed three years later offloading oysters on Tilghman, are two more. The pictures ran in *Maryland Matters*. The picking houses on Tilghman, like the picking houses on Smith, have been thinning.

Hampton, where I sit, is not Tangier or Smith. Hampton is bigger, more diversified, more capable of absorbing the slow contraction of its working waterfront into the larger economy of the city. The boats are still here, the slips are still rented, the dockmasters still know who is going out tomorrow and who is not. But I have watched the marina where I keep my own boat tip, across the years I have kept her there, from a place where the working operators had most of the slips toward a place where the recreational owners do, and on a long enough horizon the trajectory is the same trajectory. The number of working watermen on the Lower Bay has been falling for a generation. The picking houses are closing. The boatbuilders who used to build wooden deadrises are mostly gone. The supply chain that supports a working waterfront thins by one capacity at a time, the welders, the engine shops, the ice plants, the buyers, the haulers, until the community that used to come at three in the morning to help with somebody's nets does not, in some places, have the bodies to come anymore. At some point the thinning crosses a line past which the next year's young person who might have stayed cannot find the work that would have made staying possible. They leave, and the waterfront thins by one more capacity, and the next year someone else faces a slightly thinner version of the same choice.

What is being asked of these communities is something the broader economy does not have a name for. They are being asked, in effect, to disperse: to absorb their own transition costs, to relocate their families on their own dime, to retrain their young into industries that are not available locally, to abandon places their ancestors have lived in for two and three centuries, to give up languages and skills and seasonal rhythms that took those generations to develop. They are being asked to do this without compensation, without acknowledgment, and largely without notice from the upstream economy whose runoff and whose climate forcing and whose harvest pressure have, between them, made the staying impossible. The people in these places are not asking for sympathy. They are asking, mostly, to be allowed to keep doing what they know how to do, in the place they know how to do it, for as long as the water and the regulation and the market allow it.

The chapter on McDowell County described this same pattern from the inside of the hollow. Only the accent and the work are different.

At the marina one October, I asked a waterman I know, a man in his fifties on the Lower Bay, what he was telling his children. He paused a long time before answering. He glanced at the water. What came back was something about how the work is hard and the money is uncertain and the regulations are unpredictable. He may tell you he has not encouraged his son to go into it. Some sons go anyway; the son does it because it is what he knows and what his grandfather did, and the choice is the son's. He may tell you that his daughter, now in nursing school in Norfolk, has more options than he had at her age, and that he is glad about that, and that he is also aware there is something in the gain that he does not, in his bones, fully celebrate. The thinning of the family-on-the-water, across the generations, is a kind of liberation that is also a kind of loss.

What the discouragement amounts to, when you add it up across the Lower Bay and the Eastern Shore and the small islands, is a generation-scale withdrawal of the human capital that would have continued the fishery. The fathers are not investing the years of patient teaching into the sons that their fathers invested into them. They are withholding the investment because the return, for the son, is not what the return was, and they are not willing to teach a son into a trade whose floor they cannot promise. The community that took five generations to build the knowledge of where the crabs run in late October is not building a sixth generation that will know it the same way.

That is its own kind of cost, and it shows up on no stock assessment: the slow draining of an inheritance that took centuries to accumulate, into a generation that has been quietly told it cannot be passed on.

The children, for their part, are mostly going. To Norfolk, to Richmond, to the Hampton Roads tech corridor, to the medical centers, to the military bases, wherever a paycheck is more predictable than a season on the water. Most of them carry, somewhere, a respect for what their fathers did that they will not put into words. Some of them come back on weekends and ride out on the boat one more time to remember how it felt. Some of them never come back. The slip stays empty after the father retires, the waterfront becomes one boat thinner, and the next father and the next son hear the news and run their own arithmetic.

Cameron Evans, a Tangier native then studying at Virginia Wesleyan, named the question for the Thomson Reuters Foundation in his own words: *If you can't save an island a mile and a half long, how are you going to save the East Coast...* Andy Harris, who represents Maryland's Eastern Shore in Congress, has framed the same arithmetic from the other side of the aisle. *Our watermen are pretty resilient,* he has told *Maryland Matters*. *They're like our farmers. They have good years and bad years. But if you have a couple bad seasons in a row, then you're in trouble.*

A paradox has emerged in the most recent years, and it is worth setting down carefully, because it is the most precise illustration the Bay has produced of the cost-severance pattern this book is tracking.

The Maryland oyster restoration program has succeeded, by the metrics it set for itself: five tributary-scale sanctuaries, hundreds of acres of rebuilt reef, tens of millions of oysters per year added to the rebuilt structures. The oyster population in Maryland has roughly tripled since 2005. *Large-scale oyster restoration is working,* Colden has said. The reefs are setting, the oysters are spawning, the structures are accumulating. *Our effort to bring oysters back from the brink in Chesapeake Bay is working,* she has said elsewhere, adding that *continued recovery is not guaranteed.*

Mitch Tarnowski, who leads Maryland's Department of Natural Resources oyster reef surveys, summed the paradox up in twelve words. *There are plenty of oysters out there,* he told *Maryland Matters*. *They're just not being harvested.*

What Tarnowski meant was that the wild bars outside the restoration sanctuaries are not being worked. The watermen with the licenses are not on the water. The boats that used to take the oysters off those bars are mostly tied up.

In the same year the program was being celebrated as one of the largest successful ecosystem-restoration efforts in North America, the State of Maryland filed for a federal disaster declaration for the oyster fishery. The harvest had collapsed in volume and in value. The watermen who depend on the wild harvest were in financial crisis, and the market for Chesapeake oysters was being hollowed out by Gulf Coast farm-raised competition and by extreme-weather seasons that had disrupted the harvest windows. *Despite a growing oyster population,* Colden said, in support of the disaster request, *Maryland's watermen are feeling the pinch of extreme weather and market competition.*

Jason Ruth, who owns Harris Seafood Co., a Grasonville, Maryland oyster processor, told the same paper how the longer arc looks from inside an oyster house. *While the demand for boutique aquaculture oysters has grown over the last decade,* he has said, *the shucked oyster sector*, which he calls *the larger part of the industry*, *has seen a 60-year nationwide decline.*

The population is recovering, and the watermen are in disaster, and the two facts coexist. They coexist because the recovery is happening in sanctuaries the watermen are not allowed to harvest, and because the harvest from the wild bars outside the sanctuaries is being undercut, in the marketplace, by aquaculture operations that the watermen mostly do not own and by Gulf Coast operations that did not bear the costs the Bay's recovery is now being asked to carry. The ecosystem is being asked to come back. The watermen are being asked to absorb the cost of its coming back. The consumers eating the recovered oysters in restaurants are paying for an oyster, and they are not paying for what the watermen have absorbed in order for the oyster to be on the half-shell in front of them, and they cannot pay for it, because the price is not constructed to carry it.

*One of the most important ways people connect with the Chesapeake Bay,* Colden has said, *is through eating seafood and fishing.* Without watermen, *there's certainly a lot fewer eyes on the water and a lot fewer people caring passionately about the Bay.* The very people whose attention has held the Bay accountable for centuries are being squeezed out of the relationship by the same accounting that is, at the species level, beginning to recover what was lost. The recovery and the squeeze are happening at the same time, because they are the same process, viewed from two sides of the ledger.

What the Chesapeake is teaching, in this paradox, is that recovery without an honest accounting of who paid leaves the people who paid still paying. The oysters can come back without the watermen coming back. The reef can be rebuilt and the working waterfront not. So the question the paradox leaves standing on the dock is a plain one: who is the recovery for?

---

I started this chapter with the harbor at Old Point Comfort because that is the harbor I know. But what is happening here is happening up and down the Bay, and what is happening up and down the Bay is happening in the Gulf of Maine, and in Bristol Bay, and on the coast of West Africa, and in the Mekong Delta. Wherever a fishery has been pushed past the rate at which it could replace itself, the thinning runs in the same stages: first in the margins, then in the center, then in the structure that produced the catch, and by the third stage the question is no longer whether the harvest will return but whether anything about the place can be saved.

The men who work the Bay are reading a slow version of this story. The boats go out. The catch is what the catch is. The picking houses thin, and the young people leave, and a father on a Tuesday afternoon in early November watches a son tie up after a long day and thinks the thought he is not going to say out loud.

The thought is that what is being lost is not just an income or an industry but a way of knowing the world, one that took generations of careful attention to develop and that cannot be rebuilt by any decision the children's generation will be in a position to make. And under it sits the second thought: that the men whose labor and whose communities produced the value that fed the Atlantic seaboard for three hundred years are being asked, now, to absorb the cost of the value-stream's continuation, under conditions they did not design and do not control.

The men in McDowell County had been thinking the same thought for two generations before the books got written about them.

While I was finishing this chapter, I went back to McDowell County in my mind. The miner who developed black lung was not compensated for the lung; he was paid for the coal. The community that emptied out around him was not compensated for the emptying. The river that runs orange below the closed mine was not compensated for the orange. Those are accounting facts, every one of them, with nothing of the metaphor about them.

The waterman has had the same accounting done to him for longer, in a different register. He is paid for the crab and not for the Bay. He is paid for the oyster that comes up in the tongs, and not for the reef the tongs ground down across a century, not for the grass beds the runoff smothered, not for the menhaden the reduction fleet is removing from the food web his life depends on, not for the Tangier shoreline going under at fifteen feet a year, not for the daughter who has gone to nursing school in Norfolk because there was no boat for her to come back to. He is paid for what comes up in the tongs, and what does not come up in the tongs gets left in the water, and what gets left in the water has to be carried by something. By the Bay, while the Bay can carry it. By the watermen, after the Bay can no longer carry it alone. By the working waterfront, by the families, by the daughters who do not come back, by the children who never had a chance to learn the late-October crab runs, because by the time they were old enough to learn, the runs were no longer running on a schedule anyone could teach. A different resource, in a different geography, run through the same mechanism.

Put the miner and the waterman in a room together and they would not recognize each other immediately. They live in different states. They watch different news. They vote, when they vote, for different people, and they have been told different stories about who is to blame for what has happened to them. The waterman has been told that the people who would clean up the runoff are the same people who would shut down his harvest. The miner has been told that the people who would clean up the air are the same people who would close his mine. Each of them has been handed an enemy who is, in fact, the other one. The system has been extracting from them both, pricing the value of what they produce without pricing the cost of producing it, and it is maintained, in part, by keeping them in those rooms with those stories.

What the Bay and the hollow have in common is position, not resource. Both sit at the bottom of a watershed of value that flows somewhere else.

There is, in the world, a place that has handled this question differently.

A small country in northern Europe, smaller in population than the state of Virginia, came into possession of a finite resource at the bottom of one of its territorial seas in the late 1960s and early 1970s. The country's leaders made a different decision than most other countries in that position had made. They had looked at what happened to other resource-rich nations in similar circumstances, the way the rents dispersed and the costs concentrated, the way the future was spent in the present and the next generation was left to absorb the bill, and they built an instrument: rules that held the value coming out of the seabed in trust for the people of the country and for the generations not yet born, a fund with limits on what could be drawn. Against more political pressure than is usually acknowledged, they have kept the fund intact for more than thirty years.

That country is Norway, and it is the closest thing the world currently has to an existence proof: a working demonstration, in a real economy with real political pressures, that the mechanism this book is naming is not inevitable. The rents from a finite resource can be captured at the source. The costs to the present can be honored without spending the future. A country can extract from a commons without severing the costs of the extraction onto the people the commons belongs to. The fund is not perfect; no instrument is. What it answers is the question underneath every other question in this book, which is whether the alternative is even possible. It is possible; they have been doing it for more than three decades.

The watermen of the Chesapeake do not know about Norway. Most of them have never heard of the fund. Describe it to them at the marina at the end of a long day and they would, I think, listen quietly, the way watermen listen to most things, and at the end they might say something measured and short. It is a long way away, they might say. Their grandfathers fished the Bay before there was a Norway anyone had heard of, and the Bay is what they have.

The chapter that follows asks whether the instrument that was built in Oslo could, in some adapted form, be built for the Bay. Whether what was done for the oil could be done for the oyster. Whether the rents that have been quietly leaving this watershed for two centuries might, in some conceivable future, begin to stay.

For now, the boats are out at the mouth. The light has crossed the channel and reached the top of the Fort. A man two slips over has come in early, empty, and is hosing down; a truck door closes somewhere on the parking lot. The day is beginning, the day a waterman has, on the Bay he has, with the accounting the country has agreed to do. Within my father's lifetime, on this same water, a man stepped aboard at this hour with six bottles of Coca-Cola in a wire carrier and nothing else, because his lunch was in the water, waiting for him, as fresh as it could be. The reefs, where they have been rebuilt, are setting oysters again. The accounting that would let a morning like that come back has not been built yet.
