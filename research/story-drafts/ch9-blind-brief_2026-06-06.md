## 1. ROLE / PURPOSE

### What this chapter is

Chapter 9 is the book's **operationalization chapter**. The eight chapters before
it have done the work of feeling the mechanism, naming it, casing it, proving it
can be reduced (Norway), generalizing it, formalizing it, stress-testing it across
worlds, and landing a single worked dollar number on one ton of coal. Chapter 9's
job is the next question a fair reader asks after seeing that number: **what do we
actually do about it, using institutions that already exist?**

### The one-sentence irreducible job

Deliver a clean, sequential, four-step instrument the reader can hold in working
memory, and prove that every component of it already exists somewhere in the world
right now, so the honest response reads as reachable rather than utopian, while
handing the closing chapter a clean spine.

### The #1 structural mandate (this is the whole point of the redraft)

**FOREGROUND THE FOUR-STEP INSTRUMENT.** The instrument is: **Classify, Reserve,
Invest, Reassess.** It must be:

- stated explicitly and by name up front, in the opening, as a named sequence;
- given its own clean section per step, in order, each section doing one job;
- the single most prominent thing in the chapter, never buried.

The prior version of this chapter buried the instrument: it stated the four steps
near the top, then disappeared for thousands of words into a stack of eight-plus
defensive and illustrative essay-sections (theory defenses, a financial-crisis
case study, a tax-and-healthcare comparison, a second anthology of case studies)
before the actionability payoff resurfaced. The reader lost the thread between
"here are the four moves" and "and the pieces already exist." **Do not reproduce
that shape.** The chapter should read: bridge in -> four steps -> the international
/ border step -> count the pieces that already exist -> what a framework actually
does -> hand-off to the closing chapter. Objections that survive are folded as
one-to-three-sentence inoculations INSIDE the relevant step, not as standalone
essays.

### Length discipline (read carefully)

- **Minimum floor: 5,500 words. No maximum.**
- The floor is a **completeness floor, not a target.** The prior version was
  bloated (roughly eleven thousand words) precisely because it padded the spine
  with relocated-elsewhere material and a duplicated theory docket. **Do not pad
  back any of the dropped/relocated material to hit a number.** Let substance set

- Equally: **do not cut load-bearing content to hit a number.** If the instrument,

- The goal is foreground-the-instrument-plus-cut-genuine-redundancy, with the

### Voice posture (full constraints in Section 10)

Analytical but human; plain, direct, confident without overclaiming. This is a
chapter that says "here is what to do and here is proof the pieces exist," in a
register that disarms rather than lectures. No-villain throughout: the mechanism

## 2. ORDERED BEAT / SECTION INVENTORY

Eleven beats. The four-step instrument (Beats 2-5) is the load-bearing core and
must be the most prominent thing in the chapter. Each beat is abstracted; generate
fresh prose for all of it.

### Beat 1  -  Bridge in (short)

- Open by carrying forward the prior chapter's single worked number: one ton of
  mid-century McDowell County coal sold at the mine mouth for just under five
  dollars, while its honestly-priced cost ran into the hundreds of dollars per ton
  once every severed cost was counted (canonical figures in Section 6  -  use them
  exactly). State that this gap is arithmetic, not rhetoric, and that it repeats
  across every extraction the economy runs.
- Pivot: the honest response to a gap like that is not outrage; it is to ask what
  to do. This chapter is that answer.
- Set expectations honestly: not a legislative program, not a manifesto, not a
  blueprint; a sequence of practical moves operable with institutions that already
  exist, requiring no consensus on every contested philosophical question, only
  the decision to start pricing honestly and the discipline to keep doing it as we
  learn.
- **Name the sequence explicitly and up front: Classify, Reserve, Invest,
  Reassess.** This is the chapter's thesis sentence. Keep this beat tight.

### Beat 2  -  Step 1: Classify

- The first move is to sort resources by foreclosure risk, because not every
  resource carries the same risk and the framework should not treat them alike.
- Three bins, by what this brief abbreviates **ESG** (see the abbreviation note
  below): **low-ESG, medium-ESG, high-ESG.**
  - **Low-ESG:** abundant, substitutable in principle, where extracting more
    tomorrow does not meaningfully reduce what remains for the next century (iron,
    coal-as-fuel, and the renewable commons  -  sunlight, wind, atmospheric oxygen,
    regional rainfall  -  which are low by construction because they are themselves
    the renewable substrate the framework's substitute-investment rests on). The
    move for these: price the externality tail, require adequate reclamation
    bonding, let a corrected market allocate.
  - **Medium-ESG:** progressive extraction rates plus sovereign-wealth capture of
    rents.
  - **High-ESG:** geologically concentrated, chemically hard to substitute,
    required for technologies not yet invented but very likely needed (rare earth
    elements, platinum-group metals, helium / helium isotopes, phosphorus for
    agriculture, fossil-aquifer groundwater that regenerates on millennial
    timescales). These get the one move the whole instrument is organized around:
    reservation (Step 2).
- Classification is not mysterious: materials scientists, energy economists,
  agronomists, aerospace engineers, and hydrologists already hold most of the
  information needed to rank a resource by substitutability, stock dynamics,
  externality tail, and criticality of likely future uses. Existing apparatus
  does part of the work already  -  the EU Critical Raw Materials list and the USGS
  annual mineral assessments. What does not yet exist is a single legible ranking
  that integrates all four components into one ESG score; producing one is a
  matter of months, not decades, and has not been done mostly because no one has
  asked for it in this exact form.
- A rough three-bin sort is enough to drive every decision that follows;
  perfection is not required.
- **Fold-in inoculation (one or two sentences, inside this step):** name the
  resource-optimist's prior directly  -  the **Simon-Ehrlich** substitution
  tradition, the claim that scarcity summons substitutes so there is no missing
  price. Concede that substitution usually does win, which is exactly why the
  framework invests in substitution (Step 3) and only reserves the residual that
  substitution may not reach. Do not import the full debate; one honest sentence
  that engages the prior and points to the appendix for depth.

### Beat 3  -  Step 2: Reserve

- The idea that sounds strange on first hearing and obvious on the second: for
  high-ESG resources, designate a fraction of known economic deposits as strategic
  reserve and **leave them in the ground**  -  not extract-and-warehouse, not
  stockpile against a shortage. The ground is free, permanent storage requiring no
  refrigeration, security, or insurance.
- The cost of in-situ preservation, for almost every resource in this category, is
  just the foregone revenue on the reserved fraction, and that cost is small,
  because extraction is usually constrained by current demand and mining
  throughput, not by total deposit size. (You may construct an illustrative
  reserves-versus-production sketch ONLY from canonical / substrate-safe figures  - 
  see Section 7's rare-earth note; do not invent reserve or production tonnages.)
- Frame the reserve as a **free option**: it costs little to hold, appreciates as
  the non-reserved stock depletes, pays off the full substitution value if future
  scarcity develops, and can be reclassified and released if substitution makes
  the resource unnecessary.
- It requires no new international institution: strategic petroleum reserves and
  various mineral stockpiles already exist in most industrial countries. What the
  framework adds is a coherent principle for what to reserve and how much  -  the
  Step-1 ESG ranking, with reserved fractions sized to the asymmetric regret of
  the decision.
- **Discharge the Asymmetric Regret Rule here, in the spine.** State it plainly:
  if the reserve proves unnecessary, the cost is modest foregone revenue spread
  across a century and a global economy; if it proves necessary and the resource
  was already dug up, the cost is civilizational and irreversible  -  possibilities
  foreclosed forever. When the insurance is cheap and the downside is unbounded,
  reserving is the rational move. (This is an inherited promise from the
  formalization chapter; it is discharged inside the instrument, which is correct  - 
  no separate section.)
- **Fold-in inoculation (one or two sentences):** the market-oriented reader's
  objection that reservation is industrial policy / government-picking-winners /
  price distortion inviting the very capture the framework warns about. Answer
  briefly: reservation is not subsidy to a chosen producer; it is the decision to
  not foreclose an option, sized by regret, and it leaves the corrected market to
  allocate everything not reserved. One honest sentence, then move on.

### Beat 4  -  Step 3: Invest

- Reservation buys time; investment decides what we do with it. Two simultaneous
  priorities.
- **Renewable substitutes**  -  not only renewable energy (the most visible case)
  but the full substitutability research agenda: alternative battery chemistries
  to relieve pressure on lithium and cobalt; recycled and synthesized material
  flows to relieve virgin rare earths; regenerative agriculture to relieve fossil
  aquifers and phosphate; alternative proteins to relieve collapsing fisheries.
  Make the structural claim: every dollar of substitution research does three
  things at once  -  raises the substitutability function (lowering the residual
  commons value of high-ESG resources), preserves the reserve's optionality, and
  reduces the externality tail of future extraction. It is the rare expenditure
  that reduces present harm, future harm, and future need simultaneously.
- **Alternative supply sources** (compressed as "space access")  -  frame this as
  comparative residual-commons-value arithmetic, not science fiction: a lifeless
  body has no community bearing spatial cost severance, no cascading biosphere,
  an externality tail measured against empty rock; its ESG is very low by
  construction. As launch costs decline, off-world material eventually competes on
  total honest cost with terrestrial deposits whose full residual commons value
  has been priced. The model says nothing about timing; it says only that the
  eventual destination of bulk extraction for the most abundant materials is off
  this planet.
- The governing principle: the model does not say never extract; it says **extract
  the lowest-residual-commons-value source first.** For most materials over the
  next century that is renewable substitution; for a narrow set we cannot
  substitute or recycle indefinitely, it is eventually extraterrestrial. Both fall
  out of the arithmetic, not politics.

### Beat 5  -  Step 4: Reassess

- Nothing here is static. A resource classified high-ESG today can become low-ESG
  in thirty years if a substitution breakthrough matures (a non-rare-earth magnet
  chemistry; grid-parity sodium-ion storage; synthesized or designed-around
  helium). When that happens, reserved fractions can be reclassified  -  some
  released to extraction, some held permanently as a hedge against the
  substitute's failure modes.
- Reassessment runs both directions: a resource thought low-ESG can prove higher
  as new uses emerge or the externality tail lengthens. Use two substrate-safe
  reclassification examples: **cobalt** (looked abundant a generation ago; now
  treated as strategically critical by most industrial nations) and **helium**
  (once so cheap the US government effectively gave it away; now priced with the
  seriousness its unique properties deserve).
- The classification is a living document, maintained by the same scientific
  bodies that produce it, reassessed on a roughly five-to-ten-year cadence, the
  pace materials science actually moves. The institutional machinery largely
  exists (USGS, the EU science apparatus, the IEA, national stockpile programs);
  what is missing is the framework within which reassessment matters, and the
  ESG-driven reserve is that framework.
- **Fold-in inoculation (one sentence):** the **discount-rate** exposure. The
  asymmetric-regret and option logic lean on "at realistic discount rates over the
  relevant horizon." Name in one sentence that the choice of discount rate is
  contested (the Stern-versus-Nordhaus dispute) and that the quantitative
  treatment is developed in the Technical Appendix; do not re-import the debate.

### Beat 6  -  The international / border step

- Cost severance crosses borders; borders are among the mechanisms by which it
  operates. A product consumed in a wealthy country can have its full residual
  commons value paid entirely by people the consumer will never meet, in places
  they will never visit. Use AT MOST two substrate-safe cost-bearer images and
  keep them tight  -  the rare-earth tailings near Baotou and the cobalt supply
  chain in the Democratic Republic of Congo are the canonical pair (see Section 7
  for the corrected Baotou figure and the cobalt-figure attribution). Do not
  re-establish the cost-severance-crosses-borders premise at length; the book
  already built it. One compact paragraph, not a section.
- The problem this creates for honest national pricing: a country that prices
  domestic extraction honestly while importing unpriced foreign extraction has
  merely relocated where severance enters its economy. The import gap is the new
  problem.
- The response is a principle, not a drafted treaty: apply the pricing to imports
  as well as domestic extraction, mechanically a **border adjustment**  -  a charge
  scaled to the residual commons value the exporting country failed to price. The
  EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) is a partial, real, working-imperfectly
  version of exactly this for carbon alone  -  proof the move is legally,
  administratively, and diplomatically feasible at sufficient scale.
- It spreads by market access, not by treaty: it begins where any country or bloc
  decides to begin and expands by the ordinary mechanics of trade, the way carbon
  pricing spread from a handful of jurisdictions to many.
- **Fold-in inoculation (one sentence):** acknowledge the live contestation  -  CBAM
  faces WTO non-discrimination questions and retaliation risk  -  so the feasibility
  claim is not overstated. One honest clause.

- Run the **supply-side steelman at full strength first.** The strongest account
  of American housing costs is the supply-restriction case: zoning, land-use
  rules, and permitting choke new construction, so prices rise where building is
  hardest. Name its leading exponents as the serious case they are  -  the
  Glaeser-and-Gyourko supply-elasticity work and Jenny Schuetz's land-use

- Then distinguish, do not refute: supply restriction and rent extraction operate

- Ground the cost-burden fact, then flag the causal claim honestly: roughly half
  of American renters are cost-burdened (paying more than thirty percent of
  income); the documented rise in cost burden appears across both supply-restricted

### Beat 9  -  Property rights and their limits / the Christophers engagement

Keep this compact relative to the prior version, but engage **Christophers at
depth** here (this discharges an inherited promise  -  Section 5).

- The most intellectually serious objection comes from the property-rights
  tradition: well-defined property rights plus Coasean bargaining already solve the
  commons problem; assigning ownership is often the best route to preservation
  (fisheries stabilize under quota; forests regrow when owners internalize timber
  value). Concede this honestly  -  the framework agrees property rights do
  extraordinary work for rivalrous, excludable, present-tense goods.
- Then name the three things property rights structurally cannot reach, tightly:
  (1) **intergenerational foreclosure** (future generations hold no deeds and
  cannot bid in present markets, so Coasean bargaining is structurally incomplete);
  (2) **transboundary externality** (costs that escape the property line  -  drainage,
  atmospheric carbon, aquifer depletion, airborne tailings dust respect no fence);
  (3) **civilizational-scale information asymmetry** (markets price only what
  participants currently know and cannot price uses not yet invented  -  the reserve-
  under-uncertainty move property rights cannot make because the information does
  not exist at the moment of pricing). Keep these as a tight enumeration, not three
  mini-essays. You may point to the foundational chapters and the Technical
  Appendix for the full treatment rather than rebuilding the theory here.
- Reference **Pistor's** legal-architecture diagnosis (capital coded by legal
  modules that systematically externalize cost) as the reason property rights
  leave costs externalized  -  cross-reference the accountability-gap chapter where
  Pistor is engaged.
- **Engage Christophers at depth (this is the inherited "at depth" promise).**
  Christophers' argument (climate-finance direction): markets do not price climate
  solutions correctly because investment is driven by profit, not price, and
  renewable projects do not clear the profit thresholds private capital requires
  absent state support. Be fair to his stronger claim  -  that the market-pricing
  move is itself insufficient and that decommodified public provision is required  - 
  rather than reducing him to "investment follows profit not price." The
  framework's honest response: pricing the residual commons value of fossil
  extraction does not on its own redirect capital to renewables; what it does is
  make the subsidy-and-rent architecture visible, after which the political work of
  redirecting capital becomes possible. Christophers names the why; the framework
  names how to see it. Both converge on the conclusion that public investment and
  public-purpose capital-allocation criteria are not extreme but what honest
  accounting requires when the price-and-profit gap is the structural problem.
- **Polanyi / Fraser anchor (ratified positioning move  -  Section 5).** This is the
  natural home for the framework's re-embedding identity: name Polanyi (the
  fictitious-commodity thesis  -  labor, land, money treated as commodities though
  never produced as such) and Fraser (expropriation of social reproduction and
  nature behind the wage relation) as the diagnostic lineage the framework prices
  and reserves against. Frame the framework as Polanyi's double-movement made into
  an accounting instrument: society re-embedding what marketization disembedded.
  This simultaneously closes the book's biggest positioning gap AND disarms the
  financialization objection (next bullet). Land this in one tight paragraph; do
  not cold-introduce six thinkers  -  let the reader meet one lineage.
- **Financialization inoculation (one to two sentences  -  closes the strongest
  unanswered hostile line).** Confront directly: by pricing and bonding commons
  loss, are you extending the very Code-of-Capital / fictitious-commodity logic
  that turned land and nature into commodities? Distinguish: the framework prices
  the cost to force honest accounting and reserves AGAINST commodification  -  it
  does not code the commons into a new tradable asset class. Accounting the cost is
  not the same move as coding the asset. Name Pistor here by name.

### Beat 10  -  What a framework does

- Pose the hardest, most pragmatic objection, in the voice of someone who has
  actually tried to pass legislation: carbon pricing has been argued for decades
  and the most ambitious schemes are riddled with exemptions; if the world cannot
  pass a simple carbon tax after thirty years of scientific consensus, how could it
  possibly implement comprehensive residual-commons-value pricing across every
  resource and jurisdiction? Show me the legislative pathway, the coalition, the
  treaty. Without them, this is one more book that diagnoses and cannot solve.
- Give the most honest answer: this is not a legislative blueprint; it is a
  framework. The specific political pathway is beyond what any single book can
  produce, and pretending otherwise would misrepresent what a framework does.
- Then state what frameworks do that legislation cannot: they change how people
  think, which changes what legislation becomes conceivable. Make this with a
  SHORT list of vocabulary-changes-the-conceivable exemplars  -  **keep it to about
  three, not five** (the prior version over-listed). Recommended core trio:
  **Harvey** ("accumulation by dispossession" gave a name to a pattern, and the
  political conversation around enclosure / gentrification shifted), **Ostrom**
  (named the design principles existing local institutions had evolved and
  legitimized them against privatization and state management), and **Raworth**
  (Doughnut Economics adopted as a guiding frame by a city and other bodies because
  it gave them a coherent way to think about what they were already trying to do).
  Optionally include **Desmond** (Evicted shifted how housing instability is
  discussed  -  if you use Desmond, frame the effect as "helped shift the
  conversation," not as causing legislation). Treat **Susskind** as the weakest fit
  and the first to drop; do not include him unless the growth-redirection parallel
  earns its place.
- Land the move on the book itself: Commons Bonds gives a vocabulary (cost
  severance, residual commons value, severed cost, accountability bond, the
  existential substitutability gap) and a measurement tool and a way of thinking
  about resource pricing that a coal-country resident, a municipal council, a state
  legislature, or an international body can apply in their own context. The book
  does not need to pass a law; it needs to change the terms of debate so the right
  laws become thinkable.
- Point honestly back at the entering wedges that already exist. The work is not to
  build from scratch but to strengthen, connect, align, and extend what exists. The
  framework will not do the work on its own; what it gives the people who do the
  work is a common language and a common instrument. Whether the work gets done is
  the question every generation answers for the one that follows.
