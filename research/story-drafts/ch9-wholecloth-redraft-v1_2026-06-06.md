# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 9: Pricing Honestly

---

Chapter 8 put a number on the invoice.

One ton of McDowell County coal, sold at the mine mouth in 1960 for just under five dollars, actually cost somewhere around five hundred and ten dollars once every severed cost was priced honestly, component by component, on the conservative floor. The carbon damage alone, the foreclosure that combustion imposes on the atmospheric commons, came to roughly four hundred and ninety-six dollars of that. The gap between the price and the cost is not a rhetorical flourish. It is arithmetic. And it repeats, with different parameters, across every extraction the economy runs.

The honest response to arithmetic like that is not outrage. It is to ask what to do about it.

This chapter is that answer. Not a legislative program, not a manifesto, not a policy blueprint, but the set of practical moves a society would make if it decided to close the gap between what things cost and what we pay. The moves are sequenced. They are operable with institutions that already exist. They do not require consensus on every contested question about markets, or property, or the obligations of one generation to the next. They require only the decision to start pricing honestly, and the discipline to keep pricing honestly as we learn.

The sequence is four steps. Classify. Reserve. Invest. Reassess.

---

### Step 1: Classify

Before anything else, sort the cabinet.

Not every resource carries the same foreclosure risk. A ton of iron ore extracted today imposes externality costs, the mine pit, the tailings, the emissions of processing, but iron is abundant on this planet and on most of the rocky bodies around it. Substitutes are conceivable for almost every application of it, and extracting more iron tomorrow does not meaningfully reduce what remains for the century after. Coal is similar in abundance, different in its externality tail, but substitutable in principle. We already know how to heat a house and power a grid without it. Resources like these carry a low existential substitutability gap. Price their externalities, tax their extraction at a rate that reflects the damage tail, require adequate reclamation bonding, and the framework's work is mostly done.

Other resources are not like that.

The rare earth elements, the seventeen metals whose chemical and physical properties underwrite nearly every piece of advanced electronics, are geologically concentrated in a small number of deposits, chemically difficult to substitute, and required for technologies humanity has not yet invented but will almost certainly need. The platinum group metals are similar. Phosphorus, essential to agriculture, exists in workable concentrations at only a few places on Earth and has no substitute for the biological role it plays. Groundwater in fossil aquifers, the Ogallala, the North China Plain, the Guarani, regenerates on timescales of millennia and is being withdrawn on timescales of decades. These carry a high existential substitutability gap. They are the resources a civilization that plans to exist in two hundred years would want to hold in reserve.

Classification is not a mysterious exercise. Materials scientists, energy economists, agronomists, and hydrologists already hold most of the information required to rank a given resource by its substitutability function, its stock dynamics, its externality tail, and the criticality of its likely future uses. The European Union publishes a Critical Raw Materials list that does a portion of this work. The United States Geological Survey publishes annual assessments that do another portion. What does not yet exist is a single legible ranking that integrates all four components into one score. Producing one is a matter of months, not decades. It has not been done mostly because no one has asked for it in exactly this form.

The classification need not be perfect to be useful. A rough sort into three bins, low, medium, and high, is enough to drive every decision that follows. The low bin gets the corrected price signal and the reclamation bond. The medium bin gets progressive extraction rates and sovereign wealth mechanisms of the kind Norway built. The high bin gets the one move the framework is organized around: reservation.

A note on the property objection, because the most serious version of it lives right here. Ronald Coase showed in 1960 that, absent transaction costs, parties will bargain their way to an efficient handling of externalities regardless of who holds the initial entitlement, and the free-market tradition reads that result as a reason to assign ownership and step back. The framework agrees that property rights do extraordinary work for resources that are rivalrous, excludable, and valued in the present tense. It diverges only where they structurally cannot reach: intergenerational foreclosure, because future generations hold no deeds and cannot bid; transboundary externality, because dust and acid and carbon cross every fence; and uses not yet invented, because the market cannot price what no one has discovered they need. Those three gaps are the whole reason the high bin exists. The argument behind them is built in Chapter 1 and developed at depth in the Technical Appendix. Here, the classification is enough.

---

### Step 2: Reserve

Here is the idea that sounds strange on first hearing and obvious on second.

For high-gap resources, designate a fraction of known economic deposits as strategic reserve. Not to extract and warehouse them. Not to stockpile against a shortage. Leave them in the ground.

The ground is free, permanent storage. It requires no refrigeration, no security, no insurance against the warehouse burning down. Platinum that has sat in South African bushveld for two billion years will sit there another two billion unless someone goes in and takes it out. The cost of in-situ preservation, for almost every resource in this category, is exactly the foregone revenue on the reserved fraction. Nothing else.

That cost is usually small, because extraction is constrained not by total deposit size but by current demand and current mining throughput. If the USGS identifies, say, thirty million metric tons of economically recoverable rare earth reserves and current global annual production runs near three hundred thousand metric tons, then designating a fifth of reserves as strategic, twenty years of current production held back, costs almost nothing in the near term. The supply that remains for extraction still dwarfs the demand schedule. The preserved fraction simply becomes unavailable to decisions our descendants would otherwise have made for themselves.

The reserve is, in effect, a free option. It costs the foregone revenue on whatever fraction would have been extracted inside the current window, which at any defensible discount rate is a small slice of total resource value. Its payoff is the full substitution value of the preserved stock if future scarcity develops. It appreciates as the unreserved stock depletes and can be released the moment a substitute matures. Skeptics will note, correctly, that the size of the present cost turns on the discount rate, and that economists do not agree on it. The dispute between Stern's low rate and Nordhaus's higher one is real, and the Technical Appendix treats it quantitatively. The point that survives either rate is the asymmetry, not the precise figure.

The Asymmetric Regret Rule does the rest of the work. If the reserve turns out to have been unnecessary, because substitutes mature or new deposits are found elsewhere in the solar system, the cost of having reserved was a modest amount of foregone revenue spread across a century and across the global economy. If the reserve turns out to have been necessary, because some application is identified in 2150 for a material our grandchildren can no longer reach, the cost of not having reserved is civilizational. The first error is measured in dollars. The second is measured in possibilities foreclosed forever. When the insurance is cheap and the downside is unbounded, the move is obvious.

It requires no new international institution. Strategic petroleum reserves already exist in most industrial countries. China has been quietly accumulating rare earth reserves for two decades. The United States has at various points held strategic stockpiles of helium, tungsten, cobalt, and several dozen other materials. A market-minded reader will object that reservation is industrial policy by another name, that governments picking which deposits to lock away will distort prices and invite the very capture the framework warns about. The objection has force, and the design answer is to bind reservation to the published classification rather than to discretionary choice, so that what gets reserved follows a transparent rule and not a lobbyist. The architecture for reservation exists. What the framework adds is a principle for deciding what to reserve and how much.

---

### Step 3: Invest

Reservation buys time. The question is what we do with the time.

The answer is two simultaneous investment priorities. The first is renewable substitutes, and not only renewable energy, though that is the most visible case. Photovoltaics and wind and storage to displace coal, oil, and gas. Alternative battery chemistries, sodium, iron-air, solid state, to reduce pressure on lithium and cobalt. Recycled and synthesized material flows to reduce pressure on virgin rare earths. Regenerative agriculture to reduce pressure on fossil aquifers and phosphate. The details vary by resource; the structural claim does not. Every dollar of substitution research does three things at once. It raises the probability that a functionally adequate substitute exists at any future time, which lowers the residual commons value of every high-gap resource that substitute touches. It preserves the reserve's optionality, since a reserve never needed can eventually be released. And it shrinks the externality tail of future extraction, because the less we need to dig, the less damage the digging does. No other class of spending does all three.

There is an honest concession built into this step, and it is worth naming because a skeptical reader already holds it. The long tradition that runs through Julian Simon's wager with Paul Ehrlich holds that human ingenuity and substitution have defeated every Malthusian scarcity prediction so far, and that betting against substitution is a loser's game. The framework does not bet against substitution. It agrees that substitution usually wins. That is precisely why it invests so heavily in substitution and reserves only the residual, the slice where substitution might not arrive in time and the downside of being wrong is unbounded. Step 3 is the framework conceding Simon's point and pricing the tail he leaves out.

The second priority is alternative supply, which in compressed form gets called space access, and which sounds science-fictional but isn't. The argument is purely one of comparative residual commons value. An asteroid has no communities bearing spatial cost severance and no ecosystems whose collapse cascades through a biosphere. Its existential substitutability gap, by construction, is very low. A kilogram of nickel lifted from a body like 16 Psyche will eventually, as launch costs fall, compete on total cost with a kilogram mined from a terrestrial deposit whose full commons value has been priced honestly. The model says nothing about when that crossover happens. It says only that the eventual destination of bulk extraction, for the most abundant materials we use, is off this planet, and that investing in the capabilities that reach it sooner is investing in a future where Earth's deposits need not be touched at all.

This is what pricing honestly demands. The model does not say never extract. It says extract from the lowest residual commons value source first. For most materials over the next century, that source is renewable substitution. For the narrow set we cannot substitute or recycle indefinitely, it is eventually extraterrestrial. Neither conclusion was reached by politics. Both fall out of the arithmetic once extraction is priced honestly.

---

### Step 4: Reassess

Nothing about this is static.

A resource classified high today may become low in thirty years if a substitution breakthrough matures. Rare earth demand could be halved by a non-rare-earth magnet chemistry. Lithium demand could be structurally reduced by sodium-ion batteries reaching grid-storage parity. When that happens, the reserved fraction is reclassified. Some is released to the extraction market. Some is held permanently as a hedge against the substitute's failure modes. The classification is a living document, maintained by the same scientific bodies that produce it, reassessed every five or ten years at the pace materials science actually moves.

Reassessment runs the other way too. A resource that looks low today may prove higher than we thought as new uses emerge or the externality tail lengthens. Cobalt looked abundant twenty years ago and has since been reclassified as strategically critical by most industrial nations. Helium was once so cheap the United States government gave it away; it is now priced with the seriousness its properties deserve. The framework does not require us to get the classification right on the first try. It requires us to keep learning and to adjust the reservation as the learning matures. Most of the machinery already exists, the USGS, the European Commission's Joint Research Centre, the International Energy Agency, the national stockpile programs. What was missing was not the capacity to reassess but the framework within which reassessment matters.

### The international step: pricing at the border

Honest national pricing has one gap, and it is large. A country can internalize the full residual commons value of every domestic extraction and still import materials whose foreign extraction was never priced at all. It has not reduced severance. It has relocated where the severance enters its economy. The rare earths in a phone sold in California were mined where the tailings border villages that breathe the airborne dust. The cobalt in nearly every battery on the planet came, until recently, overwhelmingly from mines where children dig by hand. Trade, as it currently runs, is among other things a severance delivery system, letting a consumer in a wealthy country buy a product whose full cost was paid entirely by people they will never meet.

The framework's response is not a treaty, which this book does not pretend to draft. It is a principle. A country serious about closing its severance gap must apply the pricing to imports as well as to domestic extraction. Mechanically that is a border adjustment, a charge scaled to the residual commons value the exporting country failed to price. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, in transitional operation since 2023 and entering its definitive phase in 2026, is a partial version of exactly this for carbon alone. It is not theoretical. It exists. A trade economist will note, fairly, that border adjustments face live challenges over whether they discriminate against trading partners under WTO rules, and that retaliation is a real risk; CBAM is being litigated on precisely those grounds. The relevant point is narrower than full vindication. A large enough jurisdiction can build a border adjustment and make it stick, and once it does, the cheapest way for an exporter to keep market access is to internalize the cost at the source and capture the revenue at home rather than surrender it to the tariff. That is how carbon pricing spread from a handful of jurisdictions in the early 2000s to more than seventy today. Gap-scaled pricing can follow the same path. The international step does not wait for the world to agree. It begins where any bloc decides to begin and expands by the ordinary mechanics of market access.

### Counting the pieces that already exist

The fastest way to see that none of this is utopian is to count the pieces of it already in place.

Carbon pricing, in the narrow sense of a tax or trading scheme on greenhouse emissions, operated in more than seventy jurisdictions and covered roughly a quarter of global emissions as of the most recent World Bank survey, with new initiatives poised to push that coverage toward a third. The schemes are imperfect, the prices mostly below the social cost of carbon. None of that changes the fact that a substantial, legally functional, politically defended mechanism for pricing one slice of what residual commons value would account for already exists and expands year by year.

Sovereign wealth funds built on resource rents exist in Norway, in Alaska, across several Gulf states, and in smaller form in dozens of jurisdictions. Each implements the principle that the rents of finite extraction belong, in some real sense, to the community whose commons was drawn from rather than to the entity that did the drawing. Alaska's Permanent Fund captures a share of mineral royalty income, holds it as an irrevocable constitutional trust now worth on the order of eighty-five billion dollars, and has paid an annual dividend to every resident since 1982, recently in the range of roughly one thousand to seventeen hundred dollars. The Fund has survived boom, bust, and every administration the state has produced. Its political durability is the evidence that matters: once a community is the actual beneficiary of resource rents, a constituency forms to defend the arrangement and stays formed. Norway's fund is the larger, more disciplined national version of the same idea, sustained over fifty years through changes of government and the ordinary temptations of a wealthy society.

The reservation logic is not theoretical even at the narrow level. Several jurisdictions have built rare-earth strategic programs in response to supply-chain dependency. The United States restored Mountain Pass production after a bankruptcy and a closure that had cost it domestic capacity. The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act, in force since 2024, names a set of materials as critical and a subset as strategic, eligible for stockpiling and alternative-sourcing support. Japan and South Korea built reserve and alternative-processing capacity after trade disputes exposed their dependence. None implements the framework's full discipline. Each shows the gap-driven reservation logic is reachable once dependency raises the visibility of the underlying problem.

The accountability instruments exist too, scattered and underpowered. Mining reclamation bonds operate in the United States under the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977. They are structurally underpriced, as Chapter 2 documented, but the mechanism is sound: index them to actual remediation costs, require refresh as the liability tail lengthens, and a token gesture becomes a real instrument. Environmental impact assessments under the National Environmental Policy Act of 1970 are a procedural, non-monetized version of pricing the externality tail. The Community Reinvestment Act is a partial implementation of the principle that capital extracted from a place owes something to the place. And in finance, the Dodd-Frank reforms that followed the 2008 collapse are a partial implementation of the same accountability logic in a different domain, an attempt to make the institutions that capture the upside of risk bear more of its downside, with the severance the prior arrangement had pushed onto households made at least partly visible and chargeable. Each of these evolved piecemeal, in response to a specific crisis, under a specific coalition, carrying every compromise such politics produces. Taken together they demonstrate something important. Every component of honest pricing already exists somewhere, in some form, and has survived the political process long enough to become institutional furniture. The framework's task is not to invent the instruments. It is to connect them, strengthen them, align them around a coherent principle, and extend them to the resources and externalities they do not yet cover.

### The same logic, applied to housing

The framework is built on resources, but cost severance is not confined to them, and the clearest contemporary case is housing, which is where the company town came back.

The old company town was honest about its arrangement. The miner rented from the company, bought from the company, and owed the company, so that the wage that looked like income was substantially recaptured before it could become savings or mobility or anything a family could carry out the front gate. The arrangement has returned in distributed form. Roughly half of American renters are now cost-burdened, paying more than thirty percent of income on housing, and the surplus that would once have funded relocation, accumulation, and transfer to the next generation is captured instead by owners who are not present at the scale of the community. The bootstrap answer, that a household failed by a place can simply leave, assumes a savings cushion sufficient to absorb a move and a stretch without income. For the households the framework is concerned with, neither is available. The constrained choice is still on offer. The unconstrained one is not.

There is a serious objection here, and it deserves a full hearing because it is right about a great deal. The supply-side case, associated with Edward Glaeser and Joseph Gyourko's work on supply-constrained metropolitan areas and Jenny Schuetz's research on land-use regulation as the binding constraint in high-cost markets, holds that the affordability crisis is in substantial part a supply problem produced by local political-economy decisions over the past half-century, and that the most direct response is to permit more housing where demand exists. In markets where zoning allows dense construction, rents and prices track underlying construction costs more closely; where zoning liberalizes, rents measurably moderate. Each of those claims is sound on its own terms. The framework does not deny supply restriction or treat zoning reform as unimportant. It observes that supply analysis and rent-extraction analysis are operating at different levels of the same case.

Supply restriction explains why the number of units has not kept pace with demand in high-cost metros. It does not explain why, when units do exist, rent captures a structurally larger share of household income than it did fifty years ago across the same income strata. The share of cost-burdened renters has risen across markets with very different supply elasticities. The Joint Center for Housing Studies documented the rise across both the supply-restricted metros, San Francisco, New York, Boston, Seattle, and the supply-expanded ones, Atlanta, Phoenix, Houston, Charlotte, in its 2024 reporting. Supply restriction explains the level differences between those markets. It does not explain the rise in cost burden that all of them experienced together. Something is producing pressure on the rent-share of income across the whole supply spectrum, and supply restriction is not it.

What is producing it is the framework's subject. The consolidation of rental housing into asset-class portfolios, the financialization of single-family rental, and the documented price-coordination patterns that property-management software enabled across markets, the subject of a Department of Justice antitrust action that opened in 2022, escalated to a formal complaint in 2024, and reached a proposed settlement in late 2025, all operate on top of whatever supply elasticity a local market has. That consolidation captures rent at the household-income level whether units are abundant or scarce. It simply captures more aggressively where the supply-side leverage is strongest. Liberalizing zoning addresses the first mechanism. It does not address the second. A framework that treated them as the same thing would be wrong. A framework that priced only one would be incomplete. Both are real, both are operating, and the policy response has to reach both. That housing can be held differently is not a thought experiment: roughly sixty percent of Vienna lives in municipally owned or non-profit social housing built and sustained across a century of changing financing regimes, at rents well below the private market and without the decay the standard objection predicts.

One further case is worth naming because it is live and it exercises the full instrument on a near-term decision. The Clarion-Clipperton Zone in the eastern Pacific holds the largest known accessible deposits of polymetallic nodules containing manganese, nickel, copper, and cobalt, and the International Seabed Authority is now negotiating the code that would authorize commercial extraction. Apply the four steps. Those metals sit in the high bin. The deep-sea ecosystem sits in the externality-tail-unbounded category, close to the ocean-world treatment in Chapter 7, because the recovery timescales of disturbed seabed are unknown across multi-decadal to multi-century horizons. Asymmetric regret under those parameters points toward preserve-and-study. The honest complication, which the framework names rather than hides, is that the case is contested in both directions: a serious counter-argument holds that terrestrial cobalt and nickel mining, with their own human and ecological tolls, may be worse than seabed nodules, so the regret math is genuinely disputed. What the framework insists on is not a verdict from the armchair but that the decision be made on the full accounting, and that the verification architecture resist the kind of simulated compliance Chapter 7's immune-response stress test surfaced. That question is being answered in real time by the parties to the negotiation.

### What a framework does

The hardest question this chapter has to answer is the most pragmatic one, and it is usually asked by people who have actually tried to pass legislation. Carbon pricing has been debated for thirty years; the most ambitious implementation covers a fraction of emissions, is riddled with exemptions, and was nearly gutted by lobbying during the 2008 crisis. If the world cannot implement a simple carbon tax after three decades of scientific consensus, the argument runs, how do you propose to price every high-gap resource, every externality tail, every transboundary leak, across every jurisdiction on Earth? Show me the legislative pathway. Show me the coalition. Without those, this is another book by another person who understands the problem and cannot solve it.

The objection deserves the most honest answer I can give. This is not a legislative blueprint. It is a framework. The political pathway, which laws, which jurisdictions, which coalitions, which treaties, is beyond what any single book can produce, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest about what a framework does and does not do.

But notice what frameworks do that legislation cannot. They change how people think, which changes what legislation becomes conceivable. David Harvey's phrase "accumulation by dispossession" passed no law. It gave people a word for a pattern they had lived for decades without a name, and once the word existed, the conversation around enclosure and privatization changed, and partial, contested legislation followed. Elinor Ostrom's work on the commons created no new institution. It identified the design principles that dozens of local institutions had already evolved, legitimized those approaches against the twin pressures of privatization and central management, and has since shaped fisheries, forestry, and water governance in more places than can easily be counted. Kate Raworth's *Doughnut Economics* has been adopted as a guiding frame by cities and regional governments not because it passed anywhere but because it gave institutions a coherent way to describe what they were already trying to do.

Commons Bonds is, and can only be, an attempt at the same thing. It offers a vocabulary, cost severance, residual commons value, the accountability bond, the existential substitutability gap. It offers a measurement tool, the residual commons value model, testable against any extraction in any jurisdiction. And it offers a way of thinking about resource pricing that a coal-country resident, a municipal council setting reclamation bonds, a state legislature designing a sovereign wealth fund, or an international body debating a critical-minerals agreement can each apply in their own context. The book does not need to pass a law. It needs to change the terms of the debate so that the right laws become thinkable, and then to point, honestly, at the entering wedges that already exist. The work is not to build everything from scratch. The work is to strengthen what exists, connect what is disconnected, align the fragments around a coherent principle, and extend the principle to what it does not yet cover. That work is difficult. It is not impossible. People who have done harder institutional work before have done it with less.

---

And so, with the framework laid out and the entering wedges named, the book turns toward its closing question, which is no longer what extraction costs, or what honest pricing would look like, or how the institutions might be bent toward it. Those questions have been answered, within the limits of what a single volume can answer.

The remaining question is a different kind of question.

The miner in McDowell County. The waterman on the Chesapeake. The villager beside the tailings pond. The farmer drawing down the Ogallala. The administrator on Mars, making the resource decisions that will determine whether the habitat survives. They are not, the book has argued, separate problems. They are the same problem in different parameters. The framework that prices one prices all of them. The mechanism that severs cost from value in one place operates by the same structural logic in every other.

If that is true, then the last chapter has to ask what follows from recognizing it, not as policy but as something closer to a fact about ourselves and the generations we belong to, and the ones we owe. The next chapter names where the present-day decision points sit.

That is where the book goes from here.
