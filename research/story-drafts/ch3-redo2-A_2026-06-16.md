# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 3: The Waterman

---

The harbor wakes before the city does.

If you stand on the seawall at Old Point Comfort in the dark, before the sky over the water has gone from black to gray, you can hear the harbor before you can see it. A diesel turns over somewhere out past the rip-rap and settles into a low idle. A line slaps a piling. A man's voice carries across the water the way a voice only carries across water, flattened and clear, so that you can hear the words from two hundred yards off and never see the mouth that made them. Then the first green flash of a channel marker, steady, patient, blinking out into the dark for boats that are not there yet, and then the boats that are. The gray comes up out of the east in no hurry. By the time the city behind you has its first lights on in its first windows, the watermen have already been out on the water for an hour, and the day, for them, is half spent.

I grew up on this water. I want to tell you about it before I tell you what was done to it, because if I do it the other way you will see a column of numbers and I need you, first, to see the water.

The Chesapeake is not a gentle body. People who have only known the ocean do not understand this about the Bay, and the watermen will tell you so if you ask them, in the flat way of men telling you a thing they know in their hands. Out on the open ocean a five-foot wave is nothing. It rides on a long wavelength, a hundred feet of water between one crest and the next, and a boat in it lifts and settles like a chest breathing in sleep. The Bay does not give you that. The Bay can stack a five-foot wave on a wavelength barely longer than it is tall, a near-square wall of water arriving and arriving with no rest between, and a boat in that chop does not breathe. It hammers. The watermen call it a square chop, and it is a thing the Bay does that the ocean does not, and a man who has crossed oceans can stand in a Deadrise in a Chesapeake chop and turn green and lose his breakfast over the side while a sixty-year-old waterman who has never been more than thirty miles from the mouth of the Back River works the culling board without looking up.

The boats are called Deadrises. They are low and broad and built for this water and no other, named for the shape of the hull where it rises off the keel, and they are working boats, every line of them earned. There are places in the world where the water in front of the house is a thing you keep a yacht on, a white hull the length of a tennis court with a crew in matching shirts, and the people on that water are looking at the same sunrise from a very different life. That water is somewhere else. The water I grew up on has Deadrises on it, and the men in them are not looking at the sunrise. They are reading it.

And the sunrise on the Bay is a thing I have never found the words for and will not pretend to now. I will only tell you that on the right morning, with the haze burning off and the light coming up pink and then gold across water flat enough to walk on, a boy in the back of his father's boat can believe that the day in front of him has no edges, that the water goes on forever and gives forever, that whatever you need is already out there waiting and all you have to do is go and take your share. That is the feeling. I had it many mornings. I want you to have had it too, because the chapter turns on its loss.

---

The men who work this water are masters of a craft most people will never see done.

Watch one work a set of oyster beds with a single oar. Not rowing, exactly. Sculling, standing in the stern, the one oar working the water in a motion so small and so continuous that the boat seems to move by intention rather than force, sliding from one bed to the next, holding station over a bar against a running tide with a flick of the wrist you would miss if you blinked. It is the most economical motion I know of in any trade. There is no wasted effort in it anywhere, no stroke that does not need to be made, the way there is no wasted word in a sentence that has been worked until it is right. A man who can scull a loaded Deadrise across a tideline with one oar has spent a lifetime taking the motion apart and putting it back smaller, and he does it without thinking about it at all, the way you walk.

They work in the cold. This is the part people who have not done it do not feel in their bones. The oyster season is a winter season, and the men go out in weather that would keep most of us indoors with the heat on, and they wear oilskin slicks against the spray. The slicks keep the water off. They do not keep the cold out. So under the slicks the men wear thick base layers and sweaters and more sweaters, every warm thing they own, until they are moving in a kind of armor of wool and cotton against a cold the slicks do nothing to stop. And here is the thing they all know and none of them say. The weight of all that clothing, soaked, is enough to drown a strong man. They know that if they go over the side into that black February water, the cold will take their hands in under a minute and the soaked wool will pull them down before they can get any of it off, and they will not get any of it off. They know this. They have known men it happened to. And every winter morning before light they put the clothing on and go out anyway, because that is the work, and the work is how the family eats, and they have made a private peace with the math of it that they do not discuss. I am not going to dress that up. They go out knowing, and they go out anyway, and there is a kind of courage in it that wears no name and asks for none.

The master of them, in the stories I grew up inside, was a man my father knew, a waterman from Fox Hill the older men called Biggie. Biggie has been dead more than thirty years now. I never worked beside him. Everything I know of him I know the way you know the saints of a family, from a story my father told so many times that I can finish it myself, and have, at tables, the way you take up the second half of a hymn.

The story is this. My father went out with Biggie one morning, out before light the way they all went, and they worked the beds through the morning and into the middle of the day. And around the time a working man starts to think about his lunch, my father noticed that Biggie had brought no lunch. No cooler, no bag, nothing. There was a six-pack of Coca-Cola in a wire carrier sitting in the boat, no ice, no cooler around it, just the bottles in their carrier in the open air. But no food. So my father asked him. He said, Biggie, where's your lunch? And Biggie smiled and said his lunch was still in the water. As fresh as it could be, he said, and not a minute older than it needed to be. He would pull it up when he was hungry and not before. That was the whole of it. The water was the larder. The man did not pack a lunch because the lunch was swimming under the boat, and all the cold storage he would ever need was the Bay itself, and the only thing that six-pack was for was something to drink while he ate what the water gave him when he was good and ready to ask for it.

That is the world I am telling you about. A commons so full that a man could go to work for the day and not think to bring food, because the place he was going would feed him directly, out of itself, fresher than anything he could carry. Hold onto that picture. I am going to set another man next to it before this chapter is done, and the distance between the two of them is the distance the whole chapter has to cross.

There was more to that world than the abundance, though, and I want to be careful to name it, because it is the part that gets left out of the story people tell about places like this. These men governed themselves. There was no boss out on the water, no company that owned the beds and hired the labor and set the terms. There were independent men in their own boats, and between them, holding it all together, an unwritten set of bargains that no one had signed and everyone kept.

I will give you one. There came a morning when a waterman's nets had been torn up, a season's worth of gear gone in a single bad night, the kind of loss that can end a man on the water for good if he has to bear it alone. And the other watermen, men with their own beds to work and their own families to feed and not one spare hour in their week, set the morning aside and rebuilt his nets with him. They lost the catch of that morning to do it. Nobody made them. Nobody could have made them, because there was no one over them to make them do anything. They did it because every one of them knew, in a way too deep to need saying, that the morning was coming when the man who needed the help would be him, and that a community that lets one of its own go under has started the arithmetic that ends with all of them under. So they governed the thing. They governed their own labor, across a shared water nobody owned, with no authority above them but the bargain, and the bargain held for generations.

I raise that because there is a famous idea that says it could not have happened. In 1968 the biologist Garrett Hardin published an essay called The Tragedy of the Commons, and the argument of it, which has been taught to nearly everyone who has taken an economics course since, is that a resource held in common and open to all is doomed. Each user, acting rationally in his own interest, takes a little more than his share, because the gain from taking is his alone while the cost of the taking is spread across everyone, and so every user over-takes, and the commons is stripped and ruined, and there is nothing anyone can do about it because the logic is built into the situation itself. The tragedy, in Hardin's telling, is not a failure of character. It is a structural certainty.

The watermen of Fox Hill are the thing Hardin's certainty cannot account for. They held a commons in common, open and unowned, exactly the situation the essay says ends in ruin, and they did not strip it, and they did not ruin it, and the reason they did not is that they governed it themselves, with bargains like the one over the torn nets, for longer than the theory says was possible. The commons was not destroyed by the men who depended on it. When it was finally drawn down, and it was, the hand that did it was not the watermen's. I am getting ahead of the story. Hold the bargain in mind. We are going to need it again.

---

I have been telling you about the before. Now I have to tell you about the loss, and the hard part of telling it is that it did not arrive as a catastrophe. It arrived as a sunset.

If you have spent time on an island, or anywhere that lives on the water and keeps its own slow time, you know the particular shape of a sunset there. It seems to take forever. The light goes long and gold and then orange and the whole sky gives itself over to the going-down by degrees, and you think you have all the time in the world to watch it, and you settle in to watch it, and then you look away for one moment, you look down to coil a line or answer a question, and when you look back up it is over. The sun is gone. The thing that seemed to take forever took no time at all, and you missed the moment it happened, because it happens slowly right up until the instant it happens all at once.

That is the shape of what happened to the water I grew up on. Slow, and then sudden, and you cannot point to the moment it turned.

The fish I loved to eat as a boy got harder to find, and then they were not there at all, and I could not have told you the year it happened. The striped bass, the rockfish, the fish that more than any other is the Bay's own, the fish whose runs the whole calendar of the water used to turn around, thinned out and thinned out. There was a bad season, and then another, and the men said it was a bad season or two, the way men on the water always say it is a bad season or two, because a bad season or two is a thing the water has always done and always corrected. But one of those bad seasons was the last real season, and nobody knew it at the time, and here is the part I will not soften: we kept taking. I kept taking. There was no villain in it. There was no fleet of bad men stripping the Bay out of greed. There were good men, my own people among them, who went out into a thinning water in a bad season and still took as much as they could carry to the shore, because that is what you do, because the family eats from what you carry in, because no single morning's catch was ever the one that broke it. Everyone was complicit and no one was guilty, which is the hardest kind of loss to account for and the kind this book keeps finding.

The men who study the water now will tell you it was real, and worse than a bad season or two. Chris Moore, who directs the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's work in Virginia, put the striped bass plainly when the latest stock assessment came back. *Despite recent management actions, striped bass continue to struggle. It is obvious that conditions have changed and striped bass will not have the same recovery they had in the 1980s. Warmer water from climate change, habitat loss, and invasive species are all making it very hard for striped bass to bounce back.* That is a scientist saying, in the careful language of his trade, that the fish are not coming back the way they came back before. The recovery the watermen are waiting for, the good season that corrects the bad ones, is a recovery the science says this water may not have in it anymore.

It is not only the bass. Allison Colden, who runs the Chesapeake Bay Foundation in Maryland, has watched the watermen feel the squeeze even where the numbers look like good news. The oyster population, she has noted, has tripled since 2005, and still the watermen are feeling the pinch, because a tripled population of a thing nearly wiped out is still a fraction of what the water once held, and because there are a lot fewer eyes on the water than there used to be, fewer boats, fewer men, fewer families carrying the knowledge forward. Colden and Moore both point past the fish themselves to the menhaden, the small oily fish at the bottom of the whole chain, the fish the bass eat and the ospreys eat and the whole Bay is built on. The concern about the menhaden is theirs, the scientists', on the record, and I am going to leave it where they put it. I am not going to build a number on the menhaden. I am only going to tell you that the people who know this water best are worried about the floor of the thing, and a worry like that, from people like that, is not nothing.

I want to put one more thing on the record about these men, because it is the thing Hardin's tragedy most cannot explain. When the striped bass collapsed in the 1980s, the watermen agreed to stop. Not the government over their objection. The men themselves, with the charter captains and the recreational anglers, agreed to a moratorium, a full stop, five years of taking no rockfish at all out of the Bay so that the fish might come back. Think about what that asked. These were men whose income came out of the water, who fed their families on what they carried to the shore, and they agreed, collectively, to forgo the very thing they lived on, for five years, on the bet that the water would refill if they let it. They paid a cost now for a resource later. They governed the shared thing together, at real expense to themselves, the precise behavior Hardin's model says is impossible, and they did it not once but as a matter of course, because that is who they were. The rockfish did come back, that time. The men who let them come back were the same men, on the same unowned water, that the famous theory said would strip it to nothing.

And now I have to set the other man down next to Biggie.

I cannot tell you his name. He is alive, and a private man, and the courtesy of these pages is to leave him out of them by name. But I can tell you what I saw. He is a waterman still, from the same Fox Hill water Biggie worked, a man who can scull a Deadrise with one oar and read a chop and do every hard thing the trade has ever asked. And he cannot feed his family on the water anymore. The water will not do it. So he runs a mechanic shop now, his name on the sign out front, crab pots stacked behind a locked fence in the yard, and he works the water when the water will pay and turns wrenches when it will not, and between the two of them he gets by. That is the squeeze. That is what the loss looks like when it finishes arriving. A man as good at the water as anyone who ever worked it, one generation downstream of a man whose lunch swam under the boat, and he cannot make the water feed his own family without a second trade to carry the months the water cannot. Biggie's water fed him directly. This man's water sends him to fix other men's engines. Same Fox Hill. Same skill in the same hands. One generation between them, and the abundance is gone, and what is left is a man getting by on the margin of a thing that used to give without being asked.

---

To understand where the abundance went, you have to go back to the shells, and to my own father's hands.

There was a time when the richest oyster operation in the world ran off the Hampton waterfront, a few miles from where I grew up. It belonged to a man named J. S. Darling, and it began in the 1880s, and at its height it was a thing the scale of which is hard to hold in the mind. The oysters came in by log canoe at dawn, boats hewn from single trees the way the watermen had built them for a century. A hundred and sixty shuckers worked the houses. Two hundred thousand bushels of oysters a year came through that operation, the meat shucked out and packed in ice and shipped north by rail, to the restaurants and the oyster houses of Baltimore and New York, fifty thousand gallons of shucked oyster a year by the late 1930s, the wealth of this water going up the coast on ice to be eaten in cities that never saw the Bay it came from.

And the shells. The meat went north, but the shells stayed, and there were so many of them that they could not be got rid of fast enough. They ran the empty shells up conveyor belts and dropped them onto piles, and the piles grew, and grew, until they stood more than four stories high on the Hampton waterfront, white mountains of empty shell you could see from across the water, the discarded skeleton of an abundance that was being eaten in another city.

I never saw those mountains. Darling shut down in 1979, and I was born in 1980, and so the four-story shell piles on the Hampton waterfront are not a thing I remember. They are a thing I inherited the way I inherited Biggie, as my father's story. I was born into the after. I came into a water that had already been the richest oyster ground in the world and was already on the far side of it, and I never knew the abundance directly, only the stories of it, told by men who had stood in front of those white mountains and watched the meat go north on ice. That is a quiet thing to carry, being born just past the edge of a thing your own father stood inside. I do not know how to say it except plainly. The abundance was real, and it was here, and it was mine by inheritance and not by sight, and by the time I could have seen it for myself it was gone.

Here is what became of the shells, and it is the part that turns out to matter most. Some of them went back into the water, carted out to Back River and other grounds and returned to the bottom to give the next generation of oysters something to grow on, because an oyster needs old shell to grow on and will not set on bare mud. But most of them did not go back. Most of them were crushed, ground up, and spread across the region as cheap surfacing, because crushed oyster shell was cheaper than rock and there was a mountain of it sitting right there. They paved roads with it. They paved wharves and lanes and driveways with it, all across that part of Virginia, for years.

I know this in my own family, in my father's own hands. One college summer my father worked on the crew that poured the boardwalk at Buckroe Beach. I want to be exact about this, because the exact version is the one that is true. He poured it in concrete and rock. There was no oyster shell in that boardwalk, whatever the regional habit of paving with shell might lead you to assume, and I can tell you it had none because the boardwalk he poured is still standing, and I have stood on it. The shell in my family is closer to home than the boardwalk. It is under our own feet, in the driveway of the house I grew up in, paved with crushed oyster shell because shell was cheaper than rock, the way half the driveways in that country were. The severed substrate is not a figure in a history book for me. It is the crunch of my own footsteps on my own driveway, the ground-up skeletons of the thing that used to feed this water, laid down as cheap paving because nobody knew, or nobody counted, what the shell was actually for.

What the shell was for is the whole of it. Here is the thing about an oyster reef that a coal seam can never be. A coal seam is finite from the day it forms. There is a fixed amount of it in the ground and every ton you bring up is a ton that is never coming back, and when it is gone it is gone forever, and nothing you do returns it. An oyster reef is the opposite kind of thing. An oyster reef is an instrument. It makes more oysters every year, on its own, for free, as long as you do not destroy the instrument. The old shell is the instrument. The new oysters set on the old shell, and grow, and die, and become the old shell the next generation sets on, and the reef builds itself up out of its own dead and goes on making oysters across the generations without diminishing, as long as the people taking from it take less than it makes and leave the shell where it belongs. Take some, leave the rest to spawn, return the shell to the bottom, and the reef feeds you and your children and their children and never runs down. That is a renewable commons. That is the kind of thing that lasts exactly as long as the community holds the rate of taking below the rate of making, and not one season longer.

This is the renewable resource that Elinor Ostrom spent her life describing, the self-renewing common pool that a community of the people who depend on it can govern and sustain across generations, precisely the kind of commons the Fox Hill watermen held and kept with their bargains and their moratorium. The reef-as-instrument is the textbook case of the thing her work showed could be governed without being destroyed. And what was done to it on the Hampton waterfront was to grind the instrument up and pave the roads with it. They took the old shell, the engine of the whole renewing thing, and they crushed it for cheap surfacing because in the accounting of the day a mountain of empty shell was a disposal problem and not a reef, and so the instrument that would have gone on making oysters for free, forever, was spread under the tires of the region as the cheapest gravel anyone could buy.

And here is the full circle, the one that should make you sit still for a second. They ground up the regenerative shell, the free instrument that would have built the reef for nothing across the generations. And now, on that same water, the public is paying to build oyster reefs out of concrete. At the naval station at Hampton Roads, a crew puts concrete blocks down on the bottom, cast and stacked by hand, structures called oyster castles, built so the spat will have something hard to set on, something that does what the discarded shell would have done for free. The Navy paid for them. The public paid for them. They are pouring concrete into the Bay to do the exact job that four stories of oyster shell would have done at no cost at all, if the shell had been returned to the water instead of crushed under the roads. That is the picture I want you to hold while I do the accounting. The free thing was thrown away and paved over, and now the same job is being done in concrete on the public dime.

---

So let me do for the oyster what this book refused to leave undone for the miner. Let me add up the column.

The instrument for it has a name. I will call it the Restitution Bond, and it is the backward-looking instrument of this framework. It looks backward at a commons that was drawn down, prices the physical cost of making it whole, and names who should hold that bill. It is the partner to a forward-looking instrument that prices a cost before it lands and holds the money in trust against the day of the harm. The forward one is for later chapters and a later case. This chapter runs the backward one, on this reef, once, to a floor.

That backward-looking move is not mine. It belongs to a lineage I want to name even as I borrow it. The discipline of looking back at a documented injury, pricing it by the concrete cost of repair rather than by anyone's wounded feelings, and assigning the bill to the party that captured the value and to its successors, is the discipline the reparations economists built. William Darity and Kirsten Mullen worked it out at length in *From Here to Equality*, and Ta-Nehisi Coates made the architecture of the argument plain for a general readership in *The Case for Reparations*. What I am doing here is using their instrument inside their own typology, the backward look, the documented injury, the bill assigned to the party that took the value. The difference is only the object. They built it for a human injury that I have no standing to price and will not. I am pointing it at a reef. The thing I am putting a number on is a substrate, a physical bottom that can be measured and restored, and nothing in what follows assigns a dollar figure to any human being's loss. The lineage is theirs. The object is the oyster rock.

Now, the discipline of the floor, before any number lands. There is a great deal I am not going to price, and I want to name the things I am leaving off and tell you why. I am not going to price the lost fishery. I am not going to price the menhaden, the worry the scientists put on the record about the floor of the food chain. I am not going to price the livelihoods, the watermen who left the water, the knowledge that left with them, the man with the mechanic shop and the locked fence. I am not going to price the foregone oysters, the harvests that would have come off a reef that was never ground up. Every one of those is a real cost. Every one of them is larger than the number I am about to give you. And every one of them invites an argument I do not need to have, a fight over a causal chain or a counterfactual or a discount rate, the kind of fight that lets a man who does not want to pay spend ten years arguing about the size of the bill instead of the fact of it. So I am going to leave all of that on the table, out in the open, named and unpriced, and I am going to price exactly one thing. The reef bottom. Physical, documented, on the public record, the kind of thing nobody can argue me out of.

And I am not going to build the number from the shell mountains, tempting as they are. The four-story piles are the image of the severance, the thing you can see, and they are doing their job as an image and I am going to let them keep doing it and not ask them to be a price. Raw shell is cheap. If I built the bill out of the volume of those piles times a century of accrued anything, I would have a big, shocking, indefensible number, exactly the kind of number a critic takes apart in a single afternoon and then uses to discredit everything else I have said. The mountains are not the price. The price is built from acres.

Here is the floor. The high-quality oyster rock of the James River, which I will name as the stand-in it is for the Hampton bars right next to it, because the James is where the careful survey work was done and honesty requires me to tell you I am using it as the proxy, was mapped at a little over seven thousand acres in 1910 and a little over four thousand by 1981. That is a loss of about two thousand seven hundred and thirty-eight acres of reef bottom, a drop of about thirty-nine percent in the extent of the rock, gone. To restore an acre of reef costs real money that other people have already spent and documented. At the rate realized on the Great Wicomico restoration it runs about seven thousand three hundred dollars an acre, and the forward engineering plans for that river put it nearer thirteen thousand five hundred. Multiply the foreclosed acres by those costs and you get the floor. Roughly twenty to thirty-seven million dollars. That is the conservative number, the deliberately smallest defensible figure I can stand behind, built from documented acres and documented per-acre costs, with every contested thing left off the books.

There is a more robust figure, and I will tell you what it is and then tell you I am not leading with it. Across the whole bay-wide program, the completed restoration work has averaged closer to seventy-seven thousand dollars an acre, the real all-in cost of doing the job at scale. Run the same two thousand seven hundred and thirty-eight acres at that rate and you get about two hundred and eleven million dollars. That is the robust center of the estimate, the cherry-pick-proof middle of the thing, and it is not the floor, and I am not leading with it. I am leading with the floor, because the floor is the part nobody can take from me.

Now set that floor next to what the reef was worth while it still stood, because the gap between the two is the whole point. The oysters that come off this water sell at the dock for about thirty-five dollars a bushel, and in a good peak year the dockside value of the harvest runs around seven million dollars. That is what the water pays the men who work it. But the oyster does not stop at the dock. By the time those same oysters reach a plate in a restaurant in another city, a bushel of two hundred oysters at a few dollars an oyster is worth something closer to a hundred and twenty million dollars a year, realized in Richmond and Washington and Baltimore and New York, in the dining rooms where the meat went on ice a hundred years ago and goes still. Hold the three numbers together and you can see the shape of the thing. The table value, the hundred and twenty million, goes up and out, to other cities, other accounts. The dockside fee, the seven million, is the labor charge for shipping that wealth away, and it stays local and it is shrinking. And the cost, the floor, the twenty to thirty-seven million it takes to make the reef whole, stays right here, settles down onto this water and these men, and waits for someone to pay it.

This is the move the framework makes, and I want to be honest about exactly how much of it is new. Mariana Mazzucato, in *The Value of Everything*, drew the line between the value a thing creates and the value somebody captures, between making and taking, and named the difference. The difference between the hundred and twenty million on the plates and the seven million at the dock is that difference, made local and made literal on one reef. What I am adding to her diagnosis is the accounting, the number. David Harvey gave the mechanism a name too, accumulation by dispossession, the engine by which value is extracted upward and the cost is dispossessed onto the commons and the people who live on it. He diagnosed the mechanism. What I am doing is measuring its magnitude on this water, the dockside fee against the table value against the public restoration bill, the thing Harvey described, given a figure. I am not claiming to have discovered the mechanism. Mazzucato named the taking and Harvey named the dispossession, and they were right, and the only thing this book adds is the number on the end of it, on a specific reef, where the specificity is the point.

Now the fact that makes the whole case hard to argue with.

The reef is already being rebuilt. It is happening right now, and it is being paid for by the public. In Maryland, the state, through its Department of Natural Resources, has put more than ninety-two million dollars into oyster restoration across five sanctuary reefs covering roughly thirteen hundred acres. Bay-wide, the itemized federal and state restoration spending comes to about a hundred and fifteen million dollars, ninety-three and a half million in Maryland and nearly twenty-two million in Virginia. There is a rounder figure sometimes quoted at the program level, around a hundred and eight million; it corroborates the magnitude and nothing more, and I am not going to lean on it. And at the naval station at Hampton Roads, the oyster castles I told you about, the concrete blocks doing the discarded shell's old job, were paid for by the Navy and the federal Chesapeake Bay Program, with spat from the Bay program's own hatcheries. I am not going to invent a number for the castles to make a point. I am only going to tell you who paid for them, which was the public, and who was absent from the funding, which brings me to the column that matters most.

The oyster industry's line in this column is zero. Not small. Zero. Every dollar going into the reef restoration is public money, from the federal government, from the Army Corps of Engineers, from Maryland's Department of Natural Resources, from Virginia's Marine Resources Commission, from the Navy. The industry that took the value off this water, that shipped the meat north on ice and crushed the shell under the roads, has put nothing into rebuilding the thing it drew down. The finding here is not about the size of the bill. The bill is being paid. The finding is that it is being paid by the wrong party. The value went up and out a century ago, to other cities and other accounts, and the cost has come due now, and it has landed, every dollar of it, on the public.

I have seen this exact shape once already in this book, and so have you. Two hundred and fifty miles west of here, in a coal county where the value went up and out to the steel mills and the cities and the cost stayed in the hollows, the public is paying to reclaim the land the industry tore up, while the companies that took the coal are bankrupt or gone or were never on the hook in the first place. The defendant in that case was never in the room, and the defendant in this one is not either. It is the same severance, done wet instead of dry, on a renewable commons instead of a finite seam, but it is the same defendant, the same instrument that does not exist, the same public left holding the same bill. I am not going to re-derive that case here. I only want you to feel the rhyme.

So understand what the Restitution Bond actually does, because it is more modest than it sounds. It does not invent a new bill. The bill already exists. It is already being paid, right now, in public money, on the reefs and at the naval station. What the bond does is name who should have been holding that bill all along, which is the party that captured the value and its successors, and move the cost back onto the value where it belongs, and pay the money into the reef, into the commons, into the instrument itself, rather than as cash to any individual. It restores the thing, not the person. It makes the substrate whole.

Twenty to thirty-seven million dollars. That is the floor. The real figure is higher, and I know it is higher, and I have told you every reason it is higher and then left every one of those reasons off the books on purpose. The lost fishery, the menhaden, the livelihoods, the man at the mechanic shop, the harvests off a reef that was never ground up, all of it real, all of it larger, all of it deliberately uncounted. I left it off because the floor is the part nobody can take away from me. You can argue about everything I declined to price. You cannot argue about the acres of rock and the documented cost of an acre of rock, and so the floor stands, and the floor is enough to make the case.

What was done for the oil could be done for the oyster, and there is a country that has already done a version of it, that took the wealth of a vanishing resource and held it in trust for the people who come after instead of letting it disperse into accounts that were not theirs. That is the next chapter, and the existence proof it turns on. For now I will leave the reef where it is, half rebuilt on the public dime, the floor on the books, the instrument named, and the bill, at last, addressed to the right party.

The harbor will wake tomorrow before the city does. The diesels will turn over in the dark and the voices will carry across the water and the men will go out, fewer of them than there were, into a water that gives less than it gave. They will go out anyway. They always have. The least I can do, who was born into the after and never saw the mountains, is add up the column honestly and write down who owes it.
