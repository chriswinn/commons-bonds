# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 9: Pricing Honestly

---

In 1960 a ton of coal left a mine mouth in McDowell County, West Virginia, for just under five dollars. The Energy Information Administration's historical price tables put the figure at $4.71. The previous chapter spent its whole length pricing what that ton actually cost, component by component: the lungs, the water, the towns, the foreclosed futures, and above all the carbon, 2.61 metric tons of carbon dioxide per ton of that seam's coal by the Environmental Protection Agency's emission factors, which at the agency's 2023 central estimate of $190 per metric ton comes to $496 on its own. The honest total came to $510. The difference between the two numbers moved off the invoice and onto McDowell County: into lungs, into groundwater, and forward in time onto people who had no say in the sale.

I have run that subtraction more times than I can count, at a desk within sight of water that carries its own version of it, and it has never once come out differently.

The gap is general in a way that is easy to overstate and important to state exactly. It opens wherever extraction outruns restoration: always for the coal and the ore, because a seam laid down once is restored never; for the oyster and the forest only when the taking outpaces the regrowing; and it closes to zero for any harvest that stays inside what the system can remake. The Chesapeake chapters watched a fishery cross that line in a single working generation, and the men on the water knew the morning math before any economist did.

A reader who has accepted the arithmetic this far has earned a practical question: what do we do about it? The answer takes four steps, in order, and every component of it is already operating somewhere on Earth right now. Classify. Reserve. Invest. Reassess. Sort resources by what their loss would foreclose. Hold back a fraction of the riskiest ones, in the ground, unmined and unburned. Spend the time that buys on substitutes. Re-rank as knowledge changes. Nothing in the sequence requires a world government, a constitutional convention, or a technology that does not exist.

### Step 1: Classify

Ask a hydrologist how long the Ogallala aquifer takes to refill and the answer comes back in thousands of years; the farmer drawing it down in the Texas Panhandle is mining fossil water as surely as the men of McDowell County mined fossil carbon. Ask the same question about wind, and the question answers itself by tomorrow morning. Resources do not carry the same risk, and a pricing regime that treats them alike will be wrong about most of them.

The sorting variable is the existential substitutability gap: the distance between what a resource does for the civilization that depends on it and what anything else could do in its place once it is gone. The phrase is a mouthful; call it the gap. Three bins are enough.

The low bin holds the abundant and the substitutable: iron, or coal considered purely as a fuel, replaceable in principle by other materials and other sources of heat. The renewable commons sit here too, sunlight, wind, the oxygen fraction of the atmosphere, regional rainfall, and they are low by construction, because they are themselves the renewable substrate that every substitute in Step 3 will rest on. Used within their regeneration, their commons price is zero. For everything in this bin the move is the familiar one: price the externality tail, the correction Pigou proposed a century ago; require reclamation bonds adequate to the real damage; and then let a corrected market allocate.

The middle bin holds resources abundant enough that outright reservation would be overkill but concentrated enough that someone is collecting rents on them. The move there is progressive extraction rates plus a sovereign claim on the rents, so that the value of the commons accrues to the commons.

The high bin holds the resources whose loss forecloses: geologically concentrated, chemically stubborn, and required for technologies that have not been invented yet but very likely will be. The rare earth elements. The platinum-group metals. Helium and its isotopes, which no industrial process can manufacture in quantities that matter. Phosphorus, without which there is no agriculture at scale. Fossil groundwater that regenerates on millennial timescales. These get the move the whole instrument is organized around: reservation.

None of this requires a committee of philosophers. Materials scientists, energy economists, agronomists, aerospace engineers, and hydrologists already hold most of the needed information, and pieces of the apparatus already exist in public. The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act came into force in May 2024 carrying a list of 34 critical materials, 17 of them designated strategic; the United States Geological Survey publishes mineral assessments every year. What exists nowhere is a single legible ranking that integrates substitutability, stock dynamics, the externality tail, and criticality into one score. Producing one is a matter of months, not decades, and a rough three-bin sort is enough to drive every decision that follows.

The resource optimist will object here, in the tradition Julian Simon made famous against Paul Ehrlich, that scarcity summons substitutes and no missing price needs finding. Substitution usually does win, which is exactly why the third step of this instrument is a substitution budget; the reserve exists for the residual that substitution may not reach in time, and the Technical Appendix develops the conditions under which that residual is worth insuring.

### Step 2: Reserve

The United States already stores crude oil in salt caverns along the Gulf Coast against the day a war or a hurricane interrupts supply, and most industrial countries keep mineral stockpiles of one kind or another. The second step borrows the habit and removes the warehouse. For resources in the high bin, designate a fraction of the known economic deposits as a strategic reserve and leave it where it lies: in the seam, in the brine, in the ore body, in the aquifer.

The idea sounds strange on first hearing and obvious on the second. The ground is free storage, permanent, requiring no refrigeration, no security perimeter, no insurance premium, no decay allowance. Helium held in a tank eventually leaks; helium held in the formation that trapped it does not.

What does reservation cost? Only the foregone revenue on the reserved fraction, and less of that than intuition suggests, because extraction in any given decade is constrained by current demand and mining throughput, not by the size of the deposit. A reserved fraction mostly defers revenue rather than destroying it, and defers it into a future where, for a high-gap resource, the price is very unlikely to be lower.

Held this way, the reserve behaves like an option a careful buyer would pay real money for, except that here it comes nearly free. It costs little to hold. It appreciates as the unreserved stock depletes. If scarcity arrives, it pays out the full substitution value of the resource at the moment of greatest need. And if substitution wins instead, the reserve is reclassified and released, at which point the cost of having held it rounds to a clerical fee on a century.

Nothing here requires a new international body. The petroleum reserves and the stockpiles already exist; what they have lacked is a principle for what to hold and how much. The ranking from Step 1 supplies the what. The how much comes from the asymmetric regret rule an earlier chapter set down, which was built for exactly this decision and goes to work here. Reserve and be wrong, and the cost is modest revenue deferred, spread across a hundred years and a planetary economy. Extract and be wrong, and the cost is a foreclosed civilizational capability that no amount of regret can put back in the ground. When the insurance is cheap and the downside is unbounded, you buy the insurance.

The market-oriented reader will hear industrial policy in this, a government picking winners, a distortion inviting capture. But reservation subsidizes no producer and picks no technology; it is the decision not to foreclose an option, sized by regret, and everything outside the reserved fraction is allocated by the same corrected market Step 1 left in charge.

### Step 3: Invest

In the Texas Panhandle, a farmer is watching the irrigation pivots in the next county come off the fields one by one as the wells stop drawing what they used to draw. A reserve cannot help him; the water his neighbors already pumped is gone. What a reserve buys, there and everywhere, is time, and the third step decides what the time is for.

The first priority is substitutes, and the agenda runs far wider than renewable power. Battery chemistries that do not route through lithium and cobalt. Recycled and synthesized flows that displace virgin rare earths. Regenerative agriculture that takes the pressure off fossil aquifers and mined phosphate. Alternative proteins that take it off collapsing fisheries. Each of these does three jobs with one dollar: it lowers the residual commons value of the high-gap resource it substitutes for, which makes whatever extraction continues cheaper in honest terms; it preserves the optionality of the reserve, which can be released the day the substitute matures; and it shrinks the externality tail of future extraction. Public budgets rarely contain a line item that reduces present harm, future harm, and future need at the same time.

The second priority sounds like science fiction and is actually a comparison of ledgers. A lifeless rock in space has no community to bear severed costs, no biosphere for damage to cascade through, an externality tail measured against vacuum; its gap is about as low as a gap can be. As launch costs decline, material from bodies like that will eventually compete on total honest cost with terrestrial deposits that carry their full residual commons value. The arithmetic says nothing about when. It says only which way the comparison tilts over time: the eventual destination of bulk extraction for the most abundant materials is off this planet, for the same reason a sensible county quarries gravel from the pit outside town and never from the churchyard.

The governing principle of the third step is not never extract; it is extract first from the source whose honest cost is lowest. For most materials over the next century that means substitution. For a narrow set, eventually, it means rock that was never alive. Both conclusions fall out of the arithmetic rather than out of anyone's politics, and the option-value method behind them is developed in the Technical Appendix.

### Step 4: Reassess

For most of the twentieth century the United States sold helium so cheaply that the program amounted to a giveaway; the gas that now cools the magnets a hospital patient slides between was treated as federal surplus. Cobalt traveled the other direction: unremarkable a generation ago, it now sits on the critical lists of most industrial nations. Classification is a snapshot of knowledge, and knowledge moves.

So the fourth step makes the ranking a living document. A high-gap resource can become low-gap inside thirty years if a substitution breakthrough matures: magnet chemistries that skip the rare earths, sodium-ion storage at grid parity, instruments designed around helium. When that happens, reserved fractions reclassify, some released to the corrected market, some held permanently as a hedge against the substitute's own failure modes. The bodies that would maintain the document already exist, the Geological Survey, the European Union's science apparatus, the International Energy Agency, the national stockpile programs, and a cadence of five to ten years is enough. What none of them has ever had is a structure inside which reassessment matters. A gap-driven reserve is that structure, because every revision moves real fractions of real deposits in or out of bond.

One exposure should be named rather than buried: everything above is sensitive to how heavily the future is discounted, the ground on which Nicholas Stern and William Nordhaus famously divided, and the quantitative treatment of that dispute lives in the Technical Appendix.

### At the Border

Outside Baotou, in Inner Mongolia, a tailings pond from rare-earth processing covers roughly 4 square miles, about 10 square kilometers, its slurry carrying the radioactive leavings of the separation chemistry, and a woman this book has stood beside before has been cooking for thirty years at a kitchen window that faces it. In the Democratic Republic of the Congo, which until very recently supplied the great bulk of the world's cobalt, UNICEF estimated in 2014, in a figure Amnesty International carried forward two years later, that some 40,000 children were working in the mining of it. The phone in your pocket is priced as if neither place existed.

Honest pricing run inside one border has a leak. A country that prices its own extraction honestly while importing unpriced extraction from elsewhere has not closed anything; it has relocated the doorway through which severance enters its economy. The import is the new mine mouth.

The response is a principle, not a drafted treaty: apply the same pricing at the border, scaled to the residual commons value the exporting country declined to price. Mechanically this is a border adjustment, and it is no longer hypothetical. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, CBAM, is a partial, real, imperfectly working version of exactly this move for carbon alone, and its existence settles the feasibility question at the level that matters: such a thing can be drafted, legislated, administered, and survived diplomatically. It faces genuine challenges under World Trade Organization non-discrimination rules and genuine retaliation risk, and no one should oversell it. But it spreads the way carbon pricing itself spread, through market access rather than treaty, beginning wherever any country or bloc decides to begin.

### The Rent Check

Somewhere in every American city tonight a renter is performing this book's subtraction without knowing it, looking at what comes in and what goes out and finding that the second number has annexed the first. Roughly half of the renting households in the United States now pay more than 30 percent of their income in rent; Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies documented the figure in *America's Rental Housing 2024*. Half. The burden shows up in metros that make building nearly impossible and in metros that have built a great deal.

The strongest account of why begins with supply, and it deserves to be heard at full strength. Edward Glaeser and Joseph Gyourko showed, across two decades of work on supply elasticity, that American housing prices climb where regulation makes building hardest; Jenny Schuetz's land-use research traces the mechanism through zoning codes, lot-size minimums, parking requirements, and permitting that can stretch approval into years. Where building is easy, prices track construction costs. Where it is choked, prices leave construction costs far behind. On this account the cure is to legalize housing: upzone, streamline, let supply move. The evidence behind that account is real, and nothing in this book argues against building more housing where people want to live.

What the supply account describes and what this book has been describing operate at different levels of the same case. Supply restriction explains why the pool of housing is scarce. It does not, by itself, settle who captures the scarcity. The claim this book adds, and it should be stated plainly as the book's interpretive argument rather than a settled finding of the housing literature, since the supply-side scholars contest it, is that a meaningful share of the cost burden is rent in the older sense of the word: value captured through consolidation and coordinated pricing, stacked on top of the supply constraint rather than instead of it.

The clearest exhibit was software rather than a smoke-filled room. RealPage sold landlords an algorithmic pricing service that pooled their private data and recommended rents, and the Department of Justice treated the arrangement as what it functionally resembled: it opened an antitrust investigation in 2022, filed a formal complaint in August 2024, and reached a proposed settlement in November 2025 that awaits court approval. The litigation was conducted in the vocabulary of antitrust. It reads fluently in the vocabulary of severed cost.

The coal counties in this book's early chapters ran on a famous arrangement: work for the company, rent from the company, owe the company store. That arrangement survived by dispersing. A household that hands more than 30 cents of every dollar to a consolidated landlord class it can neither bargain with nor exit, while capturing none of the upside of the asset it is paying off, is living in the distributed edition.

### The Pieces Already in Place

The four steps can sound utopian until you count what already exists. So count.

Carbon pricing operates today across many jurisdictions, covering a substantial and growing share of global emissions; the World Bank's *State and Trends of Carbon Pricing* reports track the spread year over year. Nearly all of it prices carbon below the social cost the EPA estimates, much of it leaks at the borders, and it is real: legislated, litigated, administered, politically defended, expanding. The hardest institutional step, getting any price at all onto a severed cost, has been taken again and again.

Sovereign wealth funds show rents being captured for the commons at two different depths. Alaska has, since 1976, paid a share of its mineral royalties into a constitutional trust the Alaska Permanent Fund Corporation valued at roughly $89 billion in 2026, and the state has cut every resident a dividend each year since 1982. Norway, the existence proof of Chapter 4, is better read another way. By Norges Bank Investment Management's accounting, the fund now stands near $2 trillion, roughly $390,000 for every citizen, and read directly off the fund, Norway internalized about $48 on every barrel-equivalent it produced. That figure is a floor, what one polity demonstrably internalized, with the true residual value above it. And the fund's deeper work is what it does to irreversibility: a country holding that much restorative capacity has preserved the option of repair, and it is irreversibility that makes foreclosure expensive. The Technical Appendix carries the parameters.

Strategic reservation is already ordinary. Most industrial nations keep petroleum reserves and mineral stockpiles. The United States' one significant rare-earth mine, at Mountain Pass in California, closed in 2002, passed through the bankruptcy of its operator Molycorp in 2015, was acquired in 2017, and restarted in 2018 under MP Materials, a restoration driven by exactly the supply-chain anxiety the high bin formalizes. Japan, after its rare-earth imports were abruptly squeezed in 2010, built stockpiling, recycling, substitution research, and alternative suppliers into national policy. The European Union wrote its own anxiety into law as the Critical Raw Materials Act. None of these programs was designed in this book's vocabulary; each is gap-driven reservation arriving under its own name, wherever dependency made the classification problem visible enough to act on.

Finance received its wedge after 2008. The Dodd-Frank Act moved, partially and against sustained resistance, to make the institutions that capture the upside of financial risk hold more of its downside: capital buffers, resolution plans, retained risk. That is accountability-bond logic, the book's second instrument, operating in the sector most hostile to it. The crisis that produced the statute also showed the cost of pricing dishonestly. The response of 2008 and 2009 recapitalized bank balance sheets while roughly 4.1 million American households went through completed foreclosure, by CoreLogic's count, and about $11 trillion in household wealth evaporated, by the Federal Reserve's flow-of-funds accounting. Under honest pricing the alternative response, restructuring household debt directly, was visible in advance, and Atif Mian and Amir Sufi's analysis of the episode supports it; the full case lives in Chapter 5.

Every surface mine in the United States posts a reclamation bond before it cuts ground; the principle that the digger pre-pays the repair is settled federal law. The bonds are structurally underpriced, the pool covers a fraction of true remediation cost, and the early chapters of this book watched the difference move through bankruptcy courts onto the public. The instrument needs recalibration rather than invention: price the bond to the real cost of repair, index it, refresh it as the liability tail lengthens.

The National Environmental Policy Act monetizes nothing, and it has survived decades of hostile litigation by doing one durable thing: forcing the externality tail into the public record before the decision gets made. It is the procedural half of pricing, already built. The border adjustment, covered above, is already running for carbon in one of the world's largest trading blocs. And the Community Reinvestment Act states, in the dry idiom of bank regulation, the spatial principle this book met in coal country: capital drawn from a place owes something to the place.

A different family of pieces proves a different premise: that ordinary polities can hold serious assets in common, at scale, for a long time, in unfriendly weather. In Chattanooga, the city's municipally owned electric utility, EPB, launched a fiber network in 2010 and delivered citywide gigabit internet, now multi-gigabit, at prices below the private incumbents, profitably, cross-subsidizing a smart grid, and it survived incumbent litigation and a state law restricting its expansion. In the Basque country, the Mondragon federation of worker cooperatives has operated since 1956, grown into the largest worker-owned system in the world, and held to an internal discipline on the ratio between its highest and lowest pay, through the end of a dictatorship, accession to the European Union, the 2008 crisis, and the bankruptcy of its flagship appliance cooperative, Fagor. In Vienna, roughly 60 percent of the city lives in municipally owned Gemeindebau or non-profit cooperative housing, a program begun in 1919 and sustained across a century of regime change and financing regimes, with rents well below private comparables and no stigma attached, because when most of a city lives somewhere, there is no one left to do the stigmatizing.

Then there is the place where all four steps are being argued right now, in public, over the largest mineral province anyone has ever proposed to mine. The Clarion-Clipperton Zone, a stretch of Pacific abyssal plain covering roughly 4.5 million square kilometers, is strewn with polymetallic nodules, potato-sized accretions of nickel, cobalt, copper, and manganese laid down over millions of years. The International Seabed Authority, established in 1994 under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, has issued exploration contracts since 2001 and has been negotiating a commercial mining code under a deadline the island nation of Nauru triggered in 2021. Run the steps in order. Classify: the metals are high-gap, which is the entire commercial case for going down there. Reserve: the deposit is already in the ground; the open question is only what fraction to bond and leave. Invest: the recovery timescales of abyssal ecosystems are unknown at the scale of decades to centuries, the unbounded externality tail Chapter 7 established with its ocean-world treatment, while the substitution agenda for nickel and cobalt is the same one Step 3 already funds. Reassess: if battery chemistry designs around both metals, the commercial case evaporates on its own. Asymmetric regret points toward preserve and study. The honest counter-case belongs in the same breath: terrestrial nickel and cobalt mining carry severe human and ecological costs of their own, some of them visible from a kitchen window in Baotou and a pit in the Congo, so the regret arithmetic is contested in both directions and the preserve verdict is not obvious. What the dispute finally turns on is the stress test Chapter 7 called simulated reattachment: announce that the severed cost will be attached to the price, and watch the institutional immune response. Whether the Seabed Authority's verification machinery can withstand that response is being answered in real time, by the parties, in the negotiating record. The reading is the contribution; the ruling belongs to the parties.

Each of these pieces is partial. Each evolved piecemeal, under a specific crisis, carried by a specific coalition, and none was designed with this book's vocabulary in mind. Together they answer the feasibility question in the only way it can honestly be answered: every component of honest pricing, classification, reservation, substitution investment, reassessment, border adjustment, bonded accountability, collective holding, already exists somewhere, has been legislated somewhere, has survived elections and courtrooms somewhere, and has hardened into institutional furniture. What does not yet exist is the connection among them. The work ahead is incremental institutional work, strengthening, aligning, extending, made strategic by the principle that organizes it.

### What Ownership Cannot Reach

The most serious objection left standing comes from the tradition this book is closest to and borrows most from. Property rights work. Fisheries that were sliding toward collapse have stabilized under quota systems that make next year's fish this year's asset; forests regrow where an owner internalizes the timber value across decades. Ronald Coase showed in 1960 that where rights are well defined and bargaining is cheap, the parties will price an externality between themselves without a regulator in the room, and Elinor Ostrom spent a career documenting the institutional craft by which communities govern shared resources without either privatizers or central ministries. For goods that are rivalrous, excludable, and present-tense, ownership is often the best preservation technology civilization has found. If that is so, why is any of the apparatus above necessary?

Because three structural features of the cases in this book sit beyond what ownership can reach. First, intergenerational foreclosure: future generations hold no deeds and cannot bid, so every Coasean bargain over a resource whose loss they will bear is missing its largest party, and the bargain is structurally incomplete however clear the rights. Second, transboundary externality: drainage, atmospheric carbon, aquifer depletion, the dust off a tailings pond respect no fence and no register of title. Third, information: markets price what their participants currently know, and they cannot price uses not yet invented, which is precisely the value the reserve insures; the information does not exist at the moment of pricing. The foundational chapters built each of these slowly, and the Technical Appendix carries the formal versions. The consequence is the point here. Pigou's corrective tax, Coase's bargain, and Ostrom's design principles are not being discarded; they are being extended into the three territories none of them was built for. Honest pricing, on this account, is what completing the market looks like. Adam Smith would have recognized the project: he trusted prices because he expected them to tell the truth about cost.

If the unpaid bill is this large, why has it stayed unpaid for a century? The cleanest answer in the literature comes from the public-choice economists, and this book takes it gratefully. Mancur Olson's *The Logic of Collective Action* showed in 1965 why a benefit concentrated on a few reliably defeats a cost diffused across the many: the few can organize, monitor, and pay for the politics; the many cannot even cheaply discover what they are losing. Gordon Tullock saw in 1967 that resources spent capturing favorable rules are a pure social loss, an insight the literature later named rent-seeking, and James Buchanan and the tradition he built generalized the analysis of how rules get shaped by the organized. Nothing in this book's ledger requires a villain, and the public-choice account is the reason. A reclamation bond stays underpriced not because anyone wicked wills it but because the handful who benefit attend every hearing and the millions who pay are never in the room, and the same asymmetry that opened the gap between price and cost polices that gap against closure. Where this book parts company with the tradition is at its frequent conclusion, that the remedy is less public decision-making; what it keeps is the mechanism, the account of who shaped the rules and why the bill persists. What the tradition declined to compile, and what these chapters have compiled, is the other half: the ledger of what the open bill cost, and who has been carrying it.

Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* supplies the legal half of the same answer. Assets become capital, she showed, by being coded in the modules of private law, property, collateral, trust, corporate form, bankruptcy, and because the coding is available to whoever can hire it, it systematically protects upside and sheds cost. That is why clear title alone never closed the gap: the shedding is in the code. Chapter 5 leaned on her diagnosis, and the bonds in this book are an attempt to write the reattachment of cost into instruments as durable as the ones that detached it.

The most direct recent challenge to a pricing project comes from Brett Christophers, whose *The Price Is Wrong* argued in 2024 that the climate transition is failing on profit rather than price: renewable electricity is frequently the cheapest power in history and is still underbuilt, because investment follows expected profit, and thin, volatile margins do not clear private hurdle rates without state support. His stronger claim deserves to be met at full strength rather than in caricature: that getting prices right is itself insufficient, and that decommodified public provision, power built because people need it rather than because it clears a hurdle rate, is what the transition actually requires. The honest response is that he is right about the limit, and the limit is where the instrument's real work begins. Pricing the residual commons value of fossil extraction does not, by itself, redirect a single dollar of capital. What it does is make the subsidy visible, the precise size of the unpaid bill that current arrangements quietly socialize, and a subsidy made visible is what the political work of redirecting capital has always needed to push against. Christophers names why capital does not move; this book's ledger names what the not-moving costs, and who pays it. The two converge on the same ground: public investment, and public-purpose criteria for allocating capital, stop looking like ideological extravagance and start looking like what honest accounting requires, once the gap between price and profit is understood as the structural problem rather than a market hiccup.

Behind all of these stands the oldest diagnosis in the modern literature. Karl Polanyi argued in 1944 that land, labor, and money are fictitious commodities, bought and sold as if produced for sale though none of them ever was, and that a society which prices them as ordinary goods sets off a double movement: the market disembedding itself from social life, and society scrambling, by whatever means it can improvise, to protect itself from the consequences. Nancy Fraser carried the diagnosis forward: behind every wage the market pays, she argued, stands a hinterland of unpaid taking, social reproduction and nature expropriated rather than exchanged. The commons of this book, the seam, the reef, the aquifer, the atmosphere, are Polanyi's land under other names, and the severed costs of the early chapters are Fraser's hinterland with a dollar column attached. What these chapters add to that lineage is an instrument: the double movement, which Polanyi described as society's reflex, becomes something a polity can do on purpose, with an account that keeps the taking visible and a reserve that refuses the foreclosure in advance.

A reader of Pistor will see the trap coiled inside that sentence: price the commons, bond the loss, and have you not simply coded nature into one more asset class, extending the very commodification Polanyi diagnosed? The distinction is between accounting and coding. Pricing a severed cost forces the books to tell the truth; reserving a deposit takes it off the market entirely; and the bond trades on the liability side, where obligations live, minting no tradable claim on the commons itself. Accounting the cost is not coding the asset.

### What a Framework Does

Anyone who has spent a season trying to move a bill through a legislature has been muttering the same objection for several pages now, and it deserves full volume. Carbon pricing, the simplest possible version of this program, one resource, one externality, the broadest scientific consensus in the history of public policy, has been argued for more than thirty years and remains partial, leaky, and riddled with exemptions where it exists at all. And the proposal here is to price the residual commons value of every high-gap resource, across every jurisdiction, with border adjustments, against the most organized lobbies on Earth? Show the pathway. Name the coalition. Produce the treaty draft. Otherwise this is one more book that diagnoses beautifully and cannot solve.

The objection lands, and the honest answer concedes its premise. This book does not legislate. No book does. The specific political pathway, which committee, which coalition, which crisis, is beyond what any single volume can produce, and a volume that pretended otherwise would be misrepresenting its genre.

What a framework does comes before legislation: it changes how people think, which changes what legislation becomes conceivable. David Harvey named accumulation by dispossession, and a generation's conversation about enclosure and gentrification reorganized itself around the pattern he had made visible. Elinor Ostrom named the design principles that existing local institutions had quietly evolved, and communities that had been told for decades to choose between privatization and the state discovered that their own arrangements were legitimate, studiable, and defensible. Kate Raworth drew an economy with a floor and a ceiling, and cities adopted the Doughnut as a guiding frame because it gave them a coherent way to think about what they were already half doing. Matthew Desmond's *Evicted* helped shift how a country talks about housing, eviction, and who profits from poverty. None of these authors passed a statute. Each changed the menu.

Commons Bonds is built for that kind of work. It hands over a vocabulary: cost severance, severed cost, residual commons value, the accountability bond, the existential substitutability gap. The vocabulary comes with a measurement tool, two independent ways of estimating what a commons is worth and a third reading that takes what a polity has demonstrably internalized as the floor, testable against any extraction in any jurisdiction. And the way of thinking travels without a license: a coal-country resident, a municipal council, a state legislature, or an international seabed negotiation can apply it to the case in front of them without asking anyone's permission. The wedges counted above are where the thinking goes to work: strengthen what exists, connect what is isolated, align what points in roughly the right direction, extend what stops short. The people who will do that work need a common language and a common instrument. Whether the work gets done is the question every generation answers for the one that follows.

Five people have been walking through this book, and they can stand together for a moment before the last chapter gathers them properly.

In McDowell County, where fewer than 20,000 people remain by the 2020 census, a miner is pulling on his boots in the dark, in a county that sold the world its coal at $4.71 a ton and was left holding the rest of the $510. On the lower Chesapeake, a waterman is going out onto water his grandfather would not recognize, working bars that the dredges and the runoff priced at nothing; he works the same body of water my own mornings begin on. Outside Baotou, a woman is cooking at a window that faces 4 square miles of tailings the price of a magnet never included. In the Texas Panhandle, a farmer is watching the pivots come off the next county's fields, drawing down in decades an aquifer that refills in millennia. And somewhere ahead of all of them, on a world this book visited to test its own arithmetic, an administrator is deciding how fast to pump the subsurface water ten thousand lives depend on, in a place where a severed cost has nowhere to hide.

These were never five problems. The mechanism that severs cost from value in a coal seam operates by the same structural logic in an oyster bar, a tailings pond, an aquifer, and a habitat's ice, with different parameters and the same arithmetic. What extraction costs, what pricing it honestly would look like, and how existing institutions might be bent toward it: those questions have now been answered as well as one volume can answer them. Classify, reserve, invest, reassess; adjust at the border; count the pieces; connect them.

The question that remains is a different kind of question, not policy but something closer to a fact about ourselves: what we are willing to owe the generations we belong to, and the ones we will never meet. There are moments, present-tense and concrete, where that question stops being abstract and gets decided, and the last chapter goes to meet them.
