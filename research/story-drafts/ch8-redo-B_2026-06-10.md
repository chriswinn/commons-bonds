# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*

## Chapter 8: What Things Actually Cost

---

Suppose, for one morning, that everything had to sell for the whole of what it costs. Not just the portion that reached the invoice. All of it. No ideology is required to run the experiment. Arithmetic will do.

Take one ton of coal and price it yourself. Price the lungs that filled with dust to bring it up. Price the creek that runs orange below the mine and will run orange for a century. Price the community that absorbed the loss when the seams played out and the company left. Price what the burning forecloses for every generation that follows: a resource spent once, in one way, forever. Price the families dispersed to cities that owed them nothing. Price the knowledge that died in the dispersal, the kind that took a hundred years of staying put to accumulate. Price the political effort spent keeping all of the above off the books, decade after decade, by people paid well to keep it off. Price the speed: hundreds of millions of years of geology moved through one county in a few working lifetimes.

Add the items. Set the sum beside the price the coal actually sold for. Look at the distance between the two numbers.

---

The exercise runs once, on a single ton of coal from the county this book has followed since its early chapters, so that the gap can be seen whole: unaggregated, undissolved in policy language, priced item by item.

Some ground rules, stated once. This is an accounting exercise. It is not a degrowth argument, not an argument against capitalism, not a policy recommendation. What a society does with an honest ledger, what gets taxed or compensated or rebuilt, is political work, and the framework behind the ledger does not legislate it. That work is deliberately left to the people whose work it is.

The eight costs priced below are the ones this extraction surfaces. A different extraction surfaces a different set. The method runs anywhere; the components belong to the case. And universal does not mean indiscriminate: consumption that stays within what a commons can restore severs nothing, and the method prices it at zero. A forest cut more slowly than it regrows carries no severed cost. Coal is not that case.

A cost enters this ledger only by passing four plain tests. It has to be surfaced by the inversion, which asks not what the buyer paid but what the place gave up. It has to come in units consistent enough to add. It has to be bounded, so the number stays finite. And it has to be independent of the other entries, so nothing is counted twice. None of this is exotic. It is what any honest auditor would demand.

---

McDowell County, West Virginia, again. The county John F. Kennedy visited as a candidate in 1960. The county that out-produced every other coal county in the nation while its residents queued for government surplus food. In 1960 a ton of its coal sold at the mine mouth for $4.71, just under five dollars; the EIA's historical price series carries the figure.

As far as the market was concerned, the accounting was complete the moment the rail car cleared the last tunnel. A buyer paid. A seller was paid. The transaction cleared and the coal was gone. Everything the market needed to know about that ton was contained in one number, and the number was final.

What the invoice left out was not a rounding error. It was most of the cost.

The measurement chapter built the instruments for saying that with discipline: two independent ways of pricing what was lost, one from the cost of substituting for what the commons provided, one from the value of the options the taking foreclosed, and the two land in the same range. Beneath both runs a floor set by what polities have actually paid back when they chose to pay. Here those instruments run on a single ton, under one rule applied throughout: take the low end of every range, omit every contested item, and accept the result as a floor. The point of a floor is that a skeptic can stand on it. Even the floor sits far above the cleared price.

---

Begin with the lungs.

Coal workers' pneumoconiosis is the most completely documented externality coal has produced: federally tracked, specifically attributable, and, when the responsible companies dissolve in bankruptcy, shifted onto the public ledger, where taxpayers have carried it since. An earlier chapter walked the federal Black Lung Benefits Program and its trust fund in detail. One number from that walk matters here: allocated against the national tonnage mined since the program's establishment in 1969, the program's costs come to between one and one and a half dollars per ton. The cumulative benefits paid ran to $44 billion through 2009 (GAO and Congressional Research Service figures), and the trust fund itself carried $5.1 billion of debt as of September 2024 (Labor Department and GAO), a separate number from the cumulative total and a measure of how much liability the bankruptcies left behind. The disease takes 8 to 13 years of potential life per decedent.

The program prices only the disease the law could not look away from. Around it sit the costs the law never reached: excess cardiovascular and respiratory mortality in mining counties, documented by Epstein and colleagues (2011) and by Hendryx and Ahern (2009); elevated respiratory illness in children who never entered a mine; cancers clustered near processing facilities; the medical weight of populations whose life expectancy diverges from the national average by more than a decade. For men in McDowell County, the gap has run to 13 years.

Priced conservatively on those anchors, direct health runs between $2 and $4 a ton. The band is the framework's own allocation; no outside body has priced it, and the named studies above are its ground. The walk takes the low end: $2 a ton. Even at that floor, the single most fully documented cost coal has ever produced returns, by itself, nearly half of what the coal sold for in 1960. At the top of its band it returns most of it.

---

The creek below the mine runs orange. The color is chemistry, not metaphor. Exposed sulfide minerals oxidize in air and water; the oxidation produces sulfuric acid; the acid mobilizes heavy metals into the watershed. Once started, the process runs for a century or more, and treatment does not end when the mine closes. It is maintenance in perpetuity, on land that no longer produces anything to pay for it.

The law meant to prevent this, the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977, requires operators to post bonds against cleanup. Across Appalachia the posted bonds run about $3.8 billion against cleanup estimated at $7.5 to 9.8 billion, a gap of roughly $4 to 6 billion (Appalachian Voices, *Repairing the Damage*, 2021). Roughly 633,000 acres of mined land in Appalachia await cleanup; the federal inventory runs close to a million acres nationwide (OSMRE's e-AMLIS database). The bonds were the price of a promise. The gap measures how much of the promise was kept.

The damage is not confined to the water. Valley fills buried headwater streams outright. Soils downstream of processing facilities carry contamination that outlasts the facilities. Mercury from coal combustion has been traced into fish as far away as the North Atlantic (Lee and colleagues, 2016). And in communities near mountaintop-removal operations, Hendryx and colleagues reported approximately doubled odds of cancer, an odds ratio of 2.03, in a 2012 self-reported survey published in the *Journal of Community Health*.

Priced on those anchors, environmental degradation runs $1 to 3 a ton, again as the framework's own conservative allocation. The walk takes $1. Two components in, the severed cost runs $3 a ton at the floor of its bands and $7 at the top: already the neighborhood of the entire 1960 price, with the climate not yet counted.

---

The house in the hollow cannot be sold. There is no buyer. That single fact, multiplied across a county, is what community disruption means, and it has a number.

In 1950 the county held 98,887 people. By 2020 it held just over nineteen thousand. Mechanization cut the mining workforce by more than half within a decade of Kennedy's visit; U.S. Steel wound down its McDowell operations across the 1980s; and the people who could leave left, in waves, taking the county's working future with them. What stayed behind is in the federal statistics, date by date. In 2022 the county ranked sixteenth-poorest in the United States, with a median household income of $28,235 against a national median near $67,521, and a poverty rate of 37.6 percent (Census SAIPE and ACS). In 2015 its drug-induced death rate was 141 per 100,000 residents against a national rate near 16 (CDC mortality data); the county figure is a crude rate and the national one age-adjusted, a technical difference that does not begin to close the gap.

A county that falls to a fifth of its population still has to carry itself. Roads, water lines, schools, a courthouse: infrastructure built for a hundred thousand is maintained for nineteen thousand, and the per-capita burden rises as the headcount falls. Every dollar that once multiplied through local commerce stopped multiplying. The public programs that hold the county together are paid for by state and federal taxpayers, in a place that generated billions of dollars of coal revenue that did not stay.

The framework allocates community disruption at $5 to 15 a ton. No external body has priced regional collapse per ton of coal; the band is a conservative estimate owned by the framework itself, resting on the named series above: the Census collapse, the income and poverty figures, the mortality rate. <!-- structure-note: the $5-15 community band and the $1-5 mobility band below are framework allocations on named anchors (Path A); re-anchor if an external regional-decline costing source is later commissioned. --> The walk takes $5.

Three components in, the running total stands near $8 a ton, almost twice what the coal sold for in 1960. Carbon has not yet been priced.

---

The rail car that cleared the last tunnel carried more than coal. It carried heat. The Pocahontas seam runs about 28 million Btu per short ton in the USGS and EIA coal-quality data, among the highest of any American coal. Burned, at the EPA's AP-42 emission factor for bituminous combustion, 93.28 kilograms of CO2 per million Btu, one ton of this coal releases approximately 2.61 metric tons of carbon dioxide. The measurement chapter introduced the framework's national default, 2.32 metric tons per short ton, the national bituminous average; 2.61 is the McDowell-specific calibration of that figure, and the seam's own documented heat content, not the national average, is the honest input here. A reviewer who substitutes the national factor gets a national-basis carbon term near $441 and has priced some other county's coal.

The EPA's 2023 central estimate of the social cost of carbon is $190 per metric ton. Multiply: 2.61 times $190 is $496. Just under five hundred dollars, from a single component.

That one term, before any of the other seven, exceeds every price a ton of this coal has ever sold for, by a factor running from roughly three against today's market peak to more than a hundred against the 1960 mine mouth. Today's coal brings $40 to 140 a ton depending on grade. The 1960 mine mouth paid $4.71.

And the carbon number, large as it is, does not capture everything the burning forecloses. A ton burned for electricity was a ton made permanently unavailable for the metallurgical uses that still have no full substitute, for chemical feedstocks, for applications no one has imagined yet. Electricity can now be generated without combustion. The coal is burned anyway, drawing down a finite stock for the one use that no longer requires it. For coal burned in any era after viable renewable substitutes existed, the foreclosure stopped being hypothetical.

The term can also be zero, and it matters that the model can say so. Where a perfect substitute exists, nothing is foreclosed, and the honest foreclosure cost of using the resource is nothing. And where use stays within what a commons can restore, harvest inside the rate of regeneration, nothing is severed; the cost is zero inside that line. The number here is enormous because neither condition held. The coal is gone when burned, and for most of the century of its extraction there was no substitute for burning it.

Four components in, the conservative floor stands at $504 to 518 a ton: $496 of carbon, plus the $8 to 22 the first three components span across their ranges.

---

A county that loses four-fifths of its people does not lose them evenly, and the cost of the losing does not stay with the men who mined.

The children who stayed grew up in a food desert, the nearest grocery store an hour away, in school systems with some of the lowest graduation rates in the country. The children who left were raised by parents whose work was destructive enough that many did not live to see grandchildren. And the ordinary engine of family wealth never ran: a family whose entire asset base is a house in a hollow that cannot be sold has nothing to transmit. The data carries this. The Opportunity Atlas, built by Raj Chetty and his colleagues from anonymized census and tax records, places the county at the floor of national outcomes: a child born there into the bottom fifth of household incomes has among the lowest odds in the United States of ever reaching the top fifth. The dispersal priced the children, and the price was the shape of their lives.

There is a tradition that did this accounting long before any spreadsheet did, and it should be credited by name.

Paul Laurence Dunbar named the strategy in 1896: "We wear the mask that grins and lies, / It hides our cheeks and shades our eyes." The mask, in Dunbar's poem, is a debt paid to human guile: the face survival requires a person to present while the cost runs on underneath. The miner who has been told the dust is better now, and who half believes it, and who goes back down anyway, wears the same mask. What this ledger adds is the observation that the mask is not the wearer's alone. It belongs to the system that produces the disease at industrial scale and asks the wearer to grin while it does.

W. E. B. Du Bois named the condition beneath the strategy in 1903, in *The Souls of Black Folk*: the veil, and the doubled consciousness of always seeing yourself through the eyes of a world that looks on in contempt. Frantz Fanon carried the analysis into the colonial and economic register in *Black Skin, White Masks* (1952), the register closest to the extraction communities in these pages: the mask as a structure imposed by an economy that profits from what it conceals. And Ralph Ellison, in *Invisible Man* (1952), gave the condition its long narrative form: a man seen by everyone and witnessed by no one.

The move this book makes, pricing what hides behind the mask, is that tradition's move. Black American writing carried it for over a century. What the framework adds is narrow: the accounting by which the mask stays structurally necessary, the ledger that counts what the tradition described and names to whom the bill was sent.

My own path to the move ran through information security, where masking is a term of art: hiding a true value behind a presented one. When I began pricing what extraction hides, I reached for the word out of professional habit. The habit was late to the work. Black American writing saw the human cost of the hidden ledger long before I had a spreadsheet for it, and the credit for the move belongs to that tradition, not to the trade that taught me its name.

The component needs a number, and honesty about the number is part of the discipline. The framework allocates lineage labor at $1 to 5 a ton, resting on the mobility data above; the walk prices it at $2. Next to the carbon term, $2 is almost nothing. Next to the life of a child whose income possibilities were fixed by the zip code of birth, it is enormous. Multiply it across sixty years of extraction and decide for yourself whether it is anything like a fair accounting.

---

The retired miner who knew exactly how the West Fork ran in February did not move somewhere that needed to know it.

That sentence is the whole component in miniature. Every mining community carried a working inventory of practical knowledge: how to read the seams, how to manage risk on one specific geology, how to keep a particular valley's water system working, how the weather behaved in one hollow across a hundred years of paying attention. The knowledge existed because specific people stayed in specific places long enough to accumulate it, and it had value exactly there. When the community dispersed, the knowledge went where the people went, to cities and suburbs that had no use for it, and it ended with them.

The cultural ledger thinned the same way. Congregations, civic organizations, mutual-aid networks: the institutions that could absorb a family's crisis because everyone knew everyone took generations to build and dissolved in one. The replacement cost of the civic infrastructure of a community of a hundred thousand people is not a number anyone can state with precision. It is certainly not zero.

The walk prices knowledge and cultural loss at $1 a ton. It enters the ledger because the inversion surfaces it, and a careful accounting cannot omit a cost merely because no one ever denominated it in dollars.

---

None of the costs above were hidden by lack of information.

Black lung was identifiable as an occupational disease by the early twentieth century. Federal compensation arrived in 1969, and it arrived then only because miners forced it: a strike in the shadow of the 1968 Farmington disaster, with the West Virginia Black Lung Association carrying the demand. MSHA's institutional history and Alan Derickson's 1998 study document the sequence. For the decades in between, the industry whose invoices omitted the disease had a financial incentive to keep it unpriced, and did, through documented lobbying, regulatory capture, and the suppression of research. The mine workers' union under its mid-century leadership reportedly declined at several points to press the issue, on the grounds that pressing it would interfere with the mechanization that was raising wages. The same mechanization was producing the disease.

The pattern did not retire with the company doctors. Consider the social cost of carbon, the $190 doing the heaviest work in the arithmetic above. Under one administration, the interagency working group's technical support documents set it near $42 a ton on a global-scope calculation. The next administration cut it to between $1 and $7 by counting only damages inside the United States, a scope change that academic commentary (Wagner, Anthoff, Cropper, and colleagues, 2021) flagged as ungrounded in standard cost-benefit practice. The administration after that restored the global scope at roughly $51, and the EPA's 2023 update raised the central estimate to $190. The physics of carbon did not change across those administrations. What changed was the answer to a political question, how much of the cost would be allowed onto the books, and that is a fight in which one side has more resources than the other.

The fight itself is a severed cost. Lobbying expenditure, captured agencies, funded research built to manufacture doubt, the civic attention of whole regions diverted into arguments designed for the diverting: all of it is real expenditure aimed at keeping the ledger incomplete. Oil and gas lobbying alone ran roughly $124 to 150 million in the 2024 cycle, with the broader energy sector near $435 million (OpenSecrets).

The vocabulary for naming who benefits and who organizes comes from the reparations economists and essayists: Ta-Nehisi Coates, William Darity and Kirsten Mullen, Darrick Hamilton, Dalton Conley. The extractor's own reasoning was mapped a half-century ago by the public-choice school, James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock, whose rent-seeking vocabulary this book borrows without endorsing the school's broader political project. The next chapter develops that relationship at length.

The walk prices political capture at $1 a ton: a deliberately low floor, chosen to keep the total conservative, and almost certainly an undercount.

---

The coal sat in the ground for hundreds of millions of years. Most of it left the county within a single human lifetime.

The compression was a choice, though no one experienced it as one. Machinery raised the rate at which the seams could be emptied, the industry's financial structure rewarded speed over measure, and the bulk of the county's coal moved in the few decades after mechanization, not because the country needed coal at that rate but because the rate had become possible and profitable. The component this surfaces is the cost of when: the value of the slower path not taken, time to develop alternatives, time for communities to diversify, time for the externality tail to be absorbed at a survivable rate instead of all at once.

The slower path was available. When the oil shocks of the 1970s raised energy prices, the energy consumed per dollar of real gross domestic product in the United States fell steadily from 1973 through the 1990s; the EIA's energy-intensity series carries the whole curve. Prices signaled that efficiency mattered, and the economy adapted within two decades. Had coal carried anything near its honest cost in 1960, the same signal would have arrived a generation earlier, at a pace a county could survive. Instead the suppressed price killed the signal, the timeline compressed, and the adaptation waited until the damage forced it.

The walk prices temporal displacement at $2 a ton.

---

The ledger, then, for one ton of McDowell County coal:

- Direct health: $2
- Environmental degradation: $1
- Community disruption: $5
- Foreclosure (the carbon term): $496
- Lineage labor: $2
- Knowledge and culture: $1
- Political capture: $1
- Temporal displacement: $2

**Total: $510 a ton.**

Every entry is the low end of its range. Every contested item was omitted. The progression ran $8 across the first three components, $504 to 518 when carbon entered, $510 in full.

Set the total against the prices. In 1960 the county was paid $4.71 a ton, just under five dollars. Today coal brings $40 to 140 a ton depending on grade. Against the 1960 invoice, the honest total runs more than a hundred times what the county was paid; that is the damage lens, the carbon arithmetic set against the era's price. The framework's most conservative lens prices the gap differently: the premium of what was foreclosed over what the market paid, a ratio in which the era's price cancels out, and it still returns roughly 8.5 to 26 times, centering near 15. The appendix's full multi-generational integral returns roughly 61 to 115 times against the same prices; the Technical Appendix carries the decomposition. And the carbon term alone exceeds even today's prices by a factor of at least three.

However it is priced, the structural finding holds across every era: the honest cost of a ton of this coal has always exceeded what the market paid for it. Against the 1960 invoice, on the damage lens, the distance is roughly two orders of magnitude.

---

McDowell is not a special case. The same shape returns wherever the method runs.

In the Gulf of Mexico, the documented costs of the Deepwater Horizon blowout, the penalties, settlements, and restoration spending, run roughly 15 to 16 times the revenue the Macondo well was projected to earn across its life. The framework adds a layer the documented accounting omits. The reservoir was projected to produce 40 to 60 million barrels over its lifetime; that is the production estimate, not the spill, which the court found at about 3.19 million barrels released. Burned, that lifetime production would release roughly 17 to 26 million tons of CO2, an additional $3 to 5 billion of carbon tail at the $190 anchor. The addition barely moves the multiple, because the documented costs already exceed revenue by an order of magnitude. The consistency is what matters: the method that prices coal's combustion tail prices oil's the same way.

In Libby, Montana, the W. R. Grace vermiculite mine left a town of a few thousand people with asbestos disease the town is still dying from. Priced on the damage lens, the costs run roughly 65 to 100 times the mine's lifetime revenue.

Outside Baotou, in Inner Mongolia, the tailings lake left by rare-earth processing sits beside villages whose environmental and health damage is documented but whose full accounting is not. The inputs are too incomplete for an honest multiple, and the method declines to compute one. The documented remediation estimates alone run $5 to 15 billion, against processing margins that never carried any of it. The shape is the same shape.

The Exxon Valdez put eleven million gallons of crude into Prince William Sound, and the gap between the damage and the market value of the spilled oil defies simple calculation, mostly because the recovery was never completed.

The structure also scales past what is usually called extraction.

Run the component arithmetic against a rent-burdened household and the entries populate themselves. About half of American renters now pay more than 30 percent of their income for housing (Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies, *America's Rental Housing*, 2024). A household that cannot save cannot move toward better work: a foreclosure-of-mobility cost. Children raised at the margin inherit the margin: a lineage cost. The zoning and tenant-law architecture that holds the arrangement in place is built and maintained by the parties it benefits: a political-capture cost. Value flows to absentee asset owners; the costs accumulate at the household scale, where they compound the way McDowell's did, quietly. The arithmetic differs from a coal county's. The structure is identical. One objection deserves naming now rather than later: there is a serious supply-side argument that housing costs are driven by zoning restrictions on what can be built, not by what owners capture. The objection is not weak, and it gets a full hearing and a full answer in the next chapter.

In northern Alberta, bitumen is priced against heavy-crude benchmarks while the ecological costs fall on boreal forest and peatland, the carbon tail follows the barrels wherever they burn, and the treaty costs fall on the Athabasca Chipewyan, Mikisew Cree, and Fort McKay First Nations, whose territories under Treaty 8 of 1899 host the operations. Downstream communities have reported elevated cancer rates and contamination; the documenting studies (O'Connor's 2006 reports, the Alberta Cancer Board's 2009 review, the Mikisew Cree community study) remain contested, and causation there is alleged rather than settled. Run the framework on the case and it returns the same order-of-magnitude gap coal returns.

And in 2008 the structure surfaced at the scale of an entire economy. The housing collapse destroyed roughly $11 trillion in American household wealth (Federal Reserve Financial Accounts) and produced roughly 4.1 million completed foreclosures through 2012 (CoreLogic; filings ran far higher). The federal response deployed roughly $700 billion in direct authorization plus trillions more in liquidity and guarantees, nearly all of it aimed at recapitalizing bank balance sheets rather than restructuring household debt, a scope choice contested at the time and documented since (Mian and Sufi, *House of Debt*, 2014). What the alternative architecture would have looked like under honest pricing is a question the next chapter returns to.

Different industries, different geographies, different decades, different resources. The same mechanism, producing the same gap between what the invoice said and what the extraction cost. The accounting system was built to track one side of the ledger, and it has tracked that side faithfully.

---

Three misreadings need answering, because each is close enough to the truth to be tempting.

The first: that honest pricing means the extraction should never have happened. The arithmetic claims nothing of the kind. Cheap energy, even severely mispriced energy, lifted billions of people out of poverty, and no responsible account of the twentieth century gets to pretend otherwise. The claim is narrower. Whoever bore the cost should have been compensated at rates reflecting what they actually sacrificed. The miners, the collapsed communities, the children born into a foreclosed county, the generations absorbing the climate cost: these are real people who paid and are still paying. The question the arithmetic raises is who paid, and whether anyone asked them.

The second: that honest pricing means the market was fake. It was not. Prices cleared transactions, money changed hands, real coal left on real rail cars. The price was not false. It was incomplete. It cleared the transaction the market could see, while alongside that transaction ran a second one, unpriced, unwitnessed, binding on parties who were never consulted. The severed costs were that second transaction.

The third: that honest pricing is a ladder pulled up by nations that industrialized on cheap carbon against nations still climbing. The objection deserves full weight, because if it held it would be a moral defeater. The answer is structural. Whoever created the historical cost owes its repayment. The developed world built its wealth by severing costs onto the global commons for two centuries; honest pricing creates the revenue that funds clean-industrialization pathways for the nations that did not, as repayment of historical severance rather than charity. Solar electricity is now cheaper than coal across most of the developing world. An asymmetric transition timeline, slower where the climb is steeper, follows directly from the accounting itself.

---

Scale the finding once, briefly, with the limits stated.

The premise is the conservative one. The most cautious lens the framework runs, the foreclosure premium that does not depend on any era's price, still returns a multiple in the low double digits, roughly fifteenfold at center; the carbon term alone runs more than a hundredfold against the 1960 invoice; and the same shape returned in every case above. The global fossil-fuel industry turns over roughly $4 to 5 trillion in revenue a year (industry-revenue estimates of the IEA and BP Statistical Review class). If full-cost pricing would multiply that revenue by two to three, a conservative factor given the carbon term alone, the annual severed cost of fossil extraction is on the order of $8 to 10 trillion. Add the non-fossil forms, mining, logging, fisheries harvested above their regeneration rate, aquifer drawdown, topsoil loss, and the global figure lands somewhere between $10 and 15 trillion a year.

At human scale: larger than every government subsidy program on Earth combined, larger than all international development assistance, larger than all charitable giving on the planet combined. As far as I can tell, it is the largest continuous wealth transfer in human history, and it flows in one direction: from the communities closest to extraction to the consumers farthest from it, from the future to the present, from the people with no voice in the market to the people with one.

The figure is a gesture and should be read as one: a single line of supporting arithmetic standing under an assertion that, taken seriously as a policy agenda, would require years of sectoral simulation and implementation design that no book's closing pages can honestly compress.

What the figure does not argue is degrowth. GDP measures market transactions, not welfare. Cleanup spending counts as production. Medical bills count as production. Addiction treatment counts as production. GDP does not fall when people die young. Honest pricing would reduce GDP in the narrow technical sense while increasing welfare in nearly every sense the measurement was built to proxy, shifting the composition of economic activity rather than its total. There is precedent at smaller scale: when leaded gasoline was banned, refineries reformulated, the industry did not collapse, and children's measured blood lead fell while their cognitive outcomes improved; the EPA's phaseout record and the CDC's NHANES blood-lead series carry the whole arc. Honest pricing repeats that pattern, larger.

The number is named. I will leave it there rather than pretend it can be compressed into a program.

---

Repricing on that scale raises questions that do not go away by being deferred. What happens to energy costs in the first years, in the middle years, at the new equilibrium. Who absorbs the transition. What happens to a household in the bottom 40 percent of incomes when essentials rise sharply. Where the pricing surplus goes, and who decides. Whether the economy on the far side is still recognizably capitalist. The questions are real, and they have answers: the mathematics underneath this ledger, the simulations built on it, and the structural necessity of a universal floor produce specific ones. The answers are outside this book's scope. They belong to the policy-makers, scholars, communities, and coalitions who will do the work, and what an honest accounting at civilizational scale would hand them is not a catastrophe but a more stable, more resilient economy with a floor beneath it.

What remains is the worked example. A ton of coal from McDowell County, priced honestly, carries a floor around $510. The invoice said just under five dollars. The distance between the two numbers is the severed cost. The invoice is what the county was paid. The severed cost is what the county bore, and part of it is still being borne, in the lungs this accounting began with.

The next chapter sketches the direction a civilization committed to honest accounting would move. The final one returns to the miner, the waterman, and the others, to what they might recognize in one another across the distance the mechanism was built to maintain.
