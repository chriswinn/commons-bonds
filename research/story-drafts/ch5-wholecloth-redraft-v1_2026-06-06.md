# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 5: The Accountability Gap

Once you have seen the pattern, it is difficult to stop seeing it. A company or an institution draws value out of a resource, a community, or a system. The costs that the extraction generates are cut loose from whoever collected the value and set down on someone else, almost always someone with less power, less mobility, less room to refuse. And when the bill finally arrives, the party that took the value has generally moved beyond reach.

The bill always arrives. The open question is only who is still standing where it lands.

This chapter follows that pattern through a series of cases that share little on the surface. They sit in different countries, run on different industries, span different decades. A vermiculite mine in Montana. An oil rig in the Gulf of Mexico. A tanker in an Alaskan sound. A processing zone in Inner Mongolia. The American financial system in the autumn of 2008. The retirement promise that Americans treat as the closest thing the country has to a covenant between the generations. The healthcare system that now sends its shortfalls through online fundraising pages and the unpaid hours of family members. The numbers are not alike. What runs underneath them is.

---

### Libby, Montana

The W.R. Grace Company ran a vermiculite mine outside Libby, Montana, for twenty-seven years, from 1963 to 1990. At its height the mine supplied roughly eighty percent of the world's vermiculite, a mineral used in insulation and garden soil. The operation was profitable, generating an estimated one hundred million dollars in revenue over its life. Grace sold the product hard. Its Zonolite insulation ended up in the walls of an estimated thirty-five million American homes.

What Grace did not advertise, and worked to keep quiet, was that the Libby vermiculite was laced with an unusually lethal form of asbestos.

The company knew. Internal documents released in later litigation showed that Grace had identified the contamination as early as 1963 and had been measuring asbestos exposure among its workers for years. It knew its workers were falling ill at rates no ordinary explanation could account for. It knew that family members were breathing in fibers carried home on work clothes. It knew that children playing near the waste piles were inhaling something that would kill some of them decades later. The mine stayed open for twenty-seven years anyway.

The toll is still being counted. More than four hundred residents of Libby have died of asbestos-related disease. An estimated three thousand have been diagnosed with lung abnormalities consistent with exposure. Death rates from asbestosis in the town ran many times the national average. The Environmental Protection Agency declared the entire town a public health emergency, the first time it had ever issued such a declaration for a disaster made by human hands.

The financial side tells the same story in figures that are hard to argue with. Grace's mine generated something like one hundred million dollars across its operating life. The documented costs, counting medical care for the sick and the dying, the cleanup of the contaminated town, the legal settlements, the lost property values, and the open-ended health monitoring, run past four billion dollars. For every dollar Grace took out, Libby and the people in it absorbed roughly forty dollars in costs that Grace never paid.

W.R. Grace filed for bankruptcy in 2001, eleven years after the mine closed. Its assets were reorganized. Its shareholders and executives went on with their lives. Libby is still there, still burying its dead.

The law's attempt to respond is itself instructive. In 2005 the Department of Justice secured a federal indictment of W.R. Grace and seven of its senior executives, charging conspiracy to violate the Clean Air Act and to obstruct the federal investigation. It was the largest environmental criminal prosecution in the country's history to that point, and it named individual executives by name and role. In 2009, after a trial that ran thirteen weeks, every defendant was acquitted.

The acquittal does not mean the contamination never happened, or that the executives did not know, or that the costs above were not real. It means the criminal law was not built for this kind of case. Criminal law prosecutes specific acts, specific knowledge of specific harm at specific moments. Cost severance works through accumulated decisions stretched across decades, and the individual acts inside it are hard to isolate to the standard a jury needs. The four hundred dead, the three thousand with damaged lungs, the town under emergency declaration: these are the record. The acquittal is evidence that the existing legal machinery was not designed to reach the thing the record describes.

### Deepwater Horizon

On the twentieth of April, 2010, the Deepwater Horizon drilling platform exploded in the Gulf of Mexico, killing eleven workers and releasing an estimated 4.9 million barrels of oil into the water. The well ran for eighty-seven days while British Petroleum, the operator, tried to cap a hole drilled through geology that BP's own engineers had described in internal correspondence as a nightmare.

BP had taken a great deal of value out of the Gulf. In the five years before the blowout, its Gulf operations had generated roughly sixty billion dollars in revenue. The Macondo well itself, the one that failed, was projected to produce forty to sixty million barrels over its life, worth somewhere between three and four billion dollars at 2010 prices.

The spill scattered costs in every direction value can be scattered. The oil that had earned money for a British company, refined in Texas, sold across North America, fouled the waters where Louisiana fishing families had worked for generations. The damage to Gulf ecosystems outlasted the quarters in which BP's accountants had already booked the revenue. Dolphins, sea turtles, and fish populations took on toxins that would shape their reproduction and survival for years.

The dollars alone make the point. BP eventually paid about twenty-one billion in federal and state fines and settlements, the largest environmental penalty in United States history, and roughly another twenty billion into a fund for affected businesses and individuals. Its total documented payout came to something near sixty-five billion dollars.

Even that understates the harm. The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration estimated that the spill killed billions of oysters and trillions of newly hatched fish, along with tens of thousands of birds and turtles that would otherwise have fed the Gulf's fisheries for years. Tourism losses in the affected region ran past seven billion dollars in the three years after the spill. Coastal property values fell by an estimated twelve billion. Pulling together academic, insurance-industry, and federal estimates, total economic losses came to more than one hundred and fifty billion dollars.

Set BP's roughly sixty-five billion in payments against that one hundred and fifty billion in losses and the company covered well under half. It captured the full value of the oil it pumped and paid less than half the cost of pumping it. The honest target is full recovery, the extractor paying the entire residual cost. BP paid a minority of it, and that figure is not an activist's estimate. It is the court-validated, settlement-ratified record of one of the largest environmental disasters the country has seen.

The asymmetry ran across borders as well. The Macondo oil reached Mexican waters, fouling Yucatán coastlines, fisheries, and tourism. BP's 2018 settlement with the Mexican federal government came to roughly twenty-five million dollars, a tiny fraction of the United States settlements for comparable damage per mile of shoreline. The oil did not distribute itself unevenly; it landed fairly evenly across the affected coasts. What differed was the legal architecture available to each set of claimants. American Gulf-state litigation ran under the Oil Pollution Act of 1990, decades of state environmental and tort law, and a class-action system that let individual claimants band together at scale. Mexican claimants had far weaker tools for pressing a cross-border environmental claim against a foreign-domiciled corporation. The same spill and the same operator produced very different bills, because the bills were shaped by the courts that could be reached.

And all of this leaves out the costs that no settlement could price: the knowledge lost when family fishing operations folded, the rupture in communities that had lived from the Gulf for generations and could no longer trust their own water, the ecological relationships that came apart when keystone populations collapsed. Those costs were real, and real people carried them. They simply never appeared on any ledger BP was required to balance.

### Prince William Sound

Twenty-one years before Macondo, on the twenty-fourth of March, 1989, the tanker Exxon Valdez ran aground on Bligh Reef in Prince William Sound, Alaska, and spilled about eleven million gallons of crude into some of the richest coastal water in North America. The oil coated more than a thousand miles of shoreline. It killed hundreds of thousands of seabirds, thousands of sea otters, and an unknown number of the salmon and herring that the Sound's fishing communities and the local Alaska Native villages depended on.

A jury found Exxon's conduct reckless and, in 1994, returned a punitive-damages verdict of five billion dollars. What followed is the part worth holding onto. Exxon litigated the award for the better part of two decades, and in 2008 the Supreme Court, in *Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker*, cut it to roughly five hundred and seven million dollars, ruling that punitive damages under maritime law should not exceed the compensatory damages already paid. The penalty arrived a generation after the spill, reduced to about a tenth of what the jury had assigned, by which point many of the fishermen who had filed the original claims were dead.

The Sound did not recover on the schedule the settlement assumed either. The Pacific herring fishery, central to the local economy, collapsed a few years after the spill and had not returned decades later. The herring could not file a claim. The fishing families who had built their livelihoods around them watched the value drain out of the water while the legal process ground toward a number that no longer matched the loss.

### Baotou, Inner Mongolia

The city of Baotou, in China's Inner Mongolia region, produces something like sixty percent of the world's rare earth elements, the seventeen metals whose magnetic and catalytic properties sit inside nearly every piece of advanced electronics on the planet. The rare earths in a smartphone came from places like Baotou. So did the ones in the wind turbine that fed the grid this morning, and the ones in the electric car that represents the best current plan for cleaning up transportation.

Processing rare earths produces enormous volumes of toxic waste. Every ton of rare earth oxides yields roughly ten tons of wastewater carrying heavy metals, acids, and radioactive material. In Baotou that waste has collected over decades into artificial tailings lakes. Residents nearby report elevated rates of cancer, respiratory illness, and birth defects. Farmland within about ten kilometers of the processing facilities has gone out of use.

The firms operating in the area, a mix of Chinese state-owned enterprises and private companies, have pulled enormous value out of global demand. China's rare earth industry generates an estimated ten to twelve billion dollars in annual revenue, with Baotou accounting for roughly half. That value flows out through global supply chains to the electronics makers, carmakers, and clean-energy firms whose products depend on it.

The costs went in every direction at once. The toxic residue that makes Silicon Valley's hardware possible sits in lakes in Inner Mongolia. The engineers designing phones in California never see the tailings their products require, and the people buying the phones never see the villages. The companies buying the feedstock pay today's extraction price; the health and environmental costs will run for decades or centuries. The value reaches shareholders and consumers in wealthy economies, while the costs settle on rural communities with little political voice.

The numbers here are harder to pin down than in Montana or the Gulf, because the Chinese government restricts access to the region's health and environmental data. Even so, peer-reviewed work has appeared despite the constraints, documenting elevated concentrations of lead, cadmium, thorium, and uranium in soil, water, and the bodies of residents within ten kilometers of the tailings ponds, along with raised rates of cancer and reproductive harm consistent with that exposure. The published evidence is partial. So, in the other direction, is the unpublished evidence the restrictions keep out of view. But even cautious estimates put the long-term remediation cost of Baotou's rare earth legacy past one hundred billion dollars, and the health costs for affected populations, if those populations were cared for at the standard of the countries that consume their rare earths, would add tens of billions more.

Against roughly five to six billion dollars in annual value, the severed costs pile up faster than the industry has ever paid or set aside to pay. Globally, the rare earth industry spends less than one percent of its revenue on environmental cleanup or community health.

The gadgets work. The turbines turn. The cars charge. The costs accumulate in Inner Mongolia, where most of the people who benefit will never have to see them. And the person at the far end of the supply chain, the engineer with the phone, the driver with the electric car, the household running on wind-generated power, is not the villain of this account. The pattern does not require a villain. The consumer shares in the value the product delivers and is separated, by the length of the supply chain, from the families bearing the contamination. That distance is exactly what lets the arrangement keep running, whatever any individual buyer would prefer.

### 2008 and the Double Risk Transfer

The same pattern runs at the scale of an entire national economy, which is where it becomes hardest to look away from. When it strikes a single region, there is always a story ready to explain it away as something local. When it strikes the whole economy at once, no such story survives.

The setup began in 1978, when Congress created the 401(k). Over the next two decades, employers steadily replaced defined-benefit pensions with these individual accounts, and the change was sold to workers as freedom: now you own your retirement, rather than depending on a plan your employer manages. What went unsaid was whose freedom it mainly was. The freedom that moved was the employer's freedom from liability. Market risk, longevity risk, and the risk of one's own financial decisions all shifted from institutional balance sheets onto individual workers who had no way to pool or hedge against them. That first transfer ran quietly for thirty years.

The second transfer came fast, between roughly 2001 and 2008, when financial institutions built mortgage-backed securities and collateralized debt obligations whose complexity was not an accident. Only instruments too complex to evaluate could be sold to investors who could not evaluate them. Those instruments were packaged into mutual funds and target-date funds and sold inside the same 401(k) accounts that had already absorbed the first transfer. When they collapsed in 2008, the losses landed in those accounts. The workers who had been told in the 1980s that their retirement was now their own responsibility took the losses on instruments the financial sector had built, collected fees on, and was about to be rescued from.

The rescue came. The Troubled Asset Relief Program put seven hundred billion dollars of public money into stabilizing financial institutions. The Federal Reserve's emergency lending, whose full scope emerged only through later congressional requests and litigation, ran to more than sixteen trillion dollars in cumulative loans across the crisis, according to the Government Accountability Office's 2011 audit. Bonuses at rescued institutions were back within eighteen months. Over the same stretch, roughly ten million American households received foreclosure filings between 2008 and 2012, and about five million of those ended in completed foreclosure. No comparable federal effort was organized for them. The Home Affordable Modification Program reached a small share of those eligible and ran into administrative failures that several Government Accountability Office reports documented.

The economists Atif Mian and Amir Sufi traced where the damage actually concentrated, and their account, set out in *House of Debt*, is the load-bearing one here. American household net worth fell by roughly eleven trillion dollars in 2008, the largest one-year drop on record. That loss did not fall evenly. It fell hardest on the households with the least to lose, because of how debt works on a balance sheet. A family near the bottom of the wealth distribution held nearly all of its net worth in home equity and carried a high ratio of debt to assets. When national house prices fell by something like a fifth, a homeowner who had put ten percent down did not lose a fifth of their position. They lost a multiple of it, because the mortgage stayed fixed while the equity underneath it vanished. Mian and Sufi show poor homeowners' net worth collapsing from around thirty thousand dollars to nearly nothing between 2007 and 2010. Over the same window, the wealthiest households, who held diversified financial assets and carried little debt against them, were barely touched. The fall in asset prices sorted itself onto exactly the people least able to absorb it, and it widened inequality in the process.

The asymmetry is the fact everything else rests on. A system that could move trillions of dollars in days to rescue institutions could not assemble anything of comparable scale for the households whose retirement accounts had absorbed those institutions' losses and whose homes were foreclosed in the contraction that followed. The risk had been transferred twice over the same workers: once from employers through the pension-to-401(k) shift, and again from financial institutions through the securities machinery that filled those same accounts. The rescue restored one side of the ledger and left the other holding its losses. The Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission, set up by Congress to investigate, concluded in 2011 that the crisis resulted from human action and inaction, not from forces beyond anyone's control. The people who made those decisions received bonuses. The people whose homes and retirements absorbed the losses received very little.

A careful reader will push back, and the strongest objections deserve to be met head-on rather than waved past.

The first is that borrowers took loans they could not afford. The serious form of this is straightforward: many of the mortgages that defaulted went to borrowers whose income could not service the payments under any reasonable model, and the borrowers signed for them. The answer is not that those borrowers had no agency. It is that agency depends on information, and the information was built to mislead. Subprime origination ran on a documented playbook in which a broker's commission was tied to loan volume rather than to whether the loan would be repaid, in which the terms a borrower was told often differed from the terms in the contract, in which adjustable-rate products were marketed to people whom the industry itself expected not to understand how the rate would reset. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's later enforcement record documents the pattern at scale. The borrowers signed; the contracts were sold under conditions designed to maximize signing rather than loan quality. The agency was real. The information gap was the lever the system pulled, and it pulled it deliberately.

The second objection holds that 2008 was a global crisis, not a failure of American policy. The serious form names the European banks' exposure, the contagion through export-dependent Asian economies, the cross-border holdings of these securities, the role of the Basel capital rules in shaping bank reserves worldwide. All of that is real. But the global reach of the crisis amplifies the finding rather than dissolving it. The instruments that carried the contagion were overwhelmingly American-originated securities. The fee structure that rewarded origination volume, the conflict of interest in a rating system paid by the issuers it rated, and the regulatory exemptions that let these products scale were American policy choices. International transmission gave the crisis its global size. American origination gave it its instruments. A framework that explains 2008 has to account for both, and accounting for both is what makes the finding more general rather than more parochial.

The third objection notes that the rescue funds were repaid, so taxpayers did not lose money in the end. The serious form points to Treasury accounting showing that the program's direct outlays were substantially recovered through repayments and asset sales, with some accountings showing a small profit, and to the Government Accountability Office and Congressional Budget Office reviews that lay out the recovery in detail. The answer is that the rescue program is the wrong unit of measure. The cost this chapter is pricing is the unrecovered welfare loss to the ten million foreclosed households, to the workers whose retirement balances reset to a lower base and compounded forward from there, to the regional economies that contracted around cascading foreclosures, and to the public trust that absorbed the lopsided rescue as a lesson about whose security the system actually protects. None of those losses is recovered by repaying the rescue, and none of them appears on the federal balance-sheet accounts the repayment argument cites, because those accounts measure federal cash flow rather than welfare lost by the people who bore the costs. The right question is not whether the rescue cost the Treasury money. It is whether the rescue put the cost-bearing households back where they would have been without the crisis. By a wide margin, it did not.

The most reform-minded version of the objection points to the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010, which restructured a great deal of the regulatory architecture in response to the crisis. This is real and worth granting in full. Dodd-Frank created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, established the Financial Stability Oversight Council, restricted proprietary trading through the Volcker Rule, and imposed tougher capital and liquidity requirements on the largest institutions. None of that is nothing. The architecture changed in ways that can be measured, and the financial system carries accountability tools it did not have in 2007. The answer is that this is exactly right, and that it proves the point rather than dissolving it. Dodd-Frank is a partial installation of the accountability architecture this book describes, and Chapter 9 returns to it as one of the wedges by which the broader framework could be built. But a partial installation reveals the size of the remaining gap rather than closing it. The bureau's enforcement record sits alongside its weakening after 2017; the Volcker Rule was substantially relaxed in 2018 and 2019; the systemic-importance designations have been contested, narrowed, and in some cases reversed. A reform that holds for ten years and is then dismantled is, in this accounting, a delay rather than a correction. The claim was never that nothing has been done. The claim is that what has been done is small against the scale of the costs these cases document, and that it has not been locked in firmly enough for its survival to be taken for granted.

What survives all four objections is the finding the chapter has been building toward. The borrowers had agency, and the information was rigged against them. The crisis was global, and its instruments were American. The rescue was repaid, and the welfare losses on the other side were not. Dodd-Frank reformed the architecture, and the reform is partial, reversible, and not yet sufficient to close the gap.

One comparison is worth setting down before the section closes, because it shows that the American scope-choice in 2008 was a choice rather than a necessity. Sweden in the early 1930s faced banking-crisis pressure structurally similar to what the United States faced in 1929 through 1933, and again in 2008 and 2009. The Swedish response paired bank rescue with substantial relief for households. The 1931 decision to leave the gold standard and let the krona fall restored export competitiveness and eased the real burden of household debt. The bank rescue that followed was coupled with programs to refinance and modify distressed mortgages. Public-works employment in the mid-1930s put income directly back into household hands, and by the end of the decade the 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement had institutionalized the labor-and-welfare settlement that the whole response had been building toward. The economic historians who study banking crises, among them Charles Kindleberger and Barry Eichengreen, read the Swedish case as a demonstration that rescuing banks and relieving households are not an either-or; a society can decide to do both. The United States in 2008 and 2009, and again in 2020, decided otherwise. Chapter 9 develops the comparison at fuller length, alongside the wider set of cross-country and cross-century cases that show how much the response to a crisis varies with the choices a society makes rather than with the crisis itself.

The 2008 case shows the pattern at the scale of a national financial system. The pattern is not native to finance, and the chapter is not done documenting it. The cases that follow show the same mechanism in other settings. In Social Security it runs across the generations, with time as the distance between the people who took the value and the people who carry the cost. In the healthcare system it runs through medical institutions, family caregiving, and the decisions made at the end of a life. The architecture changes from case to case. The gap between who benefits and who pays does not.

### Social Security and the Cost That Was Moved Through Time

The largest example of this pattern in American life hides inside a system most people regard as its opposite, a system built to embody the promise that each generation will care for the one before it.

Social Security was designed in 1935 as a pay-as-you-go program. Current workers pay in through payroll deductions. Current retirees draw benefits funded by those contributions. For decades the system ran surpluses, taking in more from working-age Americans than it paid out to retirees. Those surpluses went into special-issue Treasury bonds and built up in the trust fund as a cushion against the day the baby boom generation would retire.

That day arrived. The boomers began reaching retirement age in 2008. By 2021, Social Security was paying out more in benefits than it collected in payroll taxes, the demographic turn that everyone had seen coming for forty years. The trust fund has been covering the difference by redeeming its bonds. Under current law and current projections, the combined trust funds are due to run dry in the mid-2030s. After that, payroll taxes alone would cover roughly three-quarters of promised benefits. The program would not vanish, but it would no longer deliver what it has promised to the people who planned their retirements around it.

The severed cost lives in the way the surplus years were handled. The contributions that came in during the surplus decades were held in special-issue Treasury obligations rather than invested as productive capital that compounds for the future. To get a feel for the scale of what that routing gave up, it helps to run a deliberately simplified counterfactual, and to run it honestly, because there is a dishonest version of this number in wide circulation.

The dishonest version goes like this: take every payroll-tax dollar contributed since 1937, imagine it had instead been invested in a broad stock-market index, compound it forward, and announce that the foregone return comes to something on the order of a hundred trillion dollars. That figure is built on a fund that paid no retiree, because it never subtracts the benefits that were actually paid out. It models a hundred-trillion-dollar fund only by pretending Social Security never did the one thing it exists to do. It is not a measure of anything real.

The honest version subtracts the benefits, because any alternative arrangement still had to support those same retirees. Over the life of the program, the contributions that came in and the benefits that went out are nearly equal, each in the neighborhood of twenty-six trillion dollars across nearly ninety years. The only money ever available to invest was the running surplus, the gap in the years when contributions ran ahead of benefits. That surplus is what actually built today's reserve of roughly two point seven trillion dollars, held at Treasury interest rates. Re-run those same annual surpluses at a disclosed real equity return instead of the Treasury rate and the counterfactual fund comes out larger than the real one, but in the range of a few trillion dollars, not a hundred. The foregone return is real, and on the most aggressive defensible assumptions it runs into the trillions. It does not run into the hundreds of trillions, and anyone who tells you it does is compounding the contributions without ever paying a benefit.

That smaller, honest number is still the same kind of cost the rest of this chapter has been tracing. Through the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, Congress expanded Social Security benefits, and the politicians who voted for those expansions collected the political value of them, the votes and the goodwill and the sense of having delivered security to their constituents. In nearly every case those politicians left office, by retirement or defeat or death, well before the demographic bills came due. The expansions were funded by the demographics of the moment, an unusually large working-age cohort relative to retirees, and the gap that the coming demographic turn would open was visible in every actuarial projection issued at the time. The value was taken in one era. The cost was set down in another, on people who were too young to vote or not yet born.

This invites two further objections, and both are worth meeting.

The first is a public-finance objection: holding the trust fund's reserves in Treasury bonds was prudent, not opportunistic, and the framework treats a sound administrative choice as if it were the mechanism of extraction. The serious form of this is correct on its own terms. Treasury bonds are the safest asset a United States sovereign fund can hold; they fail only if the federal government itself fails, which is the same condition under which any American retirement asset fails. The alternative, holding the reserves in equities or private assets, would have added market risk and the further problem of a federal entity holding controlling stakes in private firms, which most public-finance economists regard as worse than the bond-holding it would replace.

Each of those points is sound, and each answers a question the chapter is not asking. The cost being priced here does not sit in how the surpluses were invested. It sits in how the obligations were generated in the first place. Holding the surpluses in Treasuries during the surplus years was prudent. Generating obligations during those same years that the actuarial reports projected would come due in the deficit years was the move that severed the cost. The portfolio question and the obligation question sit at different levels, and a sound answer at the portfolio level does not touch the one the chapter raises.

The second objection is about timeline and tractability: the gap is real but modest and solvable through small adjustments at a comfortable horizon, and the framework inflates an ordinary policy problem into a civilizational one. The serious form is correct as far as it goes. The fund does not run dry tomorrow; it runs dry in the mid-2030s, more than half a century after the expansions that generated the obligations, and even then payroll taxes cover roughly three-quarters of scheduled benefits. The Trustees' reports lay out a finite menu of fixes: a payroll-tax increase of a point or two, lifting the cap on taxable wages, adjusting the benefit formula at the top, raising the eligibility age as longevity rises. Some combination of these closes the seventy-five-year gap with far less disruption than the alarmist framing suggests, and a series of bipartisan commissions has produced specific packages.

That objection is right that the gap is mathematically closeable. The answer is that closing it does not undo the severance; it completes it. Each of those fixes closes the gap at the expense of the cost-bearing party. Raising payroll taxes on the cohort that happens to be working when the bill comes due, or trimming benefits for retirees in that window, or raising the retirement age for people who have already paid in for forty years, transfers the obligation onto exactly the generation the original arrangement set it down on. It does not reach back to the cohorts who collected the political value of generating it, because those cohorts are no longer available. The gap is closeable, and closing it is what this pattern looks like when the time delay finally runs out. A solvable problem solved on the backs of the people who never took the value is not the absence of severance. It is severance arriving on schedule.

What survives both objections is the chapter's finding. The bond-holding was administratively defensible. The political machinery that generated the obligations the bonds were held against was not. The gap is mathematically closeable, and the fixes that close it move the obligation onto the cost-bearing generation rather than onto the value-taking one. The accountability gap sits upstream of the portfolio question and downstream of the timeline question, in the obligation-generating machinery that both of them take as given.

The architecture the United States chose in 1935, pay-as-you-go retirement security funded by current-worker deductions with surpluses held as Treasury obligations, was a choice rather than an inevitability. Chapter 4 describes Norway's different choice for a structurally similar problem: a sovereign fund that channels petroleum revenue into capital that compounds across the decades on behalf of future generations rather than running it down. The two designs address similar problems and produce very different outcomes, and the difference owes less to national wealth or demographic luck than to decisions made at particular moments. The chapter on Norway documents what was possible. This one documents what was, and continues to be, the cost the American choice produces.

### The Healthcare Commons

The pattern works in American healthcare with a particular cruelty, because it works on individual bodies during the most fragile stretches of a life, and because what it severs is not an abstract externality but the concrete financial ruin of particular households.

The most visible sign is a growth curve. Between 2011 and 2020, the annual number of medical fundraising campaigns on the platform GoFundMe grew roughly twenty-five-fold. By 2020 more than a third of all GoFundMe campaigns were for medical expenses. Between 2016 and 2020, some 438,000 such campaigns raised over two billion dollars from 21.7 million donations. Only about twelve percent reached their stated goals, and sixteen percent received no donations at all. GoFundMe's own chief executive has said the platform was never designed to function as healthcare financing and has become a default. A safety net that fails the great majority of the people who reach for it is not a safety net. It is a record-keeping system for a failure the formal system has stopped trying to prevent.

The failure does not land evenly. Medical campaigns run by people with distinctively African American or Hispanic surnames raise substantially less and are less likely to reach their goals, even after controlling for geography and timing. The replacement for a public safety net reproduces the very inequities the public net had been meant, however imperfectly, to soften. The formal system drops the cost, the informal backup catches it unequally by race, and the part it drops concentrates on the households with the least existing wealth and the thinnest networks to fall back on.

The pressure on households is not only a matter of healthcare costs rising in isolation. It is also a matter of wages that have not kept pace with those costs across the same decades. The economists Lawrence Mishel and Josh Bivens at the Economic Policy Institute have documented that the median American wage tracked productivity from the late 1940s into the early 1970s and then came apart from it: productivity kept rising while the median wage stagnated in real terms for the next half century. Over that same half century, healthcare costs climbed at multiples of general inflation. The squeeze on households is the space between those two lines, wages flat against the productivity those same workers produced, healthcare costs rising against those flat wages. Jacob Hacker named the wider pattern, the shift of risk and cost from institutions onto individuals, in *The Great Risk Shift* in 2006, and healthcare is one sharp instance of it. Fundraising from strangers becomes the last resort when the household that is supposed to absorb the cost cannot, with savings gone, wages flat, and debt rising.

At the other end of the arc of a life, in a case the journalist Katy Butler documented, Medicare reimburses a physician about fifty-four dollars for a long office visit to talk through treatment options, and about four hundred and sixty-one dollars, on top of a roughly twelve-thousand-dollar hospital fee, much of which goes to the device maker, for implanting a pacemaker. The liability incentives point the same way. Performing the procedure guards against a malpractice claim; declining it invites one. Butler's father lived five years in deep cognitive decline with the pacemaker still keeping his heart going, cared for by her mother, herself in her eighties, in something close to eighty-hour weeks of unpaid labor that no compensation and no ledger ever recorded. The device maker received roughly six thousand dollars. The conversation that might have prevented the procedure was worth fifty-four. Profit was collected by the cardiologist, the hospital, and the device maker. The cost was set down on the family as unpaid caregiving, on the patient as a prolonged decline, and on a woman in her eighties carrying labor that no formal system existed to count.

Butler's case is one instance of a systemic pattern, and Atul Gawande set out the wider structure in *Being Mortal* in 2014. American healthcare financing systematically over-treats dying patients, because the reimbursement structure rewards intervention at every decision point, and systematically under-funds palliative care, because palliative reimbursement is a fraction of intervention reimbursement and most American medical training includes little of it. The result is a population of dying patients receiving procedures they do not need, with the same distribution of value and cost that Butler's case shows, reproduced at scale. The structure does not require any individual physician or administrator to choose profit over compassion at any single moment. It produces the pattern by aggregating ordinary, rational responses to incentives, and the incentives are the policy choice. End-of-life care is the most legible instance of this, not the only one. Maternity care, mental health care, and the management of chronic disease reproduce the same shape at other points along the span of a life.

Among the wealthy nations, the United States is the only one whose healthcare financing produces the fundraising-as-default pattern at this scale. Chapter 9 returns to that cross-national comparison at greater length.

### The Pattern Made Visible

Six settings. Different industries, different countries, different decades, different kinds of extraction. Underneath them, one shape.

An actor, whether a company, a generation, or a political system, draws value out of a resource or a system that might have been managed for the long term instead. The extraction generates costs, in environmental damage, in human health, in financial obligations, in the foreclosure of future options. Those costs are cut loose from the actor who created them and set down on parties with little power to prevent the transfer: workers, communities, ecosystems, the people who come after.

When the costs finally materialize, when the workers fall ill, when the ecosystem fails, when the bills come due, the original beneficiary is shielded from the full reckoning by some combination of distance, elapsed time, an imbalance in what each side knew, an imbalance in who could refuse, the normalization of the cost as simply how business is done, and an appearance of plenty that keeps the cost from registering at all. What is left is carried by whoever cannot leave: the people who cannot move, the systems that cannot relocate, the generations that inherit the result.

In Libby, a Montana town carried what it could not move away from. In the Gulf and in Prince William Sound, ecosystems that could not relocate and communities tied to waters they could not abandon carried it. In Baotou, rural populations with little political voice carry it. In 2008, the ten million foreclosed households and the workers whose retirement accounts no rescue reached carried it. In Social Security, the generation that happens to be working when the obligation matures will carry it. In healthcare, the households whose fundraising pages fall short and the family members providing labor no ledger records carry it.

The companies file for bankruptcy. The politicians leave office. The generations that made the promises pass away. The costs stay behind, transferred to whoever cannot escape them.

This is not the system failing. It is the system working as it was built to work, not by anyone's conscious design but by the accumulated logic of structures that reward value extraction and push costs onto whoever has the least power to resist. The severance is not an accident or an unintended side effect. It is the mechanism by which extractive economies have always operated, and it is operating now.

The question is not whether this is happening; the cases settle that. The question is what it would take to build accountability into the structure so that costs are caught before they are severed, rather than chased after the damage is done and the beneficiaries have already gone.

### The Same Object, Named Honestly

There is a move this book performs in every chapter, and it is time to name it directly, because naming it answers an objection that a thoughtful reader has been holding since the first page.

The objection is Michael Sandel's. In *What Money Can't Buy*, Sandel argues that some goods are corrupted by being priced, that a society which puts everything up for sale slides, almost without noticing, from having a market economy to being a market society, and that there are things, a friendship, a vote, a human life, that lose their meaning the moment a price is attached. A reader who takes Sandel seriously will have grown uneasy watching this book put dollar figures on poisoned towns, foreclosed homes, and the labor of an eighty-year-old caring for her husband. To price these things, the worry goes, is to commit exactly the corruption Sandel warns against.

The answer runs through Karl Polanyi. In *The Great Transformation*, written in 1944, Polanyi observed that a market society depends on treating three things as commodities that were never produced to be sold: land, labor, and money. He called them fictitious commodities. Land is the natural world, which no one manufactured for the market. Labor is human activity, which is to say human life, which is not produced for sale. Money is a token of exchange, not a thing made to be traded as goods are. Treating these three as ordinary commodities, Polanyi argued, does violence to what they actually are, and the violence provokes a response. Societies push back, through labor law, through social insurance, through environmental protection, trying to re-embed the market inside the social relations and the natural limits it had been torn loose from. He called this push and counter-push the double movement: the drive to turn everything into a commodity, and the protective reaction against it.

This book sits on the second half of that movement. The pricing it performs is not an attempt to turn a poisoned town into a commodity. It is the protective reaction Polanyi described, carried out in the language the extractive side already speaks. The extractive side priced the vermiculite, the oil, the rare earths, the mortgages, the procedures. It declined to price the asbestos deaths, the dead herring, the contaminated villages, the foreclosed homes, the unpaid years of care. A cost set at zero is not a cost left sacred and unpriced. It is a cost assigned the most extractive price there is, the price that lets the taking continue without limit. This book does not commodify the harm. It refuses the zero the extractive accounting already assigned to it.

That is the answer to Sandel, and it is more than a debater's reply. Sandel is right that pricing can corrupt, and the book holds his warning close, which is why it never claims that a dollar figure restores what was taken or settles what is owed. A number is not a resurrection. But Sandel's worry assumes a starting point of dignity that the cases in this chapter do not enjoy. The asbestos deaths were not being treated as priceless. They were being treated as free. Between a false zero and an honest, disclosed, contestable figure, the honest figure is the one that protects the good Sandel wants protected, because it makes the harm visible to a civilization that decides what to defend by reading prices.

Nancy Fraser carried Polanyi's argument one step further, and Chapter 2 left a marker for the step where her name was first set down. In the second cycle of McDowell County's extraction, when a pharmaceutical company mapped the wreckage the coal economy had left and built a market on top of it, this book named the thing Fraser calls expropriation, accumulation by means other than the wage contract. That marker comes due here. Fraser's point is that Polanyi's land and labor were never the whole story, because Polanyi's protective movement was built mostly for the worker inside the wage contract, the one with a union card and a labor law to stand behind. Beneath and around that worker is a wider field of taking that no wage contract governs at all: the unpaid reproductive labor that keeps workers alive and raises the next generation of them, the natural world drawn down without payment, the communities mined a second time once the first extraction has hollowed them out. Fraser calls this expropriation, to distinguish it from the exploitation that happens inside the wage relation. Exploitation underpays you for your work. Expropriation takes from you without the form of a contract at all.

Almost every case in this chapter is expropriation in Fraser's sense rather than exploitation. The Libby residents were not underpaid employees; many were not Grace's employees in any sense, just people who lived in the town or carried fibers home on a relative's clothes. The Gulf fishing families had no contract with BP. The Baotou villagers had no contract with the electronics firms whose supply chains poisoned their water. The eighty-year-old caring for her husband had no contract with anyone. In every case the value was taken without the wage form that the last century built its protections around, which is precisely why the existing protections do not reach it. Fraser names the gap. This book prices what falls into it.

So the move the book performs everywhere, the move worth naming once and plainly, is this. It takes a thing the extractive accounting has priced at zero, a thing that was never produced for sale and was never protected because no contract covered it, and it sets down an honest figure for what was taken. It does this not to complete the commodification but to interrupt it, in the only language a society that reads prices will hear. That is Polanyi's re-embedding, turned into an accounting. It is the answer to Sandel and the payoff of Fraser, and it is the same object the rest of this book keeps returning to under different names.

### Restitution and Foreclosure: Two Directions of Accountability

The cases in this chapter raise a question they cannot answer on their own: what would an honest accounting of these severances actually require? The rest of this section names the framework's response at the level of structure. The methodology in Chapter 6 will formalize it, and the policy work in Chapter 9 will engage what building it would take. Here the task is only to name what the apparatus is and which established traditions it stands within.

The accountability gap has two faces in time. There is harm already done: the McDowell life-expectancy gap, the Libby deaths, the Gulf and the Sound that have not recovered, the foreclosed households that never regained their homes. And there is harm not yet done: the future cost of carbon emitted today, the materials being foreclosed for uses no one will be able to make of them fifty years from now, the retirement obligation a younger cohort has not yet been asked to bear in full. An architecture that addresses one face and ignores the other addresses half the problem.

The framework answers with two coordinated instruments. The first, the Restitution Bond, addresses harm already realized; its posting discharges the accumulated damage borne by communities, workers, and ecosystems whose costs were severed at the time of extraction. The second, the Foreclosure Bond, addresses harm not yet realized; its posting discharges the future costs that present extraction is locking in. Together they form the aggregate bond the framework's central equation calls for when it sets cost severance equal to residual commons value minus the bond posted.

The choice of the word *bond* is deliberate, and the alternatives were rejected for reasons that matter. A tax is paid at the moment of a transaction, into general revenue, with no structure tying the dollar paid to any specific future obligation. A fee licenses an activity rather than discharging a harm. A fund pools money without discharging anything against a particular liability. A bond does three things the framework needs at once. It is posted before the full cost has come due. It carries forward across the long tail of the cost it is meant to cover. And it discharges, by a stated figure, against a specific obligation when that obligation matures. The reclamation bonds that mining companies post under the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act do this. The decommissioning bonds oil and gas operators post against closing a well do this. The sovereign fund Norway built against its petroleum extraction does it at national scale. The reparations economics that William Darity and Kirsten Mullen developed treats the obligation as a documented historical debt, which is bond-like in structure if not yet in name. The framework uses the word in continuity with those traditions and in the meaning they have built into it.

A reader will notice an asymmetry worth explaining: the instrument is called Restitution, while the tradition that grounds the method is called reparations. The choice is intentional. Restitution is the older legal and philosophical term for the obligation to restore what was taken, restoration to the prior condition or its monetary equivalent, and the framework needs an umbrella broad enough to cover the full range of contexts the bond will be posted against. The reparations tradition is the framework's primary informing source, and its method and rhetoric are how the framework knows what it knows about backward-looking accountability. But calling the instrument a Reparations Bond would narrow the umbrella to one historically specific application and would make its use in environmental restitution, in occupational-disease restitution, in community and ecosystem restitution read as analogy rather than direct application. The narrower alternatives each fail in their own way. Restoration presupposes a physical restorability that many of these harms, lives lost, ecosystems extinguished, communities dispersed, do not allow. Indemnification routes through an insurance register the framework does not work in. Damages collapses the bond into the measurement instrument the framework names separately. Restitution covers what the others cannot, while leaving the reparations tradition its specific historical claim. The framework joins that tradition rather than translating it.

The Restitution Bond stands within an established lineage. The argument for reparations grounded in structural economic harm rather than individual tortious acts was articulated most influentially by Ta-Nehisi Coates in *The Case for Reparations*, published in *The Atlantic* in 2014, an essay widely credited with returning the question to mainstream American debate. Coates's pattern, name the mechanism that produced the harm, name the harm, name the debt, is the rhetorical companion to what reparations economics develops as method. The framework's cost-severance-damages instrument extends that pattern into extraction-community contexts: name the mechanism, which is cost severance; name the harm, which is the documented life-expectancy gap, the institutional collapse, the health costs pushed onto families and onto Medicare; name the debt, which is the backward-looking accounting. William Darity and Kirsten Mullen's *From Here to Equality*, published in 2020, is the contemporary landmark, and its wealth-gap method is the patient accumulation of the specific mechanisms by which harm was transferred and compounded across generations. Earlier work by Dalton Conley established the use of wealth, rather than behavior, as the structural variable, and matched-comparison studies in the same tradition showed that conventional explanations cannot account for the gap's size. The framework applies the same matched-comparison discipline to extraction communities: McDowell County's collapse against comparable non-extraction Appalachian counties; Libby's health burden against communities without Grace's asbestos. The method travels; what differs is the calibration to each case.

The legal-architecture side of the lineage is developed most rigorously in Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital*, published in 2019. Pistor shows that capital is not a natural asset class but a legal artifact, that specific legal modules, property law, collateral law, trust law, the corporate veil, bankruptcy law, choice-of-law clauses, encode assets as capital and shield that capital from accountability across jurisdictions. Libby is exactly the pattern she names. Grace's bankruptcy was not an accident but a feature of the architecture: bankruptcy law let a veil-protected entity discharge its liabilities by reorganizing, pushing the claims onto creditors and the public. The 2009 acquittal was the criminal law failing to reach what Pistor describes as a design feature rather than a defect. Where she diagnoses the modules that encode the severance, the framework prices what the modules sever.

The Foreclosure Bond meets a reader's first association head-on. Most readers will hear the second word through the 2008 housing crisis, the wave of mortgage foreclosures that pulled primary assets out of household balance sheets, concentrated in Black and working-class communities. The framework names that as continuity rather than coincidence. *Foreclose* descends from the Old French *forclore*, to bar from, and a mortgage foreclosure is one setting where the barring-from happens, a household barred from a primary asset by a legal architecture that lets the barring occur at scale. The framework names a cousin: future generations barred from commons options, a habitable atmosphere, intact ecosystems, reserves of irreplaceable materials, by a legal and economic architecture that lets the barring occur across time. The Foreclosure Bond is the instrument the framework posts against that second barring-from. The alternatives fall short. A Future-Generations Bond loses the barring structure and reads as soft. A Sustainability Bond has been worn smooth by financial marketing. A Reclamation Bond already names a narrower act, restoring a site after extraction, which is a subset of what this bond covers. Foreclosure names the mechanism the others only describe.

The Foreclosure Bond's lineage runs through environmental-bond economics: Hartwick's rule for sustainable extraction from 1977, the reclamation-bond tradition that American mining and oil and gas regulation has implemented with varying seriousness for half a century, and the sovereign-fund architecture Norway built against its petroleum. These are not reparations instruments. They are foreclosure-prevention instruments, postings made against ongoing or proposed extraction whose costs would otherwise fall on future generations, generations whose moral standing the framework takes seriously, grounded philosophically in Derek Parfit's 1984 work on outcomes that affect people who do not yet exist.

There is a political-philosophical lineage worth naming as well. The civic-republican tradition, articulated most recently in Philip Pettit's *Republicanism* of 1997 and recovered historically in Quentin Skinner's *Liberty Before Liberalism* of 1998, frames freedom as non-domination and holds that non-domination requires institutions that future generations cannot trivially be subjected to. A society that extracts now in ways that leave future generations dominated, by a destabilized climate, by commons foreclosed for present consumption, by economic arrangements rigged before those generations have any political voice, violates that commitment whether or not any specific contract has been broken. The Foreclosure Bond is the accounting instrument for that commitment. The framework does not ask the reader to accept civic-republican philosophy as the only frame; the bond works regardless of which tradition supplies the standing of future generations. But the civic-republican framing is a substantial tradition that grounds the bond's core commitment, and the framework engages it where alternative traditions converge on the same requirement.

The two instruments are independent of each other. A community whose commons has already been largely consumed, McDowell after the coal era, the Niger Delta that Chapter 4 documents after sixty years of extraction, carries a high Restitution Bond obligation and a relatively low Foreclosure Bond obligation, because the foreclosure has already happened. A context where extraction is proposed but has not yet occurred carries a low Restitution Bond obligation and a high Foreclosure Bond obligation, because the foreclosure is what is being decided now. Most real cases carry both. Norway's petroleum extraction internalized substantial restitution for Norwegian citizens through its welfare-state architecture but did not internalize the foreclosure cost borne by populations elsewhere through the atmospheric effects of Norwegian oil. Norway has paid a Restitution Bond to its own citizens; the Foreclosure Bond remains globally underdeveloped. The decomposition is what lets both obligations be seen at once.

The same apparatus runs in reverse, and Chapter 6 walks through the backward direction with its own roster of cases, from the restitution programs that followed historical atrocities to the truth-and-reconciliation efforts that priced what could be priced and named what could not. The most developed backward-direction work in the literature is Darity and Mullen's pricing of the racial wealth gap accumulated through American policy since 1619, the framework's method applied backward with Darity and Mullen as its primary developers. Their more recent work prices the life-expectancy gap between Black and white Americans as a measurable legacy effect, and McDowell County's life-expectancy gap, cited in earlier chapters, is the same kind of legacy effect applied to a coal-extraction community.

Some variables sit at the framework's boundary rather than inside it. The direct cost of coercion itself, as distinct from coercion's legacy effects, remains unresolved in the reparations-economics field, and it is worth being honest about why. The cases in this chapter mostly run on an imbalance in what each side knew: workers and consumers proceeded because they did not know what they would bear. Coerced extraction is different in kind. The person bearing the cost knows, and is forced to bear it anyway. The framework reaches coerced cases through their legacy effects, the wealth gaps, the life-expectancy gaps, the disrupted transmission of cultural knowledge, the inherited trauma, and it defers the direct pricing of the coercion vector as an open question outside this book's scope. The book demonstrates the framework forward and leaves the fuller backward application to the scholars in the reparations tradition who have been doing that work for years, whose lead the framework follows in identifying and pricing those variables.

What this chapter has documented is the empirical state of bond posting across the American extraction regimes: in nearly every case examined here, the bond actually posted falls far below the residual commons value, by orders of magnitude. The federal Black Lung Benefits Program has paid out forty-four billion dollars across half a century, a real number and not a small one, and its trust fund carried roughly five billion dollars in outstanding debt as of late 2024, with no instrument in place that prices the severance the fund was meant to compensate. The reclamation bonds mining companies post fall billions of dollars short of the actual cost of restoring the sites whose extraction generated them. The intergenerational obligation embedded in Social Security carries no Foreclosure Bond at all, which guarantees the cost will fall on whichever generation happens to be of working age when it matures. The framework names this the accountability gap, and the gap is what the next chapters' methodology and policy work set out to address.

The reparations tradition carries one further discipline worth holding onto: it treats the question of what is owed as separate from the question of what is politically feasible to collect. The Restitution Bond method applies whether or not any particular society is currently prepared to post the bond. The framework's contribution here is not advocacy but accounting. What the bond would have to be, if the severances in this chapter were accounted for honestly, is the method's question. What political path leads from current bond levels toward the residual commons value is a separate question, addressed in Chapter 9 and scoped, by the framework's own discipline, as an open political question rather than a single prescription this book tries to legislate.

This chapter has named the cost severance the American extraction regimes produce, documented the pattern across its cases at financial, intergenerational, and medical scales, met the strongest objections a sympathetic reader could raise, named the move the book performs everywhere and answered the commodification worry it provokes, and introduced the two-instrument response alongside the traditions that ground it. What remains is the methodology.

The next chapter formalizes the apparatus: the test that admits a cost claim to the accounting, the gates that filter admitted claims for consistency and independence, the three ways of estimating residual commons value when no single method suffices, and the identity that bridges the framework to standard resource economics. After methodology, Chapter 7 carries the framework through thought-experiment scaling under maximally different conditions, and Chapter 8 walks a single ton of Appalachian coal through the full decomposition that produces the book's most concrete numerical example. Chapter 9's policy economy, Chapter 10's closing reflection, and the technical appendix all build on the apparatus this chapter has now introduced.

The accounting exists. What remains is its formalization, its testing, and its honest engagement with the work of building accountability in societies that have so far declined to build it. That is the question the next chapters take up. Not whether cost severance exists, but what we do about it.

=== $108T WORKINGS ===

**Author-facing note (not for the prose body). The Social Security NET cost-severance illustration: method, inputs, arithmetic, and sources.**

**1. Why the gross $108T is banned.** The $108T figure is the gross "highest-possible" number. It takes every payroll-tax dollar contributed since 1937 and compounds it forward at a broad equity-index return, while never subtracting the benefits actually paid out. It therefore models a fund that collected contributions for ~88 years and never paid a single retiree. That is not a cost-severance figure; it is an arithmetic artifact roughly 100× the order of the ~$1T Brookings-class counterfactual. Per the action list (A0d) and canonical figure ledger, it must never be presented as a cost-severance number.

**2. The honest NET method.** Any alternative architecture still had to support the same retirees. So the only money ever available to invest as productive capital was the *running net surplus* (year-by-year contributions minus benefits paid). The honest counterfactual: compound the annual net surpluses (not gross contributions) at a disclosed real equity return, and compare against what those same surpluses actually earned at the Treasury rate (which produced today's reserve).

**3. Inputs (life-of-program, OASDI combined, nominal).**
- Cumulative income through 2009: ~$13.8T; cumulative expenditures through 2009: ~$11.3T (SSA history, *tftable*).
- Lifetime (through end-2024): cumulative income ~$29.17T; cumulative benefits paid ~$26.45T; ending reserves ~$2.72T (SSA 2024/2025 Trustees data; reserves declined $67B in 2024 to $2.72T).
- Non-payroll-tax share of total income ≈ 12% (interest + benefit taxation + transfers), so cumulative *net contributions* ≈ ~88% of income ≈ ~$25–26T, i.e., roughly equal to cumulative benefits paid (~$26.45T).
- Because lifetime contributions ≈ lifetime benefits, the cumulative *net* surplus ever available to invest is small and is, by construction, the accumulated reserve: **~$2.7T**, earned at Treasury rates.

**4. The counterfactual arithmetic (illustrative, disclosed-assumption).**
- What actually happened: annual net surpluses, reinvested at the special-issue Treasury rate, compounded to **~$2.7T** by end-2024.
- Counterfactual: the *same* annual net-surplus stream, compounded instead at a disclosed real equity return. Treasury real yields over the surplus decades averaged roughly ~2–3% real; a broad equity index returned roughly ~6.5–7% real over comparable windows. The excess return is ~3.5–4.5 points per year, applied to a surplus stream that built from near-zero (1980s) to ~$2.7T (2010s).
- Applying that excess return premium to the dollar-weighted, relatively *recent* net-surplus stream yields a counterfactual fund larger than the actual ~$2.7T but bounded in the **low single-digit trillions** on central assumptions, and only at the high end of aggressive assumptions (treating a larger share of gross contributions as "investable" and extending the high real return across the full window) does it reach **low double-digit trillions**. It does not approach $108T under any honest netting, because the netting removes the ~$26T of benefits the gross figure silently kept invested.

**5. Result presented in prose.** The chapter states the honest version explicitly: contributions and benefits over the life of the program are each ~$26T and nearly cancel; the only investable money was the running surplus (~$2.7T actual reserve); re-running it at a real equity return yields a counterfactual "in the range of a few trillion dollars, not a hundred," and the foregone return "runs into the trillions." This satisfies the spec: NET figure shown with method disclosed; gross $108T never presented; qualitative "into the trillions" floor available and used as the headline claim since the precise netting is assumption-sensitive.

**6. Sources.**
- SSA, *Social Security Trust Fund Data* (history/tftable): cumulative receipts/expenditures since 1937 (through-2009 anchor $13.8T/$11.3T).
- SSA Office of the Chief Actuary, 2024 OASDI Trustees Report and 2025 Trustees Report: end-2023 reserves $2.79T; 2024 reserves $2.72T (−$67B); 2023 cost $1,392B, income $1,351B; depletion mid-2030s; ~77% payable after depletion.
- SSA / CRFB / CBPP analyses: non-payroll-tax share of cumulative income ≈ 12%; lifetime cumulative income ~$29.17T and benefits ~$26.45T.
- Brookings counterfactual (order-of-magnitude ~$1T) referenced in the action-list diagnosis (A0d) as the sanity check that the gross $108T is ~100× too large.

(Note: figures are nominal cumulative SSA series; the equity-return premium is the disclosed, contestable assumption. If a reviewer contests the netting, the prose falls back cleanly to the qualitative "runs into the trillions," which all defensible nettings support.)
