## Per-figure findings

### 1. Bennu sample-return ~$9.5M/g — **DERIVED (reproduces); inputs SOURCED**

- **TA value:** OSIRIS-REx → Bennu; 121.6 g returned; ~$1.16B lifecycle;
  **~$9.5M/g**.
- **Verdict: DERIVED — reproduces from two independently SOURCED NASA inputs.**
  - Sample mass: **121.6 g** confirmed (NASA, final accounting announced
    2024-02-15; capsule returned 2023-09-24).
  - Lifecycle cost: **~$1.16B** confirmed (NASA New Frontiers program,
    "~$1.16 billion over 15 years").
  - Arithmetic: $1.16B ÷ 121.6 g = **$9.54M/g** → rounds to the TA's
    ~$9.5M/g. Reproduces cleanly. No discrepancy.
- **Recommended bibliography citation(s):**
  - NASA. "NASA Announces OSIRIS-REx Bulk Sample Mass." NASA Science blog,
    15 Feb 2024. https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/osiris-rex/2024/02/15/nasa-announces-osiris-rex-bulk-sample-mass/
    (sample mass = 121.6 g).
  - NASA. "OSIRIS-REx" mission overview (lifecycle cost ~$1.16B, New
    Frontiers program). https://science.nasa.gov/mission/osiris-rex/
  - The $9.5M/g figure is an author derivation from these two inputs — TA
    should present it as "≈ $1.16B ÷ 121.6 g" (DERIVED), not as a sourced
    external statistic.

### 2. Keck Institute Asteroid Retrieval Mission Study (2012) — **SOURCED, but TA has a MASS / $-per-kg DISCREPANCY**

- **TA value:** "~$2.6B for retrieval of a **7-ton** asteroid to lunar
  orbit (~**$370,000/kg** retrieved)."
- **Verdict: $2.6B is SOURCED; the "7-ton" mass and the "$370,000/kg"
  derivation are WRONG and mutually inconsistent with the actual study.**
  - The 2012 KISS *Asteroid Retrieval Feasibility Study* (Brophy, Culick,
    Friedman et al.) studied returning a **~7-METER-diameter, ~500-ton
    (≈500,000 kg) near-Earth asteroid** to high lunar orbit for **~$2.6B**.
    The "7" in the TA is the asteroid's diameter in METERS, mis-transcribed
    as a 7-TON mass.
  - Correct order-of-magnitude derivation: $2.6B ÷ 500,000 kg ≈ **$5,200/kg
    retrieved** — roughly two orders of magnitude *below* the TA's stated
    "$370,000/kg," not above it. (The contemporaneous press framing was
    that this beat conventional chemical delivery to lunar orbit, then
    quoted at ~$100k/kg, by roughly a factor of ~8–20.)
  - **This is a substantive factual error in the TA, not just a missing
    citation.** Both the mass (7 ton vs ~500 ton) and the per-kg figure
    ($370,000/kg vs ~$5,200/kg) need correction. Flag to author for a
    Stage 3.1 fact-check spot-fix. (Note: the $370,000/kg appears to be
    an unrelated/invented intermediate — it does not reproduce from either
    $2.6B÷7,000 kg ≈ $371,000/kg... **actually $2.6B ÷ 7,000 kg = $371,000/kg**,
    so the TA's per-kg figure is self-consistent WITH the erroneous 7-ton
    mass. The root error is the 7-ton mass; the per-kg figure inherited it.)
- **Recommended bibliography citation:**
  - Brophy, J., Culick, F., Friedman, L., et al. *Asteroid Retrieval
    Feasibility Study.* Keck Institute for Space Studies, Caltech / JPL,
    2 April 2012. https://www.kiss.caltech.edu/final_reports/Asteroid_final_report.pdf
    (~500-ton asteroid to high lunar orbit; ~$2.6B). Secondary/accessible:
    Wall, M. "Is Asteroid Mining Possible? Study Says Yes, for $2.6
    Billion." Space.com, 2012. https://www.space.com/15405-asteroid-mining-feasibility-study.html

### 3. Planetary Resources — $50–$500/kg at scale — **COMPANY-TARGET-OR-POSITED; the specific $50–$500/kg range is UNVERIFIABLE as a PR-attributable figure**

- **TA value:** "$50–$500/kg at scale (water; iron); not validated by
  operational missions" — already flagged in the TA as "company-stated
  targets; broadly skeptical reception."
- **Verdict: COMPANY-TARGET (correctly flagged) — but the exact $50–$500/kg
  range could NOT be located in any primary Planetary Resources document
  or on-record statement.** What is verifiable:
  - Planetary Resources' public pitch (2012–2018, defunct after the 2018
    ConsenSys acquisition / asset sale) centered on **asteroid-derived
    water for in-space propellant**, positioned against the ~$20,000/kg
    cost of lifting water/propellant from Earth (co-founder Peter
    Diamandis, on record). That ~$20,000/kg is a *launch-cost-to-beat*
    figure, not a PR delivered-cost target.
  - The closest **peer-reviewed** delivered-cost estimate for asteroid-
    derived propellant to LEO is **~$2,000–$3,000/kg** (Colvin, Crane &
    Lal 2020, *Acta Astronautica* — "comparable to delivering propellant
    via Falcon Heavy"). This is an academic estimate, NOT a Planetary
    Resources figure, and is roughly 1–2 orders of magnitude *above* the
    TA's $50–$500/kg.
  - **Recommendation:** Treat $50–$500/kg as POSITED (author-supplied
    illustrative range, not externally attributable). Either (a) drop the
    specific PR attribution and cite the academic $2,000–$3,000/kg
    estimate as the defensible per-kg anchor, or (b) keep $50–$500/kg only
    if a primary PR source can be produced; absent that, it is UNVERIFIABLE
    and should not carry a "Source: Planetary Resources" attribution.
- **Recommended bibliography citation(s) (for the verifiable substitute):**
  - Colvin, T. J., Crane, K. W., & Lal, B. "Assessing the economics of
    asteroid-derived water for propellant." *Acta Astronautica* 176 (2020):
    298–315. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S009457652030312X
    (delivered propellant to LEO ~$2,000–$3,000/kg; competitive only in
    the 2031–2050 horizon).
  - For the company itself (context / defunct-status): "Planetary
    Resources," Wikipedia (founded 2012, acquired by ConsenSys 2018).
    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_Resources — and the MIT
    Technology Review post-mortem "How the asteroid-mining bubble burst"
    (2019). https://www.technologyreview.com/2019/06/26/134510/asteroid-mining-bubble-burst-history/

### 4. Deep Space Industries — "comparable order of magnitude" — **COMPANY-TARGET-OR-POSITED; no precise per-kg figure published**

- **TA value (table, ~line 6065):** "Comparable order of magnitude to
  Planetary Resources estimates" — flagged "Same caveat" (company-stated
  target).
- **Verdict: COMPANY-TARGET (correctly flagged), and the TA wisely does
  NOT assert a specific DSI per-kg number.** Verifiable DSI public
  statements (2013 launch, FireFly prospectors / Harvestor mining craft;
  defunct after the 2019 Bradford Space acquisition) framed the value
  proposition in terms of **propellant/water delivered in orbit vs. the
  ~$10M/ton (~$10,000/kg) cost of lifting it from Earth**, and the value
  of extending commsat life ($5–8M/month). DSI did **not** publish a
  defensible delivered-cost-per-kg target; "comparable order of magnitude
  to Planetary Resources" is the most that can be said and is appropriately
  hedged in the TA.
- **Recommended bibliography citation:**
  - "Deep Space Industries," Wikipedia (founded 2013; FireFly/Harvestor
    program; acquired by Bradford Space 2019).
    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Space_Industries — primary-press
    anchor: Wall, M. "Deep Space Industries: A New Asteroid-Mining Company
    Is Born." Space.com, 2013. https://www.space.com/19462-asteroid-mining-deep-space-industries-birth.html
  - Keep the TA's "comparable order of magnitude … same caveat" language;
    do NOT introduce a fabricated DSI $/kg figure.

### 5. Falcon Heavy — $90M / 63.8 t LEO (~$1,400/kg) — **SOURCED but YEAR-STAMP / CONFLATION issue**

- **TA value:** "~$1,400/kg (LEO; $90M / 63.8 t LEO capacity); operational
  since 2018."
- **Verdict: SOURCED inputs, but the price and capacity are from different
  configurations / vintages and should be year-stamped.**
  - **63,800 kg to LEO** is the published FULLY EXPENDABLE maximum payload
    (verified, Falcon Heavy spec). Correct as a capacity ceiling.
  - **$90M** was SpaceX's **2016–2018-era list price**. By 2022 the list
    price was **~$97M**, and historically the $150M figure was attached to
    the full 63.8 t expendable performance (giving ~$2,350/kg). Pairing a
    $90M (reusable-era list) price with the 63.8 t (expendable-max)
    capacity slightly overstates the achievable $/kg — the two numbers
    are not from the same launch configuration.
  - $90M ÷ 63,800 kg = **$1,411/kg** → the TA's ~$1,400/kg reproduces
    arithmetically, but the *pairing* is optimistic (expendable max
    capacity at a reusable-era price). Recommend year-stamping: e.g.
    "$90–$97M list price (2018–2022); 63.8 t LEO expendable maximum; lower
    payloads when boosters recovered."
- **Recommended bibliography citation:**
  - "Falcon Heavy," Wikipedia (price history $90M 2016 → ~$97M 2022; 63.8 t
    LEO expendable max; first flight 2018). https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_Heavy
  - SpaceNews, "SpaceX's new price chart illustrates performance cost of
    reusability." https://spacenews.com/spacexs-new-price-chart-illustrates-performance-cost-of-reusability/
  - Note Falcon 9 row (~$3,245/kg; $74M/22,800 kg, "SpaceX 2026 list
    price") similarly should carry a dated source — verify the 2026 list
    price separately if it is to be retained.

  No reproduction issue because it is explicitly posited, not asserted as

- **Recommended bibliography citation:**
  - "Starship," and the SpaceX/Musk cost-target coverage — anchor to a

## Cross-cutting recommendations

1. **One substantive factual error to fix (Finding 2):** the Keck study is
   a **~500-ton (~500,000 kg)** asteroid, not "7-ton" (the 7 is the
   *diameter in meters*). The consequent "$370,000/kg" is wrong by ~2 orders
   of magnitude; the correct figure is **~$5,200/kg**. Route to a Stage 3.1
   fact-check spot-fix.

2. **One UNVERIFIABLE attribution to resolve (Finding 3):** the Planetary
   Resources "$50–$500/kg" range is not traceable to any primary PR source.
   Either substitute the peer-reviewed ~$2,000–$3,000/kg (Colvin/Crane/Lal
   2020) estimate, or downgrade $50–$500/kg to an explicitly POSITED
   illustrative range with the "Source: Planetary Resources" attribution
   removed. Per the no-invented-claims discipline, it should NOT remain
   attributed to PR without a primary source.

3. **Two year-stamp tightenings (Findings 1, 5):** present Bennu $9.5M/g as
   an explicit DERIVATION ($1.16B ÷ 121.6 g); year-stamp and de-conflate
   the Falcon Heavy price/capacity pairing.

4. **Findings 4 & 6 (DSI, Starship) are clean** — already correctly flagged
   as company targets with appropriate caveats; only need bibliography
   entries, no figure changes.

5. **All six need formal bibliography entries with URLs** (none currently
   in `research/literature/bibliography.md`). Citations supplied above.
