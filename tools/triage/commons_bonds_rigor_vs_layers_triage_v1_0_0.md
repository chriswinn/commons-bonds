# Commons Bonds — Rigor Tests vs. 10 Layers Triage

**Version:** 1.0.0
**Date:** 2026-04-22
**Protocol evaluated:** `commons_bonds_rigor_protocol_v1_2_0.md`
**Canonical layer set:** 10 layers per v1.21.0 (Atmospheric, Geographic, Temporal, Institutional, Demographic, Ecological, Political, Cohesion, Informational, Agency)
**Status:** Proposed assessment — input to Part 1 ratification of rigor protocol v1.2.0. Findings are my read; ratification call is yours.

---

## 1. Purpose of this triage

The v1.2.0 protocol consolidates every validated rigor test the project uses, which is the right move for lineage discipline. But with the AIT-as-load-bearing / layers-as-scaffolding positioning canonical (C9), the protocol itself is a third-order artifact: scaffolding built around scaffolding. That is fine *if* each test earns its place; it is protocol bloat otherwise.

This triage runs each of the ~26 tests against the 10-layer set and asks two questions:

1. **Does applying this test to the layer set do real work?** (Does it surface something that changes layer decisions, naming, or framing?)
2. **If it earns its keep, does it belong in the book — or only in the methodology shelf?**

The book's mission is making the unseen, seen. Tests that help the layers reveal uncaptured costs are book-critical. Tests that help the author ship the book are process-critical. The distinction matters because the protocol currently blends them.

Verdict vocabulary:

- **LOAD-BEARING** — layer-set integrity depends on passing this test; book-visible in methodology or layer-introduction content
- **SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT** — organizational test that genuinely surfaces something on the layers; book-visible through naming, case selection, or lit engagement
- **PROCESS-ONLY** — useful for author/publisher workflow but does not belong in the book
- **CONSOLIDATE** — significantly overlaps another test at the layer level; candidate for merge or cross-reference rather than standalone

## 2. Group A — Framework tests

### Test 1 — AIT Falsifiability Gate
**Verdict: LOAD-BEARING.** This is not scaffolding; it is the framework's epistemic core per C9. Applied to the 10 layers: each layer must evaporate under its corresponding abundance (atmospheric → breathable-air abundance; temporal → no-temporal-separation; cohesion → spontaneous-trust abundance). Cohesion, Informational, and Agency already passed in v1.21.0. Drop this test and the framework collapses. Must be in the book — Ch. 6 and Technical Appendix already carry it; every chapter introducing a layer should invoke it briefly.

### Test 2 — Two-Path Rigor
**Verdict: LOAD-BEARING.** This is where "the unseen becomes seen" for the reader. Each layer must reveal an allocation asymmetry (Path 1, the hidden cost) and a shield (Path 2, why no one notices). If a candidate layer can't identify both paths, it isn't a layer. Applied to the 10: every ratified layer has both paths clean; the three v1.21.0 additions were specifically ratified by clearing both. This is arguably THE book test — it's the structural form of every chapter.

### Test 3 — Merger and Primitiveness Gates
**Verdict: LOAD-BEARING for the layer set; SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT for the book.** These gates are what separate a principled taxonomy from an ad hoc list. Applied to the 10: Cohesion vs. Demographic cleared bilateral independence (Bowling Alone suburbs + small religious communities); Survivability failed primitiveness and was demoted to a cross-layer concept. This test is load-bearing for taxonomy integrity but doesn't need heavy book-front prose — a short methodology passage and an appendix table (which candidates failed which gate) carries it. This is where the book earns credibility with the Ostrom-successor reader without belaboring the reader who just wants to price their situation.

### Test 4 — Multi-Scale × 2 Environments
**Verdict: LOAD-BEARING at the multi-scale level; off-Earth arm is MOSTLY PEDAGOGICAL.** Multi-scale (individual / community / government / corporate) is the universality claim — a layer that works only at one scale isn't a layer. The off-Earth arm did real work once (Geographic failed off-Earth in v1.0.0 stress test; "Planetary" naming failed for the same reason). Beyond those specific findings, the off-Earth arm functions mostly as a forcing function for the author — it keeps you honest about which concepts are Earth-parochial. In the book it shows up concretely in Ch. 7 asteroid-miner and the universality-claim framing, not in every chapter.

**On off-Earth more generally:** I'd keep it in the protocol exactly as it is — but note in the book only where a specific case makes off-Earth visible (Ch. 7). Forcing every chapter to address off-Earth would be scaffolding-visible-in-content in a way that doesn't serve the reader.

## 3. Group B — Scope tests

### Test 6 — Trying-to-do-too-much
**Verdict: PROCESS-ONLY.** Real discipline for deciding whether to add / keep a layer; Cohesion, Informational, and Agency each had to earn their seat against this test. But readers don't see scope discipline — they see the result. This belongs in ratification memos and session handoffs, not in the book.

### Test 7 — Cross-spectrum preservation
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT.** Applied to layer names: "Political" carries partisan valence (the existing watch item); "Agency" cuts both left and right, which is actually a feature; "Demographic" reads technocratic; "Informational" reads neutral. This test drives naming and is indirectly book-visible through the defended choices. A short note in the introduction acknowledging cross-spectrum design is all it needs.

### Test 8 — Load-bearing vs. scaffolding
**Verdict: CONSOLIDATE.** This IS the AIT positioning per C9 — already canonical in the operating core. As a standalone test in Group B it is redundant. Recommendation: fold into Test 1's framing as a reminder and remove from Group B, or retain as a one-line cross-reference.

### Test 9 — Displacement
**Verdict: PROCESS-ONLY.** "If we add layer X, what chapter space gets displaced?" is real author discipline — it's why Cohesion didn't cause Book One to balloon to 12 chapters. But readers don't see the trade-off. Keep in methodology only.

## 4. Group C — Authorial / practical tests

### Test 10 — Lone-author vulnerability
**Verdict: PROCESS-ONLY, LOW DIFFERENTIATION AT LAYER LEVEL.** Layer choices rarely expose a lone author as such; the CEO-era case content within chapters does. This test does real work on cases, not on layers. Keep as process-only.

### Test 11 — Legal exposure
**Verdict: PROCESS-ONLY, LOW AT LAYER LEVEL / HIGH AT CASE LEVEL.** At the layer level: mostly non-differentiating. At the case level within Informational (tobacco, pharma, opioid) and Institutional (named CEOs, firms): essential. The test is correctly scoped to "Claim that changes the author's exposure surface" — that's case-level, not layer-level. Don't pretend it does work on layers per se.

### Test 12 — Cross-spectrum attack surface
**Verdict: CONSOLIDATE with Test 7 at the layer level.** Test 7 is positioning ("does this name read partisan?"); Test 12 is reception ("what attacks come in?"). At the layer level these converge — if a name reads partisan, partisan attacks follow. They diverge at the chapter / case level, where a non-partisan name can still produce partisan reception. Recommendation: note the convergence in the protocol and avoid running both when assessing layers.

### Test 13 — Career-risk compatibility
**Verdict: PROCESS-ONLY, NON-DIFFERENTIATING ON LAYERS.** No layer plausibly conflicts with nursing. Keep in the protocol for chapter-level / case-level use; note that it does no work at layer level.

## 5. Group D — Strategic / reception tests

### Tests 14, 15, 16 — Publisher appeal, Noema / Berggruen fit, Agent interest
**Verdict for all three: PROCESS-ONLY, LOW DIFFERENTIATION AT LAYER LEVEL.** These drive publishing decisions about the book as a whole; at the layer level they do almost no differentiating work. Ten layers vs. seven doesn't change pitch fit materially. Keep for scope-level and pitch-level decisions.

### Test 17 — Target reader recognition
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT.** Applied to each of the 10 layers: can a labor lawyer / community organizer / worker / grad student pattern-match their situation? This drives case selection within each layer and weeds out academic-only constructs. Book-visible through the cases chosen to open each chapter.

### Test 18 — Competitor / overlap
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT — and important.** Applied to each layer, this surfaces real intellectual positioning work: Cohesion ≈ Putnam's social capital (the book must engage Bowling Alone directly); Ecological ≈ Raworth's planetary boundaries (doughnut economics engagement); Temporal ≈ Parfit + finance (intergenerational ethics + discount rates); Agency ≈ Sen's capability approach and the libertarian non-coercion literature; Informational ≈ Habermas, epistemology-of-testimony literature. Book-visible in layer-introduction footnotes and the lit-review sections. This is where the novelty claim is defended.

### Test 19 — Decade-out durability
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT — with caveats on specific layers.** Geographic will feel dated as off-Earth becomes less speculative; Political is US-bound in connotation; Informational needs explicit AI-era handling. Applied to cases, it prevents the book from being pinned to 2026 artifacts. Book-visible through case framing choices.

## 6. Group E — Success criteria alignment

### Test 20 — Vocabulary portability
**Verdict: LOAD-BEARING for naming; SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT for the book.** Applied to the 10 layer names: "Atmospheric" weak, "Life-support" breaks the abundance-naming pattern, "Cohesion" is already Putnam's, "Agency" carries principal-agent baggage, "Political" too broad, "Demographic" awkward. This test is the driver of the naming cohort — it does unambiguous work. Book-visible through the chosen names and the one-time explanation of them in the introduction.

### Test 21 — Upstream-of-Goal-1 ("future generations compensated more fairly")
**Verdict: PROCESS-ONLY for layer sorting.** Applied to layers: Temporal contributes directly; Ecological yes (intergenerational); others indirectly through the accounting apparatus. This is useful sorting for chapter sequencing and for knowing which layers get more narrative weight in Book One vs. Book Two. Not a layer-ratification gate per se. Keep as methodology.

### Test 22 — Direct-to-Goal-2 ("accept a job with open eyes")
**Verdict: CONSOLIDATE with Test 17 at the layer level.** At the layer level this is "can an individual reader pattern-match?" — which is Test 17's question. At the book level they diverge (17 is general target-reader fit; 22 is specifically Goal-2 alignment). For layer assessment, run one or the other, not both.

### Test 23 — Labor-lawyer-in-2039
**Verdict: LOAD-BEARING for naming; CONSOLIDATE with Test 20 at the layer level.** Applied to layer names: "Temporal cost" reads naturally in a brief; "Political capture" does; "Informational cost" is awkward; "Cohesion cost" unusual; "Atmospheric" strange; "Agency cost" clashes with principal-agent usage. This produces the same naming pressure as Test 20. At the layer level they're two framings of one test. Recommendation: mark as companion tests in the protocol and run them together as a unit.

## 7. Group F — Content quality tests

### Test 24 — Counterargument coverage
**Verdict: PROCESS-ONLY at layer level; book-visible at chapter level.** Applied to layers, you'd list counterarguments per layer (Cohesion → "nostalgia" and "you can't price connection"; Informational → "free speech"; Agency → the libertarian/leftist cross-fire already engaged by the framework's positioning). But these are chapter-drafting concerns, not layer-selection gates. Keep in the protocol; it earns its keep at chapter level.

### Test 25 — Steelmannability
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT.** Applied to each layer: can the strongest critic engage meaningfully? If the only opponents of a layer are weak, the book's claim is fragile. This differs from Test 18 (does the lit exist?): 25 is "is the existing critique engaged rigorously?" For the 10 layers: all have sophisticated critics (Putnam's own self-critiques on Cohesion; behavioral-economics pushback on Agency; governance skeptics on Institutional). Book-visible through how the framework engages criticism rather than sidestepping it.

### Test 26 — Falsifiability
**Verdict: CONSOLIDATE with Test 1 at the layer level.** At the layer level, AIT's scarcity-inversion IS the falsifiability condition. Test 26 does distinct work on non-AIT claims (e.g., specific empirical predictions within a chapter), but at the layer level it is the AIT gate rephrased. Recommendation: annotate Test 26 as "falsifiability beyond AIT" in the protocol so the overlap is explicit.

### Test 27 — Empirical grounding
**Verdict: PROCESS-ONLY at layer level; book-standard throughout.** All 10 layers have substantial empirical literature (which is part of why Test 18 does its work). At the layer level, this test doesn't differentiate — every ratified layer is documented. It earns its keep as a prose-level standard for the whole book. Keep in the protocol for chapter-level claims; don't run as a layer gate.

## 9. Meta-findings

### The protocol is doing its job — and the protocol is partly scaffolding around scaffolding
Groups C and D (authorial, strategic) are almost entirely process-only at the layer level. That is correct; they exist to help the author ship the book. The protocol should make the layer-work vs. publishing-work distinction explicit so you can tell at a glance which mode you're in.

### The book's "make the unseen, seen" contribution lives in Tests 1 and 2
AIT and Two-Path are where the book becomes a thing the world needs. Everything else is apparatus for delivering those two findings convincingly. The load-bearing / scaffolding distinction at the C9 level maps cleanly onto the protocol: Tests 1–4 are the first-order apparatus; Groups B–F are the second-order apparatus for the first-order apparatus.

### The off-Earth arm is more pedagogical than it looks
I went into this expecting to flag it as overbuilt. It did real work on Geographic and on "Planetary" naming, and it functions as a forcing function against Earth-parochialism. Keep it — but the book shouldn't force-fit off-Earth into every chapter. Show it where it's specific (Ch. 7) and let the framework's universality claim do the rest.

### Five consolidations tighten the protocol without losing rigor
Test 8 fold into Test 1. Tests 7/12 note convergence at layer level. Tests 17/22 note convergence at layer level. Tests 20/23 mark as companion pair. Test 26 annotate against Test 1. These are cross-reference edits, not removals — each test still has distinct work at the chapter or scope level. At the layer level specifically, the unique-test count drops from ~26 to ~21.

### The character suite deserves the same triage separately
The 25-character pressure suite did not get this treatment; a companion triage ("which characters do work against the 10 layers?") would identify that Group I (intended users) and Group J (cultural readers) do most of the layer-level work, while Groups G (critics), H (gatekeepers), K (personal), and L (ethical / legacy) do most of their work at chapter / case / book level. That is a separate exercise, worth running before the Part 2 rigor pass.

### What this means for Part 1 ratification
v1.2.0 is ratifiable as-is. The triage's findings are **cross-reference edits** and a **documentation improvement**, not changes to what tests exist. I'd suggest ratifying v1.2.0 as canonical now, and queuing the consolidations as a v1.2.1 patch (clarifications, cross-references, no structural change — per §17.3 of the protocol).
