## Purpose

Defend the framework's choice of *abundance masking* as the name for the pattern by which structural-finiteness is hidden by a surface-experience of abundance, against the field of plausible alternatives: *abundance illusion*, *abundance-as-cover*, *false abundance*, *abundance concealment*. Existing Ch 7 line 119 prose introduces the term definitionally with strong concrete anchors (air, fisheries, aquifers, atmospheric carbon-absorbing capacity, soil regenerative capacity) — but in the same definitional paragraph slips between *abundance masking* (the framework's named term) and *abundance illusion* (a casual synonym): "no one prices the air we breathe; the abundance illusion is total." The metaphor's structural-vs-perceptual feature is doing load-bearing work the prose has not surfaced. The Ch 7 Mars-habitat thought-experiment ("abundance masking collapses *by construction* — there is no possible illusion of unlimited atmosphere or water in a closed habitat") makes the structural-vs-perceptual distinction operational; body prose has not defended why *masking* specifically is the right name for that structural feature.

## Stage 2 — Audience-blind flow draft

The framework's choice of *abundance masking* as the name for the pattern by which structural-finiteness is hidden by a surface-experience of abundance warrants a defense against the closest casual synonyms. *Abundance illusion* — which the framework's own Ch 7 prose occasionally uses interchangeably — implies a perceptual error: a viewer sees abundance because their perception is mistaken, and the corrective is to view more carefully. The framework's claim is structurally different. The abundance is real. The air over McDowell County in 1960 was abundant; the atmosphere did absorb the combusted carbon for two centuries without observable saturation; the Ogallala aquifer was abundant in the 1940s when central-pivot irrigation began drawing from it. What the abundance hid was not its existence but its finiteness — and the hiding was structural, not perceptual. Industrial architectures were built on the working assumption of unlimited atmospheric absorbing capacity; agricultural architectures were built on the assumption of unlimited aquifer recharge. The mask is the architecture, not the perception. *Mask* is the right word for what an architecture does to underlying structure — it covers without removing; it hides without dissolving; it produces a surface experience that operates the same way for every observer regardless of viewing care. Other names were available and each fails for a specific reason. *Abundance illusion* misroutes the diagnosis from architecture to perception. *Abundance-as-cover* is mechanically accurate but loses the active sense — masking is a thing the architecture does; cover is a passive state. *False abundance* implies the abundance was fake, which is empirically wrong — the abundance was real but local and time-bounded. *Abundance concealment* implies an actor doing the concealing, which moralizes a structural mechanism. The Mars-habitat thought-experiment the framework's earlier prose constructed demonstrates the structural feature directly: abundance masking collapses *by construction* in a closed habitat where every cubic meter of air and every gallon of water is enumerated. What collapses by physical construction cannot be a perceptual error; perception does not collapse by closed-system architecture. Structural features do. *Masking* names the structural feature; *illusion* names something close-but-not-the-same the framework specifically does not claim.

## Recommended placement

**Chapter 7, between current line 119 and current line 121** (On Other Worlds — Mars-habitat section, after the abundance-masking introductory definitional paragraph and before the Mars-habitat collapse-by-construction paragraph), as a new standalone paragraph.

### Reasoning

- **Ch 7 line 119** introduces the term definitionally with concrete anchors and the Theorem E.3 reference: *"**Abundance masking.** When a resource appears unlimited, no one prices it — and the costs it bears can flow continuously without anyone computing them. Air on Earth is the paradigmatic case: no one prices the air we breathe; the abundance illusion is total. The same illusion has applied historically to ocean fisheries before collapse, to aquifer water in the Ogallala before drawdown became visible, to the atmosphere's carbon-absorbing capacity, to soil's regenerative capacity. The framework's mathematics formalizes this in Theorem E.3 (Abundance Masking) of the Tech Appendix; the framework's Commons Inversion Test is the apparatus for detecting it."*
- **Ch 7 line 121** opens the Mars-habitat collapse-by-construction paragraph: *"On Mars, in a habitat where ten thousand people share the same atmosphere, none of the six patterns can produce concealment... And abundance masking collapses *by construction* — there is no possible illusion of unlimited atmosphere or water in a closed habitat..."*
- The natural slot for the name-defense is between (a) the definitional surfacing with the prose-slip between *masking* and *illusion* and (b) the Mars-habitat collapse paragraph that demonstrates the structural feature. The proposed paragraph slots cleanly: definition (with internal slip) → name-defense (resolves the slip) → Mars-habitat demonstration (the structural feature in action).
- The proposed paragraph resolves the internal-slip at line 119 directly — once the structural-vs-perceptual distinction is argued, the line 119 use of *illusion* alongside *masking* can be read as the prose engaging both casual-synonym and framework-term consciously, rather than as inadvertent slippage.
- Ch 2 + Ch 5 + Ch 8 use abundance masking only implicitly (the McDowell case grounds it; the framework's six-patterns list mentions it). Ch 7 is where the term is named and formalized.
- Tech Appendix carries the formal Theorem E.3 specification; the proposed paragraph is the body-prose complement.

### Tradeoffs

- **Pro Ch 7:** the chapter is the natural seat for the framework's named-pattern defenses (Ch 7 introduces the six-patterns framing in body prose); the proposed paragraph completes the name-defense pattern at the most-load-bearing pattern (the one Theorem E.3 formalizes); the Mars-habitat thought-experiment anchor is right there in the surrounding prose to reinforce.

Mitigation: the proposed paragraph names the structural-vs-perceptual distinction in language consistent with the surrounding prose's register (the Mars-habitat reference is *in the proposed paragraph itself*, which weaves the defense into the thought-experiment rather than interrupting it).

**Final placement call defers to author.** Alternative: if Ch 7's flow is judged too sensitive to the interruption, the abundance-masking defense could be placed in Ch 9 or Ch 10 where the cost-component-listing surfaces the term in summary register.
