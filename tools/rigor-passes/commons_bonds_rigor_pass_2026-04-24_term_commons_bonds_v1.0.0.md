## §2. The question under test

### §2.1 The name

**"Commons Bonds"** is the framework's identifying name and the book's title. First appears on the book cover; subsequently in every chapter header, framework document, academic citation, README canonical-state table, and ratified-decision references.

### §2.2 What it names (the polysemy)

The compound noun is doing multiple kinds of work simultaneously:

**Meaning #1 — Accountability bonds (financial-instrument sense):**
- "Accountability Bond (B)" is one side of the CS = RCV − B equation.
- Reclamation bonds, performance bonds, severance trust funds — the framework's concrete policy mechanisms.
- Direct lexical link between the title and the framework's formal math.

**Meaning #2 — Relational bonds (attachment / duty sense):**
- Bonds between generations (intergenerational duty).
- Bonds between communities and their land/ecosystem (place-attachment; Indigenous conceptions of kinship with land).
- Bonds between workers and their trade/community (labor solidarity).
- What Cost Severance breaks.

**Meaning #3 — Structural bonds (chemical / connective sense):**
- Connections that hold systems together — atmospheric bonds, ecosystem interdependencies, economic interconnections.
- When these bonds break, systems degrade non-linearly.
- Cost Severance operates by fraying these bonds without accounting for the cost.

**The polysemy is intentional and load-bearing.** Each meaning anchors a different face of the framework's argument. This isn't a "the title has many meanings, take your pick" vagueness; it's a title where each meaning is operating simultaneously in the book.

## §3. Test 1 — Individual term merits (polysemy verification)

### §3.1 Each meaning does work

**Accountability bonds (Meaning #1):**
- Appears in CS = RCV − B equation (B = accountability bond).
- Policy-actionable (reclamation bonds, community transition reserves, performance bonds).
- Direct lexical link between title and framework math.
- **Load-bearing verdict:** YES.

**Relational bonds (Meaning #2):**
- Chapter 1's knowledge-worker extension describes bonds between worker and family broken by 120-hour weeks.
- Chapter 7's Mars colony thought experiment describes the bonds the asteroid miner forms (and breaks) with colony, crew, Earth family.
- Chapter 10's reflective closing describes the bonds that would be restored under honest accounting.
- **Load-bearing verdict:** YES.

**Structural bonds (Meaning #3):**
- Ecosystem interconnections (Chesapeake food web, McDowell watersheds).
- Atmospheric / climate stability (bonds between carbon sinks and atmospheric equilibrium).
- Economic-community bonds (when an extraction industry leaves, the web of businesses + institutions unravels).
- **Load-bearing verdict:** YES.

**Verdict on §3.1:** all three meanings operate in the book. The polysemy is real and intentional.

### §3.2 Is polysemy a liability?

**Potential concern:** in academic vocabulary, multiple meanings can create ambiguity.

**Response:** each meaning is specific. Reader encountering "commons bonds" parses the contextually-appropriate meaning (accountability instrument in formal sections; relational in personal-narrative sections; structural in ecological sections). The polysemy enriches rather than confuses because contexts disambiguate.

**Parallel:** "capital" has multiple meanings in economics (financial, human, social, political). Academic writing uses "capital" without each mention requiring disambiguation because context handles it. Same for "commons bonds."

**Verdict on §3.2:** polysemy is asset, not liability.
