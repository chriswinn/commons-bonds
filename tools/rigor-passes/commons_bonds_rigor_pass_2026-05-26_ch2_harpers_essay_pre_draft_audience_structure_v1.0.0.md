## §2. Editorial-brain map for Harper's specifically

**What Harper's editors want (distinct from Boston Review, Aeon, Atlantic Ideas, Noema):**

- **Voice over apparatus.** Harper's is the magazine of the literary essay first. The argument must be carried by the prose, not by named instruments or institutional vocabulary. Harper's reader is reading for the essayist's sentences before they are reading for the essayist's argument; the argument has to earn its place inside prose that is doing literary work in every paragraph.
- **Scene-led opening.** Harper's essays open in scene. *"At 5:42 a.m. on the morning of..."* / *"The first time I saw a coal seam burn..."* / *"Robert Bailey was sixty-three years old when..."* The reader is placed in the world before the argument is named.
- **Single subject, deep.** Harper's tradition rewards the long, single-subject essay. Bill McKibben on tar sands; Wendell Berry on agriculture; Marilynne Robinson on the soul; recent Harper's essays running to 12,000+ words on Donna Tartt, on AI, on a single Appalachian community. The 6,000–15,000w envelope is real and rewarded — substance-drives-length (per `feedback_substance_drives_length.md`) but in this venue substance can go *long*.
- **Named-tradition fluency without name-dropping.** Mazzucato + Harvey + Caudill + Stoll + Catte are register-signals the Harper's reader recognizes when they appear in the right place. The discipline is selective deployment, with each citation doing a specific argumentative job — and the citations should appear in prose registers Harper's editors recognize (e.g., a citation embedded in a clause about the lineage of an idea, not a parenthetical academic citation).
- **Non-partisan framing with literary refusal of brief-register.** Harper's is center-progressive but consistently publishes essays that refuse partisan-brief register entirely. The essay must read as the writer working out the structural diagnostic in prose rather than as an op-ed marshaling evidence for a position. The Ch 2 Mechanism-Not-Motive frame is exactly the right register; preserve and intensify in the essay.
- **First-person permitted (in contrast to Boston Review).** The Harper's essayist's voice is part of the apparatus. First-person grounding ("I drove through McDowell County in October..." / "The first time I read Bailey's testimony I had to set the page down...") is on-brand if it grounds the argument. **The essay does not need to be first-person, but it can be**, and the literary-essay register typically benefits from at least intermittent first-person presence even when the structural spine is third-person reportage. 🔴 AUTHOR DECISION REQUIRED — DEFERRED TO RATIFICATION SESSION: **first-person presence in Ch 2 → Harper's essay**. The Ch 2 chapter itself uses first-person plural rarely ("we will see," "we need to understand") and does not have an authorial first-person presence; the chapter's voice is third-person reportage + cited interview material. The essay derivation has three reasonable options:
  - **Option A — Third-person throughout (preserve Ch 2 voice).** Authorial invisibility; the institutional analysis + Bailey + Latusek voices carry the essay. Closer to Boston Review register; reads as classical reportage essay.
  - **Option B — Limited first-person frame.** First-person opening scene (the writer arrives in McDowell; describes what he sees) + first-person occasional return (one or two scene-anchored returns at structural transitions) + third-person reportage spine through the rest. Closest to the Harper's literary-essay default register for this kind of essay.
  - **Option C — First-person throughout.** Authorial presence sustained across the essay's full arc. Closer to memoir register; risks reading as memoir-with-reportage rather than reportage-with-memoir. Stage 0 Candidate A explicitly named the venue's expectation as "single-subject deep memoir" register, which leans toward this option.
  
  **Recommendation: Option B (limited first-person frame).** Reasoning: (a) the Stage 0 Candidate A verdict named "deep memoir" register at the genre level but the underlying chapter's voice is third-person reportage; importing first-person sustained throughout the essay would change the chapter's voice rather than translate it. (b) Option B captures the Harper's-typical literary-essay register most cleanly — the writer's intelligence is felt in the prose, but the institutional analysis still does its work without being filtered through sustained authorial subjectivity. (c) The first-person opening scene anchors the essay in a specific moment of observation that the Harper's reader recognizes as the venue's signature opener move; the first-person occasional return at structural transitions provides voice continuity without crowding out the named-miner voices. (d) Author's lived bona fides (familiar with the place; the framework's intellectual provenance grounded in personal experience inside the systems it engages — per `manuscript/chapters/_AUTHORSNOTE_ON_WINDTUNNELS_AND_AI.md`) make Option B substantively earned rather than imposed; Option C would foreground the writer at the expense of the subjects in ways the discipline of Ch 2 specifically resisted. Author may choose Option A (stay closer to chapter) or Option C (lean fully into the memoir register Harper's named at Stage 0) but Option B is the recommended default carry-forward into Stage 2. *(See also §4 structural decisions, which assume Option B; author choice of A or C at ratification triggers a Stage 2 brief-update before Stage 2 fires.)*
- **Apparatus drops.** Per Stage 0 §4.1 apparatus-reveal cap RATIFIED at ~18–22% effective reveal: Ring-1 concept names (*cost severance* / *severed cost* / *value extraction*) may surface as plain-prose concepts; the brief MUST exclude all formal apparatus (no CS = RCV − B equation; no bond decomposition B₁ + B₂; no Three Ways of Counting; no Commons Inversion Test / CIT; no Four Gates; no Cᵢ vocabulary; no Accountability-Reckoning-Restitution / ARR; no named cost-component proper nouns). See §8 below for the full apparatus exclusion list.
- **Length discipline.** Target ~8,000–11,000w body in the brief's recommended middle of the Harper's band; let substance drive length per `feedback_substance_drives_length.md`. Ceiling 15,000w per Harper's editorial-band convention.

**What hurts Harper's submissions specifically:**

- **Apparatus jargon in body prose.** Greek letters, subscripts, formal three-component breakdowns, named-instrument acronyms — Harper's reader bails immediately.
- **Op-ed throat-clearing.** "In our current political moment, the question of how we account for industrial extraction has become..." — Harper's essays do not begin this way. Begin in scene.
- **Manifesto register.** "It is time to..." / "We must..." / "The future demands..." Harper's reads manifesto as evidence of weak argument. The Mechanism-Not-Motive register is the antidote.
- **Defensive author-register.** Long preemptive-rebuttal-of-objections passages. Harper's essays trust the reader; objection-handling happens briefly inside the prose's natural argumentative motion, not in enumerated rebuttal sections.
- **Three-in-a-row declarative LLM cadence.** Particularly dangerous in long-form; cumulative cadence over 10,000 words flattens. Vary sentence length aggressively (5-word + 25-word + 50-word sentences in the same paragraph where the argument supports it).
- **Em-dash density.** Per [`feedback_em_dash_overuse.md`](../memory/feedback_em_dash_overuse.md) RATIFIED 2026-05-21: em-dashes require active justification; default to commas / periods / restructure for smoother prose. Long-form essays compound the em-dash-as-tic-density more visibly than short-form.
- **Self-narration / meta-commentary.** "What the framework's analysis shows here is..." / "It bears noting that..." / "What this means is..." Harper's reader reads the prose, not the writer's report on the prose.
- **Throat-clearing rule-of-three closes.** "Distance enabled ignorance. Ignorance hid the cost. The cost still got paid." — the rule-of-three close is the LLM-cadence tell; restructure to mix rhythm.

### §7.7 SMCRA reclamation-bonding gap (1977–present)

- **Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act:** 1977. Required mining companies to post **reclamation bonds** — money set aside to restore the land after extraction. Theory: whoever creates the environmental liability funds the cleanup.
- **Acid mine drainage:** Begins when mining operations disturb sulfur-bearing rock; continues for **decades after the last mine closes**.
- **Documented gap (Ch 2 line 84):** Across Appalachian coal country, more than **633,000 acres require environmental remediation**. Estimated cleanup cost: **$7.5 billion to $9.8 billion**. Available bond money: **$3.8 billion**.
- **The gap:** **$4 billion to $6 billion** (Ch 2 line 86). *"not a rounding error. It is not a paperwork problem. It is the severed cost made tangible: the difference between what the industry promised to clean up and what it actually funded."*
- **2014–2016 bankruptcy-driven transfer:** Three major coal companies declared bankruptcy in this window, **transferring $865 million in liabilities directly to taxpayers** (Ch 2 line 86).
