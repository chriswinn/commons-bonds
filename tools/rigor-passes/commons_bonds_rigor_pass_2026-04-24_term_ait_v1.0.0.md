## §2. The question under test

### §2.1 The term

**AIT = Abundance Inversion Test.** The framework's epistemic methodology for identifying which costs are scarcity-grounded and therefore admissible to RCV computation.

**What AIT does (formal):** for a candidate cost C, imagine a counterfactual where the abundance-condition underlying C's scarcity is present (i.e., imagine what C depends on as abundant rather than scarce). If C vanishes under the counterfactual, C is scarcity-grounded and admissible. If C persists, C is not scarcity-grounded and not admissible.

**Named components:**
- "Abundance" — the counterfactual condition (inverted from the actual scarcity).
- "Inversion" — the logical move (reversing the scarcity condition to its abundance counterpart).
- "Test" — the epistemic procedure.

**Audit findings (per Principle #2 methodology):**

## §3. Test 1 — Individual term merits (name-mechanism match)

### §3.1 Does "Abundance Inversion Test" accurately describe what AIT does?

**The test's procedure:**
1. Identify the candidate cost C and the scarcity-condition S underlying it.
2. Invert S to its abundance counterpart A (imagine the counterfactual).
3. Evaluate whether C vanishes under A.
4. If yes → C is scarcity-grounded → admit to RCV.
5. If no → C is not scarcity-grounded → reject.

**Name components mapping:**
- "Abundance" → step 2 (the counterfactual state).
- "Inversion" → step 2 (the logical move from scarcity to abundance).
- "Test" → the full procedure.

**Match quality:** exact. The name describes the method precisely. "Abundance Inversion Test" means "a test performed by inverting to abundance."

### §3.2 Do alternative names describe the mechanism as accurately?

**"Scarcity-Grounding Test":** names the RESULT (whether a cost is scarcity-grounded), not the METHOD (how it's tested). A test for "scarcity-grounding" could in principle be performed multiple ways — direct observation, empirical measurement, AIT. Naming the test after the result creates ambiguity about the method.

**"Scarcity Test":** too generic. "Scarcity test" in general usage could mean testing for scarcity of a resource, testing a scarcity hypothesis, testing for scarcity-induced effects. The framework's specific method (counterfactual inversion) is not captured.

**"Abundance Test":** loses the INVERSION signal. A "abundance test" could be a test of something-about-abundance, not a test-by-inverting-to-abundance. The full form carries the method-description that the short form elides.

### §3.3 Test 1 verdict

Option A's name-mechanism match is exact; alternatives are either indirect (B), generic (C), or ambiguous (D). Principle #3 (misnaming is a rigor failure) check PASSES cleanly for AIT — the name does what it says.

**Test 1 verdict:** Option A preferred on name-mechanism match grounds.
