## M11 critic-survival probes against INCLUDE

### Probe A — "The defense engages five alternatives (legacy / aftermath / trail / long-run / persistent). Does the prose handle all five cleanly?"

Each rejection does specific structural work:
- *Externality legacy* — implies an inheritance with a recipient; carries obligation that the bond instrument handles separately.
- *Externality aftermath* — implies a bounded period after the event; structurally wrong for damage that continues indefinitely.
- *Externality trail* — implies a path that could be cleaned; the framework specifically rejects (no fund extracts carbon already in the sky).
- *Long-run externality* — structurally accurate but loses the distribution-shape claim.
- *Persistent externality* — names duration without naming shape.

Five distinct failures, five distinct alternatives. None reads as filler.

### Probe B — "The Weitzman 2009 citation grounds *fat-tail climate damages*. Does the framework's *tail* claim extend cleanly to non-climate damages (acid drainage; asbestos)?"

The paragraph claims the statistical-distribution loan applies across all damage substrates the framework counts. Acid mine drainage runs for generations; asbestos-related illness latency extends across multiple decades with continued mortality. The framework's distributional claim (cumulative cost dominated by the long-running tail rather than by immediate damage) is consistent across substrates. A climate-tail-risk specialist reads Weitzman as the climate-specific anchor; the framework's broader-substrate extension is defensible at body-prose register because the distributional-shape feature (non-vanishing magnitude at long durations) holds across substrates the paragraph anchors.

### Probe C — "The closing line ties the tail metaphor to the bond architecture. Is that scope-creep into the bond's defense?"

The closing line ("a bond posted against bounded aftermath could discharge when the aftermath ends; a bond posted against a tail must carry forward indefinitely, because the tail does not end on its own") closes the term-defense by naming what the metaphor enables. The bond-architecture argument is the framework's whole-book claim (already defended at Ch 5 line 192 in the why-bonds paragraph); the closing line here connects the externality-tail metaphor to that already-defended architecture. The connection reinforces both arguments without re-defending the bond. Scope-discipline holds.

### Probe D — "The heavy-tailed-distribution claim is technically a specific distributional assumption. Does the paragraph commit to a claim it can defend?"

The paragraph's specific claim is that cumulative cost is dominated by the long-running tail rather than by immediate damage — that is a heavy-tailed-distribution shape in the loose sense (tail mass dominates expected value). For carbon damage across centennial timescales, this is canonically defensible (Weitzman 2009 fat-tail; IPCC AR6 tail-risk). For acid mine drainage and asbestos illness, the claim is more loosely defensible — the damage continues for generations with non-vanishing magnitude, which is the tail-shape feature without committing to a specific heavy-tailed parameterization. The paragraph's body-prose register handles both — committing to the structural feature (non-vanishing magnitude at long durations dominating cumulative cost) without committing to a specific distributional parameterization. The Tech Appendix carries the formal specification if required.
