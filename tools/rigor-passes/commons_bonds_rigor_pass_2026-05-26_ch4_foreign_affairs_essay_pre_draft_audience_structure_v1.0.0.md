### §7.7 U.S. Social Security 1935 + Bismarck Germany 1889 comparative architecture

- **U.S. Social Security architecture (per Ch 4 lines 96–104):**
  - **Design year:** 1935.
  - **Pay-as-you-go system:** Current workers fund current retirees via payroll deductions.
  - **Surplus disposition:** Any surplus over annual outflow historically invested in **U.S. Treasury bonds** whose proceeds the federal government has spent on general operations (wars, tax cuts, budget gaps).
  - **Trust-fund mechanism:** "Accounting record of intra-government debt rather than a compounding pool of external capital" (Ch 4 line 98).
  - **Future-generation-pension dependency:** Depends on the **next generation's workforce**, not on a real reserve of saved-and-compounded wealth (Ch 4 line 98).
  - **1935 design context:** Middle of the Great Depression; immediate problem = destitution of an elderly generation with no pension reserves; political problem = buying support for transfer program from working generation (Ch 4 line 102).
  - **Design rationale:** Pay-as-you-go form chosen because it could deliver benefits immediately + political consensus to fund through current taxation was easier than consensus to capture-and-capitalize multi-decade pool (Ch 4 line 102).
- **Bismarck Germany 1889 partial-funding comparison (per Ch 4 line 104):**
  - **1889 Old Age and Disability Insurance system:** Partially-funded character.
  - **Survival:** Survived until **post-WWI hyperinflation of early 1920s**.
  - **Framework framing:** U.S. could have chosen — at 1935, or at subsequent reform moments — to capitalize a portion of payroll-tax revenue and compound it; the choice not to do so was function of which political coalition could be assembled when (Ch 4 line 104).
