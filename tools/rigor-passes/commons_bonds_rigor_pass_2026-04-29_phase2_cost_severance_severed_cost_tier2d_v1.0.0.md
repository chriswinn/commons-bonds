- **ADEQUATE** — the term lands but with friction; reader comprehension is adequate but the term is not load-bearing for this audience (typically not the term's primary cell).
- **WEAK** — the term has problematic readings or significant friction for this audience; risks distorting the framework's claim or signaling sloppy scholarship; conditional repair available via specific intervention (citation, bridge, footnote).

## §9. Severance-tax register — the Tier 2b adoption-travel opportunity

### §9.1 What severance tax is

A US state-level tax on the **severance of natural resources from the land** — i.e., on the act of extraction. 36 of 50 states levy severance taxes on some combination of: oil, natural gas, coal, minerals, timber, fish.

Examples:
- **Texas Tax Code Title 2 Subtitle I** — oil severance tax (4.6% of market value); natural gas severance tax (7.5% of market value).
- **Alaska AS 43.55** — oil and gas production tax (severance of resource from sub-surface estate).
- **Wyoming Statutes Title 39 Chapter 14** — severance tax on coal, oil, gas, trona, uranium.
- **North Dakota Century Code Chapter 57-51** — oil and gas gross production tax.
- **Montana Code Annotated Title 15 Chapter 36** — oil and gas production tax.

### §9.2 Why this is an adoption-travel opportunity for the framework

Severance tax is the **existing regulatory instrument that comes closest to what the framework's Cost Severance Damages (CSD) + Restitution Bond + Foreclosure Bond architecture is engineered to extend.** The framework can position itself as:

> *"Severance tax is what the existing legal vocabulary for this already does. It prices the severing of one specific class of resource (oil, gas, minerals, timber) from one specific source (the land). The framework's CSD extends this principle to the full set of commons that get severed by extraction — communities; ecosystems; future generations — and adds the bond-architecture (Restitution Bond + Foreclosure Bond) that makes severance accountability operable across that full set."*

This positioning is **highly favorable for Tier 2b adoption-travel** because:

- It anchors the framework in vocabulary that 36 state legislatures + thousands of tax lawyers + thousands of policy practitioners already recognize.
- It positions CSD as the bigger version of severance tax — not novel-out-of-nowhere but principled-extension.
- It establishes lineage hooks for regulatory adoption (severance-tax precedent).
- It survives the M11 critic ("isn't this just severance tax?") with: "yes, generalized; severance tax prices one form of severance; CSD prices the full set."

### §9.3 Why the current Ch 1 bridge plan misses this

Per [collision pass](commons_bonds_rigor_pass_2026-04-24_term_cost_severance_collision_v1.0.0.md), the Option C ratification 2026-04-24 was driven by the M12 collision concern around HR-severance specifically — that was the active worry at the time. The severance-tax register was not surfaced as a positive bridge-anchor because the rigor pass focused on the collision-as-liability problem, not the bridge-multipliers-available problem.

This Phase 2 audit surfaces the severance-tax register as a **complementary bridge-anchor that strengthens Tier 2b adoption-travel without weakening the existing Tier 2a bridge work.** It's additive, not replacing.
