**§15.1.6 — Commons-as-examples versus legislative enumeration**

**Movement 1 — the structural account, and what stays open.**

The framework does give a structural account of what a commons is. Definition 1.1 fixes it: a shared good drawn from a stock subject to scarcity dynamics, with a regeneration rate ρ ≥ 0, whose abundance masks a cost that its depletion or foreclosure makes legible. The account is deliberately not restricted to material stocks. ρ is a parameter, not a gate; ρ = 0 names the strictly non-renewable case and ρ > 0 the renewable one, and a good with no physical stock at all can still enter as a commons through the inversion alone, as the Autonomy and Epistemic commons do.

What the framework declines is something narrower and more specific: it does not close the substantive enumeration of commons. The ten categories it names — Habitability, Spatial, Temporal, Institutional, Kindred, Ecosystem, Political, Cohesion, Epistemic, Autonomy — are the categories that surfaced across the eighteen cases of this volume's research. They are a reproducibility record of what the method turned up, not a metaphysical claim about the complete set of commons that exist. The list is open at both ends. It is open to commons not yet imagined, and it is open to goods long taken for granted that a later scarcity reveals as commons after the fact. Air is the anchoring case: under abundance it registers as nothing, priced at zero, and only its scarcity makes its commons-character legible. The framework holds the enumeration open because the method, not the list, is what travels.

This is the same discipline an established lineage has used. Ostrom's design principles (Ostrom 1990, *Governing the Commons*) were offered as empirical regularities across hundreds of coordination-commons cases, not as a closed taxonomy of what governance principles must exist. Sen and Nussbaum's capabilities approach (Sen 1999, *Development as Freedom*; Nussbaum 2011, *Creating Capabilities*) elaborates a list of central capabilities while holding it explicitly open to refinement. Rawls's primary goods (Rawls 1971, *A Theory of Justice*) enumerate honestly without claiming closure. Each succeeded by being clear about what it had identified: examples drawn from cases, refinable, not a final inventory of what exists.

**Movement 2 — open in content, disciplined in form.**

Holding the substantive list open is not the same as holding the method open. The framework is open in *content* and disciplined in *form*. The analogy is the one decision theory and utility theory have long relied on: a theory can leave the content of a variable open — what enters as an outcome, a consequence, a state of the world — while fixing the axioms that any admitted content must satisfy. Savage's decision theory does not legislate which outcomes a decision-maker may consider; it constrains how any outcome, once admitted, must behave under the axioms. The framework's commons-content is open in exactly that sense, and its discipline lives in exactly the corresponding place: in the Commons Inversion Test and the Four Gates, which any candidate commons-grounded cost must pass, not in the word "commons" and not in the physical shape of the thing.

This is why one tempting slogan has to be refused. It does not follow, from the framework's openness, that anything assigned a dollar is thereby a commons. The dollar is the *output* of admission, never the *criterion* for it. A candidate cost earns a price only after it has cleared the inversion test and the gates against an established commons; the number is what the apparatus produces at the end, not the thing that lets it in at the start. Reverse that order and the framework would indeed collapse into circularity, pricing whatever it had already decided to price. The order of work is the safeguard: admission first, on structural grounds, and the dollar afterward, as the result.

**Rationale — why both movements are made.**

Both movements exist for a single reason, and it is worth naming as a rationale rather than as a third independent claim. What counts as a commons is contested across political and philosophical traditions, and the contestation is not a disagreement careful argument can settle; it is a divergence of starting points. Classical liberalism treats autonomy as an individual natural right and its supporting infrastructure as enabling but not commons. Civic republicanism (Pettit 1997, *Republicanism*; Anderson 2017, *Private Government*) treats autonomy as non-domination requiring shared institutions, and those institutions function as commons. Socialist and communitarian traditions treat the enabling conditions themselves as commons. Marxist analysis treats autonomy as material-conditions-dependent. Lived-oppression perspectives treat autonomy as something extracted, not shared. Indigenous political philosophies treat ancestral lands as relationally-personed, not as Western-property commons. A framework that legislated the ten as canonical would declare one tradition's commitments correct and the rest mistaken, and would smuggle its own political commitments in as a precondition for using its accounting. Keeping the enumeration open in content while disciplining the method in form is what lets the framework operate across these traditions without arbitrating among them. The universality is in the method, not in the list.

**Scope-of-applicability boundary at the extreme-scarcity end.**

At the extreme-scarcity end of the Autonomy commons — forced labor, slavery, coerced extraction — the framework reaches a boundary, and I want to be plain about where I stand at it rather than dress the limit up as a purely technical one. The framework's apparatus extends into these cases through the *legacy-effects pricing pathway*: the longevity gaps, wealth gaps, disruption of cultural-knowledge transmission, and intergenerational trauma that coerced extraction leaves behind, computed with the same Method 1 + Method 2 + Method 3 triangulation applied backward (see §5.5). The framework has room for accounting of this kind, and the people best placed to do it are already doing it. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not mine to do, and I leave that work to those who are continuing it: the reparations-economics scholars — Darity and Mullen (*From Here to Equality*, 2020), and the broader field of Hamilton, Coates, Conley, and others — who have spent decades building the methodology for exactly this. What the framework offers is the ledger. I record its structure and decline to fill in that column myself.

There is a second, narrower reason the direct pricing of the coercion vector itself sits outside this volume's scope, and it is methodological rather than moral. The framework's primary case-class operates through information-asymmetry vectors: cost-bearers proceed because they do not know what they will bear, as with NFL concussions, cigarette-industry concealment, opioid Appalachia, Libby vermiculite. Coerced extraction is structurally distinct, because cost-bearers know what they will bear and are forced to bear it regardless, and how to assign cost to the coercive dimension itself remains methodologically unresolved in the reparations-economics field. That open methodological question is real, but it is the secondary point. The primary one is the first-person abstention above: the ledger is here; the backward number is not mine to enter.

### Draft 4 — Freedom-as-commons illustration (chapter; Ch 6 placement recommended). Second-person hypothetical register.

Consider what it is to hold a freedom so completely that you never think to count it.

You take for granted the freedom to sleep in on a morning you have nothing to do. To pick up a hobby because it caught your interest. To go on a date, or to stay home with a book instead. None of these register as wealth. They are simply the texture of an ordinary day, and an ordinary day does not feel like a commons any more than the air in the room feels like a resource. Abundance is what makes a commons invisible. You only ever notice the floor when it is taken out from under you.

So imagine it taken out. Imagine that someone puts a collar on you and, by force, sets you to building their house or working their field, day after day, for the rest of your life. The hobby is gone. The date is gone. The morning you might have slept through is now owed to someone else before it begins. What was invisible because it was abundant becomes the most legible loss imaginable the moment it is foreclosed. This is the Commons Inversion Test run on a person rather than a place: invert the abundance, and the cost the abundance was hiding becomes plain.

There is a particular cruelty in the arithmetic of it. A human life is the strictest case of a non-renewable stock — irreversible, and of a quantity none of us actually knows. We carry an average life expectancy in our heads, but never our own true total, not until it is spent. Forced labor severs two commons at the same stroke: the autonomy of every coerced choice, and the lifetime itself, drawn down hour by hour with no rate of regeneration to replace it. Of everything this book prices, this is the one whose magnitude is hidden even from the one who bears it.

And here is the turn that makes the price undeniable. Suppose that, at any point across those years, you were told you could trade money for your freedom and the freedom of your family. I do believe that anyone, faced with that offer, would come to terms with the harsh reality of putting a price on it. The price is not abstract and it is not the framework's invention. History records it directly, in the established institution of manumission, of self-purchase: people did pay, in money, to buy their way out of bondage, and to buy their families out. The number was real because the freedom was real. A captive negotiating that price is not theorizing about whether autonomy is a commons. They are paying its scarcity value, in the only currency available to them.

What that does to the framework is to settle the most contested case from the inside. The hardest objection to calling freedom a commons is that freedom feels like a birthright, not a shared resource with a price. The manumission price answers it without argument. The price was paid; the freedom was bought; the commons was real all along, masked only by an abundance most of us are fortunate enough never to lose. What the framework does *not* do — what is not mine to do — is to compute the backward debt this leaves on the ledger. That accounting belongs to the people who are already doing it, and I leave it to them. The framework's contribution here is narrower and, I hope, more honest: it shows that the price of freedom is real, that its scarcity makes it legible, and that the ledger has a column for it. Who fills in that column, for whom, is not a question I will answer on anyone else's behalf.
