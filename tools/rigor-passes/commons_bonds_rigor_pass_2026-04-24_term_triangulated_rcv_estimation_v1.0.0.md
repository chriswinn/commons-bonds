## §2. The question under test

### §2.1 The methodology (per Block 2 of April 19, 2026 RCV formal-model session)

**Strategy:** Three independent methods approaching the same quantity (RCV) from different directions. If they converge, the number is robust. If they diverge, the divergence reveals exactly where the real disagreements live.

**Method 1 — Replacement Cost (Lower Bound):** What would it cost to provide the same service from the next-best source?
> *RCV_min per unit = Replacement cost from nearest substitute − Market price at extraction.*

**Method 2 — Revealed Preference via Norway Calibration (Middle Estimate):** Norway's sovereign-fund per-barrel value as anchor; sidesteps the discount-rate debate.
> *Per-barrel Norway capture − Per-unit comparison retention = Empirical measure of uncaptured RCV.*

**Method 3 — Scarcity-Adjusted Option Value (Theoretical Upper Bound):** Real-options theory specialized for declining substitutability; values the option to delay extraction under scarcity dynamics. Formal model specification pending.

Without rigor-tested estimation methodology, the framework can compute Cᵢ admission but can't defensibly produce RCV numbers — and Cost Severance (CS = RCV − B) requires defensible RCV.

## §3. Method 1 — Replacement Cost (sub-test)

### §3.1 Methodology

For a unit of resource R extracted at time t:
- Identify the next-best substitute S_R that provides the same service.
- Compute the cost of providing the service from S_R.
- Subtract the market price of R at extraction.
- Result: RCV_min per unit ≥ replacement-cost gap.

### §3.2 Boundary checks (Block 2 examples)

- **Sand:** highly substitutable, abundant. Replacement-cost gap ≈ 0. Method 1 produces low RCV. **Correct directional outcome** (sand extraction is low-stakes).
- **Helium:** essentially non-substitutable for cryogenics + superconductor applications. No synthetic production at scale. Replacement-cost effectively infinite. Method 1 produces severe RCV. **Correct directional outcome** (helium-foreclosure is high-stakes).
- **Coal vs renewables:** if a barrel of oil sold for $80 and equivalent energy from renewable cost $200, RCV_min ≈ $120/barrel. Defensible lower-bound floor.

### §3.3 Lower-bound rationale

Method 1 is a LOWER BOUND because:
- It captures only SUBSTITUTABLE component of RCV.
- Non-substitutable component (irreplaceable geological functions; uses-not-yet-invented) is unpriced.
- Method 1 prices what we KNOW we're losing; not what we don't yet know we'll need.

### §3.4 M12

**Replacement-cost methodology** is established in environmental economics:
- Costanza et al. 1997 — ecosystem-services valuation.
- Standard regulatory-economics cost-benefit analysis.
- Replacement-cost rule in eminent-domain takings law.

Framework adopts standard methodology + applies to RCV estimation. Honest M12 extension.

### §3.5 Verdict on Method 1

**PASS.** Methodologically sound + boundary-check appropriately differentiates + lower-bound positioning correct + M12 honest.

## §4. Method 2 — Norway Revealed Preference (sub-test) — REQUIRES REFRAMING

### §4.1 Methodology

Norway's Government Pension Fund Global accumulates from petroleum revenues per Hartwick-rule investment of resource rents.
- Norway fund value attributable to petroleum ÷ total barrels extracted = Per-barrel captured value.
- For comparison case (e.g., Appalachian coal): Per-unit retained value (essentially zero or negative for Appalachia).
- Gap between Norway capture and comparison retention = Empirical measure of uncaptured RCV.

### §4.2 The "sidestepping the discount rate" claim (Block 2)

> *"It completely sidesteps the discount rate debate. You're pointing at a real country, real money, real outcomes. The difference is a number, not a philosophy argument."*

This is methodologically clever — Norway's institutional architecture has effectively REVEALED a per-barrel scarcity-premium-internalization that doesn't depend on Stern-vs-Nordhaus discount-rate choice. **The number is empirical, not philosophical.**

### §4.3 The required reframing per Open Insight #14

Open Insight #14 (just ratified): Norway is the **canonical existing real-world exemplar** of B (or now B2) approaching RCV — but **Norway didn't fully capture RCV either**. Norway's Hartwick savings is impressive but bounded by:
- Climate damages from oil consumption (borne globally; Norway's fund doesn't price this).
- Future-uses-not-yet-invented (option value Method 3 captures; Method 2 doesn't).
- Pre-fund extraction (Norway started full Hartwick savings ~1990; earlier extraction is unpriced).

**Therefore:** Norway's per-barrel capture is itself a LOWER BOUND on true RCV — not a true middle estimate.

### §4.4 Reframing within triangulation

Method 2 functions as MIDDLE ESTIMATE WITHIN THE TRIANGULATION because:
- Method 1 (Replacement Cost) captures less than Norway captures (Norway internalized more than just substitutable replacement-cost).
- Method 3 (Option Value) captures more than Norway captures (theoretical upper bound including future-uses option value).
- Method 2 ≤ true RCV; positioned BETWEEN Method 1 and Method 3 within the triangulation.

**Recommended Tech Appendix passage:**

> *"Method 2 is a Norway-anchored middle estimate within the triangulation framework — Method 2 ≥ Method 1 (Norway internalized more than substitutable replacement cost) AND Method 2 ≤ Method 3 (Norway didn't capture future-uses option value). True RCV ≥ Method 2; the triangulation reveals where between Norway's actual capture and the theoretical upper bound true RCV most plausibly lies. Norway's role is calibration anchor, not absolute-middle. Per Open Insight #14 (epistemic-humility discipline ratified 2026-04-24), Norway's sovereign wealth fund is the most-impressive existing exemplar of Hartwick savings + B2 (Resource-Foreclosure Bond) approaching RCV — but Norway itself bounded by climate-cost externalization + future-uses uncertainty."*

This integrates Open Insight #14 epistemic-humility into Method 2 framing cleanly.

### §4.5 M12

**Revealed-preference methodology** is established in:
- Consumer economics (Samuelson revealed-preference theory).
- Environmental valuation (revealed-preference for ecosystem services).
- Regulatory economics (revealed-preference for safety, time, etc.).

Framework adopts revealed-preference + applies to commons-Hartwick-savings calibration. Honest M12 extension.

**Hartwick 1977** lineage already noted in Hotelling Identity rigor pass — Norway's fund operationalizes Hartwick's rule.

## §5. Method 3 — Scarcity-Adjusted Option Value (sub-test) — REQUIRES SPEC

### §5.1 Methodology (Block 2 framing)

Scarcity-adjusted option value applies real-options theory (Dixit-Pindyck 1994) to extraction decisions under declining substitutability + future-use uncertainty.

The "option to delay extraction" carries value when:
- Substitutability is uncertain (might decline; might improve).
- Future uses unknown (resource may have applications not yet invented).
- Extraction is irreversible.

**Block 2 reference to parameters σ, α, V_option** suggests:
- σ = substitutability rate (or volatility of substitutability).
- α = scarcity-rate parameter.
- V_option = option value of preservation.

### §5.2 What Tech Appendix v0.0.5 must specify

Block 2 doesn't publish the formal model. Tech Appendix v0.0.5 must include:
- **Mathematical specification:** the formula for scarcity-adjusted option value (presumably Dixit-Pindyck-style with declining-substitutability adjustment).
- **Parameter calibration approach:** how σ, α, V_option are estimated for case-study cases.
- **Upper-bound rationale:** why Method 3 is an upper bound on RCV (theoretical maximum given conservative substitutability projections + full option-value preservation).

### §5.3 Why upper bound

Method 3 captures the THEORETICAL MAXIMUM RCV under three conditions:
1. Substitutability assumed conservatively (i.e., low S(t|t₀) projections).
2. Future-uses option value fully internalized.
3. Discount rate at framework's preferred low rate (Stern-aligned ~1.4%).

Under these assumptions, Method 3 produces the largest defensible RCV estimate. Methods 1 and 2 produce smaller estimates (due to substitutable-component-only constraint and Norway-capture-bounded-by-real-instruments respectively). **Method 1 ≤ Method 2 ≤ Method 3 ≤ true RCV.**

### §5.4 M12

**Real-options theory** lineage:

- **Real-options applications in natural-resource extraction:** Brennan & Schwartz 1985; Black & Scholes 1973 (option-pricing foundation).

Framework adopts real-options theory + specializes for declining-substitutability + integrates with Substitutability Function S(t|t₀). Honest M12 extension.

### §5.5 Verdict on Method 3

## §6. Triangulation discipline (combined-methods test)

### §6.1 Convergence/divergence diagnostic

The framework's claim: if Methods 1, 2, 3 produce RCV estimates within a narrow range, the number is robust. If they diverge widely, the divergence reveals where real disagreements live.

**Logical soundness:** correct. Triangulation is standard validation methodology; convergence reduces uncertainty in unbiased measurements; divergence diagnoses model-specification differences.

### §6.2 What divergence patterns reveal

Per Block 4 sensitivity-analysis flagging:
- **If σ (substitutability volatility) dominates:** real debate is about substitutability; M2/M3 disagreement reveals different substitutability assumptions.
- **If α (scarcity-rate) dominates:** real debate is about scarcity dynamics.
- **If V_option dominates:** real debate is about future-uses uncertainty.

Each divergence-pattern produces a useful framework finding (knowing where the real argument is).
