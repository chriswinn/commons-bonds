### F-V1 — Verbatim named-inventory match: "This is the structural risk:" (HIGH)

**Location:** Line 194.
**Verbatim text:**
> "*One of the most important ways people connect with the Chesapeake Bay,* Colden has said, *is through eating seafood and fishing.* Without watermen, *there's certainly a lot fewer eyes on the water and a lot fewer people caring passionately about the Bay.* **This is the structural risk: that the very people whose attention has held the Bay accountable for centuries are being squeezed out of the relationship by the same accounting that is, at the species level, beginning to recover what was lost.** The recovery and the squeeze are happening at the same time. They are the same process, viewed from two sides of the ledger."

**Pattern category:** Meta-commentary tic — verbatim-class match against the Stage 3 framing prompt's explicit named inventory: *"This is the load-bearing observation"-class meta-commentary*. The colon-introduced "This is the structural risk:" follows the named pattern (cf. Ch 1 v2.0 Amendment B voice-polish concerns at lines 85 + 103; cf. canonical Pass 3.2 framing for §5.1).

**Severity rationale.**
- (a) Direct verbatim-class match against the prompt's *explicitly named inventory* (the voice-polish framing flagged "This is the load-bearing observation"-class meta-commentary as the high-priority category for this Pass 3.2 fire).
- (b) Position is the chapter's structural-paradox closing pivot (lines 192–196), the most rhetorically-loaded passage in the chapter — a tic at this position is editor-detectable on first read.
- (c) The "structural risk" framing names what the prose just argued (in the Colden quotes that precede it) rather than letting the prose argue it; the post-colon clause IS the substantive observation, but the "This is the structural risk:" frame announces the move rather than executing it.
- (d) Trade-press readers in 2026 are calibrated to scan for AI-prose meta-commentary patterns; this construction is on the named list.

**Per-finding format (Amendment C) — Options + Recommendation + Reasoning:**

- **Option A — Drop the meta-frame; let the substantive clause stand.** *"The very people whose attention has held the Bay accountable for centuries are being squeezed out of the relationship by the same accounting that is, at the species level, beginning to recover what was lost."* Removes the "This is the structural risk:" frame entirely; the substantive claim becomes a declarative sentence carrying its own weight. Lightest spot-fix; preserves all substantive content.
- **Option B — Replace the meta-frame with a scene-anchor pivot.** *"On the dock, both facts are visible at once: the very people whose attention has held the Bay accountable for centuries are being squeezed out of the relationship by the same accounting that is, at the species level, beginning to recover what was lost."* Trades the meta-frame for a concrete observational pivot ("on the dock, both facts are visible at once"). Preserves the pivot-rhetorical function; lands the next clause through observation rather than through abstraction.
- **Option C — Restructure the paradox-restatement as content.** *"The same accounting that is beginning, at the species level, to recover what was lost is squeezing out the very people whose attention has held the Bay accountable for centuries."* Collapses the meta-frame; lands the chiasmus (lost / recover; squeeze / hold-accountable) as a single sentence with internal mirror. Heavier rewrite; tighter rhetorical line.
- **PRESERVE option (held-as-is).** Defensible as deliberate structural-naming move at chapter-spine pivot. The "structural risk" frame does load-bearing work in a chapter centered on accounting-frame argumentation. Author may judge that the named-inventory match is acceptable cost for the clarity of the structural-pivot signal. Reader for whom the chapter is the entry-point to the book may need this kind of explicit framing.

**Recommendation:** **Option A.** Lightest possible touch; the substantive clause is the chapter's central paradox-naming and stands on its own as a declarative; removing the meta-frame strips the named-inventory match without losing substantive content. The "two sides of the ledger" closer that follows (line 194 final sentence) already supplies the structural-pivot signal through the metaphor; the "This is the structural risk:" frame is redundant atop it.

**Reasoning:** Pass 3.2 named-inventory matches at HIGH severity are the highest-confidence spot-fix calls; the prompt explicitly listed this pattern class. Option A's lightest-touch resolution preserves all substantive content + the chapter's rhetorical pivot; the redundancy with the "two sides of the ledger" closer means the meta-frame is doing duplicative work. PRESERVE-option is available if the author judges the meta-frame load-bearing for general-trade-press readers who need the structural-pivot signal explicit; the HIGH-severity weight argues for Option A unless the author has affirmative reason to hold.

### F-V3 — Em-dash density carry-forward (Stage 1a M2) at line 38 (MEDIUM)

**Location:** Line 38.
**Verbatim text:**

**Pattern category:** Em-dash density — three em-dashes in 13 words. Stage 1a invariant scan flagged this as a known density-tic pattern; HOLD-FOR-PASS-3.2 disposition per brief §1a.3 M2.

**Severity rationale.**
- (a) Three em-dashes in 13 words is a density-tic that an editor will register on first read — the named pattern category.
- (b) The em-dashes do parallelism work (four-clause progressive imperative "Take some / leave the rest / let the rest spawn / come back next year"); the rhythm enacts the cyclical-harvest discipline the sentence describes.
- (c) The sentence sits at the chapter's first sustainable-harvest-principle naming (the "instrument" frame); removing the rhythmic em-dash structure would diminish the cadence's enactive function.
- (d) Cumulative chapter em-dash density: the chapter uses em-dashes throughout (lines 10, 12, 18, 20, 47, 69, 75, 87, 103, 107, 113, 117, 119, 125, 131, 138, 154, 156, 184, 200, 212, 226 — high overall count), but line 38's three-in-13-words is the densest single-sentence concentration.

**Per-finding format (Amendment C) — Options + Recommendation + Reasoning:**

- **Option A — Replace em-dashes with commas; preserve the four-clause rhythm.** *"Take some, leave the rest, let the rest spawn, come back next year."* Drops em-dash count from 3 to 0; preserves the four-clause progressive cadence and the imperative-rhythm; commas read more conversationally and match the chapter's other imperative-cadence moments.
- **Option B — Convert two em-dashes to commas; preserve one for emphasis.** *"Take some — leave the rest — let the rest spawn, come back next year."* OR *"Take some, leave the rest, let the rest spawn — come back next year."* Drops em-dash count from 3 to 2 or 1; preserves stronger punctuation at the cadence-emphasis points; lightest-touch variant.
- **Option C — Replace with period-and-fragment cadence.** *"Take some. Leave the rest. Let the rest spawn. Come back next year."* Drops em-dash count to 0; replaces the connective-tissue with full-stop cadence; reads as four short imperative-fragments; cadence enacts the discipline more starkly. Heaviest stylistic change.
- **PRESERVE option (held-as-is).** Defensible as deliberate rhythmic enactment of the cyclical-harvest principle. The em-dashes do active work — the four clauses are not parallel sentences but a continuous-thought sequence, and em-dashes signal the continuity that periods or commas would interrupt. Reader experience: the sentence reads as a single breath of guidance; the em-dashes are the breath-marks. The Stage 1a invariant flag is procedurally legitimate (3 em-dashes in 13 words is the density threshold) but the substantive judgment is that the density is load-bearing here.

**Recommendation:** **PRESERVE option (held-as-is).** The em-dashes are doing rhythmic-imperative work that commas or periods cannot reproduce; the four-clause sequence is one continuous breath of fishing-discipline guidance, not four parallel statements. The cadence-as-content is the strongest possible defense for em-dash density, and the Ch 1 Pass 3.2 model held similar cadence-enactive em-dash work (cf. Ch 1 F-V7 "He never raised his voice. He did not even speak at a normal volume. He spoke a hair below it" — held as cadence-enactive). The Stage 1a M2 finding is correctly classified as MEDIUM (procedural flag, substantive defense available); the right disposition is HOLD-AS-IS with the audit-trail noting the consideration.

**Reasoning:** Pass 3.2's job is to surface the named-density pattern (which the Stage 1a scan did correctly); Pass 3.2 / author's job is to weigh procedural-pattern against substantive function. Here, the substantive function — rhythmic enactment of harvest-cycle discipline at the chapter's first sustainable-harvest-principle naming — outweighs the procedural density flag. PRESERVE is the recommended disposition; the audit-trail (Stage 1a → Pass 3.2 → PRESERVE) is the value-added discipline. If author judges otherwise on aesthetic grounds, Option B (one em-dash retained for emphasis) is the lightest-touch alternative.

**Cross-reference to Stage 1a M2:** Per brief §1a.3 M2, the disposition was HOLD-FOR-PASS-3.2-VOICE-POLISH-RE-FIRE. This finding closes that hold: disposition = **PRESERVE-AS-IS** (substantive function defended; procedural density flag acknowledged in audit-trail).

### F-V4 — Six-fold "He may [verb]" anaphora cluster at line 170 (MEDIUM)
