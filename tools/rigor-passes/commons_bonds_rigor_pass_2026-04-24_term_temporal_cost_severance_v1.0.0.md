## §2. The question under test

### §2.1 The concept

"Temporal Cost Severance" names the phenomenon where value is captured now while costs are borne by future generations — the intergenerational dimension of cost severance. Author articulated 2026-04-24: *"Temporal Cost Severance was illustrative of cost severance not felt now but by future generations."*

### §2.2 The options

**Option A — Retain "Temporal Cost Severance"** as glossary term + prose proper-noun. Status quo.

**Option B — Rename to "Intergenerational Cost Severance"** as capitalized glossary term + prose proper-noun. Sharpens the name; aligns with framework's dominant adjective.

**Option C — Retire the capitalized term.** The concept persists in prose as "intergenerational cost severance" (lowercase, descriptive phrase). No glossary entry. "Cost severance" is the capitalized flagship; "intergenerational" is a modifier describing a specific subtype when relevant.

### §2.3 Sub-claims per option

**Option A sub-claims:**
- SC-A1: "Temporal" is an adequate adjective for the phenomenon.
- SC-A2: The framework's current 6 references demonstrate the term's utility.

**Option B sub-claims:**
- SC-B1: "Intergenerational" is sharper than "Temporal" for this phenomenon.
- SC-B2: A capitalized proper-noun compound is warranted for adoption-anchoring.
- SC-B3: Prose economy benefits when the phrase recurs.

**Option C sub-claims:**
- SC-C1: The framework's 109 "intergenerational" uses demonstrate the concept is already carried in prose.
- SC-C2: "Intergenerational cost severance" reads naturally as a lowercase descriptive phrase without needing proper-noun treatment.
- SC-C3: Vocabulary footprint reduction serves success-criterion alignment (fewer competing named terms).
- SC-C4: The RCV formula's integration from t₀ → ∞ mathematically encodes intergenerationality already.

### §2.4 Falsifiers

**Option C is falsified if:**
- Retiring the capitalized term creates prose-economy losses that outweigh the footprint-reduction gain.
- Legal/policy adoption of "intergenerational cost severance" would benefit from a capitalized-proper-noun version.
- The concept's sub-type status (Principle #3 candidate) requires explicit naming to maintain precision.

**Option B is falsified if:**
- The capitalized compound adds to vocabulary footprint without delivering adoption-anchor benefit sufficient to justify.

**Option A is falsified if:**
- "Temporal" is confirmed weaker than "intergenerational" for the phenomenon (audit shows 18× usage ratio favoring intergenerational).

**Verdict below (§9):** Option A falsified on adjective-choice rigor + framework-usage evidence; Option B falsified on vocabulary-footprint cost; Option C survives.

## §4. M4 — Craft

### §4.1 Adjective strength

"Temporal" is a weak adjective for the intended phenomenon:
- Temporal can mean *any* time-related aspect (short-term, long-term, seasonal, diurnal).
- Intergenerational is specific — across generations, across populations-that-don't-yet-exist.

The audit confirms the framework's prose already prefers "intergenerational" by 18× margin (109:6). Prose craft argues for intergenerational language.

### §4.2 Prose-economy test

**Option A:** "McDowell's Temporal Cost Severance runs to $X per ton across 100 years."

All three parse. Option B and C are equally readable; Option B adds capitalization overhead without semantic gain. Option C is lightest.

### §4.3 Rhetorical register

Capitalized proper-noun compounds ("Intergenerational Cost Severance") read as formal-technical. Lowercase descriptive phrases ("intergenerational cost severance") read as accessible natural language. The book's rhetorical strategy favors accessibility; Option C aligns.

**Verdict on M4:** Option C PREFERRED. Option B defensible. Option A fails on adjective strength.

## §6. M6 — Academic rigor + Principle-#3 (variant-subtype) check

### §6.1 Variant-subtype check (Principle #3 candidate)

**Question:** is "Temporal Cost Severance" a genuine subtype of Cost Severance, or a distinct phenomenon being mis-absorbed into the Cost Severance concept?

**Cost Severance definition:** the structural mechanism by which extraction separates value capture from cost bearing.

**Test:** can "value captured now; cost borne by future generations" be characterized as "extraction separates value capture from cost bearing"? Yes — with the separation operating across a generational gap. The cost-bearers are future generations who cannot represent themselves in the present value-capture event.

**Contrast with Spatial Cost Severance:** Spatial was mislabeled because the phenomenon (costs appearing/vanishing with spatial distance) was actually abundance-cost mechanics, not severance mechanics. Removing spatial distance didn't undo severance; it changed which abundance-costs applied.

**Contrast result for Temporal/Intergenerational:** removing the generational gap (imagine value-capturers who live forever and bear their own long-term costs) WOULD undo this type of severance. That's the severance-mechanism test passing. Intergenerational Cost Severance IS a genuine subtype.

**Conclusion:** Principle-#3 check PASSES. This is a real subtype, not a misnaming.

**But:** passing Principle #3 doesn't require dedicated named vocabulary. It only confirms the concept is a legitimate subtype. Whether it needs a name is a separate question.

### §6.2 Academic precision

"Intergenerational" is a well-established term in academic discourse:
- Intergenerational equity (economics/law)
- Intergenerational justice (philosophy)
- Intergenerational ethics (climate literature)

Using "intergenerational cost severance" (lowercase phrase) connects the framework to this discourse without inventing new proper-noun compound. Academic readers will recognize the composition.

"Temporal Cost Severance" doesn't connect as cleanly to existing discourse because "temporal" isn't the standard adjective in intergenerational-ethics literature.

**Verdict on M6:** Option C PREFERRED on academic-precision grounds. Option B acceptable. Option A weak.
