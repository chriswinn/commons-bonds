## §2. The question under test

### §2.1 The naming drift

Per inventory + cluster vocabulary rigor pass §10.4:
- Ch 8 line 105 subsection header: *"Knowledge and Cultural Cost"*
- Glossary v3 line 373 (retired 8-tier list): *"Knowledge and Culture Cost"*

The two forms differ in part of speech of the second element: C1 uses "Cultural" (adjective) while C2 uses "Culture" (noun).

### §2.2 What the term refers to

Per Ch 8:107-113, the term refers to a Cᵢ-class extraction cost composed of two facets:
1. **Practical/ecological knowledge** — community-embedded knowledge that disperses with community dispersal (*"how to read the seams, how to manage risk on a specific geological formation, how to keep a specific valley's water system functioning, how the weather behaved in that particular hollow over a particular century"*).
2. **Cultural continuity** — *"the congregations, the civic organizations, the networks of mutual aid that could absorb a personal crisis because everyone knew everyone."*

Ch 8 prose explicitly treats the two as *compounding-together*: *"the loss compounds."* Replacement cost is named as not-zero but unpriced-by-default-accounting.

### §2.3 Three candidates

- **C1 — Knowledge and Cultural Cost** (Ch 8 form)
- **C2 — Knowledge and Culture Cost** (Glossary v3 form)
- **C3 — Split into separate Cᵢ entries** ("Knowledge Cost" + "Cultural Cost" as two distinct names)

### §2.4 Out of scope

- Whether the underlying Cᵢ category itself is well-conceived (it's already in the Cᵢ illustrative-list per terms_index lines 402, 411, 431; chapter prose treats it as load-bearing on Ch 8's argument)
- Whether the term needs a dedicated terms_index entry (per Insight #32 batch ratification, deferred until cross-chapter recurrence emerges; current footprint is single-chapter Ch 8)
- Pricing methodology (Ch 8 sets $1/ton placeholder; not in scope of naming question)

## §5. M11 critic-survival probes

### §5.1 Candidate C1 — M11 probes

**Critic C1.1 — "Mixed parts of speech in coordinated position is grammatically awkward."**

Response: True. C1 uses "Knowledge" (noun) + "and" + "Cultural" (adjective) coordinated before "Cost." English permits elliptical compound coordination ("Knowledge [Cost] and Cultural Cost") but the structure is non-standard. Mitigation: chapter context (Ch 8:107-113) explicitly treats the two facets as compounding-together; reader follows the elliptical reading. Some readers may stumble on first encounter; subsequent uses follow. M11 SURVIVES with minor reader-cognitive-load cost.

**Critic C1.2 — "Why 'Cultural' adjective but not 'Knowledge-al' adjective? The asymmetry is awkward."**

Response: "Knowledge" doesn't have a standard adjective form (no widely-accepted "Knowledge-al" — "epistemic" is the closest standard adjective but carries philosophical-epistemology connotations that distort framework meaning). The asymmetry is structural-linguistic, not authorial inconsistency. Framework's choice is to use "Knowledge" as bare noun + "Cultural" as adjective because that's what English vocabulary supports; the asymmetry is an artifact of the language. M11 SURVIVES.

**Critic C1.3 — "What does 'Cultural Cost' mean separately from 'Knowledge Cost' in the elliptical reading?"**

Response: Per Ch 8:107-111 chapter prose, the two facets are: (1) practical/ecological knowledge (community-embedded, disperses with community dispersal); (2) cultural continuity (congregations, civic organizations, networks of mutual aid). Both are real distinct facets of the same compounding loss. Reader who parses elliptically gets two-component cost; reader who parses as compound modifier gets single combined loss — both readings produce framework-meaningful interpretation. M11 SURVIVES.

**Critic C1.4 — "Tier 3b academic-trade copy editor will likely flag this and suggest C2."**

Response: Real concern. Mitigation: pre-submission editorial review can defend C1 explicitly by citing the lived-oppression-reading + Tier 2d indigenous + heterodox-alignment criteria + Ch 8 prose context. Editor may agree or insist on C2; framework can defer to editor on this naming question without losing framework meaning. M11 SURVIVES with editorial-review-defense protocol.

### §5.2 Candidate C2 — M11 probes

**Critic C2.1 — "'Knowledge and Culture Cost' could read as 'cost of knowledge and culture' (single combined loss, abstract)."**

Response: Yes — that's the parallel-structure reading. Abstract framing may be adequate for academic register but loses some concreteness for chapter prose discussing specific Appalachian community dispersal. Tradeoff: parallel-structure cleanness vs prose concreteness. M11 SURVIVES with caveat on prose register.

**Critic C2.2 — "'Culture' as bare noun is less idiomatic than 'Cultural' adjective in cultural-economics + policy-economics literatures."**

Response: True. Standard usage in cultural-economics: "cultural heritage," "cultural capital" (Bourdieu), "cultural goods," "cultural sovereignty" — all use adjective form. "Culture cost" is more idiomatic in cost-accounting register (e.g., "company culture cost"). Framework's intended register is cultural-economics + policy-economics, not cost-accounting. M11 ADEQUATE with literature-fit caveat.

**Critic C2.3 — "Are knowledge and culture really one combined cost or two separate costs?"**

Response: Same question applies to C1. Per §4.3 + Ch 8 chapter prose, the conceptual unity (loss compounds) supports single Cᵢ; splitting into two costs is rejected per parsimony. M11 SURVIVES.
