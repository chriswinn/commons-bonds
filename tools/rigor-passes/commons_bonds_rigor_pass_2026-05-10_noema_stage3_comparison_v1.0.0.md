This criterion is gating for Noema specifically (the magazine's "we only publish original content" rule).

### 3.2 Structural integrity

- Both follow the Stage 1 §4 seven-section structure. Word-target compliance is comparable.
- Essay A's Section II preserves the air-compressor anecdote as a full scene (load-bearing per v1.0.0 audience-load §2.1: "Lock"). Essay B compresses the air-compressor to a one-sentence summary of what the grandfather "talked about" — the scene's ✓✓ concrete-detail register is lost.
- Essay A's Section IV uses three cases (Libby + 2008 + Deepwater); Essay B uses two (Libby + 2008), in line with v1.0.0's "drop Deepwater" recommendation. B is slightly more aligned with the audience-load brief here.
- Both essays preserve the title-earning Fox Hill 3am mutual-aid scene. B's transition into it ("My father's people work this water.") is a single-sentence pivot that reads well; A's setup is more elaborate.
- Winner: **Mixed.** A wins on Section II air-compressor scene preservation; B wins on Section IV case-count discipline and Section VI Fox Hill pivot.

### 3.3 Hook strength (Section I opener)

- Essay A: Declarative-into-narrative ("A plane window at sunrise has been the place where I think most clearly for as long as I have done this work."). Establishes voice authority then narrativizes.

- Winner: **Tied — different strategies, both work.** B's variant is slightly more reader-pulling; A's is slightly more declarative-confident. Either works for Noema.

### 3.4 Close strength (final paragraph)

- Essay A: Dense framework restatement ("What honest accounting offers is the chance to see the costs that are still ahead of us — the ones we have not yet locked in, the ones a different architecture could yet refuse to absorb on someone else's behalf. - Essay B: Spare emotional landing ("I write this for my son. I write it for the ones who come after. The math has been quiet for a long time. It does not have to stay quiet.")
- Winner: **Mixed.** A wins on framework-restatement; B wins on emotional landing. The "I write this for my son" line in B has stronger blurb potential than anything in A's close.

### 3.5 Framework legibility

- Both load-bearing; both clean. Essay A retains the "It is not a moral charge against any individual actor. It is an accounting charge against an architecture" beat (rhetorically distinctive); Essay B drops it in favor of the tighter "The point of naming cost severance is not to scold."
- Winner: **Tied.** Different rhetorical choices; both legible.

### 3.8 Factual fidelity to author truth

This criterion was not in the v1.0.0 audience-load framework or the Stage 1 brief, but it surfaced during the Path B audit as a real concern with B that A does not have.

Essay B introduces several reconstructions where the audience-blind drafter (lacking Ch 1 as source text and improvising from beats) generated prose that diverges factually from the author's canonical narrative:

1. **Pou/Pooh nickname etymology rewritten.** Essay B Section II opens: "The nickname was older than the cartoon. He picked it up as a small boy at his grandfather's knee in coastal Virginia, where his grandfather had called him *Pou* in a soft Tidewater accent that ran the syllable into something half-French. The spelling drifted later. The sound stayed." This invents a backstory not present in Ch 1, the Stage 1 brief, or the §7 carry-forward inventory. The canonical Pou/Pooh story is the sister's-son-pointing-and-naming origin where the toddler said *Pou* trying to say *Pappou*; the grandfather had been called *Pooh* separately. Essay B has merged these and invented a Tidewater-accent provenance. **Likely fabrication risk.**

2. **Father's predawn-Appalachian drives reframed.** Essay A and Ch 1 frame these as the father teaching the son to hunt. Essay B frames them as the father's commute to a Navy field office — "a hundred and twenty-five miles before dawn into the Appalachians, to a smaller field office where parts of his work landed." This may be true (the father may have done both) but is not canonical per the source narrative; the audience-blind drafter invented the commute framing.

3. **"Sat in fifteen audiences" reframed.** Ch 1 and Essay A treat the "fifteen" as fifteen distinct reader audiences for whom the author had to make NIH research accessible (single audience-spectrum range). Essay B reframes this as "the first month I sat in fifteen audiences with senior scientists whose work I was not yet competent to evaluate" — i.e., fifteen meetings/audiences. Different referent; different scene.

4. **"Responsible for forty-five people / million dollars."** Essay B says: "By the time I left I was responsible for forty-five people and a budget of about a million dollars." Ch 1 says the project had been "planned for a team of forty-five people, one year, one million dollars" — i.e., the original scope of the project before the author cut it back. Essay B's framing reads as if the author personally managed forty-five people, which is not what Ch 1 says.

5. **Cussing scene redirected.** Ch 1: "the thing I remember is cussing at myself for not having clicked send before sleep took me." Essay B: "I sent an email at three in the morning that I do not remember writing, in which I cursed at a person whose work I respected, because the sleep had taken me before the send button had registered." Essay B has the author cursing at the recipient; Ch 1 has the author cursing at himself. Different scene; the moral weight is different.

6. **Air compressor scene omitted.** This is not a factual drift but a structural gap: the audience-blind drafter, lacking Ch 1's anecdote in front of them, rendered "air compressor in the garage" as a one-sentence summary of what the grandfather "talked about" rather than reconstructing the workshop / drill-bit / piston-machining anecdote that v1.0.0 audience-load identified as a Lock for the Section II quiet-competence pattern.

Winner on this criterion: **A, significantly.** A's Ch 1-derived prose is factually faithful to the author's narrative throughout. B's reconstructions introduce 5+ factual drift points and one structural omission, all of which need correction at Stage 3 polish before submission. (This is not a Stage 2 drafter failure — Stage 2 was instructed to draft audience-blind from beats. It is a structural feature of the experiment: audience-blind flow drafting from beats rather than from prose loses some factual fidelity. Stage 3 polish is the designed counterweight.)
