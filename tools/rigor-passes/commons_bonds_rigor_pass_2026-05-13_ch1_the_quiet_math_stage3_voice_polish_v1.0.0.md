## §1. Pass-2 scope reminder

Pass 2 audits prose for the LLM tics + voice issues trade-press editors flag. The named-inventory categories applied below:

- **Rule of three** — "A. B. C." constructions; flag if >2 in chapter.
- **Em-dash crutches** — em-dashes used as connective tissue rather than as deliberate parenthetical extension or punch.
- **Tidy parallels** — "He did X. She did Y. They did Z." structures.
- **Hedge phrases** — "I will argue that…" / "It seems likely that…" / "Perhaps…"
- **Connective-tissue clichés** — "in short" / "ultimately" / "moreover" / "furthermore" / "that being said"
- **Meta-commentary** — "That is the whole sentence." / "What I mean to say is…" / "Let me explain…"
- **Expository flatness** — "The plain definition is this:" / "Here is what I think:" / "The argument is simple:"
- **Declarative-three-in-a-row** — three short declaratives in succession with no sentence-length variance.
- **Nostalgia / sentimentality tics** — "There are not many people like that anymore" / "Back when…"
- **Cadence repetition** — "It changed me. It humbled me. It made…" patterns.
- **Apparatus residue** — Greek letters, subscripts, integrals, acronyms appearing in body prose where Ch 1's plain-language register prohibits.
- **Memoir-register breaks** — sentences that read third-person-analytical where surrounding prose is first-person-narrative.
- **Tense-consistency breaks** — present-tense leakage in past-tense scenes (or vice versa).

Ch 1's specific emphasis per the workstream handoff: memoir-register consistency; sentence-length variance; first-person tense consistency; named-subject register (son anonymized; father anonymized; grandfather as intimate "Pooh" in Ch 1, named formally only in AuthorsNote).

### F-V1 — Standalone nostalgia tic; named-inventory verbatim match (HIGH)

**Line 35:**
> "There are not many people like that anymore."

Stands alone as a one-line paragraph at the §2 close, between the Pou nickname-passing beat (line 33) and the §-break before "I learned to work from my father."

**Why this flags.** The sentence is a verbatim match against the Pass-2 inventory's named example of the *nostalgia / sentimentality tic*. The grandfather-uncommonness has already been built concretely on the page — air compressor rebuilt without comment, drill bits sharpened without comment, "Pou" nickname passing through three generations. The standalone closer reaches for an emotional landing the prior paragraphs have already earned. It does the wrong kind of work: where the chapter elsewhere shows-not-tells, this line tells what the surrounding scenes have already shown.

**Severity rationale.** (a) Named-inventory verbatim match — the exact phrase appears in the Pass-2 template as a flagged example. (b) The standalone-paragraph framing magnifies the tic. (c) §2-closer position is structurally load-bearing — a trade-press editor will notice the move. (d) Trade-press readers in 2026 are calibrated to scan for AI-prose sentimentality patterns; this line is on the named list.

**Recommended spot-fix (author selects one option):**

- **A. Cut.** Drop line 35 entirely; let line 33's Pou-nickname beat carry the section's close, with the section break supplying the silence.
- **B. Replace with a concrete return to the scene's substance.** *"He spent his whole life like that — machining what needed machining, sharpening what needed sharpening, and not thinking anything of it."* Reprises the workshop-scene concretes; earns the closer by naming what's uncommon in specific terms.
- **C. Truncate to interior-thought register.** *"I think about him often."* Strips the sentimentality posture; reads as quiet personal-fact.

### F-V2 — Cadence-repetition tic; named-inventory verbatim match (HIGH)

**Line 55:**
> "There is a particular look on a child's face when she realizes that maybe one day a child will not have to be sick the way she has been sick. I saw that look. **It changes you. It humbles you. It makes the work feel like the kind of work worth not sleeping for.**"

**Why this flags.** Verbatim match against the Pass-2 inventory's named example of the *cadence-repetition tic*: *"It changed me. It humbled me. It made…"*. The chapter's prose has the same syntactic anaphora, the same pronoun, the same three-verb-cadence — just tense-shifted to second-person-generalizing. Editors recognize this cadence as the AI-prose reach for affective landing through formulaic repetition rather than through specificity. The substantive insight (genetic-disease work giving a sick child the imagination of a different future) is among the chapter's strongest beats; the LLM-cadence undercuts it.

**Severity rationale.** (a) Named-inventory verbatim match. (b) Sits at the chapter's first emotional-stakes-naming moment (the NIH child-face beat). (c) The cadence is the failure mode — the substance is excellent. (d) A reader scanning for tics will catch the rhythm before catching the substance.

**Recommended spot-fix (author selects one option):**

- **A. Collapse to a single integrated sentence.** *"That look is the reason I stopped sleeping."* Strips the three-beat repetition; lands the work-meaning through specificity rather than through cadence-formula.
- **B. Cut the middle, vary length on the third.** *"I saw that look. It changed me, and made the work feel like the kind of work worth not sleeping for."* Drops *"It humbles you."* and integrates the third clause as a compound. Minimum disruption; named-inventory tic dissolves.
- **C. Re-write the affective claim as content.** *"I saw that look. I remember it more clearly than I remember most of what I did at the NIH. It is the reason I stopped sleeping through stretches of those years."* Trades cadence for specificity; lengthier; foregrounds memory-as-anchor.

Either A, B, or C preserves the NIH child-face beat's substantive load while removing the named tic.

### F-V4 — Em-dash density + structurally-heavy NACA → NASA appositive at line 29 (MEDIUM) [post-F-3 finding]

**Line 29:**
> "He had spent the first half at NACA — the agency that became NASA in 1958, when Eisenhower signed the act — at NASA Langley, in Hampton, Virginia. […] He was a model maker in the wind tunnels at Langley — a title he was proud of — and an inventor with his name in the patent record."

**Severity rationale.** The em-dashes are individually defensible; the cumulative density + the post-F-3 sentence-shape combine into the cadence-burden a trade-press editor would flag.

**Recommended spot-fix (author selects one option):**

He worked at NASA Langley, in Hampton, Virginia."* Removes the first em-dash pair; restores reader-flow; the trailing locative now sits in its own sentence.

Compress the appositive.** *"He had spent the first half at NACA — the agency that became NASA in 1958 — at NASA Langley, in Hampton, Virginia."* Drops *", when Eisenhower signed the act"* (date is in the appositive's first phrase). Cuts em-dash-bracketed material by half. Reorder.** *"He had spent the first half at NASA Langley, in Hampton, Virginia — the agency was called NACA when he started, and became NASA in 1958, when Eisenhower signed the act."* Locative leads; the historical detail trails; em-dash pair becomes one explanatory clause rather than an interjection between subject and place.
