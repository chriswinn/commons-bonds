## §1. Executive summary

**RECOMMENDED: Option A — Keep both terms with clear role discipline.** Cost Severance and Severed Cost are *distinct-but-related*, not fully duplicative. They serve different grammatical roles and different rhetorical purposes in the framework. Retiring either term would create a prose/argument gap the surviving term cannot fill cleanly.

**The decisive finding:** the two terms operate in different lexical slots. **Cost Severance is the mechanism name (process noun, framework-internal analytical term, used 227×).** **Severed Cost is the result noun / public-adoption phrase (what a labor lawyer writes in a brief, used 37×).** Collapsing either loses a specific rhetorical capacity the framework needs.

- Collapsing to Cost Severance alone (Option B) loses the flagship public-adoption phrase — "severed cost" is the success-criterion vocabulary target; retiring it is self-sabotage.
- Collapsing to Severed Cost alone (Option C) loses the mechanism-name — *"the severed cost mechanism"* reads awkwardly; analytical prose needs the process noun.
- Keeping both with disciplined role-assignment (Option A) preserves both capacities without vocabulary proliferation beyond what already exists.

**The apparent duplication is a semantic overlap at the edges, not full duplication.** Consider: *"injury"* (the event) and *"the injured person"* (the subject). They describe the same phenomenon from different angles but serve distinct grammatical needs. Same here.

## §2. The question under test

### §2.1 The author's hypothesis

Author 2026-04-24: *"I'd argue the word groups (Value Extracted & Value Capture) as well as (Cost Severance & Severed Cost) are very very duplicative and are likely needing to be triaged."*

The Value-Capture-vs-Extraction triage ran; Option B (Extraction) recommended. This pass runs the analogous triage on Cost Severance vs Severed Cost.

**But the underlying claim may be different for this pair.** Value Capture and Value Extraction were (per author's own admission) "interchangeable" and "duplicative in the meaning they represent." Cost Severance and Severed Cost *appear* to be duplicative by surface reading, but may be structurally distinct in a way Value Capture / Value Extraction were not.

### §2.2 Semantic structure

**Cost Severance:**
- **Grammar:** compound noun; "severance" is a noun derived from "sever" (verb).
- **Role:** names a process / mechanism / phenomenon.
- **Usage pattern:** "McDowell suffers cost severance" (subject experiences mechanism). "Cost severance operates through..." (mechanism has operation).
- **Lexical slot:** process noun, often subject of analytical sentences about the mechanism.

**Severed Cost:**
- **Grammar:** adjective-noun compound; "severed" is a past participle modifying "cost."
- **Role:** names a specific instance / result / quantity that has been severed.
- **Usage pattern:** "McDowell bears severed cost of $550/ton" (subject carries a specific quantity). "The severed cost shows up in..." (result has presence).
- **Lexical slot:** result noun, often object or quantified predicate in accounting sentences.

**The distinction:** one names the phenomenon's process; the other names the phenomenon's result as a specific quantity. These are not interchangeable in English grammar — one cannot easily say *"McDowell suffers severed cost"* (the verb "suffer" pairs with the process noun) or *"McDowell bears cost severance"* (slightly unnatural — bearing implies a specific burden, not a mechanism).

### §2.3 Options (widened per Principle #2)

**A — Keep both with clear role discipline.** Cost Severance = mechanism/process noun; Severed Cost = result/quantity/adoption-anchor noun. Current framework structure; this pass tests whether it holds up.

**B — Retire Severed Cost; use Cost Severance for both mechanism and quantity.** *"McDowell's cost severance is $550/ton"* for the quantity; *"cost severance operates"* for the mechanism. Unified single term.

**C — Retire Cost Severance; use Severed Cost for both result and mechanism.** *"McDowell's severed cost is $550/ton"* for the quantity; *"the severed cost mechanism operates"* for the mechanism. Unified single term.

**D — Rename to differentiate more explicitly.** E.g., "Severance Mechanism" (for process) vs "Severance Amount" or "Severed Cost" (for result). Makes the distinction linguistically explicit at cost of vocabulary weight.

**E — Different terminology entirely** (either or both). E.g., replace Cost Severance with "Cost Displacement" and Severed Cost with "Displaced Cost." Tests whether the "sever" root is optimal.

**F — Placeholder for other candidates** — space for any alternative that surfaces.

### §2.4 Sub-claims

**Option A sub-claims:**
- SC-A1: The two terms serve distinct grammatical functions (process noun vs result/quantity noun).
- SC-A2: Both are in active use (227 CS, 37 SC) and the usage patterns follow the grammatical distinction.

### §2.5 Falsifiers

**Option A is falsified if:**

- The adoption target shifts to making Cost Severance the flagship instead of Severed Cost.

**Option B is falsified if:**
- Success-criterion adoption depends on "severed cost" remaining the flagship.
- Cost Severance alone can't cover the quantity-noun role without awkwardness.

**Verdict below (§17):** Option A survives; B/C/D/E fail on specific structural grounds.

## §3. Test 1 — Individual term merits

### §3.1 Cost Severance — individual merits

- Process noun; names the mechanism.
- Descriptor used across methodology discussions, case studies, Technical Appendix, rigor passes.
- Lexical parallel to other process nouns in economics (externality creation, price discrimination, rent-seeking).
- Prose role: subject or predicate of analytical claims about the mechanism's operation.
- Adoption trajectory: used by academics writing about the framework; unlikely to be adopted as a legal-brief term (too abstract).

**Strengths:** precise mechanism-name; analytically versatile; framework-internal workhorse.

**Weaknesses:** abstract; doesn't quantify; less adoption-friendly in legal/policy discourse than its result-noun counterpart.

### §3.2 Severed Cost — individual merits

- Result noun; names the specific quantity.
- Descriptor used in case-specific pricing claims, flagship adoption target.
- Lexical form "adjective + cost" parallels other priced phenomena (externality cost, social cost, marginal cost).
- Prose role: object, quantified predicate, subject of specific-dollar-amount claims.
- Adoption trajectory: **this is the framework's success-criterion phrase.** The labor lawyer writes "the severed cost of $X" in a brief; doesn't write "the cost severance of $X" as naturally.

**Strengths:** concrete; quantifiable; adoption-friendly; flagship vocabulary for legal/policy use.

**Weaknesses:** less analytically-versatile; the modifier structure ("severed") doesn't carry the process-naming that "severance" does.

### §3.3 Test 1 verdict

Both terms earn their place individually. They occupy different lexical slots and different adoption trajectories. Neither dominates — they complement.

**Test 1 verdict:** Option A (keep both) preferred.

## §4. Test 2 — Causal-chain bridge performance

### §4.1 The chain (per the Value-Capture-vs-Extraction pass methodology)

*Value Extraction → Cost Severance → Severed Cost*

Three positions:
- **Position 1 (event):** Value Extraction
- **Position 2 (mechanism):** Cost Severance
- **Position 3 (result):** Severed Cost

Each position has a grammatically-natural term. The chain reads as a logically ordered sequence: the extraction event produces the severance mechanism, which produces the severed cost result.

### §4.2 Test: what happens if we try to collapse positions 2 and 3?

**Option B (Cost Severance for both mechanism and result):**

*Value Extraction → Cost Severance → Cost Severance* 

Redundant chain. Must rephrase position 3 to quantity: *"producing a cost severance of $X"* — which works grammatically but loses the distinction between mechanism-operating and specific-quantity-observed.

In book prose, a chapter that wants to describe both *"Cost Severance operates through..."* and *"McDowell's [quantity] is $550/ton"* would have to use "cost severance" in both places with different implicit meanings. Readers must disambiguate per context. Cognitive cost.

**Option C (Severed Cost for both mechanism and result):**

*Value Extraction → Severed Cost → Severed Cost*

Same redundancy. And *"the severed cost mechanism"* reads more awkwardly than *"the cost severance mechanism"* — English grammar prefers the process noun for the mechanism role.

**Option D (rename to differentiate):**

*Value Extraction → Severance Mechanism → Severance Amount* 

Works grammatically but introduces new terminology; loses "severed cost" as the flagship adoption phrase.

### §4.3 Test: what does Option A (keep both) deliver?

*Value Extraction → Cost Severance → Severed Cost*

Natural three-step chain. Each position has a grammatically-natural term. No rephrasing required. No cognitive cost.

**Additional chain position:** the framework has an unspoken fourth position — **the accountability bond B and the CS = RCV − B equation** — where "severed cost" lives as the quantity that CS computes. The chain extends:

*Value Extraction → Cost Severance → Severed Cost → RCV − B = CS*

Where:
- Severed Cost is the result (the bill the community bears).
- RCV computes the true intergenerational cost.
- B is the accountability bond covering part of that cost.
- CS = RCV − B is the computed severance.

Both Cost Severance and Severed Cost appear in the full chain; they are NOT interchangeable at any position.

### §4.4 Test 2 verdict

**Option A decisively wins the causal-chain test.** The chain requires both terms; collapsing either introduces rephrasing burden or awkward grammar. The framework's vocabulary architecture already matches the causal structure; retiring a term breaks the architecture.

## §10. M6 — Academic rigor

### §10.1 Precision

Having both terms preserves semantic precision: mechanism-naming separate from result-naming. Collapsing either option creates ambiguity in analytical prose where the distinction matters.

### §10.2 Alignment with academic conventions

Economics routinely distinguishes mechanism names from result names:
- Externality (mechanism) vs externality cost (result)
- Rent-seeking (mechanism) vs rent (result)
- Price discrimination (mechanism) vs discriminated price (result)

Keeping Cost Severance + Severed Cost aligns the framework with this academic convention.

### §10.3 Principle #3 (misnaming / duplicate check)

Does Cost Severance name a real phenomenon? Yes — the mechanism of cost-from-capturer being severed.

Does Severed Cost name a real phenomenon distinct from Cost Severance? Yes — the specific quantified cost that resulted from the severance.

These are NOT duplicates. They are two grammatical forms referring to the same underlying phenomenon from different analytical perspectives (process vs result). This is standard English and standard economics usage.

**Principle #3 verdict:** PASSES for both terms. Neither misnames; both are appropriate grammatical forms of the same root concept.

**Verdict on M6:** Option A preferred.
