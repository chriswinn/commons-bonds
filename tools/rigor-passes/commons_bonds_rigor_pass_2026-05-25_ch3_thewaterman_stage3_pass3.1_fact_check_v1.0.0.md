### F-FC7 — McMenamin "1879 vacuum-sealing process" claim (HIGH)

**Location:** Line 18 (Hampton seafood-anchor paragraph; added in F-V21).
**Verbatim text:**
> "Hampton was known up and down the coast as Crabtown. **An 1879 vacuum-sealing process invented here** put cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in tin and shipped it to tables far from the Bay."

**Canonical truth.** Per multiple sources:

- **Site Selection Magazine** (Bruns): "McMenamin Crab Co. founder James McMenamin invented and patented the modern process of canning and sealing meats in air-tight containers **in the 1870s** and gave Hampton its 'Crab Town' nickname."
- **Living Places (Hampton City Living Places)**: "By **1880** McMenamin & Company had accomplished with crabs what Darling and Son had done with oysters. McMenamin perfected a canning process which allowed the crabmeat to be stored indefinitely and shipped around the world."
- No source consulted in this session explicitly confirms the **specific year 1879** for the process or the specific US patent number; the date may be defensible but is not directly verified.
- No source consulted describes the process as **vacuum-sealing**. The technical descriptions are "canning and sealing meats in air-tight containers" / "a canning process which allowed the crabmeat to be stored indefinitely." Vacuum-sealing is a specific technical method (removing air before sealing); the historical sources describe heat-process canning + airtight sealing without specifying a vacuum step.

**Severity:** **HIGH.** Two related problems:

(a) The specific year "1879" is not directly confirmed; sources describe the invention as falling in "the 1870s" with crab-canning operational "by 1880." The 1879 date is plausibly within range but is presented in the chapter as a specific year-stamp.

(b) "Vacuum-sealing" is technically distinct from "canning and airtight sealing" and not supported by the sources. A food-history-knowledgeable reader will challenge.

**Recommended spot-fix options:**

- **Option A (soften both year + process):** "Hampton was known up and down the coast as Crabtown. **In the 1870s, James McMenamin invented and patented a canning process** that put cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in airtight tins and shipped it to tables far from the Bay."
- **Option B (verify the 1879 patent first):** Author obtains McMenamin's actual patent number + date from USPTO records or Hampton History Museum; if a 1879 patent is confirmed, preserve the year. The technical-process language should be changed regardless of year-verification — "vacuum-sealing" is not the historically accurate term.
- **Option C (minimal touch on technical-process language):** "Hampton was known up and down the coast as Crabtown. **An 1879 airtight-canning process invented here** put cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in tin and shipped it to tables far from the Bay." Preserves the year (with implicit caveat); fixes only the "vacuum-sealing" → "airtight-canning" terminology.

**Recommended:** **Option A.** Aligns date + technical-process language with the sources I could verify; introduces the inventor (James McMenamin) by name, which strengthens the historical-anchor function of the paragraph. Per Stage 1 brief §6.1 + the named-subject discipline, McMenamin is deceased (died 1901), historical-record subject — naming is permitted without consent gate.

The chapter's current phrasing introduces "sea-level rise" alongside "erosion." This violates the existing per-chapter discipline.
