## 5. Voice register (ratified)

- **Third person throughout.** No first-person memoir. No lived-experience anchoring. The author is invisible; the institutional analysis carries the essay.
- **Institutional-measurement register.** Concrete numbers anchored to institutional decisions. Named institutions (W.R. Grace; BP; EPA; SEC; Social Security Administration; Federal Reserve; Treasury; Federal Black Lung Benefits Program; Norway GPFG). Dated events.
- **Direct, plain prose.** No academic hedging ("it might be argued that..."). No throat-clearing meta-commentary ("the framework's response is..."). The argument is stated directly.
- **Sentence-length variance.** Mix 5-word and 25-word sentences. Avoid declarative-three-in-a-row LLM cadence ("It changed me. It humbled me. It made me see...").
- **No rule of three.** No em-dash crutches. No "in short" / "ultimately" / "what survives across these objections is" connective tissue.
- **No LLM tics.** Avoid: "There is something..." / "It is not lost on me..." / "We have to ask ourselves..." / "It bears noting..." / "In the end..." / "At its heart..."
- **Civic-republican vocabulary lands directly.** "Domination" / "non-domination" / "standing obligation" / "future generations" — use them in their precise senses, attributed where appropriate.
- **Apparatus drops.** Plain English throughout. The framework's load-bearing claim for the essay is: cost severance is the structural mechanism by which extraction value is captured by one party while costs are absorbed by another with less power to refuse the transfer. That is the only definition the essay needs.
- **No first-person plural "we" except where the civic-republican move requires it.** "What we owe each other" is the load-bearing collective register; "we have seen that..." is the throat-clearing register to drop.

### 7.1 Libby, Montana

- **Operator:** W. R. Grace and Company.
- **Operation:** Vermiculite mine in Libby, Montana, operated 1963–1990 (27 years).
- **Production share:** Roughly 80% of the world's vermiculite during the operation's window.
- **Product use:** Zonolite-brand insulation installed in an estimated ~35 million American homes; also sold as soil conditioner.
- **Contamination:** Vermiculite at the Libby deposit was contaminated with a particularly lethal form of asbestos (tremolite).
- **Internal knowledge:** Internal documents released during later litigation showed Grace had identified asbestos contamination as early as 1963 and measured exposure levels in workers throughout the mine's operation. Worker, family-member (dust on clothing), and child (mine waste-pile exposure) disease was internally documented before the company continued mining for 27 more years.
- **Revenue:** ~$100M cumulative lifetime mine revenue.
- **Documented costs:** $4B+ across medical care; environmental remediation; legal settlements; lost property values; ongoing health monitoring. **Cost-to-revenue ratio: 40-to-1 minimum.**
- **Human toll:** 400+ Libby residents have died of asbestos-related disease. ~3,000 diagnosed with asbestos-consistent lung abnormalities. Mortality rate from asbestosis 40-80× the national average.
- **EPA designation:** EPA declared the entire town a public health emergency — the first time EPA had ever made such a declaration for a man-made disaster.
- **Corporate exit:** W.R. Grace declared bankruptcy in 2001. Assets reorganized; shareholders and executives moved on.
- **Criminal prosecution:** 2005 DOJ federal grand jury indictment of W.R. Grace and seven senior executives on conspiracy charges to violate the Clean Air Act and obstruct federal investigation of the contamination. The indictment was the largest environmental criminal prosecution in American history at the time and named specific executives by name and by role.
- **Acquittal:** 2009 — after a 13-week trial, all defendants (corporation + seven named executives) were acquitted.
- **Framework framing:** The acquittal does not mean the contamination did not happen, that the executives did not know, or that the cost severance did not occur. It means the criminal-law architecture available to the prosecution was not structurally suited to a case involving accumulated decisions across decades. The cost-severance accounting operates at the structural-mechanism level the criminal law could not reach.

### 7.2 Deepwater Horizon

- **Date:** April 20, 2010 (platform explosion).
- **Operator:** British Petroleum (BP); platform operator at the Macondo well in the Gulf of Mexico.
- **Workers killed:** 11.
- **Oil released:** 4.9 million barrels over 87 days.
- **Pre-disaster BP Gulf revenue:** ~$60B in the five years leading up to the disaster.
- **Macondo well projection:** 40–60 million barrels over lifetime; $3–4B at 2010 prices.
- **Direct economic costs to BP:** ~$21B in federal and state fines and settlements (largest environmental penalty in US history at the time); ~$20B in compensation fund for affected businesses and individuals; ~$60-65B total documented BP cost.
- **Ecological costs (NOAA estimates):** 4–8 billion oysters killed; 2–5 trillion newly hatched fish killed; tens of thousands of birds and sea turtles; tourism revenue losses >$7B in three years following the spill; property-value declines along affected coastlines ~$12B.
- **Total aggregate economic losses:** >$150B (academic, insurance-industry, federal sources).
- **Cost recovery ratio:** $60B BP cost vs. $150B+ total cost = **40% recovery**. The framework's accountability target is 100%; Deepwater paid 40%.
- **Mexico asymmetry:** BP's settlement with the Mexican federal government in 2018 totaled ~$25M — three orders of magnitude smaller than US settlements for comparable per-mile-of-coastline damage. Asymmetry was a function of legal architecture (Oil Pollution Act 1990 + state-level tort + class-action infrastructure in the US; substantially weaker cross-border environmental architecture in Mexico for foreign-corporation claims), not contamination distribution.
- **Framework framing:** The 40% ratio is the documented, court-validated, settlement-ratified empirical magnitude of the accountability gap for one of the largest environmental disasters in American history.

### 7.3 Baotou, Inner Mongolia (reference-only in the BR essay)

- **Production:** ~60% of world rare earth elements; the seventeen metals underwriting nearly every piece of advanced electronics on Earth.
- **Waste ratio:** ~10 tons of wastewater per ton of rare earth oxides produced (heavy metals, acids, radioactive materials).
- **Annual industry revenue (China rare earth):** $10–12B; Baotou facilities ~half.
- **Documented health effects:** Elevated rates of cancer, respiratory disease, birth defects; agricultural land within ~10km radius no longer suitable for farming; peer-reviewed studies in *Environmental Pollution*, *Ecotoxicology and Environmental Safety*, *Science of the Total Environment* document elevated heavy-metal concentrations (lead, cadmium, thorium, uranium) in soil, water, biomarkers.
- **Remediation estimate:** >$100B (conservative).
- **Industry environmental spending:** <1% of revenue.
- **Framework framing:** Spatial cost severance through global supply chains; the consumer at the other end is a structural participant, not a villain.
- **BR essay use:** Reference-only in §II (one or two sentences as part of the pattern-across-cases compression); do not develop at chapter-depth in the essay.

### 7.4 2008 financial crisis

- **First risk transfer:** 1978 Congressional creation of the 401(k) retirement-account structure; replacement of defined-benefit pensions over the following two decades. Market risk, longevity risk, and behavioral risk moved from institutional balance sheets to individual workers without pooling, hedging, or insuring capacity.
- **Second risk transfer:** 2001–2008 mortgage-backed securities (MBS) and collateralized debt obligations (CDOs); instruments packaged into mutual funds and target-date funds sold inside 401(k) accounts. When the instruments collapsed in 2008, losses flowed into the accounts that had absorbed the first transfer.
- **TARP:** $700B in public funds to stabilize financial institutions.
- **Federal Reserve emergency lending:** $16T cumulative across the crisis (per GAO 2011 audit; figure emerged through later Congressional requests and litigation).
- **Foreclosures:** ~10 million American households received foreclosure filings 2008–2012; ~5 million resulting in completed foreclosure. (More households affected than there are in the entire state of Pennsylvania, in under five years.)
- **HAMP:** Home Affordable Modification Program reached a small fraction of eligible applicants; administrative failures documented in multiple GAO reports.
- **FCIC conclusion:** Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission (2011) concluded the crisis was the result of "human action and inaction — not forces beyond human control."
- **Subprime origination playbook:** Originator commissions tied to volume rather than default rates; broker-disclosed terms differed materially from contract terms; adjustable-rate products marketed to borrowers whose understanding of rate-reset mechanics was, by industry-internal acknowledgment, expected to be incomplete. CFPB post-crisis enforcement record documents the pattern at scale.
- **Sweden 1930s comparison:** 1931 krona devaluation (restored export competitiveness and household-debt purchasing power); 1933 Riksbank bank-rescue paired with mortgage-modification programs; 1934–1937 public-works employment programs; 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement institutionalized welfare-state architecture. Comparative-banking-crisis literature anchors: Kindleberger 1973; Eichengreen 1992. Demonstrates bank-rescue + household-relief is not structurally either-or.
- **Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (2010):** Created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB); established Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC); restricted proprietary trading via the Volcker Rule; imposed enhanced capital and liquidity requirements on systemically important institutions.
- **Post-2017 institutional weakening:** CFPB enforcement record contracted; Volcker Rule substantially relaxed in 2018–2019; systemic-importance designations contested, narrowed, in some cases reversed.
- **TARP repayment objection (handle in compression):** TARP direct outlays substantially recovered through repayments and asset disposals (GAO + CBO accountings). Framework's response: TARP is the wrong accounting unit; the cost severance the chapter prices is the unrecovered welfare loss to ~10M foreclosed households, displaced workers whose 401(k) balances reset to lower bases that compounded forward, regional economies contracting around foreclosure cascades, and political-trust commons that absorbed the asymmetric-rescue pattern.
- **BR essay use:** §II as one of the pattern cases (one paragraph compression — the foundational claim is the asymmetry between trillions mobilized for institutions vs. nothing comparable for foreclosed households); §V as one Restitution Bond application.

### 7.5 Social Security

- **Design year:** 1935. Pay-as-you-go system; current workers fund current retirees via payroll deductions; surpluses invested in special-issue Treasury bonds.
- **Demographic transition:** Baby boom generation began reaching retirement age in 2008.
- **Outflow > inflow:** 2021.
- **Trust-fund depletion projection:** 2033 (under current law and current demographic projections).
- **Post-2033 benefit-coverage:** Unless Congress acts, the program will pay ~77% of promised benefits.
- **Foregone-return magnitude:** $108T (compounded foregone return on 88 years of payroll-tax surpluses, comparing Treasury-IOU routing against a broad US equity index alternative across the same window). Framework anchor in Ch 5; verify Treasury-IOU-vs-equity-index magnitude with a fact-check note before submission (the figure is internally consistent with the chapter's calculation but represents a counterfactual; Stage 3 fact-check pass should flag for sourcing if used in the essay).
- **Scale framing:** ~3× the entire current federal debt; ~4× annual US GDP; ~$300K per person in the country (including children).
- **Mechanism:** Severance accomplished through time rather than space; value-extracting party (politicians and voters who extracted electoral value of benefit expansions in the 1960s–1980s) systematically separated from cost-bearing party (future-generation taxpayers funding the demographic-deficit window).
- **Norway GPFG contrast (Ch 4 cross-reference):** Government Pension Fund Global; channels petroleum rents into sovereign-fund structure that compounds across decades. Contrasting architectural choice on structurally similar intergenerational problem; choice is contingent, not inevitable.
- **Public-finance-prudence objection (handle in compression):** Treasury-bond mechanism was administratively defensible; cost severance is upstream of the portfolio question — it sits in the obligation-generation mechanism (benefit expansions enacted by Congresses whose members would leave office before the demographic-transition obligations matured).
- **Timeline-tractability objection (handle in compression):** Funding gap is mathematically closeable through adjustment (payroll-tax rate; cap removal; benefit-formula; eligibility-age). Framework response: the adjustments themselves transfer the obligation onto the cost-bearing party. Tractability is not absence of severance; it is severance unfolding on schedule.
- **BR essay use:** §II as one of the pattern cases; §VI as the principal Foreclosure Bond application (the demographic-deficit-window obligation is exactly what a Foreclosure Bond would have discharged forward against).

### 7.6 Healthcare commons (reference-only in the BR essay)

- **GoFundMe growth:** 2011–2020, annual number of US medical fundraising campaigns grew 25-fold; by 2020, more than a third of all GoFundMe campaigns were for medical expenses.
- **2016–2020 campaign data:** 438,000 medical campaigns; over $2B raised from 21.7M donations; only 12% reached funding goals; 16% received no donations.
- **CEO acknowledgment:** GoFundMe's CEO has acknowledged the platform was never designed as healthcare financing and has become the default. A safety net with an 88% failure rate is not a safety net.
- **Racial-disparity finding:** Campaigns organized by individuals with distinctively African-American or Hispanic surnames raise substantially less and are less likely to meet goals, even controlling for geography and time period.
- **Wage-productivity decoupling:** Lawrence Mishel + Josh Bivens (Economic Policy Institute) — median US wage tracked roughly with productivity through the late 1940s–early 1970s; then decoupled. Productivity continued to rise; median wage stagnated in real terms for the next half century.
- **Hacker:** Jacob Hacker, *The Great Risk Shift* (2006); broader pattern of risk + cost transfer from institutional to individual balance sheets.
- **Katy Butler / pacemaker case:** Medicare reimburses ~$54 for a long office visit to discuss treatment alternatives; ~$461 for the pacemaker-implantation procedure on top of a ~$12,000 hospital flat fee, nearly half of which goes to the device manufacturer (~$6,000). Butler's father spent 5 years in profound cognitive decline with the pacemaker still functioning, cared for by her 80-year-old mother in 80-hour weeks of unpaid labor.
- **Gawande:** Atul Gawande, *Being Mortal* (2014) — American healthcare-financing architecture systematically over-treats dying patients while under-funding palliative-care infrastructure.
- **BR essay use:** §II reference-only (one or two sentences as part of the pattern; the GoFundMe-as-default-safety-net pattern is sharp enough to land as a sentence-or-two anchor without developing the case at length).

### 7.7 Restitution Bond lineage (§V citations)

- **Ta-Nehisi Coates, "The Case for Reparations,"** *The Atlantic*, 2014. Argument-architecture anchor: name the specific mechanism by which harm was produced; name the specific harm; name the specific debt. The Restitution Bond methodology extends Coates's argument-architecture into extraction-community contexts.
- **William Darity Jr. + A. Kirsten Mullen, *From Here to Equality: Reparations for Black Americans in the Twenty-First Century*** (2020). Contemporary landmark; wealth-gap calculation methodology; $14T cumulative wealth deficit anchored to slavery, sharecropping, redlining, convict leasing, discriminatory homeownership policy, educational and employment exclusion.
- **Hamilton, Darity, Price, Sridharan, Tippett, *Umbrellas Don't Make It Rain*** (2015). Matched-comparison empirical methodology demonstrating that conventional explanations (educational attainment, employment, family structure) cannot account for the racial wealth gap's magnitude — even Black households at highest measurable achievement strata display median wealth substantially below white households at lower achievement strata. The umbrella does not make the rain.
- **Dalton Conley, *Being Black, Living in the Red*** (1999). Foundational wealth-as-structural-variable methodology that Hamilton/Darity later developed.
- **Reparations three-component program (Darity-Mullen typology):** acknowledgment-and-atonement; redress (decomposes into symbolic redress + material restitution); closure. Darity-Mullen declare in favor of restitution within redress. The Restitution Bond is positioned within their typology as the instrument that operationalizes the restitution component of redress, extended to the broader case-class.
- **Discipline note:** The framework's Restitution Bond joins the reparations-economics tradition rather than translating or rebranding it. The bond name (Restitution, not Reparations) marks the umbrella-extension to the broader case-class (Libby asbestos; black lung; McDowell; Niger Delta) while leaving the reparations tradition its specific historical claim.

### 7.8 Foreclosure Bond lineage (§VI citations)

- **Katharina Pistor, *The Code of Capital*** (2019). Six legal modules — property law, collateral law, trust law, corporate veil law, bankruptcy law, choice-of-law clauses — encode assets as capital and protect that capital from accountability across jurisdictions. Capital is a legal artifact. The Libby case maps directly: W.R. Grace's bankruptcy filing was not an accident but a structural feature of the legal architecture.
- **John Hartwick, "Intergenerational Equity and the Investing of Rents from Exhaustible Resources"** (1977). The Hartwick rule for sustainable extraction: rents from non-renewable resource depletion should be reinvested to maintain a constant capital stock. Foundational for foreclosure-prevention bond economics.
- **Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act (SMCRA) 1977.** Established reclamation-bond requirements for mining operations. Bonds posted before extraction; carried forward across the long tail of the cost they cover; discharged against specific reclamation liability when the obligation comes due.
- **Oil-and-gas decommissioning bonds.** Bonds posted by operators against well-closure costs.
- **Norway Government Pension Fund Global.** Channels petroleum rents into sovereign-fund architecture. ~$1.9T total; ~$340K per Norwegian citizen. 2001 fiscal rule caps annual spending at ~3% of returns; capital itself inviolate. Held by every government since regardless of coalition.
- **Derek Parfit, *Reasons and Persons*** (1984). Intergenerational impersonal-outcomes framework grounding the moral standing of future generations philosophically.
- **Philip Pettit, *Republicanism*** (1997). Freedom as non-domination; institutional architecture required so that future generations cannot trivially be subjected to dominated conditions.
- **Quentin Skinner, *Liberty Before Liberalism*** (1998). Historical recovery of the civic-republican tradition.
- **Foreclosure etymology:** Old French *forclore* — to bar from. The mortgage-foreclosure event (households barred from continued access to a primary asset) is the cousin-association the term carries; the framework's Foreclosure Bond names a structurally cousin instrument operating across time (future generations barred from continued access to commons-options).

### 7.9 Supporting empirical anchors

- **Black Lung Benefits Program:** $44B paid out across half a century. ~$5.1B Trust Fund outstanding debt as of September 2024. No instrument in place that prices the cost severance the trust fund was designed to compensate for.
- **Reclamation-bonds shortfall:** Reclamation bonds posted by mining companies cover $4–6B less than the actual reclamation cost of the sites whose extraction generated the bonds.
- **Social Security intergenerational obligation:** No Foreclosure Bond against it; cost is structurally guaranteed to be borne by whichever generation is in working age when the obligation matures.
