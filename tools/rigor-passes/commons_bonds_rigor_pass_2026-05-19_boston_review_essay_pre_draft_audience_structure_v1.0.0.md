### 7.1 Libby, Montana

- **Operator:** W. R. Grace and Company.
- **Operation:** Vermiculite mine in Libby, Montana, operated 1963–1990 (27 years).
- **Production share:** Roughly 80% of the world's vermiculite during the operation's window.
- **Product use:** Zonolite-brand insulation installed in an estimated ~35 million American homes; also sold as soil conditioner.
- **Contamination:** Vermiculite at the Libby deposit was contaminated with a particularly lethal form of asbestos (tremolite).
- **Internal knowledge:** Internal documents released during later litigation showed Grace had identified asbestos contamination as early as 1963 and measured exposure levels in workers throughout the mine's operation.
- **Revenue:** ~$100M cumulative lifetime mine revenue.
- **Documented costs:** $4B+ across medical care; environmental remediation; legal settlements; lost property values; ongoing health monitoring. **Cost-to-revenue ratio: 40-to-1 minimum.**
- **Human toll:** 400+ Libby residents have died of asbestos-related disease. ~3,000 diagnosed with asbestos-consistent lung abnormalities. Mortality rate from asbestosis 40-80× the national average.
- **EPA designation:** EPA declared the entire town a public health emergency — the first time EPA had ever made such a declaration for a man-made disaster.
- **Corporate exit:** W.R. Grace declared bankruptcy in 2001. Assets reorganized; shareholders and executives moved on.
- **Criminal prosecution:** 2005 DOJ federal grand jury indictment of W.R. Grace and seven senior executives on conspiracy charges to violate the Clean Air Act and obstruct federal investigation of the contamination. Largest environmental criminal prosecution in American history at the time; named specific executives by name and role.
- **Acquittal:** 2009 — after a 13-week trial, all defendants (corporation + seven named executives) were acquitted.
- **Framework framing:** The acquittal does not mean the contamination did not happen, that the executives did not know, or that the cost severance did not occur. It means the criminal-law architecture available to the prosecution was not structurally suited to a case involving accumulated decisions across decades. The cost-severance accounting operates at the structural-mechanism level the criminal law could not reach.

### 7.2 Deepwater Horizon

- **Date:** April 20, 2010 (platform explosion).
- **Operator:** British Petroleum (BP); platform operator at the Macondo well in the Gulf of Mexico.
- **Workers killed:** 11.
- **Oil released:** 4.9 million barrels over 87 days.
- **Pre-disaster BP Gulf revenue:** ~$60B in the five years leading up to the disaster.
- **Macondo well projection:** 40–60 million barrels over lifetime; $3–4B at 2010 prices.
- **Direct economic costs to BP:** ~$21B in federal and state fines and settlements (largest environmental penalty in US history at the time); ~$20B in compensation fund for affected businesses and individuals; ~$60-65B total documented BP cost.
- **Ecological costs (NOAA estimates):** 4–8 billion oysters killed; 2–5 trillion newly hatched fish killed; tens of thousands of birds and sea turtles; tourism revenue losses >$7B in three years following the spill; property-value declines along affected coastlines ~$12B.
- **Total aggregate economic losses:** >$150B (academic, insurance-industry, federal sources).
- **Cost recovery ratio:** $60B BP cost vs. $150B+ total cost = **40% recovery**. The framework's accountability target is 100%; Deepwater paid 40%.
- **Mexico asymmetry:** BP's settlement with the Mexican federal government in 2018 totaled ~$25M — three orders of magnitude smaller than US settlements for comparable per-mile-of-coastline damage. Asymmetry was a function of legal architecture (Oil Pollution Act 1990 + state-level tort + class-action infrastructure in the US; substantially weaker cross-border environmental architecture in Mexico for foreign-corporation claims), not contamination distribution.
- **Framework framing:** The 40% ratio is the documented, court-validated empirical magnitude of the accountability gap for one of the largest environmental disasters in American history.

### 7.3 Baotou, Inner Mongolia (reference-only in the BR essay)

- **Production:** ~60% of world rare earth elements; the seventeen metals underwriting nearly every piece of advanced electronics on Earth.
- **Waste ratio:** ~10 tons of wastewater per ton of rare earth oxides produced (heavy metals, acids, radioactive materials).
- **Annual industry revenue (China rare earth):** $10–12B; Baotou facilities ~half.
- **Documented health effects:** Elevated rates of cancer, respiratory disease, birth defects; agricultural land within ~10km radius no longer suitable for farming; peer-reviewed studies in *Environmental Pollution*, *Ecotoxicology and Environmental Safety*, *Science of the Total Environment* document elevated heavy-metal concentrations (lead, cadmium, thorium, uranium) in soil, water, biomarkers.
- **Remediation estimate:** >$100B (conservative).
- **Industry environmental spending:** <1% of revenue.
- **Framework framing:** Spatial cost severance through global supply chains; the consumer at the other end is a structural participant, not a villain.
- **BR essay use:** Reference-only in §II (one or two sentences as part of the pattern-across-cases compression); do not develop at chapter-depth.

### 7.4 2008 financial crisis

- **First risk transfer:** 1978 Congressional creation of the 401(k) retirement-account structure; replacement of defined-benefit pensions over the following two decades.
- **Second risk transfer:** 2001–2008 mortgage-backed securities (MBS) and collateralized debt obligations (CDOs); instruments packaged into mutual funds and target-date funds sold inside 401(k) accounts.
- **TARP:** $700B in public funds to stabilize financial institutions.
- **Federal Reserve emergency lending:** $16T cumulative across the crisis (per GAO 2011 audit).
- **Foreclosures:** ~10 million American households received foreclosure filings 2008–2012; ~5 million resulting in completed foreclosure.
- **HAMP:** Home Affordable Modification Program reached a small fraction of eligible applicants; administrative failures documented in multiple GAO reports.
- **FCIC conclusion:** Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission (2011) concluded the crisis was the result of "human action and inaction — not forces beyond human control."
- **Subprime origination playbook:** Originator commissions tied to volume rather than default rates; broker-disclosed terms differed materially from contract terms; adjustable-rate products marketed to borrowers whose understanding of rate-reset mechanics was, by industry-internal acknowledgment, expected to be incomplete. CFPB post-crisis enforcement record documents the pattern at scale.
- **Sweden 1930s comparison:** 1931 krona devaluation; 1933 Riksbank bank-rescue paired with mortgage-modification programs; 1934–1937 public-works employment programs; 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement institutionalized welfare-state architecture. Comparative-banking-crisis literature anchors: Kindleberger 1973; Eichengreen 1992.
- **Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (2010):** CFPB; FSOC; Volcker Rule; enhanced capital + liquidity requirements.
- **Post-2017 institutional weakening:** CFPB enforcement record contracted; Volcker Rule substantially relaxed in 2018–2019; systemic-importance designations contested, narrowed.
- **TARP repayment objection (handle in compression):** TARP direct outlays substantially recovered through repayments and asset disposals (GAO + CBO accountings). Framework's response: TARP is the wrong accounting unit; the cost severance the chapter prices is the unrecovered welfare loss to ~10M foreclosed households + displaced workers + regional economies + political-trust commons.
- **BR essay use:** §II as one of the pattern cases; §V as one Restitution Bond application.

### 7.5 Social Security

- **Design year:** 1935. Pay-as-you-go system; current workers fund current retirees via payroll deductions; surpluses invested in special-issue Treasury bonds.
- **Demographic transition:** Baby boom generation began reaching retirement age in 2008.
- **Outflow > inflow:** 2021.
- **Trust-fund depletion projection:** 2033 (under current law and current demographic projections).
- **Post-2033 benefit-coverage:** Unless Congress acts, the program will pay ~77% of promised benefits.
- **Foregone-return magnitude (per Decision #10 reframe):** *Tens of trillions in foregone return; an order of magnitude above the program's current trust-fund balance.* Avoid the specific $108T figure (counterfactual asset-class assumption invites adversarial pull from center-right reader #12 + environmental economist #8 + Stone Center reviewer #10); the order-of-magnitude framing preserves the scale claim without inviting calculation-specific engagement.
- **Mechanism:** Severance accomplished through time rather than space; value-extracting party (politicians and voters who extracted electoral value of benefit expansions in the 1960s–1980s) systematically separated from cost-bearing party (future-generation taxpayers funding the demographic-deficit window).
- **Norway GPFG contrast (Ch 4 cross-reference):** Government Pension Fund Global; channels petroleum rents into sovereign-fund structure that compounds across decades. Contrasting architectural choice on structurally similar intergenerational problem; choice is contingent, not inevitable.
- **Public-finance-prudence objection (handle in compression):** Treasury-bond mechanism was administratively defensible; cost severance is upstream of the portfolio question — it sits in the obligation-generation mechanism.
- **Timeline-tractability objection (handle in compression):** Funding gap is mathematically closeable through adjustment. Framework response: adjustments themselves transfer the obligation onto the cost-bearing party. Tractability is not absence of severance; it is severance unfolding on schedule.
- **BR essay use:** §II as one of the pattern cases; §VI as the principal Foreclosure Bond application.

### 7.6 Healthcare commons (reference-only in the BR essay)

- **GoFundMe growth:** 2011–2020, annual number of US medical fundraising campaigns grew 25-fold; by 2020, more than a third of all GoFundMe campaigns were for medical expenses.
- **2016–2020 campaign data:** 438,000 medical campaigns; over $2B raised from 21.7M donations; only 12% reached funding goals; 16% received no donations.
- **CEO acknowledgment:** GoFundMe's CEO has acknowledged the platform was never designed as healthcare financing.
- **Racial-disparity finding:** Campaigns organized by individuals with distinctively African-American or Hispanic surnames raise substantially less and are less likely to meet goals.
- **Wage-productivity decoupling:** Lawrence Mishel + Josh Bivens (EPI) — median US wage tracked roughly with productivity through the late 1940s–early 1970s; then decoupled.
- **Hacker:** Jacob Hacker, *The Great Risk Shift* (2006).
- **Katy Butler / pacemaker case:** Medicare reimburses ~$54 for a long office visit; ~$461 for the pacemaker-implantation procedure on top of a ~$12,000 hospital flat fee, ~half to device manufacturer (~$6,000).
- **Gawande:** *Being Mortal* (2014).
- **BR essay use:** §II reference-only.

### 7.7 Restitution Bond lineage (§V citations)

- **Ta-Nehisi Coates, "The Case for Reparations,"** *The Atlantic*, 2014.

The Restitution Bond is positioned within their typology as the instrument operationalizing the restitution component of redress, extended to the broader case-class.

### 7.9 Supporting empirical anchors

- **Black Lung Benefits Program:** $44B paid out across half a century. ~$5.1B Trust Fund outstanding debt as of September 2024. **Funding mechanism (added 2026-05-23 per Pass 3.1 F-3.1-4):** the program is supported largely by a coal-extraction excise tax (Internal Revenue Code §4121; per DOL annual Trust Fund reports) — the value-extracting side is structurally tagged for the cost-bearing side. The ~$5.1B outstanding Trust Fund debt is financed by general taxation rather than by the coal-extraction value the program was designed to track; the structural-contrast argument turns on this asymmetry between intended-design (extraction-tagged) and actual-financing (general-revenue-absorbing).

- **Social Security intergenerational obligation:** No Foreclosure Bond against it; cost is structurally guaranteed to be borne by whichever generation is in working age when the obligation matures.
