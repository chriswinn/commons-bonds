### HIGH-1: EU Critical Raw Materials Act — "thirty-four materials as strategically critical" conflates 34 *critical* with 17 *strategic*; "enacted in 2023" is loose

**Location:** [Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:230](manuscript/chapters/Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:230)

**Chapter text:**
> "The European Union enacted the Critical Raw Materials Act in 2023, formally classifying thirty-four materials as strategically critical and requiring stockpile-and-alternative-sourcing programs."

**Canonical truth (web-verified):**
- The Critical Raw Materials Act (formally Regulation (EU) 2024/1252) was *proposed* by the Commission on 16 March 2023; provisional political agreement was reached 13 November 2023; the act was *formally adopted* by Council on 18 March 2024 and *entered into force* on 23 May 2024.
- The Act identifies **34 critical raw materials (CRMs)**, of which **17 are designated *strategic* raw materials (SRMs)**. The 17 strategic-list was initially 16 in the Commission's March 2023 proposal; aluminium was added during the trilogue process to reach 17.
- The framing "thirty-four materials as strategically critical" is a portmanteau that doesn't match either official EU designation. The 34 are *critical*; a 17-member subset of those is *strategic*. "Strategically critical" is non-canonical.

**Why this is the HIGH-1 finding:** Two distinct precision gaps stack:
1. The 34/17 conflation will be caught by any EU-policy-literate reader. The strategic-vs-critical distinction is the central design feature of the CRMA — the act introduces strategic raw materials as a sharper sub-category precisely to enable stockpiling and benchmarks against a smaller priority list. Conflating the two erodes the chapter's policy-analytical credibility.
2. "Enacted in 2023" is defensible if "enacted" reads as "first proposed and politically agreed by EU institutions in 2023," which is what happened (Commission proposal March 2023 + trilogue agreement November 2023). It is misleading if "enacted" reads as "formally adopted by Council and entered into force," both of which were 2024 events. A trade-press editor or fact-checker familiar with EU legislative process will query both.

**Recommended spot-fix:** Two-component tightening.
- Option A (preserve "2023" framing + correct designation): `The European Union enacted the Critical Raw Materials Act in 2023, classifying thirty-four materials as critical raw materials and a seventeen-material strategic-raw-materials subset eligible for stockpile-and-alternative-sourcing programs.`
- Option B (precision on enactment date + corrected designation): `The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act, politically agreed in late 2023 and entered into force in May 2024, classifies thirty-four materials as critical raw materials, with seventeen of these designated strategic and eligible for stockpile-and-alternative-sourcing programs.`
- Option C (compressed precision): `The European Union enacted the Critical Raw Materials Act in 2024, classifying thirty-four materials as critical and seventeen of those as strategic — the strategic subset eligible for stockpile-and-alternative-sourcing programs.`

**Severity rationale:** HIGH because (a) the 34/17 conflation is the kind of policy-detail error a publisher copy-editor working with an EU-policy press review will flag, (b) the cross-chapter inventory (line 211 not currently in the recurring-stats table — would benefit from being added as a canonical anchor), (c) Ch 9's policy-economy density makes the chapter the canonical site for the CRMA reference in this manuscript; any inaccuracy here propagates.

### HIGH-2: International Seabed Authority "established under UNCLOS in 1982" — UNCLOS signed 1982; ISA established 1994

**Location:** [Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:232](manuscript/chapters/Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:232)

**Chapter text:**
> "The International Seabed Authority, established under UNCLOS in 1982, has issued exploration contracts since 2014 and is currently negotiating the commercial-mining code that would authorize extraction."

**Canonical truth (web-verified):**
- The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) was opened for signature on 10 December 1982 at Montego Bay, Jamaica. UNCLOS entered into force on 16 November 1994 (60th instrument of ratification was deposited 16 November 1993; 12-month entry-into-force period followed).
- The International Seabed Authority (ISA) is created by Part XI of UNCLOS and Annex II to the 1994 Implementing Agreement. The ISA was *established* on 16 November 1994 (the day UNCLOS entered into force) — not 1982.
- ISA's Regulations on Prospecting and Exploration for Polymetallic Nodules in the Area were adopted in July 2000.
- The **first exploration contracts** for polymetallic nodules in the Clarion-Clipperton Zone were issued by the ISA **in 2001**, with seven contractors signed between 2001 and 2004 (India, Japan/DORD, France/Ifremer, Russia/Yuzhmorgeologiya, China/COMRA, Interoceanmetal, and South Korea).
- The "since 2014" framing is incorrect — exploration contracts have been issued *since 2001*, not since 2014.

**Why this is the HIGH-2 finding:** Two distinct factual errors stack in a single sentence:
1. ISA established 1994 (UNCLOS entry into force), not 1982 (UNCLOS signing).
2. Exploration contracts issued since 2001 (the first 15-year contracts under the 2000 Regulations), not 2014.

Any deep-sea-mining policy analyst — and the chapter is *explicitly* engaging the live ISA negotiation — will catch both. The chapter is making a specific operational claim about the ISA's contract-issuance timeline that is wrong by 13 years.

**Recommended spot-fix:**
- Option A (date-corrected): `The International Seabed Authority, established under UNCLOS when the convention entered into force in 1994, has issued exploration contracts for polymetallic nodules since 2001 and is currently negotiating the commercial-mining code that would authorize extraction.`
- Option B (compressed): `The International Seabed Authority, established in 1994 under UNCLOS, has issued exploration contracts since 2001 and is currently negotiating the commercial-mining code that would authorize extraction.`

**Severity rationale:** HIGH because both dates are wrong, the chapter cites the case as live policy-decision-time material (CCZ + Nauru-sponsored 2-year-rule), and the next sentence ("The Nauru-sponsored 2-year-rule trigger in 2021 set a deadline pressure on those negotiations") is correct — but a reader who catches the prior sentence's errors will lose confidence in the case treatment as a whole.

### HIGH-3: Mountain Pass / MP Materials acquisition timeline — "acquisition in 2018" misattributes a 2017 acquisition + 2018 operations restart

**Location:** [Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:230](manuscript/chapters/Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:230)

**Chapter text:**
> "The United States restored Mountain Pass mine production through MP Materials' acquisition in 2018, recovering domestic supply capacity that had been lost to consolidation in the 2000s."

**Canonical truth (web-verified):**
- The Mountain Pass mine was acquired out of bankruptcy in **June/July 2017** by MP Mine Operations LLC, a consortium principally led by JHL Capital Group (Chicago), with QVT Financial LP and Shenghe Resources participating, at auction for $20.5 million.
- MP Materials (the public corporate entity) was formed via SPAC merger with Fortress Value Acquisition Corp in November 2020.
- Mining and refining operations at Mountain Pass **resumed in January 2018**.
- The loss-of-domestic-supply-capacity timeline:
  - 2002: Mountain Pass mine closed after toxic waste spill + ~60 documented pipeline spills (1984-1998) per federal investigation.
  - 2008: Chevron sold the mine to Molycorp Minerals.
  - 2012: Molycorp restarted Mountain Pass production.
  - 2015: Molycorp filed Chapter 11 bankruptcy (driven by Chinese rare-earth price collapse).
  - 2017: Mountain Pass acquired out of bankruptcy by MP Mine Operations LLC.
  - 2018: Production restart.

The chapter's claim has two issues:
1. "MP Materials' acquisition in 2018" conflates the 2017 acquisition (by the predecessor entity that later became MP Materials) with the 2018 operations restart. The MP Materials corporate entity didn't exist as a public corporation until 2020.
2. "Lost to consolidation in the 2000s" is imprecise — Mountain Pass closed due to environmental issues (toxic spills) + Chinese cost competition + Molycorp's 2015 bankruptcy, not "consolidation." (Some industry-side commentary uses "consolidation" loosely to describe the China-driven market shift, but the canonical history runs through environmental closure, cheap Chinese competition, and Molycorp bankruptcy.)

**Why this is the HIGH-3 finding:** The chapter explicitly leans on this case as an example of US strategic-resource-architecture response, and the timeline detail matters because the chapter's broader argument is that the four-step framework is reachable from existing institutional moves. A rare-earth-policy analyst will catch both errors. The "consolidation in the 2000s" framing also misdescribes the closure mechanism — the cause was environmental + competitive + bankruptcy, not consolidation.

**Recommended spot-fix:**
- Option A (date-and-mechanism corrected): `The United States restored Mountain Pass mine production through MP Materials' 2017 acquisition out of bankruptcy and 2018 operations restart, recovering domestic supply capacity that had been lost to environmental closure in 2002 and the 2015 bankruptcy of the prior operator Molycorp.`
- Option B (compressed): `The United States restored Mountain Pass mine production in 2018, recovering domestic rare-earth supply capacity that had been lost to environmental closure and Chinese cost competition over the prior two decades.`

**Severity rationale:** HIGH because the precision gap is two-component (date + cause) and the case is the chapter's anchor for US-side rare-earth-policy response.

### HIGH-4: Sweden "1933 currency devaluation" — Sweden left the gold standard September 1931

**Location:** [Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:126](manuscript/chapters/Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:126)

**Chapter text:**
> "Sweden's response to the 1929-1932 financial crisis combined bank rescue with substantive household relief: a 1933 currency devaluation that reduced real debt burdens; mortgage-modification programs that wrote down nominal mortgage debt for households unable to service it; public-works employment programs that absorbed unemployed labor at wages maintaining household solvency; and the 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement framework..."

**Canonical truth (web-verified):**
- Sweden abandoned the gold standard on **28 September 1931** (following Britain's departure on 21 September 1931). The Swedish krona depreciated significantly against gold from that point.
- In September 1931 Sweden became the first country to make domestic price-level stabilization the official goal of monetary policy (pioneering price-level targeting under the Riksbank).
- There is no canonical "1933 currency devaluation" in Sweden's depression-era response. The 1931 gold-standard departure is the canonical monetary event; subsequent monetary policy was anchored to price-level targeting under Riksbank governor Ivar Rooth and economic adviser Erik Lindahl (Wicksell-school price-level-stability framework).
- A finance minister named in the period was Ernst Wigforss (Social Democratic government formed September 1932), who argued for expansionary fiscal policy + accepting the krona depreciation.

**Why this is the HIGH-4 finding:** Specific date error in a load-bearing case (the Swedish counterfactual is the chapter's positive analog for the 2008 US-response critique). A monetary historian or Sweden-policy specialist will catch this immediately. The "1933" date is approximately two years off the canonical 1931 event, and there's no major Swedish monetary policy event in 1933 specifically that the chapter could be referring to. The reduction in real debt burdens did follow from the 1931 depreciation, so the *mechanism* the chapter describes is correct — only the date is wrong.

**Recommended spot-fix:**
- Option A (date-corrected): `Sweden's response to the 1929-1932 financial crisis combined bank rescue with substantive household relief: the September 1931 gold-standard departure and resulting krona depreciation reduced real debt burdens; mortgage-modification programs wrote down nominal mortgage debt for households unable to service it; public-works employment programs absorbed unemployed labor at wages maintaining household solvency; and the 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement framework institutionalized labor-employer wage-bargaining...`
- Option B (compressed): `Sweden's response to the 1929-1932 financial crisis combined bank rescue with substantive household relief: the 1931 departure from the gold standard reduced real debt burdens; mortgage-modification programs wrote down nominal mortgage debt for households unable to service it; public-works employment programs absorbed unemployed labor at wages maintaining household solvency; and the 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement framework institutionalized labor-employer wage-bargaining...`

**Severity rationale:** HIGH because the chapter explicitly positions Sweden's response as the positive counterfactual to the 2008 US response, and the monetary-policy date is wrong by ~2 years. The mechanism (real-debt-burden reduction via currency depreciation) is correct; only the date is wrong, but in a chapter making a sustained Sweden-as-counterfactual argument, the date precision matters.

### HIGH-5: Vienna Gemeindebau financing — Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag is post-WWII; original Red Vienna financing was Wohnbausteuer + Luxury Tax

**Location:** [Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:224](manuscript/chapters/Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:224)

**Chapter text:**
> "The program was initiated in 1919 by the Red Vienna government, it survived an interwar fascist coup and Nazi occupation, and it has been maintained across every administration and every government that has governed Vienna since the Second World War... The system is funded through a one-percent payroll contribution — the Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag — combined with tenant rents and subsidized federal loans, and it has sustained itself through a century of use."

**Canonical truth (web-verified):**
- Red Vienna's social-housing program: the SDAP (Social Democratic Workers' Party of Austria) won a majority in the 4 May 1919 Vienna Gemeinderat elections, and the housing program was developed in the early 1920s (substantial construction began 1925; Karl-Marx-Hof completed 1930). Calling it "initiated in 1919" is defensible if "initiated" refers to the political-policy commitment of the new SDAP-led municipal administration, but the actual construction program was 1923-1934 (the canonical "Red Vienna" period).
- The original Red Vienna financing mechanism was **Wohnbausteuer (housing-construction tax)** — a progressive tax on construction of larger, mainly private housing units — combined with revenues from the **Luxury Tax (Luxussteuer)** and federal funds. Wohnbausteuer alone covered ~40% of building costs.
- **Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag (Housing Promotion Contribution)** is a separate, post-WWII federal-level payroll-based contribution, currently set at 1% of gross wages (0.5% employer + 0.5% employee). It was established in 1949 as part of the post-WWII reconstruction architecture, and was significantly restructured in 1996. It is not the original Red Vienna financing mechanism.
- The two financing mechanisms have substantially different historical and structural characters: Wohnbausteuer was a 1920s progressive construction-tax for the Vienna municipal program; Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag is a post-1949 federal payroll-contribution applied across all Austrian Länder.

**Why this is the HIGH-5 finding:** The chapter conflates two distinct financing instruments separated by 30+ years and operating at different governmental scales. The Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag explanation gives an inaccurate causal story for how Vienna's century-spanning housing program has been sustained — readers familiar with Austrian housing policy will catch this. The actual story is more interesting and more supportive of the chapter's argument: Red Vienna built a sustained financing architecture through progressive construction-tax revenues + Luxury Tax + federal funds in the 1920s, and the post-WWII Austrian state maintained the housing-funding architecture through the federal Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag, with Vienna's Gemeindebau program adapted to operate within both regimes.

**Recommended spot-fix:**
- Option A (precision rewrite): `The system was originally funded through a progressive construction tax (the Wohnbausteuer) combined with Vienna's Luxury Tax in the Red Vienna period, and has been maintained since 1949 through a one-percent federal payroll contribution (the Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag) combined with tenant rents and subsidized federal loans. It has sustained itself through a century of use across multiple financing regimes.`
- Option B (compressed): `The system has been funded through a sequence of dedicated tax instruments — a progressive construction tax in the Red Vienna period, and since the post-WWII federal architecture a one-percent payroll contribution (the Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag) — combined with tenant rents and subsidized federal loans; it has sustained itself through a century of use.`

**Severity rationale:** HIGH because the precision gap involves attributing the wrong financing instrument to the wrong era, and the case is the chapter's anchor for Vienna-as-housing-commons-counter-evidence. Austrian housing-policy specialists are a non-trivial audience for this chapter; an internal-corpus reader (e.g., a Vienna-policy citation Ch 10 might pick up) would propagate the inaccuracy.

### HIGH-6: Helium-3 framing — "abundant in lunar regolith" oversells the resource; "terrestrial reserves of helium in general" conflates He-3 and He-4

**Location:** [Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:30](manuscript/chapters/Chapter__9_PricingHonestly.md:30)

**Chapter text:**
> "Helium-3, should fusion reactors ever run on it, is effectively absent from Earth's atmosphere and abundant in lunar regolith, which means terrestrial reserves of helium in general are a resource we may want our descendants to still have access to."

**Canonical truth (web-verified):**
- Earth's atmospheric He-3: approximately 7.3 parts per *trillion* (1.4 ppm He-3 / total He, in an atmosphere ~5.2 ppm He). "Effectively absent" ✓.
- Lunar regolith He-3 concentration: 1.4-15 ppb in sunlit areas, with samples averaging ~4 ppb; some sources cite up to 50 ppb in permanently shadowed regions. Calling lunar regolith "abundant" in He-3 is imprecise — it is *several orders of magnitude more abundant than Earth's atmosphere*, but the absolute concentration is still parts-per-billion, requiring extensive lunar mining infrastructure to recover industrial quantities. The framing "abundant" oversells without the comparative qualifier.
- Terrestrial helium reserves (mostly He-4, recovered as a byproduct of natural-gas extraction) are a separate concern from He-3 fusion. Earth's natural-gas helium is mostly the He-4 isotope (~99.99986% of total He on Earth) and is depleting due to use in MRI cryogenics, semiconductor manufacturing, lifting gas, leak detection, etc. He-3 fusion (D-He3 pathway) would not draw on terrestrial He-4 reserves — D-He3 requires He-3 specifically, which Earth lacks. Conflating "He-3 → fusion" and "terrestrial helium reserves we want descendants to access" links two distinct resource-economics stories.

**Why this is the HIGH-6 finding:** A materials-scientist or fusion-economist reader will catch the conflation. The chapter's broader argument (the residual commons value framework reads He-3 as a high-existential-substitutability-gap resource) is defensible — but the specific framing as written stitches together claims about lunar He-3 with claims about terrestrial He-4, which are different reserve / extraction / depletion stories. Also: "abundant in lunar regolith" is editorial; the precise framing is "orders of magnitude more abundant in lunar regolith than in Earth's atmosphere, though still at parts-per-billion concentrations" — a more careful phrasing supports the framework's argument without losing accuracy.

**Recommended spot-fix:**
- Option A (precision-tightened): `Helium-3, should fusion reactors ever run on it, is effectively absent from Earth's atmosphere and present in lunar regolith at parts-per-billion concentrations orders of magnitude higher than terrestrial sources. Terrestrial helium reserves more broadly — mostly He-4 obtained as a byproduct of natural gas extraction — are a separately-tracked depleting resource that current uses (MRI cryogenics, semiconductor fabrication, lifting gas) are drawing down on timescales of decades.`
- Option B (compressed precision): `Helium-3, should fusion reactors ever run on it, is effectively absent from Earth's atmosphere and present in lunar regolith at orders-of-magnitude higher (though still parts-per-billion) concentrations. Terrestrial helium reserves more broadly — mostly the He-4 isotope obtained from natural gas — are also a depleting resource we may want our descendants to still have access to.`
- Option C (preserve compression with hedging): `Helium-3, should fusion reactors ever run on it, is effectively absent from Earth's atmosphere and present in lunar regolith at concentrations sufficient to be the subject of serious extraction proposals. Terrestrial helium reserves — He-3 and He-4 alike — are a depleting resource we may want our descendants to still have access to.`

**Severity rationale:** HIGH because the chapter is doing classification-level work here (the He-3 case is the chapter's signal that the framework's classification methodology extends to space-resource cases), and the conflation of He-3 with terrestrial He-4 reserves obscures the resource-economics story the chapter is trying to tell. A precise framing actually *strengthens* the chapter's argument by separating the two distinct resource concerns.
