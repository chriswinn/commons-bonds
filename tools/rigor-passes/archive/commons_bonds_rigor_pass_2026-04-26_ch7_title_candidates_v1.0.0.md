## §1. Title candidates

Brainstormed list, narrowed to seven viable candidates spanning the full trade-off space. Three additional candidates were considered and excluded as inferior to ones included (rationale at §1.8).

### §1.1 Candidate A — "The Colony Administrator" (original / baseline retention)

**Setup.** The original chapter title; in-prose retention candidate per vocabulary rigor pass §10 (≤2-3 narrative "colony" usages permitted where load-bearing).

**Connotation cluster.** Sci-fi mystique (Asimov; Robinson Mars trilogy); colony-as-society resonance; settler-frontier echoes; political-history baggage (European colonialism); narrative weight implying a precarious population whose survival rests on one administrator's decisions.

### §1.2 Candidate B — "The Habitat Administrator" (current sweep state)

**Setup.** The currently-integrated title after Candidate #8 ratified vocabulary sweep. Pure-substitution form.

**Connotation cluster.** NASA / ESA engineering vocabulary; Habitation-module manager; space-station logistics; clean political-history footprint; lower narrative pull than colony; reads as job-description rather than story-figure.

### §1.3 Candidate C — "The Outpost Administrator"

**Setup.** "Outpost" replaces "colony" — small bounded human presence on a frontier; smaller in scale than colony; carries some frontier-mystique without colony's specific political-history baggage.

**Connotation cluster.** Frontier-narrative echoes; small precarious settlement; military-and-exploration register; lighter political-history baggage than colony but not zero (American-West frontier-thesis adjacency; military-outpost colonialism in some readings).

### §1.4 Candidate D — "The Off-World Administrator"

**Setup.** "Off-world" specifies the setting at the universality-claim level (the chapter explicitly argues conclusions are off-world-universal, not Mars-specific). Slightly compound-clunky; the author flagged "OffworldHabitationAdministrator" specifically as too-clunky, but "Off-World Administrator" is shorter.

**Connotation cluster.** Sci-fi-genre; futuristic-bureaucracy figure; universal scope; abstract-narrative; vague but evocative; clean political-history footprint.

### §1.5 Candidate E — "The Mars Administrator"

**Setup.** "Mars" specifies the chosen example; the chapter explicitly names Mars as the chosen example with the universality framing in the chapter-intro framing sentence (Mars-as-arbitrary-choice). Title narrowing to Mars sets up the specific scenario; body argues universality from there.

**Connotation cluster.** Mars-specific; sci-fi-narrative; Roman-god / red-planet evocation; popular-science familiarity (NASA Mars missions); minimal political baggage; risks reader inference that chapter is Mars-only despite framing.

### §1.6 Candidate F — "The Habitat Steward"

**Setup.** Replaces "Administrator" with "Steward" — same role with different connotation cluster. "Steward" carries moral-weight / preservation / Tolkien-esque echoes; the chapter is structurally about stewardship under conditions where cost-severance fails.

**Connotation cluster.** Moral-guardian; preservation-flavored; biblical / Tolkien-esque resonance; quieter than administrator; less bureaucratic; clean political-history footprint.

### §1.7 Candidate G — "The Mars Steward"

**Setup.** Combination — Mars (specific scenario) + Steward (moral-weight role). Specific + dignified; carries more narrative weight than habitat-administrator while remaining clean on rigor modules.

**Connotation cluster.** Mars-specific (popular-science familiarity); moral-guardian register; preservation-flavored; clean political-history footprint; reads as both narrative-figure and moral-figure.

### §1.8 Candidates considered and excluded

- **"The Off-World Steward"** — combines off-world + steward; clean on rigor; but "Off-World Steward" reads slightly clunky (compound-hyphenation + steward) and the author flagged compound-clunkiness specifically. Excluded.
- **"The Frontier Administrator"** — frontier carries explicit American-West-thesis baggage; M11 / M12 burden moderate-to-heavy. Excluded as inferior to Outpost Administrator (which carries lighter frontier baggage).
- **"The Steward"** — minimalist; abstract; loses chapter-specific framing entirely. Excluded as too vague for a chapter title.
- **"The Frictionless Plane"** — references the chapter's own physics-analogy; intellectually evocative for academic readers; too inside-baseball for trade readers. Excluded.

## §9. Recommendation

The scorecard surfaces two co-primary candidates that are genuinely close at the top: G "The Mars Steward" (+6 net) and E "The Mars Administrator" (+5 net). Both share the same Mars-specific M1.5 trade-off and both match the original A "Colony Administrator" on the craft axes the author flagged (★★★ on M5 / M2.5 / M4.3). They differ on a single substantive axis — moral-weight (G) vs. bureaucratic-precision-and-parallelism (E) — and that difference is closer to author-preference than to rigor-determined ranking.

### §9.1 Co-primary recommendation #1: E "The Mars Administrator"

Rationale:
- **Preserves the structural parallelism with the original "Colony Administrator"** — same role-noun (Administrator), same syntactic pattern (place-noun + role-noun); only the loaded word swaps. Reader continuity from any prior reference to the original title is preserved at maximum.
- **Title-body register consistency.** The body text uses "the administrator" extensively; if the title says "Administrator" too, no register-split. (G's title says "Steward" while body says "administrator" — small but real internal-consistency cost.)
- **★★★ on M2.5 / M4.3 / M4.7 / M5** — matches G on the four craft modules the author's concern engages.
- **Mars-explicit framing resonance.** ★★★ on M4.7 — the chapter-intro framing sentence names Mars as the chosen example; the title naming Mars is consistent with that framing.
- **"Administrator" is more universally-understood than "Steward"** — slight M5 dinner-table edge in cross-reader-comprehension; "Administrator" is plain-English-everyday, "Steward" carries some archaic / Tolkien-esque echo that may or may not land for every reader.
- **Modest rigor cost** — 2 ✗ marks (M1.5 Mars-specific; M9 LOW-MODERATE risk via Mars-Musk-rhetoric adjacency). Both ✗ marks are mitigable: M1.5 by the chapter-intro framing sentence already in place; M9 by the Element B methodological-not-advocacy guard sentence already in place.

### §9.2 Co-primary recommendation #2: G "The Mars Steward"

Rationale:
- **Adds moral weight** ("Steward") that aligns with the chapter's substantive argument: the administrator's role is structurally one of stewardship because cost-severance fails when the affected community is internal. The chapter is already about stewardship; the title naming it makes the substance visible.
- **★★★ on M5 / M2.5 / M4.3 / M4.7** — matches E on craft modules.
- **Slightly stronger M2.4 (chapter fit)** — ★★★ on M2.4 vs E's ★★. The "Steward" framing names the chapter's substantive thesis (stewardship-under-no-cost-severance) directly, while "Administrator" describes the role more bureaucratically.
- **Same Mars-explicit framing resonance** as E.
- **Same rigor cost as E** — 2 ✗ marks, same mitigation.

### §9.3 The E-vs-G choice

The choice between E and G turns on a single craft-preference axis:

- **If priority is title-body internal consistency + universal-comprehensibility + structural-parallelism with original**: choose **E "The Mars Administrator."**
- **If priority is title-as-substantive-thesis-statement + moral-weight elevation + tight chapter-fit alignment**: choose **G "The Mars Steward."**

Both are rigor-cleared. Both recover the narrative pull author identified as missing. Both are Mars-specific (mitigable via existing framing). Both are publishing-path-friendly. The choice is genuinely author-preference at this point.
