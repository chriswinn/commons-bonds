## §2. The candidate addition under test

### §2.1 The argument (compact form)

**The structural observation:** Sweden in 1932-1937 faced a banking crisis structurally analogous to the United States's 1929-1933 banking crisis, and structurally analogous in pattern to the 2008 U.S. financial crisis. The Swedish response combined bank rescue with substantial household-relief: krona devaluation in 1931 to restore export competitiveness; Riksbank-coordinated bank-rescue paired with mortgage-modification programs; expanded public-works employment programs that restored household income directly; the development of what would become the Swedish welfare-state architecture (the so-called "Swedish Model" or "Saltsjöbaden Agreement" 1938 framework) over the subsequent decade. The 1930s Swedish response is widely cited in macroeconomic-history literature (Kindleberger 1973 *The World in Depression*; Eichengreen 1992 *Golden Fetters*; subsequent comparative-banking-crisis literature) as demonstrating that bank-rescue and household-relief are not structurally either-or — a society can choose to do both, and the Swedish polity in the 1930s did.

The U.S. response to the 2008 financial crisis chose differently. The architecture chose what was in-scope (bank rescue, Federal Reserve facilities for systemically important institutions, TARP for financial institutions) and chose what was substantially out-of-scope (mortgage modification at scale; the Home Affordable Modification Program reached a small fraction of eligible applicants; comparable household-rescue infrastructure was not built). The 2020 CARES Act + PPP architecture reproduced the same pattern; the 2008 + 2020 American responses are recognizable as one scope-choice tradition, in contrast to the 1930s Swedish (and broader Nordic 1930s) tradition that chose a different scope.

The framework's contribution: the comparison surfaces that the same banking-crisis pressure has produced different scope-choices across societies and across centuries. The 1930s Swedish response is empirical evidence that the 2008 U.S. response's scope-choice was a contingent decision, not an inevitable one — the kind of evidence the framework's contingency argument requires to ground its claim that "what is built can be rebuilt; what was decided can be re-decided."

### §2.2 What the framework adds beyond existing literature

| Dimension | Existing literature | Framework's distinctive contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Sweden 1930s as macroeconomic case** | Kindleberger 1973; Eichengreen 1992; comparative banking-crisis literature; Reinhart-Rogoff 2009 historical pattern documentation | Granted as input; not the framework's distinctive claim |
| **U.S. 2008 response specifics** | FCIC 2011; Mian-Sufi 2014; standard 2008-crisis literature | Granted as input; documented in Ch 5's 2008 case |

The 1930s Sweden / 2008 U.S. comparison is paradigm material for that claim — same pressure, different scope-choices, demonstrably contingent. |

## §3. Q1A — Does the addition belong in BOOK 1?

### §3.1 Book-scope discipline (per Open Insight #13 + v1.0.3 §6.1)

**Test:** Same as Candidates #1 and #2 — apply the framework-naming-vs-applied-advocacy distinction. The Sweden 1930s candidate sits on the same Book 1 / Book 2 boundary; the question is which side of the boundary.

#### §3.1.1 Structural-observation framing (Book 1 — DEFENSIBLE)

> *"Sweden in 1932-1937 faced banking-crisis pressure structurally analogous to what the United States faced in 1929-1933 and again in 2008-2009. The Swedish polity chose, through specific historical decisions (the 1931 krona devaluation; the 1933 Riksbank-coordinated bank-rescue paired with mortgage-modification programs; the 1934-1937 public-works employment programs; the 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement framework), a scope of crisis-response that included substantial household-relief alongside bank-rescue. The U.S. polity, facing structurally analogous pressure in 2008-2009 and again in 2020, chose a scope that prioritized institutional rescue without comparable household relief. The two scope-choices produce structurally different outcomes that the cost-severance framework names as different distributions of the rescue's benefits. The framework names the choice; the framework does not prescribe which scope any particular society should adopt; the political question of which choice is right for the U.S. is downstream of the framework's contribution."*

This framing:
- Names structural-observation about scope-variation across societies
- Describes contingent-history-of-choice (specific decisions, specific moments)
- Does not prescribe transition mechanism for U.S. adoption
- Honors epistemic-humility (1930s Sweden is one historical example among several; preconditions matter; framework does not legislate which path to take)
- Pairs with framework's existing structural-observation work (Ch 4 Norway-Nigeria; Ch 9 reachable-horizon cluster; stands-the-test §1 contingency argument; Candidate #1 scope-choice argument; Candidate #2 Norway-Social-Security comparison)

**This framing PASSES Book 1 rigor per v1.0.3 §6.1.**

## §4. Q1B — If Book 1, what specific framing?

The framing must:

1. **Name the architectural / scope choice** (1930s Sweden: bank-rescue + household-relief + public-works + welfare-state-development; 2008 U.S.: bank-rescue without comparable household-relief). Concrete.
2. **Trace the contingent-history** (1931 krona devaluation; 1933 Riksbank intervention; 1934-1937 public-works programs; 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement). Specific moments.
3. **Surface the design-variable distinction** (scope is a choice; banking-crisis pressure is the constraint; same constraint across societies has produced different scope-choices). Framework's distinctive analytical contribution at this candidate.
4. **Honor Insight #14 epistemic-humility** — 1930s Sweden is one historical example; preconditions matter (Sweden's high-trust institutional environment; the specific political coalition of the 1930s; the post-WWI economic context); no claim that 1930s-Sweden-style response is universally appropriate; no prescriptive transition-mechanism for U.S. adoption.
5. **Pair with Candidate #1 + Candidate #2 + the contingency argument** — Sweden 1930s is part of a cluster, not a free-standing case. Present alongside the other two candidates so the triangulation is visible to the reader.

**Framing verdict:** structural-observation register paired with the contingency-argument form, presented as part of the cross-country / cross-architecture / cross-century comparison cluster (#1 + #2 + #3 together).
