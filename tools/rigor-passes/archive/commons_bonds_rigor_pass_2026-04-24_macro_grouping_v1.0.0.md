## §1. Executive summary

**Macro-groupings as a canonical framework structure: REJECT.** Introducing 3–4 macro-categories at the framework-specification level fails on three independent grounds: (1) alignment with the success criterion ("severed cost" adopted as legal/policy vocabulary) is negative — macro-categories dilute the vocabulary to be adopted by creating competing naming; (2) alignment with academic rigor is negative — macro-categories reproduce the same bounded-set / arbitrariness / ontological-commitment concerns that sank v10 tiers; (3) alignment with the author's prior (abstraction-hides-severance) is strongly negative — macro-categories are a second layer of abstraction above variables that obscures specificity, which is the book's rhetorical strategy.

**Macro-groupings as chapter-internal narrative headings: REJECTED per author ratification 2026-04-24.** Author chose Option A (maximum discipline) over Option B (chapter-layout permit). Chapter section headings may exist for narrative pacing but must not thematically group variables in ways resembling macro-categories. Pure variable-enumeration with narrative flow is the book's structural approach.

**What the rigor finds:** groupings offer marginal pedagogical affordance that named variables already provide through their semantic content. The marginal benefit does not compensate for the rigor + abstraction + vocabulary-dilution costs.

**The five tests and their verdicts:**

| Test | Verdict | Key finding |
|---|---|---|
| **§3 Usefulness claims** | LIMITED | Pedagogical handholds mostly already provided by semantically-named variables. Policy translation, cross-case comparison, and chapter organization have cheaper alternatives. |
| **§4 Success-criterion alignment (vocabulary adoption)** | NEGATIVE | Macro-categories dilute the single-term vocabulary goal ("severed cost"). Competing vocabulary items reduce adoption likelihood. |
| **§5 Primary-goals alignment** | NEGATIVE to NEUTRAL | Academic reception is hurt (reopens rigor questions one layer up). Publishing, long-term impact slightly hurt or neutral. |
| **§6 Informational-goals alignment** | MIXED | Movement utility slight positive (policy translation). Other goals neutral. |
| **§7 Abstraction-hides-severance test (author's prior)** | CONFIRMED | The concern is structurally real. Macro-categories erase the specificity that makes the book's rhetorical strategy work. |

**Recommended path per rigor: Option A or Option B (indifferent on major tests; B allows chapter-craft flexibility, A guarantees maximum discipline). Author ratified Option A on 2026-04-24 (see §10).** Details in §8.

## §2. The specific claim under test

### §2.1 The claim

"The book should introduce 3–4 coarse macro-categories (e.g., Household / Community / Environmental / Intergenerational) above the variable level as a pedagogical and/or organizational device, either at the framework-specification level or as chapter-layout vocabulary."

**Illustrative candidate structure (Option C — canonical framework structure):**
- **Household costs:** C_individual_risk, C_lifetime_survival, C_mission_failure, C_dynastic_labor
- **Community costs:** C_community_transition, C_knowledge_culture, C_political_capture
- **Environmental costs:** E (carbon / atmospheric / hydrological), C_ecological_carrying
- **Intergenerational foreclosure costs:** C_foreclosure (= C₁), stock-dependent-utility-loss

### §2.2 The claim decomposed

1. **SC-1 (Pedagogical usefulness):** Macro-groupings reduce reader cognitive load relative to an un-grouped variable list.
2. **SC-2 (Policy legibility):** Macro-groupings translate better to policy conversation than raw variables.
3. **SC-3 (Cross-case comparability):** Macro-groupings support cross-case comparison better than variable-level comparison.
4. **SC-4 (Framework-level appropriateness):** The gains (SC-1, SC-2, SC-3) justify placing macro-groupings in the framework specification rather than leaving them at chapter-layout level.
5. **SC-5 (Non-harm to success criterion):** Macro-groupings do not undermine "severed cost" becoming adopted legal/policy vocabulary.
6. **SC-6 (Non-harm to rigor):** Macro-groupings survive the same rigor tests that the tier schema failed (bounded-set, arbitrariness, ontological commitment, independence).
7. **SC-7 (Non-harm to specificity):** Macro-groupings do not erode the book's rhetorical strategy of naming costs specifically and concretely.

### §2.3 What falsifies the claim

**The claim is falsified if:**

- Named variables already provide the pedagogical affordance macro-groupings would add — SC-1 collapses.
- Policy conversation already has macro-vocabulary in general English (social cost, environmental cost) that the framework doesn't need to reinvent — SC-2 collapses.
- Cross-case comparison is at least as well-served by variable-level or dimension-level comparison — SC-3 collapses.
- The rigor concerns that sank v10 tiers (arbitrariness, bounded-set, ontological commitment) reappear at the macro level — SC-6 fails.
- Macro-category vocabulary dilutes or competes with "severed cost" / "cost severance" as the book's contribution-to-adopt — SC-5 fails.
- Macro-categories erase the specificity that drives the book's rhetorical strategy — SC-7 fails.

**Verdict below (§7):** SC-4, SC-5, SC-6, SC-7 fail. The claim is falsified at the framework-specification level. A weaker version (chapter-layout use only) survives with conditions.

## §3. Usefulness claims — tested one-by-one

### §3.1 Pedagogical usefulness (SC-1)

**Claim:** grouping 20–40 variables into 4 macro-categories reduces cognitive load for readers.

**Test:** what cognitive work does the reader do when encountering variables? Under the post-dissolution framework, variables have semantically meaningful names: `Community Transition Cost`, `Dynastic Labor Cost`, `Political Capture Cost`, `Black Lung Cost`, `Acid Mine Drainage Remediation`, etc. The name of each variable is itself a cognitive handhold. A reader parsing "black lung cost" and "community transition cost" does not encounter them as opaque `C₁`, `C₂`, `C₃` that need macro-structure to organize — the reader encounters them as semantically rich labels that implicitly group themselves in the reader's mind.

**Counter:** A reader at a dense listing of 25 variables might still feel overwhelmed without explicit top-level structure.

**Counter-counter:** That overwhelm is a chapter-layout problem, not a framework-specification problem. A well-designed Ch 8 can break 25 variables across multiple subsections with narrative pacing, each introducing 3–6 variables before moving on. That's craft, not framework structure.

**Verdict on §3.1:** PARTIAL PASS at chapter-layout level; FAIL at framework level. The pedagogical gain is real but small and can be achieved by chapter craft without canonical macro-categories.

### §3.2 Policy legibility (SC-2)

**Claim:** macro-categories translate the framework to policy conversation better than raw variables.

**Test:** what vocabulary does policy conversation already use? General English already distinguishes:

- Health / worker-safety costs
- Community / economic-development costs
- Environmental / pollution costs
- Long-term / future / intergenerational costs

These are *policy-general* categories — they exist in policy discourse independent of Commons Bonds. Adding framework-specific macro-categories creates a second layer of vocabulary that policymakers must translate to/from the first. A cleaner approach: the framework specifies variables; policymakers map variables onto their existing macro-vocabulary as needed.

**Counter:** Framework-specific macro-categories (if well-chosen) could become the policy-vocabulary standard.

**Counter-counter:** The book's one-term vocabulary-adoption bet is on "severed cost" / "cost severance," not on macro-categories. Betting on an additional term dilutes the single-term bet. And policy discourse is unlikely to adopt framework-specific macro-categories when general-English macro-vocabulary already works.

**Verdict on §3.2:** FAIL. Policy legibility is served without framework macro-categories.

### §3.3 Cross-case comparability (SC-3)

**Claim:** comparing cases at the macro-category level surfaces patterns better than variable-level comparison.

**Test:** two existing layers already support cross-case comparison:

1. **Variable-level** — which Cᵢ are present in each case? Most granular.
2. **Dimension-level** — which of the 10 abundance dimensions are violated in each case? Already established organizational layer under Path F.

Adding macro-categories as a third comparison layer duplicates work that dimensions already do (e.g., "Environmental" macro-category correlates with Habitability + Ecosystem dimensions; "Community" correlates with Cohesion + Kindred + Institutional). The macro-level is redundant with the dimension-level when cases are compared abstractly.

**Counter:** macro-categories might emphasize different patterns than dimensions.

**Counter-counter:** if so, this would need demonstration — a specific case-comparison pattern that macro-categories surface and dimensions miss. No such pattern has been identified in the framework's 18-case development. The burden is on the proponent of macro-categories to show a distinctive comparative affordance; that burden has not been met.

**Verdict on §3.3:** FAIL. Dimensions already provide cross-case macro-level comparison; additional macro-categories are redundant.

### §3.4 Summary of usefulness tests

| Sub-claim | Verdict |
|---|---|
| SC-1 Pedagogical | PARTIAL PASS (chapter level); FAIL (framework level) |
| SC-2 Policy legibility | FAIL |
| SC-3 Cross-case comparability | FAIL |

The usefulness case, tested independently, produces at most chapter-level affordance — not framework-level value. This already separates Option B (chapter-layout permit) from Option C (framework-level canonical).

## §4. Success-criterion alignment

### §4.1 The success criterion

From README + project memory:

> If, ten-plus years from now, a labor lawyer uses "severed cost" in a brief or in open court and the judge does not need it explained — and the author whose name is on the book never knows this happened — the book has succeeded.

**Vocabulary adoption.** One specific phrase (severed cost / cost severance) becomes terminology in legal and policy contexts.

### §4.2 How vocabulary gets adopted

Vocabulary adoption empirically requires:

- **Sharpness.** A term is more adopted when it names something specific, identifiable, concretely useful. "Externality" succeeded because it named one specific economic phenomenon. "Severed cost" has similar potential if it remains sharp.
- **Concision.** Short terms get adopted more than long ones. "Severed cost" (two words) is adoptable in a way "household severed cost" / "environmental severed cost" would be less so.
- **Single-term focus.** Adoption campaigns concentrated on one term tend to succeed more than campaigns trying to adopt four or five terms at once. Pick one, drive it.
- **No competing internal vocabulary.** If the book uses macro-categories prominently, readers may come away remembering "Household / Community / Environmental / Intergenerational" rather than "severed cost." The book's own structure competes for reader mindshare.

### §4.3 Alignment test

Macro-categories at the framework level introduce competing vocabulary. A reader of the book encounters severed cost AND the four macro-categories AND the 10 dimensions AND the variables AND the gates. Adoption bandwidth is finite. The more vocabulary items compete for attention, the lower the adoption probability for the specific term we want adopted.

If the book's macro-categories become adoption-durable terms instead of or alongside severed cost, the success criterion is PARTIALLY met at best (some vocabulary adopted, but not the target) or MISSED entirely (wrong terms adopted).

**Empirical cross-reference:** Ostrom's work on commons governance is sometimes remembered for specific terms ("commons," "polycentric governance") but the work's intellectual reception often privileged the framework structure over the sharp term. The terms that got legal adoption are the ones Ostrom made sharpest. Commons Bonds can learn from this.

### §4.4 Verdict on success-criterion alignment

**NEGATIVE.** Macro-categories dilute the single-term vocabulary-adoption bet. Option C (framework canonical) is worst-aligned. Option B (chapter-layout permit) is near-neutral — chapter-internal narrative headings don't typically compete for adoption bandwidth the way framework-canonical vocabulary does. Option A (reject entirely) is maximally aligned with the success criterion.

## §5. Primary-goals alignment

Primary goals per project documentation + 2026-04-24 rigor-protocol v2.2.0 §22.4:

1. **Publishing** — book gets to a respectable venue.
2. **Positive academic reception** — peer reviewers defend rather than reject.
3. **Success criteria** — per §4 above (already tested NEGATIVE).
4. **Long-term project impact** — per success criterion plus durability as a reference.

### §5.1 Publishing alignment

Macro-categories neither help nor hurt publishing. Editors and reviewers evaluate books on their arguments and prose; structural choices at the macro-level matter less. Slight negative if macro-categories make the book look like it's reinventing standard policy vocabulary.

**Verdict:** NEUTRAL (slight lean negative).

### §5.2 Positive academic reception

Academic reviewers would apply rigor tests to macro-categories that parallel the tests v10 tiers failed:

- **Bounded-set challenge:** why four macro-categories and not three, five, or seven? What's the decision criterion?
- **Arbitrariness challenge:** why Household / Community / Environmental / Intergenerational and not Economic / Social / Ecological / Political? Multiple defensible partitions exist.
- **Ontological-commitment challenge:** the book claims these four categories exhaustively decompose extraction costs? Defensible only with unfalsifiable assertions.
- **Independence-gate challenge:** do the four categories partition cleanly, or do they overlap? "Environmental + Intergenerational" overlap in climate; "Community + Household" overlap in family-and-kin.

These are the same concerns that sank v10 tiers. Introducing macro-categories reopens them one layer up. Macro-categories would face academic pressure that canonical categories face.

**Verdict:** NEGATIVE. Academic reception is hurt, not helped. Reviewers would apply the same rigor the book's own internal rigor just applied to tiers.

### §5.3 Long-term project impact

Long-term impact depends on (a) vocabulary adoption (severed cost — §4 NEGATIVE) and (b) durability of the framework as a reference. Macro-categories specifically hurt (a) and have mixed effects on (b) — they might make the framework look more structured to casual readers, but they also expose the framework to the rigor critiques in §5.2. The expected long-term effect is negative or neutral.

**Verdict:** NEGATIVE to NEUTRAL.

|---|---|---|---|
| Publishing | Neutral (slight negative) | Neutral | Neutral |

| Success criterion | NEGATIVE (vocabulary dilution) | Near-neutral | POSITIVE (focus on severed cost) |

## §7. Abstraction-hides-severance test (author's prior, tested explicitly)

### §7.1 The concern as stated

Author: "grouping things is going to naturally have people look at something a certain way, and then all of a sudden we are creating another layer of abstraction allowing cost severance to remain hidden."

This is a specific claim that adding abstraction layers degrades the book's rhetorical function. Tested formally here.

### §7.2 How abstraction affects cost severance visibility

The book's rhetorical strategy relies on specificity. "McDowell County lost 80% of its population over 60 years." That number is concrete, named, checkable. The reader feels it.

Roll that up to a variable: "Community Transition Cost in McDowell: ~$5–15 per ton." More abstract than the population number, but still concrete — a dollar figure on a specific cost.

Roll it up again to a macro-category: "Community Costs in McDowell are significant." At this level, the specificity is almost gone. The claim is true but doesn't hit.

Each abstraction layer loses fidelity. The book can afford one layer of abstraction (variables, which are useful for pricing) because the variable naming is still specific. Adding a second abstraction layer (macro-categories) takes the book's prose to a level where the specifics that do the rhetorical work are no longer visible.

### §7.3 The specific concern about cost severance

"Cost severance" is defined precisely in the book: CS = RCV − B. A specific dollar-quantified gap between true cost and accountability bond. Under variables, cost severance remains specific — CS is the sum-over-variables computation against B.

Under macro-categories, a reader encountering "community cost severance" is implicitly invited to treat cost severance as a category-level phenomenon. "Community cost severance of $X" is less sharp than "black lung cost severance of $0.80/ton plus community transition cost severance of $10/ton plus ..." The macro aggregate blurs which specific costs were severed and who bore them.

This is the author's concern operationalized. Abstraction doesn't eliminate cost severance from the framework, but it makes cost severance operate at a category level where the mechanism is harder to see.

### §7.4 Verdict

**CONFIRMED.** The author's prior is structurally correct. Adding macro-categories at the framework level (Option C) degrades the specificity on which cost severance visibility depends. Option B preserves specificity at the framework level while permitting chapter-layout craft. Option A is maximally specific.

## §8. Aggregate verdict + recommended path

### §8.1 The five tests summarized

| Test | Option A (reject entirely) | Option B (chapter-layout permit) | Option C (framework canonical) |
|---|---|---|---|
| §3 Usefulness | PASS | PASS | FAIL (redundant with variables + dimensions) |
| §4 Success criterion | POSITIVE | NEAR-NEUTRAL | NEGATIVE |
| §5 Primary goals | POSITIVE | NEUTRAL to slight POSITIVE | NEGATIVE on 3 of 4 |
| §6 Informational goals | slight POSITIVE | NEUTRAL | mixed, leans NEGATIVE |
| §7 Abstraction test | PASS (max specificity) | PASS | FAIL (specificity degraded) |

### §8.2 Recommended path — Option B

**Reject macro-groupings as canonical framework structure (Option C is rejected).** The framework's structural layers remain: variables (Cᵢ) + the four gates (Section L) + dimensions (the 10 v1.3.0 abundance dimensions as organizational scaffolding per Path F).

**Permit narrative section headings at chapter-author's discretion (Option B is allowed).** A chapter author drafting Ch 8 or any case-study chapter may use narrative section headings that thematically group variables — but these headings are craft decisions, not framework categories. They are not enumerated, not canonical, not cross-referenced between chapters, and not claimed as structural.

**Guidelines for chapter-layout permit:**

- Section headings that group variables thematically are allowed.
- Section heading names should be *descriptive* (e.g., "The costs borne by households") rather than *categorical* (e.g., "Household Costs"). Descriptive phrasing signals narrative organization; categorical phrasing signals structural claim.
- No chapter should enumerate the section-heading set as a canonical list.
- No cross-chapter reference to section-heading groupings should treat them as framework-level entities.
- If a chapter's section headings happen to be similar to another chapter's, that's convergent craft — not a codified macro-structure.

**Option A alternative (reject entirely):** if the author prefers even tighter discipline, Option A is available. Reject macro-groupings at every level, including chapter-layout. Chapter section headings can exist but should not group variables thematically. Pure variable-enumeration with narrative pacing is the only permitted structure.

### §8.3 Why Option B over Option A

Option A (reject entirely) is maximally pure but sacrifices chapter-craft flexibility that the author might legitimately want. Ch 8 in particular walks many variables; allowing thematic narrative organization at that chapter's discretion preserves authorial freedom without creating canonical structure.

Option B is the default recommendation. If, during Ch 8 drafting, thematic narrative headings prove unnecessary or harmful, the author can choose to drop them per chapter — returning to Option A for that chapter only.
