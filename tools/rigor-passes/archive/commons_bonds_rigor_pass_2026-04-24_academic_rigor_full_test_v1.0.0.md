## §1. M1 — Framework Integrity (CORE) — post-restructure re-pass

### §1.1 M1.1 — CIT Falsifiability Gate

**Claim under test:** The Commons Inversion Test (CIT), with its two sub-forms (Commons-Absence Inversion + Commons-Consumption Inversion), holds as a falsifiability gate for distinguishing commons-extraction cost claims from non-commons-grounded claims.

**Probe:** Apply CIT to two test claims:

(a) **A pro-extraction claim:** "Coal extraction is fairly priced because the market sets the price."
- CAI: Would the extraction price differently if there were no commons claim (no atmospheric commons, no community-water commons, no intergenerational stock commons)? **Yes** — in pure no-commons world, the externality tail vanishes and the price is just private extraction cost. Extraction prices differently. → claim PASSES CAI as commons-grounded.
- CCI: Would the extraction price differently if the atmospheric commons were inexhaustible? **Yes** — at infinite atmospheric self-restoration, climate cost vanishes; only local externalities remain. Pricing changes substantially. → claim PASSES CCI as commons-consumption.
- **CIT verdict on the claim:** Cost-Severance proper. The pro-extraction claim's "fair pricing" assertion **fails its own implicit test** — it's only fair under inexhaustibility assumption that doesn't hold.

(b) **A weak-commons claim:** "All economic activity is commons-extraction because the human attention commons is consumed."
- CAI: Would ordinary economic activity price differently if there were no human-attention commons? **Mostly no** — most economic activity prices itself on inputs and outputs without commons-claim invocation. → claim FAILS CAI.
- **CIT verdict:** Not Cost-Severance proper. The "everything is commons-extraction" claim collapses under CAI; the test correctly distinguishes load-bearing commons claims from rhetorical commons-language.

**Verdict:** **PASS** — CIT with two sub-forms successfully discriminates. The CCI sub-form (commons-consumption inversion) is the v1.x AIT thought-experiment under a different name; the CAI sub-form (commons-absence inversion) is genuinely new. CAI strengthens the framework's discrimination because it catches rhetorical-commons inflation that CCI alone could miss.

**Open work:** A philosopher-of-science reviewer (Popperian / Lakatosian) might press: is CIT *empirically* falsifiable, or only *conceptually*? The framework's position (Tech Appendix supplement §6.1; rigor protocol M6.3) is that CIT is a thought-experiment falsification gate, not an empirical falsification gate — and that empirical falsification belongs to specific cost claims tested at M3.7 + M6.3 + M6.4. This positioning is defensible if the book makes it explicit; current Ch 6 methodology section + Tech Appendix supplement do make it explicit. **No revision needed; flag as M11 Character 18 (philosopher of science) probe item.**

### §1.3 M1.3 — CS = RCV − B coherence

**Claim under test:** The named relational claim CS = RCV − B holds as a primitive equation governing both backward-looking (CSD against B₁) and forward-looking (RCV against B₂) instruments.

**Probe:** Two-Instrument Architecture (supplement §2) reads the equation in two directions:
- Backward (CSD lens): What was severed = past CSD = past CS accumulated. B₁ Restitution Bond discharges this past CSD obligation. The equation holds: realized past CS = realized past RCV − realized past B (where realized past B is what should have been posted but wasn't).
- Forward (RCV lens): What's left = current RCV. B₂ Foreclosure Bond posts against potential future CS. The equation holds: future CS = future RCV − B₂ posting (where B₂ is what's now being required at extraction-decision time).

**Verdict:** **PASS.** The equation reads coherently in both directions. The two-instrument structure is the equation's natural temporal decomposition.

**Open work:** Open Insight #19 — empirical correlation between CSD and RCV across cases. If CSD-RCV correlation is high, the two instruments triangulate. If low, the two instruments are independent governance levers. Either result is academically defensible; the empirical question matters for chapter-level argument structure (Ch 5 + Ch 6). The framework is therefore not collapsing Ostrom's distinction — it is *honoring* it via the CCI sub-form. Cases that fail CCI are Ostrom-tradition coordination cases (different governance toolkit applies). Cases that pass CCI are extraction-context cases (this framework's toolkit applies).
