## §2. The candidate addition under test

### §2.1 The argument (compact form)

**Steelman:** The 2008 foreclosure cascade was inevitable given the housing bubble's scale. Mian & Sufi (*House of Debt*, 2014) document the leverage-cascade mechanics; Reinhart & Rogoff (*This Time Is Different*, 2009) document the historical pattern of post-bubble cascades across centuries and countries. Whatever the rescue architecture had been, ten million households were going to lose their homes. The rescue is therefore not the mechanism that produced asymmetric outcomes; the bubble's scale is.

**Framework response:** Even granting scale-inevitability, the rescue's *scope* is itself the cost-severance mechanism. The architecture chose what was in-scope (the companies whose business model produced the bubble; the institutions that bundled the instruments; the executives who lobbied for the regulatory framework — Gramm-Leach-Bliley 1999; Commodity Futures Modernization Act 2000 — that made the crisis possible) and chose what was out-of-scope (the ten million foreclosed households; the workers whose 401(k) balances absorbed MBS losses; the regional economies that contracted).

The choice of scope is not a function of bubble scale. The bubble would have happened with or without TARP. The post-crisis *distribution* of who got rescued and who didn't is a function of the rescue architecture, not the bubble. A society facing inevitable foreclosure cascade could have chosen to rescue cost-bearing parties (households) rather than (or in addition to) value-capturing parties (institutions). It chose institutions. It is choosing institutions still — the 2020 CARES Act's corporate-vs-individual relief proportions; the documented Paycheck Protection Program mis-allocation; the same scope-choice reproduced.

The framework's claim is therefore not that the rescue caused the crisis. The framework's claim is that the rescue's scope demonstrates which parties the system treats as in-scope for protection and which it does not. **Foreclosure-cascade inevitability does not refute the framework; it amplifies it.** When inevitability is granted, the asymmetric-scope choice is laid bare without the cushion of "the system did its best given constraints." The constraint was scale; the choice was scope; the choice was made.

### §2.2 What the framework adds beyond Mian & Sufi / Reinhart & Rogoff

| Dimension | Existing literature | Framework's distinctive contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Scale of the cascade** | Mian-Sufi document leverage mechanics; Reinhart-Rogoff document historical pattern | Granted as input; not the framework's claim |
| **Rescue's effectiveness** | Standard policy-evaluation literature debates whether TARP / Fed facilities prevented worse outcomes | Tangentially relevant; not the framework's mechanism claim |
| **Rescue's distributional asymmetry** | Documented in critical-political-economy literature (Klein; Stiglitz; Hacker) but not isolated as a *design variable distinct from scale* | **Framework's distinctive claim:** scope is a design variable; the scope-choice is empirical evidence of which parties the system treats as in-scope; the asymmetry is structural-cost-severance, not a function of bubble scale |
| **Cross-crisis pattern** | Historical financial-crisis literature documents many crises but rarely isolates the rescue-scope-choice across them | **Framework's distinctive claim:** the same scope-choice reproduces across crises (2008 + 2020 CARES + PPP), demonstrating the scope-choice is a structural pattern rather than a one-time response |

## §3. Q1 — Does the addition belong in the book?

### §3.1 M1 — Framework integrity

**Test:** Does the argument cohere with the framework's mechanism claim (cost severance = separation of value-capture from cost-bearing through structural distance / asymmetric architecture)?

**Finding:** **Strong coherence.** The framework's mechanism claim explicitly engages "structural absence of accountability instruments" as the cost-severance enabler. The rescue's scope-choice is a paradigm case: the *available* policy instrument was rescue-of-the-cost-bearing-party (mortgage modification at scale; principal forgiveness; HOLC-style refinancing); the *chosen* policy instrument was rescue-of-the-value-capturing-party. The choice between these is exactly what the framework's "B << RCV" empirical finding is about — the accountability instruments that would internalize cost are *available* but *not deployed*; the instruments that are deployed protect value-capture, not cost-bearing.

**Verdict on M1:** PASSES. The argument extends the framework's mechanism claim cleanly.

### §3.2 M3 — Book content

**Test:** Does the addition strengthen the book's argumentative arc?

**Finding:** **Yes — significantly.** The book's central claim (cost severance is structural, not incidental) gains force when the framework can name *scope-choice as a distinct design variable* that operates independently of crisis-scale. Without the addition, the 2008 case shows that asymmetric outcomes occurred. With the addition, the framework explains *why* asymmetric outcomes are not function of scale-inevitability — the explanation that the existing literature lacks. This is the framework's distinctive contribution at this case-class.

**Verdict on M3:** PASSES. Strong addition.

### §3.3 M5 — Dinner-table

**Test:** Does the addition help non-expert readers?

**Finding:** **Yes.** The "scope is the mechanism" reframing is more reader-accessible than the existing 2008 case prose, because it gives the reader a concrete handle (*the architecture chose who to rescue and who not to rescue*) for what could otherwise be an abstract structural claim. The reader can carry "scope-choice" as vocabulary into other domains.

**Verdict on M5:** PASSES.

### §3.4 M6 — Academic rigor

**Test:** Established literature engagement; prior-art positioning; precision.

**Finding:** **Strong engagement potential.** Cites Mian & Sufi 2014 (rigorous leverage-cascade documentation); Reinhart & Rogoff 2009 (historical pattern across centuries); arguably also Hacker 2006 *Great Risk Shift* (already in framework bibliography for risk-shift framing) and Stiglitz 2010 *Freefall* (post-2008 critical political economy).

The framework's distinctive contribution is the *isolation of scope-choice as a design variable* — Mian-Sufi treat it as an outcome of bubble dynamics; Reinhart-Rogoff treat it as historical pattern; Stiglitz frames it as political-economy choice but doesn't formalize it. The framework formalizes it as structural variable in the cost-severance accounting.

**M12 collision check:** "Scope" is not a framework-coined term; using it as a structural variable is normal-vocabulary use, not coinage. No prior-art collision risk.

**Verdict on M6:** PASSES. Engagement adds academic-credibility on a load-bearing axis.

### §3.5 M9 — Risk

**Test:** Cross-spectrum; legal exposure; collision; misuse risk.

**Finding:** **Manageable.**
- **Cross-spectrum:** The argument is structurally bipartisan (the 2020 CARES Act + PPP examples cross the aisle; both Bush 2008 + Obama 2009 + Trump 2020 + Biden COVID-relief reproductions can be cited; the scope-choice pattern is not partisan-coded).
- **Legal:** No specific persons named for litigation-actionable claims.
- **Misuse:** Argument could be co-opted for libertarian "stop rescuing anyone" reading or for left-populist "rescue everyone" reading; framework's response is structural-observation neutrality (the scope-choice exists; what to do about it is downstream policy work).

**Verdict on M9:** PASSES with cross-spectrum-tone discipline.
