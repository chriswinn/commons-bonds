## §2. The question under test

### §2.1 The term

**RCV = Residual Commons Value.** The framework's central quantification — the true intergenerational cost of extraction, computed as the integral of the sum of admitted cost terms (Cᵢ) against a declining discount factor over infinite horizon.

**Formal definition (per Section M.1 of Technical Appendix v0.0.4):**
```
RCV(R, t₀, Context) = ∫ₜ₀^∞ { Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ Cᵢ(R, t, Context) } · D(t, t₀) dt
```

**The name's semantic work:**
- **Residual** — what remains unpriced after conventional accounting mechanisms (markets, accountability bonds, existing externality instruments) have been subtracted. The residual is what the framework makes visible.
- **Commons** — the shared pool bearing the unpriced cost (communities, ecosystems, future generations).
- **Value** — the dollar-denominated quantity (the unit of measurement is [$ · resource-unit⁻¹]).

**Embedded role in the framework:**
- One side of the flagship equation CS = RCV − B.
- The integrand of Section B.1 / Section M.1 RCV formula.
- Referenced in all 18 case studies.
- Central to Ch 6 methodology + Ch 8 walkthrough + Technical Appendix.

**Audit findings (per Principle #2):**
- RCV acronym: 658 occurrences in 34 files
- Residual Commons Value (proper-noun): 71 occurrences in 24 files
- "commons value" general usage: 72 occurrences (heavy overlap with proper-noun form)
- Alternative "true intergenerational cost": 10 occurrences (descriptive, rare)
- Alternative "true cost of extraction": 2 occurrences (rare)

### §2.2 Options (widened per Principle #2)

**A — Keep "Residual Commons Value (RCV)" as current** with acronym + full-form discipline across contexts.

**B — Rename to "True Intergenerational Cost (TIC)"** — more immediately understandable; loses "residual" signal (doesn't indicate what's accounted-for vs what's not).

**C — Rename to "Commons Value (CV)"** — simpler; loses "residual" signal; "commons value" is ambiguous (unpriced commons value? market value of commons? total commons worth?).

**D — Drop acronym; use full form only** — all 658 acronym uses become full form. Prose weight increase.

**E — Rename to different root entirely** (e.g., "Commons Severance Cost," "Intergenerational Debt," "Severed Commons Value"). Triggers equation rewrite.

**F — Placeholder** for any alternative.

## §3. Test 1 — Individual term merits (name-quantity match)

### §3.1 Each word does work

**"Residual":** what the framework adds. Conventional accounting (market prices, accountability bonds, existing externality pricing) captures a portion of the true cost. The residual — what's left unpriced — is what RCV computes.

Without "Residual": the term would be "Commons Value" (Option C) — ambiguous between total commons value, priced commons value, unpriced commons value, or market commons value. The structural claim (we're computing the *unpriced* portion specifically) disappears.

**"Commons":** where the cost lands. Communities, ecosystems, future generations — the shared pools bearing the residual unpriced cost. This word locates the cost structurally.

Without "Commons": the term would be "Residual Value" — ambiguous between residual asset value (finance), residual heat, residual variance (statistics), etc. Framework-specificity lost.

**"Value":** the dollar unit of measurement.

Without "Value": the term would be "Residual Commons" — sounds like a commons that's been residualized (meaningless).

**Verdict:** all three words are load-bearing. Each can be stress-tested and none can be removed without semantic loss.

### §3.2 Alternative names — precision test

**"True Intergenerational Cost":** the word "true" is editorially risky — asserting the framework's claim to truth as part of the name. Also: "intergenerational" captures only one of three locus-categories (also communities, also ecosystems). The phrase is narrower than RCV.

**"Commons Value":** loses "Residual" entirely. What commons value? Mean commons value? Total? Priced? The structural claim disappears.

**"True Cost of Extraction":** doesn't specify what makes it "true" (vs what — market-priced? already-compensated?). Less precise than RCV.

**Verdict:** RCV's name-precision is unmatched by the alternatives. Principle #3 check: PASSES cleanly.

### §3.3 Test 1 verdict

Option A wins on name-quantity match. Alternatives each lose on specific semantic dimensions.

## §4. Test 2 — Quantification-position performance

### §4.1 RCV's structural role

RCV = the right-side quantity of the flagship equation CS = RCV − B. This is not just a term in the framework — it's one of two quantities the framework computes to produce its flagship result (cost severance).

**The equation:**
- CS (cost severance — the gap)
- RCV (the true cost)
- B (accountability bond — what's been priced)
- Relation: CS = RCV − B

Renaming RCV forces the equation to be rewritten. Every reference to the equation (in Tech Appendix, all rigor passes, Ch 6, case studies) would need updating.

### §4.2 Each option's equation-integration fit

**Option A (RCV):** CS = RCV − B. Clean. Established. 658 references.

**Option B (TIC):** CS = TIC − B. TIC as acronym is clunky ("tic" has non-framework meanings). Full form "CS = True Intergenerational Cost − B" is verbose.

**Option C (CV):** CS = CV − B. CV has many academic meanings (coefficient of variation, curriculum vitae, commercial vehicle). Collision risk.

**Option D (drop acronym):** CS = Residual Commons Value − B. Long equation form; acceptable but verbose.

**Option E (different root):** equation rewrite required; all references updated.

### §4.3 Test 2 verdict

Option A's equation integration is clean; alternatives each introduce friction.
