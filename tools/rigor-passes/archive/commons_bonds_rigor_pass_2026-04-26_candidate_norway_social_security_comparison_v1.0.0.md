## §2. The candidate addition under test

### §2.1 The argument (compact form)

**The structural observation:** Norway in 1969-1990 chose to capture petroleum rents into a sovereign wealth fund (the Government Petroleum Fund, renamed Government Pension Fund Global in 2006) and to invest those rents globally on behalf of future Norwegian generations. The fund now holds approximately $1.9 trillion in assets, generates more income from investment returns than the underlying oil and gas production, and has captured roughly 70-80 cents of every dollar of net Norwegian petroleum wealth on behalf of the national community.

The U.S. in 1935 created Social Security as a pay-as-you-go system funded by current-worker payroll deductions providing benefits to current retirees, with surpluses held in special-issue Treasury bonds rather than invested as productive sovereign-fund capital. The system has, throughout the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, expanded benefits without funding the demographic-deficit window that actuarial reports identified as inevitable. The result is the $108 trillion unfunded liability the Ch 5 case prices.

The two architectures address structurally similar problems (intergenerational accumulation of social goods through fiscal architecture) and produce structurally different outcomes (Norway's fund compounds; Social Security depletes). The difference is not a function of national wealth (the U.S. could afford a sovereign-fund architecture; the U.S. simply did not build one). The difference is not a function of demographic transition (Norway also faced demographic transition; its architecture is designed to absorb it). The difference is a function of architectural choice — and the choice was contingent on specific historical decisions made at specific moments.

**The contingency-history element (the part that makes this Book 1 not Book 2):**

The U.S. was not architecturally precluded from a sovereign-fund retirement architecture; specific historical decisions chose otherwise:

- **1935 — Social Security Act:** designed as pay-as-you-go in part because the Roosevelt administration prioritized speed-to-deployment in the Depression-era political window, accepting design constraints that locked in the architecture for subsequent expansions
- **1939 amendments:** added family + survivor benefits + adjusted from full-funding-with-trust-fund-buildup toward partial-funding pay-as-you-go in response to political concerns about trust-fund-as-fiscal-overhang
- **1950s expansions:** expanded coverage substantially during the post-war demographic-surplus window without funding the demographic-deficit window the actuarial reports identified
- **1960s-1970s benefit expansions:** Medicare 1965 + Cost-of-Living Adjustments 1972 + various benefit-formula increases — all enacted during demographic surplus; all implicitly funded by the demographic-deficit window
- **1983 Greenspan Commission reforms:** raised payroll taxes + raised eligibility ages slowly + began trust-fund accumulation toward future-deficit absorption — but the accumulation was held in special-issue Treasury bonds (the General Fund borrowing facility) rather than invested as sovereign-fund capital

Each of these was a contingent decision. The architecture could have been built differently. Norway's GPFG (1990 onward) demonstrates what one such different architecture looks like, in a country that faced structurally similar pressures and made specific architectural choices the U.S. did not.

**The framework's contribution:** the framework names the architectural-choice as a design variable distinct from the demographic-transition pressure both architectures faced. Demographic transition is the constraint both architectures address. Architecture is the choice each society made. The constraint was scale; the choice was architecture; the choice was made.

## §3. Q1A — Does the addition belong in BOOK 1?

### §3.1 Book-scope discipline (per Open Insight #13 + v1.0.3 §6.1)

**Test:** Apply the framework-naming-vs-applied-advocacy distinction. Book 1 says "here is what the framework names"; Book 2 says "here is what specific society X should do, prescribed at instrument level." The Norway-Social-Security candidate sits on the boundary; the question is which side of the boundary.

#### §3.1.1 Structural-observation framing (Book 1 — DEFENSIBLE)

Frame the candidate as structural observation:

> *"Norway in 1969-1990 chose, through specific historical decisions, an architecture that captures petroleum rents into a sovereign wealth fund operationalizing Hartwick's rule for intergenerational equity. The fund accumulates; the fund compounds; the fund preserves capital across decades. The United States in 1935 chose, through specific historical decisions, an architecture that funds retirement through pay-as-you-go from current-worker payroll deductions, with surpluses held as Treasury obligations rather than invested as sovereign capital. The fund depletes; the obligations accumulate; the cost-severance pattern Chapter 5 priced is the structural outcome of that architectural choice."*

This framing:
- Names a structural fact (two architectures; two outcomes)
- Describes the contingent-history-of-choice (specific decisions; specific moments)
- Does not prescribe which architecture any particular society should adopt
- Honors epistemic-humility per Insight #14 (Norway is canonical existing exemplar; preconditions matter; no claim to having solved it)
- Pairs with the framework's existing structural-observation comparisons (Ch 4 Norway-Nigeria; Ch 9 reachable-horizon cluster)

**This framing PASSES Book 1 rigor per v1.0.3 §6.1.**

#### §3.1.2 Applied-advocacy framing (Book 2 — FAILS Book 1 rigor)

Frame the candidate as policy prescription:

> *"The United States should adopt a Norway-derived sovereign-pension-fund architecture for retirement security. The transition would proceed through these specific legislative steps: a phased-in payroll-tax increase + sovereign-fund accumulation period + transition-window benefit-protection mechanism + intergenerational-fund-governance structure. Existing pay-as-you-go obligations would be honored through the transition window via [specific mechanism]; sovereign-fund accumulation would begin in [specific year]; the architecture would be fully operational by [specific year]. The legislative pathway would require..."*

This framing:
- Names specific transition-mechanism designs
- Engages applied-advocacy on named architectural alternatives
- Triggers v1.0.3 §6.1 lone-author asymmetric-exposure concerns (the framework prescribes a specific instrument design as THE fix)
- Falls within Book 2's scope per the explicit boundary

**This framing FAILS Book 1 rigor.** Book 2 territory.

### §3.2 Framework-existing-precedent test

The framework currently uses structural-observation framing in three places that establish the precedent for the Norway-Social-Security candidate:

1. **Ch 4 Norway-Nigeria contrast** — names the same architectural choice for petroleum rents (Norway captured; Nigeria did not), produces structural-observation about cost-severance outcomes, does not prescribe which architecture Nigeria should adopt

**Verdict:** the Norway-Social-Security candidate fits the same precedent. If the existing three precedents pass Book 1 rigor (they do), the Norway-Social-Security candidate passes the same rigor under the same framing.

**The candidate belongs in Book 1 IF framed as structural observation.** Per existing precedent + v1.0.3 §6.1 scope discipline + Insight #14 epistemic-humility discipline. The structural-observation framing has been used successfully across the framework already; this candidate fits the same form. ## §4. Q1B — If Book 1, what specific framing?

The framing must:

1. **Name the architectural choice** (Norway: sovereign-fund; U.S.: pay-as-you-go-with-Treasury-trust-fund). Concrete.
2. **Trace the contingent-history** that produced the U.S. choice (1935 Social Security Act; 1939 amendments; 1950s coverage expansions; 1960s-70s benefit expansions; 1983 Greenspan reforms). Specific moments.
3. **Surface the design-variable distinction** (architecture is choice; demographic transition is constraint; the constraint was scale; the choice was architecture; the choice was made). Framework's distinctive analytical contribution.
4. **Honor Insight #14 epistemic-humility** — Norway is canonical *existing* example; preconditions matter (rule of law; institutional architecture preventing plundering); no claim to having solved it; no prescriptive transition-mechanism for the U.S.
5. **Pair with the contingency argument** (stands-the-test §1) — both arguments share the form "specific moments produced the current architecture; the architecture is contingent; what was decided can be re-decided." Norway-Social-Security adds one more pair of contingent-architectural-decisions to the contingency-argument's list.

**Framing verdict:** structural-observation register paired with the contingency-argument form. Specifically: the Norway-Social-Security comparison should be presented as evidence for the contingency argument's broader claim, not as a free-standing policy proposal.
