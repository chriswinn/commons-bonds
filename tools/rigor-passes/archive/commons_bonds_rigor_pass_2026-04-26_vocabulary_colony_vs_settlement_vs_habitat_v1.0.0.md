## §1. The three vocabulary options

### §1.1 Option C — "colony" (current usage)

**Definitional core.** A group of people living in a place, especially a self-sustaining population in a previously-unsettled or remote location. Carries biological-population sense (ant colony, bacterial colony) AND political-history sense (settler colony, mining colony, colonial possession).

**Current usage in Ch 7.** Approximately 30-40 occurrences across the chapter — "a small colony on Mars," "the administrator of the colony's resources," "any colony on any world," "the colony breathes its own emissions," etc. The chapter also uses "colonist" / "colonization" in some derived forms.

**Connotation cluster.** European colonialism (settler-colonies, slave-colonies, plantation-colonies) + biological-population + permanence + space-settlement-discourse (O'Neill 1976; Sagan; Robinson Mars trilogy; Mars Society; SpaceX rhetoric).

### §1.2 Option S — "settlement"

**Definitional core.** A place where people have established themselves to live; the act of establishing such a place. Carries population-establishment sense (pioneer settlement, refugee settlement) and (heavily, in academic discourse) settler-colonial sense (Tuck & Yang 2012; Coulthard 2014; Glenn 2015; Veracini 2010).

**Substitution path.** Direct word-substitution for most occurrences ("settlement" for "colony"; "settlers" for "colonists"; "settling" for "colonizing"). Some sentence-level rework needed where "colony" carries specific structural weight.

**Connotation cluster.** Pioneer/frontier (American West; Israeli settlements in occupied territories; Australian frontier) + refugee-resettlement + community-establishment + (in academic discourse) settler-colonialism-as-structural-category.

### §1.3 Option H — "habitat"

**Definitional core.** A natural home or environment for an organism; a structure providing living conditions for humans in extreme environments. Most politically neutral; rooted in NASA / ESA / JAXA engineering vocabulary for off-world living-structures.

**Substitution path.** Cannot be drop-in substitute for "colony" because "habitat" denotes the place/structure, not the population. Requires "inhabitants" / "population" / "residents" for the population-referent. Many sentences need restructuring rather than word-substitution.

**Connotation cluster.** Animal habitat (biological/ecological sense) + NASA Habitation module + Habitat for Humanity + space-station / Mars-base technical literature + clinical-engineering register.

## §3. Module-by-module comparative evaluation

For each module, the three options are evaluated and ranked. Notation: ★ = best for that module; ○ = acceptable; ✗ = problematic (with reason). Tie-breaks noted explicitly.

### §3.1 M1 — Framework integrity (CORE)

**M1.5 (Universality across scales × environments)** is the load-bearing sub-module. The framework's substantive claim is about bounded systems with distance-compression. Which vocabulary most aligns with that substantive claim?

- **Colony (C):** ○ population-coherence sense aligns with "people doing pricing decisions"; political-history baggage is orthogonal to framework-substance.
- **Settlement (S):** ○ same population-coherence sense; same orthogonality.
- **Habitat (H):** ★ "habitat" most directly evokes the bounded-environment / distance-compression aspect that is the framework's substantive concern. The chapter's claim is that distance-compression makes cost-severance impossible; "habitat" naturally evokes a bounded environment in a way "colony" does not.

**M1.5 ranking:** H > C ≈ S. Margin: small but real. The framework's substantive concern is bounded-environment-with-distance-compression, and "habitat" carries that substance more directly than the other two.

### §3.2 M2 — Case study tests (M2.4 chapter fit; M2.5 register fit)

**M2.4 (Chapter fit).** Ch 7 is the universality-test chapter; the chapter's purpose is testing the framework on bounded-system scenarios. All three vocabularies fit the chapter's purpose.

- **Colony:** ★ established usage; reads as already-fitted.
- **Settlement:** ○ fits but introduces pioneer/frontier register subtly.
- **Habitat:** ○ fits but pulls register toward technical/clinical.

**M2.5 (Register fit).** Ch 7's register is essayistic-philosophical with technical anchors.

- **Colony:** ★ matches the essayistic-philosophical register; reads narratively.
- **Settlement:** ○ matches; slightly more frontier-evocative than the chapter's neutral register.
- **Habitat:** ✗ pulls register toward clinical-engineering; sentences like "the habitat is ten thousand people" don't work grammatically without rework.

**M2 ranking:** C > S > H. Habitat penalized for register-shift and grammatical-rework cost.

### §3.4 M4 — Craft tests

**M4.3 (Prose voice consistency).** Which option preserves Ch 7's voice most cleanly?

- **Settlement:** ○ different voice register than colony; more frontier-evocative.
- **Habitat:** ✗ shifts voice toward technical-clinical; risks register-shift across the chapter.

**M4.6 (Prose quality / line-edit readiness).** Which option requires least rework?

- **Colony:** ★ no rework needed; current state.
- **Settlement:** ○ word-substitution suffices for most occurrences; minor sentence-level rework where "colony" carries specific weight.
- **Habitat:** ✗ many sentences require restructuring because "habitat" is place-not-people. "The colony is ten thousand people" → "The habitat houses ten thousand people" or "Ten thousand people live in the habitat." Not a substitution; a rewrite.

## §7. Where the trade-offs sit

The vocabulary decision is not unidimensional. Habitat wins most modules but loses the craft modules that matter for line-by-line readability and narrative engagement. The two primary trade-offs:

### §7.1 Craft cost of habitat

Habitat-vocabulary substitution requires real prose-rework, not drop-in substitution. Estimated effort: 20–30 sentence-level rewrites in Ch 7 (sentences where "colony" carries population-not-place weight). Some rewrites will produce slightly less narratively-vivid prose ("Ten thousand people live in the habitat" is less punchy than "The colony is ten thousand people").

**Mitigation paths within habitat option:**
- Use **"habitat"** for the bounded-environment / structure references.
- Use **"inhabitants"** or **"residents"** or **"population"** for the population references where "colony" was carrying that weight.
- Use **"colony"** in specific narrative phrases where it's load-bearing for the chapter's voice (e.g., the chapter intro "small colony on Mars" might be retained for narrative momentum if a single high-leverage usage).

This is closer to a **mixed-vocabulary** approach within the habitat-dominant option.

### §7.2 The "incumbent" advantage of colony

Colony has a real incumbent advantage: it's already integrated, already tested in the prose, and reads as part of the chapter's existing voice. Switching is real work. The question is whether the M6 / M9 / M11 / M12 advantages of habitat outweigh the M2 / M4 craft cost of switching.

**This rigor pass's view:** the academic-rigor / risk-exposure / publishing-path advantages outweigh the craft cost, especially given the Berggruen-window proximity (2026-08-17). Berggruen committee composition is unknowable in advance; the framework's resilience against a hostile reviewer flagging colonialism-redux concerns is materially improved under habitat-vocabulary.

### §7.3 The "narrative" advantage of colony

Colony reads more narratively. "The colony breathes its own emissions" is a more rhetorically-pointed sentence than "the inhabitants breathe their own emissions." Some of Ch 7's most rhetorically-charged sentences carry that punctuation by virtue of "colony" being a singular collective noun.

**Mitigation:** rework load-bearing sentences specifically. The cost-bearer/value-capturer-overlap line ("the colony breathes its own emissions") can become: "the inhabitants breathe their own emissions, which means the cost-bearer and the value-capturer share the same lungs" — adds depth, recovers some of the rhetorical punch.

### §7.4 The settler-colonialism penalty on settlement

Settlement is rated lower than this rigor pass's initial intuition would suggest. The reason: settler-colonialism academic discourse is at peak intensity (2010s-2020s); using "settlement" / "settler" vocabulary in a manuscript that engages reparations economics (Coates 2014; Darity & Mullen 2020; Hamilton et al. 2015) and civic-republican autonomy (Pettit 1997; Skinner 1998) is a positioning-mismatch a careful academic reviewer will flag aggressively. Settlement-vocabulary's lighter-than-colony surface association is offset by its heavier theoretical-apparatus capture surface.

## §8. Three implementation paths

### §8.1 Path A — Pure habitat substitution

Replace "colony" with "habitat" / "inhabitants" / "residents" throughout Ch 7. ~30-40 substitutions; ~20-30 sentence-level rewrites.

**Pros:** maximal academic-rigor / risk-exposure benefit; cleanest M6 / M9 / M11 / M12 profile.

**Cons:** highest craft-cost; loses some narrative vividness; ~30-45 minutes of careful sentence-by-sentence work.

### §8.2 Path B — Mixed habitat-and-narrative vocabulary

Use "habitat" / "inhabitants" / "residents" as primary vocabulary; retain 2-3 high-leverage "colony" usages where the narrative-vividness is load-bearing (e.g., chapter intro). Use "colony" only in contexts where the population-coherence-as-singular-noun is rhetorically essential.

**Pros:** captures most academic-rigor benefits while preserving narrative punch at key moments; lower craft-cost than Path A.

**Cons:** mixed-vocabulary risks reader confusion ("why colony here and habitat there?"); some inconsistency may need explicit footnote or in-prose acknowledgment.

### §8.3 Path C — Retain colony with enhanced guard sentence (status quo + Candidate #8 mitigation)

Keep "colony" throughout; add the enhanced guard sentence (per Candidate #8 §6.3) to Element B's prose; engage Deudney 2020 / Tuck & Yang lineage explicitly via bibliography + brief acknowledgment.

**Pros:** zero craft-cost; preserves chapter's voice as-tested; Candidate #8's own mitigation framework already addresses the colonialism-redux concern.

**Cons:** highest residual M9 / M11 / M12 risk; depends on guard-sentence sufficiency holding under hostile reviewer pressure.

## §10. Q2 verdict — Recommended path: Path B (mixed habitat-and-narrative)

The three implementation paths trade off academic-rigor benefit against craft cost. Path A maximizes the former; Path C minimizes the latter; Path B sits in the middle.

**Recommendation: Path B (mixed habitat-and-narrative vocabulary).**

Rationale:

- Path A maximizes academic-rigor benefit, but the craft cost (~30-45 minutes; ~20-30 sentence-level rewrites; some loss of narrative vividness) is real and the marginal benefit over Path B is small.
- Path C preserves craft cost-free but leaves the residual M9 / M11 / M12 risk that this rigor pass exists to address; given the Berggruen-window proximity, the residual risk is material.
- Path B captures ~85% of Path A's academic-rigor benefit at ~50% of the craft cost. The mixed vocabulary can be made coherent through consistent rules (habitat = the bounded environment; inhabitants = the people; colony retained only for 1-2 specific narrative moments where it's load-bearing).

**Specific Path B implementation rules:**

- **Default vocabulary:** "habitat" for the place; "inhabitants" or "residents" for the population.
- **Retained "colony" usages (2-3 maximum):** only where the noun is doing single-subject narrative work that "habitat" cannot do. Candidate retentions (to be evaluated during execution):
  - Chapter intro phrasing: "a small colony on Mars" — possibly retain for first-introduction; alternative "a small inhabited Mars habitat" reads less naturally.
  - The cost-bearer/value-capturer-overlap line ("the colony breathes its own emissions") — likely rework to "the inhabitants breathe their own emissions" with the population-coherence preserved by surrounding prose.
- **Cross-scenario sweep:** "any colony on any world" → "any habitat on any world" or "any settlement on any world" — wait, settlement is rejected. → "any habitat on any world" (loses some population-coherence; consider "any inhabited place on any world").
- **Derived terms:** "colonist" → "inhabitant" / "resident"; "colonization" → "settlement" (used as activity-noun rather than place-noun; this is a careful exception to the settlement-as-place rejection).

Actually — important caveat. The §3.4 settlement-rejection was about settlement as the *primary place-noun*. Settlement as an *activity-noun* (the act of establishing a settlement) is much lighter on the settler-colonialism critic-pressure surface. So "the framework's behavior on bounded settlements" would re-trigger the settler-colonial concern, but "the question of off-world settlement [as activity]" is closer to neutral.

This nuance suggests: Path B can use "settlement" sparingly as an activity-noun while using "habitat" as the primary place-noun and "inhabitants" as the primary population-noun.

## §15. Verb-and-derived-noun cluster — inhabit / inhabitants / inhabiting / inhabitation + alternatives

The Q2 / Path B recommendation in §10 invokes "inhabitants" as the population-noun complement to "habitat." Author raised a follow-up question (verbatim): *"should we also test variances of those words e.g. 'inhabiting' other worlds, asteroids, moons?"* This section evaluates the verb-and-derived-noun cluster as its own unit, distinct from the place-noun decision.

### §15.1 The inhabit cluster

"Inhabit" / "inhabiting" / "inhabitants" / "inhabitation" share the Latin root *habitare* (to dwell) with "habitat." They are the natural verb-and-population complement to habitat-as-place-noun. The cluster's components:

- **"inhabit" (verb)** — "humans inhabit Mars"; "any bounded place humans inhabit"; relational, action-oriented.
- **"inhabiting" (gerund / participle)** — "humans inhabiting Mars and other worlds"; descriptive, can serve as noun-like phrase.
- **"inhabitants" (noun, plural)** — "the inhabitants breathe their own emissions"; population-noun with population-coherence sense.
- **"inhabitation" (noun, abstract)** — "off-world inhabitation"; the state-or-act-of-dwelling sense; works as activity-noun but reads slightly stilted.

### §15.2 Module-level evaluation of the inhabit cluster

**M1.5 (universality):** ★ The verb-form "inhabit" emphasizes the relational-dwelling sense that the framework's distance-compression argument depends on. "Humans inhabiting an off-world habitat" carries the structural meaning "humans + bounded environment + dwelling-relationship-with-place" — exactly what the framework's substantive concern is.

**M2.5 (register fit):** ○ The cluster reads in Ch 7's essayistic-philosophical register without effort. "Any place humans inhabit" reads more naturally than "any habitat where people live."

**M3.5 (empirical grounding):** ○ The cluster is widely-used in scientific and popular-science writing about space and ecology; minimal sourcing burden.

**M4.6 (prose quality / line-edit readiness):** ★ The cluster solves several of the awkwardness problems Path A introduces:
- "any colony on any world" → "any place humans inhabit, on any world" (works well)
- "the colony's survival is contingent" → "the inhabitants' survival is contingent" (preserves population-coherence)
- "any thoughtful administrator of any colony on any world" → "any thoughtful administrator of any inhabited place on any world" (works)
- The verb form "inhabit" is especially useful for the chapter-introduction phrase the author flagged: "we might one day expand beyond the earth to inhabit other locations off world" reads cleanly and avoids both colony and settlement entirely.

**M5 (dinner-table):** ★ "Inhabit" / "inhabitants" / "inhabiting" are widely-understood; the verb-cluster is more accessible than "habitation" alone.

**M6 / M9 / M11 / M12 (academic rigor / risk / critic / honesty):** ★ The cluster carries minimal political-history baggage. "Inhabit" has no specific colonialist or settler-colonialist resonance in academic discourse; it's a general-purpose verb.

**Aggregate:** the inhabit cluster is the natural verb-and-population-noun complement to habitat-as-place-noun. They form a coherent vocabulary system rooted in shared etymology.

### §15.3 Alternative population-noun: "residents"

A candidate worth surfacing alongside "inhabitants":

- **"residents"** — those who live in a place; lighter population-coherence sense than "inhabitants"; even cleaner political-history profile (no associations with colonialism, settler-colonialism, or expansionism); slightly more bureaucratic register.

Comparison test in load-bearing sentence: "the inhabitants breathe their own emissions" vs. "the residents breathe their own emissions." Both work; "inhabitants" has slightly stronger population-coherence connotation; "residents" is cleaner but reads less narratively-vivid.

**Recommendation:** primary population-noun is "inhabitants" (paired with "habitat" via shared etymology); "residents" available as variation for stylistic relief and to avoid repetition.

### §15.4 Place variants for off-world locations

Author flagged that the vocabulary needs to work across "other worlds, asteroids, moons." Application of the recommended cluster across location types:

| Location | Place phrasing | Population phrasing | Verb phrasing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mars | Mars habitat / inhabited Mars | Mars inhabitants / Mars residents | inhabiting Mars |
| Asteroid | asteroid habitat / inhabited asteroid | asteroid inhabitants | inhabiting an asteroid |
| Moon (Earth's) | lunar habitat / inhabited Moon | lunar inhabitants / Moon residents | inhabiting the Moon |
| Other moons (e.g., Europa, Titan) | habitat on Europa / inhabited Europa | Europa inhabitants | inhabiting Europa |
| Exoplanet | exoplanet habitat / inhabited exoplanet | exoplanet inhabitants | inhabiting an exoplanet |
| Free-flying station | space-station habitat / orbital habitat | station inhabitants / station residents | inhabiting a space station |
| Generic | bounded off-world habitat / any inhabited place off Earth | off-world inhabitants | inhabiting any bounded off-world place |

All variants work cleanly across the location set. The cluster is robust to location-substitution — which is exactly what M1.5 universality requires.

### §15.5 Activity-noun: "inhabitation" vs "settlement"

The activity-noun question (the act of establishing a population in a place) was flagged in §10 as a possible exception where "settlement" might be acceptable as activity-noun even though rejected as place-noun.

- **"inhabitation"** — "off-world inhabitation"; works but reads slightly stilted; not commonly used.
- **"settlement"** as activity-noun — "off-world settlement [as activity]"; cleaner reading; carries lighter critic-pressure surface than place-noun usage.
- **"settling"** as gerund — "humans settling on other worlds"; works; lightest of the three.

**Recommendation:** prefer **"settling"** (gerund) for activity-references; "settlement" (activity-noun) acceptable in low-frequency usage; "inhabitation" reserved for cases where neither works.

### §15.6 Updated Path B implementation rules

Per the inhabit-cluster integration, Path B implementation rules are refined as follows:

- **Primary place-noun:** **habitat** (or "inhabited [Mars/asteroid/etc.]" for context-specific phrasing).
- **Primary population-noun:** **inhabitants** (with "residents" as stylistic-variation alternative).
- **Verb form:** **inhabit** / **inhabiting** for relational-dwelling expressions ("humans inhabiting other worlds").
- **Activity-noun:** **settling** (gerund) primary; **settlement** (activity-noun) acceptable for low-frequency usage; **inhabitation** for niche cases.
- **Retained narrative "colony" usages:** ≤ 2-3 maximum, only where load-bearing for chapter voice (chapter intro candidate; possibly one rhetorically-charged sentence).
- **"Colonist" / "colonization" / "colonize":** eliminated entirely; replaced by **"inhabitant"** / **"settling"** / **"inhabit"** / **"settle"** as context dictates.
