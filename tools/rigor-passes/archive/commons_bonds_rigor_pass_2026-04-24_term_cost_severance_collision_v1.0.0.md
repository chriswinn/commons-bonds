**The key move (Ch 1 opening framing — canonical form):**

> *"Corporate America knows the word 'severance.' When a company sheds workers to cut costs, we pay the severed employee — severance — and call it a cost of doing business. The word honors something: when you cut someone loose, you owe them. You sever the tie, and you pay for the severing.*
>
> *Cost severance works the opposite direction. When a company extracts value from a commons — a community, an ecosystem, a future generation — it sheds the COST of that value onto the source. The tie between value-capturer and cost-bearer gets severed, but the payment architecture of employment severance is absent. The cost-bearer gets stuck with the cost the value-capturer got paid to generate.*
>
> *So: severance we understand in employment. Cost severance is the same move, applied to the relationship between value-capturers and the commons they draw from — except the bill never comes due to the severing party. That's the problem this book is about."*

## §2. The question under test

### §2.1 The collision

HR/accounting "severance costs" is a well-established term in:
- International Accounting Standards (IAS 37 — provisions for employee termination)
- US GAAP (ASC 712, 715 — postemployment benefits + termination benefits)
- Big-4 accounting firms' published guidance (PwC, KPMG, Deloitte, EY)
- Corporate-law practice (severance agreements, separation payments)
- HR-professional vocabulary (severance packages, severance pay, cost-of-layoffs)

Every tax lawyer, CPA, corporate lawyer, HR professional, and large-organization finance person reads *"cost severance"* (or the slightly-different "severance costs") as **the cost of paying employees to leave**. Reading reflex happens before analytical parsing.

### §2.2 Framework meaning of "Cost Severance"

Per Terms Index (commit prior to this pass): the mechanism by which an act of value-extraction separates (severs) the value-capturer from the cost-bearer — producing a Severed Cost that lands on the party who did not benefit. Ring-1 flagship mechanism-name. Paired with Severed Cost (the result) and Value Extraction (the causal event).

**Verbal form:** active mechanism — "X cost-severs Y" (X severs the cost onto Y).

**Rhetorical image:** severance-as-rupture — the tie between who benefits and who pays gets cut.

### §2.3 The collision's severity — M12 assessment

Per M12 §16.5.4d severity matrix:

- **Target audience overlap:** Legal (HIGH — tax + corporate lawyers are central adoption target per success criterion), Policy (HIGH — many policy professionals have accounting backgrounds), Academic econ (MEDIUM), Sociology (LOW), Commons-governance (LOW), General reader (LOW).
- **Aggregate severity:** **HIGH for the legal / accounting / policy axis.** This is exactly the audience whose adoption the framework's success criterion depends on.
- **Reading reflex:** first-encounter parse will default to HR meaning for ~30-40% of target audience. Without explicit framing, these readers either (a) get confused, (b) dismiss the framework as using sloppy vocabulary, or (c) back-parse on every subsequent use.

### §2.4 Options

- **A — Keep + disambiguate.** Ch 1 + glossary + chapter first-uses include explicit "NOT HR severance" notes.
- **B — Rename.** Replace "Cost Severance" with a collision-free alternative. Candidates tested below.
- **C — Keep + lean into collision rhetorically.** Ch 1 opening uses the HR familiarity as a teaching bridge. "THIS IS the same move, applied differently."
- **D — Split: keep "Cost Severance" as technical term, use different vocabulary in public-facing prose.** Hybrid — tests below.
- **E — Retire the capitalized term; use "cost severance" as lowercase descriptive phrase only.** Closest to Option B but softer.

### §2.5 Sub-claims per option

**Option A (keep + disambiguate):**
- SC-A1: Disambiguation note at first use sufficient to shift reader reflex.
- SC-A2: Accounting-register severance-as-rupture image preserved.

- SC-B1: Rename eliminates collision entirely.

**Option C (lean into collision — RECOMMENDED):**
- SC-C1: Readers encountering "severance" in HR context have ALREADY learned the accounting-register severance-as-rupture concept. Framework borrows their existing understanding.
- SC-C2: Rhetorical bridge teaches the framework concept using familiar-phrase as on-ramp.

## §3. M12 — Intellectual honesty / Prior-art audit (decisive module for this pass)

### §3.1 Exact-phrase search

Search terms: *"cost severance"*, *"severance costs"*, *"severance cost"*, variants.

**Findings:**
- HR/accounting: "severance costs" is standard terminology (IAS 37, ASC 712/715, PwC, KPMG, Bloomberg, Proformative, Accounting Insights, Riveron, BDO).
- Corporate-law practice: severance agreements, severance packages.
- No published economics / political-economy / commons-governance usage of "cost severance" for the framework's mechanism meaning.

### §3.2 Concept-level search

Underlying concept: separation of cost-bearing from value-capture.

Established adjacent terminology:
- **Externality (Pigou 1920).** Cost imposed on third party by transaction. Closest established concept.
- **Accumulation by dispossession (Harvey 2003).** Political-economy framing of the pattern; no mechanism-name.
- **Cost shifting.** Used in healthcare-economics (shifting costs from insurers to patients) and environmental-econ (shifting costs from firm to community). Adjacent but less-established.
- **Cost externalization.** Established phrase in environmental + labor econ. Same concept, different metaphor.
- **Value extraction (Mazzucato 2018).** Political-economy — focuses on rent-seeking, doesn't name the cost-separation mechanism directly.

**Finding:** The CONCEPT (cost-bearer separated from value-capturer by an extraction mechanism) is ADJACENT to established literature but no established term captures it with the framework's specificity (mechanism-name + mathematical form in CS = RCV − B). "Cost Severance" as framework term is closer to independent rediscovery specialized for Commons Bonds than to adoption of established terminology.

### §3.3 Classification

**Per M12 §16.5.4c:** **Lexical collision (different concept)** — same phrase used in HR/accounting for a meaningfully different concept (employee-termination payment vs. value-cost severance). Framework's concept is not subsumed by HR meaning.

Not independent rediscovery (different concept, not parallel framing of same concept).
Not adoption (framework doesn't use HR meaning).
Not original coinage (phrase exists in HR).
Not adjacent-term (phrase is identical, not adjacent).

**Severity:** HIGH for legal/accounting/policy audience (per §2.3).

### §3.4 Recommended action per M12 §16.5.9 action ladder

The action ladder for M12 findings (light → heavy):

2. Glossary entry citation — necessary but not sufficient.

## §5. Option C — the rhetorical bridge in detail

### §5.1 What the bridge does structurally

Teaching move (following the template of Mazzucato's "takers and makers" — familiar-frame + inversion):

1. **Acknowledge the familiar meaning.** "Corporate America knows the word 'severance.'"
2. **Extract what that meaning honors.** "When you cut someone loose, you owe them. You sever the tie, and you pay for the severing."
3. **Introduce the parallel + inversion.** "Cost severance works the opposite direction. When a company extracts value from a commons, it sheds the COST onto the source."
4. **Make the critical move explicit.** "The tie gets severed, but the payment architecture of employment severance is absent. The cost-bearer gets stuck."
5. **Land the frame.** "That's the problem this book is about."

This is 4-5 sentences. It does M4 + M5 + M6 + M12 work in one pass.

### §5.2 What the bridge commits the book to

- **Ch 1 first use** — the framing above (or a very close variant).
- **Subsequent chapters** — use "cost severance" cleanly, without back-referencing the bridge. The Ch 1 framing has done the work.
- **Glossary v3** — includes a brief version of the bridge as the entry's opening line.
- **Tech Appendix** — more technical framing; doesn't need the bridge (academic audience either knows or will read Ch 1).
- **Author discipline** — no wavering. If Chris starts qualifying "Cost Severance — not to be confused with HR..." in later chapters, the bridge is undermined. The commitment is: bridge once, then trust.

### §5.3 Why the bridge beats disambiguation (Option A)

Option A says: "NOT what you think." Reader reflex: uncertainty, back-parse.

Option C says: "YES what you think, applied differently." Reader reflex: recognition + extension. The familiar knowledge becomes ON-RAMP not OBSTACLE.

Cognitively: Option A fights the reader's existing schema. Option C recruits it.

### §5.4 Why the bridge beats rename (Option B)

Rename dodges the collision. Bridge owns it.

The book is about cost severance — a mechanism where payment-architecture is missing when value-separates-from-cost. Explaining this mechanism using the employment-severance contrast is the book's ARGUMENT compressed into one page. Renaming loses that compression.

Rename also cascades — Severed Cost's name is less natural (severed from what?), Value Extraction → [renamed] → Severed Cost chain loses the "severance → severed" etymological bridge. Ring-1 ecosystem weakens.

### §5.5 Risks of Option C

- **Authorial voice commitment:** Chris must commit to the bridge and not waver. If chapter drafts currently show any defensive "not to be confused with..." language, that gets cleaned up to match the bridge stance.
- **Sensitivity-reader risk:** a laid-off reader encountering "Corporate America knows the word 'severance.'" may read it as tone-deaf. Mitigate: the bridge ACKNOWLEDGES severance-as-rupture respectfully ("the word honors something"), then contrasts. Not minimizing.
- **Academic-audience risk:** peer reviewers may read the opening as literary-stylistic when they expected analytical. Mitigate: Tech Appendix gives the formal version; Ch 1 opening is legitimate frame-setting.
- **Test-reader variance:** some readers will prefer Option A's clarity. Option C is a higher-risk, higher-reward choice.
