## §3. Test 1 — Individual term merits (head-to-head)

### §3.1 Linguistic analysis

**"Value Capture":**
- Verb *capture* = seize, obtain, take possession of. Emphasizes the acquisition at the capturer's end.
- Corporate/strategy usage: "capturing value" is standard in business texts (Kaplan & Norton, Porter). Neutral register — companies capture value legitimately all the time.
- Etymology: "capture" from Latin *capere* (to take). Physical connotation of trapping.
- Applied broadly: financial capture, attention capture, regulatory capture (note: different sense), market capture.

**"Value Extraction":**
- Verb *extract* = draw out, pull from, remove from. Emphasizes the separation of value from its source.
- Natural-resource/industrial usage: extraction industry, extractive economy, extractive colonialism. Slightly pejorative register — extracting often implies without full consent or fair return.
- Etymology: "extract" from Latin *extrahere* (draw out). Act-of-separating connotation.
- Applied broadly but with extractive-industry flavor: financial extraction, information extraction, attention extraction.

**Key distinction:** Capture emphasizes the *acquisition event*; Extraction emphasizes the *separation act*. A cost severance framework is fundamentally about separation — separation of cost-bearer from value-capturer, of value from source, of benefit from payer. Extraction is linguistically aligned with this theme; Capture is orthogonal to it.

### §3.2 Register fit with the book's rhetorical strategy

The book is an act of honest accounting against extraction economies. The register is bookkeeping + moral-political argument. "Extraction" names the critical valence directly; "Capture" is more neutral (companies capture value as part of normal business). For a book whose title, framing, and argumentative spine are about an *extraction* economy that produces cost severance, "Value Extraction" aligns at every level.

### §3.3 Individual-term verdict

**Option B (Value Extraction) wins Test 1** on:
- Mechanism-naming fit (separation, not acquisition)
- Register alignment with the book's critical valence
- Extraction-economy vocabulary pairing

**Option A (Value Capture) defensible** on:
- Broader applicability to non-physical extraction (financial capture, etc.)
- Neutral register (doesn't prejudge the value-capturer's morality)

But the framework's critical valence is part of its contribution. Neutralizing it via "Capture" loses rhetorical force. The book makes a moral-structural claim about extraction; the vocabulary should be aligned with that claim.

**Test 1 verdict:** Option B preferred on substantive grounds, not marginal.

## §4. Test 2 — Causal-chain bridge performance (the decisive test per author direction)

### §4.1 The chain

*Value X → Cost Severance → Severed Cost*

Author 2026-04-24: *"that causal chain not landing means I lose the reader, reviewer, editor, et al at the very beginning."*

Each option inserted at position X:

### §4.2 Option A (Value Capture) in chain

*Value Capture → Cost Severance → Severed Cost*

**Reader cognitive path:** 
- Step 1: "Value is captured" — the capturer acquires value.
- Step 2: "Cost Severance" — separate mechanism; reader must infer how capture connects to severance.
- Step 3: "Severed Cost" — the left-behind cost.

**Middle step requires inference:** capture is acquisition; severance is separation. The reader has to figure out that capture typically happens through extraction, and extraction creates severance, and therefore capture produces severance. The chain requires the reader to supply a missing mechanism.

**In a Berggruen essay's opening paragraphs, this inference is load-bearing:** the reader can miss it if the prose moves quickly. Missing the inference breaks the causal chain.

**Chain-test score:** Weaker. Works with explicit explanation; risks reader-loss without.

### §4.3 Option B (Value Extraction) in chain

*Value Extraction → Cost Severance → Severed Cost*

**Reader cognitive path:**
- Step 1: "Value is extracted" — value is pulled from its source.
- Step 2: "Cost Severance" — the act of extraction *is* the act of severing; cost stays at source while value is pulled away.
- Step 3: "Severed Cost" — the cost left at source after extraction.

**Middle step is intuitive:** extraction and severance describe the same event from different angles. Extraction emphasizes what's taken; Severance emphasizes what's left. The reader doesn't need to infer a connecting mechanism — extraction *is* the mechanism.

**In a Berggruen essay's opening paragraphs, this reads naturally:** extraction → severance → severed cost forms a linguistic and causal sequence that requires no additional explanation.

**Chain-test score:** Strong. Extraction and severance are conceptually paired (both emphasize separation); the chain flows.

### §4.4 Option C (both interchangeable) in chain

Half the time the reader sees "Value Capture → Cost Severance → Severed Cost"; half the time "Value Extraction → Cost Severance → Severed Cost." The reader must hold both as synonyms while also tracking the chain.

**Chain-test score:** Negative. Works but adds cognitive load + reduces consistency of identification. Rejected by author.

### §4.5 Option D (Value Appropriation) in chain

*Value Appropriation → Cost Severance → Severed Cost*

**Reader cognitive path:**
- Step 1: "Value is appropriated" — legal/formal register; appropriation = taking as one's own.
- Step 2: "Cost Severance" — not obviously connected to appropriation; appropriation is about claiming, not separating.
- Step 3: "Severed Cost" — what's left behind.

**Middle step weaker than Extraction.** Appropriation and severance aren't as linguistically paired as extraction and severance. Appropriation emphasizes ownership-taking; severance emphasizes separation.

**Chain-test score:** Intermediate. Stronger than Capture on academic precision, weaker than Extraction on mechanism-pairing.

### §4.6 Option E (Value Severance) in chain

*Value Severance → Cost Severance → Severed Cost*

**Structural failure:** Value Severance and Cost Severance describe the **same severance event** from opposite sides. "Value is severed from its source" = "Cost is left at source while value-capturer takes value." One event. Not two steps.

The chain collapses to: [severance event] → [severance event] → [result of severance event]. Redundant middle; not a causal sequence.

**Chain-test score:** FAILS structurally. Option E cannot support the three-step chain as designed.

### §4.7 Option G (Accumulation by Dispossession only) in chain

*Accumulation by Dispossession → Cost Severance → Severed Cost*

**Reader cognitive path:**
- Step 1: "Accumulation by dispossession" — Harvey's term; academic; requires explanation. Even after explanation, each use re-invokes the mouthful.
- Step 2–3: works once Step 1 lands, but Step 1 is the accessibility bottleneck.

**Chain-test score:** Academic-credibility high; accessibility failure breaks Step 1 for non-specialist readers.

### §4.8 Test 2 aggregate verdict

**Option B (Value Extraction) wins the causal-chain bridge test decisively.** Extraction and severance are linguistically and mechanistically paired; the chain reads as a tight causal sequence. Option A (Capture) requires reader inference. Options C/D/E/G each have structural or accessibility failures.

**This is the test the author identified as decisive.** The chain landing for the reader at the essay's opening is essay-critical; Extraction delivers; Capture risks loss.

**Libby, Montana:** Value Extraction (vermiculite + worker-health value extracted) fits. Value Capture describes corporate gain but elides the extraction mechanism.

**Indigenous land dispossession:** this case is LITERALLY what Harvey's "accumulation by dispossession" names. Value Extraction reads as Chris's accessible reframing of that academic concept. Value Capture reads as softer/less accurate.

**Chesapeake watermen (pending case):** Value Extraction of the fishery's carrying capacity. Value Capture is more indirect.

Across cases, Extraction pairs more directly with the extraction act each case describes. Capture describes the downstream acquisition but requires adding "via extraction" to cover the mechanism.

**Verdict on M2:** Option B (Extraction) fits case prose more cleanly.

**Working definition:** the act by which value is separated from its source (a community, ecosystem, or future generation) and taken by a value-capturer. The causal event that produces Cost Severance — extraction severs cost from the capturer (who benefits) and leaves it with the source (who bears). Chris's original coinage, serving as an accessible-language reframing of David Harvey's "accumulation by dispossession."
