**Author's framing of this rigor pass (verbatim):**
> "run a full depth rigor test on these if you didn't. You said the chapter title needs to carry weight, let's make sure it can and does."

The author's framing names a real craft issue: a chapter title in a framework-and-memoir hybrid book has to carry both narrative-anchor work AND framework-thesis work. Single-word titles in this register can do load-bearing work (Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* §1 = "Reflections on the Commons"; Desmond's *Evicted* §1 = "The Business of Owning the City"; Raworth's *Doughnut Economics* §1 = "Change the Goal"). The wrong title flattens the chapter to scene-anchor or memoir-prologue; the right title threads through the book's whole arc.

This rigor pass evaluates eight candidates against all 12 modules + Standing Gates + §22.4 + Working Principle #7 + pressure-test characters (with Berggruen Judge restored to active set per Insight #65 Refinement 1).

## §1. Title candidates

Brainstormed list, narrowed to eight viable candidates spanning the full trade-off space.

### §1.1 Candidate A — "The Plane" (original working title / baseline retention)

**Setup.** The original working title; serves as the baseline against which all other candidates are evaluated.

**Connotation cluster.** Concrete object; literal scene-anchor; aviation-flavored; minimal framework signal; reads as memoir-prologue style.

### §1.2 Candidate B — "Origins"

**Setup.** Single-word title proposed by author. Captures both the framework's origins (where the way-of-seeing came from) and the personal-narrative origins (where Chris started absorbing the cost-bearing pattern).

**Connotation cluster.** Common English word; "origin story" is a recognized narrative trope; gestures at lineage + framework-roots; risk of reading as generic chapter-opener; broadly accessible.

### §1.3 Candidate C — "Lineage"

**Setup.** Single-word title proposed by author. Specifically captures the grandfather → father → me arc that is the chapter's structural spine.

**Connotation cluster.** Inheritance-genealogy register; memoir-leaning; emphasizes intergenerational continuity; lighter on cost-bearing dimension than Inheritance; slightly narrower scope.

### §1.4 Candidate D — "Inheritance"

**Setup.** Single-word title proposed during this session. Carries both directions — what was passed *down* (the way of seeing) AND what was *absorbed* unwillingly (the cost-bearing pattern). Threads through the book's whole arc (intergenerational cost, what we owe descendants).

**Connotation cluster.** Morally weighted; carries dual-direction (passed-down + absorbed); legal/genealogical/spiritual register layers; resonant with framework's intergenerational-cost concern; pairs with Ch 10's "Common Bonds" close.

### §1.5 Candidate E — "Before"

**Setup.** Single-word title gesturing at retrospection. Implies the chapter happens *before* the framework existed — before Chris had language for what was happening to him.

**Connotation cluster.** Temporal-prepositional; minimalist; risk of cliché (many memoir chapter titles use single-word time-markers); thoughtful retrospective register if it lands.

### §1.6 Candidate F — "Patterns"

**Setup.** Single-word framework-adjacent title. The framework names cost-severance through patterns (the six common patterns per Insight #65); Ch 1 establishes the seeing-clearly pattern Chris inherited from grandfather and father.

**Connotation cluster.** Framework-vocabulary adjacent; signals analytical posture; cleaner on framework-book signaling than memoir-leaning options; mild risk of reading as too abstract.

### §1.7 Candidate G — "What He Passed"

**Setup.** Phrase pulled from chapter line 69: *"He passed it to me the same way."* Carries earned weight from chapter's own prose. Phrase form rather than single word.

**Connotation cluster.** Inherited from chapter's voice; understated; requires pronoun resolution (who is "he"? — answered in chapter); emphasizes the transmission dimension; lighter on cost-absorption.

### §1.8 Candidate H — "What I Learned to Call It"

**Setup.** Phrase pulled from chapter line 123 (closing paragraph): *"The framework in this book is what I learned to call what happened."* Carries earned weight from chapter's own voice; directly invokes the chapter's narrative-into-framework arc.

**Connotation cluster.** Reflective-retrospective register; inherits chapter's own voice; emphasizes naming-as-after-the-fact; signals intellectual posture appropriate to framework-from-experience book.

### §1.9 Candidates considered and excluded

- **"Before the Cloud Line"** / **"The Cloud Line"** / **"First Flights"** / **"The Plane Was Past the Cloud Line"** — variations on the plane scene; all share Candidate A's underlying weakness (scene-anchor is too literal for what the chapter does) without compensating advantages. Excluded.
- **"Pou"** — the naming-ceremony beat as title; high specificity but requires reader to reach line 57 before the title makes sense; risks alienating readers in an opening chapter. Excluded.
- **"What He Passed Quietly"** — extends "What He Passed" with the chapter's own modifier; carries more weight but adds length; "What He Passed" is tighter without losing meaning. Excluded as inferior to G.
- **"The Cost I Could Not Yet Name"** — heavier; reads as too on-the-nose; over-telegraphs the framework arrival. Excluded.
- **"The Inheritance"** — definite article weakens "Inheritance" by forcing it into a single specified inheritance rather than the dual-direction concept. Excluded as inferior to D.
- **"Before I Had the Language"** — carries the retrospection but is longer than necessary; D ("Inheritance") and H ("What I Learned to Call It") together cover this territory more efficiently. Excluded.

## §8. Recommendation

**Lock D — "Inheritance"** as Ch 1 chapter title.

**Reasoning ranked by load-bearing weight:**

1. **Single morally-weighted word that carries both directions of what the chapter does.** No other single word in the candidate set captures both "what was passed down" (skill, way of seeing) AND "what was absorbed" (cost-bearing pattern). The dual-direction reading is exactly what Ch 1 demonstrates structurally.

2. **Threads through book's whole intergenerational-cost arc.** Ch 1: *what was passed to me*. Ch 7: *what we owe to grandchildren no one alive today has met*. Ch 8: *what one ton of coal actually costs the next generation*. Ch 10 ("Common Bonds"): *what we owe to those who come after*. "Inheritance" as Ch 1 title threads through every one of these. The bookend with Ch 10 lands cleanly: opening on what was inherited; closing on what we owe.

3. **Pitchable to agents and editors.** Single morally-weighted word that does framework-thesis work. Agents respond to titles that signal the book's distinctive contribution; "Inheritance" + "Common Bonds" as bookend titles tells the agent the entire arc in two words.

4. **Survives full pressure-test character set.** Berggruen Judge reads it as morally serious; tenured economics chair finds it accurately retrospective; Harvey-Klein reader recognizes the structural register; literary agent finds it pitchable; McDowell County resident parses it cleanly; the author's grandfather and father (the chapter's lineage anchors) would recognize the moral weight.

5. **Voice-fit with the book's other accessible-vocabulary discipline.** Single common English word, no Ring-2 coinage; matches *cost severance*, *severed cost*, *value extraction*, *accountability bond* register.

6. **Pairs structurally with the Author's Note.** The Author's Note opens with the grandfather as inventor whose discipline was passed forward; Ch 1 ("Inheritance") lands what was passed forward in the author's own life. The two paratext + chapter pair land the framework's full transmission arc.

**Runner-up if you want phrase form:** H — "What I Learned to Call It." Carries unique content the single-word options can't; slightly less pitchable but does specific load-bearing work the single-word options approximate from outside. If for any reason "Inheritance" doesn't land (memoir-cliché concern that survives, voice-mismatch surfaces in revision, etc.), H is the strong fallback.
