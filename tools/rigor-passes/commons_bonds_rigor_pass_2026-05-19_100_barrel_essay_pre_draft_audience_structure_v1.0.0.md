## 2. Editorial-brain map — Phenomenal World specifically

What PW editors want (and what other venues don't equally demand):

- **Heterodox-econ-and-political-economy register specifically.** Not generic-intellectual (Aeon) and not centrist-policy (Boston Review). PW expects engagement with the political economy of capital, the state, and contested economic categories. Mazzucato/Christophers/Pistor/Mason/Gabor are the contributor-network registers.
- **Length band ~3,000–5,000w.** Pieces longer than 5,000w are typically by editorial assignment, not unsolicited. Target ~3,800–4,200w body with breathing room.
- **News-peg discipline.** Gas-price spike is the hook, NOT the register. PW publishes news-pegged pieces but the substance is durable intellectual content. The piece should engage the gas-price moment without becoming a news article — the news peg should appear in the opening to ground the thought-experiment, then the essay should pivot to the durable intellectual work.
- **Named intellectual lineage with attribution.** Marx, Ostrom, Daly, Hartwick, Stern, Nordhaus, Rawls should appear by name with one-sentence positioning of each. PW readers expect this; under-citing reads as advocacy. Over-citing (Foucault, Lacan, miscellaneous Continental references) reads as register-mismatch.
- **Concrete cases with verifiable facts.** Norway (GPFG founding facts), Nigeria (Niger Delta extraction), Appalachian coal, the pacemaker case must all carry verifiable specificity. PW fact-checks more than Aeon does.
- **Cross-domain reach.** Energy + finance + climate + healthcare + labor in a single essay is on-register for PW. Single-domain reads as op-ed-thin.
- **Architecture-fix register.** The framework should read as architectural intervention, not as political program. PW publishes both architectural pieces and political pieces — the framework's discipline locates it firmly in the architectural register.

What hurts PW submissions specifically:

- **Pure thought-experiment without policy stake.** PW wants the policy-relevance to land.
- **Single-domain piece.** Reads as op-ed-thin.
- **Missing engagement with Stern-Nordhaus.** This is the closest existing intellectual battleground. Not engaging it is a competence-signal failure.
- **Framework apparatus jargon.** Greek letters, subscripts, RCV-as-acronym, Cᵢ vocabulary, CIT methodology in body prose. PW readers are heterodox-econ-fluent but not framework-Commons-Bonds-fluent. Named-instrument plain-language treatments only.
- **Implicit Stern-endorsement (or implicit Nordhaus-endorsement).** Condition 1 requires the framework to stake position outside the axis. Either endorsement codes the piece partisan.
- **Marx-in-lineage-map without disarming move.** Reads as ideological commitment to the center-right reader. The lineage-map progression must read as scholarly inventory.
- **News-article register.** Gas-price salience is the hook; if the prose flattens to news-article reporting, the durable intellectual content collapses.

## 7. Canonical factual ground truth (Amendment A; load-bearing protection against Stage 2 factual drift)

**7.1 Hartwick's rule**

- Hartwick, John M. "Intergenerational Equity and the Investing of Rents from Exhaustible Resources." *American Economic Review* 67, no. 5 (1977): 972–974. (Bibliography entry verified at `research/literature/bibliography.md`.)
- John M. Hartwick is the economist; his institutional affiliation at time of publication was Queen's University (Canada). His subsequent career anchored resource economics at Queen's.
- The rule: if the rent on a non-renewable resource is fully invested in reproducible capital, consumption can be sustained indefinitely.
- Companion / adjacent: Hotelling 1931 ("The Economics of Exhaustible Resources," *Journal of Political Economy* 39(2): 137–175); Solow 1974 (maximin-consumption framework that Hartwick operationalizes). Ramsey 1928 ("A Mathematical Theory of Saving," *Economic Journal*) is the foundational intergenerational-discount-rate paper.

**7.2 Norway sovereign wealth fund (canonical facts)**

- **Discovery:** December 1969, Block 2/4, North Sea, Ekofisk well. Three days before Christmas.
- **Fund founding:** Government Petroleum Fund established 1990. Renamed Government Pension Fund Global (GPFG) in 2006.
- **Fiscal rule:** Adopted 2001 under Stoltenberg I (Labour). Limits annual withdrawals to expected real return on capital (~3%). Capital itself inviolate. Has held through every subsequent government: Bondevik II (center-right coalition); Stoltenberg II (Labour); Solberg (Conservative coalition); Støre (Labour-Centre coalition).
- **Fund size:** >$1.9 trillion in assets (per Ch 4 canonical statement; verify current figure at Stage 2 against ~late-2025 / early-2026 fund-published number; the source draft cited $2.2T as of 2025 — Ch 4 cites >$1.9T; Stage 2 should use the most recent Ch 4 figure or look up the official Norges Bank Investment Management figure at draft date).
- **Per-citizen:** ~$340,000 per Norwegian (Ch 4 canonical figure).
- **National oil company:** Equinor (formerly Statoil; renamed 2018).
- **Investment discipline:** Fund invests deliberately outside Norway (avoids distorting domestic economy); across diversified equities, bonds, and real estate.

**7.3 Stern Review**

- Stern, Nicholas. *The Economics of Climate Change: The Stern Review.* Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2007. (Originally released as independent review to the UK Government, October 2006.)
- 700-page review commissioned by the UK Government.
- Conclusion: climate change is "the greatest and widest-ranging market failure ever seen"; ~1% of global GDP per annum required to avoid the worst effects.
- Discount rate: ~1.4%.
- Social-cost-of-carbon recommendation (in the Stern/Nordhaus comparison frame): ~$360 per ton.
- Author: Lord Nicholas Stern, LSE; subsequently Grantham Institute on Climate Change and the Environment.

**7.4 Nordhaus DICE model**

- Nordhaus, William D., and Joseph Boyer. *Warming the World: Economic Models of Global Warming.* MIT Press, 2000.
- Nordhaus, W. D. "Evolution of Assessments of the Economics of Global Warming: Changes in the DICE Model, 1992–2017." NBER Working Paper 23319, April 2017.
- DICE = Dynamic Integrated Climate-Economy model. Foundational for social-cost-of-carbon estimation.
- Discount rate (in the comparison frame the essay engages): ~4.3%.
- Social-cost-of-carbon recommendation: ~$35 per ton (in same comparison frame as Stern's $360/ton).
- William Nordhaus received the 2018 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for integrating climate change into long-run macroeconomic analysis.
- Discount-rate-application illustration: at Stern's 1.4%, $100 benefit a century out = ~$24.90 today. At Nordhaus's 4.3%, $100 benefit a century out = ~$1.48 today.

**7.5 Weitzman fat-tail tradition**

- Weitzman, Martin L. "On Modeling and Interpreting the Economics of Catastrophic Climate Change." *Review of Economics and Statistics* 91, no. 1 (2009): 1–19.
- Fat-tail-damages framework: in heavy-tailed distributions, expected value is dominated by the tail; analysts who price only the body systematically under-estimate expected cost.
- The framework's essay invokes Weitzman as the adjacent tradition only — Weitzman's fat-tail analysis remains within the climate-damage-pricing literature (the body of the distribution is climate damages; the tail is catastrophic damages). The framework's RCV move is structurally adjacent but distinct: RCV prices the resource's permanent foreclosure, not the climate damage from its consumption. The essay can reference Weitzman as "the adjacent climate-tail-risk tradition" without conflating the two.

**7.6 Ostrom**

- Ostrom, Elinor. *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action.* Cambridge University Press, 1990.
- Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences (shared), 2009.
- Identified eight design principles distinguishing successful from failed commons governance.
- Territory: renewable common-pool resources (fisheries, forests, irrigation, groundwater) where sustained-yield management is theoretically possible.
- Framework's extension claim: non-renewable resources (liquidated permanently) and renewable resources drawn-past-regenerative-capacity sit outside Ostrom's territory; pricing of permanent foreclosure rather than sustained-yield governance is needed.

**7.7 Daly**

- Daly, Herman E. *Steady-State Economics.* W. H. Freeman, 1977. Revised edition, Island Press, 1991.
- Daly, Herman E., and Joshua Farley. *Ecological Economics: Principles and Applications.* (Textbook for the field.)
- Stock-flow accounting; natural-capital stocks vs. ecosystem-service flows; economy as subsystem of biosphere.
- Framework's extension claim: Daly tells us we are overshooting ecological limits; Daly does not produce a price for the permanent loss of a specific non-renewable resource.

**7.8 Rawls**

- Rawls, John. *A Theory of Justice.* Harvard University Press, 1971. (Revised edition, 1999.)
- Veil-of-ignorance device: a just policy is one that could be agreed to from behind a veil of ignorance.
- Original-position thought-experiment.
- Framework's extension move: apply the veil across time. A policy decided without knowing which generation you will be born into.

**7.9 Convergent-intuition philosophers (Condition 1 disarming-move references)**

- Sandel, Michael. *What Money Can't Buy: The Moral Limits of Markets.* Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2012. (Market-moral-limits tradition; "what we owe vs. what we pay" register.)
- Parfit, Derek. *Reasons and Persons.* Oxford University Press, 1984. (Population-ethics; non-identity problem; intergenerational obligation arguments.)
- The Condition 1 disarming move: Rawls is ONE frame producing the relevant intuition; Sandel and Parfit produce convergent intuitions from market-moral-limits and population-ethics registers respectively. The framework engages multiple philosophical traditions that converge on the same intergenerational-pricing intuition.

**7.10 Marx**

- Marx, Karl. *Capital: A Critique of Political Economy, Volume I.* (1867; English translation 1887, ed. Engels.) Volumes II (1885) and III (1894) published posthumously, edited by Engels.
- Surplus-value theory: capitalist extracts surplus value from labor; the locus of critique is labor exploitation.
- Framework's lineage-map positioning (Condition 1 disarming-move treatment): Marx names the surplus-value-of-labor extraction but does NOT name the permanent foreclosure of the resource itself. Marx is positioned as historical inventory of competing economic schools' contributions and gaps, NOT as authorial endorsement of Marxist political program.

**7.11 Norway-as-Hartwick-application existence proof**

- Norway's Government Pension Fund Global operationalizes Hartwick's rule at sovereign scale. The framework treats Norway as the canonical existence-proof that Hartwick's principle is implementable.
- The Norway case carries the Condition 1 cross-spectrum-political-durability signal: 2001 fiscal rule held by Labour governments (Stoltenberg I and II; Støre) AND center-right coalitions (Bondevik II; Solberg). Architecture-fix is cross-spectrum-durable, not partisan.

**7.12 Nigeria counter-case**

- Niger Delta extraction since 1950s; international oil-company structure (Shell, Chevron, ExxonMobil, Total).
- Ogoni crisis 1990s; Ken Saro-Wiwa execution 1995; subsequent Commonwealth suspension and diplomatic protests; militant insurgencies thereafter.
- UNEP 2011 Ogoniland Environmental Restoration report: initial capitalization estimate ~$1 billion; full restoration projected 25–30 years.
- Framework treats Nigeria as the counter-case demonstrating that cost severance is reducible by institutional design choice; absent the choice, the externality tail runs uninterrupted.

**7.13 Gas-price news-peg (Stage 2 verification)**

- Avoid implying a specific cause of the spike (geopolitical, refining, demand-side); the essay does not engage the proximate-cause analysis — the hook is the salience of pricing, not the explanation of the spike.
