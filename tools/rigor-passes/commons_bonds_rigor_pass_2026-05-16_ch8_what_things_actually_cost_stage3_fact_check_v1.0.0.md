A reader cross-referencing both chapters or checking a single source on Gary's history will catch the date discrepancy.

**Recommended spot-fix:**

**Severity rationale:** HIGH because it's a hard date error (1990 vs 1986); HIGH because cross-chapter consistency with Ch 2 breaks; HIGH because the "1990" figure is also factually wrong in another sense — by 1990 the area had begun a partial reopening (Gary Enterprise's purchase), so 1990 doesn't function as "the closure date" by any reasonable interpretation. This is a corpus-consistency drift that requires either (a) bringing Ch 8 back to the 33–122 canonical, (b) noting the methodological reframing explicitly in Ch 8, or (c) updating the inventory + Ch 2 + Ch 6 to reflect the dual framing.

**Recommended spot-fix:** Three options, author-ratification required.
- **Option A (canonical alignment):** Rewrite Ch 8's IPG line to use the canonical "33–122×" range, with the per-component-floor framing as a methodological note: `The Intergenerational Pricing Gap — the ratio of honest price to market price — for McDowell County coal sits between 33 and 122 times the price coal actually sold for in 1960, depending on which cost categories the accounting admits and which social cost of carbon anchor it uses. - **Option C (corpus update):** Update Ch 2 + Ch 6 + cross-chapter inventory to reflect the dual-framing canonical lock — requires cross-corpus work outside the scope of a Ch 8 spot-fix, but addresses the root inconsistency.

### MEDIUM-2: Rennert et al. 2022 *Nature* "meta-analysis" mischaracterized — is integrated estimate, not meta-analysis

**Canonical truth:** Rennert et al. 2022, *Comprehensive evidence implies a higher social cost of CO2* ([*Nature* vol. 610](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-022-05224-9)), is not a meta-analysis. It produces a new SCC central estimate ($185/tCO2 at 2% near-term discount, 2020 USD; 5%–95% range $44–$413) rather than aggregating across prior SCC estimates as a meta-analysis would. The methodological language in the paper itself, in RFF / Resources for the Future commentary, and in EPA 2023 RIA citations consistently uses "comprehensive evidence" / "integrated assessment" framing, not "meta-analysis."

**Why this is the MEDIUM-2 finding:** A methodologically-literate reader (climate economist; SCC researcher; environmental policy analyst — exactly the Tier-1 quant/economist audience the chapter targets) will catch the "meta-analysis" mischaracterization. Meta-analysis has a specific methodological meaning (aggregation across prior empirical studies) that Rennert et al. 2022 does not satisfy.

**Recommended spot-fix:**
- **Option A (methodology-precise):** `. . . anchored to the Rennert et al. 2022 *Nature* **integrated estimate** that grounded the 2023 EPA update . . .`
- **Option B (comprehensive-estimate framing):** `. . . anchored to the Rennert et al. 2022 *Nature* **comprehensive estimate** that grounded the 2023 EPA update . . .`
- **Option C (preserve methodological lineage explicitly):** `. . . anchored to the Rennert et al. 2022 *Nature* **paper whose integrated assessment grounded the 2023 EPA update** . . .` — slightly more prose but methodologically precise.

**Severity rationale:** MEDIUM because the mischaracterization is the kind a publisher fact-checker familiar with SCC literature will flag; MEDIUM not LOW because the chapter cites Rennert as the methodological warrant for the $190 anchor and the warrant should be characterized accurately; not HIGH because the underlying citation (Rennert et al. 2022 grounded the EPA 2023 update at $190) is correct and the error is just the genre-label of the paper.
