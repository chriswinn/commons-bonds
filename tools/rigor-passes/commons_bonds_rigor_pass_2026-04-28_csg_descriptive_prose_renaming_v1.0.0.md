**Total rename impact: ~25-30 chapter-prose occurrences + 3-5 supporting-document occurrences.** Substantial enough to warrant rigor; small enough to land in a single sweep.

### §1.2 Why the current name is clunky

The clunkiness is structural, not subjective:

1. **Word count + syllables.** "industrial-existential substitutability gap" = 5 words, ~14 syllables. Compounds to ~16-18 syllables in adjectival use ("high-industrial-existential substitutability gap resources").
2. **Latin-derived + technical-econometric register.** "Existential" + "substitutability" both heavy academic register; doesn't pattern-match B-audience accessibility (Mazzucato/Raworth/Sen).
3. **Pure-academic register loses adoption-travel.** Regulatory text writers, policy briefs, Ezra-Klein-style essays would NOT pick up this phrase verbatim. The framework's success-criterion (severed-cost-into-legal/policy-vocabulary) requires more migration-friendly phrasing.
4. **Adjectival deployment is the worst case.** "Low industrial-existential substitutability gap resources" is 7 words / 17 syllables before the noun. Reader cognitive load is high.
5. **The "vs" variant is even more awkward.** "industrial-vs-existential substitutability gap" introduces a "-vs-" infix that breaks the compound's flow.

### §1.3 Why renaming is worth it

- Per Working Principle #1 parsimony: the framework should bear the least vocabulary cost that still serves the work.
- Per the inherited B + supplementary D audience: clunky descriptive prose burdens both audiences uniformly.
- Per M11 critic survival: an academic reviewer reading "low industrial-existential substitutability gap resources" 5+ times in Ch 9 would find the prose register academically-strained without proportionate distinction-payoff.
- Per the success-criterion (legal/policy adoption-travel): tighter phrasing migrates better into regulatory text.

The CSG concept itself is real and load-bearing — the gap between current-industrial-substitutability and long-horizon-civilizational-substitutability genuinely distinguishes resources whose loss has different consequence profiles. The renaming is about the *prose form*, not the concept.

## §3. Methodology

For each candidate phrasing, four-axis evaluation:

1. **Audience-tier (B + D):** does the phrase serve the B-register reader without specialist gloss? Does it migrate into D-modulation regulatory text?
2. **Framework-anchor:** does the phrase connect to the Substitutability Function S apparatus?
3. **Paired-comparison signal:** does the phrase carry the "gap between two evaluations" structure?
4. **Lineage / collision check:** does the phrase pattern-match established academic / policy lineage cleanly without colliding with adjacent meanings?

Plus Tier C status check: stays as descriptive prose (no proper-noun coinage).

Per-candidate verdict: STRONG / ADEQUATE / WEAK / DISQUALIFIED.

**Paired-comparison signal:** STRONG. "Horizon" implicitly pairs current-horizon vs long-horizon; the "gap" suffix completes the comparison structure.

- Intergenerational-discount-rate literature (Weitzman declining-discount; Stern Review; Ramsey 1928) — planning-horizon lineage.

- *"high-horizon-substitution gap resources"* — slightly awkward hyphen-stack but readable.

## §8. M11 critic-survival probes against recommended verdict

### §8.1 Critic — "'Horizon-substitution gap' is just renaming the same concept with shorter words. Why is this rigor work?"

**Response:** Per Working Principle #1 parsimony, the framework's vocabulary should bear the least cost that still serves the work. The current 5-word / 14-syllable phrase deployed adjectivally 25+ times across Ch 7 + Ch 9 imposes substantial reader cognitive load + adoption-travel friction without distinction-payoff over a 3-word / 9-syllable equivalent. Renaming serves the work; the underlying concept is unchanged. M11 SURVIVES.

### §8.2 Critic — "'Horizon' could mean anything. The current name is more precise."

**Response:** "Horizon-substitution gap" is descriptive prose, not a coined framework term (Tier C, not Tier D). Precision lives in the framework's apparatus (Substitutability Function S; CSG retirement record; Tech Appendix §G formula). The descriptive prose form's job is to refer to the concept readably; the apparatus carries precision. The chapter prose can additionally specify "horizon" once on first use ("the gap between current-industrial-horizon substitutability and long-horizon civilizational-application substitutability — what the framework calls *the horizon-substitution gap*") to lock the meaning. M11 SURVIVES.

### §8.3 Critic — "Why not 'criticality gap'? The policy-lineage advantage is huge."

**Response:** Critical-raw-materials (CRM) policy lineage is established + would aid D-modulation adoption-travel. But the CRM concept is *not the same* as the framework's CSG concept. CRM means resources with both economic-importance AND supply-risk; the framework's gap concept is about the divergence between current-industrial-substitutability and long-horizon-civilizational-substitutability. Adopting "criticality gap" would import the CRM meaning + distort the framework's specific claim. The framework's success-criterion (severed-cost-into-legal/policy-vocabulary) is better served by phrasing that *complements* CRM policy without colliding with it — e.g., chapter prose can reference "the framework's horizon-substitution gap, which extends standard critical-raw-materials analysis to two-hundred-year horizons" — preserving both the framework's distinctive contribution AND the policy-lineage adoption channel. M11 SURVIVES.

### §8.4 Critic — "Renaming after the 2026-04-24 retirement was already ratified looks indecisive."

**Response:** The 2026-04-24 ratification retired the proper-noun "Civilizational Substitutability Gap" + preserved the concept in descriptive prose. The descriptive prose form was *"industrial-existential substitutability gap"* per the retirement record — a placeholder phrasing chosen during retirement-decision deliberations. The prose-form choice was not separately rigor-tested at the time; the assumption was that any descriptive prose would suffice. Subsequent chapter drafting + author readback (2026-04-28) surfaced that the placeholder is structurally clunky enough to warrant rigor on the descriptive prose form specifically. This is *refinement of an unsettled detail*, not reversal of a ratified decision. M11 SURVIVES.
