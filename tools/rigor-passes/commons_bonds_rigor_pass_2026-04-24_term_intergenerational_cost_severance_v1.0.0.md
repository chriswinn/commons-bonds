## §2. The question under test

### §2.1 The phrase

"Intergenerational cost severance" (lowercase) — the natural-language descriptor for Cost Severance operating across a generational gap. Used compositionally rather than as a capitalized proper noun: "intergenerational" modifies "cost severance" to specify the subtype.

### §2.2 Why this pass runs now

The Temporal-Cost-Severance rigor pass (2026-04-24) recommended Option C: retire the capitalized "Temporal Cost Severance" term; use "intergenerational cost severance" in prose. Option C tested the *retirement* but did not run extreme rigor on the *replacement phrase itself*. Author direction 2026-04-24: "run the full rigor suite against the new term 'intergenerational cost severance' if we have not already done so." This pass closes that gap.

### §2.3 The five options

Per Principle #2 (option-space breadth), expanded beyond the initial framing:

**Option A — Adopt "intergenerational cost severance" as the book's prose phrase** for the subtype. Lowercase, descriptive, used consistently whenever the intergenerational subtype needs naming.

**Option B — No dedicated phrase for the subtype.** The book describes intergenerational cost severance in varying prose: "cost severance that reaches future generations," "the generations who pay the bill," etc. No consistent phrase adopted.

**Option C — "Cost severance across generations"** as an alternative descriptive phrase. Same concept, different surface form.

**Option D — Context-dependent use.** Sometimes the full phrase "intergenerational cost severance"; sometimes just "cost severance" with intergenerationality implied by context.

**Option E — Reconsider: is the subtype worth distinguishing at all?** The RCV formula's integration t₀ → ∞ mathematically covers intergenerationality; prose could treat all Cost Severance as inherently intergenerational (since most real-world cases involve future generations) without distinguishing a subtype.

### §2.4 Sub-claims per option

**Option A sub-claims:**
- SC-A1: The phrase is precise enough to serve as the book's standard descriptor.
- SC-A2: Consistent lowercase use avoids jargon-inflation.
- SC-A3: The phrase is adoptable in legal/policy discourse.
- SC-A4: Precision gain over Option B (consistency) justifies the discipline of choosing the standard phrase.

**Option B sub-claims:**
- SC-B1: Prose variety serves readability.
- SC-B2: No discipline cost (no phrase to enforce).
- SC-B3: Risk of inconsistent reference when the subtype matters for comparison across chapters.

**Option C sub-claims:**
- SC-C1: "Across generations" is more concrete than "intergenerational."
- SC-C2: Longer phrase (5 words vs 3).
- SC-C3: Less aligned with existing intergenerational-ethics discourse.

**Option D sub-claims:**
- SC-D1: Flexibility across prose contexts.
- SC-D2: Consistency cost (inconsistent reference may confuse readers).
- SC-D3: Hardest to enforce — author discretion per passage.

**Option E sub-claims:**
- SC-E1: Vocabulary-footprint minimization.
- SC-E2: Loss of ability to distinguish intergenerational from non-intergenerational severance explicitly (when needed).
- SC-E3: Most Cost Severance IS intergenerational; maybe the distinction isn't worth drawing.
- SC-E4: But some Cost Severance is NOT intergenerational (e.g., miners + their current-living families bearing costs from today's extraction) — so the distinction is real when it matters.

### §2.5 Falsifiers

**Option A is falsified if:**
- The phrase reads as jargon despite lowercase discipline.
- Consistency across book prose proves impossible without heavy editing burden.
- A stronger alternative option (B, C, D, or E) beats it on major modules.

**Option E is falsified if:**
- The intergenerational/non-intergenerational distinction turns out to be load-bearing for specific cases (e.g., comparing McDowell's current-bearing costs to its future-generation costs).
- Policy discourse requires the intergenerational subtype to be named.

**Verdict below (§13):** Option A survives all modules + alignment tests; Options B, C, D, E each fail on at least one axis relative to A.

## §6. M4 — Craft

### §6.1 Prose quality test

**Option A sample:** "The framework prices intergenerational cost severance — the bill McDowell's future generations will pay for extraction that occurred during their grandparents' lifetimes — at ~$X per ton."

*Reads as precise, register-consistent accounting prose.*

**Option B sample:** "The framework prices the cost severance that reaches future generations — the bill McDowell's future generations will pay for extraction that occurred during their grandparents' lifetimes — at ~$X per ton."

*Reads as wordier; the descriptive clause adds ~10 words per instance across multiple instances in a chapter.*

**Option C sample:** "The framework prices cost severance across generations — the bill McDowell's future generations will pay..."

*Reads acceptably but "across generations" sounds slightly less polished than "intergenerational"; the extra word costs prose economy.*

**Option D sample:** sometimes uses Option A, sometimes B or C, sometimes just "cost severance" with context.

*Flexibility benefit is theoretical; in practice, readers may parse inconsistent reference as uncertainty about what's being discussed.*

**Option E sample:** "The framework prices cost severance — the bill McDowell's future generations will pay..."

*Reads fine for the single-case but doesn't support cross-case contrast ("this case is intergenerational but that one isn't" requires the subtype distinction).*

### §6.2 Register discipline

Lowercase "intergenerational cost severance" reads as natural-language composition (not capitalized jargon). The book's rhetorical strategy is accounting-register; this phrase fits without introducing jargon inflation. Crucially, lowercase discipline prevents the phrase from becoming a proper-noun compound (which was the problem with "Temporal Cost Severance").

**Verdict on M4:** PASS. Option A produces tightest, most register-consistent prose. Option B has economy cost. Option C slightly weaker. Option D inconsistency risk. Option E loses cross-case-contrast capability.

## §13. M11 — Critic pressure

### §13.1 Adversarial attack scenarios

**Critic attack 1:** "Why distinguish 'intergenerational cost severance' from just 'cost severance'? Isn't all cost severance inherently intergenerational since costs persist over time?"

**Response:** Not all cost severance is intergenerational. Consider a factory that sickens workers and bankrupts the community within the same generation — cost severance without a generational gap. The subtype distinction matters when comparing cases or when the intergenerational dimension is analytically important (climate, foreclosure, dynastic labor). Option A preserves this analytical capacity.

**Critic attack 2:** "'Intergenerational cost severance' is just a descriptor; why should it be in the framework's vocabulary at all?"

**Response:** It isn't in the framework's vocabulary as a technical term — it's a natural-language composition of two framework concepts (intergenerational + cost severance). The rigor pass sanctions *consistent use* of the composition, not *invention* of a new technical term. The phrase already exists in the framework by composition; this pass formalizes that it's the book's standard descriptor.

**Critic attack 3:** "Shouldn't 'intergenerational cost severance' be capitalized if it's a technical term?"

**Response:** No. Lowercase discipline is load-bearing. Capitalization would reintroduce the jargon problem that sank "Temporal Cost Severance." The framework uses capitalized terms for technical elements (Cost Severance, Severed Cost, RCV, AIT, Accountability Bond) but not for natural-language subtypes.

### §13.2 Adversarial survival

All three attacks defeatable with direct responses. Option A survives critic pressure without defensive posture.

**Verdict on M11:** PASS. Survives adversarial reading.
