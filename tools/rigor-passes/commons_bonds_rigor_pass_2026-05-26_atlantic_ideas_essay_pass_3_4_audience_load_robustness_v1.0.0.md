### Thread A1 — Public-Choice-tradition theorist (Buchanan / Tullock / Virginia-school)

**Adversarial reading mode:** scan for analytical-completeness gaps in the framework's rent-seeking engagement; test whether the light-touch §VI ¶2 lands as serious complement or as patronizing kissing-cousin acknowledgment.

**Primary thread-pull surfaces:**

**A1.1 — Marx in four-traditions ¶3 without Buchanan/Tullock parallel.** §I ¶3 names "Marx, Ostrom, Daly, Hartwick" as the four traditions each addressing part of what economics is for. Public Choice theorist pulls: "You name Marx (collectivist labor theory) but not Buchanan (who asked: 'who captures'). Public Choice is taxonomically a fifth tradition that addresses precisely what your §VI political-economy of resistance argues — the dynamic of how the price gets wrong stays wrong. Why is Marx in your taxonomic-lineage and Buchanan isn't?"

**Defensive position in essay:** §VI ¶2 names Buchanan + Tullock + the "public-choice extensions" of Olson's diffuse-vs-concentrated framework explicitly. The §VI ¶2 (post-Pass-3.2 cadence-collapse) is the explicit Public Choice complementarity move: "The claim is structural, not partisan, and James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock built much of their analytical tradition on the public-choice extensions of it. Honest pricing has to be designed against this dynamic, not in spite of it. It complements the rent-seeking analysis that tradition produced rather than competing with it."

**Thread analysis:** Public Choice theorist's first pull (Buchanan-absent-from-¶3-taxonomic-lineage) is partially recovered at §VI ¶2 (Buchanan-present-in-political-economy-section). But the ¶3 taxonomic-lineage paragraph functions as the essay's diagnostic framing; not including Public Choice there signals it's a subordinate / downstream tradition rather than a coordinate one. Public Choice theorist's analytical-completeness lens registers this asymmetry.

**A1.2 — Complementarity claim asserted, not argued.** §VI ¶2 closing sentence "It complements the rent-seeking analysis that tradition produced rather than competing with it" is the load-bearing complementarity-claim, but the essay does not work out HOW the framework complements rent-seeking analysis. Public Choice theorist pulls: "You assert complementarity. Demonstrate it. Rent-seeking analysis says state-led pricing IS the rent-seeking moment. Your framework's pricing-by-administrative-function is precisely what Public Choice argues against. Where's the argument that 'honest pricing' isn't itself captured?"

**Defensive position in essay:** None explicitly. The essay does NOT engage capture-resistance for its own proposed pricing instruments. §VII ¶4 cites Chattanooga EPB as evidence that municipal ownership can resist capture (Comcast lawsuits + Tennessee legislature limits + asset retained), but this is implicit-by-example rather than explicit-by-argument.

**Thread analysis:** A real positional difference. The framework's premise (administrative pricing of irreplaceability cases is possible-and-necessary) is precisely what Public Choice tradition's rent-seeking analysis interrogates. The essay's compression deliberately drops the "capture-resistance design" treatment that would address this thread directly (brief §6 + §4 compressed out the full Public Choice complementarity section per length-budget logic). The thread holds at essay length but signals a positional disagreement that the broader book argument must address.

**A1.3 — Norway + Alaska as Public Choice puzzles, not solutions.** §VII §2-3 cites Norway GPFG ($2T / 3% spending cap) + Alaska PFD ($85B / 25% of mineral royalties) as honest-pricing implementations. Public Choice theorist pulls: "These are oligopolistic-resource-state puzzles, not transferable lessons. Norway is a small-state petrocarbon-economy with a coordinated political class. Alaska is a special-status mineral-royalty regime with constitutional protection from political revision. Neither is a working model for the broader case of pricing extractions where the political class is divided and the rents are diffuse."

**Defensive position in essay:** §VI ¶6 explicit anti-Sweden-fetishism move ("The point of pairing these examples is not to argue that the United States can or should become Sweden. It is to show that the same shock can be metabolized by different responses..."). This is the strongest mitigation but it's an aspirational rather than analytical defense.

**Thread analysis:** Partial thread-pull; the §VI ¶6 disarming move handles the "shouldn't become Norway" critique. But the §VII case studies don't argue how the Norway/Alaska architecture is replicable in a non-coordinated political class. This is a Pass 3.5 RESTORATION-polarity question (would expanding §VII to discuss replicability help?) rather than a Pass 3.4 spot-fix.

**Aggregate A1 thread-pull synthesis:** Three pulls of varying strength. A1.1 (Marx-but-not-Buchanan in ¶3) is the strongest pull but is partially mitigated by §VI ¶2 explicit Public Choice complementarity. A1.2 (complementarity-asserted-not-argued) is real but compressed-out by deliberate brief §6 decision. A1.3 (Norway/Alaska replicability) is partial; §VI ¶6 disarming handles the strongest case.

**Disposition:** Thread A1 holds at essay length. Spot-fix candidate at Pass 3.5: add Buchanan/Tullock as a fifth tradition in ¶3, with one-clause gloss like "who captures the architecture" or "who shapes the rules." ### Thread A2 — Free-market-environmentalist (Anderson + Leal *Free Market Environmentalism* register)

**Adversarial reading mode:** scan for property-rights-and-their-limits compression failures; test whether Coase-Demsetz alternative is acknowledged-and-engaged or quietly dismissed.

**Defensive position in essay:** §V ¶4 Smith citation is the property-rights-tradition anchor. **Thread analysis:** Partial recovery via Smith citation. But Coase is not engaged. The free-market-environmentalist's analytical-completeness lens registers Coase's absence as the deliberate-compression decision it was. Compression is brief-§6-defensible but the thread-pull is real.

**A2.2 — CBAM as protectionism dressed up as climate policy.** §V Border argues CBAM is "the working version of this principle" and "is not protectionism in the conventional sense." Free-market-environmentalist pulls: "CBAM IS protectionism by another name. The legal scholarship calling CBAM WTO-incompatible is substantial (Pauwelyn, Kaufmann, etc.). Your essay acknowledges 'three years of objection from outside the EU' but doesn't engage the structural case that border-adjustment instruments enable rent-extraction by incumbent industries within the regulating bloc. **Defensive position in essay:** §V ¶4 explicit anti-protectionism distinction: "It does not protect domestic producers from competitors who can make the same goods more cheaply by virtue of superior process or location. **Thread analysis:** §V ¶4 handles the conceptual distinction (cost-reflecting price vs subsidy-via-externality-omission) but doesn't engage the empirical question of whether CBAM in practice produces rent-extraction by EU incumbent industries. Free-market-environmentalist pulls hard here — the WTO-incompatibility case is substantive economic-law literature. Defensible compression but real residual risk.

**A2.3 — Asymmetric-regret reasoning presupposes one-direction error costs.** §III ¶4 "The downside of one error is bounded; the downside of the other is not." Free-market-environmentalist pulls: "Bounded by whom? Your 'bounded' is the assumption that the deadweight loss of over-reserving is acceptable. The Coase theorem says markets would clear at the efficient reserve fraction; your framework says state administration knows better. Where's the case that state administration's discount rate is the right one?"

**Defensive position in essay:** §III ¶2 ("the cost of the policy is the foregone royalty income on the deferred fraction" + "if the substitutes do not materialize, and the country that holds the supply has the leverage that follows, we have foreclosed the option of doing things that depend on those materials at all") frames the asymmetry as bounded-vs-unbounded specifically for irreplaceability cases.

**Thread analysis:** §III's asymmetric-regret reasoning is restricted to irreplaceability cases (per §II classify). The free-market-environmentalist's pull on "discount-rate-presupposition" is a Coasean pull (see A4); for free-market-environmentalist specifically, the partial recovery is "we're only making the asymmetric argument for the irreplaceability subset where markets cannot clear at efficient outcome because the future generations who would bear the cost aren't seated at the bargaining table." **Aggregate A2 thread-pull synthesis:** Three pulls. A2.1 (Coase absent) is the strongest; compression-defensible but real. A2.2 (CBAM-as-protectionism) is mid-strength; partial §V ¶4 recovery. A2.3 (asymmetric-regret discount-rate-presupposition) is partial; the four-traditions ¶3 implicitly argues the case but doesn't engage Coase by name.

**Disposition:** Thread A2 holds at essay length but signals an open analytical front. Pass 3.5 RESTORATION candidate: add a one-sentence Coase engagement to §V ¶4 — "Coase argued that with low transaction costs, parties bargain to the efficient outcome regardless of initial allocation. The framework operates at the case where transaction costs include the absence of the future-generation party at the bargaining table." This would explicit-engage Coase rather than implicit-deflect. Cost: ~30w. Benefit: closes A2.1 + part of A2.3.

### Thread A4 — Coasean economist

**Adversarial reading mode:** scan externality framing for transaction-cost dismissal; test whether the Coase bargaining-in-absence-of-transaction-costs argument is engaged or quietly assumed away.

**Primary thread-pull surfaces:**

**Defensive position in essay:** §I ¶3 four-traditions diagnostic implicitly makes the Coase-defeats-only-with-transaction-costs argument: "Permanent foreclosure is not present at the moment of sale; the generation that pays for it is not seated at the table. **Thread analysis:** The argument IS there but Coase isn't named. Coasean economist's analytical-completeness lens registers this. Defensible compression (brief §4 cut the property-rights-and-their-limits section); but explicit engagement would close the analytical gap.

**A4.2 — Carry-forward F-3.1-3 Hartwick "None priced permanent foreclosure" compression.** Coasean economist (or Hartwick-aware reader within the Coasean tradition) pulls: "Hartwick's 1977 AER rule says: invest exhaustible-resource rents in reproducible capital → maintain consumption indefinitely. That IS pricing permanent foreclosure — under the substitutability assumption. Hartwick assumed strong-substitutability between natural and reproducible capital, which is precisely where the irreplaceability case falls outside his framework. Your essay's compression 'None priced permanent foreclosure' overstates Hartwick's omission. Hartwick priced the case; the case just collapses to vacuity when substitutability fails."

**Defensive position in essay:** None explicitly; the compression "None priced permanent foreclosure" is the load-bearing diagnostic move in ¶3 that earns the framework's "what's missing from the four traditions" pivot.

**Thread analysis:** Real thread-pull. Hartwick-aware reader is correct that the bare compression overstates the omission. Defensible at essay length because: (1) Hartwick's rule formally requires substitutability + the framework operates at the irreplaceability case where substitutability fails, so "didn't price permanent foreclosure of the irreplaceability case" is what the essay means by "None priced permanent foreclosure"; (2) the brief §I ¶3 four-traditions paragraph is diagnostic-set-up not detailed exposition; precision-tightening here would expand essay length. **A4.3 — Asymmetric-regret discount-rate-presupposition.** §III ¶4. Coasean economist pulls: "Asymmetric-regret reasoning under uncertainty depends on the discount rate. Your argument that the cost of reserving is 'bounded' presupposes a discount rate at which the option-value of preserving rare earths exceeds the foregone-royalty deadweight loss. With a high enough discount rate (markets revealing that future generations don't value the rare earths as much as we currently project), the asymmetry inverts. You don't make the discount-rate argument; you assume it."

**Defensive position in essay:** §III ¶2 conditional framing: "If the assumption that rare earths are irreplaceable turns out to be too strong, we have given up a small slice of present consumption." The "small slice" framing implicitly assumes the discount rate at which deferred royalty income is small. Reader has to accept this implicit assumption.

**Thread analysis:** Real thread-pull. The asymmetric-regret argument is sound under standard discount rates, but the framework doesn't argue the discount-rate case explicitly. Defensible per brief §6 (mechanism-and-arithmetic register doesn't require formal discount-rate engagement) but Coasean economist registers the gap.

**A4.4 — "Irreplaceable" as administrative judgment, not market signal.** §II classify. Coasean economist pulls: "Markets show substitution happens at the margin. Calling a material 'irreplaceable' is an administrative-political judgment, not a market signal. Your CRMA list is a political artifact — a coalition of industries that successfully lobbied for inclusion. Calling these 'the irreplaceables' is precisely what Public Choice says we'd expect."

**Defensive position in essay:** §II ¶6 explicitly addresses this: "The classification is also not just a list. It is a set of judgments about substitution horizons that have to be made under uncertainty, by people qualified to make them, and revisited when the underlying science moves. The CRMA process names that work as a continuing administrative function rather than as a one-time legislative product..."

**Thread analysis:** Strong essay-side recovery. §II ¶6 explicitly acknowledges the administrative-judgment nature of the classification + the ongoing-revision discipline. Coasean economist may still object on the political-economy of administrative-judgment (who's qualified; how do we know capture isn't happening), but §II ¶6 addresses the cleanest version of the pull.

**Aggregate A4 thread-pull synthesis:** Four pulls of varying strength.
