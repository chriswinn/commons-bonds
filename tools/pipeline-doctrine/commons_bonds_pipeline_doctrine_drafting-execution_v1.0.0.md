# Drafting-execution doctrine (RATIFIED 2026-06-04)

Three rules, learned by backing into them during the Aeon build. They govern HOW prose
gets made once the substance is known; they sit downstream of Stage 0/1 (conception +
ready-to-draft) and alongside the min-floor-only / no-max generation corollary.

## Rule 1 — Separate the decision phase from the drafting phase, and do them in that order

Two distinct phases that must not blur:

1. **Decision phase (author-in-loop).** Before drafting, decide deliberately, per artifact
   and per beat: the hook (what actually grabs — e.g. airlock-first vs the contemplative
   breath), the voice (e.g. Globally Inclusive Plain English), what each beat must do, what
   earns its place, and what is reserved for elsewhere (e.g. the book's formula). This is the
   part we did well for Aeon — just messily and iteratively. Do it up front and on purpose.
2. **Drafting phase.** Only once the decisions are locked, draft.

The decision phase is where the author's judgment is most load-bearing and most worth the
interactive cost. The drafting phase is where a focused pass executes the locked decisions.

## Rule 2 — Draft whole-cloth, not assembled-from-grafts

Once decisions are locked, draft the artifact (or each major section) **whole, in one
continuous pass, from the brief.** Do NOT build it by grafting passages from prior variants
and patching them together.

Rationale: cutting is easy; **lengthening or smoothing is the hard part.** Graft-assembly is
lengthening-by-assembly, and it fights flow — each grafted passage optimizes its own local
rhythm, not the paragraph's or the section's. The result reads "constructed" rather than
"composed." Prefer: rich whole-cloth draft (floor, no ceiling, per the min-floor corollary)
→ then cut to length. Harvest analysis is still useful for DECIDING what belongs (Rule 1),
but the chosen material should be re-drafted in one voice, not pasted in.

## Rule 3 — Use a fresh, focused pass for prose; do not build prose by serial live patching

Prose that must read beautifully should be written by a **fresh agent (or focused session)
with a clean, single-task brief and empty context** — not assembled by repeated small edits
inside a long, loaded live session.

Two mechanisms, in order of impact:
1. **Patch-assembly vs. whole-cloth (dominant).** A paragraph built by 5 successive local
   edits reads worse than the same paragraph written once, because each patch optimizes its
   own sentence, not the whole's rhythm.
2. **Divided attention in a loaded context (secondary, real).** A fresh chip with one tight
   brief spends its whole attention on the prose. A live session deep into a long
   conversation (holding state, verification, edits, commits) writes with divided attention,
   so its inline sentences are less crafted. NB: this is about divided attention, NOT a crude
   "high token count degrades quality" claim — raw token count / compaction matters less for
   prose feel than patch-assembly and divided attention do.

Corollary: when a section goes clunky from live patching, the fix is **a fresh whole-cloth
redraft of that section against a clean brief**, not a sixth patch. Keep live-session editing
for genuinely small, local fixes (a word, a figure, a cut) — not for building prose.

## Empirical anchor — the Aeon "Mask of Abundance" build (2026-06-03/04)

- The DECISION process produced clearly-right calls (airlock-first opening over the breath;
  Globally Inclusive Plain English voice; reserve the formula; cut Parfit; fold the Niger
  guard; the variables-by-environment keystone). Concept and decisions: strong.
- The DRAFTING execution was graft-assembly (base arm + harvested grafts) plus live patching,
  and a few seams show — the colony beat density, the lineage "tour," and most clearly the
  **Darity section, which read clunkier than the rest.**
- The clean correlation: **every other section was written whole-cloth in one pass by a fresh
  sub-agent; the Darity section was the only one assembled by ~5 successive live patches**
  (condense → add the $14T figure → rewrite the coercion line → fold). The patched-live
  section is the clunky one. This is the evidence behind Rules 2 and 3.
- Author's framing of the cost-asymmetry: "anything can be cut down to size; lengthening
  smoothly is the really hard part." That is the argument for whole-cloth-rich-then-cut over
  assemble-or-extend.

## Application to the book-chapter redrafts

1. Per chapter: a deliberate **decision chat** (author-in-loop) — the chapter's job, its
   beats, what earns its place, the voice, what is reserved — exactly the process we backed
   into for Aeon, done on purpose and up front.
2. Then a **fresh, focused, whole-cloth draft** of the chapter (or each major section) from
   the locked decisions, at a minimum floor with no ceiling.
3. Then **cut** with the author in the loop.
4. Reserve live-session editing for small local fixes; redraft whole when a section goes
   clunky.

No hard ceiling per chapter (per the min-floor corollary) — write rich, cut later.

## Amendment A (RATIFIED 2026-06-04) — lessons from the lens-driven test-draft value-audit

The overnight one-shot lens-drafting test (7 non-personal chapters) + its adversarial
value-retention audit produced four refinements. **Framing correction (author):** the audit
findings are NOT evidence that one-shot lens drafting is inherently weak. They are evidence of
two fixable gaps — an *unratified-proposal* gap in the prep, and a missing *dedup guard*.
One-shot whole-cloth drafting remains the method; these rules close the gaps.

### Rule 4 — Every proposal in the brief is handled (ratified, amended, or declined) before drafting

Before a one-shot draft fires, every PROPOSAL in the prep (deep-pass memos, relocation
suggestions, "move X to Ch Y," open decisions) must be **explicitly handled — ratified,
amended, or declined.** The requirement is NOT that proposals be removed, nor that they all be
accepted; it is that **none remains in an unhandled / still-proposed state** when drafting
begins. A declined proposal is handled; an amended one is handled; a ratified one is handled.
What breaks the draft is an *unhandled* proposal the drafter then guesses about — the test's
dominant failure, where a deep-pass *proposed relocation* was treated as settled fact and the
content excised though the move was never ratified or executed ("relocate became delete").
Author framing (2026-06-04): "ratify, amend, or decline all proposals in a brief, but
regardless of how they are handled, handle them all before drafting." So the ready-to-draft
brief carries only HANDLED state: each relocation is decided (its destination real and part of
the plan, or declined and the content stays), never a flag for later.

### Rule 5 — A deduplication/cut step needs an explicit retention guard

Any cut/dedup move must obey: **keep the most important instance, AND keep every load-bearing
claim stated at least once.** The test over-cut "redundant"-looking restatements to ZERO,
erasing last-instances that lived nowhere else (the 13-year life-expectancy gap; the Dunbar
couplet's "this debt we pay"; the CIT two sub-forms; the bond-forward-posting rationale). The
rule is not "remove repeated content"; it is "every load-bearing claim survives at least once,
in its strongest instance."

### Rule 6 — No scaffolding in the prose body, ever

Drafts must never emit process scaffolding inline in the prose: no `[CITATION GAP: …]`, no
`[RELOCATE TO …]`, no `[Substrate note: …]`, no editorial brackets. Citation gaps and
relocation flags are real and useful — they go in the **structured return / a separate notes
channel**, never in the running text. (The Stage-1a scaffolding-invariant scan,
`tools/quality-gates/scaffolding-patterns.yaml`, is the backstop; the rule is to not generate
them in-prose in the first place.) The author's framing: "just add a rule so future drafts do
not include any" — correct, with the one refinement that the gaps/relocations still surface
out-of-band.

### Restoration is whole-cloth, not graft (reinforces Rule 2)

When an audit finds lost value, the fix is **a fresh whole-cloth re-draft with the COMPLETE
brief** (original + the test-draft's good compression/positioning moves + the audit's
lost-value list + the resolved relocations + the canonical figures + the no-scaffolding rule),
NOT grafting the missing pieces back into the compressed draft. Author's hard-won framing
(2026-06-04): "every time we have grafted something back into prose it tends to read clunkier
than just including everything missed and re-drafting." This is Rule 2 applied to the
restoration step: the test-drafts are valuable INPUTS to the real whole-cloth draft, not a
base to patch. *(Supersedes the value-audit's "no chapter needs a re-draft / restorations
suffice" recommendation — the author overrode it on flow grounds, consistent with Rule 2.)*

## Amendment B (RATIFIED 2026-06-05) — the narrate-the-feeling rule, and the prework-vs-polish line

From the Ch1 v2 author redline. Two doctrine points.

### Rule 7 — Never narrate the feeling or the move

Cut any sentence whose job is to tell the reader **what to feel**, **why you are telling them**,
or **what the prose is doing**. Show it and stop. This is the through-line of the heaviest v1→v2
redline cuts ("I want you to sit with that"; "I want to be precise, because it is the reason I am
telling you this"; "I am going fast on purpose"; "I am moving quickly through these"; "I had no
idea, then, how I knew it"; the whole recognition paragraph that *told* the recognition instead of
letting it land). It is the same principle as the close discipline the author named: **a caption
on a feeling always reads as a small betrayal of it.** A book about making the unseen seen must
not, at its own landings, narrate the thing it just made the reader see.

**Why this is a PREWORK rule, not just a polish target.** v1's brief listed four banned phrases;
the v2 drafter excised exactly those four and let new instances of the same species through. A
blocklist of phrases does not generalize; **the principle does.** State the principle in every
chapter brief and it kills the whole class — in this chapter's next pass and in all ten chapters.

### The prework-vs-polish line (so we know which is which)

A chapter's redline splits into two buckets, and the split tells us where to put effort:
- **Prework-fixable PATTERNS** — a recurring tic (narrate-the-feeling), an over-writing habit
  (elaborating a clean phrase: "an instrument well enough to play for people" for "a musical
  instrument"), romanticized-euphemism imprecision ("the secret road" for the Underground
  Railroad). Pull the PATTERN into the brief once; it stops recurring across chapters.
- **The irreducible last mile** — word-level rhythm trims, precision swaps, add-or-cut taste
  calls, the one paragraph that "reads off." No prework eliminates these; they are what a
  sentence-level edit pass (Pass 3.2 voice-polish) + the author's own taste pass ARE. Every
  chapter, by every serious writer, gets this pass.

The signal to read from a redline: **if it is mostly last-mile line-polish and not structural
rework, the whole-cloth draft succeeded** — you are polishing sentences, not rebuilding the
chapter. Sentence-level polish at this stage applies as LINE-EDITS in place (including a clean
paragraph cut plus a one-line seam-repair); it does NOT trigger a whole-cloth re-fire. Re-fire is
for structural/scene-level restoration (Rule 2); line-polish is not graft.
