# What the new pipeline must do differently for CHAPTERS

The essay/op-ed new pipeline that's working: fresh **path-independent** draft from a fact-safe, audience-blind brief; minimum-only/no-max + write-rich-then-cut; ~0 em-dashes; **apparatus excluded** (book-thunder reserve — no formula, named-concept-only, title-pun held). That recipe is **outward-facing, self-contained, and thunder-reserving.** A chapter is the opposite on all three axes, so the recipe has to change in specific ways.

## 1. Path-independence breaks — chapters need an inward coherence spine
An essay is self-contained; you can draft it blind. A chapter is a movement in a symphony: setups and payoffs live in *other* chapters, motifs and characters recur, and the framework is revealed *progressively* across the book. A blind path-independent redraft risks re-introducing what's already established, contradicting a sibling chapter, dropping a setup another chapter pays off, or revealing the framework out of order. **Add a cross-chapter coherence block to the chapter brief:** what's established *before* this chapter, what this chapter must set up *for later*, the running motifs/characters/cases, and the apparatus-reveal state at this point. (This is the chapter-equivalent of Stage 1c, promoted from a check to a load-bearing *input*.)

## 2. The apparatus discipline INVERTS
Essays reserve the apparatus to leave the reader wanting the book. **The chapters are where the thunder lives** — the formula, the Cᵢ/Four Gates machinery, the full framework, the title-pun reveal all belong here and must be present and correctly *paced*. So the chapter pipeline must *include* the apparatus with a per-chapter reveal **target** (this chapter introduces X, develops Y), the mirror image of the essays' ≤22% reveal *cap*.

## 3. Triage by memoir/lived-density is load-bearing (and probably explains the clunk)
The essays split cleanly: analytical ($100B, FA, NYRB, op-eds) → safe to fresh-auto-draft; memoir/named-subject (Noema, Harper's, Atlantic watermen) → author-in-loop, *cannot* be auto-fresh-drafted without fabricating lived detail or breaking consent. The **same triage applies to chapters**, and the lived/named chapters (the miner, the watermen, the family/closing chapters) are very likely the clunky-but-tabled ones — because they're the hardest to smooth without either fabricating or breaking voice. **Classify each chapter first;** the class picks the mode.

## 4. A third mode: "substrate-preserving re-flow" (the chapter workhorse)
Two modes exist already: **(a) full fresh path-independent** (analytical, little locked substrate) and **(b) substrate-preserving fresh draft** (lived/quote material locked verbatim, connective/analytical prose re-flowed — what we're test-driving on Atlantic Main right now). For clunky-but-substantively-done chapters, **(b) is the fix**: preserve the lived passages, named-subject handling, verbatim quotes, and apparatus; re-flow only the connective and analytical prose to 0-em-dash smoothness. A heavier **(c) author-in-loop memoir** mode covers the most lived chapters (the Noema/Harper's treatment). The chapter pipeline formalizes all three and assigns one per chapter at triage. **The Atlantic Main draft now cooking is effectively a dry run of mode (b)** — if it lands clean, it's the template for the clunky chapters.

## 5. One voice across the book
Essays can each wear a venue voice (Aeon-literary vs FA-institutional vs PW-heterodox). The *book* needs a single authorial voice; a fresh-drafted chapter (especially by a subagent) drifts, and the reader feels the seam. **Add a voice-anchor input** (samples from the chapters that already read smoothly) and a stronger cross-chapter voice-consistency check than essays require.

## 6. Likely causes of "clunky," and what the new pipeline fixes
- **Patchwork seams** (chapters edited/restored many times) → the fresh re-flow fixes this directly. *Biggest win.*
- **Cumulative LLM-cadence residue** (em-dash crutch, rule-of-three, "It is not X. It is Y." reversal) → the 0-em-dash + cadence discipline fixes this directly. (This is exactly the Pass 3.5 developmental-edit target.)
- **Apparatus-stiffness** (formula/jargon making prose rigid) → *partially* fixable: render the apparatus in prose that breathes, but the chapter must KEEP the apparatus, so the move is "smooth the delivery," not "remove it."
- **Cut-to-fit damage** (flow lost to length-trimming) → write-rich-then-cut with the cut target set by book architecture, not a venue limit.

## 7. Cut target = book architecture, not a venue band
Essays cut to a venue word-band. Chapters cut to the book's pacing and balance against sibling chapters. The cut stage references the arc, not an external limit.

## 8. Redrafting a chapter is NOT isolated — it cascades
An essay redraft is self-contained. A chapter redraft can ripple to siblings (a changed setup/payoff, a terminology shift, a moved apparatus-reveal). The existing cross-chapter workstream lifecycle (Amendment E / v3.0) must fire on every chapter redraft: Stage 1c coherence across affected chapters, then a light re-check of each.

## Bottom line
Essay pipeline: outward-facing, self-contained, apparatus-reserving, isolated. **Chapter pipeline: inward-facing (book continuity as a first-class input), apparatus-*bearing* with paced reveal, voice-unified, mode-triaged (with substrate-preserving re-flow as the workhorse for clunky chapters), book-architecture cut targets, and cross-chapter-cascading on every redraft.** For the specific tabled-clunky chapters, the prescription is mode (b) re-flow + a cross-chapter coherence pass — NOT a blind fresh redraft (which would break continuity and, for the lived chapters, fabricate). Validate the approach on the Atlantic Main mode-(b) dry run before committing it to chapters.
