# Author substrate gets critical editorial input (ratified 2026-05-21)

**The rule:** When the author provides substrate prose for integration into Commons Bonds publisher-facing artifacts — whether inline in chat ("sample text", "consider:"), as a paste-block, or as a substantive rewrite proposal — give critical editorial input on the substrate. Critique grammar, punctuation, voice-register fit, sentence-flow, and overall improvements. Do not blindly preserve the substrate as written.

The author's role in the pipeline is substrate-generator + judgment + final decisions. Claude's role is sift + polish + critique. Polishing actively (per `feedback_voice_polish_pipeline.md`) explicitly INCLUDES editorial critique — that's the "polish" stage of the dump → sift → polish pipeline.

**Why:** Ratified 2026-05-21 during Ch 3 Pass 3.2 F-V21 chapter-wide em-dash reduction session. When the author provided substrate for line 12 (live-aboard biographical anchor) + line 40 (oyster-paradox cadence), I applied the substrate with minor polish but did not surface editorial concerns about grammar/punctuation/voice that I noticed. Author explicitly flagged: *"I don't want you to blindly accept and preserve my writing verbatim as written when I write (consider 'sample text'). I'm asking for your input. Go over the last one again and let me know if I messed up the grammar or could have used better punctuation, or really any feedback you have that would make it better."*

This corrects a default-too-conservative posture. The author is not looking for stenography; they're looking for active co-editing.

**How to apply:**

1. **When the author provides substrate** (inline in chat, paste-blocks, substantive rewrite proposals), do all of:
   - Parse the substrate for grammar/punctuation issues (missing commas, sentence-start "And" / "Because" fragments, ambiguous attachment, tense slippage, comma splices).
   - Check voice-register fit against the surrounding chapter prose (colloquial vs. literary; first-person vs. analytical-narrator).
   - Check cadence and prose-flow (anaphora-cluster density; em-dash density; rule-of-three patterns; declarative-three-in-a-row).
   - Check factual claims that are verifiable (geography, dates, counts, named-subject attributions).
   - **Surface what you'd change AND why** before applying. Don't bury concerns under "applying as drafted."

2. **Present revisions as a draft + the author's substrate side-by-side** when possible. The author can compare and ratify the polished version OR push back on specific edits.

3. **Polish actively per `feedback_voice_polish_pipeline.md`.** Fix grammar; restructure for flow; cut redundancy; translate raw images into book-register prose. The substrate is raw material; the published prose is the work.

4. **Don't over-correct either.** Some substrate is deliberately colloquial / register-breaking; flag the choice but let the author decide whether the register-break is intentional. Don't silently normalize away the author's voice. The discipline is "critique + recommend," not "rewrite without consultation."

5. **What still goes back to the author for ratification:**
   - Substantive content changes (the author wrote X but you'd recommend Y — flag X-vs-Y for ratification).
   - Factual-claim corrections (geography errors; date errors; named-subject errors).
   - Voice/register choices (colloquial vs. literary; sentence-start "And" / "Because" preservation vs. polish).
   - Polish that crosses ~10 words of new prose.

6. **What you can polish in-place without re-asking:**
   - Typo fixes (e.g., "hen" → "when").
   - Missing punctuation in clear cases (e.g., comma after "And sure" as concessive marker).
   - Pronoun/tense agreement within an obviously-intended sentence.
   - Single-word grammar fixes that don't change meaning.
