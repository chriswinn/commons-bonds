When drafting or revising Commons Bonds chapters (especially Ch 1 + Ch 3, the chapters still in β.2 prose-drafting), do not work toward an arbitrary word-count target. Focus on getting the content the chapter needs to do its work: what scenes need to be on the page, what argument needs to be made, what the chapter has to land for the book's spine to hold. Length is the output, not the goal.

**Why:** Word-count targets create the wrong optimization pressure — either compressing material that needs space (cutting content the chapter needs to land) or padding material that doesn't (adding fill to hit a number). The book's chapters vary widely in length already (Ch 4 ~existence proof short; Ch 8 ~longer cost-arithmetic walkthrough); forcing them to a target would distort the work each chapter is doing. Author confirmed 2026-05-02 going into Ch 1 + Ch 3 β.2 drafting.

**How to apply:**
- When drafting, don't track word count as a measure of progress.
- Don't propose cuts or expansions on length grounds alone — propose them only when content/argument calls for it.
- Don't surface "Ch 1 is now N words" as a status report; surface "Ch 1 has scenes A/B/C drafted; D/E remain."
- If the user asks whether something is too long or too short, evaluate against substance ("does this scene earn its space?") not against a target word count.
- Applies to both internal scaffolding (GuidanceDocs) and publisher-facing draft prose.

## Generation-stage corollary (ratified 2026-06-03): minimum floor only, never a maximum

When generating fresh drafts (the Amendment-E "new pipeline" — write rich, cut
later), give the drafter a **minimum word floor and NO maximum**. This is the
mechanism behind "substance drives length," now empirically proven and ratified.

**Why a minimum is safe and a maximum is harmful.** A minimum floor only ever
pulls length *up*, and only as far as the verified substance supports — the
drafter reaches it by reasoning more fully and explaining more deeply, not by
padding. It cannot compress prose. A *maximum* does the opposite: it forces
compression, which produces exactly the cut-to-fit damage the pipeline otherwise
spends a whole pass (Pass 3.5 developmental-edit) repairing — dropped scene
anchors, flattened reasoning, lost sensory detail, truncated breath at analytical
pivots. The author's framing (2026-06-03): a max "will cause all sorts of bad
things to happen to the prose."

**Length is a separate, post-generation concern.** If a draft overshoots the
venue's norm, cut it in a dedicated cut pass — and per the 2026-06-03
all-cuts-handled-live direction, that cut is an author-in-loop session, not a
generation constraint and not a solo trim. Generate rich; cut deliberately, later,
with a human in the loop.

**Empirical anchors — three Aeon minimum-floor experiments, no maximum ever
imposed, prose never compressed in any of them:**
- **Low floors (10 / 50 / 100 / 200w):** drafts blew past the floor to
  ~936–1,496w. The floor was irrelevant; the material's natural length set it.
  (`aeon-gather-ideas_2026-06-02.md` / `_archive/min-experiment-*`.)
- **High floors (4k / 5k / 6k / 8k):** output tracked each floor closely
  (+3–13%), filled with analytical depth, not padding. Fabrication-clean even at
  the 8k floor (the arm with the most padding pressure reached length by
  *reasoning*, and explicitly declined to invent granary particulars).
  (`aeon-hi-floor-experiment-findings_2026-06-03.md`.)
- **Plain-language floors (8.5k / 9k / 9.5k):** overshot ~28% (10,898 / 11,632 /
  12,139w), because walking each reasoning step explicitly for a layman costs
  words — again, fuller explanation, not padding. Fabrication-clean.
  (`aeon-plain-language-experiment-findings_2026-06-03.md`.)

In all three, the floor pulled length up to the extent substance supported and
never squeezed prose down. The danger case (high floor + thin substance →
padding/invention) did not occur, because the full front-end (gather + verified-
facts block) front-loaded enough real substance to fill the floor with depth.
