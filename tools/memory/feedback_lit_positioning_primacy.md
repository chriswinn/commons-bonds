Author directive 2026-06-11, superseding the beat-first comparison method:

1. **Literature positioning is the PRIMARY success criterion** for essays and
   framework chapters. The Noema/FA/Atlantic multi-draft comparisons were
   trying to identify which beats mattered; the pivot: a beat worth capturing
   should be captured, but that matters LESS than the text illustrating how
   the book *supports, contradicts, and adds to* the adjacent literature.
   Briefs must carry a named-works positioning section; judges weight it
   first; coverage audits check it before beat-tallies.
2. **Flatness watch.** Drafts produced with minimal author interaction
   before the brief was written can "sound a bit flat" — affect-neutral
   competence with no felt pressure behind the sentences. Judges and
   developmental passes should score aliveness/pressure explicitly (quote
   the flattest and the most alive passage), and the pipeline should prefer
   briefs built downstream of real author exchanges (redlines, chats,
   substrate dumps) over briefs distilled from documents alone.
3. **Standing ratification: draft every essay/op-ed as soon as its redraft
   brief is ready.** No per-essay drafting authorization needed. Gates that
   remain: author ratification at promotion, and submission itself.

**Why:** The author revealed the design intent (2026-06-11): the long pause
was a deliberate breakdown-and-rebuild, and pushing Fable's prose pushed the
author's prose. The multi-draft comparisons earned their cost by surfacing
this hierarchy: positioning > beats, pressure > polish.

**How to apply:** Amend existing redraft briefs with a lit-positioning
addendum before drafting; instruct essay judges to score positioning first
and flatness explicitly; when a draft reads flat, the remedy is usually
upstream (more author substrate in the brief), not line-editing. Related:
[[author-redline-final-stage]], [[feedback_audience_aware_drafting_discipline]].
