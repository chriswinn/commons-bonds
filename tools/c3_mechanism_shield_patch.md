## Core Architectural Principle (reference)

**Mechanism = "structural allocation"**  
The value-capture/cost-bearing asymmetry produced by extraction accounting. Operates independently of whether any party recognizes it. Not a feature of any ownership structure — a feature of the accounting separation.

**Shield = "distance"**  
The condition that prevents the mechanism from being recognized, named, or corrected. Takes geographic, temporal, institutional, cultural, and narrative forms. Distance does not produce cost severance; it sustains cost severance by blocking recognition and correction.

**The test:** Can cost severance exist with zero distance? Yes — a mine operator who lives next to the mine and breathes the same air may still structure accounting to sever costs. Distance makes the severance easier to maintain; it is not the cause.

The shield works by giving the miner and the waterman different stories, different enemies, by making the miner and the environmentalist into each other's villain when the village they share is the one being extracted from. **REPLACE WITH:**
> A colony administrator on Mars would not have this problem. On Mars, if the administrator mispriced water, the administrator's own children would drink nothing. The shield collapses. Distance between beneficiary and bearer of cost falls to zero, which means the mechanism — structural allocation, the separation of value capture from cost bearing — becomes immediately self-correcting. The mechanism could still exist on Mars; someone could still write an accounting ledger that separated the two. But without the shield, the ledger would be corrected almost immediately by the people it harmed, because those people are standing in the same room. On Earth, the mechanism persists because the shield holds. On Mars, the shield cannot hold. This is not a metaphor. It is what the distinction between mechanism and shield predicts.

### PATCH 1C — Paragraph beginning "What I said in Chapter 7..."

This is the key passage. The formulation "cost severance is a function of distance" conflates mechanism and shield.

**FIND (exact):**
> What I said in Chapter 7 — and what has stayed with me since — is that cost severance is a function of distance. It requires a gap. It requires that the person who captures the value be far enough from the person who bears the cost that neither has to look at the other. The farther the distance, the easier the severance. Distance in this sense is not only miles. It is time — the ease of severing costs onto grandchildren no one has met. It is institution — the ease of severing costs through a supply chain no one fully comprehends. It is culture — the ease of severing costs onto a community whose language or skin or religion makes them easier to see as not-us. **REPLACE WITH:**
> What Chapter 7 showed — and what has stayed with me since — is the distinction between what produces cost severance and what sustains it. The mechanism is structural allocation: the separation of who captures value from who bears cost, built into the accounting of extraction before anyone has made a decision about geography or storytelling or politics. The mechanism does not require distance to exist. A ledger can sever costs regardless of how close the parties are. What distance does is shield the mechanism from correction. The farther the distance, the harder it is for the person bearing the cost to identify the mechanism, to name what is happening, to find the person capturing the value and hold a conversation about it. Distance in this sense is not only miles. It is time — the ease of severing costs onto grandchildren no one has met. It is institution — the ease of severing costs through a supply chain no one fully comprehends. It is culture — the ease of severing costs onto a community whose language or skin or religion makes them easier to see as not-us. It is story — the ease of severing costs onto a population that has been described, by people with platforms, as deserving their condition. Each of these distances is a form of the shield. Each is a condition that prevents the mechanism from being seen.

### 4B — Add new entry: "Structural Allocation (Mechanism)"

**STRUCTURAL ALLOCATION (Mechanism)**  
The value-capture/cost-bearing asymmetry produced by the accounting architecture of extraction. The mechanism by which cost severance is generated: a ledger assigns revenue to the extractor and assigns externality costs, community costs, foreclosure costs, and dynastic costs to other parties — other communities, other geographies, future generations. Structural allocation is independent of ownership structure, geographic distance, and intent. A state-owned operation, a cooperative, and a multinational corporation can all produce structural allocation if their accounting architecture separates who captures value from who bears cost. *See also: Cost Severance, Shield (Distance).*

### 4C — Add new entry: "Distance (Shield)"

**DISTANCE (Shield)**  
The condition that prevents the mechanism of structural allocation from being recognized, named, or corrected by the parties bearing its costs. Distance operates in at least five forms: (1) **geographic** — physical separation between extraction site and consumption site; (2) **temporal** — separation between extraction decisions and downstream consequences, including intergenerational cost severance onto unborn parties; (3) **institutional** — separation of cost-bearing parties from value-capturing parties through supply chains, corporate structures, and regulatory regimes; (4) **cultural** — separation through language, identity, and narrative, making affected communities easier to see as not-us; (5) **narrative** — active construction of stories that attribute affected communities' conditions to their own choices rather than to the extraction accounting. Distance is not the cause of cost severance; it is the shield that sustains it. Reduce the distance and the mechanism becomes visible; make the mechanism visible and it becomes correctable. *See also: Structural Allocation (Mechanism), Cost Severance.*

## PATCH 5 — WS08 Technical Appendix (CommonsBondsTechnicalAppendixv0_0_2.html — KEEP .html)

Add a formal definitional pair near the definition of CS (cost severance), in the section containing Definition A.x series.

**INSERT as a new definition block, immediately after the definition of CS:**

> **Definition A.[next number]: Mechanism (Structural Allocation).**  
> The structural assignment of value-capture rights and cost-bearing obligations to distinct agents, geographies, or time periods through the accounting architecture of extraction. Formally: a partition of the extraction event *e* into a value-capture set *V(e)* and a cost-bearing set *C(e)* such that *V(e) ∩ C(e) = ∅* or *V(e) ∩ C(e) ≪ C(e)* — i.e., the parties capturing value are either entirely disjoint from or substantially smaller than the parties bearing cost. The mechanism is independent of the shield (see Definition A.[next+1]) and operates regardless of whether any party recognizes the asymmetry.

> **Definition A.[next number + 1]: Shield (Distance).**  
> Any condition *δ* that increases the expected recognition gap *E[Γ(e)]* or decreases the expected corrective capacity *κ(e)* for parties in *C(e)*. Formally: the shield is a set of conditions {*δ₁, δ₂, ..., δₙ*} — geographic, temporal, institutional, cultural, narrative — such that ∂E[Γ]/∂δᵢ > 0 and ∂κ/∂δᵢ < 0 for each *i*. The two-path rigor test (Decision A, April 2026) requires that any proposed accountability instrument address both (1) allocation symmetry — does it reduce *CS(R, t₀)*? — and (2) shield absence — does it reduce the conditions *δ* that prevent recognition and correction? An instrument that addresses only the allocation path without reducing shield conditions will be captured; an instrument that reduces shield conditions without addressing allocation symmetry will produce awareness without remedy.
