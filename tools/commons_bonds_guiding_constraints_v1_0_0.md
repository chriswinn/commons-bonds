## Purpose

Two constraints have, to this point, operated silently on Commons Bonds decisions without canonical capture: the **success criteria** defining what Book One is trying to accomplish, and the **publishing path** through which success becomes possible. Both are load-bearing on scope, architectural, and stakeholder-facing decisions. Both appear as tests in the rigor protocol (Group D strategic / reception, Group E success criteria). Neither was captured in a form a session could upload and apply without re-deriving.

This document is that capture. It exists at a different level than the scope document and the Publishing Strategy document:

- The scope document decides **what the book is** (chapters, boundaries, deferrals).
- The Publishing Strategy document decides **how the book reaches readers** (specific agents, publishers, cascade tactics, timing).
- This document carries the **constraints that flow from success criteria and publishing path into rigor-pass decisions** — what a session needs to know about both in order to apply Group D and Group E tests correctly without holding the source documents in context.

A session that uploads this document at start, alongside the current rigor protocol and session handoff, can apply Group D and Group E tests with canonical fidelity. A session that does not upload it risks silently operating against outdated or implicit versions of these constraints — the same failure mode v1.2.1 was built to prevent at the protocol level, now extended to the constraints the protocol tests against.

## 1. Success criteria — Book One

### 1.1 The canonical sentence

> If, thirteen or more years from now, a labor lawyer uses "severed cost" in a brief or in open court and the judge does not need it explained — and the author whose name is on the book never knows this happened — the book succeeded.

That is the benchmark. Not royalties. Not prizes. Not revolution. Vocabulary used by people solving real problems, without attribution, because the vocabulary is useful.

*(Source: scope v1.0.3 §7.1. Implemented as rigor protocol v1.2.1 Test 23.)*

### 1.2 Two stated goals, different mechanisms

**Goal 1 — Future generations might be compensated more fairly.** Framework-level, indirect, upstream. Book One's contribution is vocabulary and visibility. Compensation requires institutional infrastructure Book One cannot build alone, but institutional infrastructure does not get built until the thing it protects is nameable. Contribution is real but routed through vocabulary adoption and institutional uptake over multi-decade horizons.

**Goal 2 — People might accept a job or project with open eyes and honest accounting.** Individual-reader-level, direct, immediate. Book One accomplishes this reader by reader. The eight-tier decomposition in Ch. 6 and the IT / knowledge-worker framework in Ch. 1 are the primary vehicles. This is the goal the book is best equipped to serve without intermediaries.

Both goals are compatible with Book One's scope as defined. Neither requires Book Two to be written. Book Two extends Goal 1 at institutional scale; Book One alone is sufficient for Goal 2.

*(Source: scope v1.0.3 §7.3. Implemented as rigor protocol v1.2.1 Tests 21 and 22.)*

### 1.3 Success signals — primary tier (most likely if the book succeeds at its stated goals)

- A labor lawyer, community organizer, or public-interest attorney uses "severed cost" or "residual commons value" in a brief, negotiation, or publication.
- A journalist covering a mine closure, pension crisis, or environmental-justice case uses "intergenerational pricing gap" or equivalent framework vocabulary as a headline concept.
- A graduate student builds on the framework in a dissertation.
- An individual reader — coal miner, nurse, gig worker, IT consultant — reads the book and renegotiates a contract with one internal question that wasn't there before: *am I being asked to donate unpriced tiers?*
- A community negotiating an extraction agreement uses the framework to request specific tier-priced protections that would not otherwise have been on the table.

*(Source: scope v1.0.3 §7.2.)*

### 1.4 Success signals — secondary tier (possible, less likely)

- A commons-economics course at a university includes *Commons Bonds* on its reading list.
- Scholarship integrating Ostrom's commons governance with the framework's pricing apparatus appears within a decade-plus horizon.
- A state, tribal nation, or small country adopts the Community Transition Reserve (or a functionally similar ring-fenced intergenerational fund with legibility requirements) as a demonstration case.
- Space-law frameworks being drafted for asteroid mining and lunar resources incorporate tier-priced accountability because someone in the drafting process read Ch. 7.
- A second author publishes a book applying the framework to a specific domain (Book Two written by someone else).
- Framework vocabulary enters general press without explanation.

### 1.5 Non-goals — stated explicitly

Captured as *not* goals to prevent drift during rigor passes:

- Personal financial independence from book sales.
- Systemic replacement of existing economic arrangements.
- Academic prizes.
- Immediate policy adoption at scale.
- Measurable short-term behavior change at population level.

*(Source: scope v1.0.3 §7.2.)*

### 1.6 Heuristics

**When in doubt, trim.** If a proposed structure, chapter move, or architectural elaboration dilutes any of the success signals in §1.3 without offsetting gain, prefer the trimmed version. A simpler framework that travels beats a more sophisticated framework that does not.

**Vocabulary portability is the load-bearing compression test.** Can the framework's key terms be compressed into citable phrases — the Harvey / Klein / Raworth test? "Severed cost" and "residual commons value" must be usable in a brief without a paragraph of setup. If a new term cannot be compressed this way, it fails at the point of greatest leverage for Goal 1.

*(Retained from rigor protocol §9. Implemented as Test 20, companion pair with Test 23 per v1.2.1.)*

## 2. Publishing path — integral enablement of success criteria

### 2.1 The load-bearing correction

The v1.0.0 draft of the scope document characterized the Noema / Berggruen / cascade strategy as "orthogonal" to Book One's scope. The v1.0.1 rigor pass corrected this: **the publishing path is integral.** It is the vocabulary's entry vector into discourse, and it is load-bearing on every success criterion in §1.

The causal chain that makes §1's success criteria possible:

> **Essay placement → vocabulary enters intellectual discourse → literary agent notices → book publisher acquires manuscript → Book One published with non-trivial distribution → vocabulary adoption becomes possible at the scale §1 assumes.**

Without essay placement, the chain breaks at step one. A self-published book by an independent author without prior essay circulation reaches an order of magnitude fewer readers than a publisher-backed book preceded by a Noema or Boston Review essay. The 13+-year labor-lawyer criterion (§1.1) assumes the vocabulary has reached labor lawyers. That reach depends on the publishing path succeeding at non-trivial circulation.

*(Source: scope v1.0.3 §8.1.)*

### 2.2 The cascade — venue order and profiles

1. **Noema** — primary submission. Noema's cross-spectrum intellectual-policy reach is the optimal first-mover venue for this framework. Evaluates intellectual-platform fit, cross-spectrum reach, long-form coherence, originality of contribution.
2. **Boston Review** — immediate fallback (24–48 hours after any Noema decline). Forum format fits dialogue-oriented reception; no simultaneous-submission restriction once Noema responds.
3. **The Atlantic Ideas** and further cascade venues — per the consolidated Publishing Strategy document.
4. **Berggruen Prize independent rewrite** — parallel credential path, AI-free, August 17, 2026 deadline. Evaluates long-term institutional thinking, methodological rigor, philosophical depth, lived-experience grounding. This track is independent of the Noema cascade and provides a second institutional anchor regardless of the cascade's outcome.

Specific agent and publisher names identified as next-stage targets live in the consolidated Publishing Strategy document (Apr 18, 2026). This document references the strategy; it does not replace it.

*(Source: scope v1.0.3 §8.2; venue-profile language consolidated from rigor protocol v1.2.1 §11.2 characters 5 and 6.)*

### 2.3 Why the path is load-bearing, not orthogonal

"Orthogonal" would mean the publishing path affects timing but not scope, content, or success-condition probability. The path is not orthogonal because:

- **Circulation scale** is a multiplier on every primary-tier success signal in §1.3. A labor lawyer reading a book with non-trivial circulation is an order of magnitude more likely than a labor lawyer reading a self-published manuscript.
- **Venue signaling** is load-bearing at the graduate-student and Ostrom-successor level (§1.3 signal 3; §1.4 signals 2 and 5). Scholars triage what to read by signals of intellectual seriousness; Noema and Berggruen are those signals.
- **Vocabulary adoption** requires early readers who will use the vocabulary in public. That happens through venue placement, not through word-of-mouth from a self-published manuscript.

### 2.4 Scope implications

The integration does **not** expand Book One's scope. It documents a dependency. Scope decisions in the scope document's §1–§3 hold independently; they are not revised by the publishing path's load-bearing status. What changes is the recognition that Book One's success criteria depend on the publishing path succeeding at non-trivial circulation.

Specific implications:

- Scope questions under scope v1.0.3 §5's decision heuristic should **not** factor in "what increases publishing appeal" — that would be reverse-fitting the framework to the market. The framework's integrity is primary; the publishing path's job is to carry the integrity-preserved framework to readers.
- Timing of Book Two is downstream of the publishing cascade's reception, not independent of it. Scope §2.4's supporting precondition #3 (Book One circulation for three-to-five years) assumes the cascade succeeds at some level. If the cascade fails entirely, scope §9's revisit trigger #1 fires.
- Scope revisions triggered by cascade outcomes go through scope §9's revisitability process, not scope §5's decision heuristic.

*(Source: scope v1.0.3 §8.3.)*

### 3.3 When a rigor pass finds a constraint not captured here

If a Group D or Group E verdict depends on a constraint not in §1 or §2, the correct path is to surface the constraint explicitly, not to ship the verdict on an unnamed input. Three options:

1. Update this document to a new version (add the constraint to §1 or §2 as appropriate).
2. Update the scope document at §7 or §8 and propagate here.
3. Record the constraint as a conditional in the decision memo, with a measurable gate, and queue an update.

The path forward is never silence. A claim that passes the rigor battery but implicitly relies on a success criterion not in §1, or on a cascade step not in §2.2, is shipping on an unnamed constraint — the same failure mode as shipping without running the applicable tests.

## 4. When this document should be revised

Revision produces v1.0.1 or higher when one of the following fires. Each trigger is restricted to what affects success criteria or publishing path; scope-level triggers broader than that live in scope v1.0.3 §9.

1. **Cascade reception strongly contradicts the core assumption.** Noema / Boston Review / further cascade produces either (a) systematic rejection with consistent editor feedback indicating the framework isn't landing, or (b) immediate high-profile engagement that would materially shift success-criteria probabilities. Either signals an update to §2, and possibly §1 if the feedback is about vocabulary rather than venue fit.
2. **Cascade structure changes** — new venue added, venue removed, order revised, Berggruen track status changes. §2.2 updates.
3. **Success-criteria revision** prompted by real-world evidence of adoption or non-adoption patterns. §1.2, §1.3, or §1.4 updates.
4. **Implicit constraint surfaces.** A rigor pass reveals that a Group D or Group E decision was routing through a constraint not captured here. Name it and update.
5. **Scope v1.0.3 is revised at §7 or §8.** Propagate.
6. **Document structure changes** at this level — e.g., the Publishing Strategy document is retired, split, or consolidated. §2.5's separation-of-concerns framing updates.

Version numbering: v1.0.1 for clarifications and minor updates; v1.1.0 for added or restructured sections; v2.0.0 for fundamental revision of what's captured.
