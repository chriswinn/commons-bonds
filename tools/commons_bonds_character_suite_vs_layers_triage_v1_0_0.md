## 1. Purpose of this triage

The test triage (2026-04-22) ran Groups A–F against the 10 layers. This triage does the same thing to the 25-character pressure suite plus the optional sub-batteries (Methodological Defense, Stakeholder, Meta).

Same two questions:

1. **Does pressing each character against the layer set surface something the layer work needs to see?** (Does the character's pressure point differentiate between layers, or does it press identically on all?)
2. **If the character earns its keep at the layer level, what does it do that the tests don't already do?** (Or does it duplicate a test?)

Same four verdicts:

- **LOAD-BEARING** — layer-set integrity visibly depends on this character's pressure; book-visible in how the layer is introduced or defended
- **SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT** — character surfaces something genuine on specific layers; book-visible through case selection, lit engagement, or framing
- **PROCESS-ONLY** — useful for author/publisher workflow but does not differentiate layers
- **CONSOLIDATE** — substantially overlaps a test or another character at the layer level

One structural point to note up front: the character suite is pressure from imagined readers, while Groups A–F are structural/procedural tests. Characters can duplicate tests in disguised form — a character whose only pressure is "is this genuinely novel?" is Test 18 in human shape. The duplications are surfaced below.

## 2. Group G — Critics (attacking)

### 1. Hostile coal-industry defender
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT — for Ecological and Political; light-duty elsewhere.** Pressures miner-dignity framing, the "cheap energy lifted billions out of poverty" counterargument, and claims of bias. At the layer level: Ecological (carbon-pricing implication), Political (regulatory-capture implication), and Institutional (industry-state allocation). Book-visible in how Ch. 8 (coal) is framed and how the Ecological layer is introduced without moralism. For the other seven layers, this character detects little pressure.

### 2. Left critic ("reformed capitalism")
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT at framework level — LAYER-META pressure, not per-layer.** Presses "why these 10 layers instead of a class-based structure?" — forces the framework to defend its architectural choices against structuralist critique. Does not differentiate between layers (presses the whole set uniformly), but the pressure is real and book-visible in the introduction and framework-positioning prose.

### 3. Right critic ("anti-market")
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT — for Political, Institutional, and (surprisingly) Agency.** Classic pressure on Political (regulation in disguise) and Institutional (redistributive intervention). The surprising pressure is on Agency: the libertarian appropriation of non-coercion language creates a failure mode where right-coded readers assume the layer endorses a libertarian framework. The response in the book is the cross-ideological-traction feature of Agency — leftist anti-exploitation and libertarian non-coercion both land on it, which is why it was strongest on primitiveness. Book-visible.

### 4. Journalist looking for hit-piece angle
**Verdict: CONSOLIDATE with Test 18 at layer level.** At the layer level, this character's pressure ("is this just rebranding Ostrom / Polanyi / Harvey / Raworth?") is Test 18 (competitor/overlap) in human shape. At the chapter/case level the character diverges — looks for self-serving framing, selective data, author-contradiction. Keep for chapter/case work; at layer level run Test 18 and call it the same thing.

### 6. Berggruen Prize judge

### 7. Publisher's legal counsel

### 8. Literary agent

Book-visible through the economics-adjacent footnotes per layer.

## 4. Group I — Intended users

### 10. Labor lawyer in 2039
**Verdict: LOAD-BEARING.** This character IS Test 23 in human form. Layer-critical: can each layer name be used in a brief or open court without needing to explain it? Pressure differentiates layers sharply — "temporal cost" reads naturally, "cohesion cost" does not. Run as Test 23 and this character together (which v1.2.1 has now marked as a companion pair).

### 11. Community organizer
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT — overlaps partially with Test 17.** Tests whether each layer gives language for negotiation without requiring demonization of the other side. Real work on all 10 layers: does the layer name allow the organizer to sit across from a counterparty and make a claim without triggering defensive shutdown? Political is hardest (the name carries partisan valence). Agency is easiest (cross-ideological traction). Distinct from Test 17 in emphasis: Test 17 is pattern-matching; this character is negotiation-usability. Both are worth running; where Test 17 says "yes, this community organizer recognizes their situation," this character says "yes, but can they use it?"

### 12. Graduate student building on the work
**Verdict: CONSOLIDATE with Test 18.** Citability, method-portability, extendability per layer. The protocol's own language in §11.3 acknowledges this overlap: "Absorbs v1.1.0's Novelty Critic to this extent." At layer level, this character is Test 18 plus some extendability framing. Run Test 18; don't double-run this character at layer level.

### 13. Policy researcher at a think tank
**Verdict: SCAFFOLDING, EARNS IT — for specific layers.** Differentiated pressure: Ecological (strong — environmental policy), Political (strong — governance reform), Institutional (moderate — organizational policy), Temporal (moderate — intergenerational policy, retirement, fiscal), Informational (growing — AI, data policy), Agency (emerging — employment law, coercion policy). Weaker on Cohesion, Demographic, Geographic, Atmospheric. Useful signal for which layers have applied-policy traction.

### 15. Young worker (Ch. 1's audience)

## 10. Meta-findings

### Groups I and J do most of the layer-level work
As expected from the protocol's design, Group I (intended users) and Group J (cultural readers) are where the character suite does most of its per-layer differentiation. Group G (critics) presses specific layers (1 on Ecological, 3 on Agency). Group H (gatekeepers) is mostly process-only except 7 (legal counsel) and 9 (economics chair). Group K (personal) is mostly process-only except 21 (nursing colleague, corrected) and — marginally — 19 (family-member, overlapping with Test 20). Group L (ethical) is small but highly differentiated per character.

### Five characters duplicate tests at the layer level
Characters 4, 12, 18, 19, and 24 are reader-personas of tests the protocol already runs (18, 18, 20, 20, 19 respectively). The protocol already acknowledges two of these overlaps (Character 12 → Novelty Critic → Test 18; Character 24 → Future-Proofer → Test 19). v1.2.2 or v1.3.0 could formalize the other three with notes. At layer level, run the tests; keep the characters for chapter/case-level work where they diverge.

### The nursing colleague correction mirrors the Test 13 correction
The same logical structure: a character/test that looked non-differentiating at the layer level turns out to do real work at the layer level via a layer's canonical cases. This is a pattern worth watching: whenever a career-risk or retaliation pressure exists, the pressure routes through specific layers (those whose canonical cases name specific industries with retaliation capacity), not through the layer system as a whole. It's layer-inherent via cases, not layer-extrinsic.

### The Methodological Defense sub-battery is doing real work
M4 (Completeness) and M1 (Analytic Philosopher) both do layer-level work. M2 and M3 are process-only or redundant respectively. Keep the sub-battery but consider whether M3 needs to exist independently given Test 18 and Character 12 already cover it.

### Recommended sequencing for the Part 2 rigor pass
When gap-closing the 10 layers against v1.2.1, prioritize in this order:
1. Tests 1–4 (framework tests) — already done per v1.21.0
2. Group E tests 20/23 as companion pair + Character 10 (labor lawyer 2039) + M1 (analytic philosopher) — naming cohort
3. Characters 9, 11, 13 — differentiated per-layer traction
4. Characters 14, 15, 16, 21, 23, 25 — canonical-case authenticity and ethical anchoring
5. Characters 1, 3, 7 — critic/gatekeeper pressure on specific layers
6. Groups B/C/D/F as applicable
7. Characters 2, 17, X1 — framework-meta pressure
8. Skip at layer level: Characters 4, 5, 6, 8, 12, 18, 19, 20, 22, 24 (consolidated or process-only)

This sequencing runs roughly 15 differentiating characters per layer rather than all 25, which is what v1.2.1's full-vs-light rigor axis anticipated would be appropriate at the layer level.
