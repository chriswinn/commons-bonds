## §1. Executive recommendation

**Path F wins on three of four goals; Path A and Path B are second across remaining trade-offs; Paths C, D, E are dominated.**

The framework-scope question is not "how much framework should the book carry?" but "what is the framework FOR?" The answer the suite returns: the framework is for **giving readers a tool they can apply in their own contexts**, not for **giving readers a taxonomy they have to memorize**. Path F's reframing (the 10 abundances and 8 tiers as *examples of variables AIT discovered in our cases*, not as canonical ontology) makes the framework generative rather than definitional. That single reframing strengthens publishing path success, success criteria, and long-term project impact simultaneously, with only one significant trade-off — academic reception under reviewers who prefer ontological commitment to methodological generativity. Even there, Path F holds even with Path A and pulls ahead under reviewers who prize methodological contribution (which is the modal academic-reviewer position in the relevant fields).

The runner-up is Path B (compress to CORE) — strong on the same goals as Path F but gives up the dimensions and tiers entirely, which loses content the cases are organized around in current chapter drafts. Path A (status quo) preserves all current investment but is structurally weakest on success criteria and long-term impact because the 10-dimension taxonomy + 8-tier decomposition is a lot for readers to carry and a lot of surface for novelty-skeptics to attack as taxonomic restatement of existing work.

**The recommended path is Path F.** Its implementation is materially less expensive than Paths C / D / E (most current chapter drafts already contain the dimension/tier material; Path F reframes how it's presented rather than removing it). It preserves the case-study work already done. It addresses the framework smell-test concern that triggered this PCR by recasting the 10-dimension elaboration as illustrative variable-discovery rather than canonical taxonomy.

The decisive evidence: Path F is the only path where the asteroid-miner-considering-off-world-contract example works as written. In every other path, the example is either out-of-scope (Paths A, C, D — taxonomy-mode framework doesn't natively give individual readers a tool) or under-supported (Paths B, E — CORE is present but the cases the reader needs to extrapolate from are absent). Path F is the path that delivers Goal-2 (individual-reader use) at full strength.

**Decision rationale section (§8) intentionally left blank for Chris.** The PCR's job is to produce findings + recommendations; the choice is the author's. If Chris weights academic reception substantially higher than the default, Path F vs. Path A becomes a closer competition.

### Path F — Variables-not-dimensions reframing (NEW per Chris's 2026-04-23 articulation)

**Description:** CORE same as Path B (AIT + RCV + CS + formal vocabulary). The 10 abundances + 8 tiers reframed as **examples of variables AIT discovered across the cases we examined — illustrative, not ontological**. Framework becomes generative: AIT is the method; RCV is the equation; readers identify which variables are present in their own situation and plug them into the equation. The book teaches the method and provides cases as worked examples; the reader's job is to apply the method in their own contexts.

**Critical distinction from Path B:** Path B *removes* dimensions and tiers. Path F *re-positions* them as illustrative variables. The cases stay (they demonstrate variable-discovery in action). The dimensions and tiers stay (as variables that turned out to matter across the cases we examined). What changes is the reader's posture: instead of memorizing a taxonomy, the reader learns a method and applies it.

**Retained from current state:**
- CORE (AIT + RCV + CS + vocabulary).
- 10 abundances (now framed as "abundances in the sense of variables we identified across the cases — yours may include these or others").
- 8-tier-style accounting (now framed as "one way to organize per-case accounting; other organizations are possible").
- 18 case studies (now framed as "worked examples of the method, each demonstrating variable-identification in a specific extraction context").
- Chapter audit + case-study audit with reframed verdicts.

**Removed from current state:** Nothing in content, but ontological commitment to the 10-dimension taxonomy is removed. Same for the 8-tier decomposition.

**Restructured / reframed from current state:**
- Ch 6 methodology chapter: substantially reframed. From "three convergent approaches that each produce a comprehensive cost picture" → "AIT identifies variables; RCV computes cost severance; here are three convergent computational approaches that have produced consistent results across our cases." More compact, more generative.
- Ch 8 worked example: from "tier-by-tier accounting" → "AIT applied to McDowell coal yields the following variables; here's how each contributes to the RCV computation." Variables are the same as the existing 8 tiers, but presented as discoveries from the case rather than slots to fill.
- Ch 7 universality test: significantly strengthens. Mars colony administrator is a different context with different variables; the method works in both contexts and that's the universality demonstration.
- Throughout: vocabulary shifts from "dimensions" to "abundances" (variables); from "tiers" to "cost categories" (or simply "variables grouped at per-case level").

**Net new content added:**
- Brief methodological note in Ch 6 explaining the variable-discovery framing.
- One paragraph in Ch 1 or prologue setting up the framework as a tool the reader can apply.
- Strengthened end of Ch 7 (asteroid-miner-considering-off-world-contract example or similar — making explicit the framework's individual-scale usability).
- Possibly a Ch 10 brief passage encouraging the reader to apply the method in their own context.

**Implications:**
- For framework: same content surface as Path A but reframed; less ontological commitment; more methodological commitment.
- For content: chapter drafts substantially intact but reframed (Ch 6 + Ch 8 most affected; Ch 1 + Ch 7 strengthened; other chapters lighter touch).
- For positioning: strongest dinner-table pitch ("here's an equation you can apply to any extraction situation in your own life"); strongest comp-title fit (Doughnut Economics + Thinking Fast and Slow style — give-the-reader-a-tool books); strongest individual-reader (Goal-2) delivery.
- For long-term: maximum cascade durability — equation + method survives as taxonomies evolve; specific dimension/tier names can be revised by future scholars without unraveling the framework.

**Standing-gate implications:**
- Earning-its-place: dimensions and tiers earn their place as illustrative-variables (not as canonical taxonomy); the book's dimensions/tiers content does less ontological work and more pedagogical work.
- Scaffolding-vs-book-worthy: dimensions and tiers stay book-worthy but their function is teaching-method-via-example, not asserting-canonical-categories.
- Sensitivity-reader: unchanged; indigenous case remains the load-bearing Ch 10 anchor with sensitivity-reader engagement before publication.

**Provenance:** Chris's articulation 2026-04-23 (after Paths A-E proposed and v2.0.0 committed): "the small illustrative set is really used to show each Abundance that is not tested is a variable that was previously so small in the equation that we weren't pricing it. So instead of dimensions, or tiers, each identified thing is a variable we can show exists, and future work would go into pricing the variable we can now see and likely discover additional variables that are relevant and not relevant given the situation you run the RCV formula in."

Refinement: retain enough cases (5-7 minimum) covering each scale × environment cell.

The argument shifts from "the same dimensions activate at all scales" (a stronger claim that requires the dimensions to be ontological) to "AIT identifies relevant variables at any scale; RCV computes from whatever variable set the user identifies" (a weaker but more defensible claim that makes the universality demonstration cleaner).

**No path is dropped at the CORE survival gate.** All six proceed to standing-gate and goal-mapped module assessment.

## §6. Trade-off surface

This section names the cross-goal trade-offs the path comparison surfaces. Per protocol §22.7, trade-off patterns matter more than per-goal verdicts: if the same trade-off appears across multiple paths, it's structural and the author has to commit to one side.

### Trade-off 1: Ontological commitment vs. methodological generativity

**The trade-off:** Some academic reviewers prefer that frameworks commit to specific ontologies (this is what's in the world; here are the categories). Other academic reviewers prefer that frameworks contribute methods (here's how to discover what's in the world; categories are illustrative).

- **Ontology-preferring reviewers** favor Paths A, C, D over Paths B, E, F. The 10-dimension or 8-tier taxonomy is the contribution; the categories themselves do work.
- **Methodology-preferring reviewers** favor Paths B, E, F over Paths A, C, D. The equation + AIT is the contribution; categories illustrate but don't constrain.

**Why this trade-off is structural:** academic disciplines genuinely differ on this. Sociology and political theory often prefer ontological commitment (Ostrom's eight design principles is the model). Economics and decision theory often prefer methodological contribution (Pigou, Coase, Hartwick are method/equation contributions; the categories follow from application). Commons Bonds straddles both fields.

**How the trade-off resolves per path:**
- Path A maximizes ontological commitment (full taxonomy) → strong with sociology/political-theory reviewers; weak with economics reviewers who file as taxonomic restatement.
- Path F maximizes methodological generativity (equation + method + illustrative variables) → strong with economics/decision-theory reviewers; the one identified caveat (M1 sub-battery analytic philosopher) addresses ontological-commitment-preferring reviewers via Ch 6 strengthening.
- Paths B, C, D, E sit between but lean toward methodological side.

**Resolution:** **Path F's hybrid (generative method + illustrative variables) is the highest-leverage position on this trade-off** because it gives ontological-preferring reviewers something to point at (the 10 abundances + 8 tiers as illustrative) while giving methodological-preferring reviewers what they want (the equation + AIT). Path A gives only one side; Path B/E gives only the other side. Path F gives both, with the framing that determines reception.

### Trade-off 2: Comprehensiveness vs. accessibility

**The trade-off:** A more comprehensive framework (more dimensions, more tiers, more cases) is more impressive to specialists and more demanding for general readers. A more accessible framework (fewer concepts, less taxonomy) is easier for general readers and may seem under-developed to specialists.

- **Specialist reviewers** prefer Paths A, C, D.
- **General readers + dinner-table audiences** prefer Paths B, E, F.

**Why this trade-off is structural:** book audience is bimodal — academics + general readers. Trade press wants accessibility; academic press wants depth.

**How the trade-off resolves per path:**
- Path A maximizes specialist appeal at significant cost to general-reader accessibility.
- Path E maximizes general-reader accessibility at significant cost to specialist depth.
- **Path F resolves the trade-off uniquely:** the framework is generative (specialist-friendly: this is a methodological contribution), and individual readers can apply it without memorizing the taxonomy (general-reader-friendly: here's the equation, here's the method, the dimensions are examples). The taxonomy is in the book for specialists; readers don't have to carry it.

**Resolution:** **Path F is the only path that doesn't require a comprehensive-vs-accessible compromise.** Other paths must choose; Path F serves both.

### Trade-off 3: Implementation cost vs. positioning improvement

**The trade-off:** More aggressive path changes (Paths B, E especially; D somewhat) require more substantial chapter rewrites. Less aggressive paths (A, C, F somewhat) preserve more of the existing manuscript investment.

- **Path A** requires no rewriting (preserves all existing investment).
- **Path B / Path E** require substantial chapter rewrites (Ch 5 + Ch 6 + Ch 8 most affected).
- **Paths C / D** require partial chapter rewrites (Ch 6 + Ch 8 most affected; some throughout-book strip work).
- **Path F** requires reframing rather than rewriting — chapter drafts substantially intact; Ch 6 + Ch 8 most affected but reframing is lighter than rewriting.

**Why this trade-off is structural:** rewriting time is real time. Chris is constrained by nursing-school + coursework + first-time-author limitations.

**How the trade-off resolves per path:**
- Path A: 0% of new content; high cost in long-term goal compromise.
- Path B / E: 30-40% of book content needs material rewriting; high opportunity cost in time.
- Path C / D: 15-20% of book content needs rewriting.
- **Path F: 5-10% of book content needs reframing (lighter touch than rewriting).** Path F is the lowest-implementation-cost path that achieves the goal-aggregate strength.

**Resolution:** Path F again — lowest implementation cost AND highest goal-aggregate scores. This is the unusual finding the suite produces: the path that's best for the goals is also cheapest to implement.

### Trade-off 4: Author authority vs. reader authority

**The trade-off:** A framework that asserts "here are THE categories of cost severance" claims author authority. A framework that says "here's a method to discover variables in YOUR situation" delegates authority to the reader.

- **Authoritative-framework readers** favor Paths A, C, D (the book is the authority).
- **Tool-for-readers audiences** favor Paths B, E, F (the reader is the authority; the book is the instrument).

**Why this trade-off is structural:** different reader populations want different things from a book.

**How the trade-off resolves per path:**
- Path A is maximally authoritative (here is the system; learn it).
- Path E is minimally authoritative (here is an equation; figure out the rest).
- **Path F is the most generative middle:** the book is authoritative about the equation + method + vocabulary (these are precisely defined and load-bearing). The book is delegative about what variables apply to your situation (use AIT to find them).

**Resolution:** Path F is structurally the right level of author/reader authority for a book that wants to enable tool-use rather than codify a final taxonomy. This is consistent with how *Doughnut Economics* and *Thinking, Fast and Slow* operate — the books are authoritative about the framework but delegative about application.

### Trade-off 5: Indigenous case retention

**The trade-off:** Paths that retire substantial case content (B, E especially) face a decision about whether to retain the indigenous case (Lakota / Black Hills anchor in Ch 10). Path E's compression risks dropping it.

**Why this trade-off matters:** the indigenous case is load-bearing on Book 1's cross-spectrum credibility (per chapter audit + indigenous case file §7.1) and on the framework's claim of universality across foundational extraction events. Dropping it weakens both.

**How the trade-off resolves per path:**
- Paths A, B, C, D, F retain the indigenous case at Ch 10 anchor depth (varying depth for B; at-current-depth for the others).
- **Path E is the only path that risks retiring or compressing the indigenous case below the threshold.** This is a path-specific concern.

**Resolution:** Path E should explicitly retain the indigenous case at brief-anchor depth at minimum. The other paths handle this trade-off at current-state depth or deeper.
