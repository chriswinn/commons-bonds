Commons Bonds: Rigor Testing Roles & Perspectives

Adversarial Critics and Hostile Readers for Framework Stress Testing

Compiled from all Commons Bonds project conversations and documents

How to Use This Document: These roles are designed for steelmanning the strongest possible objections to the Commons Bonds framework. Each role represents a different intellectual tradition, methodological approach, or value system that would critique the framework from their specific angle. Use these when you need to stress-test arguments, anticipate counterarguments, or ensure intellectual rigor.

Academic Critics

These critics approach the framework from established academic disciplines and methodological traditions.

The Academic Economist

Looking for category errors, methodological rigor, and alignment with economic theory
Attack Style: Challenges parameter selection, questions novelty relative to existing economic frameworks, points out measurement difficulties
Example Attack: "Your model requires estimating substitutability functions, externality tails, and discount factors that are fundamentally unobservable. The IPG for coal ranges from 5x to 133x depending on which social cost of carbon you use. That's not a model - that's choose-your-own-adventure. I can get any answer I want by adjusting parameters."
Defense Strategy: Acknowledge uncertainty but argue the framework structures it rather than eliminating it. Current market pricing (externalities = $0) is precisely wrong, not uncertain.

The Development Economist

Focused on global poverty, industrialization pathways, and developing world energy access
Attack Style: "Cheap energy lifted billions out of poverty" argument, concerned about blocking developing world industrialization
Example Attack: "If you had applied RCV pricing in 1960, coal would have been $500/ton instead of $4.50. The industrial revolution would have been unaffordable. The Green Revolution that saved a billion people from famine depended on cheap natural gas for fertilizer. Your framework is environmental colonialism."
Defense Strategy: Distinguish between "shouldn't have happened" vs. "was underpriced." Advocate for differentiated transition timelines and technology transfer funded by historical cost severance.

The Novelty Critic

Asking whether each concept is genuinely new or just renamed existing ideas
Attack Style: Compares to existing frameworks (Ostrom, Hartwick, Hotelling), challenges claims of originality
Example Attack: "Cost Severance vs 'externality' or 'accumulation by dispossession'? RCV vs 'social cost of carbon'? You're claiming four novel contributions when Ostrom won a Nobel for one insight. This looks like relabeling existing concepts."
Defense Strategy: Show precisely what each concept adds beyond existing frameworks - specific mechanisms and measurements that didn't exist before.

Philosophical Critics

These critics examine the framework's conceptual coherence and underlying assumptions.

The Analytic Philosopher

Looking for ontological confusion and category errors in what types of things are being treated as equivalent
Attack Style: Examines whether pillars are the same "kind" of thing, checks logical structure and coherence
Example Attack: "Cost Severance is a mechanism operating in the world. RCV is a quantity in principle measurable. The Abundance Test is a cognitive procedure performed by analysts. These are three different ontological categories being presented as equivalent pillars."
Defense Strategy: Clarify the distinction between concepts/mechanisms and methods/procedures. Maintain clean categorical boundaries.

The Falsifiability Critic

Examining whether the framework makes testable predictions or is unfalsifiable
Attack Style: Points out that CS > 0 in all tested cases suggests the model can't be wrong
Example Attack: "You tested six cases and found CS > 0 in all six. But you selected cases that are famous environmental disasters. Show me a case where your model predicts CS ≤ 0. If no such case exists, the model is unfalsifiable."
Defense Strategy: Identify cases where CS could equal zero (sustainably managed renewables, asteroid mining) and explain the structural reasons CS > 0 is found everywhere currently.

Political-Economic Critics

These critics examine the framework through different political and economic lenses.

The Libertarian/Property Rights Theorist

Believes property rights and markets already solve commons problems optimally
Attack Style: Argues for Coasean solutions, questions government pricing mechanisms
Example Attack: "The problem isn't market failure - it's commons failure. Clear property rights solve this. If communities held enforceable rights over air and water quality, companies would have to compensate for degradation. You're replacing imperfect markets with political mechanisms subject to capture."
Defense Strategy: Acknowledge what property rights solve well, then identify structural limitations (intergenerational foreclosure, transboundary externalities, information asymmetries).

The Leftist/Anti-Capitalist Critic

Argues the framework doesn't go far enough and tries to reform rather than replace capitalism
Attack Style: Views cost severance as capitalism working as designed, not as a fixable error
Example Attack: "Cost severance isn't market failure - it's a market feature. Capitalism requires externalizing costs to generate profit. You're rescuing capitalism by absorbing the critique into a reformed version. The system that created McDowell County doesn't deserve reform - it deserves replacement."
Defense Strategy: Accept the power analysis while maintaining that pricing is necessary even if insufficient. The framework works under any ownership structure.

The Conservative/Traditionalist

Defends extraction communities' agency and dignity, skeptical of elite environmental concerns
Attack Style: Emphasizes miner pride and community choice, sees environmental arguments as coastal elite paternalism
Example Attack: "My grandfather worked in the mines and was proud of it. You're treating extraction communities as passive victims, but they chose mining and fought for mining jobs. Your 'liberation' is experienced as elite abandonment and economic destruction."
Defense Strategy: Honor the work and workers while arguing they were structurally undercompensated for what they sacrificed. Frame as workers deserving fair compensation, not environmental paternalism.

Methodological Critics

These critics focus on the framework's research methods and analytical approaches.

The AI Adversary

Systematically trying to reduce, collapse, or falsify each claim through logical analysis
Attack Style: Looks for logical inconsistencies, reduction opportunities, and false convergences
Example Attack: "Run the irreducibility test on each pillar. Cost Severance: irreducible. RCV: irreducible. Abundance Test: reducible to methodology. Two of your claimed pillars fail the test. Either demote them or admit they're not the same kind of thing."
Defense Strategy: Distinguish between substantive concepts and methodological tools. Maintain clear hierarchy of what constitutes a fundamental contribution vs. a method.

The Completeness Critic

Argues the framework's categories are chosen rather than derived, missing important dimensions
Attack Style: Questions why certain cost categories are included but not others
Example Attack: "Your damage function includes Health + Environment + Carbon + Community. Why not Political Capture, Cultural Erosion, and Information Asymmetry? Under the abundance test, any of these could become visible. Your categories are historical accidents of what's been measured."
Defense Strategy: Acknowledge limitation while arguing the framework is expandable and the included categories represent current measurement capabilities.

The Complexity Theorist

Argues Earth systems are too complex for the simplified Mars analogy
Attack Style: Emphasizes feedback loops, emergent properties, and irreducible complexity
Example Attack: "A Mars colony of 10,000 people with three water deposits is nothing like Earth with eight billion people and massive resource stocks. Your model produces 'obviously correct' answers on Mars because Mars is simple. Earth has political feedback loops that Mars doesn't have."
Defense Strategy: Acknowledge complexity differences while maintaining that the structural logic (finite resources, irreversible extraction, intergenerational consequences) remains valid.

Stakeholder Critics

These critics represent specific affected groups and their interests.

The Extraction Industry Defender

Represents mining, oil, and gas industry perspectives and concerns
Attack Style: Emphasizes job creation, economic benefits, energy security, and transition costs
Example Attack: "Energy security requires domestic production. Pricing reforms that make extraction uneconomical force dependence on unstable regions. The transition costs - stranded assets, unemployment, economic disruption - fall hardest on working-class communities."
Defense Strategy: Acknowledge legitimate security concerns while arguing for transition support funded by pricing surpluses and diversification strategies.

The Global South Representative

Argues the framework disadvantages developing nations that need cheap energy to industrialize
Attack Style: Frames full pricing as environmental colonialism that locks in global inequality
Example Attack: "Developing nations need cheap energy to industrialize. Full pricing locks them into poverty while rich nations that industrialized with cheap fossil fuels pull up the ladder behind them. This is environmental colonialism disguised as justice."
Defense Strategy: Propose differentiated pricing, technology transfer, and climate finance mechanisms that account for historical responsibility and development needs.

The Environmental Justice Advocate

Concerned that pricing alone won't address power imbalances that allow extraction to harm vulnerable communities
Attack Style: Emphasizes power dynamics, environmental racism, and the limits of market solutions
Example Attack: "Pricing doesn't change power structures. Polluting industries will still locate in poor communities and communities of color because land is cheaper and political resistance is weaker. Market solutions can't solve power problems."
Defense Strategy: Acknowledge that pricing alone is insufficient; argue it's necessary but must be combined with community empowerment and democratic reform.

Structural Critics

These critics examine how well the framework functions as an intellectual structure.

The Writerly Editor

Worried about reader comprehension, working memory limits, and structural integrity
Attack Style: Questions whether the framework is too complex for general readers to follow
Example Attack: "Readers can track two or three central concepts through a long argument. Four starts to feel like an encyclopedia entry. When you finish and try to summarize over a beer, 'here are the four pillars' is what people say about frameworks they didn't understand."
Defense Strategy: Simplify the pillar structure and ensure each element serves the core argument rather than proliferating concepts.

The Future-Proofer

Asking whether the framework architecture can scale and accommodate new discoveries
Attack Style: Tests whether new methods or concepts can be added without breaking the structure
Example Attack: "If you discover a new method later - a reversibility test, a power-distance test - does it compete for pillar status? This architecture doesn't scale. You'll either keep adding pillars or have to re-architect everything."
Defense Strategy: Design clear distinctions between core concepts and methods that allow expansion without structural disruption.

The Practical Implementation Critic

Skeptical about the political feasibility of implementing the framework
Attack Style: Points to the difficulty of passing carbon pricing after decades of effort
Example Attack: "Carbon pricing has been debated for thirty years with limited success. How do you implement comprehensive RCV pricing covering every resource and externality against the resistance of the most powerful industries on Earth? Show me the political coalition."
Defense Strategy: Frame as a conceptual framework that changes what's conceivable rather than a legislative blueprint. Identify entering wedges and incremental pathways.

Meta-Critics

These critics examine the framework from a higher level, questioning its overall approach or purpose.

The Degrowth Critic

Argues the framework is just disguised economic contraction
Attack Style: Claims that dramatically higher resource prices necessarily mean economic shrinkage
Example Attack: "When you raise the price of every material input by 2-10x, you're contracting the economy. GDP will fall, employment will fall, living standards will fall. You can call it 'honest pricing' but the effect is degrowth."
Defense Strategy: Distinguish between GDP and welfare, argue for compositional change rather than overall shrinkage, point to historical precedents of beneficial repricing.

The Mars Analogy Critic

Argues the interplanetary comparison is a false analogy
Attack Style: Emphasizes qualitative differences between planetary systems that invalidate the comparison
Example Attack: "Mars makes the model's logic undeniable because scarcity is immediate and lethal. Earth has gradual, generational feedback loops. The systems are qualitatively different. Your asteroid mining example shows the model reduces to market pricing when externalities are zero."
Defense Strategy: Argue that Mars illuminates the general case precisely because parameters are extreme enough to make logic undeniable. The structural problem is the same.

The Scientific Standards Critic

Questions whether the framework meets peer-review standards for publication
Attack Style: Examines methodological rigor against academic publication standards
Example Attack: "This framework was developed through AI collaboration rather than traditional peer review. The mathematical work hasn't been independently verified. The case studies are selected rather than representative. Academic standards require replication and independent validation."
Defense Strategy: Acknowledge the non-traditional development process while arguing the mathematical framework is transparent and testable. Frame as a contribution to public intellectual discourse.

How to Deploy These Roles

For Individual Arguments: Pick 2-3 most relevant critics for the specific claim you're testing. Role-play their strongest objections before developing your response.

For Framework Testing: Use the "Big Six" (Academic Economist, Analytic Philosopher, AI Adversary, Writerly Editor, Future-Proofer, Novelty Critic) as a comprehensive stress test battery.

For Public Presentation: Anticipate the Stakeholder Critics (Extraction Industry, Global South, Environmental Justice) as your likely audience objections.

For Academic Peer Review: Focus on Academic Critics and Methodological Critics to ensure scholarly rigor.

For Policy Development: Engage Political-Economic Critics and the Implementation Critic to test real-world viability.
