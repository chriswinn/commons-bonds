Economic & political theory — master reference

Commons Bonds project · April 2026 · Chris Winn

Part I — The ten major frameworks

Smith, Ricardo, Mill · 18th–19th c.

1. Classical liberalism / laissez-faire capitalism

Markets allocate resources most efficiently through price signals and competition. Self-interest channeled through markets produces collective benefit — the "invisible hand." Private ownership of capital, land, and resources is the assumed default.

Who captures profit
Private owners of capital — whoever owns the means of production, land, or resources.
Who owns commons / resources
Private individuals or firms. Private ownership is assumed to produce better stewardship than public ownership.

Core weakness

No mechanism for pricing externalities. The $100 barrel has no instrument for pricing permanent depletion, environmental harm, or long-term community instability. Nature is treated as a free input.

Marx, Engels · 19th c.

2. Marxist political economy

Capitalism generates wealth through surplus value extraction — workers produce more value than they are paid, and owners capture the difference. This is not an aberration; it is the fundamental engine of capital accumulation. Class conflict between owners and workers is inherent and irresolvable within the system. The commons are systematically enclosed — converted from shared use to private ownership through what Marx called primitive accumulation.

Who captures profit
The capitalist class — those who own the means of production.
Who owns commons / resources
Under capitalism, commons are enclosed into private ownership. The Marxist end-state is collective/state ownership of the means of production.

Core weakness

The classic model focuses on the labor relation. It is less developed on resource depletion, ecological limits, or finite non-renewable resources. Marx assumed industrial abundance; the framework strains at permanence of loss.

Harvey · 2004

3. Accumulation by dispossession

Capitalism grows not only by generating new value through labor exploitation, but by appropriating existing value from communities, ecosystems, and public goods. This process is ongoing, not merely historical. Privatization, financialization, and the stripping of public assets are all forms of dispossession. The mechanism follows a consistent pattern: penetrate a resource-rich community → extract at minimal cost to the firm → externalize social and environmental costs onto the community → depart when extraction is no longer profitable.

Who captures profit
Mobile capital — corporations and financial actors who penetrate, extract, externalize costs, and depart.
Who owns commons / resources
Commons are continuously converted into private profit through legal, financial, or political enclosure.

Core weakness

Harvey names the pattern but doesn't fully resolve the valuation problem — how do you price a non-renewable resource that will never exist again? The departure cost, the intergenerational dimension, and the permanent foreclosure problem are gestured at but not formally resolved.

Keynes · 20th c.

4. Keynesian economics

Markets are not self-correcting. Government intervention — public spending, demand management, social insurance — is necessary to stabilize the economy and prevent the worst outcomes of market failure. The state has a legitimate role in managing the economy in the public interest. Redistribution through public goods and social programs is a tool for correcting market outcomes, not replacing them.

Who captures profit
Still largely private, but the state recaptures some value through taxation and redistributes it through public goods and social programs.
Who owns commons / resources
Ambiguous. Accepts private ownership but argues the state must actively manage externalities and market failures. Does not resolve the ownership question.

Core weakness

Keynesianism is a management theory, not an ownership theory. It doesn't challenge who owns the oil — it just argues the government should tax the extraction and spend the revenue wisely. Still has no instrument for pricing permanent depletion.

Hayek, Friedman · Reagan/Thatcher era

5. Neoliberalism

The state itself is the primary problem. Markets are always more efficient than governments at allocating resources. Deregulation, privatization, and free trade maximize growth. Social programs create dependency and distort incentives. This framework accelerated the shift from industrial capital to financialized capital, creating conditions for large-scale risk transfer from institutions to individuals (the pension-to-401k shift is an example).

Who captures profit
Financial capital and large corporations. Neoliberalism maximizes private capture by stripping the mechanisms that previously recaptured some value for the public.
Who owns commons / resources
Everything should be privatized if possible. Water rights, broadcast spectrum, genetic information, public pension systems — all converted into market assets.

Core weakness

The externalization problem is maximized here. With regulation stripped, there is no mechanism at all for capturing the social and environmental cost of extraction. This is the system the Commons Bonds framework most directly targets.

Ostrom · Nobel 2009 · against Hardin's "Tragedy" (1968)

6. Commons theory (Ostrom)

Hardin argued shared resources would always be depleted by individual rational actors over-using them. Ostrom's empirical research proved this wrong — communities across history have successfully governed shared resources through collective institutions with clear rules, monitoring, and graduated sanctions. The commons doesn't fail by nature; it fails when undermined by enclosure, privatization, or the destruction of governing institutions. Ostrom identified community governance as a legitimate third form of property alongside private and state ownership.

Who captures profit
The community as a whole — benefit is shared, cost is shared, and long-term stewardship is built into the governance structure.
Who owns commons / resources
Collective, community-governed ownership — neither purely private nor state-owned. A genuine third form of property.

Core weakness for Commons Bonds

Ostrom's model works well for renewable or slow-depleting resources (fisheries, forests, groundwater). It is harder to apply to non-renewable finite resources that can only be extracted once. A community governing a forest can set sustainable harvest rates. A community governing an oil field is governing something that will simply cease to exist.

Daly, Georgescu-Roegen · 20th c.

7. Ecological economics

The economy is a subsystem of the biosphere, not the reverse. All economic activity depends on natural systems, and those systems have limits. Standard economics — including both capitalism and socialism — treats nature as an infinite free input and ignores thermodynamic limits. Ecological economics insists on steady-state economics: an economy operating within the regenerative capacity of ecosystems. This is the framework most directly engaged with the permanent depletion problem at the core of Commons Bonds.

Who captures profit
Largely agnostic about ownership structure — compatible with capitalism, socialism, or the commons, as long as the system operates within ecological limits.
Who owns commons / resources
Strongly argues for treating the biosphere and non-renewable resources as a commons governed for long-term sustainability.

Key contribution for Commons Bonds

The $100 barrel is structurally wrong because it doesn't include the full cost of depletion of a finite resource, the social discount rate (what future generations are owed for permanent loss), or environmental externalities. The correct price, under ecological economics, is vastly higher and would include something like a depletion rent paid to the permanent community.

Nordic models · European labor movements

8. Social democracy

Capitalism can be made to work for workers and communities if the state is powerful enough to tax it heavily, regulate it firmly, and provide robust public goods. Private ownership is retained, but profit is substantially recaptured through taxation and redistributed as universal services. Norway's Government Pension Fund — oil revenue captured by the state, invested globally, distributed as a permanent public endowment — is the paradigmatic example.

Who captures profit
Split — private actors retain profit from production, but the state claims a large share through taxes and directs it toward public benefit.
Who owns commons / resources
State-managed, with strong regulatory frameworks. Norway proves that the same finite oil resource can produce entirely different community outcomes depending on ownership and distribution structure.

Key contribution for Commons Bonds

Norway is the single most powerful empirical counterexample. Same oil. Same finite resource. Different ownership and distribution structure = entirely different outcome. Norway proves the thesis: the barrel of oil is not inherently destructive; who captures the value determines everything.

Latouche, Hickel · contemporary

9. Degrowth

The fundamental problem is that capitalism requires perpetual growth on a finite planet. The solution is not to grow more sustainably but to deliberately contract economic activity in wealthy nations — reduce throughput, redistribute existing wealth, and redefine wellbeing in non-GDP terms.

Who captures profit
Deliberately reduces the role of profit as an organizing principle. Oriented toward sufficiency and equity rather than accumulation.
Who owns commons / resources
Collective/community oriented. Skeptical of both private and state ownership as drivers of extraction.

Core weakness

Politically difficult to implement; faces the problem of who bears the cost of contraction. Also has limited answers for communities in poverty who still need growth to meet basic needs.

Chesterton, Belloc · early 20th c. · Catholic social tradition

10. Distributism

The problem with capitalism is not that there is private property, but that property is owned by too few people. The solution is wide distribution of productive property — land, tools, small businesses — rather than concentration in either corporate hands or the state. "Three acres and a cow." Decentralized profit capture through many small owners, cooperatives, and localized institutions.

Who captures profit
Many small owners — decentralized profit capture.
Who owns commons / resources
Small-holder communities, cooperatives, and localized institutions.

Core weakness

Romanticizes pre-industrial small-property arrangements. Struggles to address industrial-scale and global-scale resource systems. Also doesn't resolve the depletion problem — small owners can deplete as surely as large ones.

Part III — The missing dimension: non-renewable depletion

Every framework above has some mechanism for distributing ongoing value — profit from labor, harvest from a fishery, rent from land. The oil barrel problem is different in kind. It is:

Irreversible

Once extracted, the resource is gone permanently from the earth, not just from the community.

Intergenerational

Future generations who had no vote in the extraction decision bear permanent loss.

Asymmetric in time

The profit is realized once, in the present. The cost — environmental harm, instability, depletion — accrues indefinitely into the future.

Mispriced by every market mechanism

Because markets discount the future and have no instrument for pricing permanent loss.

The closest existing theoretical tools:

ToolWhat it doesLimitation
Hartwick's Rule (1977)A community extracting non-renewable resources should invest the rents in renewable capital, so wealth is transformed rather than consumed. Norway's oil fund is the best real-world application.Requires political will to enforce; doesn't address the valuation of what is permanently lost.
Precautionary PrincipleWhen facing irreversible harm, the burden of proof falls on those who would extract, not those who would protect.Procedural, not pricing. Doesn't assign a dollar value to permanent foreclosure.
Intergenerational equity (Rawls applied across time)A just policy is one agreeable from behind a veil of ignorance — including ignorance of which generation you will be born into.Philosophical rather than operational. No pricing mechanism attached.
Depletion rent conceptThe community should not merely receive a royalty on the $100 barrel; it should receive a permanent endowment equal to the present value of all future value permanently foreclosed by the extraction.Under-theorized in the existing literature. This is the gap Commons Bonds is designed to fill.

Part IV — What a new framework needs

Gap 1 — A theory of value that includes permanence

Current pricing theory prices scarcity in the present but has no instrument for pricing permanent foreclosure of future possibility. The framework needs a concept — residual commons value, generational equity rent — that captures this.

Gap 2 — A theory of who owns what was never made

Private property theory can justify ownership of things that were built or produced. But oil, coal, and mineral deposits were not made by anyone. The community's claim is not based on labor (Lockean theory) or contract — it is based on inheritance and presence. This is philosophically under-theorized and is the most important gap.

Gap 3 — A theory of exit costs

Harvey identifies that capital extracts and departs, but no framework adequately prices the cost of departure — the harm left behind when capital leaves. The framework needs a concept like departure liability or residual harm bond — requiring capital to hold collateral equal to the projected long-term cost before extraction begins.

Gap 4 — A solidarity mechanism

The biggest structural support for extractive capitalism is the prevention of cross-community recognition. A new framework needs institutional design that actively produces solidarity across lines currently used to divide — not just redistribution, but the architecture of common identity. This is where the sociology paper connects to the economics framework.
