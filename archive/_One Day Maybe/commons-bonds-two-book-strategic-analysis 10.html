Commons Bonds — Two-Book Strategic Analysis

Planning document (not book content) · Session: April 19, 2026

Purpose

During the April 19 session, the question arose whether Chapter 8 (What Things Actually Cost) in its current scope — the full-economy simulation, the $10–15 trillion global subsidy, the distributional crisis, the universal floor insight — was trying to do too much in a single volume. The analysis below concludes that the full-economy simulation is not too much for the project overall but is too much for Book One. It should be the spine of a separate Book Two.

The analysis also addresses the serious strategic question of whether publishing Book Two as an individual author with minimal defensive infrastructure is advisable, and what protective structures would be necessary.

Book One vs. Book Two

Book One: Commons Bonds

Spine: Every extraction in human history has been conducted with an incomplete price. Making the missing columns visible, nameable, and calculable is the first step toward honest accounting.

Contribution type: Framework-naming. Intellectual work.

Audience: General intelligent readers, policy-curious professionals, academic economists (via the Technical Appendix).

Political posture: Non-partisan. Names mechanisms, not beneficiaries.

Legal exposure: Minimal. No specific entities are accused of specific acts.

Success criteria: The vocabulary enters discourse. Terms like cost severance, residual commons value, Full Generational Cost, Dynastic Labor Cost, Community Transition Reserve, and Political Capture Cost become usable by others.

Book Two: The Subsidy Economy (working title)

Alternative titles: What Everything Actually Costs, The Invisible Subsidy, The Honest Economy.

Spine: The modern global economy is a systemic transfer from the powerless to the powerful, priced at approximately $10–15 trillion per year. Honest accounting reveals both the subsidy and the institutional infrastructure required to replace it.

Contribution type: Applied economic analysis. Policy/advocacy work.

Audience: Policy practitioners, economic journalists, organized constituencies for reform.

Political posture: Confrontational. Names patterns of industrial beneficiaries and documents their capture of political process.

Legal exposure: Substantial. Requires publisher with legal team, careful sourcing, and likely institutional affiliation.

Success criteria: The pricing methodology gets applied to specific industries. Policy conversations shift from "what does this cost" to "what has been hidden."

Why Separation Serves Both Books

  1. Book One protects Book Two. Once the framework has years of intellectual circulation behind it, Book Two's opponents cannot dismiss the underlying framework as "advocacy." They have to engage with it, which is a structurally weaker position to attack from.
  2. Book Two protects Book One. Book One avoids being pre-labeled as advocacy by keeping its beneficiary-naming for a separate volume. A reader who would engage with the framework but bounce off advocacy can engage with Book One, and some of them will be ready for Book Two when it appears.
  3. Timing allows institutional changes. By the time Book Two is ready, you may have credentials (completed CRNA, PhD, research fellowship) or affiliations that provide defensive infrastructure that does not currently exist.
  4. Co-authorship becomes available. Book Two benefits enormously from a coauthor with economics credentials, policy research affiliation, or institutional standing. Book One does not require this. Writing Book One first creates the reputational base that makes coauthor recruitment for Book Two more feasible.

Specific Vulnerabilities to Consider

Stated plainly: You are a nursing student living on a sailboat with no platform, no institutional backing, no PR infrastructure, and no legal team. Publishing Book Two in your current position is a meaningful risk to career and livelihood. This is not paranoia. It is the standard trajectory for authors who challenge concentrated interests without defensive infrastructure.

Vulnerability 1: Single-Source Discrediting

If Book One and Book Two share an author, any successful attack on Book Two's credibility becomes ammunition against Book One. Attacks on Rachel Carson and Thomas Piketty follow this pattern — find one overstated claim and generalize the discredit.

Vulnerability 2: The Advocacy Label

Once Book Two is labeled advocacy, Book One gets retroactively reframed as "the framework book by the advocacy author." Readers who would have engaged with a neutral framework may disengage from what they perceive as partisan.

Vulnerability 3: The Lone Author Problem

A single author making claims that implicate trillion-dollar industries is structurally easier to dismiss than a coalition. Ostrom had peer review, replication, and scholarly extension providing institutional cover before her work became consequential. That cover does not yet exist for this framework.

Vulnerability 4: Nursing Career Exposure

Healthcare is one of the extractive systems the framework critiques. Hospital systems, healthcare networks, and professional certification bodies are implicated by some applications of the framework. If Book Two attracts organized opposition, it could affect nursing licensing, credentialing, or employment in ways Book One probably would not.

Vulnerability 5: Life Structure Precarity

Liveaboard housing, independent car dealing, and the nursing trajectory do not provide the financial cushion to absorb a significant reputational hit. A tenured professor writing the same book has protections not available to an independent author in this specific life structure.

Protective Structures If Book Two Proceeds

Structure 1: Extended Sequencing

Release Book One. Let it circulate for three to five years. Watch who engages, who extends, who criticizes. Reevaluate whether Book Two still needs to be written and whether you are still the right person to write it.

Structure 2: Coauthorship

Book Two is substantially stronger and safer with a coauthor bringing institutional credibility. Candidates: an economist at a university, a journalist at an established publication, a policy researcher at a think tank. Coauthorship distributes political risk, strengthens empirical claims, and makes the book harder to dismiss as lone-author advocacy.

Structure 3: Institutional Affiliation

By the time Book Two is ready, institutional affiliation provides substantial defensive cover. Options: university research fellowship, foundation affiliation, NGO research position, or founding/joining a commons economics institute. Pursuing a PhD after CRNA credentials would create this affiliation naturally.

Structure 4: Publisher Legal Protection

Book One's legal exposure is minimal. Book Two's is substantial — naming beneficiaries creates defamation risk even when claims are accurate. A publisher with a strong legal department is required. Self-publishing Book Two would be genuinely dangerous.

Structure 5: The Narrower Version

Spectrum of Book Two versions:

  • Safest: Extends the framework methodology without naming specific industries. Less powerful.
  • Middle: Names patterns and industries in aggregate (e.g., "the coal industry has suppressed honest carbon pricing"). Publishable without extraordinary legal exposure.
  • Most powerful and most dangerous: Names specific companies, specific executives, specific lobbying expenditures and years. Requires substantial institutional and legal backing.

Honest Recommendation

Write Book One now. As tightly as possible, with the framework clear and the Technical Appendix rigorous and the vocabulary ready for discourse. Release it. Let it do its work. Protect the nursing career.

Start Book Two as notes only. Track lobbying histories, subsidy figures, political capture cases, industry-specific applications of the framework. Organize material systematically but do not write the manuscript.

Reevaluate in three to five years. At that point assess:

  • Whether Book One's framework has actually entered discourse
  • Whether other authors have begun applying it (which may make your Book Two unnecessary)
  • Whether your career trajectory has created institutional affiliations that would make Book Two safer
  • Whether you still want to do the political work Book Two requires, or whether your intellectual contribution is best understood as the framework itself

If Book Two does proceed, do it differently. Coauthored. Institutionally affiliated. Published by a house with real legal protection. Or not at all, if the framework has been successfully applied by others.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Your intellectual contribution may be most powerful if you write the framework that lets others do the political work safely, rather than trying to do both the framework and the politics yourself. Sometimes the most valuable thing an individual author can do is hand the tools to people with more resources and let them do the dangerous work. That is not diminishment. It is strategic humility about what one person in your current position can accomplish without endangering themselves.

What This Means for Chapter 8

Chapter 8 (What Things Actually Cost) in Book One should be reframed. Rather than attempting the full-economy simulation, it should:

  1. Take one fully worked example through the eight-tier framework — coal from Chapter 2, continued — and show what happens when a single extraction is priced honestly.
  2. End with a brief aggregation: "This pattern, applied globally, implies a subsidy of approximately $10–15 trillion annually. What happens when we price it honestly is the subject of a separate work."
  3. Signal Book Two's existence without writing it.

This preserves the jaw-dropping insight (honest pricing reveals that extraction industries are subsidized by invisible transfers from workers and communities) without requiring Book One to deliver the full policy blueprint. The insight lands. The blueprint waits.
