# Book Three: Pricing the Final Frontier

*Shelf Document — material held for a possible third book dealing with

extraterrestrial extraction, pre-institutional pricing, and the

incommensurable-externality boundary. Long-horizon work. May not be

yours to write alone.*

By Chris Winn

---

## Working Titles (all provisional)

- *Pricing the Final Frontier*

- *The Colony Administrator*

- *The Miner on the Comet*

- *Before the Institutions*

- *What We Cannot Unextract*

## One-Line Spine

Honest resource pricing is being evaded in space before the institutions

to enforce it have been built, and the framework that works on Earth

produces the answers a thoughtful interplanetary civilization would

recognize as correct — if it is applied before the mining begins rather

than after.

## Relationship to Books One and Two

Book One: names the mechanism on Earth.

Book Two: prices the full Earth economy at civilizational scale.

Book Three: extends the framework off-world and confronts the boundary

cases (Europan life, exoplanet ecosystems, time-limited access windows)

where the framework meets its honest limits.

## Timing

Genuinely long-horizon. Write as notes throughout. Not a priority for

active drafting until Books One and Two have established the vocabulary

and the first applications. May ultimately be written by others; the

framework would survive that.

**This may not be your book to write alone.** Space policy, space law,

and the political economy of extraterrestrial extraction are substantial

fields with their own communities of expertise. The right configuration

for Book Three is likely a coauthored work with a space policy specialist

or space law scholar.

## Provisional Chapter Structure

### Part I — The Colony Administrator

**Ch 1. The Mars Thought Experiment.** The ten-thousand-person colony

with three water ice deposits and a fifty-year supply at current

consumption. Walk through the RCV framework: S ≈ 0 for biological

applications, U is life-or-death, Q is small and fixed, D is near zero.

The model produces the obviously correct answer: strategic reserves,

progressive pricing, aggressive investment in recycling.

The pivot: Earth is also a bounded system. The Mars case is not an

analogy. It is the limiting case whose answers we already accept,

showing us what answers we have been refusing on Earth because our

bounds feel distant.

**Ch 2. Cost Severance as a Function of Distance.** The deepest

insight from the Mars colony scenario: when the extractor and the

affected community are the same people, cost severance goes

automatically to zero — not because costs are low, but because there

is nowhere to sever them to. You cannot hide costs on Mars. The

atmosphere is recycled. The water is recycled. The CEO of the mining

operation and the family downwind are probably neighbors.

This is the structural reason cost severance exists on Earth: spatial,

temporal, and social distance enable ignorance, and ignorance enables

severance. The framework does not just price resources. It prices

distance — the distance between those who benefit and those who pay.

**Ch 3. The CSG Classification.** Civilizational Substitutability Gap

as the practical sorting of every off-world resource by extraction

priority:

- CSG ≈ 0: asteroid iron, lunar regolith, cometary volatiles. Extract

  freely, manage debris, proceed.

- Low CSG: most abundant materials with many substitutes.

- Moderate CSG: materials with emerging but incomplete substitutes.

- High CSG: rare earths, platinum group metals, He-3 under fusion

  viability.

- Conditionally high CSG: resources whose value depends on

  technological breakthroughs that may or may not occur.

The insurance cost argument: in-situ preservation is nearly free. You

leave the asteroid alone, you lose nothing. You mine it, you can never

un-mine it. Under asymmetric regret, preservation is the dominant

strategy for any high-CSG resource at almost any probability estimate.

### Part II — The Universality Test at Scale

**Ch 4. Eight Scenarios, Eight Answers.** The cross-scenario results

from the Mathematical Proof Development work, told as a walk through

the framework's different outputs:

- Asteroid iron (uninhabited, abundant): RCV near zero. Extract freely.

- Mars water (pre-colony, life-or-death): RCV extreme. Preserve

  absolutely.

- Mars rare earths (inhabited colony): CS goes to zero because

  extractor = affected community.

- Lunar He-3: conditionally high CSG; preserve (insurance is free).

- Lunar regolith (construction): RCV near zero; extract freely.

- Europa ice (possible subsurface biosphere): boundary case.

  Framework honestly says it cannot price and defaults to asymmetric

  regret.

- Comet flyby: RCV moderate but the framework inverts — extract

  aggressively because the access window closes permanently.

- Exoplanet rare earths (complex alien ecosystem): genuine tension.

  Framework resolves through large B (accountability bond) rather

  than prohibition.

The same formula, the same terms, the same logic produces eight

different answers across eight scenarios. That is what a useful model

does. A model that said "extract everything" or "preserve everything"

would be wrong. The framework says "it depends on the parameters" and

produces different recommendations for different parameters. That is

universality.

**Ch 5. The Model Is Not Anti-Extraction.** Sometimes honest pricing

says extract aggressively (comet flyby, asteroid iron, lunar regolith).

Sometimes it says preserve absolutely (Mars water, lunar He-3 pending

fusion). Sometimes it says "extract but pay full costs" (exoplanet

ecosystem with large B). The model is a navigation system, not a

prohibition. The framework's outputs depend on the parameters of the

situation, not on an ideological preference.

This matters for Book Three especially because space-law debates often

caricature environmentalist positions as reflexively anti-extraction.

The framework is not. The framework is pro-honest-pricing. The

framework's answers depend on what's being extracted, from where, for

whom, with what externalities, and over what time horizon.

### Part III — The Incommensurable Boundary

**Ch 6. Europa and the Value We Cannot Price.** Europa's subsurface

ocean is one of the most likely locations for extraterrestrial life in

the solar system. If its ocean harbors life — even microbial life — the

externality of destroying it is unlike anything in the terrestrial

framework.

The framework's E(R,t) term can technically accommodate this: you

assign a value to the destruction and integrate. But the value is

unestimable. It is not V_civ (too large, unfalsifiable). It is the

value of *knowing whether we are alone in the universe*, a question

that, once answered in the negative by destroying the evidence, can

never be asked again.

The framework's honest response: "The RCV of this extraction includes

an externality term I cannot price, but whose irreversibility and

potential magnitude are sufficient to recommend extreme caution.

Asymmetric regret applies: wait, study, decide later."

This is the correct response. A model that claimed to price everything,

including extraterrestrial life, would be dishonest. A model that says

"I can price most things; for what I cannot price, irreversibility

governs" is honest and useful. Book Three is the book that argues for

the honesty of the limit.

**Ch 7. The Exoplanet Case.** A terrestrial exoplanet with a complex

non-sentient ecosystem (equivalent to Earth's Carboniferous) has a rare

earth deposit the fifty-thousand-person human colony needs for

interstellar propulsion. The framework produces genuine tension:

foreclosure says extract (low S, high U — you need this for survival),

externality says caution (large E — you are disrupting a unique

biosphere).

The framework does not collapse this tension into a simple yes or no.

It resolves it through the accountability bond: extract if necessary,

but only with B set proportional to ecosystem damage, only with

minimized-footprint extraction methods, only after exhausting

alternatives. "Responsible" is not a vague aspiration. It is a

mathematical constraint on B.

### Part IV — Pre-Institutional Pricing

**Ch 8. Space Law as It Is.** The Outer Space Treaty of 1967. The

Moon Treaty (1979) and its near-universal non-ratification. The U.S.

Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act (2015). The UAE Space

Resources Law (2019). Luxembourg. Japan. The emerging legal regime for

asteroid mining and lunar resource extraction.

What the current regime captures: property rights for extractors.

What the current regime does not capture: cost severance,

intergenerational equity, incommensurable externalities, the CSG

classification, the asymmetric regret principle, the distance-based

nature of the mechanism.

The space-law window is open now. The frameworks being drafted will

govern extraction for centuries. This is not a future problem. It is

a present one.

**Ch 9. The Pre-Institutional Pricing Problem.** On Earth, honest

pricing is retrospective: we are trying to fix an accounting that has

been running since the industrial revolution. In space, honest pricing

is prospective: we can set the accounting before the extraction begins.

The pre-institutional window is rare in the history of resource

regimes. Fisheries were not priced until collapse. Forests were not

priced until deforestation. Fossil fuels were not priced until climate

damage began compounding. Space offers a chance to price first and

extract second. That chance closes as soon as the first major

extraction operation becomes economically viable, which is within a

decade or two.

**Ch 10. What Honest Pricing Would Look Like for the Asteroid

Miner.** Proposed framework:

- CSG classification required before extraction permits.

- Accountability bonds proportional to externality profile.

- Intergenerational trust funds for high-CSG extractions.

- International coordination through a body analogous to the ISA

  (International Seabed Authority) but with CSG-informed mandates.

- The asymmetric regret principle formalized as a legal standard.

- Specific treatment of possible life-bearing bodies (Europa, Enceladus,

  potentially Titan) with extractive activity suspended pending

  scientific characterization.

### Part V — The Long Arc

**Ch 11. The Interstellar Case.** If humanity reaches other star

systems, the framework continues to apply. The parameters change —

S(t) is supply-chain-dependent and supply chains may not exist; U is

survival-dependent; E includes the ecosystems of worlds we will

encounter; D collapses because there is no earthly reference for

"far future" at interstellar distances — but the structure holds.

The framework is not tied to Earth or to the solar system. It is tied

to the mathematical structure of irreversible extraction in bounded

systems. Any civilization, anywhere, extracting finite resources from

shared or contested territory, will face versions of the same problem.

The book's long arc is the claim that honest resource pricing is a

universal requirement of sustainable civilization, not a human or

terrestrial one.

**Ch 12. The Final Externality.** The one the framework still cannot

price: the civilizational value of having remained honest. The answer

to the question "was this civilization a fair steward of its resources,

its commons, its future?" The thing we will want to be able to say

yes to.

This chapter closes the trilogy. Book One made the invisible visible.

Book Two priced the Earth economy honestly. Book Three extends the

framework to the species' next thousand years and confronts the one

value the framework cannot compute: its own success. That question is

answered not mathematically but historically. We find out whether the

framework worked by finding out whether honest pricing became

possible, whether the universal floor was built, whether the

extraction communities were compensated, whether the subsurface

biospheres were preserved, whether the colony administrators could

look their grandchildren in the eye.

The framework does not promise the answer. It promises a shared

accounting. What we do with the accounting is always, always, the

question that matters.

## Boundary Notes for When This Book Gets Written

**Expertise gap.** You are not a space lawyer. You are not a planetary

scientist. You are not an astrobiologist. Writing Book Three requires

either substantial original research in each of these fields (years of

work) or collaboration with people who already have the expertise.

Collaboration is the right answer.

**Political stakes.** Space-resource law is being drafted now, in

real time, by companies and governments with substantial financial

incentives. Critiques of that regime will encounter the same organized

opposition that Book Two would encounter. Collaborative authorship

and institutional affiliation are not optional.

**The alternative.** If the framework from Book One circulates

successfully, and if Book Two is written (by you or others), Book

Three may simply not need to be yours. Space policy researchers may

adopt the vocabulary and build the applications. That outcome would

be a success, not a loss. The goal is that honest pricing reach the

institutions that govern extraction. The author identity of the

applying books matters far less than the framework's actual adoption.

## A Closing Note to Your Future Self

The reason this book lives on a shelf is not that the material is

weak. It is stronger than Book Two's material in some ways — cleaner

test cases, less political resistance, a genuine intellectual frontier.

The reason is that Book Three's natural author is not necessarily

the person who wrote Book One. The framework matters more than the

trilogy. If you never write Book Three, and someone else applies the

framework to space law usefully, the work has succeeded. If you

write Book Three yourself, with the right collaborators, years from

now, the work will have succeeded differently.

Either outcome is a good outcome. The shelf is where the material

waits to find out which one happens.
