## §1. The story

It is worth telling about hiring people to go work on networking in Afghanistan for the US military — designing a mesh system in-country.

People were getting paid through the company, and the company was getting paid through the federal contract. For each person, the contract structure was: about six months of the contract you would spend getting ready to go — training, security-team coordination, learning what signals from the security team meant, learning to follow security protocols without questioning. Then you would be in Afghanistan in a war zone for about a year and a half.

The entire time you were in-country, you were earning money that was not federally taxed because it was a war zone.

It was, in its own particular way, kind of amazing. Chris was offering jobs to people he had been working with for years, and actually being able to pay them what they were worth. That part was incredibly satisfying. The terrifying part was real too — you were in a war zone. But you were making a quarter of a million dollars a year, federally untaxed.

So you go there. You maybe have a wife and a newborn at home, or one expected. And when you come back, you suddenly have enough money to buy a house outright. Maybe not the biggest house in the world. A house. In cash. Or, if you wanted a larger house, a substantial down payment was sitting in your account the day you got home.

It was really nice being able to pay people what they were actually worth.

Where the [Mirage callback](#) (Chris-as-CEO calling Neeraj for cert use) is the small Pattern 2 demonstration, the Afghanistan war-zone hiring is the same Pattern 2 mechanism operating at life-changing economic scale: a single year and a half of in-country work yielding a paid-off house.

The structural mechanism that makes the war-zone tax exemption work is itself instructive: the federal government recognizes the EXTRAORDINARY cost-bearing of being in a war zone (mortality risk; family-separation cost; psychological cost) and creates a tax architecture that partially internalizes that cost-bearing back to the worker rather than fully severing it. This is one of the rare cases where the federal-contracting labor-economics mechanism produces a *non-severance* outcome at the worker scale — because the structural cost (war-zone risk) is too large for the system to plausibly externalize.
