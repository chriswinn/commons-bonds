# Book Two: The Subsidy Economy

*Shelf Document — material extracted from Chapter 8 scaffold and associated

conversations; held for future development once Book One is published and

the framework has had time to circulate.*

By Chris Winn

## Working Title

*The Subsidy Economy* (primary)

Alternatives: *What Everything Actually Costs* / *The Invisible Subsidy*

## One-Line Spine

The modern global economy is a systemic transfer from the powerless to the

powerful, priced at $10–15 trillion per year, and honest accounting reveals

both the subsidy and the infrastructure required to replace it.

## Relationship to Book One

Book One (*Commons Bonds*) names the mechanism and introduces the

vocabulary. Book Two takes the framework as given and applies it at

civilizational scale. Book One is framework work. Book Two is advocacy work.

The books share an author and a vocabulary but not a job.

## Timing

Not before Book One has been published for 3–5 years. Write as notes now;

do not draft as manuscript until the framework has entered discourse and

the political infrastructure for publication (coauthor, institutional

affiliation, legal protection) is in place.

## Provisional Chapter Structure

### Part I — The Subsidy Made Visible

**Ch 1. The $10–15 Trillion Subsidy.** Open with the aggregate claim and

the arithmetic that produces it. Per-resource mispricing ratios from Book

One's cases scaled to global extraction.

**Ch 2. Who Pays the Difference.** The geography and demography of where

severed costs land: extraction communities, future generations, ecological

systems. The flow is always from concentrated-loss bearers to diffuse-gain

capturers.

**Ch 3. Who Captures the Gain.** Sectoral breakdown of which industries

derive what share of their profitability from unpriced externalities.

Fossil fuels, industrial agriculture, primary mining, industrial fisheries,

fossil aquifer agriculture, the pharmaceutical industry's long-tail

externalities, financial capture of pension-system severance.

### Part II — The Full-Economy Simulation

**Ch 4. Fossil Fuels Repriced.**

- Coal: $500–700/ton; uncompetitive with renewables; mining ceases for

  energy applications within years; metallurgical coal persists at high

  prices, accelerating EAF and hydrogen steel

- Oil: $250–400/barrel; gasoline triples to quadruples; EVs become

  obviously economic; air travel doubles (back to 1960s inflation-adjusted

  levels); petrochemicals restructure toward bio-based alternatives

- Natural gas: $8–15/MMBtu; heat pump transition accelerates; "bridge

  fuel" framing collapses into transitional framing

**Ch 5. Minerals and Metals Repriced.**

- Rare earths: 2–3x current prices (~$150–250/kg neodymium oxide);

  modest consumer-electronics impact (~$1–2 per smartphone → $2–4);

  strategic reserves for conditionally high-CSG deposits; recycling

  research accelerates

- Lithium: 2–3x; water-table depletion externalities in South American

  salt flats; moderate CSG; alternative battery chemistry research

  accelerates; circular lithium economy becomes viable

- Iron ore: 20–40% increase; modest; CSG near zero

- Copper: 50–100% increase; recycling economically dominant; aluminum

  substitution accelerates

**Ch 6. Water, Soil, Forest, Fish.**

- Fossil aquifer drawdown (Ogallala, North China Plain, Arabian

  Peninsula): dramatic cost increase; Great Plains industrial agriculture

  restructures; drought-resistant crops dominate

- Old-growth timber: effectively priced out of market; engineered wood

  and plantation timber dominate

- Overfishing: economically impossible because externality pricing

  exceeds market value of catch; aquaculture dominates; fish prices rise

  modestly

- Soil depletion as slow-motion non-renewable extraction: industrial

  agriculture loses cost advantage over regenerative; food prices rise

  short-term, stabilize long-term; regenerative farming becomes

  cost-competitive or cheaper

### Part III — The Distributional Crisis

**Ch 7. The Cost-of-Living Arithmetic.** Representative household:

- Energy (7% of budget × 3x) = up 14 points

- Food (12% × 1.4x) = up 4.8 points

- Transport (16% × 2.5x) = up 24 points

- Housing (30% × 1.2x) = up 6 points

- Total: ~49 point increase in cost of living, or 40–50% for average

  developed-world household.

**Ch 8. Why the Bottom 40% Cannot Absorb This.** Current essential

spending for bottom 40%: 80–95% of income. After 40–50% increase in

essentials: 112–142% of income. Mathematically impossible without

additional income or subsidized essentials. The transition itself, if

unmanaged, becomes a new form of cost severance.

**Ch 9. Where the Money Goes.** Three options for the pricing surplus:

- **Option A:** To the extractors (higher profits). Outcome: massive

  wealth concentration, devastating cost-of-living increases, no

  improvement in accountability. The worst and most likely default

  outcome under unregulated pricing.

- **Option B:** To governments (through taxation/fees/bonds).

  Trillions per year globally. Question becomes: what do governments

  do with it?

- **Option C:** Directly to affected communities and future-generation

  funds (through Norway-style sovereign funds, community benefit

  agreements, spatial cost severance compensation, intergenerational

  trusts).

**Options B and C are the only paths that avoid the distributional

catastrophe.**

### Part IV — The Structural Discovery

**Ch 10. The Universal Floor Is Arithmetic, Not Ideology.** The math

forecloses the alternative. If essentials rise by 40% and the bottom 40%

already spends 65–95% of income on essentials, you need to either give

them more money or provide the essentials outside the market. There is

no third option.

The universal floor is not a moral preference. It is a structural

requirement of honest pricing. Without it, honest pricing creates a new

form of cost severance: the transition extracts from the poor.

**Ch 11. Cash Transfers vs. Universal Provision.** Two mechanisms for

recycling the pricing surplus. Alaska's Permanent Fund Dividend as the

cash-transfer example (oil revenue → citizen payments). Nordic models

as the universal-provision example (healthcare, education, housing

support). The model does not dictate which is correct. The math dictates

that the surplus must flow back in some form.

**Ch 12. The "Completed Capitalism" Insight.** Full externality pricing

is not socialism. It is the completion of the market mechanism Adam Smith

described. Smith argued explicitly against producers externalizing costs.

The current system is a market where certain costs are hidden. Full RCV

pricing corrects the market's largest systematic error. The result is

*more* efficient allocation, not less.

### Part V — The New Equilibrium

**Ch 13. Medium-Term Adjustment (Years 5–15).**

- Massive capital flight into renewables

- Wholesale electricity prices begin falling as renewable capacity

  exceeds fossil capacity

- Food prices stabilize at a new level, then decline as regenerative

  practices mature

- Recycling becomes economically dominant; urban mining emerges

- Employment disruption in extraction communities (analog to McDowell

  County if unmanaged)

- Demand destruction and efficiency gains (analog to post-1970s

  energy intensity decline)

**Ch 14. Long-Term Equilibrium (Years 15–50).**

- Energy cheaper than today (renewables at $20–40/MWh wholesale with

  mature storage; below current fossil electricity)

- Materials more expensive but more efficiently used; circular flows

  reduce virgin throughput

- Food moderately more expensive but more stable and less volatile

- Essential services publicly funded from the pricing surplus

- Extraction communities compensated via spatial cost severance pricing

  (McDowell-equivalent communities receive billions over the life of

  extraction)

- Strategic reserves exist for high-CSG resources

- Economy still capitalist (private ownership, market prices, profit

  motive, competition) but with a robust public floor funded by

  externality pricing rather than income taxation

- Structurally similar to existing Nordic models; government spending

  approaches 45–55% of GDP

### Part VI — The Politics of the Subsidy

**Ch 15. The Political Economy of Resistance.** The subsidy benefits

concentrated, organized interests. The costs are borne by diffuse,

unorganized populations. This is the organizational asymmetry that has

kept the accounting invisible. Naming specific beneficiaries and

specific lobbying histories. **(Requires coauthor and legal protection.)**

**Ch 16. Implementation Pathway.** Existing entering wedges:

- Carbon pricing in dozens of jurisdictions

- Sovereign wealth funds

- Strategic petroleum reserves

- Mining reclamation bonds (currently failing — document the failure)

- Environmental impact assessments

The path forward is strengthening and connecting existing mechanisms

rather than building new ones from scratch.

**Ch 17. International Coordination.** Cost severance operates across

borders. Rare earths from Baotou for iPhones in California. Oil from the

Niger Delta for European refineries. International trade as a cost

severance delivery system. The WTO/trade framework. Carbon border

adjustments. The developing-nation differentiated-timeline requirement.

### Part VII — What Honest Accounting Would Build

**Ch 18. The Community Transition Reserve.** Named policy instrument.

Ring-fenced, spatially targeted, intergenerationally binding fund

financed by extraction-sourced pricing surplus. The institutional

answer to the McDowell question: what should have existed in 1960 that

would have prevented the collapse.

**Ch 19. The Pricing Surplus Dividend.** Named policy instrument for

the cash-transfer path. Alaska PFD scaled globally and sectorally.

**Ch 20. The Honest Economy.** Closing chapter. What the reader's life

looks like in the new equilibrium. The argument that honest accounting

produces a better economy, not a contracted one. The completion of

Adam Smith's original vision.

## Counterarguments to Address at Book Scale

(Already sketched in Book One Chapter 8 and elsewhere, but require full

chapter-length treatment in Book Two.)

1. "This is degrowth in disguise." GDP vs. welfare distinction;

   composition vs. total; leaded gasoline precedent.

2. "This would prevent developing-world industrialization." Differentiated

   timelines; technology transfer; historical severance repayment;

   leapfrog precedent.

3. "You can't fix a trillion-dollar system by naming it." Named

   frameworks changing political possibility: Harvey, Ostrom, Raworth,

   Desmond. The slow but real effect of vocabulary on policy.

4. "The bottom 40% will vote against the transition before it produces

   benefits." Phased implementation; carbon dividend front-loading;

   transition support funding as first call on pricing surplus.

5. "International coordination is impossible." WTO/trade mechanisms;

   carbon border adjustments already emerging in EU; Norway model as

   unilateral existence proof.

## Structural Notes for When This Book Gets Written

**Authorship.** Coauthor strongly recommended. An economist with

institutional backing distributes the political risk, strengthens the

empirical claims with additional expertise, and makes the book harder to

dismiss as advocacy by a lone nurse. The Raworth/Piketty pattern:

coalitions of named authors before the solo book.

**Legal.** Publisher with strong legal department is non-negotiable.

Self-publishing this book would be dangerous.

**Tonal register.** Book One can be politically neutral because it names

a mechanism. Book Two cannot be fully neutral because it names

beneficiaries. The tonal target is the middle path: name the patterns

and reference specific industries in aggregate rather than specific

companies in the particular. "The coal industry has suppressed honest

carbon pricing" rather than "[Named Company] specifically funded the

following suppression efforts."

**Length target.** 80,000–95,000 words. Longer than Book One because

the simulation work requires space that the framework work did not.

**Relationship to the Technical Appendix.** Book One's appendix covers

the RCV framework. Book Two's appendix would cover the aggregation

methodology — how per-resource RCV estimates roll up to sector and

national scale, with sensitivity analysis. This is new mathematical

work, not a reprint of Book One's appendix.
