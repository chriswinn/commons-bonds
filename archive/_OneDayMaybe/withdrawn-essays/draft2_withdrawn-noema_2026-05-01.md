Commons Bonds
By Chris Winn

[§I — wife's-illness opening passage — EXCISED 2026-05-08 per Candidate #4 deletion discipline. See archive header above for context.]

This essay is about that structure — or rather, the absence of it. It is about what happens when value is extracted from people, communities, and resources, and the costs of that extraction are cut free and left for someone else to carry. It is about a missing instrument in our economic thinking that allows this to happen at every scale, from a single family to an entire region to the planet itself. And it is about what it might look like to begin building something different.

II. The Mechanism Named
Let me start with a mine.
In the coalfields of central Appalachia, the mechanism I am describing has been operating for more than a century, and its arithmetic is now a matter of public record. When Congress passed the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act in 1977, it required coal companies to post bonds — financial collateral guaranteeing that when extraction was finished, the land would be restored. In theory, this was accountability made manifest: you take the coal, you pay for the cleanup, the community is made whole. The principle is elegant. The practice has been catastrophic.
Across Appalachia, bonds fall $3.7 to $6 billion short of addressing actual reclamation costs — a bill created by coal companies that leaves taxpayers on the hook. Appalachian Voices The reasons are a catalog of evasion techniques. Self-bonding allows a mine operator to get a permit essentially by claiming they can cover reclamation costs, without actually putting money into a secured account. Appalachian Voices Bond amounts are calculated at flat per-acre rates unrelated to actual cleanup costs. In West Virginia, mine-specific bonds cover only ten percent of projected reclamation costs, and the state pool bond covers less than forty percent of liability projected over the next twenty years. Appalachian VoicesAnd as coal companies declare bankruptcy with increasing regularity — taking their money and running from their responsibilities, leaving taxpayers to cover the costs Theallianceforappalachia — the system designed to prevent exactly this outcome is collapsing under the weight of the very decline it failed to prepare for.
Meanwhile, Congress has proposed raiding $500 million from abandoned mine land cleanup funds Appalachian Voiceseven as communities continue living with the toxic legacy. The community that provided the coal, absorbed the blasting, drank the water downstream of the mountaintop removal site, and buried the miners who died of black lung is now also expected to fund the cleanup. Coal companies have taken mountains, poisoned waterways, and devastated community health — from rare cancers to widespread black lung disease. Theallianceforappalachia
I want to name what is happening here with precision, because precision is what has been missing.
There is a mechanism — older than capitalism, present in feudalism and colonialism and Soviet industrial planning and every other system that has ever concentrated power — in which value is extracted from a person, a community, or a resource, and the costs generated by that extraction are deliberately severed from the entity that profited and transferred to those least equipped to bear them. I call this mechanism cost severance. It is not a market failure. It is not an externality in the economist's polite sense — a minor friction in an otherwise efficient system. It is a structural feature of how extraction operates when no countervailing force prevents it.
The word severance is deliberate. It carries the violence of cutting — a deliberate act that separates things that were once connected. Profit and the cost of producing it were, in any honest accounting, joined. Cost severance is the act that cuts them apart. And it carries an ironic echo: severance is also what you receive when you are let go. The community gets its severance — the tailings ponds, the contaminated groundwater, the healthcare costs, the hollowed-out economy — and the entity that created those costs moves on, structurally unburdened.
Cost severance is not capitalism's invention. Capitalism is merely its most efficient current vehicle. Feudal lords extracted agricultural surplus while severing the costs of famine and soil depletion to the peasantry. Colonial empires extracted raw materials while severing the costs of ecological destruction and social disruption to colonized populations. Soviet planners extracted industrial output from the Aral Sea basin while severing the costs of its desiccation to the communities that depended on it. The system changes. The mechanism does not.
What makes naming this mechanism different from merely describing injustice is that it identifies a specific operation — the moment the cost is cut free from the profit. It is not enough to say that Appalachian communities were exploited. They were exploited through a specific act: value was captured upward, and the costs — environmental, medical, economic, generational — were severed and transferred downward. Naming the operation matters because it gives people a tool they can apply to situations no one has yet described for them. Once you see cost severance in a coal mine, you see it everywhere.
You see it in the pension-to-401(k) shift, where the employer's obligation to fund retirement was severed and transferred wholesale to the individual worker, who now absorbs all the market risk that corporations once bore. You see it in the 2008 financial crisis, where banks designed toxic mortgage instruments, collected fees, distributed the risk to pension funds and retirement accounts, and when the instruments collapsed, received $700 billion in public rescue while ten million American families lost their homes — risk transferred twice on the same money. You see it when a state bans abortion while funding nothing that follows — no prenatal care infrastructure, no childcare support, no maternal mental health services. The mandate is issued. The cost is severed. The family absorbs it.
And it operates inside the body itself. Journalist Katy Butler documented how Medicare reimbursed a cardiologist $54 for a long office visit to discuss treatment alternatives for her elderly, stroke-damaged father — the conversation that might have spared him years of diminished existence. The pacemaker implant procedure, by contrast, generated $461 for the surgeon, roughly $12,000 for the hospital, and nearly $6,000 for the device manufacturer. The financial incentive pointed toward the procedure. The liability architecture reinforced it: a physician who performs a procedure is protected; one who declines it faces malpractice exposure. Both incentives pointed the same direction. Butler's father spent more than five years in profound decline. His eighty-year-old wife worked eighty-hour weeks as his unpaid caregiver — labor for which no compensation existed and no training was provided. The conversation that might have prevented all of it was worth $54 to the system. The procedure that foreclosed that conversation was worth $12,000.
The extraction does not stop at the grave. It is organized around reaching into it.

III. The $100 Barrel
There is a barrel of oil underground. Someone puts a price on it. Call it one hundred dollars. That price reflects what a buyer will pay today — a function of supply, demand, refining costs, transport logistics, and speculative futures contracts. It is a number generated by the present, for the present. It does not account for what the earth permanently loses.
Consider what happens when that barrel is extracted. The community above it absorbs environmental disruption, potential groundwater contamination, air quality degradation. Some of these costs are partially captured by regulation — imperfectly, as we have seen. But there is a deeper cost that no existing framework addresses: the barrel is gone. Not consumed the way a forest is consumed, where sustainable harvest is theoretically possible. Not depleted the way a fishery is depleted, where population recovery can occur if pressure is removed. The barrel is liquidated. Permanently. Every future generation, from now until the end of human civilization, will never have access to that barrel or the energy it contained. The price captured the present. The cost extends to permanence.
I call this residual commons value — the worth of what was permanently foreclosed. Not the market price of the barrel, but the value of having that barrel available to every future generation that will now never have it.
No existing economic framework prices this. Classical economics treats resources as inputs with market-determined prices and assumes substitutability — that technology will provide alternatives when a resource is exhausted. This may prove true, but it is an assumption, not a guarantee, and no mechanism exists to compensate future generations if the assumption fails. Marxist economics addresses who captures the surplus value of labor but not the permanent foreclosure of the resource itself. Elinor Ostrom's Nobel-winning work on commons governance is brilliant for renewable resources — fisheries, forests, groundwater — where sustainable harvest rates can be calculated and enforced. But a barrel of oil cannot be sustainably harvested. It can only be extracted once. The community is not managing a commons. It is liquidating one.
Herman Daly's ecological economics comes closest. Daly insists — correctly — that the economy is a subsystem of the biosphere, not the reverse, and that all economic activity must operate within the regenerative capacity of natural systems. His steady-state framework challenges the foundational assumption of perpetual growth on a finite planet. But even Daly's framework, powerful as it is, does not produce a price for permanent foreclosure. It tells us that we are overshooting ecological limits. It does not tell us what the permanent loss of a specific non-renewable resource is worth to every generation that will never have it.
Hartwick's Rule comes closer still: a community extracting non-renewable resources should invest the rents in reproducible capital, so that the aggregate wealth is transformed rather than consumed. Norway's sovereign wealth fund is the most successful application of this principle. But Hartwick prices the revenue as something to be preserved. He does not price the resource itself as something whose permanent absence constitutes a loss beyond what any reinvestment can replace. A barrel of oil converted into financial assets is not the same as a barrel of oil available for future use. The revenue is preserved. The resource is still gone.
And here is where the problem becomes not merely economic but philosophical, because it collides with a scandal at the heart of how economics values the future. Standard economic practice discounts future costs and benefits — a dollar of damage a century from now is worth pennies today. The Stern Review on climate change used a discount rate of 1.4 percent, under which a $100 benefit occurring a century from now is worth $24.90 today. William Nordhaus advocated 4.3 percent, under which the same $100 benefit is worth only $1.48. Core-econ The difference between those two numbers produces radically different policy prescriptions — Nordhaus proposed a carbon price of $35 per ton while Stern recommended $360. Core-econ
The philosophical core of this dispute is not mathematical. It is moral. Stern's approach reflects the belief that we should not undervalue future generations' wellbeing. Nordhaus takes a market-based view, arguing discount rates should reflect the rate of return observed in the marketplace. Medium Critics of Nordhaus pointed out that our own impatience — how much we value present consumption over our own future consumption — is not a reason to discount the needs and aspirations of other people in future generations. Core-econ
Apply this to residual commons value and the implications are stark. At Nordhaus's discount rate, the interests of a generation born two hundred years from now are worth essentially nothing. Their permanent loss of access to a resource extracted today does not register in the calculation. They are, for the purposes of our economic models, invisible. Standard discounting does not merely undervalue future generations. At long enough time horizons, it erases them.
John Rawls proposed that a just policy is one that could be agreed to from behind a veil of ignorance — without knowing which position you will occupy in the resulting society. Apply that veil across time: a policy decided without knowing which generation you will be born into cannot endorse a system that makes your interests worth less simply because you arrive later. If residual commons value were priced under a Rawlsian framework — where the interests of future generations are weighted equally with those of the present — the price of every barrel of oil, every ton of coal, every ounce of extracted mineral would be vastly higher than any market currently reflects. The gap between the market price and the Rawlsian price is residual commons value. It is the difference between what we pay and what we owe.
Solving this formally — producing a number that an economist could defend and a policymaker could implement — is genuinely difficult work. It involves infinite time horizons, contested discount rates, and unanswerable questions about technological substitutability. It is the work of decades, not paragraphs. But naming the gap is the necessary first step. You cannot solve a problem that has no name. And until now, this problem has had no name.

IV. The Existence Proof
If cost severance were an inevitable law of nature — if extraction always and everywhere required that communities absorb the costs while distant shareholders captured the value — then the framework I have described would be nothing more than a diagnosis of the human condition. Interesting, perhaps, but useless.
But cost severance is not gravity. It is a choice. And we know this because one country made a different one.
When Norway discovered oil in the North Sea, it faced the same structural pressures that have governed resource extraction everywhere else: industry demanded rapid drilling, politicians saw revenues they could spend, and the logic of immediate profit argued for extraction at maximum speed. Norway resisted every one of those pressures. It imposed heavy taxes on the energy sector and placed strong regulatory controls on the industry. Fortune It established the Government Pension Fund Global and instituted a rule that has held across every change of government for three decades: no more than three percent of the fund's value can be diverted annually to the national budget, preserving the wealth for future generations. Fortune It told oil companies they could not extract immediately. It told taxpayers they would not receive the money right away. It told politicians they could not campaign on spending it.
The results are extraordinary. The fund holds assets of $2.2 trillion — equivalent to $385,000 for every Norwegian man, woman, and child — and finances roughly twenty-five percent of the country's fiscal budget. CommBank In 2025 alone, the fund generated a profit of $247 billion. CNBC And critically for the argument I am making: the fund now generates far more income for Norway's population than oil and gas production itself. Fortune They converted a depleting resource into a permanent income stream. They captured the value for their own citizens rather than severing it to distant shareholders.
Now consider what happened when countries did not make Norway's choice. Nigeria sits atop the same kind of resource wealth. The Niger Delta's communities have absorbed decades of ecological destruction — oil spills, gas flaring, water contamination, displacement — while the revenue flowed to multinational corporations and a narrow political elite. The contrast is not geological. Both countries had oil. The difference is entirely a matter of whether the political economy captured the resource value for the community or allowed its extraction by external capital. What appears as natural economic outcome in the American and Nigerian cases is, as the Norwegian case proves, a political choice embedded in institutional arrangements.
But I must be honest about Norway's limitations, because they matter for understanding why the concept of residual commons value is necessary even when cost severance is addressed.
Norway still extracted the oil. The resource is still permanently gone. The fund converts the financial proceeds into something durable, but it does not address the permanent depletion itself. There are growing concerns that the economy has become so dependent on fund income that domestic industries are becoming less innovative and dynamic. FortuneAnd most importantly: Norway had strong democratic institutions, low corruption, and high social trust before the oil was discovered. The foundations of its welfare state — universal healthcare, education, pension systems — were already in place. The preconditions that made Norway's choice possible are precisely the preconditions that Appalachian coal communities have never had.
This is why naming the mechanism matters. Cost severance does not just transfer costs. It degrades the institutional capacity to resist the transfer. Coal companies fund political campaigns. Extractive industries capture regulatory agencies. Communities dependent on a single employer cannot afford to demand accountability from that employer. The mechanism is self-reinforcing. And this is why the most common response to the framework I am proposing — "why don't communities just do what Norway did?" — misses the structural point entirely. Norway could make a different choice because its institutions had not already been hollowed out by the mechanism. For communities where extraction has been operating for generations, the mechanism has already consumed the institutional capacity that would be required to resist it.

V. Accountability and Its Failures
If you want to see cost severance reassert itself even when accountability is theoretically enforced, look at the Gulf of Mexico.
The Deepwater Horizon disaster resulted in the largest environmental damage settlement in United States history. BP's total costs exceeded $65 billion. That number sounds like accountability. But look closer. In a 2012 settlement for health impacts, individual payouts to ill workers and coastal residents amounted to no more than $1,300 each for nearly eighty percent of claimants. Of roughly 4,800 lawsuits seeking health compensation, all but a handful were dismissed. Fifteen years later, effects of the spill are still being felt in Gulf communities, and a flagship $3 billion restoration project has been paused. Congress did not act on the recommendations of the bipartisan commission appointed to investigate the disaster. No new drilling safety legislation resulted.
The pattern is consistent: even when accountability is attempted, the corporation's legal apparatus outlasts the community's capacity to pursue claims. The tobacco Master Settlement Agreement extracted $206 billion from cigarette companies over twenty-five years, but the money was often diverted by states to general budgets rather than the health programs it was nominally intended to fund. The cost was severed from the companies, partially reattached through litigation, then severed again by the states from the communities that bore it. Superfund was supposed to make polluters pay for cleaning up contaminated industrial sites; in practice, when the responsible company dissolves through bankruptcy, taxpayers fund the cleanup. The "orphan sites" problem is cost severance through corporate dissolution: a legal mechanism by which an entity ceases to exist precisely to escape the obligations it created.
These are not failures of enforcement. They are the predictable result of a system that has no structural mechanism for preventing cost severance. Each of these programs was designed by, negotiated with, or subject to capture by the entities creating the costs. Mining reclamation bonds are set at rates the industry will accept, not rates that reflect actual cleanup costs. Settlement amounts are negotiated against the threat of protracted litigation that communities cannot afford. The accountability infrastructure is, in each case, structurally inadequate by design.
What would real accountability look like? Let me be specific, because the argument is not persuasive if it remains at the level of aspiration.
It would require, first, that reclamation and restoration bonds be set by independent assessment at full projected cost before extraction begins, with mandatory annual adjustment as conditions change — not flat per-acre rates that bear no relationship to actual cleanup expenses. West Virginia's pool bonds cover ten percent of projected costs. That is not a bonding system. It is a permission slip.
It would require, second, that environmental and health obligations survive corporate dissolution. When a coal company declares bankruptcy, its reclamation obligations should transfer automatically to an independently funded trust seeded by extraction fees collected during the company's operating years — not evaporate into the "orphan site" category where they become the public's problem. The legal fiction that a corporate entity can create permanent damage and then cease to exist, taking its obligations with it, is the single most effective tool of cost severance in the modern economy.
It would require, third, that communities bearing severed costs have funded legal standing to pursue claims — not pro bono representation that runs out when the firm's budget tightens, but a permanent litigation fund financed by a small surcharge on extraction permits, managed independently of both industry and government. The asymmetry that allows a corporation to litigate for decades while a community's legal capacity exhausts in months is not an accident. It is cost severance operating through the judicial system.
None of these proposals is utopian. Each extends principles that already exist in fragmentary form. Norway's sovereign wealth fund is pre-funded intergenerational accountability. Reclamation bonds are prepaid cost internalization — they are simply set too low and enforced too weakly. The concept is not radical. The gap between principle and implementation exists because the entities with the power to close it are the same entities that benefit from leaving it open.

VI. The Human Scale
I want to return, briefly, to where I began. Not to the theory, but to the cost.
I started my working life at an IHOP. No degree. No connections. No family money. I taught myself to code because the alternative was to stay where I was, and I had seen enough of where I was to know I wanted something different. I turned out to be good at sales — genuinely good, in the way that surprises you when you discover it — and over the years I worked my way from developer jobs to technical sales positions at companies whose names signal that you have arrived. I co-founded a healthcare IT company that built patient registries for Johns Hopkins. I consulted for NASA, which felt like a homecoming — my grandfather had been a model maker in the wind tunnels at Langley Research Center, building the physical shapes that let engineers test how air moves over surfaces. I wrote the front end of the NIH Children's Center website, and the question that came with that work — how do you explain to a child with cancer that the research happening around them might mean future children will not have to be sick the way they are? — has never left me.
By the measures the American economy uses to define success, I succeeded. Four hundred thousand dollars a year. A house in a historic district. Healthcare that could handle a catastrophe. I had done what the story says you are supposed to do: started with nothing, worked relentlessly, and arrived at something. The bootstrap narrative made flesh.
And the system ground me through anyway.
The marriage did not survive. There were reasons beyond the ones I have described here — there always are — but the structural reality was inescapable: the same career that gave us the means to survive a medical crisis required me to be absent for the aftermath. My wife, who came from a family that never went to hospitals, that viewed therapy as weakness, who had no framework for processing the fact that she had died and been brought back — she needed me there. Not sometimes. There. And the system that captured my value had no mechanism, and no obligation, to account for what my absence would cost. The company that employed me did not end my marriage. But the architecture of high-stakes professional work — the unspoken assumption that the person producing value will simply absorb the personal costs of producing it — made the ending more likely. The cost was never on anyone's ledger. It was just mine.
After the marriage ended, I noticed something I had been too busy to see. My parents were not standing as tall as they used to. They were not moving as fast. And I realized, with the particular clarity that comes from loss, that if I kept accepting jobs in whatever city had a company hiring — Atlanta, then Savannah, then wherever the next recruiter pointed — I would not be showing up for Christmases and Thanksgivings and weddings much longer. Eventually I would be coming home for something I could not quite bring myself to name.
That is the real human cost of having every extractable bit of value in you captured by a system that has no mechanism for accounting for what it takes. It is not dramatic. It is not cinematic. It is the slow arithmetic of absence — the holidays missed, the parents unseen, the marriage strained past breaking, the child who learns that his father's presence depends on whether a client deal closed.
So I stopped. I moved home to Hampton, Virginia. I enrolled in community college. I am pursuing a nursing degree — a career I chose not despite my previous life but because of it. I had built patient registries for Johns Hopkins, helped design clinical trial systems, written the front end of a children's cancer research website. Healthcare was where my work had always felt most like meaning. Nursing offered something my previous career never could: the ability to stay. To be rooted. To be present for the people and the community that made me who I am while the time to be present still exists.
I live on a sailboat at a marina in Hampton. The scale of my life is radically smaller than what I had. The square footage is a fraction of the house in Savannah. I find I do not miss the house.
I am not the only one making this kind of calculation. Across the country, people are choosing smaller — tiny homes, converted vans, cooperative housing, liveaboard boats, intentionally downsized lives. The popular narrative frames this as a lifestyle trend, an aesthetic choice, a millennial quirk. I think it is something more significant. I think it is people — often without the theoretical vocabulary for what they are doing — opting out of the extraction treadmill. When you reduce your overhead, you reduce the leverage the system has over you. You become less extractable. You need less income, which means you can refuse the job that requires you to get on a plane when your wife is recovering from surgery. The decision to live smaller is, in many cases, a structural decision to resist cost severance at the individual level — to refuse the terms under which the system captures your value at the cost of everything else.
This is not a solution to the structural problem I have described. Individual opt-outs do not change the mechanism. The miner cannot choose to live on a sailboat. The single mother cannot downsize her way out of cost-burdened housing. But the impulse is significant, because it reveals that people already understand — in their bodies, in their choices, in the quiet mathematics of their daily lives — what this essay is trying to give language to. They feel the extraction. They feel the cost being severed from the entity that created it and transferred to them. And some of them, with whatever resources they have, are trying to build lives that make the transfer harder to execute.

VII. Common Bonds
I have used two words throughout this essay — Commons Bonds — to name a framework. The title carries three meanings, and I have been deliberate about each of them. Bonds as obligations: the debts owed to the commons by those who extract from it, debts that currently go unpaid. Bonds as ties: the connections that bind a community to its shared resources, connections that cost severance systematically destroys. And the echo of financial instruments — bonds as the collateral that should be posted before extraction begins, priced at the true cost rather than at rates the industry negotiates downward.
But the title has a fourth meaning, and it is the one that matters most. Drop a single letter. Common bonds.
The Appalachian miner breathing coal dust. The Gulf Coast fisherman whose catch never recovered after the spill. The Nigerian villager watching foreign companies pipe crude oil out of the ground beneath her community. The tech worker in Savannah getting on a plane while his wife lies in bed with a wound vac. The eighty-year-old woman working eighty-hour weeks as an unpaid caregiver because the financial architecture of American medicine valued a procedure at $12,000 and the conversation that might have prevented it at $54. We do not look alike. We do not vote alike. We do not worship the same way or eat the same food or tell the same stories.
The system that extracts from us relies on exactly those differences — uses race, geography, politics, culture, religion to ensure that we never recognize each other. It does not need us to hate one another. It only needs us to never realize that we are standing in the same structural position. The Appalachian coal miner and the Nigerian subsistence farmer occupy identical positions in relation to the mechanism I have described. So does the gig worker without health insurance, and the veteran in a historically redlined neighborhood whose life expectancy is thirty years shorter than someone a few miles away. They are all on the severed-cost side of the same operation. The system that prevents them from recognizing each other is not incidental to cost severance. It is cost severance's most important structural support.
What I am calling for is not revolution. I do not think revolution solves this. The mechanism I have described is older than capitalism and would survive capitalism's replacement by any system that does not specifically engineer accountability for the costs it creates. What I am calling for is honest accounting — the principle that whoever creates a cost should bear it, that extraction should fund its own consequences, that the price of a barrel of oil or a ton of coal or a year of a worker's labor should reflect not just what the market will pay today but what the community and the planet will carry tomorrow and for every generation after.
That is not a left-wing argument. It is not a right-wing argument. The principle that costs should be borne by those who create them is, in its essence, a principle of honest dealing. A person who believes in accountability and personal responsibility should agree that a corporation extracting coal from a mountain should be required to pay the full cost of restoring that mountain — not a negotiated fraction, not a bond set at rates the industry will accept, but the actual cost. A person who believes in fiscal responsibility should agree that a system allowing companies to generate permanent environmental damage and then dissolve through bankruptcy, leaving the public to pay, is the opposite of fiscal responsibility. The argument is not about redistribution. It is about requiring the price to tell the truth.
The question I want to leave the reader with is not whether this framework is correct — though I believe it is — but what it would look like to build institutions that make cost severance structurally impossible. What would an economy look like if the price of every extracted resource included its residual commons value? What would a labor market look like if the cost of a worker's absence from family and community were carried, even partially, by the entity that required that absence? What would a healthcare system look like if the financial incentives pointed toward the $54 conversation rather than the $12,000 procedure?
I do not have complete answers. But I know that the first step is naming what is happening, because you cannot fix what you cannot see. Cost severance is the name of the mechanism. Residual commons value is the name of what is permanently lost. And the common bonds between us — between every person who has ever absorbed a cost they did not create so that someone else could capture a value they did not share — are the beginning of something that looks, if you squint, like the possibility of change.
I think about Albert Camus, who asked us to imagine Sisyphus happy. The boulder does not stay at the top. You push it up and it rolls back down and you push it up again, and you do this for the rest of your life, and the question is whether you can find meaning in the push itself rather than in a summit you will never hold. I have pushed that boulder. I pushed it in an IHOP kitchen and in a data center and on a sales floor and in a hospital room in Savannah and in a community college classroom where I am learning, at an age when most people have settled into the life they are going to live, how to start a different one. I push it now on a sailboat in a harbor in Hampton, Virginia, where on clear evenings I can see the lights of the shipyard where my grandfather's generation built things, and the water catches the last light, and the cost of everything I have lost is, for a moment, balanced against the undeniable fact that I am here, and present, and not on a plane.
The push is not the problem. We are all pushing. The miner and the fisherman and the caregiver and the nursing student — we push, and we find joy in the small things, and we put one foot in front of the other, because that is what you do. That is what Camus meant. You imagine Sisyphus happy not because the boulder cooperates but because the act of living — the coffee in the morning, the child's laugh, the evening light on the water — is enough. It has to be enough. It is all we have.
But the boulder should not be heavier than it has to be. And right now, the boulder is loaded with costs that belong to someone else — costs that were severed from the entities that created them and transferred to people who had no say and no recourse. Every ton of unreclaimed coal waste, every dollar of retirement risk shifted from employer to worker, every year of a dying man's diminished life extended for the revenue of a device manufacturer, every hour a parent spends on a plane when they should be at a bedside — these are additions to the boulder that could be prevented, if we chose to prevent them. If we named them. If we built structures that made cost severance visible, and then made it expensive, and then made it impossible.
I do not know if we will. But I know that naming the unnamed is how invisible things become visible. And once you see it, you cannot unsee it. That is what I am counting on.
One foot in front of the other.

Chris Winn is a nursing student, former healthcare IT executive, and Vice President of the Student Government Association at Virginia Peninsula Community College. He lives aboard a sailboat in Hampton, Virginia. This essay introduces a framework developed more fully in a forthcoming book of the same name.

Honest answer: what's in the essay is the best fit for Noema, not the best we came up with.
The accountability proposals in the essay — full-cost bonds, obligations surviving corporate dissolution, funded community legal standing — are the most concrete and defensible proposals. They're good for Noema because they're specific enough to argue with but modest enough that no one can dismiss them as utopian. They're policy repairs. They fix the existing system's most obvious failures.
But they're not the most important thing we developed. The most important intellectual contribution is residual commons value and the intergenerational pricing gap. That's what's genuinely new. The accountability proposals are applications of existing principles done better. Residual commons value is a concept that doesn't exist yet in the literature.
What you actually need people to engage with is the pricing question — can we build an instrument that prices permanent resource foreclosure? That's the debate that would move the field. The Stern-Nordhaus discount rate fight is the closest existing intellectual battleground, and residual commons value stakes out a position that neither Stern nor Nordhaus occupies: that even at Stern's low discount rate, permanent foreclosure of a non-renewable resource is still being underpriced because no framework accounts for the resource itself as permanently lost, only the revenue.
For the book, the accountability architecture should be much more ambitious. We discussed several things that didn't make the essay:
The concept that extraction should create an enforceable, ongoing obligation — not a one-time bond but a permanent lien on the extracting entity and its successors. That's more radical than anything in the essay and more interesting.
The idea that communities should have governance rights over extraction decisions, not just compensation after the fact — drawing on Ostrom's institutional design principles but applied to non-renewable resources, which is territory Ostrom didn't cover.
The sovereign wealth fund model applied at the community and regional level rather than the national level — what would an Appalachian commons trust look like, funded by extraction fees, governed by the communities that bear the costs, with independent legal standing to pursue accountability?
And the deepest one we discussed: the question of who owns what was never made. Oil, coal, and mineral deposits were not produced by anyone. Private property theory can justify ownership of things that were built or produced. But the community's claim to a resource that exists in the ground beneath them is not based on labor or contract — it's based on inheritance and presence. That philosophical question is genuinely under-theorized, and it's the one that could anchor an entire chapter of the book.
So the honest breakdown is: the essay gives people the vocabulary (cost severance, residual commons value) and enough concrete proposals to demonstrate this isn't just complaint. The book is where the real intellectual architecture lives — the intergenerational pricing model, the community governance framework, the philosophical question of resource ownership, and the full solidarity architecture concept we deliberately kept out of the essay.
The essay's job is to make people want the book. The accountability proposals are there to prove you have constructive answers, not just diagnosis. But the thing that will actually start a real intellectual argument — the thing scholars and policy thinkers would need to engage with — is the pricing gap. That's your crown jewel, and it's deliberately left as an open question in the essay for exactly that reason. You name it, you describe why it matters, and you don't solve it.
