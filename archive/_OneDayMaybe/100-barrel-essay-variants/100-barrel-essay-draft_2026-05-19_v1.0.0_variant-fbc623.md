## I. The Barrel

Drive any stretch of American highway and the signs at the pumps tell you what is being paid for a gallon. The number is salient — high enough that the political class is arguing about it again on cable news, salient enough that ordinary people are deciding which errands to skip. Set aside the immediate cause. Set aside the question of who is to blame. Imagine instead a barrel of oil still in the ground.

It is somewhere in the Permian, or under the North Sea, or off the Niger Delta. Geology is not the question. Imagine someone walks up to that barrel and offers a price for it. The barrel is purchased. It is brought to the surface. It is refined and burned. The price was one hundred dollars.

A hundred dollars sounds high or low depending on which decade of pump prices you remember. The number is not the point. The point is what the transaction did. Someone paid a hundred dollars for a thing that, before the transaction, sat in geological storage on a timescale measured in hundreds of millions of years. After the transaction, the thing is gone. Not consumed in the way a loaf of bread is consumed and then baked again next week. Not harvested in the way a forest is logged and then replanted. Not depleted in the recoverable sense, where the well sits idle for a season and then refills. Gone, as in foreclosed; gone, as in unavailable to anyone, ever, who comes after.

The hundred dollars priced something. It priced the willingness of a buyer to pay. It priced the cost of getting the barrel out of the ground. It priced a refining margin and a distribution chain. It did not price the absence of the barrel. The absence runs forward through every generation that will not be able to use it. There is no party in the transaction whose business it is to charge for that absence. There is no line item in the contract that names it. The market that produced the price is silent on the question.

This silence is what the essay is about. It is not a complaint that markets fail to do something they were never built to do. It is a description of an accounting gap. Standard pricing handles the present competently. It handles foreseeable scarcity through expectations, futures contracts, and inventory management. What it does not handle, anywhere in its machinery, is the permanent absence of the resource from the menu of options available to a future to which the present is not directly accountable.

I will call that gap *residual commons value*. Naming the gap is the first step. The rest of the essay walks the lineage of those who have worked the adjacent territory — Marx, Ostrom, Daly, Hartwick, the climate economists from Stern to Nordhaus to Weitzman, and the philosophers from Rawls to Sandel to Parfit — and shows where each contribution stops short. It then asks what an honest accounting answer would have to do. There is one example of a government that has done part of the work. There are many examples of governments that have not.

## II. What Classical Economics Misses

Classical economics handled resources the way it handled most other inputs to production. A resource has a price, and the price reflects the marginal cost of supplying one more unit. If the resource becomes scarcer, the price rises. Higher prices attract capital toward substitutes. If oil grows expensive, the economy turns toward natural gas, and then toward solar, and then toward hydrogen, and then toward whatever the next decade's research produces. The substitution will happen on its own. The price is the signal that orchestrates the transition.

This is the substitutability assumption, and it is doing a great deal of work. It says that what matters about a resource is the service it provides, not the substance itself. Oil is fungible with whatever else can move a vehicle or heat a building. A forest is fungible with whatever else can supply lumber and pulp. The good in question is the function, not the particular geological or biological thing that performs the function. If the function can be performed by a substitute, the original resource was, economically speaking, not unique.

The assumption may prove true. Technology has surprised the pessimists before. Coal was supposed to run out in the nineteenth century and did not. Estimates of peak oil have moved by decades depending on extraction technique. There is a long tradition, going back to Malthus and forward through the Julian Simon side of the Simon–Ehrlich wager, of betting that the next substitute will arrive in time, and the bet has often paid.

But the assumption is an assumption, not a guarantee. The bet has paid because human beings invented things; nothing in the economics says they had to, and nothing in the economics compensates a future born into a regime where they did not. A market that prices a barrel of oil today does not price the risk that the substitute fails to arrive on schedule. It does not price the risk that the substitute arrives but at a quality that fails to do everything the original did. It does not price the welfare loss imposed on the generation that needed petroleum chemistry for medicines, fertilizers, plastics, and lubricants and discovered that combustion had exhausted the supply before chemistry was ready.

The market price reflects today's substitutability expectations and today's discount rate. It does not reflect tomorrow's regret if the expectations turn out wrong. The future does not have a seat at the auction. That is the gap classical economics leaves open. Filling it is the work the rest of this essay is about.

## III. The Lineage Map

Several traditions have worked on the territory next door to this gap. They have done serious intellectual labor on adjacent questions. None of them has named the gap as such, and the reasons they have not are instructive.

Karl Marx, writing the first volume of *Capital* in 1867, located the central economic distortion of his era in the appropriation of surplus value from labor. The capitalist pays the worker the cost of reproducing the worker's labor power and pockets the difference between that cost and the value the worker's labor produces. The critique is structural; it is about who captures the surplus generated in production. It is not principally a critique of resources at all. Resources appear in Marx as raw materials whose price is set by the labor required to extract them. The permanent disappearance of the resource from the productive base, after extraction, is not Marx's question. Marx is in the lineage map here as a matter of intellectual inventory: a major nineteenth-century critic of how surpluses are captured. The question of whether his analysis of labor is correct, or whether his political conclusions follow, is a separate question from the one this essay is asking. The point is that even a thinker who pressed hard on what capitalism extracts, and from whom, did not press on what extraction permanently removes from the world that comes after.

Elinor Ostrom won the Nobel Memorial Prize in 2009 for *Governing the Commons*, a book that overturned the standard reading of the tragedy of the commons. Ostrom showed, through fieldwork on fisheries, forests, irrigation systems, and grazing lands across continents, that human communities have repeatedly invented governance arrangements that keep common-pool resources from collapsing. She identified design principles — clearly defined boundaries, congruence between rules and local conditions, collective-choice arenas, monitoring, graduated sanctions — that distinguished the successful cases from the failures. The work was empirical and humane, and it changed what economists thought commons were. But the resources Ostrom studied were renewable. A fishery can be governed to a sustained yield because the fish reproduce. A forest can be managed to indefinite harvest because the trees regrow. A non-renewable resource sits outside the territory her design principles were built to cover. There is no sustained-yield management of a barrel of oil. Once it is out, it is out. Ostrom told us how to govern what can be governed; she did not tell us how to price what cannot be governed because it is, by its nature, finite.

Herman Daly, writing *Steady-State Economics* in 1977 and developing the field of ecological economics across his subsequent career, made the case that the economy is a subsystem of the biosphere, not a freestanding system that exists alongside it. The throughput of materials and energy from the environment into the economy, and back out as waste, is bounded by the biosphere's capacity to supply the materials and absorb the waste. Daly insisted on stock-flow accounting: keep track of natural-capital stocks separately from the income flows they generate, because the stocks are what makes the flows possible. Run the stocks down and the flows go with them. This is a powerful frame, and most of what is right in modern climate economics descends from it. But Daly's argument is about the overall scale of the throughput, not about the pricing of any particular resource's permanent foreclosure. The biosphere-supersystem frame tells you the throughput is too high. It does not produce a number that names the missing cost on a specific barrel of a specific resource permanently removed from the geological inventory.

John Hartwick, writing in the *American Economic Review* in 1977, produced a result of unusual precision. The Hartwick rule states that if the rents earned from extracting a non-renewable resource are fully reinvested in reproducible capital — factories, infrastructure, education, financial assets — consumption can be sustained indefinitely. The depletion of the resource is offset, in welfare terms, by the buildup of the capital stock that replaces it. Norway, which began extracting North Sea oil in 1969 and built a sovereign wealth fund into which extraction revenues are deposited, is the canonical real-world application. The rule is elegant and the example is real and there is a great deal to be learned from it. But Hartwick's accounting prices the revenue from the resource, not the resource itself. The barrel of oil is sold; the revenue is invested; the capital stock grows. The barrel, however, is still gone. If the reproducible capital fails — if it depreciates faster than expected, if the next generation's investment options do not match the function the resource performed, if the political compact that keeps the capital in trust unravels — the Hartwick offset does not run. The principle gets us most of the way. It does not name, as a separate thing requiring its own price, what extraction permanently forecloses.

## IV. Rawls Across Time

To name the missing cost, we need a way of saying what the foreclosed future is owed by the present. Several philosophical traditions point to the same answer from different angles.

John Rawls published *A Theory of Justice* in 1971. The device for which the book is best known is the original position behind a veil of ignorance: imagine you are choosing the basic structure of society without knowing in advance which position in that society you would occupy. The veil strips out the partial perspectives that bias real political deliberation, and the policies that would still be agreed to under the veil are, on Rawls's account, the policies a just society would adopt. The original position has been argued about for fifty years, but the device retains its grip because it captures something most people recognize as a precondition of fairness.

Take the veil and apply it across time. Imagine you are choosing the basic rules under which a society manages its non-renewable resources, but you do not know whether you will be born in 1850, in 1950, or in 2150. You do not know whether the resource in question will be available to you at high price, at low price, or at any price. You do not know whether the substitute technology will have arrived. Would you sign onto a system in which the present generation can liquidate the geological inventory at a price that reflects only the present generation's marginal supply curve and discount rate? Would you sign onto a system in which the future generations whose access is foreclosed are represented at the table by no one? The Rawlsian intuition is that you would not.

Rawls is one philosopher producing this intuition. Michael Sandel, in *What Money Can't Buy*, argues from a different starting point — that there are goods which can be corrupted by being priced at all, and that even when pricing is unavoidable, the prices we set carry moral weight beyond their efficiency properties. Derek Parfit, in *Reasons and Persons*, worked the problem of obligations to future generations from the angle of personal identity and the non-identity problem; his conclusions are technical, but they converge on the same place. You do not have to pick one of these three thinkers and bet on him against the others. The intuition that a present generation owes the future an account of what it has permanently removed is robust across philosophical methods that disagree about almost everything else. The veil, the moral limits of markets, the non-identity problem — these traditions argue with one another, but they agree that the question is real and that current market prices answer it inadequately.

The gap between the market price of a barrel of oil and the price that would result from honoring the convergent intuition is what I will call *residual commons value*. The phrase is plain. It is the worth, to the people who come after, of what was permanently removed. It is what is missing from the hundred dollars. Naming this gap as a discrete thing — not as a vague unfairness, not as a sentiment about future generations, but as a specific component of the price that an honest accounting would have to estimate — is what makes solving for it possible. The substantive work of producing the estimate is hard. The naming is the precondition of that work.

## V. The Existing Battleground

There is already a vigorous economic literature on what the present owes the future, and it has not been organized around the foreclosure question. It has been organized around the climate question. The framework I am describing has implications for that literature, but it sits at an angle to it, and the angle matters.

The existing battleground was set, in its current configuration, by the publication of *The Economics of Climate Change: The Stern Review* in 2007. Commissioned by the United Kingdom government and released in draft as an independent review in October 2006, the seven-hundred-page Stern Review argued that climate change was the greatest market failure in history, that allowing it to run uncorrected would cost something like one percent of global gross domestic product per year and rising, and that the appropriate response — at a social cost of carbon of roughly three hundred and sixty dollars per ton — would be far more aggressive than what governments were then doing. The technical move that did the work was a low discount rate, around 1.4 percent, applied to future damages. A low discount rate means future damages count for almost as much as present damages; a high one means they fade quickly to nothing.

The Stern Review was answered by William Nordhaus, who has worked on the climate-economy problem since the late 1970s and who received the Nobel Memorial Prize in 2018 for integrating climate change into long-run macroeconomic analysis. Nordhaus's Dynamic Integrated Climate-Economy model used a higher discount rate, around 4.3 percent in its earlier iterations, and arrived at a social cost of carbon closer to thirty-five dollars per ton in the same comparison frame. The arithmetic of compounding does almost all the visible work. A hundred dollars of welfare a century out, discounted at Stern's rate, is worth roughly twenty-five dollars today. The same hundred dollars, discounted at Nordhaus's rate, is worth about a dollar and a half. The same physical world produces wildly different policy prescriptions depending on which rate you write into the spreadsheet, and the dispute between Stern and Nordhaus has occupied a generation of climate economists.

Martin Weitzman, in a 2009 paper on catastrophic risk, opened an adjacent front. Even at high discount rates, he argued, the expected value of climate damages is dominated by the heavy tail of catastrophic outcomes, and pricing only the body of the distribution systematically understates the cost. The fat-tail tradition has its own internal debates, and they cut across the Stern-Nordhaus axis without simplifying it.

The position I want to stake out is not that Stern is right and Nordhaus is wrong, or the reverse. Both economists are doing serious work. The discount-rate question is a real one. The point is that the entire Stern-Nordhaus debate, and the Weitzman extension of it, is a debate about how to price climate damage. Stern prices the damage at a low discount rate and gets a high social cost of carbon. Nordhaus prices the damage at a higher discount rate and gets a lower social cost of carbon. Weitzman prices the catastrophic tail and gets a still different number. None of these three pricings, however, is a pricing of the resource's permanent foreclosure as such. They price the damage from burning the resource, or the catastrophic risk from doing so. They do not price the absence of the resource from the menu of options available to a future that arrives after combustion is done.

This matters because the foreclosure cost survives even Stern's already-low discount rate. Adopting Stern's preferred rate would not close the gap I am describing. The gap is structurally outside the discount-rate axis. It is about what the price is pricing, not about how heavily future versions of that price are weighted. Both Stern and Nordhaus could be right about everything they argue about with each other, and the resource would still be gone, the future would still be foreclosed from access, and the market that produced the original transaction would still be silent on the question. The honest accounting move is not to win the discount-rate debate from one side; it is to notice that the debate, as currently structured, is about a different thing.

## VI. Honest Accounting

One country has done part of the work. The example is worth dwelling on, because it cuts against the assumption that the architectural move requires a particular political configuration.

Norway found oil in the North Sea in December 1969 — a discovery well at the Ekofisk field, in Block 2/4, three days before Christmas. The country was a modestly prosperous social democracy with a fishing fleet and a merchant marine and a population then under four million. It became, over the next three decades, the holder of the largest sovereign wealth fund in the world. The mechanism was deliberate. In 1990 the government established the Government Petroleum Fund, into which the state's share of petroleum revenues would be deposited. In 2006 the fund was renamed the Government Pension Fund Global. In 2001 the parliament adopted a fiscal rule: the government could spend no more from the fund each year than the expected real return on its capital, treated over time as roughly three percent. The principal stays. The fund's holdings exceed nineteen hundred billion dollars at the time of this writing. Per Norwegian citizen, that is something on the order of three hundred and forty thousand dollars.

What is striking about the Norwegian arrangement, for the argument here, is not the size of the fund. It is the political durability of the rule. The 2001 fiscal rule was adopted under the first Stoltenberg government, a Labour government. It was held in place by the Bondevik II coalition, a center-right coalition. It was held in place again under the second Stoltenberg Labour government, then under the Solberg Conservative coalition, then under the Støre Labour-Centre coalition that came to office in 2021. The rule has survived election cycles, oil-price shocks, the global financial crisis, the pandemic, and the political-energy debates of the present decade. Whatever else one thinks about Norwegian politics, the architecture of saving extraction revenue rather than spending it is not a left or a right project there. It is the inherited public infrastructure of an extraction economy that elected to behave responsibly toward generations who are not yet voting.

Norway is the existence proof. It does not solve the foreclosure problem in full — its fund prices the revenue, in the Hartwick sense, not the resource itself — but it has built the institutional capacity to keep the proceeds of extraction in trust across changes of government. That capacity is the part of the architecture you cannot price your way into. It has to be built politically, by a country that decides to build it. Once built, it can carry richer answers about pricing.

The counter-case is Nigeria. Oil was found in the Niger Delta in the late 1950s, and large-scale production began in the 1960s. The extraction was structured around international oil companies — Shell, Chevron, ExxonMobil, Total — operating in concession with the Nigerian state, with revenue flows that, over decades, financed central-government operations and elite consumption rather than building durable national assets or addressing the costs absorbed by the people in the Delta. The Ogoni people, on whose lands much of the early Shell operation took place, organized a movement in the early 1990s under the writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa. In 1995 the Nigerian military government executed Saro-Wiwa and eight of his colleagues after a tribunal that international observers regarded as fraudulent; Nigeria was suspended from the Commonwealth. The United Nations Environment Programme issued a report on Ogoniland in 2011, finding contamination of soil and groundwater across hundreds of square kilometers. The initial estimate of remediation costs was on the order of a billion dollars, with twenty-five to thirty years projected for full restoration. The work has barely begun.

Nigeria is not a story about the absence of oil revenue. The revenue was there, in volumes comparable to what Norway received. It is a story about an institutional architecture that did not deposit those revenues into trust for the generations whose ground was being foreclosed. The two cases together — Norway and Nigeria — demonstrate that the architecture is a choice. Geology gave both countries an extractive endowment. Political construction determined what each generation did with it.

What I am calling for is not revolution. I do not think revolution solves this. The work I am describing — naming residual commons value, producing a number for it that an economist can defend and a policymaker can implement, building the institutional architecture that holds the resulting accounts in trust across changes of government — is engineering work. It is not a left-wing argument. It is not a right-wing argument. It is the principle that whoever creates a cost should be the one to bear it, applied honestly to the kind of cost that extends past the lifetime of the people who created it. Norway is one country that built that architecture without first agreeing on which political coalition would build it. The construction is harder than the politics. The politics, where there is real political will, can be lower than the construction.

## Close

The hard work of producing a defensible number for residual commons value, of building the institutional channels through which that number is collected and held in trust, of integrating the answer with the climate-discount debate and the surplus-value tradition and the commons-governance literature and the steady-state framework — that work is the work of decades, not paragraphs. The point of this essay is more modest. You cannot solve a problem that has no name. Marx named who captures the surplus generated by labor and stopped there. Ostrom named how renewable commons can be governed and stopped there. Daly named the throughput and stopped there. Hartwick named the reinvestment rule and stopped there. Rawls, Sandel, and Parfit named the moral structure of obligations to people not yet born and stopped before the question of resources. Stern, Nordhaus, and Weitzman named the climate damage at various discount rates and stopped before the foreclosure. Each of them built a piece of the scaffolding around the gap. The gap itself — residual commons value, the worth of what was permanently foreclosed — is the load-bearing piece they all left unbuilt. The first step toward building it is naming it. That is what this essay has done.
