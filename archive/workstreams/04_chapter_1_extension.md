## What this workstream does

Extends Chapter 1 ("The Plane") so that the mechanism the book will later name is *demonstrated* through the knowledge-worker experience before the framework is introduced. The extension adds one paragraph universalizing the mechanism between the existing personal narrative and the existing pull-back sentence, plus optional stories drawn from the IT consulting years.

Why this matters: the book risks being read as "about coal miners and watermen." It is not. It is about anyone whose value is captured while costs are severed — which includes the reader, probably including the reader holding the book. Chapter 1 is where that identification happens. If the reader does not recognize themselves in Chapter 1, they read the book as a diagnosis of other people's problems. If they do, they read every subsequent chapter as a continuation of their own story.

## The proposed paragraph

Source: `commons-bonds-chapter-1-extension 14.html`. In your voice, to be rewritten — this is scaffolding, not prose you paste verbatim.

> There is a specific kind of 120-hour week that does not appear in any ledger. Not the hours that were billed — those were billed accurately, at rates both parties had agreed to. What does not appear is the cognitive overhead that followed the hours home, the pattern-matching fatigue that meant I was not present even when I was physically home, the compounding weight of problems that did not fit in the contract but followed me anyway because I was the person hired to carry them. The contract captured the billable time. It did not capture what the billable time was displacing. My body was in the meeting. My attention was partitioned across four open problems, six stakeholder relationships, and two deadlines that would arrive before the plane landed. The work captured my attention. The attention it captured was taken from somewhere — from my wife recovering from dying, from my son who could have been held longer, from the version of my life that required me to be physically, cognitively, emotionally present. What was taken was not billed. The party that took it did not pay for it. I did.

This paragraph does two things simultaneously. It universalizes the mechanism — any reader who has experienced a 120-hour week recognizes the structure immediately. And it plants the eight-tier framework before the framework is named: the reader will later recognize, in Chapter 6, that the paragraph described in sequence Lifetime Survival Cost (cognitive overhead, pattern-matching fatigue), Dynastic Labor Cost (time taken from son), and what the book will eventually call the absence of a commons for attention and presence.

## The story menu (editorial, not mandatory)

Choose as many as serve the chapter without turning it into memoir. The Make It Better doc's rule applies: Chapter 1 is a bridge from personal story to universal mechanism, not a memoir in disguise.

1. **The 120+ billable hour week.** The logistics — time zones, continuous handoffs, inability to stop. The moment where the framework becomes illegible because there is no time to think about what is being taken, only time to deliver what has been promised.
2. **The wife's illness and the paternal leave expiration.** Already in the chapter. The extension makes explicit what is currently implicit: her recovery, the surgical dressings, the ileostomy bag, the emotional processing of near-death, were all being displaced by the contract the family's income depended on. Don't over-describe — a sentence or two at most. Medical specificity is her story, not yours to put on paper.
3. **The consulting handoff that never happened.** If there is a story of a client engagement where the handoff to another consultant was structurally impossible — because the knowledge was only in your head, and no one had been paid to extract it from your head and distribute it — that is Knowledge and Culture Cost at the individual scale. The firm extracted the value you could produce; it did not extract the knowledge that made you able to produce it. When you left or burned out, that knowledge disappeared from the firm.
4. **The sales cycle that determined Thanksgiving attendance.** The specific moments when a business calendar determined a family calendar. The parents visibly moving slower. The calculation of how many more Thanksgivings are left versus how many more deals must close. This is Dynastic Labor Cost made personal. If this story is already in the RAW draft, preserve it — it is one of the strongest single passages available.

## The consent problem in its sharpest form

This is the part of the extension that does the most structural work and should not be cut for length.

A free-market economist reading Chapter 1 will say: *you made voluntary trade-offs*. The chapter should address this directly — and the specific form the addressing should take is: *you had options a coal miner in McDowell County does not have*. You had elite technical skills, multiple employers, geographic mobility, and significant compensation. And you still ended up on a plane while your wife recovered from dying.

This is the most interesting version of the consent problem in the entire book, because it rules out the easy explanation. You did not lack alternatives. You did not lack information. You were not coerced in any conventional sense. And the framework still applies, because the voluntariness of the exchange was nominal rather than real — the contract priced the billable time, not the displacement. The choice was not between mining coal and investment banking. It was between billing 120 hours and losing the income trajectory that funded the healthcare the family depended on. A choice made under those conditions is a choice, but it is not the free choice that free-market theory presupposes.

This is the sharpest demonstration of the framework available, because it forecloses the easiest dismissal. If the framework applies to someone with these resources, it applies with greater force to people with fewer.

## Watch items

- **Do not name the eight tiers in Chapter 1.** The paragraph demonstrates them in sequence without labeling. First formal naming is Chapter 6. The reader should recognize the list when they get there, not meet it for the first time. This is a deliberate structural choice.
- **Do not let Chapter 1 become a memoir.** The target length for Chapter 1 per the Make It Better doc is 5,000–6,000 words. The extension adds ~300 words. The story menu is editorial — pick what serves the universalization, not what is biographically interesting.
- **Do not describe your wife's medical details beyond what is structurally necessary.** Her story is not yours to put on paper. The mechanism is that her recovery was displaced by the contract; that is the point, and that can be made in one sentence.
- **Do not soften the consent-problem passage.** It is the sharpest single demonstration of the framework available. Keep it.
- **Do not preempt Chapter 2.** McDowell County is Chapter 2's. Chapter 1 may reference the miner once, in the consent-problem passage, but the case study is not here.
