## What this workstream does (and no longer does)

The original Workstream 13 scope was "Implementation Pathway" — a full policy roadmap covering legislative pathways at local, state, federal, and international levels; entering-wedges strategy for carbon pricing, sovereign wealth funds, reclamation bonds, and environmental justice law; political coalition building; risk mitigation; and a three-phase timeline for framework adoption. That scope is **Book Two** material. It violates the two-book split that Decision D finalized.

What Book One needs from this workstream is much smaller: **a compressed theory-of-change section**, roughly one to two pages, placed in Chapter 9 or Chapter 10, that explains why an intellectual framework is a genuine contribution even when no legislation is passed. The argument is the Harvey / Ostrom precedent — frameworks change what is conceivable before they change what is possible, and they change what is possible before they change what is law. Naming the mechanism is the contribution. Legislative passage is somebody else's job, later.

The substantial policy-pathway content that existed in the original workstream scope moves intact to `/_One Day Maybe/Book-Two-Subsidy-Economy/` where it becomes source material for the applied book, if and when that book gets written.

## Why this re-scoping matters

From the two-book strategic analysis: Book One's defensive posture is that it is a *framework*, not a *policy proposal*. That distinction is what protects it from the advocacy-labeling reflex that would retroactively label the whole framework as ideological. A "framework" that comes with a legislative blueprint, coalition-building strategy, risk mitigation matrix, and implementation timeline *is* a policy proposal, regardless of how it labels itself. Readers, critics, and opposing researchers can see this immediately.

The compressed theory-of-change section does not carry that exposure because it is not proposing specific legislation, naming coalitions, or laying out timelines. It is making a meta-argument about what intellectual work contributes to democratic discourse. That is a philosophy-of-scholarship question, not a policy question.

## What stays in Book One

**Target deliverable:** One section of approximately 500–800 words in Chapter 9 (Renewable Imperative) or early Chapter 10 (Common Bonds) titled something like *"How Frameworks Change Things"* or *"What Naming Does"*.

### Content in the Book One section

1. **The Harvey / Ostrom precedent (short).** Before accumulation-by-dispossession had a name, it was a pattern people experienced. Naming it did not pass any legislation, but it changed what labor lawyers, journalists, and movement organizers could say about extraction. Before polycentric commons governance had a name, Ostrom's subjects were already doing it. Naming it changed what development economists and policy designers could propose. **Naming creates the vocabulary the discourse needs to engage the phenomenon.**
2. **The Nordic precedent (one paragraph).** Norway's Government Pension Fund Global was not created by a legislative miracle in 1990 — it was created by decades of accumulated intellectual work making the concept of intergenerational commons management conceivable. The framework came first. The legislation came later, because the framework made the legislation thinkable. This is the model, not the outlier.
3. **Asymmetric returns to vocabulary.** An hour of legislative advocacy reaches a committee. A concept that enters the discourse reaches everyone who reads, writes, argues, or legislates about resource extraction, for as long as the concept is useful. The multiplier is enormous. The success criterion from Workstream 01 applies: if in ten years a labor lawyer uses "severed cost" in a brief and the judge does not need it explained — and this book's author never knows — the book succeeded.
4. **The honest concession (brief).** Book One does not pass legislation. Book One does not name specific industries, executives, or political figures who should be accountable for the costs this framework measures. Book One does not design a regulatory apparatus. Those are other books, written by other people, or by this author later under different conditions. Book One's job is to make the invisible visible and hand the vocabulary to the discourse. Anything beyond that job risks weakening the book's ability to do its actual job.
5. **The handoff.** Close the section by noting that the framework is designed to be picked up by practitioners with more standing in specific domains — environmental lawyers, policy designers, economic theorists, labor organizers, Indigenous rights advocates, international development specialists. The framework's success is measured by how widely it is adopted and extended by others, not by what its author does with it.

## What NOT to move

A few items from the original WS13 are not Book Two material and should not travel with the rest.

- **The success metric.** "Vocabulary adoption by practitioners in specific domains" is the right Book One success criterion. It stays in Workstream 01 (Master Decisions) and any scaffolding that references what the book is trying to accomplish. Do not relegate it to Book Two notes.
- **The Harvey / Ostrom precedent analogy.** Stays in Book One as part of the compressed theory-of-change section. Its job is to explain why vocabulary adoption is a legitimate success metric, not to argue for specific policies.
- **The general observation that frameworks change possibility space.** This is the meta-claim Book One needs to make once, briefly, in the compressed section. It is not policy advocacy; it is philosophy of intellectual work.

## Success metric (for Book One specifically)

> If in ten years a labor lawyer uses "severed cost" in a brief and the judge does not need it explained — and this book's author never knows it happened — the book succeeded.

From Workstream 01, Decision D. Book One's success is measured by vocabulary adoption and conceptual uptake, not by legislative passage. This metric should appear once in the compressed section and nowhere else. Repeating it dilutes it.

## Counterargument engagement in this section

Per Workstream 14's weaving strategy, the compressed theory-of-change section is the natural place to engage one specific counterargument: "the framework is not politically feasible — you don't offer implementation." The response is that implementation is not what frameworks are for. Frameworks change what is thinkable; implementation follows thinkability; legislation follows implementation; measurement of success lags all three.

Do not engage the full implementation-feasibility debate here. That expands the section beyond its 500–800 word target and drifts toward Book Two territory.

## Watch items

- **Do not let the compressed section grow back into a policy roadmap.** The section is 500–800 words. If it drifts toward 1,500, something Book-Two-shaped is creeping back in. Cut.
- **Do not name specific legislation, agencies, or coalition partners in Book One.** The Harvey / Ostrom precedent and the Norway case reference already-historical institutional outcomes. Those are fair. Proposing new ones is not.
- **Do not re-frame Book One as Phase 1 of a three-phase strategy.** Book One is a book. It is not a phase. Framing it as a phase implies Phase 2 and Phase 3 are forthcoming, which commits to a Book Two that may or may not get written. That commitment increases rather than reduces exposure.
- **Do not retain "entering wedges" vocabulary in Book One.** Entering wedges are tactical moves in a policy campaign. Book One is not a policy campaign.
- **Do not preserve risk-mitigation content in Book One.** Book One does not have implementation risks because Book One is not implementation.
- **Do not delete the original Workstream 13 content.** It is Book Two source material. Preserve intact in the Book Two folder.
- **Do not treat this re-scoping as a commitment to write Book Two.** Book Two remains an "if conditions permit, in 3–5 years" project. The move is housekeeping, not planning.
