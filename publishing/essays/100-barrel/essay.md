# The $100 Barrel

## I. The Barrel

The price of gasoline has crept up again. Anyone who has filled a tank this spring has watched the number on the display run past where it sat last year and pulled the nozzle out before the total settled into a figure worth a second look.

Consider, then, a barrel of oil still in the ground. The geologist has mapped it. The lease has been signed. Nothing has been pumped, refined, or sold. The barrel sits there in some Permian sandstone or some North Sea seabed or some Niger Delta wetland, in the dark.

Someone puts a price on it. The price is one hundred dollars.

What does that number represent? It represents, in the first place, what the barrel will fetch when it is brought up and sold. That is the spot price, more or less, minus the cost of extraction. It represents a forecast: what the market thinks demand will look like next quarter and next year. It represents a discount, applied because the seller would rather have a hundred dollars today than the slightly larger sum the barrel might fetch in 2030. In the technical literature it represents what economists call the Hotelling rent — the scarcity premium an owner gives up by extracting today rather than tomorrow.

What the price does not represent is anything about the barrel's permanent disappearance from the world.

The barrel will be liquidated. Burned, mostly. Combined with oxygen, broken apart, dispersed as exhaust and heat and carbon dioxide. It will not regrow next season the way wheat regrows. It will not refill the way a reservoir refills after spring rain. Once it is gone, it is gone, and it is gone for everyone who has not yet been born.

The price is for the present. The cost extends to permanence. The gap between what one party pays today and what the world loses forever is the subject of this essay. I call it *residual commons value*.

## II. What Classical Economics Misses

Start with what economics already does.

Classical economics — the running tradition from Adam Smith through the modern textbook — treats a non-renewable resource as an input with a market-determined price. Coal goes in; iron and electricity come out. Oil goes in; transportation and plastics come out. The price of the input adjusts to scarcity. When the resource gets harder to extract, the price rises; rising prices reward substitution and innovation; innovation finds a substitute; the economy adjusts.

This is the substitutability assumption. It is the single most important load-bearing claim in the way mainstream economics treats exhaustible resources. The claim is that when one resource runs short, technology will provide an alternative, and the alternative will be close enough in capacity that the loss of the original does not constrain the next generation's welfare.

The assumption is not idle. Coal was substituted for charcoal; whale oil was substituted by kerosene; copper telephone lines are being substituted by fiber. There is a real historical record of substitution working. Mainstream economics has earned the assumption a kind of presumption.

But it is just an assumption. It is not a guarantee. The substitution may not arrive in time, or the substitute that arrives may be inferior in ways that matter. The substitution may itself depend on a different non-renewable input that runs into the same wall a generation later. Lithium and cobalt and rare earths are non-renewable in their own right; a transition that swaps petroleum for lithium has not retired the underlying problem, only shifted its location.

And here is the part the rest of this essay turns on. Even when the substitution does arrive, no mechanism in the system compensates the people who lived through the gap. If the substitution arrives in 2080 and the resource runs out in 2050, then thirty years of people — the child born in 2055, the grandparent who outlives the resource by a decade, the working family that loses the input their livelihood depends on — get poorer transportation, poorer healthcare, poorer everything that depended on the missing resource. Classical economics, properly understood, prices the spot transaction. It does not price the absence.

Several traditions have noticed pieces of that absence. None of them has named the piece that survives all of their analyses.

## III. The Lineage Map

Begin with Marx, because every reader expects it. Marx wrote about labor. His central object of analysis was the surplus that the worker produces and the capitalist captures. In *Capital, Volume I*, published in 1867, the locus of critique was the extraction of value from human labor — the worker who creates more value than he is paid, and the system that makes that extraction structural rather than incidental. The political program that grew out of Marx's analysis varied enormously by country and decade, but the analytical core stayed where Marx put it: in the relationship between labor and capital.

What Marx did not name, and what his lineage has not named in the century and a half since, is the analogous extraction from the non-renewable resource itself. A barrel of oil sits in the ground for some hundreds of millions of years; it is brought up; it is liquidated; the surplus over the cost of extraction is distributed between the firm and the state. Marx's apparatus tells us a great deal about how that surplus is split between firm and worker. It tells us very little about the absence the barrel leaves behind. That absence is neither a labor cost nor a capitalist's profit. It is the resource itself, gone forever, owed to no one in the present and to everyone in the future. The analytical hole Marx leaves on this question is real, and it is not closed by any subsequent tradition in his lineage.

Note the move I have just made. I have placed Marx in the lineage map as one tradition among several. I am not endorsing his political program. I am noting where his analytical apparatus does and does not reach. The same discipline applies to the figures who come next.

Elinor Ostrom — who shared the 2009 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for her work on commons governance — gave the field something Marx did not. In *Governing the Commons*, published by Cambridge University Press in 1990, Ostrom identified eight design principles distinguishing successful from failed common-pool resource management. Fisheries that did not collapse. Forests that were not clearcut. Irrigation systems that did not dry. Her work was a careful empirical demolition of the Hardin-style "tragedy of the commons" pessimism that had dominated the field. Communities, she showed, were capable of governing shared resources without enclosure or central authority, provided certain conditions held.

But Ostrom's territory was renewable resources where sustained harvest was theoretically possible. The fishery can be fished forever if it is fished within its regenerative capacity. The forest can be cut forever if it is cut on a rotation. The aquifer can be drawn forever if it is drawn at recharge. Non-renewable resources sit outside that frame. There is no design principle for governing a fishery that will be entirely caught once; there is no rotation for a coal seam that will be entirely mined once. Ostrom told us a great deal about how renewable commons survive. She did not tell us what to do about the commons that will be liquidated.

Herman Daly, who died in 2022, came closer than anyone. Daly's *Steady-State Economics*, originally published by W. H. Freeman in 1977 and reissued by Island Press in 1991, framed the economy as a subsystem of the biosphere rather than the other way around. The economy lives inside the ecology; the ecology has finite capacity to absorb waste and finite capacity to regenerate stocks. Throughput — the flow of matter and energy from environment through economy and back — must stay within the regenerative limits of the biosphere or the system overshoots. A fishery taken faster than it regrows; an atmosphere given more carbon than it can absorb. The system that consumes faster than it regenerates eventually breaks. Daly's contribution was foundational, and the field that grew up around him (ecological economics, as Daly and Joshua Farley named it in their textbook of that title) has been doing serious work for half a century.

What Daly produced was a framework for thinking about throughput, stocks, flows, and limits. What he did not produce was a price for the permanent foreclosure of a specific non-renewable resource. Daly tells us we are overshooting. He does not tell us what the overshoot is worth in dollars, or what compensating instrument would close the accounting. The contribution and the gap are both real.

John Hartwick made it more nearly operational than anyone. In 1977, in a short paper in the *American Economic Review* (volume 67, issue 5, pages 972 through 974) titled "Intergenerational Equity and the Investing of Rents from Exhaustible Resources," Hartwick proved a result that resource economics has worked through ever since. The result, in plain language: if the rent earned on a non-renewable resource is fully reinvested in reproducible capital, consumption can be sustained indefinitely. Take the surplus from the oil; put it into roads, schools, factories, productive plant; the productive plant generates returns that replace the resource's contribution to consumption; the next generation does not have less because the previous generation drank from the well.

This is Hartwick's rule. Norway, more than any other country, has operationalized it. We will come to Norway in due course.

What Hartwick gives us is a rule about revenue. Reinvest the revenue from extraction, and consumption is preserved. What Hartwick does not give us — does not claim to give us — is a price for what was extracted as such. The hundred-dollar barrel was paid for; the revenue was reinvested; consumption flows forward. But the barrel is still gone. The hundred dollars captures present transaction value. It does not capture the worth of the barrel's permanent absence from the geological record of the planet. Hartwick is the closest the canon gets, and Hartwick stops at the revenue.

Four traditions: Marx, Ostrom, Daly, Hartwick. Each addressed part of what economics is for: who labors, who governs the commons, who lives inside the biosphere, who inherits what is left. None priced permanent foreclosure. And neither does the everyday model the rest of us reason inside — supply and demand at the moment of sale. Supply and demand clears the transaction between the parties present at it. Permanent foreclosure is not present at the moment of sale; the generation that pays for it is not seated at the table. The price clears without them. The decision gets made. The bill comes due in their lifetimes rather than ours.

## IV. Rawls Across Time

The piece that survives them all is a question of obligation across time. To name it requires a different lineage than the one just walked. It requires a tradition of moral philosophy.

In *A Theory of Justice*, published in 1971 by Harvard University Press, John Rawls described a thought-experiment that has shaped political philosophy for half a century. Imagine you are choosing the principles that will govern your society, but you are choosing from behind a veil that hides who you will turn out to be. You do not know whether you will be rich or poor, talented or struggling, healthy or sick. You do not know your race, your gender, your generation. Behind that veil, what would you agree to?

Rawls used the device to argue about distribution within a society. The argument applies, with no real strain, across time. A policy decided without knowing which generation you will be born into cannot endorse a system that makes your interests worth less because you arrive later. The unborn person who will live in 2080 has the same claim, behind the veil, as the person living now. You cannot, behind the veil, agree to a discount rate that values that future person's wellbeing at a hundredth of your own. You would not be sure who you were going to be.

That is Rawls applied across time. I am using Rawls as one frame among several that produce the same intuition. Other traditions converge on the same point. Michael Sandel, in *What Money Can't Buy: The Moral Limits of Markets*, published by Farrar, Straus and Giroux in 2012, has spent a career arguing that some goods cannot be priced without being corrupted by the pricing, and that markets have a moral domain beyond which their application produces distortion rather than allocation. Sandel's signature examples — the paid kidney donor, the wealthy parent who pays for the priority school admission slot — work because the pricing changes what the thing is, not just who can have it. The permanent foreclosure of a non-renewable resource sits squarely inside Sandel's territory. So does the question of whether discounting an unborn person's welfare is a category error rather than a calculation.

Derek Parfit, in *Reasons and Persons*, published by Oxford University Press in 1984, worked out the population-ethics arguments more carefully than anyone. Parfit's non-identity problem — the puzzle that the future people we harm by our present choices might not have existed at all under different choices — is a hard puzzle, and Parfit did not pretend to solve it. But Parfit's analysis pushes in the same direction as Rawls applied across time and as Sandel's market-moral-limits argument. There is something owed to people whose existence depends on our present choices, and the something is not adequately captured by a discount rate.

What I am not doing is deriving policy from Rawls. The Rawlsian intuition is a frame. So is Sandel's. So is Parfit's. They converge. The convergence is what does the work; the appeal to any single tradition does not. Readers who find Rawls unpersuasive should ask themselves whether they find Sandel and Parfit equally unpersuasive. The intuition that compounds across all three is what the essay relies on.

The intuition: a barrel of oil priced at a hundred dollars today is priced at less than the value of what is being permanently removed from the world. The gap between the market price and the price that would survive the veil — between the hundred dollars the present can find and the larger figure that obligation across time would require — is the gap I have been calling *residual commons value*.

Naming the gap is what makes solving it possible.

## V. The Existing Battleground

The argument just made — that there is something the market price systematically misses across generations — is not the argument that climate economics has had with itself for the last twenty years. The argument climate economics has had with itself is about discount rates. That is the existing intellectual battleground, and the framework I am sketching has to engage it directly.

In October 2006, Nicholas Stern delivered to the UK Treasury a review of the economics of climate change that ran to seven hundred pages and was published in book form by Cambridge University Press the following year. *The Economics of Climate Change: The Stern Review* concluded that climate change was, in Stern's well-known phrase, the greatest and widest-ranging market failure ever seen. The review's central recommendation was that around one percent of global gross domestic product per year should be spent on mitigation; failure to act would cost some multiple of that.

Stern's most contested move was a discount rate of roughly 1.4 percent. The number is low. It reflects a moral position more than an empirical one. Stern argued that the welfare of future generations should not be discounted simply because they are future; the only legitimate discount is for the small probability that the future will not exist. From that low discount rate followed a recommended social cost of carbon in the range of three hundred and sixty dollars per ton.

William Nordhaus, who received the 2018 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for integrating climate change into long-run macroeconomic analysis, has argued in the other direction. Nordhaus's DICE model (Dynamic Integrated Climate-Economy) has used a discount rate in the region of 4.3 percent in its early iterations, on the grounds that a credible cost-benefit analysis must reflect the rate at which the economy actually trades present for future — observed market rates of return on capital, not a rate chosen on first principles. From that higher rate followed a much lower social cost of carbon, in the range of thirty-five dollars per ton in the same comparison frame.

The numbers are doing enormous work. At Stern's rate, a hundred-dollar benefit a century from now is worth about twenty-five dollars today. At Nordhaus's, the same hundred dollars a century out is worth about a dollar and a half. The same future event, priced through the same equations, registers as either an emergency or a rounding error depending on which rate goes into the denominator.

Martin Weitzman, who died in 2019, opened a third front. In a 2009 paper in the *Review of Economics and Statistics* (volume 91, issue 1, pages 1 through 19), titled "On Modeling and Interpreting the Economics of Catastrophic Climate Change," Weitzman argued that in heavy-tailed distributions of climate damage, expected value is dominated by the tail; an analyst who prices only the body of the distribution systematically underestimates expected cost. The fat-tail tradition gave climate economics a way to engage catastrophic risk without first agreeing on a discount rate.

Stern, Nordhaus, Weitzman — three positions on the same field. The field is climate damage. The argument is how to price the damage that combustion produces. The discount rate determines how much weight present analysts put on future damage. The fat-tail tradition determines whether the analysts have looked at the right part of the distribution.

None of these three positions, however they differ from one another, prices the foreclosure of the resource as such. Stern and Nordhaus and Weitzman are all pricing the climate consequences of consuming the resource. They are not pricing the absence of the resource from the world for everyone who comes after. The resource has been treated, in this whole conversation, as a means of producing damage. It has not been treated as a thing that exists in its own right and that is permanently subtracted by extraction.

The framework I am sketching is not a fourth position on the discount rate but a structural observation: the underpricing it identifies survives even Stern's already-low rate. The argument is not that Stern is right and Nordhaus is wrong. The argument is that the climate-damage line of analysis, however it resolves its discount-rate question, does not address the line item this essay has been about. *Residual commons value* is a different gap. It needs different work.

## VI. Honest Accounting

A framework that names a gap and stops is half a framework. The question that follows — what would it look like to close the gap, in practice, in actual jurisdictions — is the next piece of work.

I will not pretend in this essay that I have produced the answer. The hard work of constructing a number an economist can defend and a policymaker can implement is the work of decades, not paragraphs. What can be shown is that the work has, in adjacent territory, already been started. The existence proof and the counter-case both happen to involve oil.

Norway found oil in December 1969. The discovery well was Ekofisk, in Block 2/4 of the North Sea, three days before Christmas. The country was small, the find was enormous, and the politicians of every party who happened to be in office during the development decades had a chance to spend the windfall on the present generation's appetites. Most countries with a comparable discovery have done exactly that. Norway, eventually, did not.

In 1990, the Norwegian parliament established the Government Petroleum Fund, into which the state's petroleum revenues were deposited. The fund was renamed the Government Pension Fund Global in 2006. Eleven years after the founding, in 2001, the Storting adopted a fiscal rule that limited annual government withdrawals to the fund's expected real return — initially set at four percent, lowered to three percent in 2017 as long-run return expectations declined. The capital itself stays inviolate. The rule was adopted under the first Stoltenberg government, which was Labour. It has been honored through every subsequent government. Bondevik II, a center-right coalition, kept it. Stoltenberg II, Labour again, kept it. Solberg, a Conservative-led coalition, kept it. Støre, the Labour-Centre coalition currently in office, has kept it. The fund holds, as of the most recent reporting, more than two trillion U.S. dollars. That is approaching four hundred thousand dollars for every Norwegian citizen alive.

What is worth noticing is not the fund's size but that no Norwegian government of any political color has unwound the rule. The architecture has survived political transitions that have changed every other element of Norwegian economic policy. Whatever the Norwegian electorate's preferences about taxation, healthcare, immigration, defense, or any other contested question, the principle that the resource's revenue belongs to the future rather than to the present has been treated as constitutional in a way nothing else has. Hartwick's rule is being run, at sovereign scale, by a country whose government changes hands like every other parliamentary democracy.

The counter-case is Nigeria. Commercial oil extraction in the Niger Delta began in the late 1950s. The major operators have been the international integrated firms — Shell, Chevron, ExxonMobil, Total — operating through joint ventures with the Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation. Over more than half a century the Delta has produced, by various estimates, more than thirty billion barrels of crude. Over the same span, the Delta has become one of the most polluted places on earth. The Ogoni crisis of the early 1990s culminated in the 1995 execution by the Abacha regime of the writer and environmental activist Ken Saro-Wiwa, along with eight of his colleagues. The Commonwealth suspended Nigeria's membership in protest. Subsequent insurgencies in the Delta have continued, in various forms, ever since.

In 2011, the United Nations Environment Programme published its environmental assessment of Ogoniland, the area most associated with the Saro-Wiwa case. The report concluded that full environmental restoration of the area would require an initial capitalization of around one billion U.S. dollars and a sustained effort over twenty-five to thirty years. That is the floor estimate. The actual costs are almost certainly higher. The costs were borne, over the half-century of extraction, almost entirely by the people who lived in the Delta. They were not absorbed by the companies that did the extraction or by the governments that licensed it. The revenue went elsewhere. The costs stayed. This structural pattern — the party that captures the value of an extraction is not the party that absorbs the burden, and there is no instrument that bills the costs back — is what the framework calls *cost severance*.

Norway and Nigeria are not natural opposites. They are two countries that found oil within roughly two decades of each other and made different institutional choices. Norway treated the resource revenue as something owed to the future and built a structure that held across political transitions. Nigeria did not. The choices were not predetermined by culture, geography, or moral character. They were institutional decisions made by political actors operating under particular constraints. The Norwegian institutions could fail tomorrow if a future government tore them down; the Nigerian institutions could be rebuilt if a future government chose. The choice is the thing.

What I am calling for, then, is not revolution. I do not think revolution solves this. The principle that whoever creates a cost should bear it — and that the cost of permanent foreclosure runs to the people who come next — is not a left-wing argument. It is not a right-wing argument. It is a principle of honest dealing. The framework I am sketching is named because nameless problems do not get solved. The instrument that closes the named gap will be built by careful institutional work, much of which already exists in adjacent traditions, none of which is complete.

## Close

What this essay has done is name a gap. It has not closed it. It has made something currently invisible, visible. The hard work of producing a number an economist can defend and a policymaker can implement is the work of decades, not paragraphs.

But a problem with no name does not get solved.

The lineage that has done the work on adjacent territory has been doing real work for centuries. Marx looked at the surplus and where it went. Ostrom showed how communities sustained renewable commons. Daly framed the biosphere as the system inside which the economy lives. Hartwick proved a rule for keeping consumption going when a resource runs out. Rawls, Sandel, and Parfit asked what is owed across time. Stern, Nordhaus, and Weitzman fought about how much climate damage is worth in present dollars.

Each of these traditions contributed something the framework I am sketching depends on. None of them named the worth of the resource's permanent absence from the world. That is the load-bearing piece they all left unbuilt. *Residual commons value* is its name. The architecture that prices it is the next century of careful institutional engineering. There is no shortcut. There is also no longer a missing word for what we are trying to build.
