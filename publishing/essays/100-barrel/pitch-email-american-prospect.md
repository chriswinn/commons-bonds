**Subject:** Pitch — "The $100 Barrel": what the discount-rate debate leaves unpriced

Dear Prospect editors,

I'd like to propose a long-form essay, "The $100 Barrel" (about 4,200 words, complete and ready to read), on a gap in how economics prices the resources we are now racing to pull out of the ground.

The argument begins with a simple observation. When a barrel of oil sells for a hundred dollars, that price is struck by and for the present; it measures present scarcity at the margin. What it cannot capture is the worth of the barrel's permanent absence to everyone who comes after, because the parties that worth is owed to are not at the table, and most of them are not yet born. I call that unpriced remainder the residual commons value, and the essay's central claim is structural: it sits entirely outside the discount-rate debate that has occupied climate economics for twenty years. Stern and Nordhaus, and Weitzman's work on fat-tailed catastrophe, are all pricing the damage flow from combustion, discounted back to the present. None of them prices the resource's absence as such, and that absence survives even Stern's low rate, because it is not a future flow to be discounted but a fact about the present.

Why it would be compelling for the Prospect's readers: this is, finally, a political-economy story about who bears a cost and who escapes the bill. The essay grounds its practical half in two oil economies that made the same physical extraction run through opposite architectures. Norway built a sovereign fund (now well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand per citizen), held inviolate across every government for a generation — Hartwick's rule made real, the proceeds of a finite thing converted into something that outlasts it. The Niger Delta ran the identical act through an architecture that severed the costs onto people who could not bill anyone for them. The difference was not geology. It was a choice about who absorbs the remainder, and that choice was institutional, which means it was available to both. The essay engages the literature by name rather than around it — the lineage that has circled this gap without building on it (Marx on the surplus, Ostrom on the renewable commons, Daly on throughput, Hartwick on reinvested rents), and the claim of future generations drawn from Rawls, Sandel, and Parfit.

How it fits the current political moment: the energy transition has opened a new extraction frontier — critical minerals, lithium and cobalt and rare earths, the early push toward deep-sea mining — and it is being financed and permitted on exactly the accounting that left the oil column blank. The same severance is being built into the supply chains of the green economy in real time. The essay's frame is architectural rather than partisan on purpose: the principle that whoever creates a cost should bear it is one a conservative and a progressive can both reach, and Norway is the proof that they have. But the policy weight of that principle — who owes what to whom, and which institutions make the bill collectible — lands squarely in the Prospect's lane, in the company of the political-economy and accountability reporting you publish.

A note on me: I am a nursing student at Virginia Peninsula Community College and a former healthcare-IT executive whose career spanned the National Institutes of Health during the Human Genome Project, federal research and consulting, and the CEO seat of a healthcare-IT firm. My forthcoming book, *Commons Bonds*, develops a formal framework for pricing the intergenerational costs that resource extraction severs from the populations that bear them — the framework this essay applies to oil. I live aboard a sailboat in Hampton, Virginia.
