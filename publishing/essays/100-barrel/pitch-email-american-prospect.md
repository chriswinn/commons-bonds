**Subject:** Pitch — "The $100 Barrel": what the discount-rate debate leaves unpriced

Dear Prospect editors,

I'd like to propose a long-form essay, "The $100 Barrel" (about 4,600 words, complete and ready to read), on a gap in how economics prices the resources we are now racing to pull out of the ground.

The argument begins with a simple observation. When a barrel of oil sells for a hundred dollars, that price is struck by and for the present; it measures present scarcity at the margin. What it cannot capture is the worth of the barrel's permanent absence to everyone who comes after, because the parties that worth is owed to are not at the table, and most of them are not yet born. I call that unpriced remainder the residual commons value, and the essay's central claim is structural: it sits entirely outside the discount-rate debate that has occupied climate economics for twenty years. Stern and Nordhaus, and Weitzman's work on fat-tailed catastrophe, are all pricing the damage flow from combustion, discounted back to the present. None of them prices the resource's absence as such, and that absence survives even Stern's low rate, because it is not a future flow to be discounted but a fact about the present.

Why it would be compelling for the Prospect's readers: this is, at bottom, a political-economy story about who bears a cost and who escapes the bill. Its claim is not that extraction is unjust — that diagnosis is a century old, and the essay walks the lineage that built it (Marx on the surplus, Ostrom on the renewable commons, Daly on throughput, Hartwick on reinvested rents). The claim is that the discipline has never put a number on the specific thing that is lost when a finite resource is liquidated for good, and that the number survives even the most future-friendly discount rate the field has on offer — so it does not depend on winning the Stern–Nordhaus fight that has occupied climate economics for twenty years. The contribution is the magnitude, not the mechanism. The essay grounds the practical half in two oil economies that ran the same physical extraction through opposite architectures. Norway built a sovereign fund (now well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand per citizen), held inviolate across every government for a generation — the proceeds of a finite thing converted into something that outlasts it. The Niger Delta ran the identical act through an architecture that severed the costs onto people who could not bill anyone for them. The difference was not geology; it was a choice about who absorbs the remainder, and the choice was institutional, which means it was available to both. The future generations whose claim the price ignores are drawn from Rawls, Sandel, and Parfit, who reach the same conclusion from three different starting points. The essay closes on the deeper structure the whole argument rests on: that the foreclosed barrel, the poisoned delta, the dispossessed community, and the unborn generation are one thing and not four, a shared inheritance the market treats as a commodity it was never made to be. That is what Karl Polanyi, and Nancy Fraser after him, diagnosed but never priced, and it points to two missing numbers rather than one — the cost of the damage already done, and the cost of the damage still to come.

How it fits the current political moment: the title is closer to the news than I would like. This year's oil shock briefly sent crude past a hundred dollars before it eased back, and the windfall flowed, as it always does, to whoever owns the spigot — a live test of which countries built the escrow and which did not, the question behind your own recent reporting on Guyana's resource fund. At the same time the energy transition has opened a new extraction frontier — lithium, cobalt, rare earths, the early push toward deep-sea mining — and it is being financed and permitted on exactly the accounting that left the oil column blank. The same severance is being built into the supply chains of the green economy in real time. The essay's frame is architectural rather than partisan on purpose: the principle that whoever creates a cost should bear it is robust enough that even Norway's conservatives have defended it for a generation — which is the strongest evidence I know that the accountability it asks for is durable rather than a passing wish. The policy weight of that principle — who owes what to whom, and which institutions make the bill collectible — lands squarely in the Prospect's lane, in the company of the political-economy and accountability reporting you publish.

A note on me: I am a nursing student at Virginia Peninsula Community College and a former healthcare-IT executive whose career spanned the National Institutes of Health during the Human Genome Project, federal research and consulting, and the CEO seat of a healthcare-IT firm. My forthcoming book, *Commons Bonds*, develops a formal framework for pricing the intergenerational costs that resource extraction severs from the populations that bear them: the framework this essay applies to oil. I live aboard a sailboat in Hampton, Virginia.

*Disclosure:* the book this essay derives from was developed in collaboration with Claude (Anthropic) as a research and rigor-testing partner, across the framework's mathematical formalization, cross-scenario stress testing, and structural editing. I offer this proactively, in keeping with the honest-accounting discipline the essay itself argues for.

The full essay is finished and I'd be glad to send it along; it's ready whenever you'd like to read it. Thank you for considering it.
