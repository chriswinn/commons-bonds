## 5. Prose-Flow Lessons Learned (read before drafting)

The session that produced this handoff hit prose-quality issues. Causes diagnosed; mitigations below.

**What degrades prose:**
1. Self-imposing tight word targets at draft time (compresses out rhythmic breathing).
2. Treating Path B as a draft-time tax (forces clunky near-paraphrases of sentences that already work).
3. Compressing many source paragraphs into one essay paragraph.
4. Late-in-session context load (post-summary, long conversations).

**Drafting discipline for this work:**
- **Draft long, cut later.** Past the target. Trim at polish.
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Let some sentences run 25-30 words; let others land at 5. Counters declarative-three-in-a-row.
- **Read each paragraph aloud before showing.** Flag clunky cadence. Self-edit for rhythm.
- **Preserve paragraph-level breathing.** If Ch 1 covers a beat in three paragraphs, the essay version may need two — not always one.
- **Path B audit is a SEPARATE later pass.** Draft for flow with echoes allowed; vary surgically at polish.
- **One constraint at a time.** Don't try to solve content + voice + length + Path B simultaneously.

**Author's voice register (from prior ratifications):**
- First person, plain, unsentimental.
- Short paragraphs OK but vary sentence length within them.
- Italicize remembered direct speech and key terms (em-dash + italics for spoken lines).
- Avoid emojis, avoid LLM tics (rule of three, em-dash crutches, tidy parallels, "in short" / "ultimately" connective tissue).
- Specific concrete details over abstract claims (recliner, drill bits, fifty-eight countries).

### Section I — full v2 draft (~620w)

By the time we cleared the cloud line, the sun was up behind us. The first flights out of Savannah leave at five forty-five, and I had taken so many of them by then that I had developed a discipline about it: never fly in the night before a meeting, because every airline delay stacks on every other one until the crews time out and the last flights of the day go nowhere. First flights almost always leave on time, and weather around them costs you twenty minutes at most. I was flying north — Philadelphia, or Boston; two deals that had been dormant for months had both come back to life over the same weekend. My garment bag was overhead. My grip was under the seat in front of me. The grip is leather, from the age of steam locomotives, and it had carried me through most of the fifty-eight countries I have worked in by now. It holds a laptop and enough essentials for two weeks of meetings before laundry becomes a crisis.

I am a daydreamer, and have been for as long as I can remember. A plane window at sunrise is one of those places that makes the daydreaming worse, or maybe better — I am never sure which. Half of me is in the cabin. The other half has slipped sideways into something that might have happened decades ago, or might be happening on the ground a few hundred miles below me, or might be something I do not yet have a name for.

That morning, what I was thinking about was my son.

He was in a NICU in Savannah. He had been born in August when his due date had been October, and he would stay in the unit for the better part of two months. The nurses had told me, almost apologetically, that babies suffered when they went without being held — they tried, they always tried, but there were too many babies and too few hands, and could I come and lie back in the recliner beside his bed and let them set him on my chest, and let him feel the weight of another person against him. There was nothing in the world more important to me than giving him that. He was forming the first connection he would ever form with another human being. I came every day I could. In practice that was five days out of seven. Some days the nurses were too overwhelmed to have me in the room.

The thinking, as it does on planes, slipped backward.

I thought of the mornings my father drove me into the Appalachians when I was a boy. He did not talk much on those drives. He sat beside me in the dark, the headlights laying down the empty road in front of us, and we would go deep into the mountains before the sun came up. He was teaching me to hunt. We rarely brought anything home. He would point out the birds. He would name the trees. Every once in a while, almost wistfully, he would say — *the best part of any trip is the journey.* When I was young I thought he was talking about the scenery. Later I thought he was offering some kind of Stoic counsel about not being too fixed on the goal. It was not until we had stopped hunting, years afterward, that I understood. He was talking about getting to spend time with me.

That morning on the plane, the recliner beside my son's NICU bed sat empty. The nurses kept the routines — they fed him, they cleaned him, they did the same for every other baby in the unit — but the holding he needed was not part of any routine, and there was never enough staff for it. I thought about my own father in a way I had not before. Every father knows this math. Come what may, you are there for your family. My father had gotten that part. I did not yet have a name for what pulled me away.

### Section II — Pooh / Father / NIH — full v2 draft (~870w)

The man my son would never meet was my grandfather. They called him Pooh — a detail my father did not share with me until decades after the fact. The first story I knew about him was the air compressor.

He had built a workshop behind his house and spent the second half of his life there, mostly. The first half he spent at NACA, the agency that became NASA in 1958 when Eisenhower signed the act, at the installation in Langley, Virginia. They tested how things fell. They wanted to know what the shape of an object did to its impact zone, what different materials did under impact load, where things gave way and where they held. He was a model maker in the wind tunnels, and his name sat on two patents in the federal record. My father told me, more than once, that he had a way of solving things other people would walk past, give up on, or simply avoid because they were too hard.

The compressor story went like this. My father stopped by the workshop one afternoon and noticed the air compressor was not in its usual place. In a shop where every tool had an assigned spot, that detail registered. He asked to borrow a drill bit. My grandfather said the bits were worn — not ready to use — and, almost as an afterthought, that the compressor's engine had given out. A week or two later my father came by again. The compressor was running. He asked what had happened. My grandfather, without turning his head, said he had machined new pistons and new rings and rebuilt the engine. He nodded toward the drill bits. *Those are good to go now.* He had sharpened them. He didn't think anything of it.

Years later, my sister had a son. He was at the age where he was learning everyone's names. He pointed at her and said *momma*. He pointed at me and said *Uncle Chris*. He pointed at my father and tried to say *Pappou*, the Greek word for grandfather. What came out of his mouth was *Pou*. My father got a particular kind of smile on his face, and something in his eye that I did not at the time understand, and he nodded. *Pou it is*, he said. He did not explain anything to anyone in the room. I would not learn for another six or eight years that his own father had been called Pooh.

I learned to work from my father. He had a way of giving advice that was almost not advice. When I was a boy he would say — even if I decided to dig ditches for a living — that at the end of the day I should be able to look at my work and know in my heart that no one had ever done a better job of it. He said the pride in your own work was what made the hard days a little easier; he said knowing you did a good job is often enough, even when no one else will ever see what your contribution was. He had a way of asking me, before he came to look at something I had been doing, whether I would be proud of what he was about to see. He did not need me to answer. The question did the work.

He was a naval architect by trade, a civil servant employed by the Navy. He held a high clearance and traveled on a political passport. He was tight-lipped about what he actually did day to day. He sat in rooms with captains and admirals. From the vague stories he was willing to share, what I learned was never about subject matter; it was about how to hold a room of large personalities. He never raised his voice. He spoke a hair below the room's volume, only when he had something worth saying, and waited for the room to come to him. The reaction in a room of loud, talking adults when somebody with something worth listening to begins to speak just below the room's volume is a thing I have used my entire career.

I started my IT career at the National Institutes of Health, twenty-two years old, on what was supposed to be a three-month contract. The Human Genome Project was completing during my time there. My job sat downstream of the science: take rigorous research, run it through peer review, and put the same paper in front of fifteen distinct readerships at once — the senior investigators in the field on one end, a child living with the genetic issue on the other. There is a particular look on a child's face when she realizes that maybe one day a child will not have to be sick the way she has been sick. I saw that look. It changed me. It humbled me. It made the work feel like the kind of work worth not sleeping for.

The first project I was assigned had been scoped for forty-five people, one year, a million dollars. By the time I walked in, it had spent two and a half million and was twelve months overdue, and the team was preparing to ask for more time, more money, and more people. I cut everything that was not aligned with the original scope. By month three I had a working proof of concept. The renewals kept coming.

That was where I first learned that a particular kind of grinding — the willingness to ask the question nobody else had thought to ask, to work the seams the documentation had not covered — could carry an adult through weeks of unsleeping work until something workable came into focus. The lesson was already in me. My father had put it there. The NIH was where it set.
