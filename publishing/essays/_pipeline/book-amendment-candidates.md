# Book-amendment candidates (essay→book flow)

Per Amendment E §3.1 (the X.6 essay→book sub-cascade): when essay-side work
surfaces a formulation sharper than, or a change relevant to, the book's
treatment, it is **logged here as a candidate** rather than auto-applied. The
full six-step sub-cascade fires only when a batch is worked, and only for
candidates that clear the hard threshold:

- **(a)** makes a load-bearing claim more rigorous OR more clearly expressed, AND
- **(b)** the book's current treatment does not already convey what it adds, AND
- **(c)** propagation does not break cross-chapter coherence.

**Class-boundary flag (load-bearing):** book chapters are **end-user-facing
prose** — any application here is governed by **merge-on-ratification**, NOT the
auto-merge that governs scaffolding. Nothing in this log touches the book
without an explicit author-ratified book-amendment session.

## Candidate 1 — Four-traditions gloss: Marx → Polanyi (or reconsider)

- **Surfaced:** Atlantic Ideas §7-5 cut/focus, 2026-06-02 (Cut-phase trigger).
- **Essay-side decision (applied to the Atlantic essay only):** the
  four-traditions gloss replaced **Marx** ("who labors") with **Polanyi**
  ("what happens when labor and land are treated as ordinary commodities").
  Rationale: for the Atlantic essay's center-right dispositive audience, Marx is
  the most charged element of the gloss AND the least load-bearing for an essay
  about *extraction* costs (vs labor). Polanyi keeps the human-cost dimension,
  carries equivalent heft, and maps cleanly onto cost-severance without
  tripping the communism reflex.
- **Book state:** the canonical four-traditions taxonomy (Marx / Ostrom / Daly /
  Hartwick) appears in Ch 9 and likely recurs across chapters + the
  bibliography. (Verify all occurrences before any book-side action.)
- **Threshold assessment:**
  - (a) NOT a rigor/clarity gain — it is *audience-attack-surface* reduction,
    which is venue-specific, not a universal improvement.
  - (b) The book uses Marx deliberately, and a book can carry Marx more safely
    than a center-right-targeted Atlantic essay (different audience math).
  - (c) The taxonomy recurs cross-chapter; changing it is a cross-chapter
    coherence operation, not a one-line edit.
- **Author signal (2026-06-02):** leaning toward removing Marx from the book
  too — "its attack is highly likely and I'm guessing well funded so no reason
  to widen our attack surface for zero gain." Not yet a decision.
- **Recommendation:** **DO NOT auto-propagate.** Hold for an explicit
  book-amendment session. If pursued, it is a cross-chapter change (identify all
  Marx/four-traditions occurrences → amend → cross-chapter coherence check →
  brief refresh where any §7 anchor moves → sibling-essay alignment) requiring
  author ratification per the class-boundary flag.
- **Status:** LOGGED — author-leaning-toward; deferred to a future
  book-amendment session.

## Candidate 2 — The "ethical line I will not cross" deferral on pricing coercion

- **Surfaced:** Atlantic Ideas §7-5 cut/focus, 2026-06-02 (author direction,
  resolving the reparations/longevity attack vector).
- **The move (applied to the Atlantic essay):** after citing Darity & Mullen's
  $14T wealth-deficit and the JAMA longevity finding as *quantifications* of the
  cost of inequality, the essay's voice steps forward once, in the first person,
  to mark a boundary it will not cross: *"Putting a price on the costs of
  coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not mine to do, and I leave
  that work to those who are continuing it. What the framework offers is the
  ledger. What belongs on it, looking backward, is theirs to enter."* It is
  paired with the symmetry framing (the accounting runs forward and backward),
  so reparations reads as one symmetric application of a general principle, not
  the essay's agenda.
- **Why it earns a look in the book:** it does triple duty — defuses the
  "Trojan horse for reparations" attack by *declining* to prescribe; honors the
  Darity relationship by crediting his quantification while deferring the
  coercion-pricing he himself has said he has not attempted; and states a
  principled authorial boundary (not speaking for / pricing a harm to a group
  the author is not part of). The author flagged it as feeling like it has "a
  very earned place in the book."
- **Threshold assessment:**
  - (a) Clarity/rigor gain: yes — it sharpens the framework's *scope claim*
    (the ledger is general; what goes on it, looking backward, is for those
    affected to enter) and adds an integrity move the book currently may not
    state explicitly.
  - (b) Does the book already convey it? Unverified — check whether the book
    already draws this boundary; if not, it is additive.
  - (c) Cross-chapter coherence: the book's first-person register differs from
    the essay's; a first-person ethical aside would need to fit the book's
    voice (Ch 9 or wherever the reparations/backward-looking accounting lives).
- **Recommendation:** **DO NOT auto-propagate.** Hold for the same
  book-amendment session as Candidate 1 (they are related — both concern the
  book's handling of the backward-looking / inequality material). The book's
  audience math differs, and the first-person move must be fitted to the book's
  voice. Author-ratified book-amendment session required (class-boundary:
  chapter prose = merge-on-ratification).
- **Status:** LOGGED — author flagged as likely-earned; deferred.
