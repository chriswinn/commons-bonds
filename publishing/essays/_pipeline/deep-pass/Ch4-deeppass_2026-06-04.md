## 1. The chapter's irreducible job / spine

Ch4 is the book's **existence proof** and its **differentiation proof**. Its irreducible job has exactly three load-bearing moves:

1. **Cost severance is a policy choice, not a natural law.** Same barrel, same ocean, same market → radically different community outcomes. The variable is institutional architecture, which is engineered and therefore re-engineerable. (The chapter's title claim.)
2. **The framework differentiates correctly.** Run residual commons value against the *best-managed* extraction regime on Earth and it returns a small-but-nonzero cost severance; run it against the worst and it returns an enormous one. A framework that called both "disaster" would be useless. This is what makes it a **policy instrument, not a moral gesture** — the single most important sentence in the chapter (line 90).
3. **Even Norway has not solved it.** The best case still leaves permanent foreclosure, the externality tail, the global-distributional problem, and Intergenerational Option Value unpriced — which is precisely the agenda the *remaining* chapters carry. Norway proves the gap can be narrowed by orders of magnitude; it does not prove anyone has closed it.

Everything else in the chapter — Alberta/Alaska, the NBIM/ethics-council/Storting mechanism inventory, the Social Security comparison, the Bismarck digression, the substitution-application catalog — is **supporting apparatus around those three moves**. The spine is a contingency argument (it could be otherwise) bracketed by a calibration argument (the tool discriminates) and a humility argument (even the best is partial). The chapter is currently ~30% longer than that spine requires.

**One-line spine:** *Norway proves cost severance is an engineered choice and the framework can tell a good architecture from a bad one — and even the best architecture leaves the deepest costs unpriced, which is the rest of the book's job.*

## 7. OBJECTIONS — engaged vs exposed

**Engaged (well):**
- **Norway-not-transferable** — engaged head-on and *judo-flipped* (lines 62–72): "this objection is correct, and it strengthens the argument" → the mechanism preys on weak institutions *by selection*, so weak-institution communities are the reason to build defenses deliberately, not abandon the project. This is the chapter's best objection-handling and a model for the rest of the book.
- **International-macro / local-optimum** — engaged explicitly (line 122): "if every country built a Norway-sized fund there would not be enough external economies to invest them in… a local optimum, not a universal template." Rare and credible self-limitation.
- **Unfalsifiable** — *implicitly* engaged (line 88): the framework makes a discriminating prediction (small CS for Norway, large for Nigeria) rather than a one-size verdict. Convert to explicit (see §5-L).
- **Just-a-moral-gesture / ESG-reformist-capture** — engaged via line 90 ("policy instrument rather than a moral gesture") and the differentiation result.
- **Too-statist (partial)** — *partially* engaged: the chapter is careful that Norway "does not say the U.S. should adopt Norway's architecture" (line 106) and "the framework's reading is structural and not prescriptive." Good defensive posture against the statist read.

**Exposed (left open):**
- **Financialization / Pistor — bonds extend commodification (the GAP).** The chapter's entire argument is "build a *financial instrument* (a sovereign fund) to capture commons rents." The obvious objection — that financializing a commons is *itself* the Pistor commodification move, and that Norway's fund compounds on the global economy's surplus (a point the chapter *itself raises* at line 122!) — is exposed. The chapter names the tension ("a portion of the return… is the reinvested surplus generated by workers in other jurisdictions") and does not answer it. This is the matrix's flagged GAP and Ch4 is where it most naturally bites.
- **Cost-severance-metaphor-commodifies-labor / Fraser-absent.** No engagement (not this chapter's natural home, but the human-commons equivalence is nowhere here — Norway is purely a natural-resource case, so the reef/labor/future-generation "same object" meta-claim is invisible in Ch4).
- **Coase / future-generations-cannot-bid.** Adjacent but unstated. The chapter says future generations "do not regain access" (line 52) — the perfect setup for the Coase-contradiction (future generations cannot participate in the bargain), but the chapter never names that the voluntary-trade objection fails *because the affected party cannot be at the table.*
- **Simon-Ehrlich substitution-optimism.** The foreclosure argument (line 52) is *exactly* the rebuttal to substitution-optimism — "no substitute exists for these applications" — but it's not framed as answering Simon-Ehrlich. A named engagement here would be high-value and cheap.
- **Discount-rate-fight.** Not engaged (correctly deferred to Ch6/Ch8).
- **Public-Choice / capture (partial).** Partially engaged — the NBIM-at-arms-length + supermajority + public-ethics-council inventory (line 36) is *implicitly* a capture-resistance argument, but it's never named as answering the Public-Choice objection ("why won't politicians loot it?"). The chapter has the answer in hand and doesn't badge it.

Recommended landing: **~3,200 words (Tier-2 with two carve-backs)**, plus the four cheap named-positioning additions and the F-1 definitional fix.
