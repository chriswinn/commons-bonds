# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 5: The Accountability Gap

The pattern repeats with such mechanical precision that once you learn to see it, you cannot stop seeing it. A company or an institution extracts value from a resource, a community, or a system. The costs generated by that extraction are severed from the party that benefited and pushed onto someone else, usually someone with less power, less mobility, less ability to refuse the transfer. And when the reckoning comes, when the true scale of the severed costs becomes impossible to ignore, the original beneficiary is somehow no longer available to pay.

The reckoning always comes. The only question is who carries it.

This chapter traces that pattern across six cases separated by geography, by industry, and by decades, yet united by a single underlying mechanism. A vermiculite mine in Montana. An oil platform in the Gulf of Mexico. A rare earth processing complex in Inner Mongolia. A national financial system that transferred the same risk twice in 2008. The retirement promise meant to be America's most sacred compact between generations, the Social Security system itself. And a healthcare system that now routes its shortfalls through online fundraising campaigns and unpaid family labor during the most vulnerable stretches of life.

Each case tells the same story. The arithmetic is different. The mechanism is the same.

A word about what this chapter is doing, and what it is not. It is not diagnosing a new pathology. Scholars before this book named the disease. What this chapter adds is measurement: how large the severed cost actually is, counted conservatively, in figures that have survived courts and settlements. The value added here is magnitude, not mechanism. The naming of the pattern as the framework's two-instrument response, and the lineages that name belongs to, comes in the chapter's second half. The cases come first.

### Libby, Montana

The W.R. Grace Company operated a vermiculite mine in Libby, Montana, for twenty-seven years, from 1963 to 1990. The mine produced roughly eighty percent of the world's vermiculite, a mineral used for insulation and soil conditioning. The operation was profitable, generating an estimated one hundred million dollars in lifetime revenue. Grace marketed the product aggressively. Zonolite insulation filled the walls of an estimated thirty-five million American homes.

What Grace did not advertise, what Grace actively concealed, was that the vermiculite in Libby was contaminated with a particularly lethal form of asbestos.

The company knew. Internal documents released during later litigation showed that Grace had identified the contamination as early as 1963 and had measured asbestos levels in its workers throughout the mine's operation. The company knew that its workers were developing lung disease at rates that defied statistical explanation. It knew that the families of workers, exposed to dust carried home on clothing, were developing mesothelioma and asbestosis. It knew that children who played near the mine's waste piles were inhaling fibers that would kill them decades later.

And it mined for twenty-seven more years.

The human toll is still being counted. Over four hundred residents of Libby have died from asbestos-related disease. An estimated three thousand have been diagnosed with lung abnormalities consistent with asbestos exposure. The mortality rate from asbestosis in Libby ran forty to eighty times the national average. The Environmental Protection Agency declared the entire town a public health emergency, the first time it had ever made such a declaration for a disaster of human making. [CITATION GAP: Libby mortality 40–80× national average; 400+ dead; ~3,000 lung abnormalities — needs ATSDR / EPA / peer-reviewed source for publisher-facing prose.]

The financial reckoning tells the story of cost severance in numbers that cannot be argued with. Grace's mine generated roughly one hundred million dollars in revenue over its lifetime. The documented costs, including medical care for the sick and the dying, environmental remediation of the contaminated town, legal settlements, lost property values, and ongoing health monitoring, exceed four billion dollars. The cost-to-revenue ratio is forty to one, at minimum. For every dollar Grace extracted, Libby and its residents absorbed forty dollars in costs that Grace never paid. [CITATION GAP: Libby ~$4B documented costs (denominator of the 40:1 ratio) — needs EPA/ATSDR or litigation-record source; $100M revenue figure likewise.]

W.R. Grace declared bankruptcy in 2001, eleven years after the mine closed. The company's assets were reorganized. Its shareholders and executives moved on. Libby is still there, still dying, still counting its dead.

The law's response to Libby is its own case study in cost severance. In 2005 the United States Department of Justice obtained a federal grand jury indictment of W.R. Grace and seven of its senior executives, charging conspiracy to violate the Clean Air Act and to obstruct the federal investigation. It was the largest environmental criminal prosecution in American history at the time, and it named specific executives by name and by role. In 2009, after a thirteen-week trial, every defendant was acquitted. The acquittal does not mean the contamination did not happen, or that the executives did not know, or that the cost severance described above did not occur. It means the criminal law was not built to reach the case the prosecution was trying to make. Criminal law prosecutes individual acts, identifiable knowledge of identifiable harm at identifiable moments. Cost severance is a structural mechanism that operates across decades through accumulated decisions, and its individual-act elements are hard to isolate at the threshold criminal guilt requires. The framework's accounting does not need that threshold. It works at the structural level the criminal law could not reach. The four hundred dead, the three thousand with lung disease, the town under emergency declaration: these are the full record, and the law's failure to reach them is exactly what the framework's accounting is built to make visible.

### Deepwater Horizon

On the twentieth of April, 2010, the Deepwater Horizon offshore drilling platform exploded in the Gulf of Mexico, killing eleven workers and releasing an estimated 4.9 million barrels of oil into Gulf waters. The spill continued for eighty-seven days while British Petroleum, the platform's operator, attempted to cap a well that had been drilled through formations BP's own engineers had described as nightmare geology.

BP had extracted substantial value from the Gulf's offshore resources. In the five years before the disaster, the company's Gulf operations generated roughly sixty billion dollars in revenue. The Macondo well itself, the specific well that blew out, was projected to produce forty to sixty million barrels of oil over its lifetime, worth between three and four billion dollars at 2010 prices.

The spill severed costs in every form cost severance can take. Across space: the oil that had generated revenue for a British company, refined in Texas, sold across North America, poisoned the waters where Louisiana fishermen had worked for generations. Across time: the damage to Gulf ecosystems persisted long after BP's quarterly reports had moved on to other projects. Across the living world: dolphins, sea turtles, and fish populations absorbed toxins that would affect reproduction and survival for decades.

The economic costs alone tell the story. BP ultimately paid twenty-one billion dollars in federal and state fines and settlements, the largest environmental penalty in United States history. The company paid an additional twenty billion dollars into a compensation fund for affected businesses and individuals. Its total documented cost approached sixty-five billion dollars.

Even that number understates the true cost. The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration estimated that the spill killed four to eight billion oysters and two to five trillion newly hatched fish, along with tens of thousands of birds and sea turtles that would have supported Gulf fisheries and ecosystems for years. Tourism revenue losses in affected areas exceeded seven billion dollars in the three years following the spill. Property values along affected coastlines declined by an estimated twelve billion dollars. Aggregate estimates from academic, insurance-industry, and federal sources place total economic losses at over one hundred and fifty billion dollars. [CITATION GAP: Deepwater ~$150B total economic losses — needs the specific academic / insurance-industry / federal sources named; this figure is the denominator of the load-bearing 40% recovery ratio.]

BP's sixty billion dollars in penalties against one hundred and fifty billion dollars in total costs yields a recovery ratio of forty percent. The company captured the full value of the oil it extracted. It paid less than half the cost of extracting it. The framework's accountability target is one hundred percent, extractors paying the full residual cost. Deepwater paid forty. That figure is the documented, court-validated, settlement-ratified magnitude of the accountability gap for one of the largest environmental disasters in American history. It is worth pausing on what kind of number this is. It is not modeled. It is not estimated by the framework. It is what BP itself paid, set against losses that courts, agencies, and insurers independently tallied. When the chapter says the gap is empirically large, this is the kind of evidence it means.

The asymmetry also crossed a border. The Macondo spill reached Mexican Gulf waters, fouling Yucatán coastlines, fisheries, and tourism economies. BP's 2018 settlement with the Mexican federal government totaled roughly twenty-five million dollars, three orders of magnitude smaller than the United States settlements for comparable damage per mile of coastline. The slick did not distribute itself unevenly. The legal architecture did. American Gulf-state claimants had a strong toolkit. Mexican claimants, pressing cross-border environmental claims against a foreign-domiciled corporation, did not. Same spill, same operator, same physical damage, structurally different bonds posted. [CITATION GAP: Deepwater Mexico settlement ~$25M, 2018 — needs a source; "three orders of magnitude smaller" is a strong comparative claim.]

And these calculations exclude entirely the costs no one can monetize: the generational knowledge lost when family fishing operations went under, the cultural rupture when communities that had lived from the Gulf for centuries could no longer trust their waters, the ecological relationships severed when keystone populations collapsed. These are real costs. Real people absorbed them. They simply never appeared on any ledger BP was required to balance.

### Baotou, Inner Mongolia

The city of Baotou, in China's Inner Mongolia region, produces roughly sixty percent of the world's rare earth elements, the seventeen metals whose magnetic and catalytic properties underwrite nearly every piece of advanced electronics on Earth. The rare earths in your phone came from Baotou. The rare earths in the wind turbine that generated some of today's electricity came from Baotou. The rare earths in the electric vehicle that represents our best current plan for decarbonizing transportation came from Baotou.

Extracting and processing rare earths generates enormous quantities of toxic waste. For every ton of rare earth oxides produced, the process generates roughly ten tons of wastewater laced with heavy metals, acids, and radioactive material. In Baotou this waste flows into artificial lakes that have grown over decades of industrial activity. Residents near these tailings ponds report elevated rates of cancer, respiratory disease, and birth defects. Agricultural land within ten kilometers of the processing facilities is no longer fit for farming. [CITATION GAP: Baotou ~10 tons wastewater per ton oxide; 60% of world rare earths — needs source.]

The companies operating in Baotou, a mix of Chinese state-owned enterprises and private firms, have extracted enormous value from global demand. China's rare earth industry generates an estimated ten to twelve billion dollars in annual revenue, with Baotou facilities accounting for roughly half of that total. The value flows out through global supply chains to electronics manufacturers, carmakers, and renewable energy firms whose products depend entirely on Baotou's rare earths.

The costs have been severed in every direction. Across space: the toxic waste that makes Silicon Valley's technology possible sits in lakes in Inner Mongolia, and the engineers designing devices in California never see the radioactive tailings their products require. Across time: the companies buying rare earth feedstock pay today's extraction price, while the health and environmental costs persist for decades or centuries. Across society: the value flows to shareholders and consumers in wealthy economies, while the costs settle on rural Chinese communities with minimal political voice.

The numbers are harder to pin down here than in Montana or the Gulf, because the Chinese government restricts access to health and environmental data from the region. Some peer-reviewed work has nonetheless been published. Environmental epidemiology studies have documented elevated heavy-metal concentrations, including lead, cadmium, thorium, and uranium, in soil, water, and resident biomarkers within ten kilometers of the Baotou tailings ponds, along with elevated rates of cancer and reproductive abnormality consistent with the chemical and radiological exposure. The published evidence is partial in one direction; the unpublished evidence is partial in the other. Even conservative estimates suggest that long-term remediation of Baotou's rare earth legacy will run into the tens of billions of dollars, and possibly far higher. [CITATION GAP: Baotou specific peer-reviewed studies — earlier draft named journals (*Environmental Pollution*; *Ecotoxicology and Environmental Safety*; *Science of the Total Environment*) without specific papers, which reads as citation-theater; either cite specific papers or keep the remediation magnitude as a soft band. The prior "$100B+ remediation" claim is HIGH-RISK numbers-are-invented exposure and has been softened to a band pending a source.]

Against roughly five to six billion dollars a year in extracted value, the severed costs accumulate at a rate that dwarfs what the industry has ever paid or set aside. Globally, the rare earth industry spends less than one percent of its revenue on environmental remediation or community health. [CITATION GAP: rare earth industry <1% of revenue on remediation — needs source.]

The gadgets work. The turbines spin. The electric vehicles charge. The costs gather in Inner Mongolia, where the rest of the world does not have to see them. And the consumer at the far end of the supply chain is not a villain in this account. The framework's mechanism does not require villains. The consumer shares in the extracted value and is structurally separated from the cost-bearer by the supply chain itself. That separation, not any individual's preferences, is what lets the architecture keep operating.

### 2008 and the Double Risk Transfer

The same mechanism operates at the scale of a national financial system, which is where its scale becomes hardest to ignore, because unlike a single town or coastline it strikes the whole economy at once, leaving no geographic or cultural explanation intact.

The setup began in 1978, when Congress created the 401(k) retirement-account structure. Over the next two decades, defined-benefit pensions were systematically replaced by 401(k)s, and the replacement was sold to workers as freedom, as finally owning their own retirement rather than depending on an employer's plan. What went unsaid was that the freedom was mostly the corporation's freedom from liability. Market risk, longevity risk, and behavioral risk all moved from institutional balance sheets onto individual workers who had no way to pool, hedge, or insure against them. That first transfer ran quietly for thirty years.

The second transfer came in a compressed window between 2001 and 2008, when financial institutions built mortgage-backed securities and collateralized debt obligations, instruments whose complexity was not incidental but structural, because only instruments too complex to evaluate could be sold to investors who could not evaluate them. They were packaged into the mutual funds and target-date funds sold inside the same 401(k) accounts that had absorbed the first transfer. When the instruments collapsed in 2008, the losses flowed into those retirement accounts. The workers told in the 1980s that they were now responsible for their own retirement bore the losses of instruments the financial sector had created, collected fees on, and would shortly be rescued from.

The rescue did come. The Troubled Asset Relief Program provided seven hundred billion dollars in public funds to stabilize financial institutions. The Federal Reserve's emergency lending facilities, whose full scale emerged only through later congressional requests and litigation, exceeded sixteen trillion dollars in cumulative emergency loans across the crisis, per the Government Accountability Office's 2011 audit. Executive bonuses at rescued institutions resumed within eighteen months. Meanwhile, roughly ten million American households received foreclosure filings between 2008 and 2012, with about five million ending in completed foreclosure, more households than there are in the entire state of Pennsylvania, in under five years. No comparable federal intervention was organized for them. The Home Affordable Modification Program reached a small fraction of eligible applicants and suffered administrative failures documented across multiple Government Accountability Office reports.

The asymmetry is the load-bearing fact. A system that could mobilize trillions of dollars in days to rescue institutions could not mobilize comparable resources to rescue the households whose retirement accounts had absorbed those institutions' losses and whose homes were foreclosed in the contraction that followed. The risk was transferred twice on the same money, once from corporations to workers through the pension-to-401(k) shift, and again from financial institutions into those same workers' accounts through the mortgage-backed-securities machinery. The rescue restored one side of the ledger and left the other carrying its losses. The Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission, established by Congress to investigate, concluded in 2011 that the crisis resulted from human action and inaction, not from forces beyond human control. The people who made those decisions received bonuses. The people whose retirements and homes absorbed the losses received nothing.

A sympathetic reader will press several objections here, and each is worth naming, because the structural finding survives all of them. *Borrowers took loans they could not afford.* True, and yet agency requires information, and the information architecture was built to be adversarial: originator commissions tied to volume rather than to default rates, broker-disclosed terms differing materially from contract terms, adjustable-rate products marketed to borrowers whose grasp of rate-reset mechanics the industry itself expected to be incomplete. This is, in effect, the answer to the Coasean intuition that voluntary trades take care of themselves. They do not, when one party holds information the other cannot get and the structure is designed to keep it that way. *The crisis was global, not a United States policy failure.* Its global reach is real, but the instruments that carried the contagion were predominantly United States-originated, and the rating-agency conflicts and regulatory exemptions that let them scale were United States policy choices; the global character amplifies the mechanism finding rather than dissolving it. *TARP was repaid, so taxpayers did not lose.* TARP is the wrong accounting unit. The cost severance this chapter prices is the unrecovered welfare loss to the foreclosed households, the workers whose retirement balances reset to lower values that then compounded forward from a lower base, the regional economies that contracted around foreclosure-cascade neighborhoods, and the public trust that absorbed the asymmetric rescue as evidence of the system's real priorities. None of that is recovered by a federal balance sheet breaking even. *Dodd-Frank already fixed this.* It did real work, and Chapter 9 names it as one of the entering wedges by which broader accountability architecture gets built. But the reform is partial and reversible: the Volcker Rule was substantially relaxed in 2018 and 2019, systemic-importance designations have been contested and narrowed, and a reform that holds for ten years and is then dismantled is, in cost-severance terms, a delay rather than a correction. The full adversarial engagement with these objections, in the form an academic reviewer would want, lives in the Technical Appendix; the point here is only that the pattern is robust to each.

What survives is the structural finding. Borrowers had agency; the information was adversarial. The crisis was global; the instruments were American. TARP was repaid; the welfare losses on the cost-bearing side were not. The architecture reformed; the reform is partial, reversible, and has not yet closed the gap.

One contingency deserves a single sentence before the section closes, because it bears on whether the United States' 2008 choice was inevitable. It was not: other polities facing structurally similar banking crises have combined bank rescue with substantial household relief, demonstrating that the two are not an either-or. ### Social Security: Cost Severance in the Financial Commons

The most devastating example of cost severance in American history is hiding in plain sight, embedded in a system most people assume is the opposite of extraction: the promise that each generation will care for the one that came before it.

Social Security was designed in 1935 as a pay-as-you-go system. Current workers pay into the trust fund through payroll deductions. Current retirees receive benefits funded by those contributions. For decades the system ran surpluses, with more coming in from working-age Americans than going out to retirees. Those surpluses were invested in special-issue Treasury bonds and accumulated in the Social Security Trust Fund as a cushion for the time when the baby boom generation reached retirement age.

The baby boom generation began reaching retirement age in 2008. By 2021, Social Security was paying out more in benefits than it was collecting in payroll taxes, the demographic transition everyone had seen coming for forty years. The trust fund has been making up the difference by redeeming its Treasury bonds. Under current law and current projections, the trust fund will be depleted in 2033. Unless Congress acts, Social Security will then be able to pay only seventy-seven percent of promised benefits. The program will not disappear, but it will not deliver what it promised to the generations who planned their retirements around it.

The severed cost sits in that gap. The trust fund's surpluses, accumulated from 1937 onward, were held in special-issue Treasury obligations rather than invested as productive sovereign capital. The opportunity cost, the gap between what the fund holds today and what it would have held had those surpluses earned a broad equity-market return across the same window, is on the order of one hundred and eight trillion dollars. That figure is roughly three times the entire current federal debt, nearly four times annual gross domestic product, about three hundred thousand dollars for every person in the country, children included. [CITATION GAP: Social Security $108T foregone return — this is the single largest figure in the chapter and a counterfactual-model output, not a documented number. It is highly sensitive to the equity-index chosen, contribution timing, and drawdown assumptions, and is the chapter's highest-risk "numbers-are-invented" exposure. Before submission it needs either (a) an explicit model-assumption disclosure stating the equity-return series used, or (b) demotion to a band with the methodology footnoted. As written, a hostile reviewer can dismiss the chapter on this one number.]

This is cost severance operating in the financial commons. The generations that voted for expanded Social Security benefits across the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s extracted the political value of those promises: the votes, the electoral support, the sense of having delivered security to constituents. The foregone-investment cost was severed from the politicians who chose the Treasury-IOU routing and the voters who rewarded the benefit expansions it underwrote, and transferred forward in time to people who were too young to vote or not yet born.

A careful reader will press two objections, and both clarify rather than weaken the finding. The first is a prudence objection: holding the surpluses in Treasury bonds was administratively sound, the safest asset available to a sovereign fund, and the alternative of holding equities would have exposed retirees to market risk and given a federal entity controlling stakes in private firms. Each part of this is correct, and beside the point. The cost severance is not located in how the surpluses were invested. It is located in how the obligations were generated in the first place. Throughout the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, benefit expansions were enacted by Congresses whose members would, in nearly every case, leave office before the demographic-transition obligations matured, funded by the unusually large working-age cohort of exactly those decades, against a funding gap that every actuarial projection of the period openly identified as inevitable. Holding the surpluses in Treasuries during the surplus window was prudent. Generating obligations during that window that the actuaries projected would deplete in the deficit window was the severance.

The second is a tractability objection: the gap is real but solvable through modest adjustments at a manageable horizon, so the framing inflates a soluble policy problem. The actuarial work is robust, and a combination of payroll-tax adjustments, taxable-wage-base cap changes, benefit-formula refinements, and eligibility-age changes can mathematically close the seventy-five-year gap. But notice what closing it does. Raising payroll taxes on the deficit-window cohort, or reducing their benefits, or raising the eligibility age for people who have paid in for forty years, transfers the obligation onto the cost-bearing party, not onto the cohorts who extracted the electoral value of generating it. Tractability is not the absence of severance. It is severance unfolding on schedule. The accountability gap is upstream of the portfolio question and downstream of the timeline question; it sits in the obligation-generation mechanism that both objections take as given.

Social Security is not a Ponzi scheme. It is a genuine intergenerational transfer program providing essential benefits to millions. But the specific way it has been funded reveals cost severance at civilizational scale: a vast foregone return, produced by a routing choice earlier generations made and handed to generations who had no say in it. The largest cost severance in human history, hiding inside the program Americans think of as the most sacred commitment one generation can make to another.

The architecture the United States chose in 1935, pay-as-you-go security funded by current-worker payroll deductions with surpluses held as Treasury obligations rather than invested as productive sovereign capital, was a contingent choice, not an inevitable one. Chapter 4 documents Norway's contrasting choice for an analogous problem: a sovereign fund that compounds petroleum rents across decades on behalf of future generations rather than depleting them. The two architectures address structurally similar problems and produce structurally different outcomes, and the difference is not a function of national wealth or demographic luck but of architectural choice made at specific historical moments. (Whether Norway's model transfers to a country of America's scale and politics is a fair question, and Chapter 4 answers it; the point here is only that the choice was a choice.)

### The Healthcare Commons

The mechanism operates in American healthcare with a particular cruelty, because it operates on individual bodies during the most vulnerable stretches of life, and because the costs it severs are not abstract externalities but the specific financial ruin of specific households.

The most visible indicator is a growth curve. Between 2011 and 2020, the annual number of United States medical fundraising campaigns on the platform GoFundMe grew twenty-five-fold. By 2020 more than a third of all GoFundMe campaigns were for medical expenses; between 2016 and 2020, 438,000 such campaigns raised over two billion dollars from 21.7 million donations. Only twelve percent reached their funding goals. Sixteen percent received no donations at all. GoFundMe's own chief executive has acknowledged that the platform was never designed to function as healthcare financing and has become the default. A safety net with an eighty-eight-percent failure rate is not a safety net. It is a documentation system for a severance the formal system has stopped trying to prevent. [CITATION GAP: GoFundMe figures (25-fold growth; 438,000 campaigns / $2B / 21.7M donations; 12% reach goal; 16% zero donations) map to identifiable academic papers — name them in publisher-facing prose.]

The severance lands unevenly. Medical fundraising campaigns organized by individuals with distinctively African American or Hispanic surnames raise substantially less and are significantly less likely to meet their goals, even after controlling for geography and time period. The replacement for a public safety net reproduces the very inequities the original net had been designed, however imperfectly, to reduce. This is cost severance through a second layer: the formal system drops the medical cost, the informal backup does not catch equally by race, and the severed cost concentrates on the populations least able to absorb it. [CITATION GAP: racial-disparity finding in medical crowdfunding — from an identifiable academic paper; name it.]

The pressure on households is not only about healthcare-cost growth in absolute terms. It is also about wages that have not kept pace. The economists Lawrence Mishel and Josh Bivens at the Economic Policy Institute have documented that the median United States wage tracked productivity growth from the late 1940s into the early 1970s and then decoupled: productivity kept rising while the median wage stagnated in real terms for the next half century. Over the same half century, healthcare costs grew at multiples of general inflation. The pressure on households is the gap between those two curves, wages flat against the productivity wage-earners produced, healthcare costs climbing against the same wages. Jacob Hacker's *The Great Risk Shift* (2006) named the broader pattern of risk and cost moving from institutional to individual balance sheets that healthcare-cost severance is one instance of. GoFundMe-as-default is what cost severance looks like when the cost-bearing party simply cannot absorb the transfer's magnitude.

At the other end of the arc sits the case the journalist Katy Butler documented. Medicare reimbursement pays a physician fifty-four dollars for a long office visit to discuss treatment alternatives, and four hundred sixty-one dollars, on top of a roughly twelve-thousand-dollar hospital flat fee, nearly half of which goes to the device manufacturer, for the implantation of a pacemaker. The liability architecture pushes the same way: performing the procedure protects against malpractice exposure, declining it creates exposure. Both incentives arrive at the same outcome. Butler's father spent five years in profound cognitive decline with the pacemaker still functioning, cared for by her eighty-year-old mother in eighty-hour weeks of unpaid labor for which no compensation exists and no ledger accounts. Eighty hours a week, for five years, is more than twenty thousand hours of caregiving by a woman in her eighties, given to keep a heart beating that the conversation worth fifty-four dollars might have allowed to stop. The device manufacturer received roughly six thousand dollars. The conversation that might have prevented the procedure was worth fifty-four. This is cost severance on the dying body: profit extracted by the cardiologist, the hospital, and the device manufacturer, cost pushed onto the family as unpaid labor, onto the patient as prolonged decline, and onto an eighty-year-old spouse absorbing work that no formal architecture existed to account for. [CITATION GAP: Butler's specific figures ($54 office visit; $461 pacemaker implantation; ~$12K hospital flat fee; ~$6K device) need a page-cite to Butler's book or essay.]

Butler's case is one archetype of a systemic pattern. Atul Gawande's *Being Mortal* (2014) documents the broader structure: the American healthcare-financing architecture systematically over-treats dying patients, because the reimbursement architecture rewards intervention at every decision point, while substantially under-funding palliative care, because palliative reimbursement is a fraction of intervention reimbursement and most American medical training carries little palliative instruction. The architecture does not require any individual cardiologist or administrator to choose extraction over compassion at any individual moment. It produces the pattern by aggregating rational responses to incentives that are themselves the policy-design choice the framework names. End-of-life care is the most emotionally legible illustration, not the only one; maternal care, mental-health care, and chronic-disease management reproduce the same pattern at other points along the arc of a life.

There is a real tension worth naming at this exact spot, because it is the chapter's emotional peak and a careful reader will feel it. The framework's instinct is to put the eighty-year-old's twenty thousand hours on a ledger, to give the uncounted labor a number. A reader in the tradition of Michael Sandel will object that some things degrade when priced, and that the move to account for a spouse's devotion in dollars is itself a version of the commodification the chapter is protesting. The objection is serious and the book engages it directly elsewhere. The short answer here is that the framework is not pricing the devotion. It is pricing the severance, the gap between the value the architecture extracted and the cost it refused to recognize, precisely so that the labor stops being invisible. Naming what was taken is not the same as buying it. Among the wealthy nations, the United States is the only one whose healthcare-financing architecture produces the GoFundMe-as-default pattern at this scale. Chapter 9 develops the cross-national comparison at fuller depth.

### The Pattern Made Visible

Six cases. Six industries. Four continents, two of them represented twice. Six entirely different kinds of extraction, and one identical pattern.

An entity, a company, a generation, a political system, extracts value from a resource or system that could in principle be managed for the long term. The extraction generates costs: environmental damage, health impacts, financial obligations, the permanent foreclosure of future options. Those costs are severed from the entity that created them and transferred to parties who had little power to prevent the transfer: workers, communities, ecosystems, future generations.

When the costs finally materialize, when the workers get sick, when the waters collapse, when the bills come due, the original beneficiary is shielded from the full reckoning by some combination of six covers: distance, time, the asymmetry of what is known, the asymmetry of who can refuse, the normalization of the cost as routine, and the appearance of abundance that keeps the cost from registering at all. What remains is absorbed by those who cannot leave, the systems that cannot relocate, the generations who inherit the mess.

There is a name for this object, older than the framework, and naming it matters because the six cases are not six analogies. They are one thing. Karl Polanyi argued that land, labor, and money are fictitious commodities: they were never produced for sale, and treating them as ordinary market goods tears the social and ecological fabric they are made of, provoking what he called a double movement, society's attempt to protect itself from the damage the market inflicts. Nancy Fraser has extended the analysis, naming the expropriation, distinct from ordinary exploitation, by which capital draws on nature, care, and political capacity without paying for their replenishment. Read through Polanyi and Fraser, the reef and the atmosphere, the community and the labor and the unborn generation are not separate problems with a family resemblance. They are the same fictitious commodity, severed and sold in different settings. Cost severance is the empirical face of that severing. What the diagnostic tradition named, this book proposes to price.

This is not a failure of the system. It is the system working exactly as it was built to work, not by conscious intention but by the accumulated logic of structures that reward extraction and externalize cost onto those least able to resist. Cost severance is not an accident or an unintended consequence. It is the mechanism by which extractive economies have operated, and it is operating today.

It is worth being precise about what would disconfirm this. The framework is not a story that fits any case after the fact. Applied to a well-managed regime that has internalized its costs, it returns a small number, not a large one; Chapter 4's account of Norway is the differentiating case, the regime where the gap nearly closes. A framework that returned a large severed cost everywhere it looked would be unfalsifiable and worthless. This one varies, and the variation is the test.

The question is not whether this is happening. The six cases prove that it is. The question is what it would take to build accountability that interrupts the pattern before the costs are severed, rather than trying to recover them after the damage is done and the beneficiaries have departed.

### Who Shaped the Architecture: A Complementary Tradition

The cases share a structure: a commons is consumed, the costs are absorbed by parties downstream or downwind or downstream in time, and the accountability architecture as it stands does not require those costs to be priced. The framework this book develops is the response. It focuses on the side of the architecture that absorbs the cost: who bore it, how much, and what an honest accounting would require.

A complementary question, necessary but distinct, is who shaped the architecture that distributed the cost the way it did. The Public Choice tradition of James Buchanan, Gordon Tullock, and their successors built rigorous tools for that question, identifying the concentrated interests and lobbying coalitions that produce a given institutional design. Several of this chapter's cases have well-documented rent-seeking histories. The Minerals Management Service that oversaw Deepwater was a textbook captured regulator, its personnel travel, gifts, and revolving-door employment so thoroughly funded by the offshore-oil industry that post-disaster reform split the agency into three. The deregulatory architecture behind the 2008 crisis, from Gramm-Leach-Bliley in 1999 through the various preemption rules of the 2000s, was shaped by financial-industry lobbying that successive congressional hearings documented in detail.

The two traditions describe the same phenomenon from two angles, and the angles are not symmetric. The framework supplies the accounting of what the architecture cost and to whom; Public Choice supplies the analysis of how rent-seeking shaped the architecture in the first place. The framework's ledger is the upstream layer to which the Public Choice actor-identification is best applied. Readers who want the architecture-shaping analysis the framework does not perform should read Buchanan and Tullock in parallel. This book draws on the rent-seeking vocabulary, which survives many readings of Buchanan's broader political project, without endorsing the project.

### Restitution and Foreclosure: Two Directions of Accountability

The cases produce a question: what would an honest accounting of these severances actually require? The framework's answer is its two-instrument response, and the rest of this chapter names it at the level of structure. The methodology that follows in Chapter 6 will formalize it; Chapter 9 will take up the politics of building it. Here the work is only to name what the apparatus is and which established traditions it stands inside.

The accountability gap has two temporal faces. There is harm already done: the Libby asbestos deaths, the Gulf ecosystems that have not recovered, the foreclosed households that never regained their homes, the McDowell County life-expectancy gap this book documents at depth in Chapter 8. And there is harm not yet done: the future-generation climate cost of carbon emitted today, the petroleum hydrocarbons that will be foreclosed for non-energy uses fifty years from now, the Social Security obligation a younger cohort has not yet been asked to bear. An architecture that addresses one direction and not the other addresses only half the problem.

The framework's response is two coordinated instruments. The first, the Restitution Bond, addresses harm already realized: its posting discharges the cumulative damage borne by communities, workers, and ecosystems whose costs were severed at the time of extraction. The second, the Foreclosure Bond, addresses harm yet to be realized: its posting discharges the future-generation costs that current extraction is foreclosing. Together they form the aggregate accountability bond the framework's central equation requires when it states that cost severance equals residual commons value minus the bond posted. (How that accounting avoids simply re-deriving a Pigouvian externality tax, and how it avoids double-counting, is the work of Chapter 6's gates; the cases here are priced as residual costs in anticipation of that machinery.)

The choice of the word *bond* is deliberate. A tax is paid at the moment of transaction, against general revenue, with no architecture for matching a posted amount to a specific future obligation. A fee licenses an activity rather than discharging a harm. A fund aggregates without discharging. A bond does three things the framework needs at once: it is posted before the full cost has been borne, it carries forward across the long tail of the cost it covers, and it discharges, observably and by a specific figure, against a specific liability when the obligation comes due. The reclamation bonds mining companies post under the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act do this. The decommissioning bonds oil-and-gas operators post against well closure do this. The sovereign fund Norway built against its petroleum extraction does this at national scale. The framework uses the term in continuity with these traditions, and in the meaning they have long built into it.

The Restitution Bond stands inside an established academic and legal lineage. The argument-architecture for reparations claims grounded in structural-economic harm, rather than in individual tortious acts, was articulated most influentially by Ta-Nehisi Coates in *The Case for Reparations* (*The Atlantic*, 2014), an essay widely credited with returning reparations to mainstream American political discourse. Coates's pattern, name the mechanism by which harm was produced, name the harm, name the debt, is the rhetorical companion to what reparations economics develops methodologically. The framework's Cost Severance Damages instrument is a structural extension of that pattern into extraction-community contexts: name the mechanism (cost severance), name the harm (the documented life-expectancy gap, the institutional collapse, the health costs externalized to families and to Medicare), name the debt.

Reparations economics develops Coates's framing into formal method. William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen's *From Here to Equality: Reparations for Black Americans in the Twenty-First Century* (2020) is the contemporary landmark, and its wealth-gap calculation is the careful accumulation of the specific economic mechanisms by which intergenerational harm was transferred and compounded. Hamilton, Darity, Price, Sridharan, and Tippett's *Umbrellas Don't Make It Rain* (2015) extends the method empirically through matched-comparison work, demonstrating that conventional explanations for the racial wealth gap (educational attainment, employment, family structure) cannot account for its magnitude, since even Black households at the highest achievement strata hold median wealth well below white households at lower strata. The structural variable is wealth, not behavior. Dalton Conley's earlier *Being Black, Living in the Red* (1999) established the wealth-as-structural-variable method these works develop. The framework applies the same matched-comparison discipline to extraction communities: McDowell County's wealth collapse against comparable non-extraction Appalachian counties, Libby's cumulative health-cost burden against comparable communities without Grace's asbestos, the opioid-Appalachia mortality demography against comparable counties where the documented marketing targeting did not concentrate. The method is portable; the per-context calibration is what differs.

Darity and Mullen structure reparations as a three-component program: acknowledgment and atonement; redress, which decomposes into symbolic redress and material restitution, with the authors declaring in favor of restitution; and closure. The framework's Restitution Bond is positioned, then, not as a concept alongside reparations but as the instrument that operationalizes the restitution component of redress within their typology, extended to the broader class of cases this book engages. When the framework calls something a Restitution Bond it is using their language inside their framework, not against it. The umbrella term *restitution* rather than *reparations* keeps the instrument portable across the full field of cost-severance contexts, from environmental restitution at Libby to occupational-disease restitution to community restitution at McDowell, without narrowing it to one historically specific application or making that portability read as analogy rather than direct application.

The legal-architecture lineage the framework engages is most rigorously developed in Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* (2019). Pistor shows that capital is not a natural asset class but a legal artifact: specific legal modules, property law, collateral law, trust law, corporate-veil law, bankruptcy law, and choice-of-law clauses, encode assets as capital and shield that capital from accountability across jurisdictions. Libby is exactly the pattern she names. Grace's bankruptcy was not an accident but a structural feature of the architecture, the corporate-veil-protected entity discharging liabilities by reorganizing and transferring claims onto creditors and the public; the 2009 acquittal was the criminal law failing to reach what Pistor names as a design feature, not a bug. This is also where the chapter answers the objection that the framework merely re-describes financialization. It does not. Where Pistor diagnoses the legal modules that encode cost severance, the framework prices what those modules sever, and in pricing it provides the lever by which the severed cost can be re-embedded into the accountability the modules were built to evade, which is the Polanyian double movement made operational rather than merely hoped for. The Foreclosure Bond stands in adjacent but distinct territory. Its lineage is environmental-bond economics: Hartwick's rule for sustainable extraction (1977), the reclamation-bond tradition that mining and oil-and-gas regulation in the United States has implemented at varying levels of seriousness for half a century, and the sovereign-fund architecture Norway built for its petroleum extraction. These are not reparations instruments. They are foreclosure-prevention instruments, postings made against ongoing or proposed extraction whose costs would otherwise be inherited by future generations whose moral standing the framework takes seriously. Derek Parfit's 1984 work on impersonal intergenerational outcomes grounds that commitment philosophically. So, from another direction, does the civic-republican tradition recovered in Philip Pettit's *Republicanism* (1997) and Quentin Skinner's *Liberty Before Liberalism* (1998), which frames freedom as non-domination and treats a society that extracts now in ways that subject future generations to dominated conditions as violating that freedom regardless of whether any specific contract has been broken. The framework does not require the reader to adopt either philosophy; the bond operates regardless of which tradition supplies the moral standing of future generations. It engages both because they converge on the same accountability requirement.

The two instruments are structurally independent. A community whose commons has already been largely consumed, McDowell after the coal era, carries a high Restitution Bond obligation and a relatively low Foreclosure Bond obligation, because the foreclosure has already happened. A context where extraction is proposed but has not yet occurred, the deep-seabed mining now under policy debate, carries a low Restitution Bond obligation and a high Foreclosure Bond obligation, because the foreclosure is what is being decided now. Most real cases carry both. Norwegian petroleum extraction internalized substantial restitution through the welfare-state architecture for Norwegian citizens but did not internalize the foreclosure cost for the non-Norwegian populations affected by Norwegian oil's atmospheric externalities; Norway has paid a Restitution Bond on its own citizens while the Foreclosure Bond remains globally underdeveloped. The decomposition is what makes both visible at once.

The framework's apparatus, the gates and the methods that Chapter 6 formalizes, prices each case forward, as the book has been doing: McDowell's coal, Norway's oil, the Macondo well, Libby's vermiculite, Baotou's tailings. The same apparatus runs in reverse.

In later chapters the book walks through a use case of the abundance of clean air being stripped away, by an off-world colony engineering its own atmosphere, or by a climate crisis on Earth, until the costs of atmospheric services that the abundance had masked become visible. The framework gates and triangulates them. That is the forward case. When the abundance of freedom is stripped away, by slavery, by forced labor, by the variants of coercion that have organized large parts of human history, the costs of the autonomy commons surface that the abundance had hidden. The same apparatus reaches these too, applied backward: a replacement-cost method that measures remediation rather than substitute-engineering, a revealed-preference method that reads revealed restitution rather than revealed restraint, and a scarcity-adjusted option-value method that evaluates the option extinguished at the time of past extraction from what we know now. The most developed backward-direction work in the contemporary literature is Darity and Mullen's *From Here to Equality* (2020), which prices the racial wealth gap accumulated through American policy choices since 1619. The method is the framework's, applied backward, with Darity and Mullen as the primary developers. Their more recent work prices the longevity gap, the six-to-seven-year average life-expectancy differential between Black and white American populations, as a measurable legacy effect. McDowell County's thirteen-year life-expectancy gap, cited in earlier chapters, is the same legacy-effect variant applied to a coal-extraction population.

Some backward variables sit at the framework's analytical boundary rather than inside it. The coercion vector itself, what should be assigned to coerced labor as a cost dimension distinct from its legacy effects, remains unresolved in the reparations-economics field. Slavery's structural distinctness from the asbestos and opioid cases is that those cases operate through information asymmetry, where workers and consumers proceeded because they did not know what they would bear, while coerced extraction is different: the cost-bearer knows, and is forced to bear it anyway. The framework reaches coerced cases through their legacy effects, the wealth gaps, the longevity gaps, the disruption of cultural-knowledge transmission, the intergenerational trauma. The direct pricing of the coercion vector remains an open question, acknowledged as outside this book's empirical scope. Voices in the reparations-economics tradition, Darity, Mullen, Hamilton, Coates, Anderson, and the broader field, have been doing this work for years, and the framework recognizes and defers to them in identifying and pricing these variables.

What this chapter has documented is the empirical state of bond posting in the American extraction regimes: the bond posted is far below the residual commons value, in nearly every case examined, by orders of magnitude. The federal Black Lung Benefits Program has paid out forty-four billion dollars across half a century, a real number and not a small one, and its Trust Fund still carries roughly five point one billion dollars in outstanding debt, with no instrument in place that prices the cost severance the fund was designed to compensate. The reclamation bonds mining companies post cover four to six billion dollars less than the actual reclamation cost of the sites whose extraction generated them. The intergenerational obligation embedded in the Social Security trust-fund accounting has no Foreclosure Bond against it at all, so the cost is structurally guaranteed to fall on whichever generation happens to be of working age when the obligation matures. The framework names this the accountability gap. One discipline carries through all of this. Reparations economics treats the question of what is owed as separate from the question of what is politically feasible to collect, and the framework follows it. What the bond would have to be, if the cost severance documented in this chapter were accounted for honestly, is the methodology's question. What political path leads from current bond levels toward the residual commons value is a separate question, taken up in Chapter 9 and scoped, per the framework's discipline, as an open political-philosophical question rather than a single-instrument prescription this book attempts to legislate. The framework's contribution here is not advocacy. It is accounting.

This chapter has named the cost severance the American extraction regimes produce, documented the pattern across six cases at financial-system, intergenerational, and institutional-medical scales, engaged the strongest counter-positions a sympathetic reader could press, and introduced the framework's two-instrument response alongside the lineages that ground it. What remains is the methodology.

The next chapter formalizes the apparatus: the Commons Inversion Test that admits cost claims to the accounting, the four gates that filter admitted claims for unit-consistency, boundedness, and independence, the three ways of estimating residual commons value when no single method suffices, and the Hotelling Identity that bridges the framework to standard resource economics. Chapter 7 then walks the apparatus through thought-experiment scaling, asking whether it holds under the maximally different conditions of off-Earth extraction, and Chapter 8 walks one ton of Appalachian coal through the full cost decomposition that produces the book's most concrete numerical example. The policy economy of Chapter 9, the closing reflection of Chapter 10, and the technical appendix that operationalizes everything for academic review all build on the apparatus this chapter has now introduced.

Not whether cost severance exists, but what we do about it. That is the question the next three chapters answer.
