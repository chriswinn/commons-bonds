# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*




## Chapter 7: On Other Worlds

---

Imagine, for a moment, that the argument this book has been making is wrong. Not wrong in its moral framing, and not wrong in its sympathy for the communities the framework is meant to protect, but wrong in its technical claim. The claim is that extraction is systematically underpriced because no existing framework captures the full intergenerational cost of foreclosure. Perhaps, a careful reader might say, this is a feature of the particular political economy of extraction on Earth, the specific way lobbyists and governments and industry groups have colluded to keep certain costs off the books, rather than a structural fact about how resource pricing works anywhere.

If that is right, the framework is a political critique of a particular earthly arrangement. It is not a general theory of how rational agents price resources, and it deserves to be read as the former rather than the latter.

There is one way to settle the question. Take the framework off Earth and see whether it still produces the answers a thoughtful planner would recognize as correct.

That is what this chapter does. The thought experiment that organizes it is a small habitat on Mars, where ten thousand people are trying to make sensible decisions about the water in the ground beneath them. The chapter then runs the same framework through a series of increasingly different scenarios, asteroid iron, lunar helium, comet volatiles, the possible ice of Europa, the hypothetical ecosystems of a distant exoplanet, and a self-imposed commute lease closer to home, to see whether the answers stay sensible as the parameters change.

Mars is the setting for one reason: the parameter ranges are real enough to be defensible. It is the off-world body human civilization has been actively trying to reach for half a century, which means the numbers for resource extraction in Martian regolith are measured rather than invented. But the thought experiment is not Mars-specific. The framework's behavior on a small Mars habitat is its behavior on any small bounded off-world habitat where survival depends on a few finite local resources, whether that habitat ends up on Mars, on the Moon, on a free-flying station, or in a configuration none of us has yet imagined. The conclusions are not Mars-conclusions. They are bounded-system conclusions, and they hold wherever a habitat fits that description. Mars functions here as a stress-test setting for the framework's logic, not as advocacy for off-world settlement. Whether humanity should pursue Mars settlement is a policy question the chapter takes up later on its own terms, and the framework holds whatever answer that question turns out to have.

Along the way the test reveals something about why cost severance operates the way it does on Earth, a function of a variable the framework quietly relies on, which this chapter will make explicit. That variable, and the structural definition behind it, turns out to be the deepest thing the off-world scenarios expose.

---

### The habitat

The habitat houses ten thousand people. It is established, self-sustaining, several decades into its existence. It relies on three known subsurface water ice deposits accessible from its habitation zone. At current consumption, drinking, agriculture, oxygen production through electrolysis, industrial processing, the deposits represent roughly a fifty-year supply. The administrator of the habitat's resources has been asked by a trading consortium for permission to accelerate extraction. The request is profitable in the near term. It would deliver a return on the consortium's investment within five years. It would also reduce the habitat's remaining water supply to something closer to a twenty-year horizon.

The administrator has to decide.

She is not burdened, in her situation, by the philosophical debates that have structured terrestrial resource economics for a century. She is not wondering about the social cost of carbon or the appropriate discount rate for intergenerational transfers. She is wondering whether it is acceptable to reduce her habitat's remaining water supply by sixty percent in exchange for a financial return to a specific consortium over the next decade. She knows what happens to a habitat that runs out of water. Her grandchildren will be among the inhabitants the math is being done for. The habitat's kindergarten classroom is two corridors from her office. The agricultural domes she walks past on her way to the consortium meeting are the same domes the inhabitants will or will not be able to keep watered in thirty years. She also knows that the consortium, having captured its return, will be operating under somewhat different incentives by then.

Walk the residual commons value framework through her situation, term by term.

*Substitutability.* The probability that an adequate substitute will exist at any given future time. For drinking, for agriculture, for electrolysis into oxygen, water has essentially no substitute at the chemistries involved. The administrator cannot run the habitat on ammonia. She cannot breathe synthesized oxygen produced by some non-water chemistry at any pace that would keep the life-support systems functional. The substitutability function for Mars water is approximately zero for the applications that matter most, the ones that keep her inhabitants alive. She can, in the very long run, imagine atmospheric water harvesting, cometary water imports, or large-scale synthesis from rarer feedstocks. None of these is currently operational at the scale the habitat requires, and none is on a development trajectory that guarantees availability within the extraction horizon being considered.

*Stock-dependent utility.* The value of each remaining unit as the stock declines. Water is abundant to an uninhabited Mars. Water is not abundant to a habitat of ten thousand people with three known deposits. Each unit of the remaining stock becomes more valuable as the stock depletes, approaching, as the quantity shrinks, the value of survival itself. The utility function is not smooth here. It is a ramp that steepens as the stock approaches the inhabitants' survival threshold, and then, at some point not known exactly in advance, it becomes a cliff.

*Externality tail.* The ongoing damage that extraction imposes after it ends. Extraction of subsurface ice risks contaminating the remaining stock with drilling chemicals. It risks destabilizing the geological formations holding adjacent deposits. It risks venting volatiles that the habitat's atmospheric management systems must absorb. Beyond these direct externalities there is a deeper one. Mars may harbor subsurface microbial life. If it does, extraction that contaminates or destroys the subsurface hydrosphere may end the only confirmed extraterrestrial biosphere in the known universe, a loss that cannot be priced in dollars because it involves scientific and philosophical values that are not denominated in any currency. This is the point at which the framework does what Michael Sandel insists a serious moral economist must do: it refuses to assign a money price to a thing whose value is not a money value, and says so plainly, rather than manufacturing a number to keep the ledger looking complete.

*Discount factor.* The weight placed on future value relative to present value. On Earth, economists argue about the right discount rate for decades without resolution. On Mars the discussion is different. The inhabitants' survival is contingent, in a very direct way, on resources the administrator manages today. Discounting fifty-year horizons at the rates typically used in terrestrial cost-benefit analysis is suicidal. The future is not a discountable abstraction here. It is the same population's own next decade of continued existence. The discount factor approaches unity, which is to say the future is weighted almost as heavily as the present, because the same people expect to be alive for both. What John Rawls reached for behind a veil of ignorance, the demand that we choose as though we did not know which generation we would turn out to be, the Mars habitat supplies as a brute physical fact. The administrator does not have to imagine she might be her own grandchild. She has, in effect, already drawn that lot.

Every term is at an extreme value. The substitutability is near zero. The utility is life-or-death. The stock is small and fixed. The externalities are potentially lethal to the inhabitants themselves or, at the outer edge, civilizationally significant. The discount factor is essentially unity.

The residual commons value is extraordinarily high, and the administrator has not even reached the end of the calculation. The model's recommendation is not subtle. It says: reserve the deposits, ration aggressively, price each unit on a progressive scale that rises as stocks decline, invest heavily in recycling and closed-loop water management, and under no circumstances permit the consortium to accelerate extraction for near-term financial return. The math says the return the consortium is offering is trivial against the foreclosure cost the inhabitants would bear, and bear themselves, in the decades after the contract completes.

Any thoughtful administrator of any inhabited place on any world would recognize this as obviously correct. That is exactly the point.

### Earth is also a bounded system

Now take the model, with every parameter exactly where it sat for Mars water, and change one number at a time.

Increase the stock. The deposit becomes larger. The stock-dependent utility pressure drops. At some stock size, a unit of extraction no longer meaningfully reduces what remains, and the extraction recommendation loosens.

Increase the substitutability. The resource becomes one for which alternatives exist. The foreclosure cost drops, because future generations who need the resource can turn to the substitutes the model is accounting for, and the recommendation loosens further.

Decrease the externality tail. The extraction process becomes cleaner, the remaining stock uncontaminated, the ecological damage minimal, and the recommendation loosens again.

Increase the discount factor. The future becomes less weighted relative to the present, and the recommendation loosens further still.

At some combination of these changes, the model's recommendation converges on market pricing. The resource is abundant, substitutable, clean to extract, and the future can be reasonably discounted. Honest price approximately equals market price. There is no meaningful cost severance. Extract freely.

At another combination, the recommendation diverges wildly from market pricing. The resource is scarce, substitution is uncertain, the externality tail is long, and the future is weighted heavily because of some structural feature, ecological, intergenerational, or existential, that prevents heavy discounting. Honest price vastly exceeds market price. Cost severance is large. Extract only with extreme accountability, or do not extract at all.

The model does not change in any of this. The parameters change. The recommendations change because the parameters change. That is what a properly calibrated pricing instrument does. It is worth being precise about the lineage here, because the calibration is the whole claim. Harold Hotelling showed in 1931 that the price of an exhaustible resource, correctly managed, should rise over time at the rate of interest, so that the owner is indifferent between extracting now and leaving the resource in the ground. The residual commons value is that rent logic carried to its honest conclusion: it prices not only the owner's foregone future sale but the foreclosure borne by everyone the owner does not represent, the unborn included. Where the externalities and the foreclosure happen to be zero, the framework reduces to the ordinary market rent Hotelling described. Where they are not, it adds exactly what Hotelling's rent omits, and no more.

Earth, it turns out, is not categorically different from Mars. Earth is the same kind of system with different parameters. The stock of coal is much larger than Mars's water ice, but it is not infinite. The substitutability of coal for electricity is today much higher than Mars water for drinking, but the substitutability of specific hydrocarbon chemistries for specific industrial applications is lower than a casual reader might expect. The externality tail of coal extraction includes black lung, acid mine drainage, and atmospheric carbon, costs that persist long after the mine closes. The discount factor for intergenerational carbon harm is the exact subject of decades of economic debate whose underlying question is whether the children of 2200 count as much as the shareholders of 2030.

That debate has structure worth naming. The 2006 Stern Review argued for a near-zero pure rate of time preference, on the explicit ethical premise that future generations cannot be discounted simply for being further away in time. William Nordhaus's DICE model and its successors used a higher discount rate calibrated to observed market returns, on the methodological premise that climate policy must be evaluated against what the same capital would have been worth had it been deployed productively elsewhere. The gap between these two approaches, roughly 1.4 percent versus 4 percent in the original exchanges, translates in present-value terms into trillions of dollars of difference in what counts as the right level of mitigation effort today. [CITATION GAP: Stern Review 2006 near-zero PRTP; Nordhaus DICE higher market-calibrated rate; the specific "1.4% versus 4%" figures need a public source.] Martin Weitzman's contribution to that debate is the one the framework leans on most directly: where the far future is deeply uncertain, the rate at which we discount it should itself decline over time, because the worst plausible outcomes dominate the expected value of the calculation. [CITATION GAP: Weitzman on declining/uncertainty-adjusted discount rates needs a citation.] John Hartwick's 1977 rule sits adjacent to both, and the framework generalizes it. Hartwick said that the rents from exhaustible-resource extraction should be invested in reproducible capital sufficient to leave future generations no worse off. [CITATION GAP: Hartwick 1977.] That rule works cleanly when the lost resource has a substitute that reproducible capital can build toward. The framework's contribution is to notice where it does not work: when the maximum future substitutability of a resource is at or below one, when nothing reproducible can stand in for what extraction forecloses, no amount of invested rent restores the option, and Hartwick's compensation logic has nothing to compensate with. That boundary, where substitution can no longer rescue the next generation, is where the framework's reservation logic takes over from Hartwick's investment logic. And it is the boundary Herman Daly drew when he distinguished weak from strong sustainability: some natural capital is not substitutable by manufactured capital at any price, and for that capital the rule is not to invest the rents but to preserve the stock. The Mars administrator who refuses the consortium is applying strong sustainability to her water. The framework's job is to say, for any resource, which rule applies.

The point of citing the lineage is not to settle the discount-rate debate inside this chapter. The point is that the question, what weight do future people carry against present people, is not avoidable. Mars makes the answer easier because the future people in question are the same habitat's own next generation, and the inhabitants cannot pretend otherwise. Earth makes it harder because the future people in question are strangers, and therefore easier to discount. The framework's discipline, here as everywhere, is to refuse the easier answer where the easier answer is structurally tempting but logically unsupported.

Every parameter that drove the Mars administrator's decision exists on Earth. The values are different. The structure is the same. The instrument that told her to preserve Martian water is the same instrument that tells us, when applied honestly to Appalachian coal, that the resource has been severely mispriced for a century.

This is the universality claim the chapter is establishing. The framework is not an Earth-specific critique. It is a general theory of pricing resources, tested at the limits where the parameters are stark enough to make the correct answer unambiguous, and then applied on Earth where the parameters are moderate enough to have allowed centuries of systematic mispricing without anyone being forced to notice.

### Running the test

A single calibrated case is an anecdote. The universality claim becomes credible only by running the framework through enough different scenarios that no one could plausibly say the answer was rigged. What follows is a series of extractions, run quickly, with the framework's recommendation noted in each case and the parameters that drove it.

*Iron from an uninhabited asteroid.* The resource is abundant across thousands of similar bodies in the accessible solar system. Substitutability is high: iron from other asteroids, iron from Earth, iron from recycled orbital structures are all available. The externality tail is minimal, no atmosphere to pollute, no ecosystem to disrupt, no community displaced by the mining operation. Stock-dependent utility is low because the stock is effectively unlimited. The model's output is that residual commons value approaches the market price of the extracted material. Cost severance, under any reasonable accountability bond for orbital debris management and energy accounting, is near zero. Extract freely. This is the lowest residual commons value the framework can recognize.

*Helium-3 embedded in lunar regolith.* The resource has essentially no current industrial use. Its potential value depends on whether fusion reactors built on a specific deuterium-helium-3 cycle ever achieve net energy gain, a technology horizon that has moved repeatedly over decades and whose arrival, if it arrives, cannot be dated. Substitutability is the inverse question: for the hypothetical future fusion application, helium-3 has no substitute by definition. Stock is very large, the Moon's regolith is thought to contain roughly a million tons, but extraction is energy-intensive and disturbs the lunar surface in ways that would be difficult to reverse. [CITATION GAP: lunar helium-3 stock estimate (~1 million tons) needs a sourced origin; the figure is widely cited but contested.] The model's answer here is interesting. It does not pretend to know the probability of fusion viability. It observes that the insurance cost of preserving lunar helium-3 is nearly zero, not mining the Moon costs nothing, and that the Asymmetric Regret Rule applies cleanly. If we preserve and fusion never works, we have lost nothing. If we extract and fusion later becomes viable, we have lost potentially the most important fuel source in the solar system. The model says: preserve. Wait. Reassess as the technology matures.

*Common regolith on the Moon, for construction of lunar habitation.* The same celestial body, a different resource. The regolith is abundant beyond any meaningful depletion horizon; the Moon is made of it. Substitutability is high for construction applications. The externality tail is modest, limited to local dust management and operational contamination. Stock-dependent utility is negligible. The model's answer is the opposite of its answer on lunar helium-3: extract freely, manage dust, proceed. The same location produces different recommendations for different resources because the parameters are different for different resources. A framework that said "preserve everything on the Moon" or "extract everything on the Moon" would be useless. The capacity to differentiate within a single location is what makes the framework an instrument rather than an ideology.

*Volatiles from a long-period comet during flyby.* The situation inverts. The resource is accessible only during a short extraction window, weeks to months, after which the comet departs for millennia. The externality tail is near zero: no life, no community, no atmosphere disturbed. Substitutability is moderate; other comets exist. The asymmetric regret flips here. The cost of not extracting, once the window closes, is the permanent loss of access to this specific comet's material. The cost of extracting is modest. The model says: extract aggressively during the window. This is the answer that disposes of the suspicion that the framework is a degrowth program in disguise. It is not conservation-biased. It is parameter-sensitive, and when the parameters favor extraction it recommends extraction, as bluntly as any market would.

*Subsurface ocean of Europa.* Here the model encounters its boundary. The scientific and moral value of potentially destroying an independent biosphere, if one exists in Europa's ocean, is genuinely not priceable in dollars. The externality term cannot be filled in with a number. The model does not pretend to fill it in. It says, honestly, that it cannot price this particular externality, and that the Asymmetric Regret Rule therefore dominates: the irreversibility of losing the only other known biosphere is absolute, the cost of waiting and studying is time, and the decision to extract should be deferred until the scientific question can be answered. This is not a failure of the framework. It is the framework behaving correctly, naming a limit where a limit exists rather than manufacturing a price to maintain the appearance of completeness. It is the same Sandelian refusal the Mars administrator made about possible microbial life, and it marks the framework's outer boundary: the domain where the right answer is "this is not a thing money measures," and the instrument's job is to say so.

*Rare earth elements near an inhabited Mars habitat.* This is the scenario that reveals the deepest structural insight the chapter has to offer. The inhabitants mine rare earths locally to manufacture electronics rather than importing from Earth. The substitutability question becomes supply-chain dependent, moderate if Earth imports are reliable, near zero if they are not. The utility is high for strategic independence. And here is the crucial part: the externality tail falls on the inhabitants themselves. Dust contaminates the habitat's air filtration. Chemical waste must be managed in a closed system where there is no downstream somewhere-else. Worker health affects the habitat's own limited medical resources. The inhabitants breathe their own emissions.

Cost severance, under these conditions, collapses toward zero. Not because the costs are small, but because the extractor and the affected community are the same people. There is no one to sever the cost to. The model's recommendation is that extraction, if it occurs, must be priced at its full internal cost, because the inhabitants will bear the full internal cost whether or not it is priced honestly. What the inhabitants actually pay and what the extraction actually costs converge automatically. This is the inverse of the divided-stakeholder commons the book treats elsewhere, where the people who capture the value and the people who bear the harm are different populations, often divided along the exact lines stratification economics studies. Here they are one population, and the severance has nowhere to go.

*A self-imposed commute lease on Earth.* The last case has no Mars and no asteroid in it. A person living in suburban Maryland signs a lease that commits them to a long daily commute to an office in Washington, D.C. There is no extracting corporation, no community being displaced; the cost-bearer made the decision themselves, with available information about the destination and the travel time. The framework computes the case the same way it computes asteroid iron and McDowell coal: hours per day, across the work days of a year, multiplied by the years committed, thousands of hours of life. Against any defensible valuation, lost wages, lost family time, lost rest, depleted attention, accumulated chronic stress, the value of what was actually being signed away is many multiples of the roughly twenty thousand dollars a year saved on cheaper housing. The mechanism that severed cost from the decision was not a corporation extracting from the lease-signer. It was past-self under-pricing future-self's time. The framework's Commons Inversion Test, applied at decision time, would have surfaced the actual price of the lease before signing. Same instrument; no bad actor required; the structural test is the same.

One honesty is owed here, because a Sandelian reader will reach for it before the next page. Pricing thousands of hours of a life against a dollar figure is not the same act as pricing thousands of tons of coal. The framework is not claiming that an hour of a person's life is worth the wage it would have earned, any more than the Europa case claimed a biosphere is worth a number. It is claiming something narrower and survivable: that a decision is being made *as if* the hours had a price of zero, and that surfacing the real magnitude of what is being given up is better than leaving it concealed. The number is a disclosure, not a valuation. The framework prices the severance precisely so that the thing severed does not have to be silently treated as worthless. That is the difference between measuring a harm and endorsing the exchange that produced it, and it is a difference the framework keeps in view wherever a human cost enters the ledger.

What the cross-scenario sweep makes visible, from asteroid iron through Mars rare earths to a Maryland commute lease, is that the framework is a pricing instrument operating across the entire range of cases where costs are systematically under-priced, regardless of whether there is a counterparty doing the under-pricing on purpose.

### What cost severance requires, and the commodity underneath it

Cost severance, the framework has argued from the beginning, is the act of cutting the cost of extraction loose from the profit of extraction and transferring it to people who had no say in the transaction. The transfer requires a transferee, people who can be made to bear what the extractor does not. It also requires a structural arrangement that lets value flow in one direction while cost stays in the other.

It is worth naming what kind of thing is being severed, because the answer is the same thing in every case the book has examined, and naming it closes a gap that the diagnostic tradition left open. Karl Polanyi argued that the market's great fiction is to treat land, labor, and money as though they were ordinary commodities produced for sale, when in fact none of them is. Land is the rest of nature. Labor is human life itself. Money is a token of social trust. Polanyi called these the fictitious commodities, and he argued that a market society which prices them as if they were real commodities tears the social and ecological fabric it depends on, until a counter-movement, what he called the double movement, rises to re-embed the market in society. Nancy Fraser later extended this: behind every act of capitalist exploitation, the wage paid for work done, stands a hidden act of expropriation, value seized from nature, from unpaid care, from colonized peoples, from the future, without any pretense of exchange at all.

The framework's six patterns are the mechanisms by which that expropriation is hidden, and the residual commons value is the price of the thing expropriated. McDowell County's water and air, the Appalachian future foreclosed by mountaintop removal, the hours of a commuter's life, the habitat's water, the unborn generation that cannot bid, a biosphere on Europa: these are not seven different objects. They are one object under seven descriptions, the fictitious commodity, the part of nature or life or futurity that the market prices at zero because it was never produced for sale. Polanyi and Fraser named the wound. What the diagnostic tradition stopped short of was a number. This framework prices the expropriation Polanyi and Fraser diagnosed, and reserves the commons they showed could not survive being treated as a commodity. The double movement, in this framework, is not a spontaneous social convulsion. It is an accounting discipline: re-embedding the market by making it pay the price it has been concealing.

The framework names six common patterns through which the concealing arrangement gets produced, alone or in combination. These six are illustrative, not exhaustive, empirical examples drawn from the cases this book examines, in the same spirit as Elinor Ostrom's eight design principles, Amartya Sen's ten capabilities, and Rawls's primary goods. The structural test is the operational arrangement itself, not whether any specific named pattern is present; readers applying the methodology to their own cases may surface additional patterns or refine these.

**Geographic distance.** When a ton of coal is extracted from McDowell County and burned to generate electricity in Chicago, the value is captured by shareholders and consumers a thousand miles of highway and regulatory separation away from the people who pay in their lungs and their watersheds.

**Temporal asymmetry.** The intergenerational case, costs borne by grandchildren no one alive today has met, is the same structural arrangement at a different scale. The extractor is not far in space but far in time; the cost-bearer is unborn and cannot bid in the transaction. This is where Ronald Coase's solution runs out. Coase argued that if property rights are clear and bargaining is cheap, the parties to an externality will negotiate their way to an efficient outcome regardless of who holds the right. But the future generation that bears the foreclosure cost cannot sit at the table. It does not yet exist; it has nothing to offer in trade; it cannot threaten to walk away. Coasean bargaining requires all affected parties to be present and able to transact, and the largest affected party to most extraction decisions is structurally absent. Derek Parfit pressed the further difficulty: the people who will exist in 2200 depend on the very choices we make now, so in a strict sense we cannot harm specific future individuals, because different choices would have produced different individuals. The framework does not need to resolve Parfit's non-identity problem to do its work. It is not pricing a harm to a named person who would otherwise have been better off. It is pricing the foreclosure of options for whoever the future turns out to contain, which is a cost the present can compute without knowing the names of the people who will pay it.

**Informational asymmetry.** The 2008 housing collapse, which the book treats in full elsewhere, is the clearest exemplar. Bankers and underwater homeowners shared a country, a legal system, often a metro region. What severed costs from value was not distance but what one side knew, when, and what the other side did not. The cost-bearer cannot refuse what they cannot see in time.

**Power asymmetry.** Slavery and forced labor sever costs from value through coercion alone. Enslavers and enslaved often shared the same plantation, within eye-contact distance; the pyramids' conscripted laborers worked within sight of the pharaohs whose monuments they built. Geographic distance was zero. Informational asymmetry was minimal; both parties knew what the work cost. What severed the cost was that the bearer could not refuse. The framework prices this severance like any other, but it does not flatten it. The hours taken by coercion are the same kind of expropriated commodity as the hours under-priced by routine, and the framework can measure both. It does not follow that they carry the same moral weight, and the framework does not claim they do. Measuring the magnitude of a severance is one act; weighing the wrong of how it was produced is another, and the book defers the second to where it belongs. The pattern operates wherever economic systems have no use for the appearance of consent.

**Normative routinization.** When cost-bearing has gone on long enough to become accepted as a baseline, the cost of doing business, the routine subsidy everyone knows is there but no one prices, severance can operate with full information and no formal coercion, because the cultural infrastructure has done the work that distance and force did in earlier configurations. The miner accepts the lung damage as part of the job. The town accepts the eventual mine closure as the way these things go. The consumer accepts cheap electricity as honestly priced because the cost has never appeared on any invoice they have seen.

**Abundance masking.** When a resource appears unlimited, no one prices it, and the costs it bears can flow continuously without anyone computing them. Air on Earth is the paradigmatic case: no one prices the air we breathe; the abundance illusion is total. The same illusion has applied to ocean fisheries before collapse, to the Ogallala aquifer before drawdown became visible, to the atmosphere's carbon-absorbing capacity, to soil's regenerative capacity. The distinction that matters is that the abundance was real, not imagined. The air over McDowell County in 1960 was genuinely abundant; the atmosphere did absorb combusted carbon for two centuries without observable saturation. What the abundance hid was not its existence but its finiteness, and the hiding was structural, not a failure of anyone's perception. Industrial architectures were built on the working assumption of unlimited atmospheric absorbing capacity. The mask is the architecture, not the eye. The framework formalizes this in Theorem 10.3 (Abundance Masking) of the Technical Appendix, and the Commons Inversion Test is the apparatus for detecting it. [RELOCATE TO Technical Appendix: the full four-way defense of the term *masking* against *illusion*, *abundance-as-cover*, *false abundance*, and *abundance concealment* belongs in the apparatus, not the chapter spine; the load-bearing real-but-finite-versus-perceptual-error distinction is retained above.]

On Mars, in a habitat where ten thousand people share the same atmosphere, none of the six patterns can produce concealment. Geographic distance collapses by construction. Informational asymmetry collapses; the closed system is small, feedback is direct, no party can know less than the others know. Power asymmetry collapses; the administrator's family is among the cost-bearers, with no coercive infrastructure available to deploy. Normative routinization has not had time to develop in a 10,000-person system. Temporal asymmetry remains technically available, present-self can under-price future-self, but the closed feedback loop makes the consequences visible immediately, in a way the same mechanism on Earth would conceal for years or generations. And abundance masking collapses by construction; there is no possible illusion of unlimited atmosphere or water in a closed habitat. Every cubic meter of air and every gallon of water is enumerated. Every breath and every drink visibly counts against finite stock.

Severance is therefore not impossible on Mars. It is impossible to *hide*. The administrator who under-prices for short-term gain still produces severance against future-self and future-population, but the closed feedback loop makes the cost visible immediately, and the framework's logic requires that what is visible be priced. What Mars exposes, by removing concealment, is not just the scarcity of specific resources but the entire ambient abundance Earth provides. A Mars resident visiting Earth would not see Earth as a place with abundant resources. They would see free air. Free water falling from the sky on a regular schedule. Free temperature regulation. Free solar irradiance, continuously and without maintenance. Free gravitational stability provided by the Moon, including the tidal cycle that drives nutrient circulation through the oceans. Free magnetic-field shielding against radiation that would otherwise strip the atmosphere. Free atmospheric filtering of the ultraviolet wavelengths that would otherwise sterilize the surface. All the conditions Earth provides as ambient are the conditions a closed-habitat resident would calculate maintenance budgets for. Earth's ambient abundance is the dominant cost-hiding apparatus on this planet, and the Commons Inversion Test is the tool for detecting which parts of it are actually finite and being drawn down.

This has an implication for the policy work the later chapters take up. The closer the policy brings cost-bearers and value-capturers, by collapsing geographic distance through spatial cost severance pricing, by collapsing temporal asymmetry through intergenerational accounting, by collapsing informational asymmetry through transparent supply-chain disclosure, by collapsing power asymmetry through institutional accountability requirements, by collapsing normative routinization through legible pricing of what the baseline has hidden, by collapsing abundance masking through honest stock accounting, the more automatic honest pricing becomes. This is Polanyi's re-embedding stated as engineering: shorten the distance, restore the feedback loop, and the market re-internalizes the cost it was severing. None of these interventions addresses the structural test by itself, which is why a policy regime that addresses each named pattern with surface-level interventions can still leave cost severance fully operational.

### Not all resources matter equally

A natural consequence of the cross-scenario testing is the recognition that the framework does not treat all resources the same, because the framework's parameters do not treat all resources the same. For some resources the residual commons value collapses to near the market price; the framework adds little, and market pricing is approximately correct. For others it is orders of magnitude higher than the market price; the framework reveals severe and systematic underpricing. The difference between these two classes is a property of the resource itself, not a choice made by the analyst. This is also the answer to the charge that the framework is a choose-your-own-adventure machine that can manufacture any number it wants. The parameters drive the output, and the parameters are facts about the resource, not preferences of the person running the model.

The book calls this property the *existential substitutability gap*. A resource's existential substitutability gap is the difference between its substitutability for current industrial applications and its substitutability for possible long-term civilizational applications, the applications, known and unknown, that a species with a two-hundred-year planning horizon would want to keep options open for. In the framework's notation it is the difference between maximum substitutability for industrial uses and maximum substitutability for existential ones. The concept borrows the existential-risk lineage's concern with resources critical to civilizational continuity without adopting longtermism's specific ethical commitments; the framework needs no claim about aggregating welfare across all possible futures to observe that some materials cannot be replaced if they are used up. [CITATION GAP: if the existential-risk lineage (Bostrom, Ord, MacAskill) is to be named at all, the citations belong in a footnote; the chapter no longer wages the EA-positioning argument inline, so confirm whether even the bare lineage reference is wanted here.]

Coal has an existential substitutability gap near zero. Electricity from coal is readily substitutable today, solar, wind, nuclear, hydro, geothermal, and natural-gas generation are all available alternatives for the same kilowatt-hour, and no foreseeable civilizational application requires coal as a specific input. Sand, common iron ore, and common stone are similar: abundant, substitutable, zero gap.

Conventional oil, copper, and zinc have a low gap. They are substitutable for most current applications, with no clear civilizational applications that uniquely require them.

Uranium, lithium, and cobalt have a moderate gap. They are substitutable for some applications, uranium where thorium fuel cycles or eventual fusion offer alternatives, lithium where sodium-ion and solid-state research is advancing, cobalt where iron-phosphate chemistries are gaining, but they hold important strategic roles in specific technologies, and future applications are plausibly though not certainly dependent on these particular materials.

Rare earth elements and platinum group metals have a high gap. Their specific magnetic, catalytic, and electronic properties are required for technologies that have not yet been invented but which almost any civilization with advanced manufacturing will need: the neodymium and dysprosium that make permanent-magnet motors possible at usable energy densities, the platinum and palladium that catalyze hydrogen-fuel-cell and electrolyzer chemistries, the gallium and germanium and indium that enable the semiconductor architectures of next-generation computing. Substitutes exist for some current applications; there is no clear path to substitutes for all applications; there is a high probability that at least some future applications cannot be served without them.

Helium-3 occupies a unique category, a conditionally high gap. If deuterium-helium-3 fusion becomes viable, helium-3 becomes one of the most strategically important resources in the accessible solar system. If it does not, helium-3 is a curiosity. The classification is contingent on a technology that has not yet been demonstrated.

The ranking is not a complete map of what to reserve and what to extract. It is the input that, combined with the stock estimate and the externality tail for each resource class, produces the reservation policy the later chapters will develop. For the moment it is enough to observe that the ranking produces intuitively correct answers, and that the framework's logic supports those intuitions: the things we should worry about reserving for our descendants are the things whose existential substitutability gap is high, because those are the things our descendants might need and cannot replace. This is also the framework's reply to the substitution optimism associated with the Simon side of the Simon-Ehrlich wager. The framework grants the optimist everything where the gap is near zero, coal, sand, common ore, and refuses the optimism only where substitution genuinely cannot be assumed. It is selective optimism, applied resource by resource, rather than a blanket faith or a blanket fear.

The hand-forward sentence is retained below.] Chapter 9 returns to this ranking when it asks what the policy architecture for the renewable-energy transition has to accomplish, since getting the transition wrong on exactly these high-gap resources would foreclose the very civilizational capabilities the transition is meant to deliver.

### Mars is a false analogy

A careful reader will push back on the universality claim in a specific way that deserves a direct response.

Mars is a simple system: ten thousand people, three ice deposits, a closed habitat with transparent feedback loops. Misprice the water and people die within a decade; the consequence is immediate and lethal. Earth is a complex system with unclear boundaries, vast and partially understood stocks, continuous discovery of new deposits, ongoing technological innovation that can change the substitutability of almost any resource, and political and economic feedback loops that operate on timescales the Mars thought experiment does not reproduce. Saying "the math is the same" ignores that the systems are qualitatively different. On Mars, mispricing kills quickly. On Earth, mispricing kills slowly, distantly, in populations whose deaths are not easily attributed to the specific extraction that contributed to them. The habitat administrator gets the answer right because she cannot afford to get it wrong. The terrestrial policymaker, extractor, and consumer operate under no comparable constraint.

The objection is legitimate and deserves a specific response.

It is correct that Earth is more complex than Mars. It is not correct that this complexity makes the framework inapplicable. Complexity affects the parameters the framework uses; the stock estimates are less certain, the substitutability curves are more contested, the externality tails are harder to bound, the discount factor is the subject of decades of unresolved argument. Complexity does not affect the structure of the pricing problem, which is: a finite commons is being extracted, costs and benefits are being distributed across space and time, and the question is how to price the extraction in a way that reflects those distributions.

The reason the Mars thought experiment is useful is not that Mars is Earth. It is that Mars strips away the layers of uncertainty that allow Earth's cost severance to persist under the cover of ambiguity. On Mars the parameters are stark enough that the correct answer is unmistakable. On Earth the parameters are moderate enough that reasonable people can disagree about them, which is precisely the condition under which cost severance has been permitted to operate at the scale it has. The framework's function is not to pretend Earth is simple. Its function is to make the structural logic visible where Earth's moderate parameters would otherwise obscure it.

This is how physics uses frictionless planes and thermodynamics uses perfect insulators, not because the world contains such things, but because the limiting case exposes the underlying law. The law, once exposed, applies in the messy real world with the appropriate adjustments for friction and leakage. The Mars administrator's water ice problem is the frictionless plane of resource economics. The principles it reveals apply, imperfectly and messily, with all the real-world noise Earth provides, to every extraction decision that has ever been made on this planet.

A different reader will object on different grounds. Mars is a thought experiment. There is no actual habitat, no administrator, no consortium, no decision pending. The entire scenario is, in the dismissive sense of the word, science fiction, and importing fictional scenarios into a serious argument about resource economics is a category error.

The objection is one this book takes seriously, and one the methodological literature took up long ago and answered. Thought experiments are not predictions; they are stress tests of the logical apparatus. A frictionless plane is fictional. So is an ideal gas, a perfectly rational agent, a market without transaction costs, an electron at rest in an inertial frame. Each of these has been more useful to the development of its discipline than any number of empirically attested cases, and each is foundational to disciplines that are now, in the most banal real-world sense, obviously not science fiction. The point is not that Mars is real. The point is that the framework's structure produces outputs that can be checked against intuitions thoughtful readers actually hold. If the framework gave the wrong answer in the Mars habitat, if it told the administrator to accept the consortium's terms, or if it said the rare-earths-as-self-bearing case was indistinguishable from the asteroid-iron case, readers would notice, and the framework would be falsified by an exercise that costs nothing but careful reading. The thought experiment does work the empirical record cannot do: it subjects the framework to scenarios where the right answer is unambiguous, and checks whether the framework gets there. The framework that passes that test is the one worth applying to the harder, noisier cases the empirical record actually contains.

The thought experiment also has, on a long enough horizon, a structural reason for being non-fiction. The Sun's main-sequence luminosity is increasing on a timescale that mainstream solar-physics models project will make Earth uninhabitable for biological civilizations on the order of 500 million to 1 billion years from now, well before the red-giant phase that fully engulfs the planet roughly 7.5 billion years out. [CITATION GAP: solar luminosity making Earth uninhabitable in 500M-1B years, red giant ~7.5B years, needs a solar-physics citation.] On those timescales, whether human civilization or its descendants will attempt to inhabit other worlds in order to continue surviving is not a question of whether but of when. The framework's behavior on bounded off-world habitats is therefore not a methodological abstraction; it is a coherent forward-looking civilizational concern, even if the timescale is far enough out that no present-day decision turns on it. This argument is methodological, not advocational: the framework holds whatever the answer to the off-world-settlement policy question turns out to be, and there are serious arguments on both sides of it.

And to the related objection, that for an uninhabited asteroid the framework reduces to market pricing because the externality terms collapse to zero, the response is that this is not a weakness. It is a strength, and it is the precise answer to the reader who says the framework is just Pigou in new clothes. Arthur Pigou's tax internalizes an externality the market has missed. The framework agrees with Pigou exactly to the extent that the externality is the whole of the missing value. Where the social cost of carbon is the only thing the market is failing to price, the framework's number and the Pigouvian number converge. Where the missing value is larger than the priceable externality, where the foreclosure of an irreplaceable option for an unborn generation is at stake, the bond exceeds what a Pigouvian tax would charge, because the residual commons value exceeds the measurable externality. A well-calibrated instrument should agree with existing instruments where those instruments are correct, and diverge only where they are incorrect. The framework agrees with market pricing for asteroid iron because in that specific case market pricing is approximately right. When all the novel terms collapse to zero, the residual commons value collapses to the market price, and the framework's recommendation is the market's recommendation. The framework adds value only where the market misses something. It correctly adds nothing where the market misses nothing. A framework that always disagreed with market pricing would be as useless as one that always agreed. The one that correctly identifies when, and by how much, the market errs is the one worth having.

### The framework across time, and the immune response

Two more pressure points on the universality claim deserve a direct response.

The first is temporal. This book has described cost severance in the industrial and post-industrial economies of the last two centuries. A careful reader might ask whether the pattern is a feature of capitalism specifically, whether a different economic system would produce a different result. The answer is no. This is, in Fraser's terms, expropriation rather than exploitation, and expropriation predates the wage relation by millennia. The clearest way to see it is to look at the pattern operating in civilizations that had no contact with each other and no shared cultural or ideological inheritance.

The pyramids at Giza were built, over roughly seventy years in the Egyptian Old Kingdom, by a workforce of skilled craftsmen and conscripted rotational laborers drawn from the agricultural population. Modern archaeology has substantially revised the older image of chattel slavery under the lash: the workers were paid in rations, medically cared for, and properly buried. [CITATION GAP: the revised consensus on Giza labor conditions, attributed in the prior draft to Mark Lehner's Giza Plateau Mapping Project and Zahi Hawass's workers'-cemetery excavations, needs those citations.] They were not slaves in the plantation sense. They were also not free in any sense a modern reader would recognize. The centralized pharaonic economy left no alternative employment and no political structure within which refusal was possible. The pharaoh extracted the value: political legitimacy, divine kingship, a monument guaranteeing afterlife provisions. The workers bore the cost. The monument is still visible, and its visibility is permanent evidence that the mechanism operated four and a half millennia before capitalism existed.

Twenty-three hundred years later, on the other side of the world, the first centralized Chinese empire commissioned the initial unified Great Wall and the First Emperor's mausoleum complex near modern Xi'an, both built by mass conscripted labor whose exit was structurally foreclosed and whose mortality the records report as severe. [CITATION GAP: Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian*, compiled approximately a century after the Qin's fall, reports the labor figures and the dynasty's collapse within a few years of the First Emperor's death; cite Sima Qian and a modern scholarly source, and keep the labor figures hedged as contested reports.] The legalist doctrine that organized this extraction was explicit: strict penalties for missed conscription quotas, efficient production regardless of cost-bearer welfare. The dynasty's harshness was so extreme that it collapsed within a few years under the rebellions it provoked. The pattern itself, value extracted by the imperial state, cost borne by labor that could not refuse, is recognizable across every line of the record. The Great Wall is still standing. So are the figures.

Egypt and Qin China had no contact. Their cosmologies, religions, political systems, and philosophies of rulership were incommensurable. The mechanism operated in both. Closer to our own time, feudal lords extracted agricultural surplus from serfs who worked land they did not own, bearing the costs of that extraction in their bodies, their families, and a poverty from which exit was structurally foreclosed. The lord extracted the value. The serf bore the cost. The narrative that accompanied it, divine order, natural hierarchy, the Great Chain of Being, the serf's station as God-ordained, is structurally the same rhetorical move that later ideological languages continue to perform with updated vocabulary. The lord called it protection. The twentieth-century company town called it paternalism. Today's extraction apologists call it culture. The function is identical: a narrative that frames the cost-bearer's condition as a property of the cost-bearer rather than a product of the extraction.

Cost severance is not a feature of capitalism. It is a pattern that finds whatever economic system is available, and the system in place becomes the delivery vehicle, not the root cause. Capitalism is the current and most efficient delivery vehicle. Feudal agriculture was a previous one. Pharaonic divine kingship was an earlier one. Qin legalism ran its own compressed version. Soviet industrial planning ran a post-capitalist version. The mechanism does not require a specific ownership structure. It requires the conditions Chapter 5 named, and those conditions are not ideologically parochial. If a reader wants evidence that the mechanism is older than any of the institutions we argue about, the pyramids and the Great Wall are that evidence. They have been standing longer than any of our political vocabularies. [RELOCATE TO Ch 5: the full pyramids/Qin/feudal mechanism-is-older-than-capitalism treatment overlaps Ch 5's mechanism work; if Ch 5 already enumerates the across-time evidence, this section should compress to its structural claim and reference Ch 5 as owner. Flagged as a cross-chapter MOVE for deliberate decision, not executed here.]

The second pressure point is the mechanism's ability to generate its own immune response, and it is the one a financialization-minded reader will press hardest. When cost severance becomes publicly visible and consumers demand accountability, the extraction architecture can manufacture a performance of accountability that does not actually reattach the costs it claims to. The Kimberley Process certification of conflict-free diamonds is the textbook case. The consumer pays an ethical premium. The diamond is certified. The premium stays with the retailer and the certification infrastructure; it does not reach the miners whose conditions the cost was born in. [CITATION GAP: the empirical claim that the Kimberley "conflict-free" premium stays with retailer/certification infrastructure rather than reaching miners needs a source.] The bond is severed. The certificate is a performance of non-severance.

This is exactly the objection Katharina Pistor's work on the legal coding of capital raises against any pricing instrument: that capital's defining talent is to absorb a reform and re-emit it as a tradable, fee-generating, ultimately extractive instrument, so that a residual commons bond risks becoming one more financialized asset that satisfies fiduciary appearance without altering capital flow. The pattern generalizes beyond diamonds, to carbon offsets that reduce the marketing cost of emissions without reducing emissions, to ESG screens that satisfy fiduciary appearance without redirecting capital. A framework that ignores this adaptation will be captured by it. The answer is not to abandon pricing but to design the instrument so that re-embedding, in Polanyi's sense, is enforced by its verification structure rather than left to good intentions: an accountability bond that does not demonstrably reach the cost-bearing party is, by the framework's own definition, not a bond at all but a simulated reattachment, and is to be scored as severance still operating. Whether the verification structure can actually resist the financialization adaptation is the central design problem, and it is Chapter 9's, not this chapter's. [RELOCATE TO Ch 9: the design problem of building verification structures that resist simulated reattachment, including the Public Choice rent-seeking-architecture analysis and the inventory of partial implementations, is owned by Ch 9; this chapter raises the objection and names the answer-in-principle (Polanyi re-embedding enforced by verification), then hands forward. If this material is cut further here it MUST be guaranteed delivered in Ch 9.]

Together these two pressure points show that the mechanism is older than its most visible contemporary forms and more sophisticated than its most visible contemporary critics, which is exactly why the framework needed to be tested at the limits, on Mars, on asteroids, across centuries, and against the system's own immune responses. What survives these tests is an instrument that holds where the parameters change and names its limits where it cannot.

### What comes next

The universality test establishes, as cleanly as a thought experiment can establish anything, that the framework is an instrument rather than an ideology. It produces different answers for different situations. It agrees with market pricing where market pricing is correct and diverges where market pricing is wrong. It names its own limits where its externalities are not priceable. And it prices the one object underneath all the cases, the fictitious commodity Polanyi and Fraser diagnosed but never costed, so that re-embedding the market becomes a matter of accounting rather than hope.

Before turning to what that response would look like in practice, the subject of the remaining chapters, the book has one more accounting to do. The framework has been presented in abstract terms, validated against terrestrial cases, and stress-tested across the solar system. What has not yet been done, in concrete aggregate, is to ask what the honest invoice looks like when the framework is applied to a single real extraction we can walk through tier by tier.

The next chapter does that, for the resource and the community the book opened with. One ton of Appalachian coal, from a specific mine, in a specific county, in a specific year. Every cost category named, every tier priced conservatively, every number defensible. And the result placed against the four-dollar-and-fifty-cent figure the market cleared the transaction on.

Look at the number. Look at the price on the invoice. Notice the gap.
