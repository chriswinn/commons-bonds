# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 6: Three Ways of Counting

---

If cost severance is real, if market prices systematically understate the true cost of extracting from the commons, then how large is the gap? The previous chapters have shown, case by case, that the gap exists. Appalachian coal. Gulf oil. Libby vermiculite. A Chesapeake that is not the water it once was. Each is an illustration of the mechanism.

None, by itself, answers the question a serious reader is entitled to ask: how do we know the cases are representative? How do we know we are not simply selecting disasters?

The answer matters because the framework's credibility depends on it. A mechanism demonstrated once is an anecdote. A mechanism demonstrated across cases is a pattern. A mechanism whose price can be computed, by multiple independent methods, and whose direction never flips across them, is a structural claim about how the economy works.

This chapter does the computing. Three approaches, built on different foundations, applied to the same historical cases. If the approaches converge, if three different accounting methods produce estimates in the same range, then the gap is not a methodological artifact. It is a measurable feature of the extraction economy.

The through-question the chapter holds is simple. Am I looking at the full honest accounting? Not the accounting the market already does, which prices the coal and the labor and the lease. The full accounting: what extraction takes from the people downwind, from the county that empties out, from the generations who will need what was burned before they were born. The chapter closes by saying plainly what the computation does not resolve. The carbon term dominates the fossil-fuel numbers and is politically volatile. The substitutability estimates are themselves uncertain. The honest answer is not that the model eliminates uncertainty. It is that the model structures uncertainty, telling us which parameters matter and by how much each one moves the price.

The three approaches are bottom-up damage accounting, a real-options model, and the residual commons value framework. I will introduce each, apply each to McDowell County coal and to several other cases we have already met, and then compare their outputs in a single table.

---

### Approach 1 — Bottom-Up Damage Accounting

Start with the method that needs no theory. Take one ton of Appalachian coal. Walk through every cost category the extraction produces. Enumerate them. Price each. Add.

Begin with health. The federal Black Lung Benefits Program's roughly forty-four billion dollars in distributions through 2009, allocated across the national tonnage of coal produced since the program's 1969 establishment, runs between one and one and a half dollars per ton. Chapter 2 walks the program's Trust Fund, its funding gap, its debt trajectory, and its structural mechanics, in detail. Add Medicare and Medicaid costs shifted to the public ledger by miners whose employers went bankrupt before the obligations matured. Add excess cardiovascular mortality from particulate exposure in communities downwind of coal plants. Add the respiratory illness in children who were never in the mine. A conservative national figure sits between two and four dollars per ton.

Walk outside. Price the creek running orange with acid mine drainage. Price the tailings ponds whose liners were advertised for a century and are already failing at forty years. Price the headwater streams buried under mountaintop-removal overburden. Price the reclamation bond that was supposed to cover the closure, and note, as Chapter 2 did, that across Appalachia those bonds fall billions short of the work actually required. Environmental remediation, allocated nationally, runs another one to three dollars per ton.

Price the communities. Not the extraction jobs, those went onto the benefit side of the ledger when the mine was operating. Price what remained. Price the outmigration that stripped the tax base. Price the schools that closed because the children left. Price the drug-epidemic costs that mining communities have borne at rates the rest of the country does not match. Price the life-expectancy gap: thirteen years in McDowell County, against the national average. [CITATION GAP: McDowell County thirteen-year life-expectancy gap vs. national average — needs a county-health-rankings, CDC, or academic source inline.]

That thirteen-year gap is a legacy effect, a measurable consequence of cost-severance harm that persists across generations rather than dissolving when the mine closes. The methodology for pricing legacy effects of this kind travels from William Darity Jr. and collaborators, whose work prices structural longevity gaps as legacy effects of extraction across generations; Chapter 5 walks that lineage at depth. McDowell's thirteen-year gap is the same methodology calibrated to a coal-extraction-affected population. The mechanism is portable; the per-context calibration is what differs.

A conservative community-cost estimate comes in between five and fifteen dollars per ton.

Add them. Health yields two to four. Environment yields one to three. Community costs yield five to fifteen. A conservative bottom-up total, absent carbon, is eight to twenty-two dollars per ton. Against a 1960 mine-mouth price of roughly four dollars and fifty cents, even without climate accounting, the market has been capturing somewhere between a quarter and a half of the actual cost. Not a rounding error. A factor.

---

Then add the carbon. This is where the arithmetic stops being conservative and starts being contested. One ton of bituminous coal from the Pocahontas seam, fully combusted, releases approximately 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide. At the Environmental Protection Agency's 2023 estimate of one hundred ninety dollars per ton of carbon dioxide, drawn from Rennert and colleagues at a two-percent discount rate, the carbon externality alone is approximately four hundred ninety-six dollars per ton of coal: an order of magnitude larger than every other tier combined.

Add the carbon to the rest, and the honest total for a ton of McDowell coal lands at roughly five hundred ten dollars per ton. Standard externality pricing, the Pigouvian floor that charges only for health, environment, and carbon, sits between five hundred four and five hundred eighteen dollars per ton. Against a mine-mouth price of four dollars and fifty cents, the market is capturing well under one percent of the true cost. The carbon term dominates. (The full ton-by-ton decomposition of these McDowell figures is Chapter 8's work; this chapter uses the figures, and the convergence across methods is what it tests.)

Apply the same exercise to other cases. Deepwater Horizon: production revenue of roughly four and a quarter billion dollars against documented litigation costs, restoration obligations, ecological losses, and public-health spending in the range of sixty-five to seventy billion dollars. (A one-event disaster, so the cost figure is bounded by what has been counted rather than by an ongoing extraction lifetime.) Call this ratio of true cost to market price the **Intergenerational Pricing Gap**: fifteen to seventeen times for Deepwater Horizon. Libby, Montana: around one hundred million dollars in lifetime mine revenue against five to eight billion dollars in total documented cost, the sum of direct settlements, federal cleanup spending, and the long tail of asbestos-related illness across thousands of cases. A pricing gap of fifty to eighty times. Exxon Valdez: five and a half million dollars in product value of the oil spilled, against seven to ten billion dollars in cleanup, litigation, and long-run ecological damage. A gap in the range of a thousand times and more. [CITATION GAP: the prose figures here (Deepwater $65–70B; Libby $5–8B; Exxon Valdez $7–10B and $5.5M product value) should carry the same public sourcing as the convergence table below — NOAA, DOJ, the 2016 BP Consent Decree, the EPA Libby Superfund record, the Exxon Valdez Trustee Council — rather than running bare in prose.]

Bottom-up accounting is a blunt instrument. It captures only the costs somebody has thought to count. It cannot price what no one has yet noticed. It is the accounting equivalent of a flashlight in a warehouse: what it illuminates is real, and what it misses is whatever is dark. And its output for fossil fuels lives or dies by the social cost of carbon, which lives or dies by whichever administration happens to be in office. The Obama EPA said forty-two dollars per ton of carbon dioxide. The first Trump administration said one to seven. The Biden EPA said one hundred ninety. The same molecule. The same atmosphere. The same physics. Three estimates spanning two orders of magnitude.

The fight over the social cost of carbon is itself a proxy war over acceptable levels of cost severance. Every dollar of social cost that is not priced is a dollar of cost severed onto communities and future generations. The choice of three dollars per ton is not a conservative estimate in any meaningful sense. It is a political statement, an announcement that the extractor should pay almost nothing for the carbon released. The choice of one hundred ninety is a different political statement, informed by a larger body of climate science, but still a choice. There is no price-free default. The available alternative, current market pricing with no externality adjustment at all, is not neutral either. It is a specific and aggressive assumption: that the health costs borne by McDowell County are worth zero, that the climate damage from atmospheric carbon is worth zero, that permanently foreclosing a non-renewable resource imposes zero cost on future generations. That assumption is not more rigorous than the framework's. It is less rigorous, because it contradicts every piece of available evidence.

The framework does not eliminate uncertainty; it structures uncertainty. It tells the reader which parameters matter and by how much each one moves the output. A weather model does not tell you with certainty whether it will rain Thursday; it tells you the probability, given what we know. The weather model is not useless because it contains uncertainty. It is useful because it expresses the uncertainty in a form that supports decisions. The same logic holds here. The qualitative result, that the market underprices extraction, is robust across every social-cost-of-carbon estimate above zero. The quantitative result is not; it is only as precise as the parameter chosen. What the framework does is make that dependency explicit, telling the reader which parameter moves the output by how much, rather than hiding the judgment behind a single headline number.

One distinction belongs here, because it is the easiest methodological error a careful reader could make. The bottom-up accounting prices what carbon *does* once emitted: the damage side, anchored by the social cost of carbon. The framework's forward-looking residual commons value, in Approach 3 below, also asks a different question: what would it cost to engineer the substitute for the function the commons was performing? For atmospheric carbon, the answer to the second question is direct-air-capture, which currently runs in the hundreds of dollars per ton of carbon dioxide and is projected to fall as the technology scales. Both numbers belong in honest carbon accounting; they answer different questions, and the framework's reporting discipline keeps them separate so the reader is not tempted to add them together or mistake one for the other. (The direct-air-capture horizons that anchor the replacement-cost method are detailed under Method 1 below.)

Even with its blind spot, the bottom-up method gives a floor. It says the market underprices extraction by at least a factor of several, and potentially by factors of tens or hundreds. It does not tell us the ceiling. For that, we need a method that does not rely on enumerating costs one by one.

### Approach 2 — The Real Options Model

The second method speaks the language of Wall Street.

A barrel of oil in the ground is not merely a stock of energy. It is an option: the right, but not the obligation, to extract at any future time. Options have value. The longer you wait, the more you learn about what the resource will be worth, what substitutes will exist, and what the externality costs will prove to be. To exercise an option early is to destroy the information value that waiting would have produced.

This is standard finance. It is how every oil major already values its undeveloped reserves internally. The model is not radical. It is the mainstream model.

What the standard model omits is the social side of the ledger. The private option value, what a resource owner gains by waiting, is only a fraction of the total option value. The social option value also includes the externality costs avoided by extracting later, when remediation technology is better and substitutes have arrived; the substitutability that develops while you wait, such as solar cells at three cents per kilowatt-hour that did not exist in 1960 [CITATION GAP: 3¢/kWh solar figure — needs an LBNL or Lazard LCOE source]; and the information about resource criticality that accumulates over decades. We did not know, in 1960, that certain rare earth elements would be required for every piece of modern electronics.

When the full social option value of an unextracted barrel exceeds the net revenue from extracting it today, extraction is socially premature. Not suboptimal. Premature. The economy is exercising an option before the information the option contains has been collected.

Apply this to McDowell County coal in 1960. The mine-mouth price was roughly four dollars and fifty cents. Net revenue per ton, after extraction costs, was well under that. The social option value of the same ton, priced by a later substitutability window where renewables are already cheaper than coal in most markets, by the avoided externalities, and by the information that would have been collected about Appalachian community collapse, is not four dollars and fifty cents. It is on the order of tens of dollars at minimum, and plausibly hundreds once the long tail of externalities is included.

So 1960 coal was socially premature. The option should not have been exercised. That we exercised it anyway is not a failure of information, much of which was suppressed [CITATION GAP: claim that information about coal's community costs was deliberately suppressed — needs a source or softening]; it is a failure of pricing. The market gave the option to the extractor at no cost. The social side of the ledger was never a line item.

The real-options model is useful because it reaches an audience the damage-function approach does not. A chief financial officer who would dismiss a bottom-up list of externalities as bleeding-heart arithmetic will recognize option value as a real quantity. She values her own company's reserves this way every quarter. The only change is the addition of the social terms.

Apply it across cases, and the same answer emerges. Every extraction event we have examined produced net revenue substantially below the social option value of leaving the resource in place. The direction does not flip.

### Approach 3 — The RCV Model

The third approach is the one the book has been building toward, and its architecture differs from the first two. The bottom-up method enumerates costs. The real-options method prices what is lost by exercising early. The residual commons value model prices something neither captures: what every future generation loses by not having the resource, weighted by the probability that no substitute will exist by the time they need it.

Before the formula, the plain-English version of what the formula computes, because the argument has to survive without the mathematics for readers who would rather not do integrals.

The true cost of extracting from the commons, most dramatically for a non-renewable resource and, with adjustment, for a renewable one extracted faster than it regenerates, has two components the market does not price.

The first is the foreclosure cost. When you extract a barrel of oil, every future generation loses access to that barrel. If a substitute exists, if renewable energy can do everything oil does for every application oil is used for, then the foreclosure does not matter; future generations do not miss the oil because they have something better. But if no substitute exists for certain applications, if you need physical petroleum for specific materials, specific chemicals, certain industrial processes that nothing else can replace, then the foreclosure is real. The future generation that needs the oil and cannot get it bears a genuine cost.

Foreclosure cost depends on substitutability: how quickly, how completely, and at what cost alternatives can replace the resource. For coal in electricity generation, substitutability is high and rising fast; renewables are already displacing coal at scale [CITATION GAP: "renewables displacing coal at scale" — pairs with the 3¢/kWh figure above; one shared source would cover both]. For rare earth elements in advanced electronics and propulsion, substitutability is low; no alternative provides the same magnetic, catalytic, or luminescent properties, and materials scientists have been looking for decades. For helium in cryogenic applications, substitutability may be effectively zero. For fossil aquifer water, substitutability is zero on any timescale that matters to the people drinking it.

The second component is the externality tail. When coal is mined, the health damage does not stop when the mine closes. Black lung kills miners decades after their last shift. Acid mine drainage runs for generations. Carbon stays in the atmosphere for centuries. These ongoing costs are real, measurable, and currently unpriced. A substitute for coal does not clean up the mine; a substitute for oil does not pull carbon back out of the atmosphere. The externality tail runs independently of substitutability. The word *tail* is borrowed deliberately from statistics, where the tail of a distribution is the region where cost survives at extreme values rather than collapsing to zero; for these damages the cumulative cost is dominated by the long-running tail rather than by the immediate harm. The usage joins the tradition of Weitzman's work on fat-tailed climate damages. (The full defense of the term, and why weaker alternatives fail to carry the distribution-shape claim, is in the Technical Appendix.)

The residual commons value combines the two components: foreclosure cost, weighted by the probability that no substitute exists at each future time, plus the externality tail, both integrated across the full horizon the extraction affects. When the residual commons value exceeds the market price, which, as I will show, it does in every case we have tested, the difference is the cost that has been severed.

Now the formula, which is a summary of the plain-English argument just made:

$$\mathrm{RCV}(R, t_0) = \int_{t_0}^{\infty} \bigl\{[1 - S(t \mid t_0)] \cdot U(R, t, Q(t)) + E(R, t)\bigr\} \cdot D(t, t_0) \, dt$$

(formal articulation in [Technical Appendix §3 — RCV Quantification — Three Ways of Counting](../../core/technical-appendix/TechnicalAppendix_v2.0.0.html#sec-3-rcv-quantification))

The integral sign announces that we are summing contributions across all future time. The bracketed expression is the per-period cost: what is lost in year *t*, discounted back to year *t₀*, weighted by how likely it is that anything in that year can substitute for the lost resource.

Term by term:

- $[1 - S(t \mid t_0)]$ is the probability that no adequate substitute exists at time *t*, given that extraction occurred at *t₀*. If a perfect substitute exists, this term is zero and the foreclosure cost vanishes for that period. If no substitute exists, this term is one and the full foreclosure cost counts.
- $U(R, t, Q(t))$ is the utility future generation *t* derives from having one unit of the resource available in place, given the remaining stock at time *t*. As the stock depletes, this term rises, because each remaining unit becomes more valuable.
- $E(R, t)$ is the externality flow at time *t*: ongoing health damage, remediation, climate damage, community disruption. It runs independently of substitutability and independently of whether the resource has been fully depleted.
- $D(t, t_0)$ is the social discount factor, following Weitzman's declining-rate framework: under uncertainty about the long-horizon rate, the certainty-equivalent rate declines toward the lowest plausible value. This converts an unresolvable philosophical question, what is the correct discount rate, into a treatable empirical one, what is the certainty-equivalent given uncertainty.

Cost severance is what remains after you subtract from the residual commons value whatever accountability bond has actually been posted:

$$\mathrm{CS} = \mathrm{RCV} - B$$

And the intergenerational pricing gap is the ratio of true cost to market price:

$$\mathrm{IPG} = \mathrm{RCV} / P_{\text{market}}$$

That is the entire mathematical apparatus this chapter requires. The Appendix derives the convergence conditions, proves the integral is finite under reasonable assumptions, and demonstrates that for non-renewable resources under any existing accountability regime, cost severance is strictly positive. The reader who wants the proofs will find them there. The reader who wants the argument already has it.

Any cost admitted into the integral has to pass four gates, and the discipline is worth naming where the formula is specified, because it is what keeps the accounting honest rather than expansive. The first gate is the **Commons Inversion Test**: imagine the same physical extraction with the underlying commons made non-scarce, and ask whether the cost would still arise. If the cost survives the inversion, it is intrinsic to the activity, not a cost of commons-extraction, and it does not enter. If it vanishes, the cost is grounded in the commons-extraction relationship and is admitted. The second gate is **units consistency**: a candidate must be expressible as dollars per unit of resource per unit of time, which excludes costs specified only as ordinal scores until they are converted to dollar-equivalents with transparent methodology. The third gate is **boundedness**: the candidate's contribution to the integral must converge to a finite value, which excludes unbounded theoretical constructions that would otherwise swamp any finite total. The fourth gate is **independence**: a candidate must not double-count what an existing term already captures, which is the discipline that answers the overcounting objection directly. Each gate does work the others cannot; the full formal statements, worked examples, and step-by-step protocol are in the Technical Appendix's [Four Gates Admission Apparatus](../../core/technical-appendix/TechnicalAppendix_v2.0.0.html#sec-7-four-gates), which also derives the formula's general *n*-term form and shows the two-term expression above is the special case the convergence table uses.

### The Three Ways of Counting Inside RCV

Inside the residual commons value computation, a triangulation operates that the formula does not display on its face. The estimate is not produced by one method; it is produced by three. The reason is an institutional asymmetry that financial economics is well placed to recognize. Markets have a mechanism for pricing future profitability: discounted-cash-flow analysis, aggregated through equity and debt markets and embedded in every asset price as an implicit expectation about future returns. Markets have no equivalent mechanism for pricing future harms. No aggregating institution produces a comparable price signal for the externality tail, for foreclosure cost, for the long-tail damage of cost severance. The three estimation methods are the framework's substitute for that missing market mechanism on the harms side. Each anchors its estimate in a different empirical pathway; the convergence range across the three is what the framework reports.

**Method 1 — Replacement Cost.** What would it cost to engineer a substitute for the commons function extraction is consuming? For McDowell coal through its carbon dimension, the substitute for atmospheric carbon-removal is direct air capture. Climeworks's Orca facility in Iceland captures carbon dioxide at roughly a thousand dollars or more per ton at first-of-a-kind scale; the company's Mammoth facility and later generations project costs falling toward four to six hundred dollars per ton of net removal by 2030. Carbon Engineering's Stratos facility in Texas is projected at three to six hundred dollars per ton at full operation. The International Energy Agency's *Direct Air Capture 2022* review places scaled-up cost in the two-hundred-thirty to six-hundred-dollar range; the IPCC's AR6 Working Group III, under more optimistic learning-curve scenarios, projects mid-century costs as low as one to three hundred dollars per ton. Translated to a per-ton-of-coal figure across those horizons, Method 1 lands in the low hundreds to roughly two thousand dollars per ton of coal. This is the most directly priced of the three and the smallest step in epistemic ambition: the substitute is buildable, the cost is engineered, the floor is real. Its limit is that it anchors only the *substitutable* component; direct air capture engineers atmospheric removal but does not replace foreclosed petroleum hydrocarbons or restore destroyed communities, so Method 1 is treated as a floor. (Formal specification in [Technical Appendix §3.3](../../core/technical-appendix/TechnicalAppendix_v2.0.0.html#sec-3-rcv-quantification).)

**Method 2 — Revealed Preference.** What does a polity that explicitly chose to capture the rent from its extraction and pass it forward reveal about commons value through its observed restraint? Norway is the primary anchor: its sovereign wealth fund is a polity's revealed valuation of forward foreclosure-prevention, observed at scale, with a documented figure of approximately forty-eight dollars per barrel of oil equivalent. (Chapter 4 carries the fund's history and mechanics; here it serves only as the revealed-preference anchor.) The method does not translate Norway's per-barrel figure arithmetically to other extractions; it extracts what Norway has revealed about a polity's willingness to pay for foreclosure-prevention and applies that reasoning elsewhere. Applied to McDowell coal under comparable reasoning, Method 2 lands in the eight-to-eighty-eight-dollar-per-ton range. This is the empirically grounded middle of the triangulation: a real polity has actually paid for a real instance of forward foreclosure-prevention. Its limit, what Norway's accounting does not capture, is worked through in the Norway Backtest below; for that reason Method 2 sits as a lower bound on true residual commons value. (Formal specification in [Technical Appendix §3.4](../../core/technical-appendix/TechnicalAppendix_v2.0.0.html#sec-3-rcv-quantification).)

**Method 3 — Scarcity-Adjusted Option Value.** What is the option value of preserving the commons under explicit scarcity-and-irreversibility weighting? Applied to McDowell coal via Dixit and Pindyck's 1994 real-options framework, with the framework's scarcity multiplier (logarithmic in the ratio of commons stock to sustainable flow) and an irreversibility premium calibrated to the probability that the commons cannot be restored at any finite cost once extracted, Method 3 produces a wide per-ton range running into the thousands, with a mid-range estimate of a few thousand dollars per ton. For regimes where coal extraction operates as a commons-extinction event, the irreversibility premium dominates the scarcity multiplier. This is the framework's upper bound: a method that prices what extraction forecloses under explicit recognition that the foreclosed commons cannot be brought back at any finite cost. The wide range reflects parameter uncertainty in scarcity and irreversibility, not methodological imprecision. (Formal specification in [Technical Appendix §3.5](../../core/technical-appendix/TechnicalAppendix_v2.0.0.html#sec-3-rcv-quantification).)

The three methods together, Replacement Cost as floor, Revealed Preference as middle, Scarcity-Adjusted Option Value as ceiling, produce a convergence range rather than a point estimate. The reporting discipline requires that every estimate publish all three method figures plus the convergence range plus the identified sources of divergence; no single-method claim has standing. The same triangulation runs in reverse, where extraction has already occurred and the apparatus is applied backward to legacy effects already realized, the backward direction Chapter 5 develops. Run backward, Replacement Cost specializes to remediation cost, Revealed Preference specializes to revealed restitution (the case lineage of reparations economics that Darity and Mullen developed for harm accumulated through policy choices over generations), and Scarcity-Adjusted Option Value specializes to the option value extinguished at the time of past extraction, evaluable retrospectively from parameters known at analysis time. The methodology is direction-agnostic; the calibration adjusts per case. (The bidirectional structure is formalized in [Technical Appendix §5.5](../../core/technical-appendix/TechnicalAppendix_v2.0.0.html#sec-5-5-bidirectionality).)

### The Substitutability Function

Before the convergence result, a word about what the substitutability function is doing, because it is the model's most novel move and also its most vulnerable.

Previous approaches to pricing intergenerational costs relied almost entirely on the discount rate. How much do future generations matter compared to this one? Economists have argued about the answer since Ramsey in 1928 and have not converged. The Stern Review used 1.4 percent. Nordhaus used roughly 4 percent. Weitzman argued for a declining rate. Philosophers have argued that any positive discount rate on well-being is unjustifiable. The philosophical question refuses to be settled.

The substitutability function sidesteps the impasse. Instead of asking how much future generations matter, it asks how quickly we can develop alternatives. That is an empirical question. Engineers and materials scientists can estimate substitution timelines. Energy economists can model the cost trajectory of renewable technologies. Hydrologists can estimate aquifer recharge rates. The question is technical, not philosophical, and technical questions admit of progress.

The function has four natural properties. It starts near zero at the moment of extraction, because if a cheaper substitute already existed we would not be extracting. It rises over time, because substitutes, once invented, do not un-invent themselves. It approaches some asymptotic limit at or below one, because for some resources complete substitution may never be physically possible. And it depends on investment, because the timeline for a given substitute is not fixed but responsive to the price signals an honest pricing regime would generate.

The critic's objection is fair. The substitutability function does enormous work in the model, and in practice it has to be estimated. A critic can say, correctly, that we have moved the hard problem from choosing a discount rate to choosing a substitutability curve. True. But it is a better hard problem. Substitutability is empirical, and its error bars shrink with research; the discount rate is philosophical, and its error bars have not shrunk in a century. (Formal specification in [Technical Appendix §13](../../core/technical-appendix/TechnicalAppendix_v2.0.0.html#sec-13-substitutability-function-and-gap).)

### The Convergence

The three approaches, bottom-up, real-options, and residual commons value, are built on different foundations. They use different mathematical structures and make different assumptions about what to count and how to aggregate. If they all produced similar answers by accident, one would be entitled to suspicion. If they all produced similar answers because the underlying reality is real, one would expect exactly that.

A simpler framework would have offered a single estimation approach. It would have been simpler to teach, simpler to implement, simpler to defend, and it would have inherited that single approach's pathologies. Bottom-up accounting cannot capture costs no one has thought to count. The real-options model inherits the parameter uncertainty in the substitutability function. The residual commons value model depends on the stock-dependent utility and externality-tail estimates the analyst chooses. Each approach has a strength the other two lack and a blind spot the other two illuminate. Triangulation is the standard scientific response to estimation under uncertainty: produce three independent estimates from three independent angles, report the convergence range, treat divergence as informative rather than as failure. (The three-model convergence is formalized in [Technical Appendix §9](../../core/technical-appendix/TechnicalAppendix_v2.0.0.html#sec-9-three-model-convergence).)

The framework is not committed to a single number; it is committed to a method that makes the time-horizon attribution explicit. (Full reconciliation in [Technical Appendix §11.11](../../core/technical-appendix/TechnicalAppendix_v2.0.0.html#sec-11-11-ipg-reconciliation).)

The methods converge. Different assumptions, different foundations, the same qualitative conclusion and, in most cases, similar magnitudes. The direction never flips. In every case tested, market prices understate the true cost, always by a meaningful factor, sometimes by three or four orders of magnitude.

This is the chapter's central finding, and it is worth sitting with. Three independent accounting methods, built on economics from different decades and different schools, applied to the same historical extractions, agree, not because they were designed to agree but because the thing they are measuring is real. The gap between what extraction costs and what extraction charges is not an artifact of any one method. It is a feature of how the extraction economy actually works. That difference is not a rounding error. It is the subsidy by which the entire extraction economy runs.

---

Three questions arise at this point, and the chapter owes the reader an answer to each.

The first is the choose-your-own-adventure objection: that the model's output depends on parameter choices the analyst makes, and that different choices produce different numbers. This is partly correct, and the convergence above is itself the answer. If the output were purely an artifact of arbitrary choices, three methods built on different foundations would not converge. They do. The convergence is evidence that the models are measuring something real, however imprecisely.

The second is the falsifiability question. A model that says extraction is always underpriced, the philosopher of science will say, is unfalsifiable. The correct response is that the model is falsifiable; the book has simply not yet found a case where it fails. Recall the definition: cost severance equals residual commons value minus the bond posted. The model produces a positive cost severance only when the bond is smaller than the true intergenerational cost. Cost severance can in principle equal zero or go negative. A sustainably managed renewable resource with full ecosystem accountability, timber from a plantation forest harvested at or below regrowth rates, with full replanting and habitat maintenance bonded to the operation, approaches zero: the foreclosure component is zero because the resource regenerates, and the bond covers the remainder. That prediction is testable and would be falsified by a clean counterexample. The stronger response is structural. The reason cost severance is positive in every fossil-fuel case we have tested is not that the model is rigged. It is that no existing accountability regime incorporates substitutability-weighted intergenerational foreclosure pricing; the instruments do not yet exist. The prediction will be falsified the day a jurisdiction implements full intergenerational foreclosure pricing and demonstrates cost severance at or below zero for a non-renewable resource. This is the claim, made in 1940, that no existing airplane could fly faster than sound: true, not because it was unfalsifiable, but because the necessary technology had not been built. It was falsified in 1947 when Chuck Yeager flew through the barrier.

The third is the reproducibility question: two analysts applying the framework to the same case will not, in general, produce the same number. They will choose different substitutability curves, different social-cost-of-carbon estimates, different discount factors, different assumptions about the externality tail. Their outputs will overlap but will not be identical. This is how applied cost-accounting works across every framework in active use: benefit-cost analysis of environmental regulations, health-technology assessment, nuclear-decommissioning cost estimates, actuarial forecasts. None treats the presence of a range as a failure of the method. What they treat as a failure is an estimate whose sensitivity to parameter choice is hidden, or whose central case is invented rather than derived from data. The framework guarantees not uniform outputs but transparent disagreement: outputs whose dependence on each parameter is exposed, and whose disagreements resolve to specific empirical questions that further research can answer.

### The Norway Backtest

To test whether the model only screams disaster, apply it to the best-managed extraction case in the world.

Norwegian oil. The country's sovereign wealth fund holds a portfolio invested on behalf of present and future generations against its cumulative oil revenues, and it is the closest working example the world has of Hartwick's Rule: extract a non-renewable, invest the rents in renewable capital, let consumption depend on the interest rather than the principal. Chapter 4 walks the fund's structure, its spending rule, its early carbon tax, and its ethics-council divestments in detail; the point here is only what the framework finds when it is applied.

Apply the residual commons value framework. The fund is an enormous accountability bond. The country has stringent environmental regulation and has invested in full-cost accounting for the externalities of its extraction industry. The externality tail is smaller than in Appalachia because the regulatory regime is stricter. The substitutability picture for oil is the same as everywhere else.

The result: cost severance is small, but positive. The fund captures most of the financial value, for Norwegians. The barrel is still physically gone; no fund can manufacture more subsurface petroleum. And the fund's accounting is per-Norwegian-citizen accounting. The atmospheric externality that non-Norwegian populations bear from Norwegian oil burned anywhere on Earth is not in the fund. The externality tail, smaller than in Appalachia, is not zero. The residual commons value is not much larger than the bond, but it is still larger.

The model does what a good model should. It differentiates. It does not say extraction is always bad at the same rate. It says extraction under these conditions produces this much cost severance, and extraction under those conditions produces that much. Norway is closer to honest pricing than anywhere else. It is still not all the way there.

### What Kind of Commons the Framework Prices

A reader from the Ostrom tradition will hold a particular question at arm's length when this framework is introduced: is the Commons Inversion Test filtering *the same kind of commons* that Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* describes, and if so, is the framework competing with Ostrom-tradition commons economics or extending it? The honest answer requires distinguishing two structurally different commons-classes that have been treated under one heading.

The first class, call it the coordination commons, is what Ostrom's eight design principles address. A fishery shared by twenty boats. A grazing pasture shared by forty households. An irrigation system shared by a hundred farms. The commons is finite, multiple users draw from it, and the risk Ostrom names is that uncoordinated drawing produces collective ruin: Hardin's "tragedy." Her contribution was to demonstrate, empirically across hundreds of cases, that communities of shared stakeholders can self-govern these commons through specific institutional architectures. The cost the framework would have to price, applied to a coordination commons, is the cost of coordination breakdown, and that is not what this framework prices. Coordination breakdown is a cost the existing Ostrom-tradition apparatus already prices, and prices well, where the institutional architecture is in place.

The second class, call it the extraction commons, is what *this* framework addresses. The atmospheric carbon-stability that makes a habitable climate. The Appalachian mountain habitat that exists or doesn't. The ancestral land whose dispossession is permanent. The cumulative collective attention that platforms can deplete. Multiple users still draw from these commons. But the actors *capturing* value through extraction are not, in any meaningful sense, shared stakeholders with the actors *bearing* the cost. The mining company is not a member of the McDowell community. The platform is not a member of the user population whose attention it monetizes. The carbon emitter is not a member of the future generation whose habitable climate is being foreclosed. The asymmetry between value-capture and cost-bearing is structural, not contingent, and the cost the framework prices is the severance that asymmetry enables. Ostrom operates on coordination commons; this framework operates on extraction commons; the two domains are adjacent, and the methodologies extend rather than compete with one another. Where a real case carries both elements, a forest that is both a shared-stakeholder pastoral commons and a target of external timber extraction, the analyses run in parallel.

It is worth saying plainly what these extraction commons have in common, because naming it is the framework's strongest structural claim. The reef, the atmosphere, the Appalachian hillside, the lifetime hours of a worker's labor, the habitable climate owed to a generation not yet born: in the accounting these are the *same object*. Each is something a market treats as a commodity, prices, and consumes, that was never produced for sale and cannot be produced for sale. Karl Polanyi called these fictitious commodities: land, labor, and money are bought and sold as though they were ordinary goods, but they are the substance of society itself, and treating them as goods for sale is what produces the social damage. Nancy Fraser extended the analysis, naming the expropriation of nature, of care, of community as the hidden condition of accumulation rather than an unfortunate side effect of it. The diagnostic tradition that runs from Polanyi to Fraser *named* this commodification. What it never did was price it, or build the instrument that reserves against it. That is precisely the work the residual commons value formula performs: it takes the object the diagnostic tradition identified, the fictitious commodity, the expropriated commons, and computes what its consumption costs the parties who depend on it. The framework is the measurement the diagnosis was missing. This is the same relation the framework holds to David Harvey's account of accumulation by dispossession: Harvey diagnosed the mechanism by which value is captured through enclosure of the commons; the residual commons value formula measures the magnitude of what that mechanism takes. [CITATION GAP: Polanyi *The Great Transformation* (fictitious commodities), Fraser (expropriation as condition of accumulation), and Harvey (accumulation by dispossession) — these positioning references need inline citations to the specific works.]

A second break-point Ostrom's framework does not reach is the divided-stakeholder commons: a community drawing from a shared commons that is divided on ethnic, racial, class, or other grounds, carrying conflicting preferences about the commons itself. Ostrom's design principles presume substantially aligned stakeholders capable of negotiating governance; when the stakeholders are structurally divided, the design principles do not produce a solution, because the problem is not coordination of mutually acceptable use but contestation over what acceptable use is. This framework's apparatus reaches such commons, because the Commons Inversion Test operates on the commons-extraction relationship rather than on the stakeholder-coordination relationship: the severance the framework prices is the cost of extraction *from* the commons by parties structurally outside the commons-bearing community, and that cost is measurable whether the community is internally aligned or divided. This connects the framework to the analytical apparatus of stratification economics (Darity, Hamilton, and collaborators), which analyzes structural extraction operating through hierarchical group-divisions of race, class, and ethnicity. The commons-governance extension applies that analysis to commons that are both contested across stakeholder-group lines and extracted from by structural outsiders.

There is a sharper objection waiting here, and the chapter should meet it rather than route around it. If the framework's answer to commodified commons is to *price* them and post financial *bonds* against their consumption, has it not simply deepened the commodification it claims to oppose? This is the financialization critique, made most forcefully by Katharina Pistor's account of how legal coding turns assets into capital: to render the atmosphere or a community as a line item in a bond is, on this view, to extend the reach of capital rather than to constrain it. The objection is serious and the chapter does not dismiss it. The framework's answer is Polanyi's own: the double movement. Pricing the commons is not the end state; it is the instrument by which a society *re-embeds* the market in social and ecological limits it had escaped. A bond sized to the full residual commons value does not make the commons more tradable; it makes its consumption more expensive than its extraction is worth, which is the opposite of a market in the commons. The full development of this answer, how the two instruments re-embed rather than financialize, and why a reserve held against harm is not the same as an asset traded for gain, is Chapter 9's work; the chapter notes the objection here so the reader carrying it does not mistake silence for an absence of an answer.

### What the Framework Adds

What, specifically, does the residual commons value framework add to the traditions it grew from? The framework extends two intellectual lineages into a setting neither addressed: extraction contexts where commons are drawn down rather than managed in place. From Pigou comes the move to internalize externalities through pricing instruments; from Ostrom comes the recognition that commons can be governed sustainably under specific conditions. The framework adds what neither developed: a discovery method (the Commons Inversion Test), a quantification framework (residual commons value), and a pricing instrument (the accountability bond) sized to the full residual value rather than to a single externality. The name itself carries the lineage: *residual* in John Hartwick's sense of what is owed forward after standard accounting has done its work, *commons* in Ostrom's sense of a resource governed by or extracted from a community of users, *value* in Mariana Mazzucato's sense of value created rather than value extracted. (The full defense of each term, and why the alternatives were rejected, is in the Technical Appendix.)

Three additions, each consequential.

The first is **substitutability-weighting**. Standard externality analysis prices damage when damage occurs. A ton of coal emits carbon; the carbon does damage; the external cost is that damage. The calculation is time-of-damage. What it misses is the counterfactual every future generation faces: if the resource had been left in the ground until a substitute existed, the damage would never have been done. Foreclosure cost, the cost of having extracted early weighted by the probability that no substitute exists at each future time, is a category standard externality analysis does not have. The substitutability function is the instrument that translates that counterfactual into a quantity. This addition has a formal precedent worth naming. Hotelling's 1931 exhaustible-resource economics prices the rent rule for private extraction of finite resources but not the option-value of leaving resources in place for future generations. Pigou's framework prices the harm extraction does to third parties but not that option-value either. Neither alone prices what commons-extraction costs when extraction-rent dynamics and ongoing harm-to-others operate at once. The Hotelling Identity is the bridge: per-unit residual commons value minus per-unit standard Hotelling rent equals per-unit cost severance. Commons-extraction cost is precisely the residual beyond standard Hotelling rent under honest accounting. The identity does not replace either tradition; it formalizes what each leaves out.

The second is **integrated architecture**. Standard practice treats foreclosure, externalities, community disruption, intergenerational equity, and epistemic loss as separate concerns governed by separate methods, each with its own literature and its own reviewers. What no single existing method delivers is a formula that integrates them: one expression whose output is the total cost severance for a given extraction, with each component contributing additively under a shared set of gates. The integrand unites the foreclosure and externality terms, and the general form extends the sum to any additional term that passes the gates. This is not a replacement for the component methods. It is a synthesis that lets analysts produce a single number when a single number is what a decision requires, a reclamation bond, a remediation fund, a severance levy, while showing transparently how the components contribute.

The third is the **Commons Inversion Test** as a discovery method. Standard externality analysis prices what the analyst has already decided to price; the category of costs is inherited, and the only question is how much. The Commons Inversion Test asks, of any proposed cost, whether the same physical extraction would still impose it if the underlying resource were non-scarce. If yes, the cost is intrinsic to the activity and does not enter; if no, the cost is a scarcity-grounded intergenerational claim and belongs in the integrand. This gives analysts a way to *discover* previously unpriced costs, or to rule out costs that are attention-capturing but not structural, before committing numbers to them. It is what makes the framework generative: new variables can be added as the test reveals them, each arriving with its own worked reasoning rather than by assertion.

The cleanest way to see all three at once is to set the framework beside standard Pigouvian accounting on a case we have already walked. Under standard Pigouvian accounting, McDowell coal prices health, environment, and carbon, landing at the Pigouvian floor of roughly five hundred four to five hundred eighteen dollars per ton. Residual commons value adds the foreclosure term, what 1960 coal foreclosed against 2020's renewable alternatives, weighted by substitutability, and lets the Commons Inversion Test probe the community-disruption costs that Pigouvian accounting treats as separate policy questions rather than as components of the extraction's true cost. Same case. Same historical data. A larger, more complete number, because it integrates more of what was actually severed. Standard externality analysis had no principled reason to exclude the community-disruption and foreclosure costs; it simply had no method for including them. The framework has a method.

The three additions are real, and the framework is non-trivial relative to the tradition it grew from. It is not a rejection of externality economics. It is an extension.

### What the Chapter Leaves for Later

Several threads the formula touches are owned by other chapters, and the chapter names them rather than re-walking them. The framework prices what extraction severs from generations not yet born, and the deepest objection to any such pricing is Derek Parfit's non-identity problem: how can extraction wrong people whose very existence depends on the extraction policy? The framework's answer follows Parfit's own, operating at the level of outcomes rather than of specific persons, asking whether the future-generation outcome is worse than it would have been under different policy rather than whether any particular future person was wronged. This is what grounds the two-instrument architecture: the Foreclosure Bond is the forward-looking, impersonal-outcomes instrument; the Restitution Bond is the backward-looking, person-affecting instrument for harm already realized. [RELOCATE TO Ch5: the full Parfit non-identity treatment and the Amartya Sen capability-valuation grounding belong with the two-instrument apparatus Chapter 5 introduces; Chapter 6's role is methodological, and a one-paragraph nod is sufficient here so the non-identity objection is not left fully exposed.] The Coasean objection, that the parties could simply bargain to an efficient outcome, fails on the same ground the non-identity answer rests on: future generations cannot bid, so there is no bargaining table for them to sit at, and the price has to be set on their behalf rather than discovered through their consent.

The framework's two contested commons categories, the autonomy commons and the time commons, are owned by Chapter 1. [RELOCATE TO Ch1: the autonomy-commons walk through the knowledge-worker billing 120 hours per week, and the classical-liberal / civic-republican / lived-oppression readings of whether autonomy is a commons at all, belong with Chapter 1's account, which introduces the example and the readings; Chapter 6 should reference them, not re-render them.] Across the eighteen cases the book examines, the Commons Inversion Test has surfaced ten illustrative commons categories, named and catalogued in the Technical Appendix; this chapter has walked one of them, habitability, at depth through the McDowell case, and the methodology works the same way on the rest. The ten are examples drawn from cases, not a closed enumeration; a reader whose tradition handles them differently can apply the same method and surface different categories. The through-line is the mechanism, not the list.

Two forward dependencies remain. Chapter 7 applies the same framework in a different setting, the Mars colony thought experiment, to test whether the framework is Earth-parochial or general. Chapter 8 takes one extraction, one ton of McDowell County coal, and walks it through the full cost-decomposition that produces the canonical figures this chapter has used. And the framework's decision rule under deep uncertainty, the Asymmetric Regret Rule, which specializes Savage's minimax-regret discipline to commons-extraction decisions where one direction of regret is commons-extinction, is specified in the Technical Appendix and applied to specific decisions in Chapter 9; the chapter names it here only so the reader knows where the decision-rule lives. Standard cost-benefit analysis performs well when costs and benefits are symmetrically reversible, expected values are reliable, and the horizon is short. Commons-extraction decisions violate all three: irreversible commons-loss cannot be undone, expected values are subject to deep uncertainty over century-scale horizons, and the asymmetry between extracting and not-extracting makes the regret structure asymmetric in a way the standard tool does not capture. The Asymmetric Regret Rule does not replace cost-benefit analysis where it works; it specializes for the case-class where its symmetry assumption fails.

This chapter has done the mathematics. The next chapter tests whether the mathematics is universal. The chapter after that makes the number concrete for one extraction we have followed from the book's beginning.

Three methods. Different foundations. Same direction. When markets do not price what honest accounting prices, the difference is not a rounding error. It is the subsidy by which the entire extraction economy runs.
