# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 8: What Things Actually Cost

---

What if everything cost what it actually costs?

Ask the question without political coding, without ideology, and the framework we have been building starts answering it before the argument even begins. Not from preference. From arithmetic. Take one extraction, any extraction, though the one we have been tracing since Chapter 2 will do, and walk it through the full accounting the previous chapters made legible: price the health damage, the poisoned ground, the community that bore the weight, the foreclosure carried forward to every generation that follows. Add them together. Look at the number. Look at the price on the invoice. Notice the gap.

This chapter does that exercise for one ton of Appalachian coal. The result is not degrowth. It is not anti-capitalism. It is not, in this book, a policy recommendation. It is an accounting exercise, and what we choose to do with the accounting is a separate question, a political one, and one this book is deliberately not trying to answer. The book's job is to make the numbers visible. What gets built from the visibility is the work of policy-makers, communities, and political coalitions who carry the framework's vocabulary into the rooms where the decisions get made.

What follows walks each cost component the framework's Four Gates admit, every Cᵢ that passes the discipline of the Commons Inversion Test, units consistency, boundedness, and independence, through one specific extraction. The eight components below are the ones McDowell County coal surfaces. A different extraction surfaces a different set; the methodology is universal, the components are case-specific.

---

### The coal, again

Return to McDowell County. Return to the county Kennedy visited in 1960, the county that produced more coal than any other in the United States while its residents queued for surplus food. A ton of coal sold at the mine mouth that year for about four and a half dollars. By the time the rail car cleared the last tunnel, the accounting was complete as far as the market was concerned. A buyer paid. A seller was paid. The transaction cleared. The coal was gone.

What the market did not price, what Chapter 6 spent its pages showing every serious pricing methodology agrees it did not price, was not a rounding error. It was the majority of the actual cost.

What follows is conservative throughout. Where an estimate has a range I use the lower figure, and where an externality is contested I omit it. The goal is not a ceiling but a floor, the smallest honest number the framework can defend, and to show that even the floor sits orders of magnitude above the price that cleared the market.

---

### Direct Health Cost

Begin with the lungs. Black lung is the most completely documented externality coal has ever produced: federally tracked, specifically attributable, and severally accumulated onto the public ledger when coal-company bankruptcies discharge the original liability. Chapter 2 walks the federal Black Lung Benefits Program's Trust Fund, its funding gap, its debt trajectory, and its mechanism of severance, in detail. For this chapter the relevant arithmetic is the all-in figure.

Allocated against total United States coal production since the Program's establishment in 1969, the Program alone yields between one and one and a half dollars per ton. Add the costs that did not pass through the Program at all: the excess cardiovascular mortality from particulate exposure, the respiratory illness in mining communities whose children were never in the mine, the cancers near coal-processing facilities, the Medicare and Medicaid costs shifted to the public ledger, the uncompensated care, the private bills of miners whose employers went bankrupt before the obligations matured. A conservative national figure sits between two and four dollars per ton.

Two to four dollars. Against a market price of four and a half dollars in 1960. The most documented component, on its own, already returns most of what the coal sold for.

### Environmental Degradation Cost

Walk outside the mine. The creek running orange with acid mine drainage is not a metaphor. It is a specific hydrochemical process in which exposed sulfide minerals oxidize in contact with water and air, producing sulfuric acid that mobilizes heavy metals into the watershed for a century or longer. Treatment is perpetual. There is no version of acid mine drainage that ends when the mine closes.

Across Appalachia, the reclamation bonds coal companies were required to post under the 1977 Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act fall billions of dollars short of the documented remediation need. Hundreds of thousands of acres across Appalachia await cleanup; close to a million nationwide. The available bonds total roughly three point eight billion dollars against cleanup costs estimated between seven and a half and nine point eight billion. [CITATION GAP: bond total and remediation-cost band need a dated public source (GAO / OSMRE / Appalachian Voices); the ledger keeps "$4.5B (2021)" as a vintage anchor with its year, which is a different figure set than the one stated here. Reconcile.] The gap is the severed cost sitting in the federal ledger in plain sight, waiting for a funding source the law assumed would exist and does not.

Add the sixty thousand Appalachian cancer cases linked to mountaintop-removal mining [CITATION GAP: Hendryx et al. is named but the 60,000 figure needs a specific paper and year], the long-tail ecosystem losses from streams buried under valley fill, the soil contamination downstream of processing facilities, and the atmospheric mercury from combustion that now shows up in fish as far away as the North Atlantic.

Conservatively allocated per ton: one to three dollars. Added to direct health, the national severed cost already meets the 1960 market price, at three to seven dollars per ton, before any foreclosure pricing at all.

### Community Disruption Cost

The county peaked near one hundred thousand residents in 1950. By 2020, fewer than twenty thousand remained. Mechanization cut the workforce by more than half within a decade of Kennedy's visit. The U.S. Steel operations wound down across the 1980s, and personal income in the county fell by two-thirds in the years that followed. In 2016, the last Walmart left. In 2022, McDowell County ranked sixteenth-poorest in the United States, with a median household income near twenty-eight thousand dollars and a poverty rate close to thirty-eight percent, in a country whose median was over sixty-seven thousand. In 2015, the county recorded the highest drug-induced death rate of any county in America: one hundred forty-one per hundred thousand, close to ten times the national rate of roughly sixteen that year. [CITATION GAP: Census/CDC figures (income, poverty rank, drug-death rate) are time-sensitive; need source and year-lock.] This is the human-scale shape of community disruption: a county the size of a single high-school district losing residents to overdose at a rate an actuary would flag as a disaster zone.

A community does not drop to twenty percent of its population without carrying costs. There are infrastructure costs, schools and fire departments and water systems and hospitals, which must be maintained at some minimum regardless of headcount and whose per-capita burden rises as the headcount falls. There are economic multiplier losses, every local business that closed, every institution that could not sustain itself, every dollar that did not circulate locally because the earner left. There are public-program costs, food stamps and disability payments and Medicaid, absorbed by federal and state government in a county that once generated billions of dollars in coal revenue, almost none of which stayed.

Economists who study regional collapse have methodologies for pricing this; the figures they produce for sustained regional decline land in the range of five to fifteen dollars per ton of extracted coal when allocated to the extraction that drove the collapse. [CITATION GAP: regional-collapse pricing methodology needs at least one named source from the regional-economics literature; this is the least-sourced and second-largest non-carbon head.] I will use the low end. Five dollars per ton.

Running total across the first three components: roughly eight dollars per ton, already approaching twice the 1960 market price. We have not yet priced carbon.

### Foreclosure Cost

This is the cost component Chapter 2 named through the cost-walkthrough that lands many orders of magnitude above the price coal actually sold for, and Chapter 6 formalized through the residual commons value integral. It is also the component that, for fossil fuels, dominates every other.

One ton of coal, when burned, releases approximately 2.61 metric tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. The EPA's combustion factor for bituminous coal, applied to McDowell County's production-weighted Pocahontas-seam heat content of roughly twenty-eight million Btu per short ton, yields 2.61 metric tons of carbon dioxide per short ton of coal. This is the McDowell-specific calibration of the framework figure Chapter 6 introduces under the national-bituminous-average heat content, which yields 2.32 metric tons per short ton. [CITATION GAP: Chapter 6 must actually introduce the 2.32 national-average basis, or this footnote is stranded; verify on the Ch6 cascade.]

The EPA's 2023 estimate of the social cost of carbon, the economic damage caused by each ton of carbon dioxide released, anchored to the Rennert et al. 2022 *Nature* integrated estimate, is one hundred and ninety dollars per metric ton. The arithmetic is not subtle. The climate cost of burning one ton of McDowell County coal, at that estimate, is approximately four hundred and ninety-six dollars, which is 2.61 multiplied by one hundred and ninety.

Just under five hundred dollars. Against a market price that has oscillated between about four and a half dollars and a hundred and forty dollars over the life of the industry. The carbon term alone, before any of the other seven components are added, exceeds every era's market price for a ton of coal, by a factor running from roughly three against today's market peak to more than a hundred against the 1960 mine mouth.

And the carbon number is itself conservative. The framework prices the foreclosure tail using a declining discount rate, which following Weitzman biases the estimate downward rather than upward; the long-dated damages enter the sum at less than a constant-rate accounting would carry them. There is also the option-value foreclosure the carbon number does not capture at all: every future metallurgical application that cannot be served by a substitute that does not exist, every future chemical-feedstock use, every use we cannot yet imagine for a carbon resource whose existing stock is being burned for electricity in a world where electricity can now be generated from sources that do not require combustion at all.

The foreclosure component, for coal burned in any era after viable renewable substitutes existed, is a minimum of four hundred and ninety-six dollars per ton, and that is before option value, before ecosystem losses from atmospheric warming, before the climate-induced mortality and migration costs the social cost of carbon understates.

Here it is worth pausing on what kind of move this is. The standard economist's instrument for a cost the market does not price is Pigou's: a tax set equal to the marginal external damage, enough to make the polluter face the harm at the margin. The framework extends Pigou rather than replacing him. A Pigouvian correction prices the external damage of the act of burning. The honest cost is larger, because the foreclosure component prices something a marginal-externality tax was never built to see: the permanent removal of a finite stock from every future that might have used it. That is why the four-component subtotal here, the Pigouvian floor, runs to between five hundred and four and five hundred and eighteen dollars per ton, while the full eight-component total, which we reach below, runs higher. Pigou priced the smoke. The framework prices the smoke and the fact that the coal is now gone.

### Lineage Labor Cost

Now the harder components. Lineage Labor Cost is the one the Commons Inversion Test reveals: the cost borne not by the worker but by the worker's family and the generations downstream of that family's dispersal.

A mining community that collapses by eighty percent does not take its children with it uniformly. The children who stay grow up where the nearest grocery store is an hour away and the local school's graduation rate is among the lowest in the country. The children who leave are raised by parents whose work was physically destructive enough that many of them did not live to see grandchildren. Intergenerational wealth transmission, which in most American communities is the single largest driver of which lives are possible and which are not, becomes impossible when the family's entire asset base is a house in a hollow that cannot be sold.

This is not a rhetorical claim. It is visible in the data. A child born in McDowell County in the bottom income quintile has among the lowest odds of reaching the top quintile of any location in the United States; the Opportunity Insights mobility data ranks the county at the floor of national outcomes. [CITATION GAP: cite the specific Opportunity Atlas data point.] The extraction did not just take the coal. It took the child's probability of a different life.

The framework is naming something Black American writing has been naming for over a century. Paul Laurence Dunbar wrote *We Wear the Mask* in 1896: *We wear the mask that grins and lies, it hides our cheeks and shades our eyes.* The miner who has been told the dust is better now is wearing the same mask. The framework's claim is that the mask is not his alone. It belongs to the system that produces black lung at industrial scale and asks the wearer to grin while it does. Fanon carried the same analysis into the colonial-economic register the extraction-community cases sit closest to, in *Black Skin, White Masks* in 1952. The literary tradition named the strategy of survival and the structural condition that required it. The framework adds the cost-accounting mechanism by which the mask remains necessary, the line item that turns the condition into a number. [RELOCATE TO Ch1: the fuller four-author lineage (Du Bois's veil, Ellison's long-form narrative) is Ch1's established "the pricing is new, the move is old" move; establish it once in Ch1 and reference it here rather than re-expositing.]

The lineage labor cost is real, but the data attributing specific dollar figures per ton is sparse. Development economists estimating intergenerational mobility losses have produced regional estimates in the single-digit billions for counties of McDowell's scale; per-ton allocations, though imprecise, sit in the range of one to five dollars. [CITATION GAP: these are internally estimated, not externally sourced; flag for the pre-publication queue.] The number is small next to the carbon term. It is enormous next to the life of a child whose fiftieth-percentile income was set by the zip code they were born in. This component is priced at two dollars per ton as a conservative placeholder. The reader can decide whether two dollars per ton of coal, multiplied across sixty years of extraction, represents anything like a fair accounting for what it cost a generation to be born in a county that had been priced at nothing.

### Knowledge and Cultural Cost

This component is hardest to see while it is happening because it is invisible until it is already lost. Every mining community carried practical knowledge: how to read the seams, how to manage risk on a specific geological formation, how to keep a particular valley's water system functioning, how the weather behaved in that particular hollow over a particular century. That knowledge existed because specific people stayed in specific places long enough to accumulate it.

When the community dispersed, the knowledge went with it. Not to somewhere it could be preserved. To places where it had no application. The retired miner who knew exactly how the West Fork ran in February did not move to somewhere that needed to know how the West Fork ran in February.

And that is just the ecological and occupational knowledge. Add the cultural continuity, the congregations, the civic organizations, the networks of mutual aid that could absorb a personal crisis because everyone knew everyone, and the loss compounds. The replacement cost for the civic infrastructure of a community of a hundred thousand people is not straightforwardly priced, but it is certainly not zero.

This component is priced at one dollar per ton. It is not the point of the chapter. But it belongs in the accounting, because the Commons Inversion Test surfaces it, and because the reader who thinks carefully about what the coal cost cannot simply omit the part that fell outside the ledger because it was never denominated in dollars.

### Political Capture Cost

The reason the first six components are visible in hindsight and were invisible in the moment is not that the information was unavailable. It is that the industry whose invoices did not reflect the costs had a substantial financial incentive to keep the costs unpriced, and did so, for decades, through documented lobbying, regulatory capture, and the strategic suppression of research.

The clearest single illustration is the social cost of carbon itself, the very number this chapter's largest component rests on. Under the Obama administration it was set at forty-two dollars per ton, anchored to a global-scope calculation of the externality. Under the Trump administration it was cut to between one and seven dollars by narrowing the calculation's geographic scope to the United States alone rather than the global externality, a methodological change academic commentary flagged as ungrounded in standard cost-benefit practice (Wagner, Anthoff, Cropper, et al., 2021). [CITATION GAP: the $42 and $51 interim values are public in the Interagency Working Group technical support documents; cite them.] Under the Biden administration it returned to fifty-one dollars on a return to the global-scope methodology, and the EPA's 2023 update raised it to the one hundred and ninety dollars used here. The number is not uncertain because carbon physics is uncertain. The number is uncertain because how much of the cost should be priced is a political fight, and one side of that fight has more resources than the other.

The cost of the fight is itself a severed cost. Democracies spend real money, through lobbying expenditures, captured regulatory agencies, think tanks funded to produce the conclusions their funders need, and the civic attention diverted from other questions, to keep the honest accounting from happening. Estimates of the annual lobbying spend of the fossil-fuel industry alone run into the hundreds of millions of dollars, and that figure captures only the visible fraction. [CITATION GAP: needs OpenSecrets or comparable public source.]

The framework's contribution here is worth naming precisely, because it is easy to mistake for something it is not. There is a tradition that supplies the actor-and-coalition vocabulary for who shaped this architecture and who absorbed its costs, the reparations-economics tradition associated with Darity and Mullen and the writers around them; and there is a tradition that supplies the vocabulary for the extractor's reasoning, the rent-seeking analysis associated with Public Choice. The framework draws on the analytical vocabulary of both without endorsing the larger political projects either has been attached to. Its own move is different in kind: not to describe the actors or their reasoning but to count what the arrangement has been costing, and to whom, so that McDowell County's life-expectancy gap appears as a number on a ledger rather than only as a story about blame. [RELOCATE TO Ch9: the fuller framework-versus-Public-Choice positioning, and the reparations-economics actor-and-coalition development, belong in Ch9, which develops both at length; keep only this one positioning sentence here.]

This component is conservatively assigned one dollar per ton, a floor so low it is almost certainly an undercount, chosen to keep the running total conservative. The reader who has followed the argument this far can adjust.

### Temporal Displacement Cost

This component, revealed by the Commons Inversion Test most starkly of all, is the cost of *when*.

The coal in McDowell County had sat in the ground for hundreds of millions of years. Its extraction was compressed into roughly a century, with the bulk of the tonnage moved in the forty years after mechanization. This was not because the country needed coal at that specific rate. It was because the rate at which coal could be extracted had been raised by machinery, and because the financial structure of the industry rewarded rapid extraction over measured extraction.

The temporal displacement cost is the cost of foregone optionality: the value of having extracted more slowly, over a longer period, with time to develop alternatives, time for communities to diversify, time for the externality tail to be absorbed at the rate it could have been absorbed rather than all at once. The oil shocks of the 1970s produced a large improvement in U.S. energy intensity over the following two decades because prices signaled that efficiency mattered. [CITATION GAP: the energy-intensity improvement figure needs an EIA source.] Had coal been priced closer to its honest cost in 1960, the same signal would have driven the same kind of adaptation, at a pace communities could have survived.

Instead, the mechanization compressed the timeline, the price suppressed the signal, and the adaptation did not happen until the accumulated damage forced it. This component is conservatively assigned two dollars per ton. Again, almost certainly an undercount.

### The sum

The framework sums each admitted cost component across the relevant time horizon; the per-ton arithmetic this chapter performs is the time-snapshot version of that summation, applied to a single extraction. The Technical Appendix carries the formal integral for readers who want it, including the identity that ties the framework's residual commons value to standard resource economics: the residual commons value of a unit, minus the Hotelling scarcity rent the market already captures, is the cost severance per unit. The market sees the rent. The framework prices the remainder.

Running the total at the low end of every component:

- Direct Health: $2

- Environmental Degradation: $1

- Community Disruption: $5

- Foreclosure: $496

- Lineage Labor: $2

- Knowledge and Cultural: $1

- Political Capture: $1

- Temporal Displacement: $2

Total: $510 per ton.

Two numbers in this chapter are easy to confuse, and the whole argument turns on keeping them apart. The four-component Pigouvian floor, the figure a careful externality economist would defend, is between five hundred and four and five hundred and eighteen dollars per ton. The full eight-component honest total, which adds the four harder components the Commons Inversion Test surfaces, is five hundred and ten dollars per ton. They overlap because the carbon term dominates both; the difference between them is exactly the part of the cost the standard externality account leaves out and the framework puts back in.

Against a 1960 mine-mouth price of about four and a half dollars, and against forty to a hundred and forty dollars today depending on grade, and against every price coal has ever sold for, the total holds.

The Intergenerational Pricing Gap, the ratio of honest price to market price, for McDowell County coal sits somewhere between thirty-three and one hundred and twenty-two times the 1960 sale price, depending on which cost categories the accounting admits and which social cost of carbon anchor it uses. The triangulated central estimate, inflation-corrected, sits near fifty times. Even at today's higher market prices, the carbon term alone exceeds the market price by a factor of at least three. The structural finding holds across every era the industry has operated: the honest cost of a ton of McDowell County coal has always exceeded what the market paid for it, often by more than two orders of magnitude, which is the upper end of the range Chapter 2 promised this chapter would land.

A fair reader will want to know where this could be wrong, because a measurement that can never come out the other way is not a measurement. The framework's answer is built into its own structure. Eight components, every one of them adding cost, none of them subtracting, is exactly the pattern a skeptic should distrust. So the framework states the condition under which the gap closes: where a resource is fully renewable and harvested at or below its regeneration rate, the foreclosure term goes to zero, and with it the dominant component of the gap. The cost severance of a unit drawn from a stock that fully replaces itself is zero by construction. That is the falsification surface. The framework finds extraction underpriced because the cases the book examines are non-renewable foreclosures and renewable stocks drawn down past renewal, not because the arithmetic is rigged to always return a gap. Hand it a genuinely sustainable harvest and it returns nothing to price.

### The pattern made legible

If this were an isolated case, the reader would be entitled to ask whether McDowell County is a special exception, whether the arithmetic that works here fails somewhere else. Chapter 5 answered that question with case after case. The ratios were not identical; the mechanism was. Deepwater Horizon's documented costs against its projected well revenue produce a gap of fifteen to seventeen times. The framework adds a foreclosure layer the documented-costs accounting omits: the Macondo well's projected lifetime production would release tens of millions of tons of carbon dioxide when the oil was burned, which at the chapter's social cost of carbon anchor is several billion dollars in additional carbon tail. The addition shifts the ratio only slightly, because Deepwater's documented direct costs already exceed its revenue by an order of magnitude, but the methodological consistency is the point: the framework that prices coal's carbon tail prices oil's the same way, and the answer is the same in shape if not in scale. The W. R. Grace vermiculite mine in Libby, Montana produced a gap between forty and eighty against its lifetime revenue. The Baotou rare-earth extractions, priced against the documented environmental and health costs to the villagers living next to the tailings lake, land in the same range. The Exxon Valdez spill, measured against the market value of the oil spilled into the sound, produced a gap that resists simple calculation only because the recovery was never completed. [CITATION GAP: the cross-case figures (Deepwater 15–17×, Libby 40–80×, Baotou, Exxon Valdez) should match Ch5's numbers exactly; verify, then a "see Ch5" pointer can replace the inline restatement.]

Across different industries, geographies, decades, and resources, the same mechanism produces the same structural gap between what the invoice said and what the extraction actually cost.

The pattern does not stop at what conventionally counts as extraction. The housing pattern Chapter 2 named, nearly half of American renters cost-burdened, the surplus that would otherwise have funded mobility captured by an asset-owning class largely absent from the local community, produces the same shape at the scale of a single household. The value flows to owners who are not present at the scale of the community. The costs accumulate at the scale of the household whose savings do not. The arithmetic differs from McDowell's; the structure is identical. ### What the market left out, and who named it

It is worth stopping, before the disclaimers, to say plainly what the eight-component walk has just demonstrated, because the demonstration has a lineage older than the arithmetic.

What the market priced was the extraction of the coal. What it did not price, the lung damage, the poisoned creek, the collapsed community, the carbon in the atmosphere, the dispersed families, the child's foreclosed probability of a different life, was a second transaction running alongside the first: unpriced, unwitnessed, and binding on parties who were never consulted, including parties not yet born. The market is not fake. Prices cleared transactions, money changed hands, real coal left McDowell County on real rail cars. The market is incomplete. It cleared the transaction it could see and left the second one off the books.

That second transaction has a name in the tradition that first diagnosed it. Karl Polanyi called it the fictitious-commodity move: the treatment of things that were never produced for sale, land, labor, the living world, as though they were ordinary commodities, which lets a market price the part it can see and quietly consume the part it cannot. Nancy Fraser gave the same move its contemporary form as expropriation, the unpaid appropriation of the social and ecological substrate that sits behind every priced exchange, the community and the future generation and the living system that the wage relation depends on and never compensates. The framework's structural claim is that these are all, at bottom, the same object. A reef, the atmosphere, a community, a body of labor, a generation not yet born: each is a commons, something depended on and shared but not produced as a commodity, and each is priced at nothing precisely because its abundance once masked its cost. The diagnostic tradition named the move. What it never did was price it, or reserve against it. That is the work the eight-component walk performs and the residual commons value integral formalizes: not a new diagnosis of commodification but a measurement of it, component by component, on a single ton of coal.

This matters for a worry the careful reader will already be carrying, that to price the commons is to commodify it, to turn the reef and the community and the future into one more asset class to be traded. The framework's answer runs through the same lineage. Polanyi's account of the market society came paired with what he called the double movement: when marketization disembeds the things a society depends on, the society moves to re-embed them, to put them back under collective protection. The framework is an instrument of the second movement, not the first. It prices the commons in order to reserve against its loss and to fund its repair, not in order to sell it. The number it produces is not a ticket to a market; it is a measure of a debt. The distinction between pricing-to-sell and pricing-to-protect is the whole of the difference, and Chapters 9 and 10 carry it into the question of whether the accountability bond, the instrument the framework proposes, can hold that line or whether it slides back into the financialization it was built to answer.

### What honest pricing does *not* mean

Three misreadings have followed this argument every time I have offered it, and I want to name them before the chapter ends.

The first is that honest pricing claims the extraction should never have happened. It does not. Cheap energy, even severely mispriced energy, lifted billions of human beings out of poverty, and no responsible reader of this book should accept an account that pretends otherwise. The argument here is narrower: whoever bore the cost of extraction should have been compensated at rates reflecting what they actually sacrificed. The miners who developed black lung, the communities that collapsed when the coal was exhausted, the children born into a county whose economic future had been extracted before their birth, the generations that will absorb the climate costs. These are not hypothetical entities. They are people. They paid. They are still paying. The question the framework asks is not whether extraction occurred. The question is who paid for it and whether they consented.

The second misreading is that honest pricing claims the market is fake. It is the worry the previous section already answered: the market is not fake, it is incomplete, and the job of the framework is to name the part that got left out.

The third misreading is that honest pricing is a tool by which already-industrialized nations pull up the ladder behind developing economies. This is the objection that has to be taken seriously, because if it were true it would be a compelling moral defeater. It is not, and the response is structural rather than rhetorical: whoever created the historical cost owes its repayment, and differentiated transition timelines are a structural feature of honest accounting, not an afterthought. Developed nations built their wealth by severing costs onto the global commons for two centuries. The atmospheric carbon, the disrupted climate, and the industrial infrastructure that benefited from the free disposal of externalities were built by specific countries whose present wealth reflects costs borne by populations who were never compensated. Honest pricing creates the revenue to fund clean industrialization for developing nations, not as charity but as the repayment of historical severance. Solar electricity is now cheaper than coal in most of the developing world. The technology exists. The transition timeline should be asymmetric, and the framework requires exactly that.

One further reading deserves a direct answer, because it is the one most likely to be raised in good faith: that the chapter is doing degrowth in a different vocabulary. It is not. GDP measures market transactions, not welfare. Every dollar spent cleaning up an oil spill counts as economic production. Every medical bill from black-lung treatment counts as medical output. GDP rises when the children of McDowell County develop addiction disorders and spend money on treatment; GDP does not fall when they die at fifty-eight. Honest pricing reduces GDP in the narrow technical sense while increasing welfare in every sense the measurement was meant to proxy. It shifts the composition of economic activity, not the total. Leaded gasoline was banned, refineries reformulated, the industry did not collapse, and children's cognitive outcomes improved. That is the pattern honest pricing repeats at a larger scale.

### The closing line

If coal is mispriced locally by between thirty-three and one hundred and twenty-two times the 1960 sale price, and if the same pattern appears across every extraction case the book has examined, then the aggregate global severed cost across all resource extraction sits in a range the framework can bound but cannot fully specify from here.

Order of magnitude: the global fossil-fuel industry alone generates on the order of four to five trillion dollars in annual revenue. If full-cost pricing would raise those revenues by a factor of two to three, a conservative estimate given the carbon term alone, the annual global severed cost from fossil extraction is in the range of eight to ten trillion dollars. Add the non-fossil extraction sectors, mining, logging, fisheries harvested above regeneration, aquifer drawdown, agricultural topsoil loss, and the global figure lands somewhere between ten and fifteen trillion dollars a year.

Ten to fifteen trillion. Annually. It is larger than every government subsidy program combined, larger than all international development assistance, larger than all charitable giving in every country on Earth, combined. It is, as far as I can tell, the largest continuous wealth transfer in human history. And it flows in one direction. From the communities closest to extraction to the consumers farthest away. From the future to the present. From the people who have no voice in the market to the ones who do.

That figure is the argument's civilizational-scale implication. It is also, in this book, a gestured claim: one sentence of supporting arithmetic against an assertion that would, if taken seriously as a policy agenda, require years of sectoral simulation and implementation work that cannot honestly be compressed into the closing pages of a framework book. So I am not going to compress it. I will name it and leave it there.

What happens if an entire global economy begins to reprice at honest rates, resource by resource, sector by sector, country by country? What happens to energy costs in the short term, and in the new equilibrium? Who absorbs the transition? What happens to a household in the bottom of the income distribution when essentials rise sharply? Where does the pricing surplus go, and who decides? These questions are real, and they are consequences of the pricing exercise this chapter has just performed on a single ton of coal. They are also outside the scope of this book. Their pursuit belongs to the policy-makers, scholars, communities, and political coalitions who carry the framework's vocabulary into the rooms where the decisions get made. What honest accounting at civilizational scale would reveal, when it is performed by those who do that work, on terms not legislated by these pages, is not a catastrophe but a better, more stable, more resilient economy with a floor beneath it.

For now, what this chapter gives the reader is the one worked example: a ton of coal, priced honestly, at a floor of roughly five hundred and ten dollars. The gap between that number and the four-and-a-half-dollar invoice is the severed cost. The invoice is what McDowell County was paid. The severed cost is what McDowell County bore.

The next two chapters return to what that means, for McDowell County, for the waterman, for the reader's own life, and for the century ahead.
