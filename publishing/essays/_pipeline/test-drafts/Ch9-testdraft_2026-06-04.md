# Commons Bonds
*By Chris Winn*



## Chapter 9: Pricing Honestly

---

Chapter 8 put a number on the invoice.

One ton of McDowell County coal, sold at the mine mouth in 1960 for four dollars and fifty cents, actually cost about five hundred and ten dollars when every severed cost was priced honestly, conservatively, floor-estimated, one cost component at a time. The carbon term alone came to four hundred and ninety-six dollars. The gap between four and a half dollars and five hundred and ten is not a rhetorical flourish. It is arithmetic. And it repeats, with different parameters, across every extraction the economy runs.

The honest response to arithmetic like that is not outrage. It is to ask what to do about it.

This chapter is that answer. Not a legislative program, not a manifesto, not a policy blueprint, but the set of practical moves a society would make if it decided to close the gap between what things cost and what we pay. The moves are sequenced. They run on institutions that already exist. They do not require agreement on every contested question about markets, or property, or what one generation owes the next. They require only the decision to start pricing honestly, and the discipline to keep pricing honestly as we learn.

The sequence is four steps. Classify. Reserve. Invest. Reassess.

Then the chapter does one more thing. It says plainly what kind of work this is, names the partial versions of it already running in the world, and answers the objection a reader who has tried to pass a law will be holding by now: show me the pathway. That answer is the hinge into the book's closing chapter.

---

### Step 1: Classify

Before anything else, sort the cabinet.

Not every resource carries the same foreclosure risk. A ton of iron ore extracted today imposes externality costs, the pit, the tailings, the emissions of processing, but iron is abundant on this planet and on most of the rocky bodies around it, substitutes are conceivable for almost every application, and pulling out more tomorrow does not meaningfully reduce what remains for the century after. Coal is similar in abundance, different in its externality tail, but substitutable in principle. We already know how to heat a house and power a grid without it. These resources sit at the low end of what the framework calls the existential substitutability gap. Price their externalities, tax their extraction at a rate that reflects the damage tail, and the framework's job is mostly done.

Other resources are not like that.

Rare earth elements, the seventeen metals whose chemical and physical properties underwrite nearly every piece of advanced electronics, are concentrated in a small number of deposits, chemically difficult to substitute, and required for technologies we have not yet invented but will almost certainly need. Platinum group metals are similar. Phosphorus, essential for agriculture, exists in workable concentrations at only a few places on the planet and has no substitute for the biological role it plays. Groundwater in fossil aquifers, the Ogallala, the North China Plain, the Guarani, regenerates on timescales of millennia and is being drawn down on timescales of decades. Helium, mostly the He-4 isotope recovered from natural gas, is a depleting resource we may want our descendants to still have.

These are high-gap resources. They are the ones a civilization that plans to exist in two hundred years would want to hold in reserve.

Renewable commons sit at the other end and need only one clause. Sunlight, wind, regional rainfall, atmospheric oxygen: these read as low-gap by construction, because they are the renewable substrate the rest of the framework's investments rest on. The general rule is use freely. The framework speaks only to the edge cases where a renewable commons can be captured or contested, and there it prices the capture the same way it prices any capture, by the cost severance it imposes on the bearers.

Classification is not a mysterious exercise. Materials scientists, energy economists, agronomists, aerospace engineers, and hydrologists already hold most of the information required to rank any given resource by its substitutability, its stock dynamics, its externality tail, and the criticality of its likely future uses. The European Union publishes a Critical Raw Materials list that does part of this work. The United States Geological Survey publishes annual assessments that do another part. What does not exist yet is a single, legible ranking that pulls all four components into one score. Producing one is a matter of months, not decades. It has not been done mostly because no one has asked for it in this form.

The classification does not have to be perfect to be useful. A rough sort into three bins, low, medium, and high gap, is enough to drive every decision that follows. Low-gap resources get the carbon-tax treatment: price the externality tail, require adequate reclamation bonding, let the market allocate inside a corrected signal. Medium-gap resources get progressive extraction rates and sovereign-fund mechanisms of the kind Chapter 4 examines. High-gap resources get the one move the whole framework is organized around.

Reservation.

### Step 2: Reserve

Here is the idea that sounds strange on first hearing and obvious on second.

For high-gap resources, designate a fraction of known economic deposits as strategic reserve. Not extract them and warehouse them. Not stockpile them against a shortage. Leave them in the ground.

The ground is free, permanent storage. It needs no refrigeration, no security, no insurance against the warehouse burning down. Platinum that has sat in South African bushveld for two billion years will sit there another two billion unless someone goes in and takes it out. Rare earths in the Bayan Obo deposit are as stable today as they were when the deposit formed. Helium trapped in a gas reservoir asks for no attention until the moment it is pulled up and vented. The cost of leaving a resource in place, for almost everything in this category, is exactly the revenue foregone on the reserved fraction. Nothing else.

That cost, for most critical minerals, is small, and it is small for a reason worth stating. Extraction is usually limited not by the size of the deposit but by current demand and current mining capacity. Suppose the Geological Survey identifies thirty million metric tons of economically recoverable rare earth reserves and current global production runs around three hundred thousand tons a year. Setting aside twenty percent of reserves as strategic, six million tons, twenty years of current output held back, costs essentially nothing in the near term. The remaining stock still dwarfs the demand schedule. The reserved fraction simply becomes unavailable to extraction decisions our descendants would in any case have made for themselves.

The reserve is, in effect, a free option. It costs the revenue foregone on whatever fraction would have been extracted anyway inside the current window, which, at realistic discount rates over the relevant horizon, is a small slice of total value. Its payoff is the full substitution value of the preserved stock if and when future scarcity arrives. It appreciates as the unreserved stock depletes. It depreciates only if successful substitution makes the resource unnecessary, at which point it can be reclassified and released. It is a hedge that costs almost nothing to hold and pays off exactly when needed.

And it asks for no new international institution. Strategic petroleum reserves already exist in most industrial countries. China has been quietly building rare earth reserves for two decades. The United States has at various points held strategic stockpiles of helium, tungsten, cobalt, and several dozen other materials. The architecture for reservation is in place. What the framework adds is a principle for deciding what to reserve and how much: the gap ranking from Step 1, applied to every high-gap resource class, with reserved fractions sized to the asymmetric regret of the decision.

The Asymmetric Regret Rule does most of the work. The same rule the Appendix derives formally is the one a reader can feel intuitively here. If the reserve turns out to have been unnecessary, substitutes mature, demand falls, new deposits are found elsewhere in the solar system, the cost of having reserved was a modest amount of revenue, spread across a century and across the whole global economy. If the reserve turns out to have been necessary, some critical use identified in 2150 for a material our grandchildren can no longer reach because we dug all of it up, the cost of not having reserved is civilizational. The first error is measured in dollars. The second is measured in possibilities foreclosed forever. When the insurance is cheap and the downside is unbounded, the rational move is not in doubt.

### Step 3: Invest

Reservation alone does not complete the framework. It buys time. The question is what we do with the time.

The answer is two investment priorities at once.

The first is renewable substitutes, and not only renewable energy, though that is the most visible case. The full substitution agenda. Solar and wind and storage to displace coal, oil, and gas. Alternative battery chemistries, sodium, iron-air, solid state, to take the pressure off lithium and cobalt. Recycled and synthesized material flows to take the pressure off virgin rare earths. Regenerative farming to take the pressure off fossil aquifers and phosphate. Plant-based and cellular protein to take the pressure off collapsing fisheries. The details vary by resource. The structural claim does not.

Every dollar of substitution research does three things at once. It raises the probability that a workable substitute exists at any given future time, which directly lowers the residual commons value of every high-gap resource that substitute touches. It preserves the optionality of the reserve, because a reserve that is never needed can eventually be released for other uses. And it shrinks the externality tail of future extraction, because the less we need to pull out, the less damage extraction inflicts on the communities and ecosystems that bear the direct costs. No other class of spending does all three. Not infrastructure, not defense, not even education, though education compounds with all of them. Substitution research is the only expenditure that reduces present harm, future harm, and future need in a single move.

The second priority is alternative supply, what gets called, in compressed form, space access. This is the piece that sounds science-fictional and isn't. The argument is purely one of comparative residual commons value. An asteroid has no community bearing spatial cost severance. It has no ecosystem whose collapse cascades through a biosphere. Its externality tail is measured against an empty rock in an empty orbit. The substitutability gap of a lifeless body is, by construction, very low. A kilogram of nickel lifted from 16 Psyche and returned to Earth orbit will eventually, as launch costs fall, compete on total cost with a kilogram mined from a terrestrial deposit whose full residual commons value has been priced honestly. The model says nothing about when the crossover comes. It says only that the eventual destination of industrial extraction, for the most abundant materials we use, is off this planet. Investing in the capabilities that reach that destination sooner is investing in a future where Earth's deposits do not need to be touched at all.

This is what pricing honestly demands. The model does not say never extract. It says extract from the lowest residual commons value source first. For most industrial materials, over the next century, the lowest-RCV source will be renewable substitution. For the narrower set we cannot substitute and cannot recycle indefinitely, the lowest-RCV source will eventually be off-world. Neither conclusion was reached by politics. Both fall out of the arithmetic once extraction is priced honestly.

### Step 4: Reassess

None of this is static.

A resource classified today as high-gap may become low-gap in thirty years if a substitution breakthrough matures. Rare earth demand could be halved by a non-rare-earth magnet chemistry. Lithium demand could be structurally reduced by sodium-ion batteries reaching grid parity. Helium could be synthesized, or made unnecessary by a fusion approach that does not use it. When that happens, the reserved fraction can be reclassified. Some of it released to the extraction market. Some of it kept as a hedge against the substitute's failure modes. The classification is a living document, maintained by the same scientific bodies that produce it, revisited on a regular cadence, every five or ten years, at the pace materials science actually moves.

Reassessment runs the other way too. A resource classified today as low-gap may turn out higher than we thought, as new uses appear or the externality tail lengthens past what current models capture. Cobalt looked abundant twenty years ago. Most industrial nations have since reclassified it as strategically critical. Helium was once so cheap the United States government gave it away; it is now priced with the seriousness its properties deserve. The framework does not require us to get the classification right on the first try. It requires us to keep learning, and to adjust the reservation policy as the learning matures.

The discipline this imposes is modest. Most of the machinery already exists: the Geological Survey, the European Commission's Joint Research Centre, the International Energy Agency, the national stockpile programs. What is missing is not the capacity to reassess but the framework within which reassessment matters. The reserve driven by the substitutability gap is that framework.

### The international dimension

Cost severance does not stop at national borders. Borders are among the mechanisms by which it operates.

The rare earths in a phone assembled in Zhengzhou and sold in California were mined in Baotou, in Inner Mongolia, where the tailings pond borders villages whose residents breathe the airborne dust. [CITATION GAP: "seven square miles of radioactive slurry" Baotou tailings figure needs a public source.] The oil refined into diesel for a European truck may have been pumped from the Niger Delta, where decades of spillage have left mangrove and farmland uninhabitable. [CITATION GAP: Niger Delta "fifty years of spillage ... thousands of square kilometers" needs a source; UNEP Ogoniland 2011 is the usual anchor.] The cobalt in nearly every lithium-ion battery on the planet was, until recently, overwhelmingly extracted from the Democratic Republic of Congo. [CITATION GAP: DRC artisanal cobalt "estimated forty thousand children" needs a source; Amnesty 2016 is the usual anchor; verify the temporal hedge.] The people who capture the value of these materials do not share a border with the people who bear the costs. Often they do not share a continent.

International trade, as it currently operates, is among other things a cost severance delivery system. It lets a consumer in a wealthy country buy a product whose full residual commons value was paid entirely by people the consumer will never meet, in places they will never visit, in amounts they have no way to perceive. The geographic distance is part of what makes the severance possible. If the tailings pond were in New Jersey and the refinery in Connecticut, the framework's effects would show up immediately in domestic politics. Because they are in Inner Mongolia and the Niger Delta, the politics are displaced.

Honest pricing at the national level does not, by itself, fix this. A country that internalizes the residual commons value of its own extraction while importing materials whose foreign extraction was never priced has not reduced severance. It has relocated where the severance enters its own economy. The import gap is the new problem.

The framework's response is not a treaty, which this book does not pretend to draft. It is a principle: a country serious about closing its severance gap must apply the pricing to imports as well as to domestic extraction. Mechanically, that looks like a border adjustment, a charge scaled to the residual commons value the exporting country failed to price. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, in transitional operation since 2023 and entering its definitive phase in 2026, is a partial version of exactly this for carbon alone. It is not theoretical. It exists. It works imperfectly, as every institution built inside political constraints works imperfectly. But it shows that border adjustment is legally, administratively, and diplomatically feasible when the jurisdiction doing it is large enough to matter.

A generalized border adjustment, scaled to the substitutability gap and applied to a widening set of resource categories as the classification matures, is the international analog of what national pricing does at home. It makes imported goods carry the same severance charge domestic production would. It removes the incentive to relocate extraction to the jurisdictions with the weakest accountability. Over time it raises the floor for every exporting economy, because the cheapest path to keeping market access in the border-adjusting countries is to internalize the costs at the source and capture the revenue at home rather than surrender it to the tariff.

Is this a treaty? No. Is it coordination? Partly. Is it unilateral action that drags other jurisdictions along over time? Mostly. The international dimension does not wait for the world to agree. It begins wherever any country or bloc decides to begin, and it expands by the ordinary mechanics of market access. That is how carbon pricing spread from a handful of jurisdictions in the early 2000s to more than seventy today. [CITATION GAP: "more than seventy jurisdictions ... roughly a quarter of global emissions" needs a source; World Bank State and Trends of Carbon Pricing is the usual anchor.] Gap pricing can follow the same path.

### What the framework re-embeds

It is worth stopping, before the objections, to say what kind of move all four steps add up to, because naming it answers the deepest version of the worry this chapter raises.

Karl Polanyi described the long arc of the market economy as a double movement. First the disembedding: land, labor, and money, which are not produced for sale and which Polanyi called fictitious commodities, get pried out of the social relations that held them and run through the market as if they were ordinary goods. Then the countermovement: society, finding the disembedding unbearable, pushes back to re-embed those things in protective institutions. Nancy Fraser extended the account, adding that the market does not only exploit labor inside production; it expropriates the conditions that make production possible at all, the unpaid work of care, the integrity of nature, the public powers that hold the whole arrangement together.

Read through that lens, the severed cost this book has been measuring is the cost of disembedding. The reef, the atmosphere, the community, the labor, the access of generations not yet born: these are not separate problems with separate price tags. They are the same object seen from different sides, the fictitious commodities, each torn out of the relations that gave it meaning and run through the market as if its full value were the part the market could see. The diagnostic tradition named this. It gave us the vocabulary of disembedding and expropriation. What it did not do, because it was not its task, was price the thing it named. The framework prices it. It puts a number on exactly the severance the double movement is the historical reaction to.

And this is what the four steps do, structurally. They re-embed. Classification re-embeds a resource in the long horizon of its future uses. Reservation re-embeds a deposit in the claims of generations who cannot bid. The accountability bond re-embeds extracted value in the community whose commons it was drawn from, with disbursement matched to the full life of the externality tail. Each step takes something the market disembedded and puts it back inside an institution that holds the relation the market severed.

Which lets the framework answer the objection it has the most to fear. Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* (engaged at depth in Chapter 5) shows how legal coding turns ordinary assets into capital and externalizes the costs, and a careful reader will press the charge directly at the bond: is issuing a bond against a commons not itself a coding move, one more turn of the same wheel that re-encodes the commons as capital? The financialization worry is serious and deserves a serious answer rather than a deflection. The answer is the polarity of the move. Financialization disembeds: it abstracts a stream of value out of its social setting so it can be traded freely, severed from the people and places it came from. The accountability bond runs the polarity the other way. Its whole structure ties the value back to a specific community, a specific externality tail, a specific horizon. It is designed to be the opposite of liquid and the opposite of placeless. A financial instrument that re-embeds is not a contradiction; it is the countermovement using the tools of the thing it answers. The test of any particular bond design is empirical, and it is exactly Pistor's test: does the structure hold the value in place, or does it quietly let it slip its tether and become tradeable capital again? A bond that fails that test has been captured. A bond that passes it is re-embedding. The framework's job is to make the difference visible so the difference can be defended.

The same polarity disarms the worry that pricing severed human cost in dollars commodifies the people who bore it. The framework prices the severance by the same mechanism whether the bearer is a reef or a person, but pricing by the same mechanism is not valuing by the same worth. A dollar figure on what McDowell County lost is not a claim that a life is worth that many dollars. It is a claim that the cost was real, that it was borne, and that it can be entered in the column where it belongs so it can be rebalanced. The moral weight of the human cost is not collapsed into the number. It is deferred, deliberately, to be carried by the people and institutions the number hands the work to. The dollar figure is the instrument that makes the rebalancing legible. It is not the measure of the loss.

That deferral is also where the backward-looking half of honest accounting lives. The question the whole book turns on can be asked forward or back. Forward it asks: am I counting the costs of the decision in front of me, to myself, my community, my country, the colony I might someday help settle, across the long horizon of survivability, including the severing costs I am tempted not to count? Backward it asks the same thing of what has already happened: who bore the cost that was severed, and what would it take to rebalance the harm? The stratification economics of William Darity and his colleagues gives that backward question its discipline. The harm of severance does not fall evenly; it lands on populations sorted by the same lines the economy already sorted them by, and an honest ledger reads the harm where it actually landed rather than averaging it into a national figure that hides it. The accountability bond's community designation is the forward instrument built to honor the backward claim: capture the rent at the source, send the disbursement to the bearer, match the schedule to the life of the harm. The framework does not adjudicate the moral weight of restitution. It measures the severance precisely enough that restitution becomes a question a society can actually answer, rather than a sentiment it can only feel.

### The political economy of resistance

It is worth saying aloud why none of this has happened yet, and why the inertia is deeper than the idea is new.

The ten to fifteen trillion dollars a year of severed costs that Chapter 8 bounded are not a deadweight loss. [CITATION GAP: the aggregate "ten to fifteen trillion dollars per year" figure should point explicitly to the Chapter 8 / Appendix derivation.] They are a wealth transfer. Someone receives them. The beneficiaries are concentrated, organized, well represented in every jurisdiction that matters, and able to deploy resources in defense of the arrangement. The bearers, coal-country residents, the Baotou villagers, the Niger Delta farmers, future generations who do not yet exist and cannot vote, are diffuse, unorganized, and, in the case of the unborn, entirely unrepresented. The arithmetic of the political economy is simple. A concentrated interest defending a billion-dollar position reliably beats a diffuse interest trying to reclaim a hundred-dollar share, even when the aggregate loss dwarfs the aggregate gain.

This is not a discovery. Mancur Olson described it in 1965. The reason honest pricing has not happened is not that the case has not been made. It is that the case has been made against resources, organization, and institutional access the countervailing interest does not possess. No individual coal-country resident or Baotou villager can outspend the industries whose pricing they oppose. No coalition of future generations can lobby.

The framework does not wish this problem away. It shifts it, partially, in two specific ways.

First, by pricing the severance in dollars, it turns a moral argument into a fiscal one. "The coal industry is harming Appalachian communities" is a sentence that has been spoken for a hundred years without producing durable policy, because it asks for action on behalf of a group the decision-makers do not identify with. "The coal industry left about five hundred and ten dollars per ton of unpriced cost on the public while the coal sold at the mine mouth for four and a half" is a sentence that describes a subsidy. Subsidies have constituencies on both sides. The side that pays, which for coal is the public broadly, has reasons to care that the side receiving the morality lecture never did. When the severed cost is denominated in money, a different coalition becomes possible.

Second, by making the beneficiary concrete and local, it changes who shows up for reform. The plain-accounting name for the problem is a subsidy running in the wrong direction: bearers subsidizing extractors, communities subsidizing supply chains, the future subsidizing the present. The architecture that reverses it follows from the accountability-bond logic: capture the rents at the source of extraction, designate disbursement to the community bearing the direct spatial cost, match the schedule to the full life of the externality tail. Chapter 4 develops the comparison between Norway's sovereign-fund architecture and the United States retirement-security system, and shows that arrangements like these are contingent choices rather than facts of nature. What matters here is the structural shape, not a specific statute the book has no business legislating: rent-capture at extraction, community designation at the bearer level, life-cycle-matched disbursement. The community that becomes the actual beneficiary becomes the constituency that defends the arrangement. Reform gains a local beneficiary to argue for it, not only a diffuse population waiting to receive abstract improvements.

This dynamic is not abstract. It operates at full scale, in real crises, when the architecture of response is decided in real time. [RELOCATE TO Ch5: the ~1,400-word 2008-TARP-vs-Sweden-1932 financial-crisis case study (Mian and Sufi welfare math, Saltsjöbaden, 1992 Swedish bank crisis) belongs in the Accountability Gap chapter, where it demonstrates the architecture-is-a-choice thesis at macro scale without burying Ch9's four-step spine. The one-sentence thesis it proves is retained here.] The lesson that case carries is the one this section already states: financial-crisis architecture is not a market outcome but a policy choice, made under specific political-economy constraints, that the accountability-bond logic prices structurally. The bank-rescue path won in 2008 because its beneficiary class could organize at the speed the moment demanded; the household-debt-restructuring path lost because its beneficiaries, millions of foreclosed households spread across every district, had no vehicle to demand it. Sweden in the 1930s combined bank stability with household stability and produced a settlement that has lasted generations. The framework's contribution is to make the choice visible as a choice. What was chosen once can be re-decided when the next crisis arrives.

Neither of these moves is decisive on its own. Together, and combined with the entering wedges already in place, they begin to shift the arithmetic.

### Public Choice and the complete ledger

The framework's central question is who bears the cost. A related question, equally serious, is why those bearing the cost cannot stop the cost from being assigned to them. The Public Choice tradition, founded by James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock, supplied the vocabulary for that second question. Tullock's 1967 paper introduced rent-seeking, the move of treating political behavior as rational actors maximizing the value they can extract from politically mediated arrangements. [CITATION GAP: verify exact title and journal of Tullock 1967, commonly cited as "The Welfare Costs of Tariffs, Monopolies, and Theft."] What that paper named is the extractor's reasoning under incomplete cost-visibility. The framework's work is to make the costs visible, which changes what the extractor's reasoning is reasoning about.

The two traditions describe the same phenomenon from two angles, and the angles are not symmetric. Where Public Choice tracks deadweight loss, the wasted resources spent competing for politically created rents, the framework tracks what the rent-seeking-shaped architecture has been putting on the ledger, and on whose side. McDowell County's life-expectancy gap is a number. The Niger Delta's uninhabitable mangrove is a number. Baotou's airborne dust is a number. The framework does not assign moral weight to these numbers. It counts them and assigns each to the column it belongs in: the cost borne by communities that never negotiated the architecture absorbing them, the cost borne by generations who cannot vote, the cost borne by ecosystems whose recovery exceeds any political horizon. The rent-seeking the extractor historically maximized was extraction under severed costs, costs assigned to parties the extractor did not have to pay. The same reasoning under unsevered costs runs different math and produces different decisions.

The asymmetry runs cleanest where extractor and cost-bearer are distinct populations: a corporate operator and a downstream community, a financial industry and a distressed household, a trade association and a worker. There the Public Choice chain runs cleanly, and the framework's identification of the cost-bearing party gives that chain something concrete to point at. Where extractor and bearer overlap, as with electronics consumers who are both the demand behind rare-earth mining and the eventual absorbers of its supply-chain externalities, the rent-seeking framing blurs and the framework's structural accounting carries the weight rent-seeking cannot isolate. The framework is honest about both kinds of case and treats neither as the default.

The practical implication is that the framework is not a competitor for the analytical space Public Choice occupies. Public Choice supplied the vocabulary for the extractor's reasoning. The framework supplies the cost-visibility that reasoning then has to incorporate. Someone designed the architecture, in Public Choice's idiom, and we can identify them; the ledger then shows what they designed has been costing, and to whom, in the framework's. Both are needed. Neither does the other's work. What the same extractor's reasoning produces under complete cost-visibility is a different question, and an open one.

### Property rights, and their limits

The most intellectually serious objection to anything like this framework comes from the property rights tradition, and it deserves an honest hearing before the chapter closes.

The argument is that well-defined private property rights, enforced by credible institutions, already solve the commons problem for every resource that matters. Ronald Coase's 1960 demonstration, that in the absence of transaction costs externalities resolve through bargaining regardless of who holds the initial entitlement, is one version. Elinor Ostrom's work on common-pool resources governed by local institutions is another. The free-market environmental tradition argues, with real empirical support in some cases, that assigning ownership to something is usually the best way to ensure its long-term preservation. Fish populations stabilize when fisheries are quota-allocated. Forests regrow when landowners internalize the value of standing timber. Aquifers survive when the water is priced and metered. Private stewardship, the argument runs, reliably beats public neglect.

The framework does not deny any of this. It agrees with most of it. Property rights do extraordinary work for a specific class of resources, those that are rivalrous, excludable, and valued mostly in the present tense. For those, the price system aggregates dispersed information and rewards stewardship far better than any central plan. The argument against the property rights solution is not that property rights fail. It is that they structurally cannot reach three problems the residual commons value framework exists to address.

The first is intergenerational foreclosure. Property rights are held by living agents. Future generations hold no deeds, sign no contracts, cannot join the bargaining Coase described, and cannot bid against current uses of a finite stock. A mine owner and a mining community can negotiate every term of today's extraction. Neither can negotiate with the humans alive in 2200 whose access to the deposit is being foreclosed by today's decision. The property rights solution, in its pure form, assumes that if those future humans mattered they would bid, and reads the absence of their bid as evidence that they do not matter at the margin. The framework rejects the assumption. Substitutability, stock depletion, and irreversibility are real constraints whether or not anyone alive today prices them, and a pricing system that ignores them is not efficient in any defensible sense. It is incomplete.

The second is transboundary externality. Property rights work when the owner bears the costs and benefits of the asset's use. They work less well when the costs escape the property line. Acid mine drainage crosses property lines. Atmospheric carbon crosses property lines. Aquifer depletion crosses property lines. The Baotou tailings pond sits on a specific parcel, but the dust that blows off it respects no fence. Property rights solve internal optimization, not external leakage. The residual commons value framework is, at its core, a systematic accounting of the leakage, the component of value no single owner can capture because it is held in common by everyone downstream, downwind, and downstream in time.

The third is information asymmetry at civilizational scale. Property markets price what participants currently know. They do not price what we will discover we needed only after the resource is gone. A helium-3 deposit would have priced at zero in 1900, because helium-3 had no industrial use then. Rare earths were of limited commercial interest until their electronic applications emerged late in the twentieth century. The current price of any high-gap resource is a function of current substitutability estimates and current demand. It does not, because it structurally cannot, incorporate applications not yet invented. The framework's insurance logic, hold a reserve when substitutability is uncertain because the downside of under-reservation is potentially unbounded, is the move property rights cannot make, because the information required to make it does not exist at the moment of pricing.

This is not a refutation of property rights. It is a map of the domain within which they are complete and the domain within which they are not. For most goods, property rights are the right tool and we should leave them alone. For high-gap resources, intergenerational stocks, and transboundary externalities, they are a tool doing what it can do but unable to reach the full problem. The framework is the supplement, not the replacement. Adam Smith, who spent a good part of *The Wealth of Nations* arguing against producers who externalized costs onto the public, would have recognized the move. It is not a departure from the market mechanism. It is the completion of it.

### The Nordic objection, in brief

A reader following the argument will be holding one objection at arm's length: that Nordic outcomes, however attractive, were achieved under conditions a continental democracy of three hundred and thirty million people cannot reproduce. Scale, demographic homogeneity, resource base, and historical contingency are the four legs of the serious version. Each names a real difficulty. None names a structural impossibility, and the difference is the whole of the answer.

The cost-severance mechanism the framework describes is not Nordic-culture-dependent. It operates structurally regardless of a country's size or composition. The corrective architectures, in turn, already have United States partial implementations at state and municipal scale. Medicare and Medicaid demonstrate federal universal-coverage administrative capacity for a population segment. The Alaska Permanent Fund, examined among the entering wedges below, is a state-scale sovereign mechanism. The resource-base objection inverts on inspection: a multi-trillion-dollar economy produces a far larger tax base in absolute terms than Norway's oil wealth, so the binding constraint is not revenue capacity but the political will to direct revenue to public provision, which is the ordinary work of democratic politics rather than a question the framework legislates. And the contingency objection cuts the right way once it is stated precisely. Yes, specific decision points have passed. But contingency runs from the present forward as well as into the past, and Chapter 10 names the decision points that lie ahead. The serious response to a Nordic example is therefore not "those conditions do not exist here" but "which components of those architectures can be integrated at this scale, starting from here, and what would that look like." That is an empirical-political question, and it is the question the next chapter takes up.

### Entering wedges

One useful way to see that none of this is utopian is to count the pieces of it that already exist.

Carbon pricing, in the narrow sense of a tax or trading scheme on greenhouse emissions, operates in more than seventy jurisdictions, covering roughly a quarter of global emissions. [CITATION GAP: see the carbon-pricing source flagged above.] The schemes are imperfect. The coverage is uneven. The prices, in most cases, sit below the social cost of carbon as estimated by every major body that has tried. None of this changes the fact that a substantial, legally functional, politically defended mechanism for pricing part of what residual commons value would account for, the externality tail of fossil combustion on the atmospheric commons, already exists and expands, year by year, to cover more sources and more jurisdictions.

Sovereign wealth funds built on resource rents exist in Norway, Alaska, several Gulf states, and in smaller form across dozens of other jurisdictions. Each is a partial implementation of the principle that the rents of finite extraction belong, in some meaningful sense, to the political community whose commons was drawn from, not to the entity that extracted it. Norway has shown, over fifty years, that it is politically possible to sustain the discipline required to capture resource rents and preserve them for intergenerational equity, through changes of government, industry pressure, and the ordinary temptations of a wealthy society with a large pool of available capital. Chapter 4 develops the Norway-versus-retirement-security comparison that makes the architectural-choice point in full. It is not a miracle. It is an institution, built deliberately and maintained deliberately.

The narrow-domain reservation logic is not theoretical either. Several jurisdictions have built rare-earth strategic-resource architectures in response to supply-chain dependence on Baotou and the broader Chinese processing infrastructure. The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act, in force in 2024, classifies a list of materials as critical and a subset as strategic, eligible for stockpile and alternative-sourcing programs, and ties directly to the classification machinery Step 1 describes. [CITATION GAP: verify CRMA's "34 critical / 17 strategic" counts and the May 2024 in-force date.] The United States restored domestic rare-earth production at Mountain Pass after the prior operator's bankruptcy; Japan and South Korea built alternative-sourcing capacity following export-restriction crises of their own. [CITATION GAP: verify Mountain Pass / MP Materials dates (2002 closure, 2015 Molycorp bankruptcy, 2017 acquisition, 2018 restart) and the Japan-Lynas / South Korea sourcing specifics.] None of these implements the framework's full reservation discipline, and none was designed with its vocabulary in mind. Each shows that gap-driven reservation is operationally reachable once supply-chain dependence raises the visibility of the underlying classification problem.

Mining reclamation bonds exist in the United States under the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977, and in various forms in most mining jurisdictions. They are structurally underpriced, as Chapter 2 documents, the bond pool covers a fraction of actual reclamation cost and the gap has been papered over by bankruptcy-driven transfer of liability to the public, but the mechanism itself is sound. Price the bonds correctly, index them to actual remediation cost rather than nominal amounts set decades ago, require refresh as the liability tail lengthens, and the reclamation bond becomes a serious accountability instrument rather than a token. Environmental impact assessments, established under NEPA in 1970 and adopted in similar forms by most jurisdictions, are a procedural, non-monetized but legally durable partial implementation of pricing the externality tail. The border carbon adjustment now partially operating in the European Union is a partial implementation of transboundary pricing. Health-impact fees on extractive industries fund black-lung trust funds in several states. The Community Reinvestment Act, for all its limits, is a partial implementation of the spatial-justice principle, the idea that capital extracted from a place owes something to the place it was extracted from.

[RELOCATE TO macro chapter: the three reachable-horizon set pieces on non-extractive commons, Chattanooga's municipal EPB fiber network, the Mondragon worker-cooperative federation, and Vienna's Gemeindebau social housing, prove "collective capture can work at scale, durably, in a diverse political economy" rather than the extraction-pricing spine of this chapter. They are among the book's strongest "it works" demonstrations and should land where the macro collective-capture argument lives, with a one-clause reference here at most.]

Each of these is a partial, imperfect, politically achievable implementation of one slice of the framework. None was designed with the framework in mind. They evolved piecemeal, in response to specific crises, under specific coalitions, carrying all the compromises such politics produce. Taken together they show something important: every component of honest pricing already exists in some form, in some jurisdiction, and has survived the political process long enough to become institutional furniture. The framework's task is not to invent the instruments. It is to connect them, strengthen them, align them around a coherent principle, and extend them to the resources they do not yet cover.

This is the path forward. It is neither revolutionary nor utopian. It is incremental institutional work, made strategic by the framework that organizes it. And because it is incremental, it invites a fair suspicion: that strengthen-and-align is exactly the posture markets absorb without changing, that this is reform capital will simply capture, relabel, and carry on. The re-embedding test from earlier is the answer to that suspicion too. The question to ask of any of these wedges is not whether it bears the framework's vocabulary but whether it holds the severed value in place or lets it slip back into tradeable capital. A reclamation bond indexed to real cost and tied to the community holds the value. A reclamation bond set at a nominal figure decades out of date and discharged in bankruptcy does not. The framework's contribution is to make that difference legible, so a captured instrument can be told from a working one rather than waved through on the strength of its name.

### What a framework does

The hardest question this chapter has to answer is the most pragmatic one, and it is usually asked by people who have actually tried to pass legislation.

Carbon pricing has been debated for thirty years. The most ambitious implementation, the EU Emissions Trading System, covers a portion of emissions, is riddled with exemptions, and was nearly destroyed by lobbying during the 2008 financial crisis. If the world cannot implement a simple carbon tax after three decades of scientific consensus, the argument runs, how exactly do you propose to implement a comprehensive residual commons value pricing regime covering every high-gap resource, every externality tail, every intergenerational foreclosure, every transboundary externality, across every jurisdiction on Earth? Show me the legislative pathway. Show me the coalition. Show me the treaty. Without those, the argument concludes, this is another book by another person who understands the problem and cannot solve it.

The objection deserves the most honest answer I know how to give.

This is not a legislative blueprint. It is a framework. The political pathway to implementation, which laws, which jurisdictions, which coalitions, which treaty architecture, is beyond what any single book can produce, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest about what a framework does and does not do.

But note what frameworks do that legislation cannot. They change how people think, which eventually changes what legislation becomes conceivable.

David Harvey's "accumulation by dispossession" did not pass a single law. It gave people a word for a structural pattern they had been living for decades without one. Harvey diagnosed the mechanism; he named the move by which value gets stripped from the dispossessed. What he did not do, because diagnosis was the task, was measure how much. That is the relation the framework keeps repeating with the diagnostic tradition: it measures the magnitude of the mechanism the tradition named. Once the word existed, the conversation around gentrification, enclosure, and privatization changed, and legislation, uneven and partial and contested, followed.

Elinor Ostrom's commons governance work created no new institution. It identified design principles that dozens of existing local institutions had independently evolved, and once the principles were named and systematized they legitimized the local approach against the twin pressures of privatization and state management. They have since shaped fisheries, forestry, and water governance in more jurisdictions than can easily be counted. Kate Raworth's *Doughnut Economics* has not yet passed a law, and has been adopted as a guiding framework by Amsterdam, by regional governments, and by parts of the European Union's climate architecture, because it gives those institutions a coherent way to think about what they were already trying to do.

Commons Bonds is, and can only be, an attempt to do the same. It gives people a vocabulary: cost severance, residual commons value, severed cost, accountability bond, the substitutability gap. It gives them a measurement tool: the RCV model, in its convergent forms, testable against any extraction scenario in any jurisdiction, with the formal machinery, the Hotelling-identity bridge and the Asymmetric Regret Rule that Chapter 8 and the Appendix build, standing behind the four steps so they are not free-floating intuition. It gives them a way to think about resource pricing they can carry into their own contexts: a coal-country resident asking what their community was worth; a municipal council setting reclamation bonds; a state legislature designing a sovereign fund; an international body debating a critical-minerals agreement. The book does not need to pass a law. It needs to change the terms of the debate so the right laws become thinkable.

And it needs to point, honestly, at the entering wedges that already exist: carbon pricing, sovereign wealth funds, reclamation bonds, critical-minerals stockpiles, environmental impact assessments, border carbon adjustments. The work is not to build everything from scratch. The work is to strengthen what exists, connect what is disconnected, align the fragments around a coherent principle, and extend the principle to the resources and externalities it does not yet cover. That work is difficult. It is not impossible. People who have done harder institutional work before have done it with less.

The framework will not do this on its own. No framework ever does. What it does is give the people who do the work a common language and a common instrument to do the work with. Whether the work gets done is the question every generation answers for the one that follows.

---

And so, with the framework laid out and the entering wedges named, the book turns toward its closing question. Which is no longer what extraction actually costs, or what honest pricing would look like, or how the institutions might be bent toward it. Those questions have been answered, within the limits of what a single volume can answer.

The remaining question is a different kind of question.

The miner in McDowell County. The waterman on the Chesapeake. The villager in Baotou. The farmer drawing water from the Ogallala. The off-world administrator on Mars, making the resource decisions that will decide whether the habitat survives.

They are not, the book has argued, separate problems. They are the same problem in different parameters. The framework that prices one prices all of them. The mechanism that severs cost from value in one context runs by the same structural logic in every other. The reef, the atmosphere, the community, the labor, the access of the generations who come after: one object, severed in one way, measured by one ledger.

If that is true, then the last chapter has to ask what follows from recognizing it. Not as policy, but as something closer to a fact about ourselves, and the generations we belong to, and the ones we owe.

That is where the book goes from here.
