## The honest verdict: NOT a wash, but NOT a transformation

The locked versions were already strong, Condition-1-compliant, dispositive-passing essays. The new pipeline did **not** rescue a failing argument. So on the **core acceptance axis — does the argument land for the dispositive reader — it is essentially a wash** (all three pass either way; the recurring verdict both ways is NET INCLUDE DECISIVE + CONDITIONALLY ROBUST). What the new pipeline changes is **defensibility and gatekeeper-feel**, i.e. it raises *expected* publishability by removing downside-risk, not by strengthening the argument.

The gains are real and concentrated in three axes:

### 1. Fact-defensibility — NEW WINS clearly (the biggest mover)
This is where it is most *not* a wash. Each locked draft carried fact-checkable errors that a hostile reader (A1 industry economist, A5 orthodox) or the venue's fact-checker would weaponize:
- **$100 Barrel locked:** "30 billion barrels" (unsupported), fiscal rule "4%→3%/2017" (wrong), Ekofisk "three days before Christmas" (wrong). **3 errors.**
- **Boston Review locked:** stale Norway figure ($1.9T/$340K era), foreclosure filings-vs-homes-lost conflation. **2 errors.**
- **Atlantic locked-lineage:** the $600 ungrounded coal figure. **1 error.**
A caught fact error is a credibility hit and a real rejection risk. The new versions remove the ammunition. This axis alone moves expected publishability meaningfully.

### 2. Voice / AI-tell — NEW WINS (modest, but it's the deciders' axis)
Em-dash density (a proxy for LLM-clunk / "this reads like AI") went from 2.11–7.53/1k to **0.0 across all three**. The characters who actually decide — PW editorial brain, the literary agent, the acquisitions editor — explicitly read for "trade-press-adjacent voice vs academic-thesis / AI-clunky." The new versions score *higher* with them. Not a wash; a modest dominance on the gatekeepers' axis.

### 3. Length-fit — MIXED
- **Boston Review:** improved (4,859 → 4,448, toward the 2,500–4,000 sweet spot).
- **$100 Barrel:** neutral (within PW's ~3,500–5,000 band).
- **Atlantic:** **went the other way (4,272 → 5,113, +841w).** This is the one axis where a new version may be *worse* for its venue — 5,113w is long for an Atlantic Ideas piece. Flag: verify against Atlantic Ideas norms, and consider a trim pass on the Atlantic new draft. (It bought smoothness + the fact-fix with length; the trade may not be worth it at that venue.)

## Ranking by "how much more likely to publish"
1. **$100 Barrel** — biggest cleanup (worst em-dash density in the portfolio + 3 fact errors fixed). Clear net gain.
2. **Boston Review** — length-fit improvement + 2 fact fixes + em-dash. Clear net gain.
3. **Atlantic** — em-dash + 1 fact fix, but **+841w**; net gain *contingent* on whether the added length fits Atlantic Ideas. Recommend a trim look before this one is unambiguously ahead.

## Bottom line
The new versions are the right versions going forward on **two** of three (BR, $100 Barrel) without qualification — cleaner, more defensible, equal-or-better length. **Atlantic** is the right version on prose + facts but should get a length check / trim before it clearly beats the locked at that venue. None of this is the pipeline turning a reject into an accept; it is the pipeline removing the specific failure modes (a fact error a checker catches; an AI-tell an editor winces at) that turn a borderline editorial call against a piece. The argument was always going to land. The new pipeline protects it on the way in.
