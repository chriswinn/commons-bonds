# What McDowell County Paid

The road into Welch comes down out of the mountains along a creek, and the creek runs orange. Not the orange of clay after rain. A mineral orange, settled on the stones like paint that has been drying for fifty years, which is roughly what it is. You notice it before you notice anything else, and then you notice that you have stopped noticing it, the way the people who live along the creek stopped noticing it a long time ago, because a thing that is always there stops being information.

The hollow narrows as you come in. Houses stand close to the road, some kept, some going, some gone, with the kudzu doing what kudzu does to whatever stops resisting it. Then the valley opens just enough to hold a town, and Welch appears all at once, brick and stone, four and five stories, a county seat built tall because the land gave it no room to be built wide. The courthouse sits up on its hill above the main street, Romanesque, turreted, the kind of building a place constructs when it believes it will need a century of records kept. Below it, the storefronts. Some are open. Many are not. The buildings are the buildings of a small city. The streets are the streets of a town waiting to learn what it is now.

I came into McDowell County the way most outsiders with a question have come into it, by the road, slowly, watching. I was not the first. The county has been receiving visitors with questions for a long time, and it has learned what visitors with questions usually do, which is to look at what is broken, write down that it is broken, and leave. I want to do something different here, and the only way I know to begin is to say plainly what the question is. The question is what McDowell County paid, to whom, and what it got back. What went wrong would be the easier question, the one the county has been asked for 60 years, but wrong suggests an accident, and very little in what follows happened by accident.

That is an accountant's question to bring to a place this beautiful, and I am aware of it. The mountains here do not photograph the way the brochures of other mountains photograph; they crowd in, steep and close and green, and the sky is a ribbon. People have loved this place for as long as there have been people in it. The question of what it paid is not a question against the love. It is the question the love eventually forces.

* * *

In May of 1960, John F. Kennedy came down these same roads. He was a candidate then, fighting Hubert Humphrey in the West Virginia Democratic primary, and the primary mattered to him for a reason that had little to do with coal: West Virginia was overwhelmingly Protestant, and the country had not yet decided whether it would elect a Catholic president. Theodore White's account of that campaign, and Ted Sorensen's after it, both turn on what the state was supposed to prove about the Catholic question. McDowell County was not the test Kennedy came to take. It was what he saw out the window while he was taking it.

The primary itself is a famous story, told in White's pages and retold in the campaign memoirs of Sorensen, Arthur Schlesinger, and Pierre Salinger: the long duel with Humphrey, the religion question put directly to a Protestant electorate and answered decisively enough that the nomination followed. West Virginia gave Kennedy his proof. What the state got in return was a candidate who had now seen it, which is not nothing, and is also not payment. I linger on the asymmetry because it is the first clean instance of a pattern that will keep returning here: the county renders up something of national value, the value departs and does its work elsewhere, and what comes back is attention.

What he saw, by the accounts of the men who traveled with him, unsettled him more than the religion question did. Children in the hollows with the distended bellies of malnutrition. Families lining up for surplus government commodities, the flour and cornmeal and tinned meat the federal government distributed to people the economy had finished with. Mineworkers idled in their thousands, not because the coal was gone but because the machines had come, mechanization moving through the seams and shedding men while the county's production held at scale. The machines did the loading that crews of men had done; the tonnage held, and the payroll did not. A man displaced by a machine in a one-industry county is not unemployed in the ordinary sense, because there is no next industry over. He is simply outside the arrangement, in place, with his family, watching the coal still move. The poverty was not the poverty of a played-out place. The county was still producing. That was the thing that did not fit, and Kennedy carried it out of the state with him.

Four months later, in September 1960, campaigning in Canton, Ohio, he said this: "McDowell County mines more coal than it ever has in its history, and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than any county in the United States."

Read it again, slowly, because the whole of this essay is folded inside that sentence. More coal than ever in its history. More surplus food packages than any county in the United States. The same county. The same year. Kennedy meant it as an indictment of national neglect, a rich country averting its eyes from its poor. He was right that it was an indictment. He was wrong about the defendant.

The neglect reading says the wealth was elsewhere and McDowell had been left out of it. The sentence itself says something stranger: the wealth was being produced in McDowell, at record volume, at that very moment, by the fathers of the children in the commodity lines. The county was the source of the very prosperity it was starving inside. What was failing in McDowell County in 1960 was an arrangement in which a place could give at full capacity and go hungry at the same time, and the arrangement was working exactly as designed.

The country answered Kennedy's indictment in the register of the neglect reading. In May of 1961, the first food stamps issued under the modern program's pilot went to a household in Welch; the United States Department of Agriculture still cites the recipient by name, a Mr. Alderson Muncy, May 29, 1961. Relief came, in other words, and kept coming, and it was needed, and it was not nothing. But relief is what you send when you believe the problem is that a place has too little. Nobody in 1961 was prepared to act on what Kennedy's sentence actually described, which is a place that had too much taken.

* * *

To understand what was taken you have to hold in mind what McDowell County was, and what it was is hard to believe from the road today.

In 1950 the census counted 98,887 people in McDowell County. Call it 100,000, in a county of steep hollows in the far southern corner of West Virginia, and nearly all of them there for one reason. Under those mountains lay the Pocahontas seams, low-volatile metallurgical coal, among the richest coking coal anywhere on earth. This was not the coal that warmed houses. This was the coal that made steel. The steel of the Golden Gate Bridge began in these hollows. So did the steel of the Empire State Building. When the Navy built the carrier fleet that fought the Pacific war, the coal that made the plate came in meaningful part out of McDowell County. The county's miners knew this, and it is important to say that they were proud of it, with the specific pride of men whose work is skilled, dangerous, and visibly attached to the largest projects of their civilization. The mines were not done to McDowell County. They were, for three or four generations, what McDowell County did, and did better than almost anyone.

The county that work built was not a camp. Welch called itself the little New York of the coalfields, and on a Saturday in midcentury, by every surviving account, you could not move on its sidewalks. The courthouse on the hill had been there since 1894, designed by Frank Pierce Milburn, an architect whose courthouses anchor county seats across the South; McDowell built in stone early because the money was real. Down the valleys were Keystone and Kimball and a town called War, each with its banks and theaters and lodges, the dense civic furniture of places where tens of thousands of paychecks circulated.

Then the arrangement finished with the place. Not abruptly, whatever the shorthand says. U.S. Steel, whose captive mines and company towns had organized whole districts of the county, wound down its McDowell operations across the 1980s; when its Gary complex closed in 1986, the company's own annual report recorded the end of more than 1,200 jobs in a county that had no second industry, because the first one had never needed it to have one. The federal Bureau of Economic Analysis keeps the county's income ledger, and the ledger shows what leaving looked like: not a single cliff but a long bleed. The worst single year, 1986 into 1987, took roughly 13 percent of the county's personal income. From the 1985 peak through 1988 the loss ran to about 15 percent, and the bleed kept on through the closures of the late 1980s and early 1990s and the out-migration that followed them, four decades of income loss that never found a floor to stand on. People left the way income leaves, steadily, the youngest and most employable first. The ones who stayed were the ones with reasons, and the reasons were rarely economic.

I will come back to what the county looks like in the ledgers now. But ledgers count what is easy to count, and the first cost McDowell paid was never in them at all. It was carried out of the mines in the bodies of the men, one shift at a time, and to see it you have to follow one man all the way through.

* * *

Robert Bailey worked underground for 36 years. He started in McDowell County and finished in Boone County, one county over, which is a sentence that contains an entire life: the work moved, and he moved with it, and the work was the same work. Thirty-six years is longer than most professional careers in this country, longer than most marriages last, long enough that the dark and the dust stop being conditions and become the medium a man's competence lives in.

The dust was the cost. Coal dust, and in the later decades silica dust too, as the machines cut rock to reach thinning seams. It goes in with every breath and some of it does not come back out. The lungs respond the way lungs respond to what they cannot expel: they scar around it. The disease is pneumoconiosis, black lung, and there is no cure for it, only stages. Bailey described the late stages in six words. "It's almost like drowning," he said. Slowly, on dry land, over years, with everyone watching and no water anywhere.

The disease earned him a double lung transplant. Here is where his story stops being a story about a disease and becomes a story about an arrangement. The company Bailey had given his lungs to went bankrupt, and in bankruptcy its obligation to him dissolved with the corporate shell. This is legal. It is ordinary. It is how the structure is built to work. The benefits he lived on came instead from the federal Black Lung Disability Trust Fund, which is to say from the public, which is to say from you. Hold that transfer in your mind, because it is small, personal, and exactly the shape of transfers we will count in the billions before this is over: the value of Bailey's working life went to a company that no longer exists, and the cost of it was forwarded to the taxpayers.

Bailey understood his position with a clarity that the people who administered it never quite matched. He went to Washington twice to testify before Congress about the backlog of black lung claims, the sick miners waiting in a queue while their diseases outran the paperwork. He was not bitter in the testimony; he was precise, which is more dangerous. And he explained, near the end, why he kept doing it: "Most laws across the nation, most laws have been written with blood. I don't want the things I went through just to drop and no knowledge to be gained, no people to be helped."

In February 2019 he gave a final interview to Jessica Lilly of West Virginia Public Broadcasting, for a program called Inside Appalachia. His voice on the recording is thin but his sentences are intact. Twenty-eight days after that interview, he died. What killed him was a cancer brought on in part by the anti-rejection medication his transplanted lungs required him to take. Follow the chain backward and there is no link in it that is not the dust: the medication for the transplant, the transplant for the lungs, the lungs for the mine, the mine for the steel, the steel for everything. Robert Bailey paid for some measurable fraction of the twentieth century's infrastructure with the last 28 days arriving on schedule, and the company that collected his payment had already left through the bankruptcy door.

* * *

Stand at the creek in Welch with Bailey's chain in your mind and the orange water starts to read differently. Something was taken from this place, and something was left in this place, and the two were never entered in the same book. There is a name for that, and the name is worth giving carefully, because the name is the thing I have come to believe the existing shelf of books about places like this is missing.

Call it cost severance: the value of what is taken leaves the place it comes from, and the costs of the taking are cut loose from the value and left behind, unpriced, for whoever stays. The cost is real either way. Severing it does not shrink it. Severing it only changes who pays, and when, and whether the payment ever appears on a bill anyone can contest. A severed cost does not show up at the mine gate with an invoice. It settles down into the place and stays: in the water, in the lungs, in the county budget, in the difference between what a child born there can expect and what a child born elsewhere can.

Not every taking severs a cost. A forest cut slower than it regrows, a fishery harvested inside its capacity to replenish, can carry its costs honestly; the severance begins where the taking forecloses the future faster than the place can restore itself. Coal forecloses absolutely. The seam does not regrow on any human calendar, and the bodies and watersheds spent in reaching it do not either. Every ton out of McDowell County carried some quantity of foreclosed future with it, and the price of the ton, which at the mine mouth in 1960 was just under five dollars by the federal Energy Information Administration's series, carried none of it.

The diagnosis has a lineage, and the lineage matters, because part of what I am arguing is that the tools to see this have been lying on the table for years, built for other rooms. Mariana Mazzucato, in The Value of Everything, took apart the modern economy's habit of confusing taking value with making it; her cases were finance and pharmaceuticals, sectors where extraction wears a suit. What this account attempts to add is the extension of that diagnosis into physical extraction, where the taking is not an abstraction in a fee structure but lands on specific bodies in specific places, with names and chest X-rays. David Harvey gave the pattern its bluntest name, accumulation by dispossession, two decades ago; what a county like McDowell offers his theory is the thing theories rarely get, a complete worked example with the receipts attached.

And there is a closer shelf, the books about these mountains themselves. Harry Caudill's Night Comes to the Cumberlands, published in 1962, two years after Kennedy's drive, made the despoliation of the Appalachian coalfields a national scandal before most of the country could find them on a map. Ronald Eller's Uneven Ground traced the region's so-called development as the uneven arrangement it was. Steven Stoll's Ramp Hollow reconstructed how mountain people were separated from the land that had sustained them. Elizabeth Catte's What You Are Getting Wrong About Appalachia stood guard over the region's dignity against its caricatures. That lineage documented the harm with a thoroughness this essay leans on at every turn. What none of it does, quite, is price the severance: name the ledger entry, follow the cost to whoever actually paid it, and total what an honest account would have charged the taking at the time of the taking. The harm has a literature. The bill does not.

One more thing belongs in the mechanism's portrait, and it is the thing that the word victim gets wrong. The miners saw all of this before any economist did, and they fought it with the only instruments they had. In 1968, after decades in which black lung officially did not exist, miners in the Black Lung Association walked out in wildcat strikes that no union office had sanctioned, and West Virginia answered with the country's first state pneumoconiosis compensation statute. That same year, 1968, an explosion at the Farmington mine killed men whose names the state still reads aloud, and the following year Congress passed the Federal Coal Mine Health and Safety Act of 1969, the first federal law to treat the dust itself as the enemy. Inside the United Mine Workers, the insurgency called Miners for Democracy set out to take the union back from a leadership grown comfortable with the operators. The price of that fight is on the record too: Joseph Yablonski, the insurgent candidate, was murdered in his home in 1969, along with his wife and his daughter. Whatever else the coalfields were, they were not passive. The protections that exist were written, as Bailey said, with blood, most of it theirs.

I am not asking you to supply a villain, and I want to be honest that the story reads strangely without one. Some operators were cruel and some were decent, by the standards of their day; some company towns were brutal and some were, by the men's own testimony, good places to raise a family. It does not matter for the arithmetic. Kind and cruel operators sold into the same market at the same price, and the price was the problem, because the price contained no line for the lungs or the water or the county that would be left holding them. A decent man running a clean mine severed costs all day long without ever once deciding to. That is what it means for the defendant to be an arrangement rather than a person. Kennedy stood in Canton with a true sentence in his hand, the coal and the hunger rising together, and the country heard an accusation of neglect because neglect was the only crime on the books. The crime that fit the evidence had no name yet. It was the pricing.

* * *

Ted Latusek did not work in McDowell County, and I bring him in deliberately, because what happened to him shows that what happened there was not a McDowell peculiarity but the system's standard behavior. Theodore Latusek Jr. mined for Consol Energy, the descendant of Consolidated Coal, out of the Fairmont area in northern West Virginia, a longwall operations coordinator whose crew, by the company's own measures, outmined the others two to one. He was, by every account in the record, exactly the man the industry says it honors. The dust got him anyway. "A lot of times, you couldn't see your hand in front of your face," he told the investigative reporter Chris Hamby, whose series on the black lung claims system for the Center for Public Integrity won the Pulitzer Prize. "It felt like somebody had put a bag over my face."

Latusek filed for the federal benefits the 1969 law promised him. The claim took 19 years. Not because his lungs were ambiguous; because each time he won, the company appealed, with a thoroughness his lungs could not match, until a federal appeals court, the Fourth Circuit, reversed what he had won. "My goal in life was to outlive Consol. I thought that I had. And then it goes to the Fourth Circuit Court, and they take it all away from me." And then the sentence I have not been able to put down since I first read it: "I was loyal to the company, but the loyalty wasn't there for me. I felt like somebody just put a knife in my gut. I told my wife: 'I can take the lung disease because a lot of that's my problem. I went in that mine and ate that dust and knew better. I should have known better, but I thought I was invincible. But I always thought Consol would take care of me.'"

Read the inventory of what that man is doing in those lines. He is taking the disease onto his own account. He is apologizing for trusting. The arrangement had so thoroughly severed its costs that the man carrying them in his chest believed they were his fault. Latusek died in October 2022, still inside the fight. He and Bailey are two men. The ledger they were caught in can be read at national scale, and it should be, column by column.

Start with the lungs, because the federal government actually keeps this column. The Black Lung Benefits Program, established in 1969, was supposed to be financed by the industry, through an excise tax on each ton of coal sold. Through 2009, by the Government Accountability Office's and the Congressional Research Service's accounting, the program had paid out about $44 billion. The tax has never reliably covered the obligations; the Trust Fund has borrowed from the federal general fund almost every year since 1979, and as of September 2024 it stood roughly $5.1 billion in debt, a figure the GAO's simulations project could reach some $15 billion by 2050. And the Bailey pattern, the company dissolving out from under its sick, is not anecdote but line item: in three coal bankruptcies between 2014 and 2016, Alpha Natural Resources, Patriot Coal, and James River Coal, the GAO found about $865 million in black lung benefit liability moved off the companies' books and onto the public's. Someone always pays the difference. It has never yet been the coal industry, not net, not once, in the program's whole history.

The disease itself, meanwhile, is not history. Among central Appalachian miners with 25 or more years underground, federal surveillance published in the American Journal of Public Health in 2018 found more than 20 percent, one working miner in five, with black lung; the national figure exceeds 10 percent. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention reckons each black lung death takes 8 to 13 years of potential life from the man who dies it. The disease is rising, not falling, fastest among the youngest miners, in its most aggressive forms, despite every dust regulation on the books. The column is still being written.

The second column is the land and the water. Federal law has required mine operators to post reclamation bonds since 1977, money held against the day the mine closes and the cleanup comes due. The creek through Welch is the reader's measure of how that has gone. When mining breaks open sulfur-bearing rock, the exposed minerals react with air and water and the result is acid mine drainage, which runs orange and keeps running for decades after the last coal leaves; the Tug Fork and the upper Guyandotte carry it through this country. Across Appalachian coal country, by the 2021 accounting in Appalachian Voices' report Repairing the Damage, more than 633,000 acres of mined land still need remediation. The cleanup cost runs $7.5 billion to $9.8 billion. The bond money actually available is about $3.8 billion. The gap, $4 billion to $6 billion as of that accounting, has no payer of record. Which means it has a payer. The payer is whoever lives at the bottom of the watershed, paying in the currency of orange water, for as long as it runs.

The third column is the county itself, and this is the one the visitor sees from the road. The 98,887 people of 1950 were 19,111 by the 2020 census, a loss of four in five. A man born in McDowell County could expect, by county-level estimates published around 2013 in the work of the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation, about 63.5 years of life, against 76.5 for American men at large: 13 years, the difference between meeting your grandchildren and not. In 2015, the year the second wave crested, the CDC's data put McDowell's drug death rate at 141 per 100,000 residents, against a national rate of about 16. As of 2022, by the Census Bureau's estimates, 37.6 percent of the county lived in poverty, on a median household income of $28,235. Of West Virginia's 55 counties, the 2020 County Health Rankings placed McDowell 55th. I have read these numbers so many times that I have to make myself feel their weight again, and the way to do it is to remember that every one of them is a roomful of people: the 13 missing years are missing from particular kitchens; the 141 are buried in particular churchyards.

The fourth column has no agency that keeps it, and it may be the largest. A county of 100,000 builds people: trains them, disciplines them, hands them the dense practical inheritance of skilled work and functioning institutions. When the income left, the children left, and they took the county's whole investment in them wherever they went, to Columbus and Charlotte and the Carolinas' factory towns, where their work raised other places' ledgers. The cost of raising and forming them stayed in McDowell. The return on them landed elsewhere, uncounted, like everything else in this story. No federal series tracks it. You can only see it negatively, in the institutions that could not be staffed and the storefronts that could not be filled, the civic shell of a place built for five times its population.

Four columns. I have not totaled them, and I am not going to. The full accounting, with its methods shown, belongs to a longer work than this one; an essay that announced a single number here would be trading in exactly the false precision this arrangement has always used to defend itself. What the columns establish does not need a total: the coal was priced at just under five dollars a ton, and the costs that price excluded were large enough to consume a county. McDowell County was not poor. McDowell County was a creditor. It still is.

* * *

In 1996 a second company came to the coalfields, and it did not come for anything under the ground.

We know it came on purpose because a country doctor wrote it down. Art Van Zee practiced in the Virginia coalfields, one mountain ridge from McDowell, and in 2009 he published in the American Journal of Public Health a history of what he had watched arrive: Purdue Pharma's 1996 launch of OxyContin into, in his words, the "economically distressed areas in which mining and logging had already taken their toll in injuries." Read that phrase the way you would read a geological survey. The injuries were the seam. Decades of underground work had loaded these counties with broken backs and crushed knees and the chronic pain of men like Bailey and Latusek, and a market for a powerful opioid was already lying there in the rock, waiting for a company with the will to mine it.

The will is no longer a matter of inference, because the documents came out. When the Massachusetts attorney general's complaint against Purdue was unsealed in 2019, it quoted the company's own assessment of its opportunity: a "large unmet need for vulnerable, underserved and stigmatized" market. Vulnerable. Underserved. Stigmatized. Somebody typed those words into a business document as a description of customers. The consulting firm McKinsey, whose work for Purdue surfaced in Justice Department and 50-state settlement records, produced strategies with names like "Evolve to Excellence" and "Turbocharge" for sharpening the sales effort, and the machinery for aiming it already existed: commercial prescriber data, doctor by doctor, that let a sales force find the physicians writing the most pain prescriptions and visit them again and again. None of this is allegation anymore. In 2007, Purdue's parent pled guilty to a federal felony for misbranding the drug's addiction risk, paying $600 million, with three executives, Michael Friedman, Howard Udell, and Paul Goldenheim, taking misdemeanor counts, all of it entered in the Western District of Virginia, coalfield jurisdiction, in May of that year. In November 2020 Purdue pled guilty again, to three federal felonies. Between 2008 and 2016, by the record before the Supreme Court in Harrington v. Purdue Pharma, the Sackler family took roughly $11 billion out of the company before steering it into bankruptcy. The three big distributors who carried the pills, McKesson, Cardinal Health, and AmerisourceBergen, agreed in 2022 to pay $21 billion under the national opioid settlement, $7.4 billion, $6 billion, and $6.1 billion respectively, without conceding they had done anything wrong.

Set the two cycles side by side and the shape is the same shape. Value out, in railcars the first time, in armored cars the second. Costs severed and left where they fell: in the lungs the first time, in the churchyards the second, 141 dead per 100,000 in McDowell County in 2015 against a national 16. The company dissolving through bankruptcy ahead of its obligations, Bailey's employer the first time, Purdue the second. Even the geography held constant, and this time the geography has been confirmed by methods that leave little room for argument: economists led by Abby Alpert and David Powell, publishing in the Quarterly Journal of Economics in 2022, showed that where OxyContin was marketed early and hard, overdose deaths followed, and the sociologist Shannon Monnat's work for the Appalachian Regional Commission mapped the so-called diseases of despair onto the counties of economic distress with terrible fidelity. The second extraction located itself by the damage of the first, the way a vein of ore locates a mine.

I am laboring this because of what the country chose to believe instead. The bestselling account of Appalachia in the years the churchyards were filling was a memoir arguing, in its essence, that the region's crisis was cultural: learned helplessness, family chaos, a people who had given up. Hillbilly Elegy was one man's story and entitled to itself, but as regional diagnosis it had the defendant problem again, the same one Kennedy's audience had in 1960, upgraded from neglect to character. Against the culture reading, set the discovery record. The pattern attributed to a culture of despair follows, county by county and year by year, a documented sales strategy, planned in business language, executed through prescriber data, admitted in two federal felony pleas. A culture did not choose McDowell County. A company did, and its consultants wrote memos about it. Elizabeth Catte made the dignity argument from inside the region; the litigation record now makes it from inside the companies. What looks from a distance like a people failing is, up close and in the documents, a people being mined.

Two books stand closest to this ground, and this account is in both of their debts: Patrick Radden Keefe's Empire of Pain, which followed the Sackler money to its sources, and Chris Hamby's Soul Full of Coal Dust, which followed the black lung claims war that Latusek died inside. Each tells one cycle whole. What neither quite frames, because each had a single industry to indict, is the ledger that joins them: coal and opioids as the same transaction repeated, the same severed cost settling on the same hollows, two generations apart. The first extraction created the conditions for the second. The second priced its product against the damage of the first. McDowell County paid both bills, and neither one, to this day, has been presented to the parties who collected.

* * *

Where did it all go? This is the question I found myself asking on the drive out of Welch, back up along the orange creek, and it has a literal answer. It went into the bridges. It went into the towers of New York and the mills of Pittsburgh and the gray hulls that staged out of Norfolk, into the bone structure of the American century. Some of it is over your head right now, holding up the room you are sitting in. The value did not vanish. It is the most visible value on the continent. It simply does not say where it is from.

And the people who received it owed McDowell County nothing they could have been expected to see. A driver crossing the Golden Gate Bridge in 1960 was not cruel. A family in a steel-framed apartment block was not stealing. The price of the coal had been paid in full at the mine mouth, just under five dollars a ton, every ton, fair and square and final. That is precisely the genius of the arrangement, if genius is the word: it laundered an immense taking into millions of honest little purchases. Distance did the rest. The costs stayed in the hollows, two days' drive and one mountain wall away from everyone the value reached, and no line on any receipt anywhere asked a buyer to look back up the seam to the county at the end of it. Nobody had to refuse to look. The arrangement was built so that looking was never required.

It did not have to be built that way. The accounting that would have closed the gap is not exotic; any household runs a version of it. Price what is taken at something near what its taking costs, at the time of the taking, and hold what is owed for the place and the people who will be left with the consequences. Had even a small honest fraction of the coal's true cost been kept against McDowell's future, the county that built the carriers would not be ranked 55th of 55 in the state its coal enriched. No law of economics prevented this. The beneficiaries were far away, the payers had no seat at the table where prices were set, and so the books were kept the way books are kept by whoever holds the pen.

I have tried to write all of this without partisan language, and the choice is worth making explicit, because the subject is usually handed straight to one of the two teams and I think that is one more way of not looking. A reader of the left will see in McDowell County a dispossession, and the record supports it. A reader of the right will see something the left's vocabulary misses: promises broken, loyalty unreciprocated, Latusek's voice saying I always thought Consol would take care of me, a man asking not for redistribution but for the deal he was promised to be honored. Both readers are looking at the same ledger. The mechanism that emptied McDowell County never checked a voter registration in its life, and a politics that makes the coalfields a possession of either party is one more entity extracting value from these mountains without paying for it.

On the way out, the courthouse holds the hill in the rearview mirror, still Romanesque, still keeping the records, 130 years of deeds and claims and judgments, the county's long honest attempt to write down who owes what to whom. I grew up far from here, beside tidewater that keeps a different version of the same account, and I do not pretend the road out of Welch is mine the way it is theirs. The creek runs beside the road, orange over the stones, and it will run orange tomorrow, and for decades after every reader of this essay is gone, a debt recorded in the only ink the arrangement left behind. Kennedy's sentence was true in 1960 and the indictment in it is still open. The coal is weighed, the steel is standing, the men are testifying or buried. What remains is the bill, drawn up at last with the right defendant's name on it, and the question his sentence has been asking for 65 years: not whether McDowell County was paid, because it was, just under five dollars a ton, but whether anyone is prepared to look at what it paid.
