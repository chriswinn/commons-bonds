## §0. Why this pass matters now

Pass 3.2 is the voice-polish pass — the polarity-CUT pass that chisels LLM-tics, em-dash crutches, meta-commentary, throat-clearing, and declarative-three-in-a-row LLM cadence out of the post-Pass-3.1 prose. The Harper's editorial register is **literary-essay register dispositive** (per brief §2: voice over apparatus; scene-led opening; named-tradition fluency without name-dropping; non-partisan framing with literary refusal of brief-register; first-person permitted but not memoir-leaning; apparatus drops; substance-drives-length). The cumulative effect that Pass 3.2 protects against is **prose that reads as competent but Claude-shaped** rather than as competent and Harper's-native. The audit bar is the Tier 1 #1 Christopher Beha three-second-read filter + Tier 1 #2 Harper's reader: would this open as a Harper's piece in §I, hold across §II–§VII institutional-and-biographical material, and close as a Harper's piece in §VIII?

The polarity discipline is strict: **Pass 3.2 cuts; Pass 3.5 (later session) restores.** If a cut feels like it loses substance, flag for Pass 3.5 consideration rather than holding the cut.

The em-dash discipline ([`feedback_em_dash_overuse.md`](../memory/feedback_em_dash_overuse.md) 2026-05-21 RATIFIED) is one of the dominant Pass 3.2 calibrations: **treat em-dash use as a flag requiring active justification; default to commas / periods / restructure for smoother prose.** Cumulative-essay em-dash count is itself a voice-polish concern. The post-Pass-3.1 essay carries **18 em-dashes across 11 lines (~2.5 per 1,000w)** — moderate cumulative density that includes four sites where Pass 3.1's H4 + H5 + M8 + L5 spot-fixes ADDED em-dashes (lines 93, 119, 123, 139). Each Pass-3.1-added em-dash gets fresh Pass-3.2 evaluation against the active-justification standard.

The headline diagnostic clusters are:

1. **"What I want to do / I want to" meta-commentary tic.** 7 instances across §I + §IV + §V + §VI + §VII + §VIII. Per brief §2 hurts-list: *"Self-narration / meta-commentary. 'What the framework's analysis shows here is...' / 'It bears noting that...' / 'What this means is...' Harper's reader reads the prose, not the writer's report on the prose."*

**Issue.** Seven "I want to / What I want to" meta-commentary lead-ins distributed across the essay. Each is a small surface; cumulatively they read as a Claude-prose tic — the essayist narrating the act of doing the essay rather than doing it. Worst offenders are line 11 + line 43 (both opening "What I want to do is to try" — double-hedge "want to" + "to try"); and line 159 (most explicit: meta-commentary about the act of closing, with the trailing *"a sentence that I have, by this point in the essay, perhaps earned"* doubling down on the act-of-essayist self-narration). The Harper's literary-essay tradition — Berry, Robinson, McKibben, Sullivan per brief §3 comp cluster — does not narrate its own moves in this register; the essayist's intelligence is felt in the prose itself, not announced.

**Note on Option-B first-person frame:** PC-4 §2 first-person-frame ratification preserved the limited first-person frame; Pass 3.1 §6 forward-flag #7 routes authorial-presence density audit to Pass 3.2.
