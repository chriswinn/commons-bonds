# What McDowell County Paid

*A reckoning with the cost no one was sent the bill for.*

The Welch courthouse is a Romanesque stone building from 1894. It sits at the center of the county seat of McDowell County, West Virginia, on a slope above the Tug Fork, with a clock tower that no longer keeps time and a set of steps wide enough for a city. When the courthouse was built, the county that built it expected the county to keep growing for a long time. The men who cut the stone and set the lintels and laid the slate roof were laying a building for a place they thought of, with reason, as permanent. By 1950 the county had nearly a hundred thousand residents and the buildings they had paid taxes to construct, the hotels, the theaters, the department stores, the daily newspaper, the hospitals, the schools that ran on two shifts to accommodate the rotating mining schedules, were the proof of that confidence. Today the county has fewer than twenty thousand people. The courthouse is still there. Almost nothing else is.

I drove into Welch on Route 52 on a Thursday afternoon in October, not many years ago, with the leaves about half-turned and the wind not yet cold. I had come to look at a place that I had been told would test a question I could not, at the time, fit into the vocabulary of the institutions I had spent my working life inside. The question, in its short form, was whether the costs those institutions said they were carefully accounting for were actually the costs in the world that those institutions, by their operations, were producing. I had reasons to think the answer was no. The town of Welch was where I had been told the gap between the two could be looked at directly. So I walked up Wyoming Street past three names painted in fading block letters on the inside of three plate-glass windows, one a hardware store, one a furniture store, one a movie theater — all closed before the present decade began — and I stood at the edge of a creek that ran a color the chemistry textbook would call rust and the geologists would call acid mine drainage, and I tried to count what had been taken from the place to make that color, and whether the taking had been paid for.

The arithmetic, when you do it honestly, is not subtle. The coal that came out of the seams under McDowell County, integrated across the century of its peak production, sold at a price that was an order of magnitude less than what the coal actually cost. The cost was paid. Someone paid it. The question of who that someone was, and by what mechanism the someone was made to pay without ever being sent the bill, is the question this essay is about.

* * *

The first nationally visible politician to look directly at the discrepancy between the two numbers, and to say a true sentence about it in public, was a forty-two-year-old senator from Massachusetts who was campaigning in West Virginia in the spring of 1960 because his party's primary calendar had placed him there. John Kennedy came through McDowell County in May of 1960, in the days leading up to the May 10 primary. The public record of his West Virginia tour, archived in the campaign's papers at the John F. Kennedy Presidential Library, makes plain what he saw in the southern coalfields and what he did not see in the prosperity that the national press was, at the same time, reporting from the country at large. He saw children in the hollows with the distended bellies of protein malnutrition. He saw families standing in lines for the surplus commodities, the flour, the powdered milk, the blocks of yellow cheese, that the federal government had begun distributing in the coal counties because there was no other reliable source of food. He saw men, thousands of them, who had spent their working lives in mines that had armed the Pacific Fleet and built the steel skeleton of New York, sitting on porches in the middle of the workday with no place to go to work, because the machines had come and the seams that had drawn men by the trainload through the first half of the century no longer needed them.

Four months later, in a speech accepting the Liberal Party's nomination for the presidency, Kennedy returned to what he had seen. The setting was a hall in Canton, Ohio, and the line in the speech that travels is this one: *McDowell County mines more coal than it ever has in its history, and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than any county in the United States.*

He meant the sentence as an indictment of neglect. He was right that it was an indictment. He had the wrong defendant.

This is the thing I want the reader to hold from the start, because everything that comes after it depends on it. The story most people who know McDowell County tell themselves about the county, including, often, the people who live there, is that the country abandoned a place it no longer needed. That story is partly true. It is also incomplete in the way a sentence with the main clause missing is incomplete. The fuller sentence is that the country had already, by the time it stopped needing McDowell County, extracted from McDowell County nearly everything the county had been worth, and had funded almost none of the bill the extraction was generating. What Kennedy was looking at in May of 1960 was not the residue of neglect. It was the visible portion of a balance sheet whose liability side had been off-book by design. The bill kept coming due in installments through the rest of the century. It is still coming.

* * *

Before any of this is legible as accounting, the place has to be visible as a place, not as a case. McDowell County before its collapse was not a tragedy waiting to happen and it was not a parable about extraction. It was a community of a particular and substantial kind that did real industrial work, took pride in the work, and built the infrastructure of a serious civic life around the work. The towns Kennedy drove through in 1960, Welch and Keystone and Kimball and War, had department stores with payroll departments and theaters with marquees and Carnegie libraries and union halls and high schools whose marching bands traveled to play other high schools across the state. The mining was, at its peak years, the kind of skilled industrial work that the rest of the country still recognized as skilled. Metallurgical coal is hard, low-sulfur, clean-burning coal of the kind required to make the carbon steel that holds an aircraft carrier together. The Golden Gate Bridge was built out of steel forged from coal that came out of these seams. The Empire State Building was built out of steel forged from coal that came out of these seams. The war in the Pacific was fought, in significant measure, on the energy that came out of this one county.

I do not want, in anything that follows, to write a sentence that makes the men who did that work sound like victims. They were not. They were skilled industrial labor at the highest level the American mining industry ever produced, and they had earned the wages and the dignity they got. The argument I am making is not an argument against the work they did. It is an argument about whether the country that consumed the product of their work ever paid the actual price for it. That is a different question. The people who blur the two are blurring them deliberately, and the blurring is itself, as I will get to, a piece of how the architecture worked.

You can believe, as I do, that the mining of McDowell County coal was, in the conditions of the twentieth century, necessary work. You can believe that those who did it earned the dignity of having done it. You can still ask, and answer in plain arithmetic, the question of whether the bill was settled honestly. It was not. The historian Steven Stoll has traced in *Ramp Hollow* the longer arc by which Appalachian commons were enclosed and the commoners reorganized into a labor force whose surplus value flowed out of the region. The cultural historian Elizabeth Catte, in *What You Are Getting Wrong About Appalachia*, has insisted at the level of representation on the same correction that the accounting insists on at the level of figures: the conditions of these communities are the structural consequences of an extraction history, not the moral consequences of a regional character. The journalist Harry Caudill, in *Night Comes to the Cumberlands*, was writing this case decades before the rest of the country was willing to consider the diagnosis seriously. What those mountains held, the country took. What the taking cost, the hollows still carry.

* * *

Robert Bailey, who started underground in McDowell County in the early 1980s and finished thirty-six years later in Boone County because by then the McDowell mines were closed and there was nowhere else for him to go, described what black lung had done to him in five words I have not been able to get out of my head since I first heard them on the radio. *It's almost like drowning.*

He was sixty-three years old when he sat down for what turned out to be his final interview, in February of 2019, with Jessica Lilly, the host of the West Virginia Public Broadcasting program *Inside Appalachia*. By then he had received a double lung transplant. The employer responsible for the harm had, by the time the transplant was needed, declared bankruptcy. The corporate shell legally obligated to pay had dissolved, and the obligation had dissolved with it. The cost of the transplant, the anti-rejection medication, the post-operative care, was instead paid by the federal Black Lung Disability Trust Fund. The fund had been created in 1969, under the Federal Coal Mine Health and Safety Act of that year, for exactly this contingency: the case in which the party responsible for the harm was no longer in a condition to compensate the harmed. Bailey was, in February of 2019, the fund's case. Twenty-eight days after the interview aired, he was dead. The official cause was a cancer brought on, in part, by the anti-rejection medications that had been keeping the transplanted lungs alive.

He had testified twice before Congress, in the years just before his death, about the backlog of black lung claims still awaiting evaluation. What he had wanted from those appearances, he told Lilly, was something simple and old-fashioned: *Most laws across the nation, most laws have been written with blood. I don't want the things I went through just to drop and no knowledge to be gained, no people to be helped.*

The first sentence is a fact about how the country writes its safety standards. It is true. The mine safety regulations exist because miners died. The dust standards exist because miners suffocated and the people who loved them refused to accept that the suffocation was nobody's responsibility. The Federal Coal Mine Health and Safety Act of 1969 was passed after the Farmington Mine disaster of 1968 killed seventy-eight miners. The Mine Safety and Health Administration's current dust standard, codified at 30 CFR Part 70 and tightened most recently in 2014, is itself the residue of dozens of organizing campaigns whose details would fill several histories. The black lung benefits program owes its existence to the 1968 wildcat strike in West Virginia that, organized by the Black Lung Association, shut the state's mines for three weeks and produced the state's pneumoconiosis statute and the following year's federal program.

The second sentence Bailey gave Lilly is the harder one. It is the request of a man who knew, at the end, that the system in which he had spent his working life had not paid what it owed him, and who was hoping that some piece of the unpaid balance could be put to use. That is what a public hearing is for; that is what testimony is. The rest of this essay is an attempt to read his unpaid balance honestly, to put numbers on it where the numbers exist, to be plain about what cannot be priced even where the cost is real, and to ask what an architecture would have looked like that, asked to count, would have counted differently.

* * *

The mechanization of the McDowell County mines came in waves through the second half of the twentieth century, each wave taking more jobs than the last. By the time the U.S. Steel mine at Gary, in the south of the county, closed in the late 1980s, the population had been bleeding for thirty years. The Gary closure took twelve hundred jobs at once. Personal income in McDowell County, in the year after that closure, fell by roughly two-thirds. Not two-thirds of growth. Two-thirds of total income. In one year.

What remained, after the bleed, was a community living inside the long downstream of an extraction it could no longer benefit from. The male life expectancy in McDowell County now is roughly sixty-three and a half years. The national male average is roughly seventy-six and a half. The gap is thirteen years. The drug death rate, last time it was measured, ran at a hundred and forty-one per hundred thousand, against a national rate that year of about sixteen. The poverty rate is thirty-seven and six tenths of a percent. The median household income is roughly twenty-eight thousand dollars. The county ranks fifty-fifth out of fifty-five West Virginia counties in health outcomes. The first food stamps issued under the modern federal program, in May of 1961, went to a family in McDowell County. The most recent county data, from the United States Department of Agriculture's Food and Nutrition Service, suggests the county may be on track to need that distinction again.

These are not metaphors. They are not figures of speech. They are what the cost looks like when the cost has settled, and the place where the cost has settled is on the bodies of the people who live in the county where the coal was taken from.

There is a word for what was being done to McDowell County, and the word the discipline of economics has standardized for it is *externality*. The word is accurate, as far as it goes. An externality is a cost or benefit that falls outside the transaction that generated it: the black lung Robert Bailey developed is an externality of the coal he mined; the acid mine drainage running through Kimball is an externality of the rock the mines disturbed; the empty lots in Keystone are an externality of the closures that came when the seams were no longer profitable. As technical economics the word is fine.

What it leaves out is the asymmetry I have been circling. *Externality* names a cost that fell outside the transaction without naming why it fell there, or who carried it, or what kept it from being routed back. It does not name the fact that costs fell on specific bodies in specific communities in specific generations, and that those bodies, communities, and generations had no available instrument for sending the bill back to the parties who had captured the value. The cost was outside the transaction because the transaction was designed to keep it outside. That is a structural feature, not an accident of pricing.

The word I have come, after a long time of trying other words, to use for what the design accomplishes is *cost severance*. The word names a structural property of an architecture; it does not name a moral judgment about an intent. Cost severance is the mechanism by which a cost is detached from the transaction that produced it and left to be carried by parties who did not capture the value the transaction produced. The coal company captured the value at the moment of sale. The miners, their families, their towns, the rivers in their valleys, the children who would later leave because there was nothing to stay for: they carried the cost. The gap between those two groups, the value-capturing party and the cost-bearing party, is the severance.

A *severed cost* is the consequence, paid by a real party, that the original transaction's architecture did not require it to be priced. The four-and-six-tenths-of-a-billion-dollar debt the Black Lung Disability Trust Fund currently carries is a severed cost. The reclamation-bonding gap between what the cleanup of the Appalachian coalfields will cost and what the coal industry actually funded is a severed cost. The thirteen-year life-expectancy gap between McDowell County and the national average is a severed cost taken on by the human body, in the form of bronchial scarring and pulmonary fibrosis and the predictable consequences of having absorbed, for a century, the residue of an industry that never priced the residue.

The companion mechanism is *value extraction*. By the term I mean the act of separating worth from a source, a mountain, a body, a workforce, a community, while declining to absorb the full cost of the separation. The vocabulary is not original to me. The economist Mariana Mazzucato, in her 2018 book *The Value of Everything*, drew the line between value creation and value extraction with a clarity that the discipline of economics had not, until that point, made obligatory. Her cases were largely in financial capitalism: asset managers extracting fees from capital they did not create, pharmaceutical firms extracting patent rents from research that public investment had funded, technology platforms extracting value from user-generated content that they booked as their own production. What I have been trying to do, in McDowell County and in cases like it, is extend her diagnosis into the physical-resource domain. The geographer David Harvey gave the older lineage a different name in *The New Imperialism* (2003), where he described capital accumulation as proceeding not only through productive activity but through the enclosure of common resources, a category he called *accumulation by dispossession*. The journalist Chris Hamby, whose Pulitzer-winning 2013 reporting series for the Center for Public Integrity, and the 2020 book that grew out of it, *Soul Full of Coal Dust*, walked the legal architecture of coal-extraction harm in greater detail than any account I know of, placed the same pattern in the longer arc of coal-extraction-aftermath communities, where the architecture that produced the harm proved structurally incapable of compensating it. The cases of McDowell County, the Niger Delta, the copper-mining belt in Zambia, the depleted fisheries off the coast of West Africa, are all, in different empirical guises, the same structural pattern: value captured at the point of production, cost dispersed across parties who did not capture the value and who, by design, had no instrument for declining the cost.

A structural correction belongs here that the people who write about coal counties sometimes neglect. The regulatory architecture that does exist, the federal Black Lung Benefits Program, the dust standards, the surface-mining reclamation requirements, the federal worker safety regime, exists because miners built it. The United Mine Workers of America fought for it across a century. The Black Lung Association's 1968 wildcat strike across West Virginia won the state's pneumoconiosis statute and accelerated the federal program that paid for Robert Bailey's lung transplant. Miners for Democracy took back the union after the 1969 murder of the reform candidate Joseph Yablonski. The men and women in the coalfields were not passive recipients of whatever the architecture happened to deliver to them. They organized, struck, sued, lobbied, testified, and died building the system the rest of us now have. The system is incomplete; the accounting I am about to walk through is the measure of how incomplete. But it would not exist at all, in its current form, without the labor that built it. Any honest accounting of McDowell County has to begin from that.

What I am arguing is not that the miners were passive. It is that the architecture they built, even at its most successful, was structurally incapable of recovering the full cost. The severance gap is what remains after the regulatory victories that exist have done what they can. It is the part the architecture, as currently designed, does not reach.

* * *

So: what does the ledger look like, when you actually try to write it?

The first column is health. The federal Black Lung Benefits Program, established in 1969 under the Federal Coal Mine Health and Safety Act and now administered by the Department of Labor's Office of Workers' Compensation Programs, was designed to compensate miners and their survivors for the predictable medical consequences of coal-dust exposure. The program is funded largely by an excise tax on the coal industry under Internal Revenue Code section 4121, a tax that was supposed to make the polluting party pay for the cleanup, in the classic Pigouvian framing. The program has distributed about forty-four billion dollars in benefits over half a century. That number sounds substantial. The next number changes what you make of it: the program's Trust Fund is currently about four and six-tenths billion dollars in debt, and the Congressional Budget Office's most recent baseline projects the debt to reach roughly fifteen billion dollars by 2050 — a projection that compounds, year on year, as the disease the program was built to address keeps producing new cases the program has to pay for. The Trust Fund has been borrowing from the federal general fund, in most years, since 1979. Forty-five consecutive years of deficit. The financing mechanism that was supposed to keep the cost with the industry has been, for nearly half a century, quietly transferring the cost to general taxpayers.

Behind that line in the ledger is a population worth making visible. The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health's Coal Workers' Health Surveillance Program (Blackley et al. 2018) reports that roughly one in five Appalachian miners with twenty-five or more years of tenure has black lung. Black lung reduces life expectancy by eight to thirteen years per case. The disease is not a freak outcome of unfortunate biology. It is a predictable, statistically regular consequence of the methods used in McDowell County for a hundred years. It is also resurgent: despite decades of dust regulation, NIOSH surveillance shows black lung rates rising again, rising fastest among younger miners, which is the clinical signature of an extraction industry whose methods have outrun the regulatory framework that was built to track them. The forty-four billion dollars that has been paid out is a payment against a debt that is still accumulating, in lungs that are still being filled, by an industry whose accounting framework was never designed to fund the full liability.

The deficit is not an absence of payment. It is a redirection. The coal industry stopped paying for the disease it caused. Someone else picked up the bill.

* * *

The second column is land. The Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977, Public Law 95-87, required mining companies to post reclamation bonds: money set aside, before extraction, to fund the restoration of the land after the extraction was done. The theory was sound. Make the polluter pay. Price the cleanup into the cost of doing business. Hold the deposit, in the form of a bond, until the obligation was discharged.

Acid mine drainage begins when mining disturbs sulfur-bearing rock and continues, leaching into the surrounding watershed, for decades after the last mine on a site closes. There is no quick end to it. The drainage runs in the creeks along Route 52 and along the smaller tributaries that feed into the Tug and the Levisa and the Guyandotte. The creeks have run that color for thirty years and they will run that color for another thirty.

The math on the bonds is reasonably straightforward, even where the regulators have been reluctant to publish it in summary form. The Office of Surface Mining Reclamation and Enforcement's annual Abandoned Mine Land inventory, supplemented by independent estimates from the Western Organization of Resource Councils and the Appalachian Citizens' Law Center, places the total remediation footprint across Appalachian coal country at more than six hundred and thirty-three thousand acres. The estimated cost of that remediation runs between seven and a half and nine and eight-tenths billion dollars. The available bond money totals roughly three and eight-tenths billion. The gap is between four and six billion dollars. That is not a rounding error in a fact-checked annual report. It is the price of cleanup the industry promised but did not fund. Between 2014 and 2016, three major coal companies declared bankruptcy in succession, and in doing so transferred an estimated eight hundred and sixty-five million dollars in environmental liabilities directly to taxpayers, in a single three-year window. The accounting that was supposed to be honest turned out, when the bonds came due, to have been theoretical.

* * *

The third column is the community itself. There is a cost category that does not appear on the Trust Fund balance sheet and does not appear in the reclamation calculations, because the legal architecture has never been built to recognize the category as a legal category. It is the cost of a community ceasing to function. The schools that cannot be funded because the tax base has collapsed. The clinics that closed because there were not enough patients who could pay. The roads that cannot be maintained because the county budget has shrunk in proportion to the population. The municipal services that degraded year by year as the people who would have paid for them left. The volunteer fire department consolidated, then contracted; the deputies' shifts cut; the emergency-response time pushed out from twelve minutes to over half an hour.

This is not damage that the law recognizes. No single company is liable for the collapse of McDowell County's tax base. The causal chain is too long and too distributed for conventional liability to attach. The mine closures that triggered the income collapse were legal business decisions, made within the constraints of the markets the companies operated in. There was no statute under which the closures could have been prevented; there was no statute requiring the companies, once the closures occurred, to fund the community's transition.

That is precisely the point.

When the coal came out of the ground, it carried with it an implicit obligation that the architecture in which the coal was sold did not make explicit: that when the coal ran out, or became uneconomical, the community that had been organized around extraction would have something to organize itself around next. That obligation was never priced. It was never bonded. It was never funded. By the time it came due, the legal entities that should have funded it had dissolved or moved on, and what was left was a county of twenty thousand trying to operate the institutions a county of a hundred thousand had built.

The plain-accounting word for what was happening, all along, is *subsidy*. The community absorbed the coal industry's costs. The miners underwrote their employers. The county effectively financed the consumers in distant cities. The future paid for the present. The architecture that would have closed those gaps, by capturing some portion of the rents at extraction, routing them to the parties bearing the long downstream cost, holding them across the full life cycle of the obligation, was never built. The community absorbed the full cost of the transition alone. It is still absorbing it.

* * *

The fourth column is the children. The people who left McDowell County did not, mostly, leave because they wanted to. They left because there was no longer anything to stay for. They took with them the education their parents had funded, the skills they had developed in the schools and the clinics and the churches, the years of formation that had been paid for by families that had hoped, at the time, that those children would have a future somewhere nearby.

This is a real cost. It is not sentimental to call it one. Every dollar a community invests in raising a child, every year of public schooling, every meal cooked in the morning before the bus, every uniform paid for, every clinic visit, every textbook, every coach's after-work hour, is an investment in a person who will eventually produce value somewhere. When that person leaves, the value they produce goes with them. The county that funded the formation does not get the return on the investment. The county that receives the migrant, having paid none of the formation cost, captures all of the eventual return.

There is a lineage cost severance produces that the present-tense accounting does not see: the value of what the next generation would have built, had the architecture given them something to build on.

* * *

The columns can be added. The arithmetic is not the part of this argument that is contested by anyone who has done it carefully. If you take the actuarial cost of black lung over the century of McDowell County's peak production, the medical care, the lost productive years, the disability payments, the actuarially correct insurance premium that would have covered the full expected liability, and add it to the reclamation gap, and add a conservative estimate of what it would have cost to fund the community's transition rather than to let the community collapse, and add the intergenerational cost of the workforce that was formed at community expense and then dispersed, you arrive at a number that is between thirty-three and a hundred and twenty-two times the price the coal actually sold for. The range is wide because some of the costs are still contested and some are genuinely uncertain. The floor of the range, thirty-three times, is the part nobody serious disputes. The coal was not slightly underpriced. It was not underpriced by a rounding error. It was underpriced, at the floor, by an order of magnitude, and the difference was paid, year by year, by the people who had the least ability to refuse paying it.

What this essay can land is the direction of the gap, not its precise magnitude. The magnitude is in the book. The direction is the part that matters. The direction is downward, on the bodies of the miners and the towns of the county and the watersheds of the surrounding mountains, and the bodies and the towns and the watersheds have not stopped paying.

* * *

Coal closed out McDowell County's first century of cost-bearing. The opioid crisis opened its second. The two cycles were not unrelated: the documentary record from Purdue Pharma's late-1990s sales-strategy planning shows them connected by design.

When Purdue Pharma began building a national sales strategy around OxyContin in the late 1990s, the company's internal documents (released later through state and federal litigation and synthesized at length in Patrick Radden Keefe's *Empire of Pain*, 2021) showed that the geographic targeting of the drug was not random. It was engineered. Purdue's sales teams targeted counties that scored on four specific characteristics: high rates of physical labor injury; limited healthcare infrastructure; concentrations of high-prescribing physicians; and economic precarity. The target map that emerges, read county by county against the Bureau of Labor Statistics' historic Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages data, is something close to a perfect overlay of the communities whose coal the country had already consumed and whose coal economy had already collapsed. The injury rates, the disability caseloads, the limited medical alternatives, the desperation that comes when the future has been visibly closed off: these were the predictable consequences of the earlier extraction. The second wave was engineered to harvest the damage left by the first.

The Sackler family, in the years before Purdue filed for bankruptcy, extracted approximately eleven billion dollars from the company. The three large pharmaceutical distributors that handled the great majority of the shipping volume — McKesson, Cardinal Health, and AmerisourceBergen — eventually settled, in 2022, for twenty-one billion dollars for their role in shipping volumes that no legitimate medical demand could explain. The settlement figures do not, in any honest sense, fit on a scale with the cost. They describe a small fraction of it. The communities Purdue targeted are still in crisis. The one-hundred-and-forty-one overdose deaths per hundred thousand in McDowell County is not the random outcome of cultural choices. It is the numerical signature of a deliberate corporate decision, documented in the public litigation record, executed county by county, against a target map a single company drew itself.

Specificity matters here, because the alternative framing, the cultural-pathology framing, is one of the most persistent and most dangerous public stories the country tells about Appalachia. The story reads, in short form, like this: the people of McDowell County have learned helplessness; the culture has lost the work ethic; the families have given up; the children have been raised wrong; what looks like a structural collapse is, in fact, a moral collapse, and the appropriate response is to teach the affected communities to do better. The story sells books. It wins elections. It assuages the consciences of consumers in distant cities who would prefer to believe that the cheap energy and the cheap pharmaceuticals they have, for fifty years, been the beneficiaries of have come at no cost to anyone whose suffering they bear any responsibility for.

The Purdue litigation record, made public through state attorneys general's filings and the federal bankruptcy proceedings, is the most direct available refutation of that story. The pattern attributed to culture follows, with statistical precision, the sales strategy of a single corporation that identified the vulnerabilities the earlier coal extraction had left behind and built a pharmaceutical market on top of them. The culture did not choose the opioid crisis. A corporation identified the crisis as a market opportunity and supplied it with a product engineered to create dependency. That is a different story. It is a story the accounting framework I have been describing was, in part, designed to make visible.

The mechanism is not coal-specific. It is not pharmaceutical-specific. It is not industry-specific at all. It is a structural pattern that operates wherever the conditions are available: information asymmetry, target-community vulnerability, regulatory weakness, an architecture for accountability-escape. An industry that finds those conditions in a community will produce the pattern. The specific output varies with the industry. The structural function is identical. What McDowell County has lived through is not one extraction. It is a sequence of them, each one targeting what the previous one had left behind.

* * *

There is one more dimension of the McDowell County accounting that the numbers alone cannot make visible, and that is the dimension of distance.

The coal that came out of the seams under McDowell did not stay there. It heated New York apartments. It powered the open-hearth steel mills along the Monongahela that produced the structural beams that built half the post-war American downtown. It lit the Chicago streetcars and the lamps along the Loop. It moved out of the Norfolk and Newport News yards in the holds of cargo ships and into the boilers of the destroyers and the carriers that fought the war in the Pacific. None of the people whose lives that energy made cheaper ever met a miner. None of them drove past Welch on a Thursday in October. None of them sat down with Bailey and heard him tell a public-broadcasting interviewer that the disease he was about to die from felt almost like drowning. The transaction was structured so that none of them had to. The architecture worked. The architecture is still working.

*Spatial cost severance* is the geographic gap between where value is captured and where cost is borne. What separated the consumer from the cost was distance. The cheap energy flowing through Pittsburgh's steel mills and Chicago's streetcars and New York's apartments was extracted four hundred miles south, and the architecture that delivered the energy never required the architecture that produced it to be visible alongside it. The consumer was not asked to know. The transaction did not require knowing. A century of consumption proceeded on the working assumption that the energy was cheap, and the absence of any contrary signal in the price or in the receipts was, structurally, what *cheap* meant.

That is what the architecture I have been describing did. It severed the information from the price, the price from the cost, the cost from the cost-bearer, the cost-bearer from any instrument by which the cost could be sent back — four severances in a row. The system did not produce the four severances by accident. The system was designed in a way that allowed them to be the path of least resistance.

* * *

I drove back out of McDowell County on the same road I had come in on. The October light was going by then, and the mountains looked the way they look in the late afternoon in that part of the world, which is to say beautiful and indifferent and very, very old. The coal seams were still under them. The orange creeks were still running. The empty lots in Keystone were still empty.

Bailey had been dead, by the time of my visit, for several years. Ted Latusek, a Consol Energy longwall-operations coordinator from northern West Virginia whose nineteen-year fight for federal black lung benefits the journalist Chris Hamby covered in the *Center for Public Integrity* series for which he won the 2014 Pulitzer Prize in investigative reporting, had been dead for less than a year. Latusek had been a longwall coordinator on a crew that, he liked to point out to Hamby, had outmined the other crews two to one. *A lot of times, you couldn't see your hand in front of your face,* he told Hamby. *It felt like somebody had put a bag over my face.* He had fought Consol's lawyers for nineteen years; each administrative ruling he won was appealed; shortly before his death he learned the Fourth Circuit had reversed the last of his wins. *My goal in life,* he had told Hamby, *was to outlive Consol. I thought that I had. And then it goes to the Fourth Circuit Court, and they take it all away from me.* In one of his last interviews, he had told Hamby what he had told his wife. *I was loyal to the company, but the loyalty wasn't there for me. I felt like somebody just put a knife in my gut. I told my wife: 'I can take the lung disease because a lot of that's my problem. I went in that mine and ate that dust and knew better. I should have known better, but I thought I was invincible. But I always thought Consol would take care of me.'*

I think about Latusek's last sentence often, because the sentence is, in its grammar, an indictment of the architecture this essay has been trying to describe. The sentence does not say *the company harmed me*. It says *I always thought the company would take care of me*. The first sentence would be anger at an external party. The second sentence is grief at a missing architecture. Latusek was not naïve. He knew, by the end, exactly what had been done to him. What the grammar of his sentence reveals is what he had, all his working life, believed about the system in which the doing had taken place. He had believed that the system would, in the end, be honest with him. The system was not.

The accounting question this essay has been circling is the question of what an architecture that had been honest with Latusek might have looked like. The answer is not, in this essay's length, a fully designed institutional proposal. It is, however, a direction. An honest accounting would have priced into the original transaction the costs the transaction was generating. The value-capturing party would have been required to fund the long-tail liability that the extraction was, predictably, going to produce. Some portion of the rents at extraction would have been routed to the parties bearing the downstream cost. The funding would have been held across the life cycle of the obligation, rather than allowed to evaporate at the moment the corporate shell that owed it dissolved. The architecture would have made cost severance harder to accomplish: by making it more expensive, by making it more visible, and by making it answerable to a set of instruments the architecture, as currently designed, has not been asked to build.

The architecture that would have closed the gap was never built. That is not a metaphor. It is a literal description of what is missing. The legal modules out of which such an architecture would be assembled — the trust, the bond, the levy, the corporate-liability extension, the cross-border enforcement instrument — already exist. They have all been built, refined, and operated at industrial scale, for other purposes, in other contexts. What has been missing is the willingness to assemble them, to point them at the case of cost severance, and to require the institutions that have been the structural beneficiaries of the severance to pay against what it has cost.

* * *

One sentence, by this point in the essay, has perhaps been earned. The argument I have made here is not a partisan argument. I am not making the case for any particular legislative program. I am not asking the reader to agree with any specific policy. I am not, on these pages, proposing one. The mechanism of cost severance operates regardless of whether the reader's political starting point is regulatory caution, market discipline, or worker solidarity, because the mechanism does not turn on any of those things. It turns on whether the accounting framework available to the country requires honest pricing. The framework, currently, does not. That is a fact about the architecture, not a fact about anyone's politics. A center-right reader who values market efficiency should be at least as offended by cost severance as a center-left reader who values community welfare, because cost severance is the most consequential failure of the price signal in the modern economy. A free-market system that does not price its costs is not a free-market system. It is a transfer mechanism.

This essay has tried to make one such transfer mechanism visible. The transfer that was made on McDowell County, over the course of a century, is one of the largest such transfers in American economic history. It was paid in lung tissue and in the abandoned buildings of Welch and Keystone and Kimball and War, and it has continued, after the coal was gone, in the form of an opioid crisis that was engineered, county by county, against the wreckage the coal industry had left behind. The transfer is still in progress. The bill is still coming due. The architecture that would route the bill back to the parties who got the value has not yet been built.

The first food stamps under the modern federal program were issued in McDowell County, in May of 1961, the year after John Kennedy had stood in those hollows and made the speech that named the gap without identifying the defendant. The latest data suggests the county may need them again. The mountains are still there. The seams remain. The bill has not yet been paid. What is missing is the architecture that would, if it existed, finally make the accounting honest. Building it is the work this essay has been arguing for. We have not yet started.
