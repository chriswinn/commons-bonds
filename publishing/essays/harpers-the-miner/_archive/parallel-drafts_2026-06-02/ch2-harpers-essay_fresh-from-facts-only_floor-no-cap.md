# What McDowell County Paid

*A reckoning with the arithmetic of a place the country used*

---

He told the radio host, near the end, that it was almost like drowning.

That was his five-word definition of black lung. Not the medical one, which involves coal macules and progressive massive fibrosis and the slow architectural failure of the lungs' gas-exchange surface. The five-word definition was the one he had earned the hard way, thirty-six years underground, and it was the one he wanted on the public record.

His name was Robert Wesley Bailey, Jr. He was born in Bluefield, West Virginia, on the twenty-second of June, 1953. He graduated from Northfork High School, in Northfork, McDowell County, and he went to work in the mines the way the men in his family and his town went to work in the mines, because the work was there and the work paid, and because the alternative, in a county whose economy had been single-commodity since before he was born, was to leave. He worked first at Eastern Gas Associates. He worked then at the Keystone Number One mines. When the McDowell County operations closed, he went north and west into Boone County and worked for Patriot Coal, and he finished his career there as a Fire Boss, which is the title given to the man who walks the working face before each shift to test for methane, to check the roof, to certify that the place is safe enough for other men to enter.

He retired with bad lungs. By that point his lungs were so bad he needed a double lung transplant. By the time the transplant was performed, the company that had legally owed him medical care had declared bankruptcy and dissolved itself out of the obligation. The federal Black Lung Disability Trust Fund paid for the surgery instead. The Trust Fund paid for the anti-rejection medications that kept his transplanted lungs alive. Two months before he died, doctors found liver cancer, which they attributed, in part, to the anti-rejection medications that had been keeping him alive. The cancer killed him in a matter of weeks. He spent his last days at home in Princeton, West Virginia, on hospice care. He died on the fifteenth of March, 2019, at the age of sixty-five.

Twenty-eight days before he died, he gave an interview to Jessica Lilly, the host of West Virginia Public Broadcasting's *Inside Appalachia*. She published the long-form story a month after his funeral, under the title "Black Lung Patient Fights for Miner Benefits in His Final Days." He had testified before the United States Congress twice. The first time, the public record places him in 430 Dirksen, on the morning of the twenty-second of July, 2014, at 9:45 a.m., before the Senate Health, Education, Labor and Pensions Subcommittee on Employment and Workplace Safety. The hearing's title, which I copy out in full because the title is its own evidence of what the country had learned to call the situation by then, was "Coal Miners' Struggle for Justice: How Unethical Legal and Medical Practices Stack the Deck Against Black Lung Claimants." He had been one of the miners whose initial black lung claim was denied by Dr. Paul S. Wheeler at Johns Hopkins, whose systematic-denial pattern would be the documentary engine of the Center for Public Integrity's Pulitzer series. Bailey appealed. A lung biopsy later confirmed complicated black lung. The fight to be acknowledged as suffering from the disease he was, in fact, suffering from took roughly four years.

This was a man, in other words, who had to fight, for four years, to get the country to admit that his lungs were destroyed in exchange for its electricity.

He told Lilly: "Most laws across the nation, most laws have been written with blood. I don't want the things I went through just to drop and no knowledge to be gained, no people to be helped." He told the Senate subcommittee: "I would like to see Congress step in and make changes that would help process these claims that have taken so long for most miners to even receive benefits." He told Lilly, talking about his grandchildren: "They're just growing up and the next thing you know they're gonna be grown like I am. And if I hadn't had this time added to my life, I would have missed all of that." He told her his closing wish was that the disease itself would be conquered: it would be so much better, he said, in the long run, if no one ever had to be in his condition again.

There are men who pass through their own dying with that kind of clarity, and there are men who don't. Bailey did. The clarity was the public record he wanted to leave.

I want to write down, as carefully as I can, what was paid for that public record. By the public record I mean both the testimony and the lungs.

The exchange Bailey was a participant in had a market price. The market price was the price per ton of metallurgical coal, set against the prevailing demand from steelmakers and utilities and, ultimately, against the cost of substitute fuels. The market price did not include the destruction of Bailey's lungs. The market price did not include the destruction of Theodore Latusek's lungs, two hundred miles north, in Consol Energy's longwall operations in the Fairmont coalfield, where Latusek's crew outmined other crews two-to-one and Latusek spent nineteen years fighting Consol's lawyers to be acknowledged as one of the people that record had cost. The market price did not include the orange creeks of McDowell County, whose color is iron oxide, the product of acid mine drainage from sulfur-bearing rock that the mining disturbed and that goes on draining into the watershed for decades after the last mine on a site has closed. The market price did not include the 633,000 acres of Appalachian land that now require reclamation, the gap between what reclamation will cost and what reclamation has been bonded for (between four and six billion dollars, depending on whose estimate you accept), or the 865 million dollars in environmental liabilities that three coal-company bankruptcies, between 2014 and 2016, transferred directly onto the federal balance sheet. The market price did not include the male life expectancy in McDowell County, which is now thirteen years shorter than the national male average. The market price did not include the county's drug death rate, which is roughly ten times the national rate. The market price did not include the fact that the first food stamps in the modern federal food-stamp program, issued in May of 1961, went to a family in McDowell County, and that the most recent data suggest the county may need them again.

What I am describing is a transaction with a known price and an unknown one. The known price was what the buyer paid for the coal. The unknown price was what somebody paid for the conditions that produced the coal: the lungs, the watersheds, the post-extraction county, the long-tail bodily and economic and ecological aftermath that did not appear on the bill of sale. The buyer of the coal did not pay the unknown price. The seller, in the sense of the corporate entity holding the lease, did not pay it either, because the corporate entity was permitted by law to dissolve before the bill came due. The price was paid. It is being paid. It will be paid for at least one further generation, possibly two. It was simply paid by a different party than the one that captured the value of the transaction.

This is what I mean by a severed cost. I do not mean a hidden cost, exactly, because the cost was not hidden, in most cases. Bailey knew about it. Latusek knew about it. The men's wives knew about it. Their fathers had known about it. There is a substantial regional literature on the subject, going back at least to Harry Caudill's *Night Comes to the Cumberlands*, in 1962, and reaching forward through Ronald Eller's *Uneven Ground*, Steven Stoll's *Ramp Hollow*, Elizabeth Catte's *What You Are Getting Wrong About Appalachia*, and Chris Hamby's *Soul Full of Coal Dust*, each of which takes a different cross-section of the same anatomy. The cost was known. The cost simply did not bind on the price. The price could rise or fall with the steel market and the seaborne coal market and the natural-gas substitution market, and the cost, separately, would be paid by whoever it landed on, with no mechanism in place to bring the two columns of the ledger back into the same accounting.

That is the part of the story I want to walk through, carefully, because it is structural rather than moral and structural arguments are easy to lose. The moral version of this story has been told. It has been told by Caudill and Catte and Stoll. It has been told by the reformers inside the United Mine Workers of America, by the widows of Joseph Yablonski and Margaret Yablonski and Charlotte Yablonski (whose father was murdered in his bed at the end of December 1969 because he had run as a reform candidate for the union's presidency against Tony Boyle), by the journalists at the Center for Public Integrity who pursued Dr. Paul S. Wheeler's reading of nine hundred chest X-rays. I am not going to retell that version. The moral version has been received by readers who already grant it, and dismissed by readers who do not. The structural version has not been received as widely as the moral version, because the structural version is harder to dramatize. But it is the version, I think, that has to be made portable. Otherwise the next century looks exactly like the last one.

So let me try to make it portable. The arithmetic, first. Then the place. Then the architecture that was missing.

The arithmetic is this. If you set out to estimate what a ton of McDowell County coal actually cost, across the century of extraction, on an honest-accounting basis, you have to add up at least six categories. You have to count what the operator paid (wages, equipment, royalties, transport, federal and state taxes), which is the only category that historically made it onto the price. You have to count what the workers paid in foregone life expectancy, in the difference between a sixty-five-year life that ends on hospice with two transplanted lungs and the life that man would otherwise have had. You have to count what the watershed paid, which is the cost of cleaning, over a multi-decade horizon, the acid mine drainage that the disturbed sulfur-bearing rock will continue to produce. You have to count what the land paid, which is the reclamation cost net of the bonded amount, including the 633,000 acres I have already named. You have to count what the receiving community paid, which is the difference between the McDowell County of 1950, with a hundred thousand people, full schools, a daily newspaper in Welch, a Romanesque stone courthouse in the county seat, baseball teams and civic clubs and hospitals, and the McDowell County of 2020, with under twenty thousand people, a thirty-seven and a half percent poverty rate, a median household income of twenty-eight thousand dollars, and the bottom rank, fifty-fifth of fifty-five, in West Virginia's county health-outcomes index. You have to count what the receiving country paid in second-cycle costs: the federal Black Lung Disability Trust Fund's forty-four billion dollars in cumulative payments, its 4.6 billion in debt, its forty-five consecutive years of annual borrowing from the general fund, its projected fifteen billion by 2050; the public-health costs of the resurgence of black lung in younger miners, in a generation whose dust regulation was supposed to have ended this disease; the public-health costs of the opioid second cycle that landed, in the late nineteen-nineties and through the next two decades, on counties whose coal economy had collapsed in the previous decade, on bodies already broken by the work, on families already grieving the husbands and fathers and brothers the work had killed before the pills arrived.

If you add up those categories, with the discount rates and the inflation adjustments and the contested per-life and per-watershed and per-acre numbers each category requires, you arrive at a range. The range I have arrived at, on a conservative reading, lands at approximately thirty-three times the market price. The full range, given how much is genuinely uncertain about the long-tail valuation, is between thirty-three and a hundred and twenty-two times. The range is wide. The floor is the part nobody serious disputes. It is the part I want to keep my hand on: thirty-three times. Not three times. Not seven times. An order of magnitude, and then some.

That is what the country paid for a ton of McDowell County coal. The country paid it because the country, when the coal was bought and burned and exported, paid only the part of the cost that the market price contained. The rest of the cost was paid in installments. It is still being paid.

The thing I want to keep emphasizing is that this is not a complaint about the existence of cost. Every economic activity has costs. The complaint is about who pays them. In the case of McDowell County, the buyer of the coal paid a portion. The seller, in the sense of the operator at the time of extraction, paid a portion. And then the operator, or its corporate successor, or some shell entity legally distinct from the corporate successor, declared bankruptcy and walked away from the rest, and the rest was paid by the men who had worked underground, by their wives and their children, by the watershed, by the county, by the country in the form of the Trust Fund and the medical-care infrastructure and the surplus-commodity distribution, and then again, decades later, by the same county and the same country, in the form of the opioid second cycle and the federal litigation and the settlements that recovered some fraction of that cycle's cost from a different set of corporate balance sheets that, in turn, also went into bankruptcy.

The mechanism that should have made the buyer of the coal pay the full price was never built.

There is a word for what should have been built, and I have used it already, but I want to use it as carefully as I can: a severed cost is a real cost that has been cut loose from the transaction that produced it. The severing is not always intentional. Sometimes the severing happens because the cost is genuinely difficult to measure (the cost to the watershed of a hundred years of acid mine drainage is hard to quantify even with good models). Sometimes the severing happens because the cost is genuinely temporally remote (Bailey's transplant in his sixties, for coal that was mined in his thirties and forties, is hard to bring back into the price of a 1985 ton of coal). Sometimes the severing happens because the corporate entity that ought to be the natural payer no longer exists by the time the cost matures (Patriot Coal had dissolved by the time Bailey's transplant was needed). But the severing also happens because the architecture that would have prevented it was never built, or was built and not enforced, or was enforced and undermined, or was put in place too late to be retroactive. It is the absence of that architecture that I want to look at next, because the absence is the whole point. Without the architecture, the severance is the system's working assumption. With the architecture, the severance becomes the exception rather than the rule.

I will name some pieces of the architecture by way of saying what was missing. None of these is a fix in itself. I am not interested, in this essay, in advocating for one or another reform. I am interested in describing what an honest-accounting framework would have demanded, so the reader can see what was not done.

The federal Black Lung Benefits Program, established under the Federal Coal Mine Health and Safety Act of 1969 and signed by President Nixon at the end of that year, was a partial piece of the architecture. It set up an excise tax on coal extraction, codified at Internal Revenue Code section 4121, the proceeds of which were supposed to fund the Trust Fund. The Trust Fund was supposed to pay, on a no-fault basis, for the medical care of miners disabled by pneumoconiosis. In its forty-five years of operation, the Trust Fund has paid out forty-four billion dollars and is in debt to the federal general fund by 4.6 billion. It has been in deficit every year since 1979. Projections have its debt at fifteen billion by 2050. The Trust Fund is a partial piece of the architecture not because of any moral failure on the part of the people running it. It is a partial piece of the architecture because the excise tax is too low to fund the benefits the disease actually generates, because the eligibility process is too adversarial to capture the affected population, because companies like Patriot Coal can declare bankruptcy and transfer their obligations to the Trust Fund without consequence, because the disease has been getting worse rather than better in the last two decades and the funding has not adjusted, and because nothing in the law requires the price of coal, today, to reflect the long-tail cost of the lungs that mining today will destroy.

The Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977 was another partial piece. The act required mining companies to post a reclamation bond before extraction, with the bond functioning as a financial commitment to restore the land after extraction was done. The theory was clean: whoever creates the environmental liability funds the cleanup, with the bond held in escrow against the liability's eventual maturation. In practice, the bonded amount, across the Appalachian coal country, has been roughly four billion dollars short of what reclamation will actually cost. Between 2014 and 2016, three major coal companies declared bankruptcy and transferred 865 million dollars of environmental liability directly to taxpayers. The bond was meant to make the bankruptcy non-load-bearing on the cleanup. The bond did not, in practice, make the bankruptcy non-load-bearing.

I am writing all of this out because the architecture's incompleteness is not, when you look at the documentary record, a matter of incompetence or of corruption (though there has been both). The incompleteness is what happens when you try to build cost-internalization architecture inside a legal and economic system whose default unit, the limited-liability corporation, is structurally designed to externalize cost across time and across the corporate veil. A bond can be set high enough to cover reclamation, but only if the regulator is willing to make extraction uneconomic at the bonded price, and political economy being what it is in single-commodity counties, the regulator is rarely willing. An excise tax on extraction can be set high enough to fund the Trust Fund's long-tail medical liabilities, but only if the country is willing to make coal more expensive at the wellhead, and the country, throughout the period when it could have made that choice, mostly wasn't. A liability regime can pierce the corporate veil and follow the assets back to the holding company and the principals and the financial intermediaries, but the law, as written, mostly doesn't.

So the architecture is partial. The cost is severed. The severance accumulates. The accumulation, over a century, lands somewhere, on someone.

In McDowell County's case, the accumulation has landed in the obituaries.

Let me go back to Bailey for a moment. I want to write down the family substrate so the reader knows what kind of accumulation I mean. Bailey's father, Robert W. Bailey, Sr., was living in Princeton at the time of his son's death; that is, the father survived the son. Bailey's mother, Ruth Irene Dills Bailey, had died before him. Bailey had grandchildren. The funeral was held at the Princeton Church of God on the Oakvale Road on the nineteenth of March, 2019; the entombment was at Roselawn Memorial Gardens. There is now a memorial 5K race held in Princeton in his name, and the National Black Lung Association meets at the Bluestone Health Clinic on the second Monday of every month, at ten in the morning, in part because of him. He was sixty-five years old when he died. The national male life expectancy in 2019 was seventy-six and a half. Bailey was eleven and a half years short of the national male average, which means his life, in the actuarial sense, was about fifteen percent shorter than it should have been. That fifteen percent was a real cost. Somebody paid it. It was not, by any meaningful measure, the buyer of the coal.

I have driven through McDowell County. The first time I drove through, I was on my way to somewhere else; I was passing through. The second time I drove through, I was paying attention. The county is what its history has left it. The Welch courthouse, the Romanesque stone building that the architect Frank Pierce Milburn finished in 1894, is still there. The schools that used to run two shifts to accommodate mining families on rotating schedules are mostly not. The two-thirds drop in personal income, which happened in a single year after U.S. Steel closed the Gary mine in the late nineteen-eighties, is something you can still feel in the storefronts and in the way the towns sit on the road. The creeks are orange in the places where the drainage has not been mitigated. The hollows have the kind of quietness that is not the quietness of a place that was always quiet. It is the quietness of a place that used to be loud.

I drove through, and I want to be careful here about what I claim to have seen, because the rule for an essay like this one is that observation is observation and only observation. The orange creeks are an observation; the orange creeks were there. The boarded-up storefronts in Welch are an observation; the boarded-up storefronts were there. The reduced traffic on the highways is an observation. The eight or nine houses, on a single hollow road, with for-sale signs in the yard, were an observation. The cemetery with the freshly-cut grass and the relatively young dates on the stones was an observation. I do not, in this essay, want to imagine the inner lives of the people I did not meet. I want to say what I saw and let the reader infer what the reader is going to infer.

What I will write down with more confidence is what I have spent twenty years doing for a living, which is not directly relevant to McDowell County but is, I have come to think, indirectly relevant in a way I did not see when I started. I have spent twenty years inside institutions that managed costs by passing them to others. I came up in finance and operations roles that involve, among other things, the design of severance arrangements (the language is its own confession) and the structuring of liabilities so that they fall, on a probability-weighted basis, somewhere other than the institution's own balance sheet. I have done this professionally, competently, in some cases with people I respected and respect. I am not writing this as a confession; the work was, on its own terms, legal, conventional, and, on a per-transaction basis, defensible. What I want to say is that twenty years of doing that work, and watching other people do that work, gives a person a strong working knowledge of how cost migrates inside a system whose default unit is the limited-liability corporation. It migrates downward, outward, and forward. It migrates toward the parties with the least bargaining power, the least documentation, the longest delay between the action and the harm, and the most plausible alternative explanation for the harm when the harm appears. I learned how the migration works by watching the migration work. I did not, for most of those twenty years, see McDowell County in those terms. I saw McDowell County, when I saw it at all, as a sad story about coal. It took standing in the place, looking at the creeks and the courthouse and the for-sale signs, to put the words *systematic migration of cost* and *McDowell County* in the same sentence.

The point I am trying to make is that the migration is structural. It is not exotic. It is not the particular evil of the coal industry. It is the way the system, as currently constituted, processes long-tail liability. The coal industry is one of the clearest cases of it because the long tail is unambiguously long (lungs take decades to fail) and the corporate veil is unambiguously porous (Patriot Coal dissolved on schedule) and the cost is unambiguously real (Bailey is dead, and Latusek is dead, and the cemetery is full of stones). But the same migration operates in pharmaceuticals, in financial services, in heavy industry, in extractive industries that are not coal, and in the categories of harm we have not yet learned to put on a balance sheet, such as data harms and climate harms. The coal case is not exceptional. The coal case is legible.

It is legible because Kennedy went to look.

I want to write down what Kennedy saw and what he said about it, because the documentary record is unusually clear and because the documentary record is what an honest-accounting framework would have responded to but did not.

In May of 1960, John F. Kennedy drove through McDowell County during the West Virginia Democratic primary. The primary was a test of whether a Catholic candidate could win in an overwhelmingly Protestant state; Hubert Humphrey was the Protestant opponent. Kennedy's campaign had to demonstrate that the religion question would not be disqualifying in a Protestant electorate. McDowell County was on the route. Kennedy drove through. The documentary record of what he saw, by his own account in the period that followed, includes children with distended bellies; families lined up in the hollows for surplus government commodities (flour, powdered milk, blocks of yellow cheese) distributed because there was no other food; and thousands of mineworkers, with twenty and thirty years of underground tenure, sitting idle in their porches and yards, because mechanization had moved through the industry and reduced the need for their hands even though the mines themselves were still producing coal.

Four months later, in September, Kennedy stood in Canton, Ohio, in front of an audience that was not primarily Appalachian, and used a formulation that I want to put on the page exactly as he used it. "McDowell County mines more coal than it ever has in its history," he said, "and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than any county in the United States." I do not think you can do better than that, as a one-sentence formulation of severed cost. The coal is being produced. The county is producing it. The county is going hungry. The producing of the coal and the going hungry are not opposites; they are simultaneous; the simultaneity is the part Kennedy was trying to surface.

What is striking about the formulation, from a sixty-five-year remove, is that it did not produce a corrective architecture. There were responses to McDowell County, in 1960 and 1961, that were not nothing. The first food stamps under the modern federal program were issued in McDowell County in May of 1961, and the food-stamp program is one of the things Kennedy is remembered for. But the food-stamp program is not architecture for the cost-severance problem. The food-stamp program is a downstream subsidy on the cost-severance problem. It picks up part of the price of the severance without doing anything to bring the cost back into the transaction that produced it. The downstream subsidy is necessary as long as the upstream architecture is missing, and the downstream subsidy is corrosive precisely because it is necessary; it preserves the buyer of the coal in a state of believing the coal was cheap. The food-stamp program made it possible for McDowell County to keep being a coal county. It did not make it possible for the price of the coal to reflect the cost of being a coal county.

There were other responses, in the years that followed. The Farmington Mine disaster on the twentieth of November, 1968, in Consolidation Coal's Number Nine mine near Farmington in Marion County, killed seventy-eight miners and was the proximate catalyst for the Coal Mine Health and Safety Act of 1969. The West Virginia Black Lung Association's wildcat strike of February and March 1969, which mobilized roughly forty thousand miners across the state and which ended on the eleventh of March when Governor Arch Moore signed the West Virginia pneumoconiosis statute, was the proximate catalyst for the federal Trust Fund. The murders of Joseph Yablonski, his wife Margaret, and his daughter Charlotte, in the family's home in Clarksville, Pennsylvania, on the night of December 30 and 31, 1969, set in motion the Miners for Democracy reform movement inside the United Mine Workers of America that, in the years that followed, took control of the union. Tony Boyle, who had ordered the murders, was convicted of first-degree murder in 1974. Brit Hume's *Death and the Mines*, in 1971, is the book-length account of how that fight ran.

Each of those was an attempt at architecture. Each of them was partial. The Trust Fund is partial because the excise tax is too low; the strike's gains were partial because the workplace dust regulations have been undermined; the union's reform was partial because the industry consolidated above the union's level into corporate forms (holding companies, asset-stripped operating subsidiaries, shell entities organized to fail) that the union could no longer effectively bargain against. Bailey's lungs failed in 2014; Bailey's transplant was paid for by the Trust Fund in the years that followed; Bailey died in 2019, fifty years after the Coal Mine Health and Safety Act was signed. Latusek's nineteen-year fight against Consol Energy was happening inside a regulatory framework that the 1969 Act had created. The architecture was there. The architecture was not strong enough to hold against the cost.

I want to insist, as I make this point, that the partiality is not a regional failure. It is not even an industry failure. It is what happens when a country tries to internalize cost without fully redesigning the legal and corporate forms that produced the externalization in the first place. The Trust Fund is, in its design, a federal-government balance-sheet absorption of liability that should have stayed on the operator's balance sheet. The reclamation bond is, in its design, an attempted ex-ante absorption of liability that the operator can still escape through bankruptcy. The black lung benefits process is, in its design, an adversarial claim-by-claim adjudication that requires each disabled miner to prove, individually, that the disease that disabled him was caused by the work that disabled him, against medical-record readers paid by employers to read the X-rays as negative. The architecture was put in place. The architecture's design was, throughout, the design of a system that does not really want to internalize the cost. It wants to look like a system that has internalized the cost. The looking-like-and-being are different, and the difference accumulates, and the accumulation is paid by the parties with the least bargaining power.

The thing that has been hardest for me to write down, in the years I have been thinking about McDowell County, is that the cost-severance pattern did not stop when the coal ran out. The coal economy in McDowell County had structurally collapsed by the late nineteen-eighties; U.S. Steel's Gary mine closure took 1,200 jobs in a single event and dropped county personal income by two-thirds in a single year. (Two-thirds of total income, not two-thirds of growth.) That was the bottom of the first cycle. The first cycle had taken roughly a century. The second cycle began, almost immediately, in the same county and in the counties around it, and the second cycle ran through the human nervous system instead of through the human lungs.

Purdue Pharma's marketing strategy for OxyContin in the late nineteen-nineties, as documented through state and federal litigation, targeted counties with four specific characteristics: high rates of physical labor injury, limited healthcare infrastructure, concentrations of high-prescribing physicians, and economic precarity. When you take a map of the United States and shade the counties that fit those four characteristics, the map shades, county by county, almost exactly the communities whose coal economy had just collapsed. The shading was not coincidence; the shading was a marketing plan. The marketing plan was developed and executed by people, in offices, with budgets and projections and quarterly targets. It was developed, in other words, with the same instruments by which any corporate sales operation develops a plan. Patrick Radden Keefe's *Empire of Pain*, in 2021, walks through the family's role in the documentary record.

The result of the second cycle, in McDowell County and in the counties that share its history, was a drug death rate that has reached roughly ten times the national rate. The first cycle damaged the lungs and the watershed; the second cycle damaged the bodies and the families that had been left behind by the first cycle. The first cycle's price did not include the second cycle. The second cycle's price did not include the first one. The total cost of the two cycles, taken together, is not on any company's balance sheet; it is on the bodies, the obituaries, and the federal balance sheet, in the form of the Trust Fund's debt, the food-stamp program's allocations, the opioid settlement disbursements (Purdue's bankruptcy estate; the Big Three pharmaceutical distributors' twenty-one billion dollar settlement in 2022 against McKesson, Cardinal Health, and AmerisourceBergen for shipping volumes that no legitimate medical demand could explain), and the medical-care infrastructure that absorbs the bodies after their corporate paymasters have dissolved.

The Sackler family, before Purdue's bankruptcy, extracted approximately eleven billion dollars from the company. That extraction is in the documentary record because the family's own litigation discovery put it there. The bankruptcy plan that followed, and the settlements that followed the bankruptcy plan, returned some portion of that extraction to public-health and victim-compensation funds. The portion returned is, by any honest accounting, considerably less than the cost the family's company produced; even framing the costs and the extraction as a single comparison concedes a level of legibility that the documentary record does not, in all cases, support. But the basic structure is the structure I have been writing about. Value extracted; cost severed; cost paid by parties other than the extractors; partial recovery, decades later, through downstream litigation and downstream federal balance-sheet absorption. The architecture for ex-ante prevention was not in place. The architecture for ex-post recovery captured a fraction of what had been extracted. The recovery is necessary because the prevention is missing; the recovery is corrosive because it allows the system to keep believing that it has, in some adequate sense, internalized the cost.

There is a vocabulary for what I have been describing that has been developed inside a particular intellectual tradition over the last quarter-century. I want to point at the vocabulary briefly, not because the essay needs the academic register, but because the reader who finds these arguments familiar from other contexts deserves to know what I am drawing on. Mariana Mazzucato, in *The Value of Everything* in 2018, drew the distinction between value creation and value extraction inside the apparatus of financial capitalism. Her argument is that a great deal of what is conventionally measured as value creation, in modern economies, is in fact value extraction: rent-collection on assets created elsewhere; capture of returns disproportionate to contribution; structuring of incentives so that the recorded value of a transaction accrues to a party other than the one that produced the underlying productivity. David Harvey, in *The New Imperialism* in 2003, developed the term *accumulation by dispossession* to name the contemporary form of what Marx called primitive accumulation: capital accumulating not through the productive activity of capitalists but through the enclosure of common resources, the privatization of public assets, and the systematic conversion of common-pool resources into private balance sheets. The terms are useful but partial. They name the pattern at the level of finance and at the level of macro-historical political economy, respectively, but the McDowell County case demands a finer-grained vocabulary, because the cost in McDowell County is not abstract financial capture and not abstract enclosure; it is Bailey's lungs and Latusek's lungs and the orange creek and the post-extraction county. The vocabulary I have been working with — severed cost, cost severance, the architecture of cost-internalization — is meant to operate at the granularity that an honest-accounting framework would require, which is the granularity of the actual harm and the actual harmed.

I have already used the phrase honest accounting more than once. I want to be specific about what I mean by it, because the phrase is doing the most important work in the essay. By honest accounting I do not mean a particular set of accounting standards, though revisions to accounting standards would be one of the architectural pieces a serious approach would have to develop. I mean a framework, holding across the legal, regulatory, financial, and corporate domains, in which the price of a transaction reflects the full cost of producing the transaction, with appropriate accounting for temporal remoteness, distributional impact, and uncertainty. Honest accounting requires that the buyer of the coal pay, at the wellhead, a price that reflects the lifetime cost of the lungs that mining today will destroy. Honest accounting requires that the bond posted at the start of extraction reflect the cleanup cost of the watershed the extraction will damage, with the bond held in escrow against a corporate-form failure of the operator. Honest accounting requires that the corporate veil be permeable, in cases of long-tail liability, to the parent company and the holding company and the financial intermediaries and the principals, so that the failure of the operating subsidiary is not the end of the responsibility. Honest accounting requires, in the more difficult cases, a public reckoning with the long-tail costs that no single private party can be held responsible for, and a willingness to fund those costs from the present price of the activity that produces them, not from the future general fund of a country whose legislators have not yet been elected.

None of that architecture is in place. Some pieces of it are partially in place. Other pieces are present in the law but underfunded or underenforced. Other pieces have been proposed and not enacted. The honest-accounting framework, taken as a whole, is closer to a sketch than to an installation. The McDowell County case is what you get when the sketch is not installed.

The argument I am making, finally, is not that McDowell County is the worst possible case. The argument is that McDowell County is the legible case. The lungs are countable, the deaths are dated, the courthouse is photographable, the creek is its own color. You can stand on the bridge in Welch and see the river. You can drive the road and see the for-sale signs. You can read Bailey's testimony and Latusek's testimony and Kennedy's speech and Caudill's and Stoll's and Catte's and Hamby's books and assemble the picture without having to imagine anything that is not on the page already. The honest-accounting framework's absence, in this case, is documented. The cost is documented. The corporate dissolutions are docketed; the bankruptcies are filed; the obituaries are published; the cemetery is photographable; the orange creek is photographable; the empty schools are photographable. The case is legible because it has been forced into legibility by a century of people who refused to let it disappear.

What is not legible is everywhere else.

I mean this in two directions. In one direction, the McDowell County case is replicated in coal counties across the Appalachian region (Catte's *What You Are Getting Wrong About Appalachia* is, in part, an argument that Appalachia is not, regionally, what it has been characterized as, that the region's conditions are the structural consequences of an extraction history that the rest of the country has not yet fully reckoned with, not the moral consequences of a regional character). In the other direction, the McDowell County case is replicated in any setting where long-tail cost is being severed from current transaction. The setting changes; the structure does not. Tar sands. Petrochemical-corridor communities. Refinery towns. Public-pension obligations of municipalities whose tax base has been transferred to elsewhere through accumulated decisions that no individual decisionmaker was responsible for. Climate harms that are accumulating on bodies and watersheds and coastlines whose connection to any specific past transaction is so attenuated that the legal system does not, at present, know how to hold any particular party responsible for them. Data harms. Algorithmic harms. The list will lengthen, in the years ahead, in proportion to the country's willingness to put more of the cost of its modern economic activity on parties other than the ones capturing the value. The legibility of McDowell County is, in this sense, a gift. The case is documented enough to be argued from. Most cases will not be. Most cases will be diffuse, attenuated, distributed across categories of harm that the country has not yet learned to name. The McDowell County case is the case where the country can still see, with reasonable clarity, what the structure looks like. It is the place to do the seeing.

Bailey, on the radio with Lilly, said: "It's almost like drowning."

Latusek, in the documentary record Hamby assembled at the Center for Public Integrity, said: "A lot of times, you couldn't see your hand in front of your face. It felt like somebody had put a bag over my face." He said: "My goal in life was to outlive Consol. I thought that I had. And then it goes to the Fourth Circuit Court, and they take it all away from me." He said: "I was loyal to the company, but the loyalty wasn't there for me. I felt like somebody just put a knife in my gut. I told my wife: 'I can take the lung disease because a lot of that's my problem. I went in that mine and ate that dust and knew better. I should have known better, but I thought I was invincible. But I always thought Consol would take care of me.'"

I want to sit, for a paragraph, with what Latusek says about loyalty. He says he was loyal to the company. He says he thought, the whole time he was loyal, that the company would in some adequate sense be loyal back to him. He found out, in the end, that the loyalty was an asymmetry. The loyalty he gave was a personal loyalty by a person to a company. The loyalty he expected back was, structurally, impossible: the company was not a person; the company had no inner life that could be loyal; the company was a legal form whose obligations were defined by statute and contract and bankruptcy law, and the legal form was, as written, perfectly capable of dissolving its obligations when those obligations became inconvenient. Latusek's sense of betrayal is a moral sense applied to a structure that has no moral capacity. It is not that the company chose to be disloyal. It is that the company, as a corporate form, has no capacity for the loyalty Latusek had assumed. He worked thirty or forty years before that asymmetry became visible to him. It became visible only when it cost him.

Bailey's grief, as Lilly's interview captures it, is differently inflected. He is not bitter about Patriot Coal, exactly, or about the Trust Fund process, or even about Wheeler's reading of his X-ray. He is bitter, if at all, only about the years; the years that the disease took; the years of his grandchildren's lives he would have missed if the transplant had not given him an extension. He says, of his grandchildren: *they're just growing up and the next thing you know they're gonna be grown like I am.* That is the sentence. The sentence does not require commentary from me, but I will say what I think it is doing, because the essay is in part about what to do with the sentences the men left us. The sentence is making a measurement. The measurement is: a man's relationship to his grandchildren has a duration, and the duration was shortened, and the shortening was not the man's fault, and the shortening was not nobody's fault, and the public record will, if we are honest, work out whose fault it was. The sentence is not a complaint; it is a statement of what is being measured. The country's accounting framework declined to make that measurement at the time the coal was burned. The country's accounting framework is still declining to make that measurement on the coal being burned today.

Bailey's other sentence, the one about the laws being written in blood, is doing different work. He says: *Most laws across the nation, most laws have been written with blood. I don't want the things I went through just to drop and no knowledge to be gained, no people to be helped.* He is making a hopeful statement, despite the medical context. He is saying that the laws that protect workers, the laws that established the Trust Fund, the laws that mandated reclamation bonds and the laws that piece together what little architecture there is, were written in response to specific deaths. He is saying his own death, and his testimony, and the lungs that lasted only sixty-five years instead of seventy-six, should also be the kind of testimony that gets written into law. He is saying he is not willing for his life to be a useless data point. He is putting the data point on the table and asking the country to do something with it.

This is the question I want to leave with the reader: what would it look like, sixty-five years after Kennedy stood in Canton, Ohio, for the country to do something with the data point Bailey put on the table?

I do not have the architecture sketched. I am suspicious, on principle, of essays that close on architecture they have not also tested. But I can describe the conditions any serious architecture would have to satisfy, so that the reader, going back to the documentary record and to the lives that produced it, can see what is missing.

The first condition is that the price of extraction, at the wellhead, has to reflect the lifetime cost of the extraction. That means the excise tax that funds the Trust Fund has to be set at a level that pays for the lungs the current mining will destroy, with appropriate adjustment for the disease's resurgence and for the difficulty of the disability-claims process. It means the reclamation bond has to be set at a level that pays for the cleanup the current mining will produce, with the bond held in an escrow vehicle that cannot be dissolved by the operator's bankruptcy. It means the price of the coal has to rise to reflect the additional excise and the additional bonding, and the rise of the price will reduce extraction at the margin, and the reduction of extraction at the margin will reduce the future cost. This is not a moral claim about coal. It is an arithmetic claim about what an honest price would look like.

The second condition is that the corporate veil has to be permeable, in cases of long-tail liability, to the parent company and the holding company and the financial intermediaries and the principals who structured the company to fail. Patriot Coal's bankruptcy should not have been the end of Patriot Coal's responsibility for Bailey's transplant. The dissolution should have been the trigger for a documented transfer of the liability up the chain, to the entities that had arranged for the operating subsidiary to be the legally responsible party. Bankruptcy law has handled this kind of problem in other contexts (asbestos trusts, for example, were a partial recognition that operating-subsidiary dissolution should not, in cases of long-tail bodily harm, terminate the liability). It can be made to handle this kind of problem in the coal context too. The architecture exists in legal form for adjacent problems; it has not, in the coal case, been built.

The third condition is that the public balance sheet, in any case where the private architecture is insufficient or has failed, has to be funded by the present price of the activity that produces the cost, not by the future general fund of a country whose taxpayers had no role in setting the price. This is the part that the food-stamp program and the Trust Fund's accumulated debt and the Big Three opioid settlement do not, in the aggregate, satisfy. They are downstream absorptions. They are corrective downstream of an upstream architecture that should have prevented the cost from being severed in the first place. The downstream absorptions are necessary, given that the upstream architecture is missing, but they are not the architecture. The architecture is upstream.

The fourth condition is the one that is hardest to write down, because it is the one that the country has the least vocabulary for. The fourth condition is that places like McDowell County, after a century of supplying a commodity at a price that did not reflect the full cost, are owed a reckoning. Not in the form of an apology and not in the form of a charitable initiative; in the form of the architecture being installed, retroactively where possible and prospectively going forward, so that what was paid by the place can be in some accounting credited back. I will not pretend I know what that looks like. Catte's argument, that the conditions are structural, is the place to start; the regional self-determination conversations inside Appalachia (which I am not, as an outsider, equipped to summarize but which the reader can find in Catte's book and in the regional press) are the place to listen. The point is that the architecture is owed not only to the bodies and the watersheds but to the place. The place was the bargaining counter-party to the country's extraction. The place paid the bill. The accounting framework should know how to credit the place back. The accounting framework, at present, does not.

I am aware that those four conditions are large. I am aware that they require legal, economic, regulatory, and political-economic redesign that is not in any reform package on offer at the federal level today. I am not claiming the redesign is easy. I am claiming the redesign is necessary, if the country is going to honor the data point Bailey put on the table and the data point Latusek put on the table and the data point that Kennedy, in Canton, Ohio, in September of 1960, put on the table when he named McDowell County in front of an audience that mostly did not, at the time, know what he was naming.

There is a passage in Wendell Berry, in *The Unsettling of America*, where he writes about the country's habit of treating its own places as expendable in the service of an aggregate economic abstraction. There is a passage in Marilynne Robinson where she writes about the country's habit of refusing to see the consequences of its own choices when the consequences land on people the country has decided are someone else's responsibility. I am not going to quote either of them, because the registers of their prose are not mine and I do not want to borrow against credit I have not earned in the writing. But I will say that Berry's and Robinson's questions, which are at root questions about whether the country is willing to do the accounting honestly, are the questions I think McDowell County is asking the country, and have been asking the country, for a century or more.

The county does not have a single voice. Phrasing it as if it does is the kind of move I have been trying to avoid. What the county has is roughly twenty thousand current residents, the descendants of roughly eighty thousand more who have left, a courthouse, a set of schools, a set of churches, a set of cemeteries, a set of orange creeks, and an archive of obituaries that runs longer than any single life can read. Each of those is its own piece of evidence. Each of them deserves to be heard separately. The county is not, in this essay, asked to speak as one. The county is asked, by the country, to keep paying. The essay is, finally, an argument for the country to stop asking that of the county. The mechanism by which the country can stop asking, on my reading of the documentary record and on the evidence of the lives I have been able to follow, is the architecture of honest accounting. There are other mechanisms. The architecture is the one I can describe.

Bailey died on the fifteenth of March, 2019. The country owed him, by then, sixty-five years of life that had been at least eleven and a half years too short. The country owed him a transplant that had to be paid for, in the end, by the Trust Fund, because the company that had owed him the transplant had dissolved on schedule. The country owed him four years of administrative fighting, because the disease he had had to be acknowledged through an adversarial process that pretended, against substantial medical evidence, that his X-ray was negative. The country owed him fifty years of work, and the lungs that work had cost. The country owed him the testimony he gave to the Senate, in 2014, and the testimony he gave to Lilly, in 2019, twenty-eight days before he died. The country owed him the public record his five-word definition of black lung had earned.

The country can pay Bailey, now, in only one way. It can install the architecture his life would have required. The architecture would not bring him back. The architecture would mean the next Bailey, in the next coal seam, in the next extractive cycle, in the next category of long-tail harm we have not yet learned to name, has a different ending. That is the only payment the country can still make.

The man wanted his life to be useful. The country, if it is going to accept the gift, has to do something with it.

I do not write essays about places I have not been to and people I have not read carefully. I have been to McDowell County. I have read Bailey's testimony, and Latusek's, and the books I have named, and the obituaries and the regional press and the litigation discovery and the Center for Public Integrity's series, and the speeches Kennedy gave, and the records of the strikes, and the documented testimony at the Senate hearing in July of 2014, and the records of the bankruptcies of Patriot Coal and the three coal companies between 2014 and 2016 and Purdue Pharma and the Big Three pharmaceutical distributors' settlement. I have read enough of the documentary record to know that the documentary record is not the problem. The documentary record is rich. The problem is that the documentary record has not been honored in the form of architecture. The documentary record has been honored in the form of essays, books, journalism, and partial reforms. None of those, taken together, has been honored architecture.

I am writing this essay because I believe the architecture is still possible, if the country can see what is owed.

What is owed is roughly thirty-three times what was paid, on a conservative reading, against a hundred years of metallurgical coal whose buyers got a discount because the seller, in the structural sense, was not the corporate entity at the lease but the bodies and the watershed and the county. The discount has been paid. The discount is being paid. The discount will be paid by the bodies and the watershed and the county and the country for at least one more generation, probably two, unless somebody installs the architecture.

That is the arithmetic.

That is the place.

That is what the country owes.

## §Z. Structural decisions

1. **Open at Bailey, with the five-word definition.** The substrate's most singular line, in voice register, is Bailey's. Opening there is scene-led and earns the essay's length immediately. Kennedy enters later, where the structural argument can carry his weight; the arithmetic enters in the middle, where the place has already been established.

2. **First-person enters explicitly at the McDowell drive + at the twenty-year-career disclosure.** The AuthorsNote substantiates both. The first-person is bounded by what the substrate supports; the essay declines to render specific scenes from either domain. The first-person voice's authority comes from the disclosure + the observational register + the willingness to admit what is not being claimed.

3. **The arithmetic block sits in the middle, not the end.** Standard essay craft would close on the numerical anchor; the chip's choice here is to put the arithmetic in the middle and close on the four conditions + the Bailey-as-data-point payment frame. Rationale: the arithmetic is necessary but is not the climactic move; the climactic move is the question "what does it look like for the country to do something with the data point Bailey put on the table?"

4. **No section headers.** A Harper's literary essay in the Beha-era register typically does not partition by numbered sections. The essay moves by section-break (which here is essentially paragraph-break in a flowing argument) rather than by H2-headed sections. The cut version below reads as one piece of prose, modulated by paragraph rhythm.

5. **Apparatus exclusion held.** No equations, no Greek letters, no acronyms for framework concepts. The phrases *cost severance* + *severed cost* + *value extraction* (with Mazzucato attribution) + *accumulation by dispossession* (with Harvey attribution) appear as plain-prose concepts only. The phrase *honest accounting* appears repeatedly; it is doing the spine work.

6. **Em-dash discipline.** I counted nine em-dashes in the final cut. Each survives because (a) it is doing genuine rhetorical work that a comma or period would lose (parenthetical extension with strong syntactic break; emphatic punch at a sentence-spine pivot; deliberate breath-mark in a clause whose comma-density is already high); (b) the cumulative count is under the 10-em-dash cap; (c) the chip-default was period-or-restructure-first.
