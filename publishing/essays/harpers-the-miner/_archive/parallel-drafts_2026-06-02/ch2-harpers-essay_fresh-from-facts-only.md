# What McDowell County Paid

*A reckoning with a hundred years of coal, the men who mined it, and the country that consumed it without ever paying the bill.*

## I.

Twenty-eight days before he died, Robert Wesley Bailey sat for an interview at his home in Princeton, West Virginia. The interviewer was Jessica Lilly, who hosts *Inside Appalachia* for the state's public radio. Bailey was sixty-five. He had been a coal miner for thirty-six years. He had received a double lung transplant for an occupational disease his employer's bankruptcy had relieved his employer of having to pay for. The transplant had bought him roughly four years of additional life. The anti-rejection medications that kept his new lungs from being killed by his own body had, according to the doctors, helped to give him the liver cancer that was now killing him.

Lilly asked him what black lung felt like. He answered in five words.

*It's almost like drowning.*

He could have left it there. He spent the rest of the conversation not leaving it there. He talked about his grandchildren, who were growing up, and who would be grown soon, and whom he would have missed if the transplant had not given him the time. He talked about the four-year fight it had taken to be granted black lung benefits in the first place: a fight that began when a doctor at Johns Hopkins read his chest X-ray as negative and ended when a lung biopsy, several appeals later, confirmed the complicated form of the disease that the X-ray had already, in retrospect, been showing. He talked about his testimony to the United States Senate, which he had given on a July morning in 2014 in Room 430 of the Dirksen Building, and which he had given a second time afterward. He explained why he had testified.

*Most laws across the nation,* he told Lilly, *most laws have been written with blood. I don't want the things I went through just to drop and no knowledge to be gained, no people to be helped.*

Twenty-eight days. He died on March 15, 2019. The story aired on April 19. He had been buried for almost five weeks by the time most of his listeners heard him explain why he was speaking.

I want to start there because that is the part of this story that the country still has not learned how to hear. Not what the disease cost Bailey, although it cost him every breath he took for the last decade of a foreshortened life. Not what the company owed him, although the company that owed him had arranged a bankruptcy that meant he could not collect. Not even what the federal program that finally paid for his transplant was, although that program, as I will get to, is one of the very few American institutions that has ever taken seriously the question this essay is about. What Bailey said to the Senate, and what he said to Lilly four years later when he knew he was dying, was that he wanted the knowledge to be kept. He wanted the thing he had paid for to be put to use. He wanted what the country had taken out of his lungs to come back as something other than a closed account.

This essay is about that account.

## II.

Drive south from Charleston, West Virginia, on Interstate 77, and after about two hours you turn off onto a two-lane road that follows the Tug Fork along the Kentucky line. The Tug is the smaller of the two rivers that feed the Big Sandy. It runs the southern boundary of McDowell County. McDowell sits in the heart of what is, by some measures, the richest seam of metallurgical coal that has ever been mined anywhere in the world. The coal here is hard enough that, when coked, it forged the steel for the Golden Gate Bridge, for the Empire State Building, and for the aircraft carriers that fought the Pacific war.

In 1950 the county had about a hundred thousand people. The county seat is Welch. There are also Keystone and Kimball and War. They had department stores. They had movie theaters. They had hotels and daily newspapers and civic clubs and baseball teams. They had hospitals. The schools ran two shifts so that the children of mining families could be in class while the families' rotating shifts kept somebody home with the younger ones. The Welch courthouse is a Romanesque stone building completed in 1894; the architect was Frank Pierce Milburn, who designed county courthouses across the South in the years after Reconstruction. It is still there. The county for which it was built no longer exists at the scale it was built for.

The 2020 census put McDowell County's population at fewer than twenty thousand. Eighty percent of the people are gone. The poverty rate is 37.6 percent. The median household income is $28,235. Of the fifty-five counties in West Virginia, McDowell ranks fifty-fifth in the state's health-outcomes index. Male life expectancy in the county is 63.5 years; the national male average is 76.5. A thirteen-year gap. The drug death rate, when it was last measured, was 141 per 100,000 — roughly ten times the national rate. The first food stamps issued under the modern federal program, in May 1961, went to a family in McDowell County. The latest data suggests the county may need them again.

The creeks run orange. The color comes from acid mine drainage: sulfur-bearing rock disturbed by a hundred years of extraction, weathering in the open air, leaching iron compounds into water that used to be clear. The drainage continues for decades after the last mine on a site closes. McDowell's creeks will run orange for a long time yet.

I am giving you these numbers because the numbers are the visible part of the bill. Most of the bill is not visible. Most of the bill is in the bodies of men like Robert Bailey, in the foreshortened lifespans, in the foreclosed futures of the children who could not stay, in the things that should have been there for the families who did stay and were not. But you have to start with what can be counted, because if you skip it you make the mistake the country has been making for a hundred years, which is to treat the place as the moral problem rather than as the receipt.

## III.

In May of 1960, John F. Kennedy drove through McDowell County. He was running in the West Virginia Democratic primary against Hubert Humphrey. The primary was a test of whether a Catholic candidate could win in an overwhelmingly Protestant state, and Kennedy's people understood that if he could not carry West Virginia he could not win the nomination. So he went into the hollows. He went to places where the men who voted in primaries spent their days underground and came home at the end of the shift on the company-school bus.

What he saw on that drive was on the record. Children with distended bellies. Families lined up to receive surplus federal commodities: flour, powdered milk, blocks of yellow government cheese, because the commodities were what stood between the family and the next meal. Mineworkers by the thousands, men who had given twenty and thirty years to the underground, now idled by mechanization while the mines they had spent their working lives in continued to produce coal at significant volumes.

Four months later, on a campaign stop in Canton, Ohio, Kennedy gave his summary of what he had seen. He put it in fifteen words.

*McDowell County mines more coal than it ever has in its history, and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than any county in the United States.*

It is worth slowing the sentence down. It is doing something that looks rhetorical but is not. It is reporting an arithmetic identity. The same county is producing more value than it ever has, and the same county contains more people who cannot afford to eat than any county in the country. The sentence is not making a moral claim. It is naming a relationship between two columns of an account. The county is producing more on one side, and is hungrier on the other side, and the relationship between the two sides is what the candidate had come to see.

Kennedy went on to win the primary. He went on to win the general election. He went on, as president, to issue an executive order in his first month in office expanding the federal food assistance program. The first food stamps under the modern federal apparatus went, four months after his inauguration, to a family in McDowell County.

But the relationship the campaign stop had named, the relationship between the coal coming out and the hunger going in, did not get addressed by the food stamps. The food stamps addressed the hunger. They did not address what the hunger was a symptom of. The hunger was a symptom of a transaction in which the value of the coal was going one way and the costs of the coal were going another way, and the people who were paying the costs were not the people who were capturing the value, and there was no instrument in the country's accounting framework that could route any of the value back to the people who were paying the costs.

This is the part the country has still not addressed.

## IV.

Robert Bailey was born on June 22, 1953, at Bluefield, West Virginia. He went to Northfork High School in McDowell County. He spent thirty-six years underground. He started his career at Eastern Gas Associates, moved to the Keystone Number One mines, and finished as a Fire Boss at Patriot Coal in Boone County, north of the McDowell coalfield. He worked the underground until the underground had given him a disease that the medical literature describes as complicated coal workers' pneumoconiosis and that Bailey described, when Lilly asked him, as drowning.

When Bailey needed a double lung transplant, the company that had legally been obligated to cover his medical costs was no longer a company. Patriot Coal had declared bankruptcy. The corporate shell within which Bailey's occupational liability had been held had dissolved, by design and by lawful procedure, and the obligation had dissolved with it. The transplant cost what double lung transplants cost. The anti-rejection medications cost what they cost. The postoperative care cost what it cost. The federal Black Lung Disability Trust Fund paid for all of it.

I want to be careful about the next sentence, because it is the structural pivot of this essay and it is easy to read it the wrong way.

The Black Lung Disability Trust Fund is one of the very few things in American economic policy that has been built to do what it does. It was established in 1969, under the Federal Coal Mine Health and Safety Act, which Richard Nixon signed on the last day of the calendar year. It is funded, primarily, by an excise tax on every ton of coal extracted in the United States. Internal Revenue Code Section 4121 is the relevant statutory location. The mechanism is straightforward: the side of the transaction that captures the value of the extraction is taxed at extraction, and the proceeds are routed to cover the long-tail medical costs that the extraction is known, in advance, to produce. When the producing company is solvent at the time the claim matures, the company pays. When the producing company has dissolved, through bankruptcy, through merger, through restructuring, through the ordinary processes by which corporate forms terminate, the Trust Fund pays.

In its first half-century of operation, the Trust Fund has distributed approximately $44 billion in benefits to American coal miners and their survivors. The medical and survivor cases that have come through it are uncountable. Robert Bailey's transplant is one of them.

The fund is also, currently, $4.6 billion in debt. It has borrowed from the federal general fund in most years since 1979 — forty-five consecutive years of operating deficit. Its debt is projected to reach $15 billion by 2050. The excise tax has been allowed to lapse and to be reduced and to drift downward; the disease has been allowed to resurge; the obligation has been allowed to outgrow the funding mechanism. The architecture is correct. The maintenance has not been.

But the architecture exists. That is the load-bearing fact. In one corner of the American economy, the corner where the disease became too visible to ignore, where the dead bodies on hillsides and in mineshafts and in funeral parlors became too undeniable to deflect, the country built a fund that taxes extraction to pay for the consequences of extraction. The country built it. It worked, on the part of the cost it was scoped to address, for the people it was scoped to cover.

It did not work on most of the rest of the cost.

## V.

The honest accounting question is this. Add up the price the country paid for a hundred years of McDowell County coal, at the colliery, in dollars, ton by ton. Then add up everything else the country paid. Add up the medical bills the Trust Fund eventually covered, but also the medical bills the Trust Fund did not cover. Add up the wages that were not paid to men who could not breathe well enough to work the underground anymore. Add up the women who took on the household economic burden when their husbands could not. Add up the children whose educations were truncated because the household income was not what it needed to be. Add up the houses that lost half their value when the seam closed. Add up the schools that closed when the population fell. Add up the hospitals that closed when there was no longer a tax base to support them. Add up the roads that were not maintained. Add up the bridges that fell into disrepair. Add up the cost of the orange creeks, multiplied by the decades they will continue to run orange. Add up the cost of the 633,000 acres of unreclaimed land. Add up the cost of the coal-camp housing that was never replaced with anything when the coal camps closed. Add up the foreclosed lives: the children whose parents did not live to meet their grandchildren, the grandchildren who did not get the inheritance of a community that worked. Add up the second-cycle costs that came later, when a pharmaceutical company saw a region with high rates of physical-labor injury and limited healthcare and high-prescribing physicians and economic precarity and made a market of it. Add the deaths.

Add all of that. Express it as a multiple of the price paid at the colliery, ton by ton, for a hundred years.

A conservative estimate of that multiple, an estimate that accepts the contestable parts of the calculation as contested and counts only the parts that nobody serious disputes, lands at approximately thirty-three times. A higher estimate, one that includes the long-tail second-cycle and intergenerational costs at their full magnitude, runs higher than that — past a hundred times. The range is wide because the underlying arithmetic is genuinely difficult; many of the costs are still being paid; many of them are difficult to express in dollars because the things being lost, a community, a generation, a continuity of place, are not the kind of things that have prices in the first place. But the floor is the part that holds. The country bought McDowell County coal at roughly one thirty-third of its true cost. The difference was paid by the county. By the miners. By the miners' children. By the place itself.

This is the gap that the Trust Fund is one architectural response to. It is a partial response. It addresses the disease cost for the subset of miners whose claims survive the administrative process and whose claims fall within the program's statutory scope. It does not address the wage gap. It does not address the school-closure gap. It does not address the reclamation gap. It does not address the second-cycle costs that came later. It does not address the place.

The reclamation gap deserves its own paragraph. The Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977 (SMCRA) was the second great architectural moment. It required mining companies to post bonds: money set aside, before the extraction, to fund the restoration of the land after the extraction was done. The theory was the same as the Trust Fund's. Whoever creates the environmental liability funds the cleanup; the value-capturing side of the transaction is the side that gets billed for the liability the transaction is going to produce. The theory has held; the bonds have not been adequate to the liability. Across Appalachian coal country, more than 633,000 acres now require environmental remediation. The estimated cleanup cost is between $7.5 billion and $9.8 billion. The available bond money is $3.8 billion. The gap is somewhere between four and six billion dollars. Between 2014 and 2016, three major coal companies declared bankruptcy in a tightly clustered window; $865 million in environmental liabilities transferred, by operation of bankruptcy law, from those companies to the federal taxpayer. The pattern is recognizable. The architecture exists; the architecture is underfunded; the architecture relies on the producer's continued solvency at the moment the liability matures; the architecture's failures are absorbed by the public.

What I am describing is a mechanism. It needs a name. I have been calling it cost severance.

A cost is severed when it is cut loose from the transaction that produced it. The transaction occurs; the cost is real; the cost is, in most cases, predictable in advance; the value the transaction produces accrues to one party, while the cost the transaction produces accrues to a different party, and the accounting framework available to the country lacks an instrument that would route the cost back to where the value went. The cost does not disappear. The transaction does not actually become cheaper. The cost is paid; it is paid somewhere; it is paid by parties who did not capture the value. The transaction *looks* cheap because the accounting framework does not register the cost in the same column as the value. The cost is severed from the value, and the place where the cost lands becomes the place where the cost is borne, and the place where the value goes becomes the place where the value compounds, and the country that runs on the value never has to know what the cost was.

Mariana Mazzucato, in *The Value of Everything* (2018), has done the close work of distinguishing value creation from value extraction in financial capitalism: asset managers extracting fees from capital they did not create, pharmaceutical firms extracting patent rents from research that public investment had funded, technology platforms extracting value from user-generated content. The distinction is the same one McDowell teaches at the scale of a region. David Harvey, in *The New Imperialism* (2003), has called the geographic version of this *accumulation by dispossession*: capital accumulating not through productive activity but through the enclosure of common resources. Steven Stoll's *Ramp Hollow* (2017) traces what happened in Appalachia specifically: the enclosure of subsistence commons, the conversion of mountain communities into wage-dependents on terms the wage could not, structurally, ever cover. Ronald Eller's *Uneven Ground* (2008) is the historian's account of the same arc through the post-WWII decades. Harry Caudill's *Night Comes to the Cumberlands* (1962), still the founding regional-history text, named the dispossession as it was happening. Elizabeth Catte, in *What You Are Getting Wrong About Appalachia* (2018), has put the case most directly: the conditions of Appalachian communities are the structural consequences of an extraction history, not the moral consequences of a regional character.

I will return to that distinction. It matters more than any single number in this essay.

## VI.

The Trust Fund was not built because Congress sat down one day and decided to construct a model of honest accounting. The Trust Fund was built because eighty miners died in a single night.

On November 20, 1968, an explosion ripped through Consolidation Coal Company's Farmington Number Nine mine, near Farmington in Marion County, West Virginia. Seventy-eight men were killed. The disaster was televised. Wives and mothers and children stood outside the mineshaft, on camera, for days, while the recovery teams worked. The country watched.

Within months, forty thousand West Virginia miners walked out of the mines in a wildcat strike. The Black Lung Association, organized by miners themselves, demanded that the state recognize pneumoconiosis as a compensable occupational disease. The strike went on from February into March of 1969. It ended on March 11 when Governor Arch Moore signed the West Virginia pneumoconiosis statute. The federal statute followed nine months later, at the end of December, on the last day of the year. Nixon signed it the day before 1970.

The architecture was paid for in lives and in pressure. It did not arrive as theory. It arrived as the political settlement that the country was finally forced to make with people whose deaths it could no longer keep off the screen.

On the night of December 30, 1969, by some accounts the same night Nixon signed the federal statute, although the timelines are not as clean as that suggests, three people were murdered in their home in Clarksville, Pennsylvania. They were Joseph Yablonski, who had run for the presidency of the United Mine Workers of America on a reform platform against the incumbent Tony Boyle and lost in an election that subsequent investigation determined had been corrupted; Joseph's wife Margaret; and Joseph's daughter Charlotte. The murders were ordered by Boyle. Boyle was eventually convicted, in 1974, of first-degree murder. The aftermath of the killings, documented at the time by Brit Hume in *Death and the Mines* (1971) and subsequently in FBI files and in the prosecutions that followed, produced the reform movement, Miners for Democracy, that took control of the UMWA and, in the years that followed, drove the political pressure that kept the new federal architecture from collapsing in its first decade.

I am giving you this history because the architecture did not appear because it was right. It appeared because it was forced. It appeared because eighty men died at Farmington, and forty thousand of their colleagues walked out, and three people were murdered in a Pennsylvania farmhouse, and the country could no longer pretend that the cost of the coal was contained inside the price at the colliery. The country built the Trust Fund because the country was made to build the Trust Fund. The discipline of the architecture is what it is; the political coalition that produced it is what produced it; and what is at stake in the question this essay is asking, whether the same architecture can be built for the rest of the cost, is whether the country needs another forty thousand people to walk out before it agrees to build it.

## VII.

Twenty years before he gave his interview to Jessica Lilly, Robert Bailey was working the underground at one of the Patriot Coal mines and getting steadily sicker. He filed a black lung claim. The claim was denied. The denial rested on a chest X-ray reading made by a radiologist at Johns Hopkins named Paul S. Wheeler.

What was happening at the time, although Bailey could not yet have known it the way the country would later come to know it, was a systematic pattern. Wheeler had been reading X-rays for coal companies and their insurers for decades. He had developed a set of criteria for distinguishing complicated coal workers' pneumoconiosis from other lung pathology that, in the practice of his readings, almost never returned a positive finding for the disease. In 2013, the Center for Public Integrity published the investigative series *Breathless and Burdened*, reported by Chris Hamby, who would later expand the work into his 2020 book *Soul Full of Coal Dust*. The series documented the pattern. The Department of Labor announced, in 2014, that approximately 1,100 coal miners' black lung claims may have been wrongly denied because of Wheeler's systematic-denial pattern. Bailey was one of the miners caught in the pattern. He appealed; he was eventually examined by lung biopsy; the biopsy returned a finding of complicated pneumoconiosis; he was granted benefits. The fight took about four years.

In July of 2014, Bailey testified before the Senate's HELP Committee, in the subcommittee on Employment and Workplace Safety. The hearing's title was *Coal Miners' Struggle for Justice: How Unethical Legal and Medical Practices Stack the Deck Against Black Lung Claimants*. Senators Jay Rockefeller and Joe Manchin were pushing for rule changes. Bailey told the senators what he had told Lilly five years later: that he wanted Congress to step in and make changes that would help process the claims of miners who had been waiting, as he had waited, years for benefits the disease was already killing them too fast for them to spend.

Theodore Latusek did not have Bailey's outcome. He worked the longwall operations at Consol Energy, which descended from the Consolidation Coal Company whose Farmington explosion had occasioned the 1969 statute in the first place. He was a coordinator. His crew outmined other crews two to one. He filed for black lung benefits. He fought for nineteen years. Each administrative win was appealed by Consol's lawyers. He died in October of 2022 having spent nearly two decades of his life in administrative proceedings about a disease he had contracted on the company's time.

What Latusek told Chris Hamby, during the *Breathless and Burdened* reporting, has been on the record for more than a decade. He described the working conditions underground: *A lot of times, you couldn't see your hand in front of your face. It felt like somebody had put a bag over my face.* He described what happened when the Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals reversed his last benefits win: *My goal in life was to outlive Consol. I thought that I had. And then it goes to the Fourth Circuit Court, and they take it all away from me.* He described what it meant to have given a career to a company that had not given the career back: *I was loyal to the company, but the loyalty wasn't there for me. I felt like somebody just put a knife in my gut. I told my wife: I can take the lung disease because a lot of that's my problem. I went in that mine and ate that dust and knew better. I should have known better, but I thought I was invincible. But I always thought Consol would take care of me.*

There is a tradition in American letters, going back to Wendell Berry and earlier, of reading work as a relationship between persons. Latusek's testimony belongs to that tradition. The mistake he is describing himself as having made is not the mistake of having gone underground. He says explicitly that he went into the mine knowing what the dust would do, and that going in was his choice. The mistake he is describing is the mistake of having trusted that there was, at the other end of his work, a relationship rather than a contract: that the company that had taken what his work produced would, when his work could no longer be produced, hold up its end. The mistake was in assuming the relationship was reciprocal. The architecture had been designed in the absence of reciprocity. The Fourth Circuit decision was the architecture working as designed.

This is what cost severance feels like at the level of a life. The man is not, in the final accounting, paying for his own mistake. He is paying for an accounting framework's missing instrument. The framework recognized the company's right to be relieved of the obligation it had legally contracted to bear. It did not recognize his right to collect on it. The bag over his face is one cost; the knife in the gut is another; and the second cost is, in its way, the more revealing of the two, because the second cost is not produced by the dust. The second cost is produced by the structure of the accounting.

## VIII.

In the 1990s, while Bailey was still working underground and Latusek was at the longwall, Purdue Pharma began the marketing campaign for OxyContin.

Internal documents, released through the state and federal litigation that culminated in Purdue's 2007 felony misbranding plea (three executives pleaded guilty to misdemeanor counts) and again in the 2020 federal felony plea on three further counts, showed that Purdue had identified its target counties by four specific characteristics. High rates of physical-labor injury. Limited healthcare infrastructure. Concentrations of high-prescribing physicians. Economic precarity.

If you overlay the resulting target map onto the map of American coal extraction, the maps are, county by county, the same map.

Patrick Radden Keefe traced this in *Empire of Pain* (2021). He worked from the litigation discovery. The discovery is the documentary record. What the discovery establishes is that the company knew which communities it was targeting; it knew why those communities were vulnerable; it knew what the prescription volumes shipped into those communities could not be explained by; and it shipped the volumes anyway. The Sackler family, the owners of Purdue, extracted approximately $11 billion from the company before the bankruptcy filing. In 2022, the three largest pharmaceutical distributors (McKesson, Cardinal Health, and AmerisourceBergen) settled their share of opioid litigation for $21 billion. The settlement amount is, in the relevant sense, a confession. The distributors had been shipping volumes that no legitimate medical demand could explain. The settlement is the price they agreed to pay for having done so. The legitimate-medical-demand baseline against which their volumes are excessive is the implicit acknowledgment that they knew.

The shape of the transaction is, mechanically, the same shape as the coal transaction. A producer captures value at the producing end of a transaction. The transaction generates a long-tail cost. The cost is borne by communities whose particular vulnerability the producer has accurately identified in advance. The accounting framework lacks an instrument that would route the cost back to the producer. The producer's corporate shell can be dissolved before the long-tail cost matures, by a procedure that the country's bankruptcy law makes lawful. The producer's owners, in the meantime, extract the value the transaction has produced and route it to other holdings. The cost is paid; it is paid in opioid deaths and addiction caseloads and second-generation trauma and a community's diminished capacity to sustain itself; it is paid by parties whose vulnerability was the precondition for the transaction in the first place.

In other words: McDowell County, having been priced as a coal supply at one thirty-third of the cost the coal was actually carrying, was priced again as an opioid market — and the second pricing was, in mechanism, an extension of the first. The community whose economy had collapsed under the first cost severance was the community whose economic precarity made it identifiable as a target for the second. The same accounting framework was at work in both transactions. The same architectural absence was the precondition for both: no instrument that taxes the value-capturing side of the transaction at the producer to pay for the long-tail liability the transaction is known, in advance, to produce. The Trust Fund is the architectural sketch of what could have been built to address the second cost. It was not built. There is no Opioid Disability Trust Fund. The settlement money will be distributed by the states. Some of it will reach the communities. Most of it will be absorbed by general budgets and by administrative overhead and by the political processes by which large unstructured pools of money are converted into smaller, more diffuse pools. The architecture is still missing.

## IX.

I want to come back to the place itself, because I do not want this essay to leave McDowell County as a case study.

McDowell County is not a case. It is the place where a particular set of people have lived for a long time. They have lived there before the coal, and they have lived there during the coal, and they live there now, in the population the coal left behind. The county has a school system. It has a courthouse. It has a high school football tradition. It has Friday night dinners and Sunday morning services and the small accumulated rituals by which a community sustains itself across generations. The orange creeks are unbearable to look at but the people who live along them are not unbearable to talk to; they will tell you exactly what the creek used to look like, what fish used to live in it, where the mine was that began discharging into it, when the discharge began, how the company explained the discharge at the time, and what happened to the company after the discharge began. They have not forgotten any of it. They are the keepers of an institutional memory the rest of the country has been allowed not to keep.

Elizabeth Catte's argument, which I am taking from her 2018 book *What You Are Getting Wrong About Appalachia*, is that the moralization of Appalachian decline is itself part of the cost-severance mechanism: the framing of the place's condition as the moral consequence of a regional character, of choices made by the people who live there, of a culture out of sync with modernity. The moralization functions, accounting-wise, as a way of making the cost disappear into the column labeled *the people who paid it deserved to pay it*. If the cost is the consequence of the community's character, then the cost was, in the moral sense, already priced in. If the cost is the consequence of an extraction transaction in which the price did not include what the country was actually buying, then the cost is, in the accounting sense, unpaid.

The first reading lets the country off. The second reading does not.

I have spent twenty years inside institutions that managed costs by passing them to others. I want to say something carefully here, because the next sentence is the one I have been working toward. The cost severance I am describing is not a story about coal, although coal is the story I am telling. It is the operating mechanism of a significant share of the value the American economy has produced for at least a hundred years and probably longer. The financial sector, as Mazzucato has documented, extracts fees on capital it did not create. The pharmaceutical industry, as the Sackler litigation has documented, extracts patent rents on research that public investment seeded. The technology platforms, as a generation of digital-economy critics has documented, extract value from user-generated content and from labor performed by users who do not register as workers within the platform's accounting. Each of these is, mechanically, a version of the McDowell County transaction. Each of these depends on the same architectural absence: no instrument that requires the value-capturing side of a transaction to bear the long-tail cost the transaction is known to produce.

The 1969 Trust Fund is not a marginal historical curiosity. It is, in the structure of American political economy, an existence proof. It demonstrates that an instrument of the relevant kind is possible: that a country whose accounting framework normally severs costs from values can, under sufficient pressure, build an architecture that does not sever them. The instrument can be built. It is not built for most of the economy. The question is why.

## X.

I want to end by going back to what Bailey said to Lilly twenty-eight days before he died. He had testified before the Senate. He had fought the four-year benefits fight. He had survived the lung transplant. He had used the years the transplant bought him to be with his grandchildren. He was now dying of a cancer that the medications keeping him alive had given him. He had every reason to be done explaining anything to anyone. And he was still explaining.

*Most laws across the nation,* he said, *most laws have been written with blood. I don't want the things I went through just to drop and no knowledge to be gained, no people to be helped.*

The blood is the empirical record. The knowledge is what the empirical record has to teach. The laws written with blood are the architecture that, when the country has been forced to acknowledge what a transaction has actually cost, the country has occasionally agreed to build. The Trust Fund is one of those laws. The 1969 Coal Mine Health and Safety Act is another. SMCRA is, in principle, a third. They are not enough. They are not maintained. They are partial. But they are real, and they were built by people whose blood was the substrate the laws were written from.

What Bailey was asking the country to do, what he had asked the Senate to do, what he was asking the listeners of *Inside Appalachia* to do, what he was asking Lilly to carry to whoever would listen after he was gone, was to keep doing what he had spent his last years doing. Take the knowledge. Put it to use. Build the instrument that, the next time the country buys something at one thirty-third of its true cost, registers what the true cost was. Stop letting the cost be paid by the people whose work produced the value. Stop letting it be paid by the place. Stop letting it be paid by the children of the place, and the children of the children, and the orange creeks running thirty and fifty and a hundred years past the last shovel of coal lifted out of the seam.

What McDowell County paid is what the country is still being asked to pay back. The Trust Fund is the down payment. The architecture for the rest of the payment is what is still missing. The substance of an honest accounting is what would build it.

I drove out of the county late in the afternoon. The road climbed up out of the Tug Fork valley and back into the mountains. The creeks I crossed were orange. They will be orange for a long time. The men who made the coal that built the country are mostly gone. The country has not finished paying them. It has barely begun.
