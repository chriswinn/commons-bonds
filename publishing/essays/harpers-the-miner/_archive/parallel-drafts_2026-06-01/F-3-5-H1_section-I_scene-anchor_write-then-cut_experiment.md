# F-3.5-H1 §I scene-anchor — write-then-cut experiment

## §0. Discipline frame

The default Claude drafting pattern: try to honor every dimension of the brief + every audience + every craft constraint simultaneously, and compress sentences to fit them all in. This produces dense jargon and obscures load-bearing ideas.

The write-then-cut discipline this experiment tests:
1. **Write phase:** write the full version including everything plausibly serving. Don't worry about word count. If a sentence has more to say, let it say it.
2. **Cut phase:** in a separate pass, cut what doesn't carry the piece. Cut IDEAS, not sentences. If something stays, write it fully.

The anti-pattern: "this idea won't fit in the word budget; let me compress sentence X to make room." Either an idea earns its place or it doesn't.

## §3. Phase 2 — CUT VERSION (proposed §I restoration prose)

---

The first thing you notice, driving south through the county on Route 52, is what is not there. Buildings come down in the mountains the way leaves come down in the fall: a steady, unsurprising loss, indifferent to whether anyone notices. A storefront in Welch has a hand-painted sign in the window for a business that closed a decade ago. A church in Keystone is missing its roof; the pews are still inside, in rows, as if the congregation might come back. Outside Kimball there is a creek the color of a rusted nail, draining toward the Tug Fork that runs along the county's southern boundary. The county had a name for that color once, back when there were enough mining men to need the word, but the men are not here anymore and the name has gone with them.

The creek runs out of a hillside that has been letting the iron go for thirty years. There is no agency that comes to clean it. There is no fund that exists to pay anyone to. The water carries the color downstream, toward the Tug, and the Tug carries it toward the Big Sandy, and at no point in the journey does the color answer to a balance sheet.

* * *

I drove down through this country on an October afternoon, a few years ago, when the leaves were just beginning to turn and the wind had not yet picked up its winter weight. I had come because of a question that I could not, at the time, fit into ordinary economic language. I had spent the better part of twenty years inside institutions that prided themselves on rational accounting, and the longer I watched those institutions work, the more I came to suspect that the most important costs they handled were the ones they did not, by design, see.

The institutions I had worked in did not call it that. They called it externalities, or operating risk, or someone else's problem. After twenty years a person stops thinking the absence of a line item is an accident.

McDowell County, I had been told, was a place where that suspicion could be tested against the ground. So I went, and I stood at the edge of a hollow where a mine had been, and I looked at what was left, and I tried to count.

The county sits on top of one of the richest seams of metallurgical coal in the world. It produced more coal, during its peak years, than most countries. By every conventional measure of value its mountains were extraordinary. By every measure of who was the better for it, the county is not. The mountains are still there. Almost everything else is gone.

What follows is an attempt, in plain English, to add up what was actually paid for the coal that came out of that ground. Not what it sold for. What it cost. The arithmetic, when you do it honestly, is not subtle. The market price of McDowell County coal, integrated across a century of extraction, was less than what its true cost worked out to by an order of magnitude. The difference was paid. Someone paid it. The question of who is the question this essay is about.
