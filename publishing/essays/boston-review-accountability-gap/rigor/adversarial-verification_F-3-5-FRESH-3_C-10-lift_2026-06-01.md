## 2. Skeptic A — Data-source-rigor discipline skeptic

### Perspective

This skeptic reads V-E §V cohort-definition triplet as a Stone Center methodologist evaluating whether the prose has met the bar Stone Center work routinely meets in published research. They are asking: when Hamilton / Darity / Price cite a wealth-gap calculation, what level of source-naming do they actually use, and does V-E's SCF + DFA naming match that bar or fall short of it?

### Reading the V-E §V sentence as a Stone Center methodologist

The Stone Center publication pattern (the *Insight Center / Duke Center for Social Equity* outputs cited in the Stage 1 brief §7.7 — *Umbrellas Don't Make It Rain* 2015, *From Here to Equality* 2020, Conley 1999) consistently names the data instruments: SCF for wealth measurement; PSID for longitudinal income; CPS for labor-market measures; ACS for demographic controls; HMDA for mortgage-origination data when the analysis touches mortgage markets. The named instrument is the standard rigor signal in this tradition — the unnamed reference to "the wealth, employment, and household-formation data the Federal Reserve has tracked" is exactly the un-anchored phrasing a Stone Center reviewer would flag as falling short of the tradition's data-source-naming bar.

The V-E sentence as drafted names the SCF and the DFA. The SCF is the triennial Federal Reserve household-wealth survey — the primary data source Hamilton / Darity / Price and successors use for the wealth-gap calculations the Restitution Bond's methodology directly extends. The DFA (quarterly, Z.1 distributional decomposition, Table B.101.h integration of SCF with Financial Accounts of the United States) is the more recent Federal Reserve product (launched 2019) that provides quarterly distributional wealth measurement at the cohort level — exactly the post-crisis-window measurement granularity the V-E sentence claims tracks "across the post-crisis window."

For a Stone Center methodologist, the naming is at the standard rigor bar, and is also specifically the right naming for the claim being made. The sentence claims the Federal Reserve has tracked the cohort-level wealth / employment / household-formation differential across the post-crisis window; the SCF + DFA are precisely the Federal Reserve products that support that claim.

### Test: does the naming pass first-principles substantive evaluation, or is it ornament?

Three sub-tests:

**Sub-test 2.A — Source-claim alignment.** Does the named source actually support the claim made in the sentence? *Yes.* SCF tracks household wealth at the demographic-cohort level (Federal Reserve Bulletin SCF release every three years documents wealth, income, employment status, household composition — all four outcome categories the V-E sentence names). DFA tracks the same household-balance-sheet measures at quarterly granularity post-2019 with backward integration to 1989. The Federal Reserve has, in fact, tracked exactly the outcomes the V-E sentence claims at exactly the granularity the V-E sentence implies.

**Sub-test 2.B — Tradition-fit.** Is this the naming a Stone Center publication would itself use? *Yes.* The 2015 *Umbrellas Don't Make It Rain* uses SCF data extensively; Darity's 2020 work cites SCF for wealth-gap calculations; Hamilton's recent work on baby bonds cites SCF for the cohort-level distributional baseline. DFA is newer but is the Federal Reserve's own integration of SCF with FAUS data — a Stone Center reviewer would recognize it as the right contemporary source for cohort-level quarterly tracking.

**Sub-test 2.C — Discipline against citation-padding.** Would a Stone Center reviewer suspect this naming was added for ornamental purposes (essay-length padding; signal-density theater)? *No.* The two named instruments are minimal — there are at least four other Federal Reserve products that could be named alongside them (Z.1 Financial Accounts; Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey; Survey of Household Economics and Decisionmaking; Consumer Credit Panel). The V-E sentence names the two that most directly support the cohort-level claim and stops. This is discipline-correct restraint, not citation-padding.

### Verdict on the C-10 lift claim

**RATIFIED — ✓✓✓ substantive lift confirmed.** The SCF + DFA naming meets all three sub-tests at the Stone Center methodologist bar. The pre-graft V-E sentence (which referenced only "the wealth, employment, and household-formation data the Federal Reserve has tracked") had the structural shape but lacked the data-source anchor. The post-graft sentence has both. A Stone Center reviewer reading the post-graft sentence would register the cohort-definition triplet as having cleared the data-source-rigor bar that the cohort-comparison methodology presupposes.

## 4. Skeptic C — Per-cohort calibration faithfulness + ornamental-vs-load-bearing tension skeptic

### Perspective

This skeptic is the strongest adversarial position the task prompt explicitly flags: the SCF + DFA name the wealth/employment/household-formation **outcome data** at the cohort level, but they do NOT directly support the **cohort identification methodology** ("working-age population in regions where mortgage securitization penetrated less deeply"). A Stone Center reviewer working through the V-E methodology paragraph might note: the SCF + DFA naming is technically correct for the *outcome-measure* source but is silent on the *cohort-identification methodology*, which is the methodologically prior step.

This is the ornamental-vs-load-bearing tension. If the lift claim rests on "the SCF + DFA naming makes the cohort definition rigorous," and the reviewer notes that the cohort definition's rigor actually depends on a separate (unnamed) data source for the regional-securitization-penetration designation, the lift could collapse from ✓✓✓ to ✓✓ or worse — a Stone Center reviewer who notices the methodological gap reads the SCF + DFA naming as covering the easy half of the methodology while leaving the harder half unaddressed.

### Reading the V-E §V sentence as a per-cohort calibration faithfulness reviewer

The full V-E paragraph 3 sentence under audit:

> For 2008, the comparison cohort is the working-age population in regions where mortgage securitization penetrated less deeply; the differential reaches the wealth, employment, and household-formation data the Federal Reserve has tracked across the post-crisis window through the Survey of Consumer Finances and the quarterly Distributional Financial Accounts series.

Two methodological steps are compressed into this sentence:

- **Step 1 — Cohort identification.** Identify the comparison cohort: "working-age population in regions where mortgage securitization penetrated less deeply." This requires a data source that measures regional securitization penetration. HMDA loan-level data + GSE / private-label securitization tagging (e.g., CoreLogic, Black Knight, or academic datasets like the McDash-Equifax linked dataset) is the conventional source.
- **Step 2 — Outcome measurement.** Measure the differential between the comparison cohort and the cost-bearing cohort on wealth / employment / household-formation outcomes. SCF + DFA are the right sources for this.

The V-E sentence names a source only for Step 2. Step 1's data source is implicit. A Stone Center reviewer working through the sentence as a methodology specification would notice this asymmetry.

Two competing readings:
