## Per-candidate rigor

### 1. The Mars Test

**Editor 3-second read:** Philosophical thought-experiment piece using Mars as the test arena. Plausible Aeon territory.

**Misreadings:** Cold War nuclear-test connotation possible (low risk; "Mars" wasn't a known test program). Alternative read: aerospace/medical test administered on Mars.

**Genre-coding:** Mostly thought-experiment register; some sci-fi spillover.

**Frame-lock:** Locks to Mars specifically. Version B (reader visits closed habitat — Mars OR Moon OR space station) wouldn't work cleanly with this title.

**Pressure-test breadth:**
- ✓ Ostrom-successor / Berggruen Judge / Aeon editor: philosophical-test framing on-brand
- ⚠ Coasean economist: may read as economic test of Mars colonization
- ⚠⚠ McDowell County resident, Black readers (generational dispossession), indigenous readers, working-class readers without lineage, first-gen immigrants: "Mars" reads distant from lived cost-bearing experience; sci-fi-coded language can feel elitist or escapist for readers carrying real extractive cost
- ⚠ Literary agent: categorizes as sci-fi-adjacent literary; mixed reception

**Voice/register match:** Matches pitch's thought-experiment register; slightly cold compared to pitch's lead.

**Memorability/distinctiveness:** Three words; easy recall; search-distinct enough.

**Procedural rigor concerns:** None — *"test"* is accurate to the Kantian universalizability move (Mars tests whether the framework holds).

**Agent/publisher lens (mid-pass addition):** Sci-fi categorization risk substantial. Comp titles hard to find — closest are Kim Stanley Robinson essays or Tim Mulgan's *Future People* (smaller-audience books). Agents have been more cautious about sci-fi-coded literary titles since 2020. Acquisitions: ambiguous shelf placement (Sci/Phil/Econ).

**Verdict:** Strong philosophical-test framing. Costs: sci-fi categorization risk; frame-lock to Mars; weaker downstream-pipeline signaling.

### 2. The Mask of Abundance ← RATIFIED

**Editor 3-second read:** Literary-philosophical noun phrase. Editor expects an essay about how abundance disguises something. Pure Aeon territory.

**Misreadings:** Most plausible read is metaphorical (abundance hides cost). No clean misreading paths.

**Genre-coding:** Pure literary-philosophical; no sci-fi categorization.

**Frame-lock:** Frame-agnostic. Works for Version A, Version B, or any future variant. Most flexible candidate.

**Pressure-test breadth:**
- ✓ Ostrom-successor: connects to commons-management literature
- ⚠ Coasean economist: economists don't typically conceptualize abundance as masking; mixed
- ✓ Berggruen Judge / Aeon editor / Literary agent: distinctive philosophical title
- ✓ McDowell County resident: "mask hides something" connects to lived extraction experience
- ⚠⚠ **Black readers carrying generational dispossession: cultural-resonance concern.** *"The mask of X"* construction echoes Paul Laurence Dunbar's *We Wear the Mask* (1896) — foundational in Black literary tradition about masks Black Americans wear to navigate white-supremacist society. The pitch (and book) MUST engage that lineage explicitly to avoid reading as appropriation. **Load-bearing flag.**
- ⚠ Indigenous readers: mask is sacred ritual object in many traditions (Hopi katsina, Northwest Coast); metaphorical use generally fine but worth noting
- ✓ First-generation immigrants: connects to American Dream critique

**Voice/register match:** Threads to pitch's new Mars sentence (*"the mask of abundance is removed"*). Strong connection.

**Memorability/distinctiveness:** Four words; distinctive concept-anchor; uncommon phrase in search.

**Procedural rigor concerns:**
- Inverts framework's term *"abundance masking"* (literary readers reconcile)
- **Cultural-resonance concern with Dunbar tradition** is the load-bearing rigor flag — addressed via Config A engagement plan

**Agent/publisher lens (mid-pass addition):** **STRONGEST.** *"The X of Y"* construction is the dominant title shape in contemporary literary nonfiction. Comp titles immediate and strong: Sandel *The Tyranny of Merit*, Pistor *The Code of Capital*, Giridharadas *Winners Take All*, Raworth *Doughnut Economics*, Mazzucato *Mission Economy*. An agent reads this title and immediately knows where it shelves, who buys it, how to pitch it. Acquisitions: literary-nonfiction shelf (Current Affairs / Phil / Econ); strong review network (NYRB, NYT, Atlantic, Aeon, Foreign Affairs).

**Verdict:** Strongest on Aeon-register, frame-flexibility, and agent/publisher signaling. **Cultural-resonance concern is load-bearing** — must be addressed via explicit engagement (Config A): brief acknowledgment in Aeon essay + substantive engagement in book Ch 8 (McDowell coal miner) and Ch 1 (framework's intellectual lineage).

### 3. What Earth Costs

**Editor 3-second read:** Question-form title; editor expects an essay answering what Earth costs. Could read environmental, economic, or philosophical.

**Misreadings:** *"What Earth costs us"* (we pay Earth back); *"What Earth costs to provide"* (the bill nature is owed); *"What it costs to be on Earth"* (existential). Genuine ambiguity in *"costs."*

**Genre-coding:** Question-form Aeon pattern; environmental-coded by default.

**Frame-lock:** Frame-agnostic.

**Pressure-test breadth:**
- ✓ Ostrom-successor / Coasean economist / Berggruen Judge: economic-philosophical question
- ✓ McDowell County resident: "what Earth costs" connects to extraction reality directly
- ✓ Aeon editor / Literary agent: question-form Aeon pattern; punchy
- ✓ Black readers (generational dispossession): connects to land-loss, Black agrarian dispossession
- ✓ Indigenous readers: connects strongly with indigenous critique of extraction
- ✓ Working-class readers / immigrants: direct, accessible, non-academic

**Strongest across the pressure-test set.**

**Voice/register match:** Less philosophical-mystical than *Mask of Abundance*; more direct. Threads to pitch's payoff (*"the price tag on Earth's ambient abundance"*).

**Memorability/distinctiveness:** Three words. Strong recall. Search may compete with environmental/climate content.

**Procedural rigor concerns:** *"Costs"* ambiguity (costs whom? what?); could be misread as climate-economics piece.

**Agent/publisher lens (mid-pass addition):** Strong but **genre-narrowing**. Comp titles: Hickel *Less Is More*, Nixon *Slow Violence*, Sassen *Expulsions* — all environmental-shelf books. The book isn't only environmental (commute leases, healthcare, work culture, intergenerational debt are all in scope) — the title risks painting the publisher into the climate corner.

**Verdict:** Strongest pressure-test profile. Loses some philosophical mystique but gains accessibility and lived-experience connection. **Strong-second** as fallback if Mask of Abundance is rejected.

### 4. On Closed Worlds

**Editor 3-second read:** Aeon's signature *"On X"* pattern. Philosophical essay about closed-world systems. Aeon-archetypal.

**Misreadings:** Game design ("closed world" gaming term); physics/systems theory; sci-fi literal closed planets.

**Genre-coding:** Pure philosophical.

**Frame-lock:** Plural *"Worlds"* — works for Mars, Moon, space station, or any closed-system thought experiment. Frame-flexible.

**Pressure-test breadth:**
- ✓ Ostrom-successor: "closed worlds" connects to commons-closure literature
- ✓ Berggruen Judge / Aeon editor / Literary agent: Aeon-archetypal register
- ⚠ McDowell County resident: abstract; doesn't connect to lived experience
- ⚠ Black readers, indigenous readers: abstract; could connect to closed-territory/reservation framings (mixed)
- ⚠ Working-class readers: abstract; may feel academic
- ⚠ Coasean economist: less obvious connection to economics

**Stronger with literary readers, weaker with lived-experience readers.**

**Voice/register match:** Pure Aeon literary-philosophical. More austere than pitch's visceral lead.

**Memorability/distinctiveness:** Three words. Less concrete anchor than other candidates.

**Procedural rigor concerns:** *"Closed worlds"* plural ≠ framework's term *"closed habitat"* singular (minor precision issue).

**Agent/publisher lens (mid-pass addition):** **WEAK.** Reads as academic philosophy rather than literary nonfiction. Hard to find comp titles. Agent worries about audience size; acquisitions worries about category fit (academic philosophy doesn't sell broadly).

**Verdict:** Most Aeon-register-pure but most abstract. Strong with editorial gatekeepers, weaker with the broader audience. **Weak agent/publisher signal.**

**Editor 3-second read:** Gerund-construction title; editor expects essay about cost transparency. Could be business/policy/design.

**Misreadings:** Plausibly read as business-strategy, policy-transparency, or accountability-dashboard piece. Less philosophical-coded.

**Genre-coding:** Slightly business/policy register; less literary than other candidates.

**Frame-lock:** Frame-agnostic.

**Pressure-test breadth:**
- ✓ Ostrom-successor / Coasean economist: directly addresses externality-internalization
- ⚠ Berggruen Judge: would prefer more philosophical framing
- ⚠ Aeon editor / Literary agent: less Aeon-register-pure; verb-gerund titles uncommon in Aeon archive
- ✓ McDowell County resident: direct, accessible
- ✓ Black readers (generational dispossession): connects to making invisible labor/cost visible (Black feminist tradition)
- ✓ Indigenous readers: connects to making extraction costs visible
- ✓ Working-class readers / immigrants: direct, accessible

**Voice/register match:** More journalism / business than pitch's literary register. Mismatch.

**Memorability/distinctiveness:** Generic phrasing — *"making X visible"* used widely in design, business, policy. Search-distinctiveness low.

**Procedural rigor concerns:** Doesn't carry the universalizability move; doesn't carry the Mars/closed-habitat thought experiment; **generic problem** (could describe many essays).

**Agent/publisher lens (mid-pass addition):** **WEAK for literary track.** Generic phrasing puts it in the business-popular shelf rather than literary nonfiction. Comp titles in the accountability/transparency wave that lacks lasting traction. Could work for business-pop track but that's not where the book should go.

**Verdict:** Strong on accessibility, weak on Aeon register and distinctiveness. Loses what makes the pitch distinctive in service of broad accessibility.

## Recommendation (ratified by author 2026-05-08)

**RATIFIED: "The Mask of Abundance" (Config A — title + cultural-engagement commitment).**

### Rationale

1. **Strongest agent/publisher pipeline signaling.** Matches contemporary literary-nonfiction "X of Y" title pattern (Sandel, Pistor, Mazzucato, Raworth comp territory). The book's downstream goal isn't just an Aeon publication — it's an Aeon publication used as proof-of-concept to attract literary agents and a publisher. The title needs to do double duty: land at Aeon AND signal a marketable book.

2. **Frame-agnostic.** Works for Version A (Mars admin visits Earth) AND Version B (reader visits closed habitat — Mars OR Moon OR space station). Doesn't lock the pitch to a specific thought-experiment direction.

3. **Threads to pitch's signature phrasing.** "The mask of abundance is removed" appears in para 2; the title surfaces what the pitch is already doing.

### Cultural-engagement commitment (load-bearing — Config A)

The Dunbar resonance is real and must be addressed explicitly. Three configurations were considered:

- **Config A (ratified):** Title + brief acknowledgment in essay + substantive engagement in book. Title strength stays at strongest-of-all-candidates; intellectual lineage gets claimed cleanly; book becomes more interesting because it joins a tradition.
- **Config B (rejected):** Title + silent structural use only. Title strength holds at first contact; intellectual lineage stays unaddressed; risk of late-stage flag during publishing process (sensitivity readers, cover designers, reviewers).
- **Config C (rejected):** Different title that dodges the Dunbar question entirely. Title strength drops slightly (less Aeon-register-pure, narrower book positioning) but the question is dodged.

### Engagement plan

1. **Aeon essay** — brief in-text aside near the abundance-mask passage, acknowledging the structural kinship to Dunbar's *We Wear the Mask*.

2. **Book Ch 8 (SCC/coal arithmetic, McDowell County case-walk)** — substantive integration. The miner whose lungs sound like his father's IS wearing the mask Dunbar named. The framework's claim is that this mask is not his alone; it belongs to the system that puts him in front of the X-ray. Explicit Dunbar threading.

3. **Book Ch 1 (or wherever framework's central rhetorical move first surfaces)** — paragraph acknowledging that the framework's central move (unmasking systemic harm to reveal who bears the cost) inherits a tradition that includes Dunbar, Du Bois, Ellison, Fanon. The framework extends the move into pricing; the move itself is older than the framework.

4. **Bibliography** — new section §21 *Black literary tradition — unmasking systemic harm (Mask of Abundance title-engagement)* with foundational citations (Dunbar 1896, Du Bois 1903, Fanon 1952, Ellison 1952). Contemporary extensions can be added as engagement deepens.

5. **Darity live-call companion** — prepared pivot for May 12 interview if Sandy raises the mask-terminology question. Posture: lead with acknowledgment, make structural correspondence explicit, invite his guidance.

### Strong-second alternatives if editorial pushback

- **What Earth Costs** — strongest pressure-test breadth; environmental-genre-narrowing risk
- **The Mars Test** — strong distinctiveness; sci-fi categorization risk + Mars frame-lock
