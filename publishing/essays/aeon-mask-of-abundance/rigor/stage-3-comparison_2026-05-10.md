## 1. Cross-cut criterion-by-criterion comparison

For each criterion: **A** — Pitch A on this dimension; **B** — Pitch B on this dimension; **Winner** + reasoning.

### 1.1 Editor 3-second read — first thing the editor sees

**A.** Opens straight into the imperative scene: *"Imagine landing at a closed habitat — Mars, the Moon, an orbital station, the analog at Devon Island or HI-SEAS."* No salutation, no preamble. The pitch body starts with the thought experiment.

**B.** Opens with two-line epistolary preamble: *"Dear Mr Haselby, / I would like to propose an essay, The Mask of Abundance, for Aeon."* Only at the third line does the actual pitch body begin: *"Spend a month inside a closed habitat: Mars, the Moon, an orbital station, or an Earth-side analog like Devon Island or HI-SEAS."*

**Winner: A, decisively.** The editor reads the first line and decides whether to keep reading. A's first line is the thought experiment; B's first line is a salutation. Stage 1 brief §2 explicitly listed *"Defensive register ('I am addressing the obvious objection because…')"* and *"hedge phrases ('I will argue that…')"* as Aeon-hurting; B's *"I would like to propose an essay"* sits in that anti-pattern register. The aeon.co/pitch portal is a pitch portal, not a query-letter form; salutation+intent-to-pitch is convention drift toward NYRB/Harper's submission style, not Aeon's editorial-brain expectation. (The salutation is removable as a fix on B, but A simply doesn't have the problem.)

### 1.2 ¶1 hook strength (full opener block)

**A.** ~101w. Two-beat structure: (1) airlock scene → "*you understand viscerally, then and there, what you have been taking for granted all your life — someone else has been paying.*" (2) Return-home reversal → *"The abundant heat, light, water and even the air is free again — all of it suddenly costless. That first morning back, you laugh at the absurdity of it. Then you discover, slowly, that the costs are still being paid. Just not by you."*

The load-bearing rhetorical devices are: the airlock-question (a singular focal moment forcing the insight); *"you laugh at the absurdity of it"* (a small narrative beat that humanizes the reader-character); and *"Just not by you."* — a four-word devastation closer.

**B.** ~96w. Different structure: (1) habitat-immersion → "*Spend a month inside a closed habitat… You learn to read the manifest fast. Someone hauled in the lithium. Someone metered the water for your shower. The CO₂ scrubbers do not refill themselves. You ask, automatically, who paid for the air you breathed at lunch.*" (2) Return-home → *"Then you fly home and walk outside. The river is free. So is the soil. So, somehow, is the air. Someone has always been paying. The bills route to people, places, and futures you cannot see."*

B brings stronger concrete texture (lithium, manifest, CO₂ scrubbers, river, soil) — vivid materiel.

**Winner: A, by a clear margin.** Three reasons:

1. **Compression vs. diffusion.** A focuses the insight on a single concrete demand (the airlock question: *how will you be paying for your air?*). B distributes it across multiple manifest items. A reader's "viscerally implicating" moment is sharper when one demand is asked, not five inventory items are listed. Stage 1 §4 explicitly named the load-bearing element as "airlock / payment question → 'you understand viscerally what you've been taking for granted'" — A executes that beat; B substitutes a manifest-reading scene that diffuses it.

2. **Closer punch.** A's *"Just not by you."* is four words doing the work of a paragraph. B's *"The bills route to people, places, and futures you cannot see"* is a serviceable line but the rhetorical climax is buried inside an explanatory clause.

3. **LLM-tic exposure.** B has three rule-of-three structures in ¶1 alone:
   - *"Someone hauled in the lithium. Someone metered the water for your shower. The CO₂ scrubbers do not refill themselves."*
   - *"The river is free. So is the soil. So, somehow, is the air."*
   - *"people, places, and futures"*
   
   Stage 2 brief §3.4 explicitly listed *"rule of three"* as an LLM tic to avoid. A has lists (the habitat enumeration; *"heat, light, water and even the air"*) but they are list-of-four / list-of-five rather than tidy parallels. The Stage 1-flagged anti-pattern fires three times in B's ¶1.

B's ¶1 is genuinely good prose with vivid material. A's ¶1 is sharper, doesn't trip the LLM-tic rule, and lands a four-word closer that is the essay's argument-in-miniature.

### 1.3 ¶2 framework + range

**A.** *"This essay walks a pricing framework through what you discover. Built outside the academy across a decade, the framework names patterns through which costs get hidden, including geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry, power asymmetry, normative routinization, and abundance masking. The closed habitat is the limiting case that exposes Earth's open system. These patterns route the costs that stopped at the airlock to communities downstream, to atmospheres a generation has not yet been born to breathe, to ecosystems that cannot send a bill. The framework is not anti-extraction. Asteroid iron, with no community or ecosystem to bear the cost: extract freely. Coal in McDowell County in 1960: severely mispriced. A self-imposed commute lease whose hours of life were never computed: same framework, no external bad actor, structural test the same."*

**B.** *"The experience names a framework: cost severance. Goods are mispriced when the people, ecosystems, or future generations who bear the costs are absent from the trade. Severance operates through recognizable patterns: distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry, power asymmetry, normative routinization, and the mask of abundance that makes a renewable resource appear free. The closed habitat is the limiting case that exposes Earth's open system. Three cases mark the range. Iron mined from a lifeless asteroid bears no severed cost; extract freely. Coal in McDowell County in 1960 was severely mispriced, and the framework specifies which costs and to whom. A self-imposed commute lease whose stolen hours of life never appeared in any spreadsheet fails the same test, with no villain in the story."*

**Sub-winners:**

- **Naming the framework.** B explicitly names *"cost severance"* and italicizes it; A names it only as *"a pricing framework."* Stage 1 §5 permitted italicized *cost severance* as a key term but warned against framework apparatus. B's choice is editorially defensible; A's choice keeps the pitch term-light. **Slight edge to B** for giving the editor a handle on the concept — but only slight, because A's restraint is also defensible.
- **Definition.** B contributes a clean philosophical sentence: *"Goods are mispriced when the people, ecosystems, or future generations who bear the costs are absent from the trade."* A does not have an equivalent definitional sentence. **Edge to B** on definitional clarity (especially for the Coasean-economist audience).
- **Rhetorical extension of the patterns.** A: *"to communities downstream, to atmospheres a generation has not yet been born to breathe, to ecosystems that cannot send a bill."* B has no equivalent. This is the literary register Aeon publishes. **Edge to A**, large.
- **Philosophical anchor sentence.** Both pitches use the identical sentence: *"The closed habitat is the limiting case that exposes Earth's open system."* B italicizes; A does not. Stage 1 §5 said italicize the philosophical-anchor sentence sparingly. **Slight convention-edge to B**; substantive wash.
- **Explicit "not anti-extraction" reframing.** A: *"The framework is not anti-extraction."* — explicitly named as a structural claim. B folds this implicitly into the asteroid case (*"Iron mined from a lifeless asteroid bears no severed cost; extract freely"*) without explicit reframing. The reader infers the not-anti-extraction claim; A states it. **Edge to A** on argumentative clarity.
- **Three cross-parameter cases.** Both name asteroid iron / McDowell coal / commute lease. A's case-statements are tighter (*"Coal in McDowell County in 1960: severely mispriced."* vs. B's *"Coal in McDowell County in 1960 was severely mispriced, and the framework specifies which costs and to whom."* — B's clause is true but adds words without adding content). **Edge to A** on case compression.
- **Commute-lease language.** A: *"A self-imposed commute lease whose hours of life were never computed: same framework, no external bad actor, structural test the same."* B: *"A self-imposed commute lease whose stolen hours of life never appeared in any spreadsheet fails the same test, with no villain in the story."* B's *"stolen hours"* introduces moral framing (theft-by-someone) that the example specifically does not warrant — the case is *self-imposed*. The phrase contradicts the no-villain payoff three words later. **Edge to A**, and this is a real flaw in B that would bother the analytic-philosophy register Stage 1 §2 prizes.

**¶2 winner: A, by a clear margin.** B contributes two real strengths (the explicit *cost severance* naming + the philosophical definition); A wins on rhetorical density, argumentative explicitness, case compression, and avoiding the "stolen hours" misstep. The flaws in B are more rhetorically costly than B's strengths.

### 1.4 ¶3 universal claim + close

**A.** *"The central claim is that the framework is universal — a pricing framework first, a critique second. Most discussions of resource pricing are political. This one is structural. If the framework is right, the price tag on commons is many multiples of what we have been paying as a species. The math, finally done, is what a thoughtful reader on any world — including this one — would recognize as honest accounting."* (~69w)

**B.** *"The framework is universal: a pricing framework first, a critique second. Most discussions of resource pricing are political quarrels about who deserves what. This one is structural. If the framework is right, the price tag on the commons is many multiples of what we have been paying as a species. What would count as honest accounting in a closed habitat should count in this open one too."* (~67w)

**Sub-winners:**

- **Universality framing.** A: *"The central claim is that the framework is universal —"* (positions the universality move as the central claim, unmistakably). B: *"The framework is universal:"* (more compressed, but loses the "central claim is" framing that an editor specifically reads for). **Edge to A** on rhetorical positioning.
- **Political-quarrel elaboration.** B adds *"political quarrels about who deserves what."* This is true but adds editorializing voice (*"quarrels"*) that A's clean *"are political. This one is structural."* avoids. The compression in A is more philosophically poised. **Edge to A**.
- **Close.** A: *"The math, finally done, is what a thoughtful reader on any world — including this one — would recognize as honest accounting."* B: *"What would count as honest accounting in a closed habitat should count in this open one too."*
  
  A's close is doing four things at once: (1) *"The math, finally done"* — the framework as a calculation that should have been performed but wasn't (the entire book's claim, packed into four words); (2) *"a thoughtful reader on any world"* — Kantian-universalizability landed in seven words; (3) *"including this one"* — the perspective-reversal completed; (4) *"honest accounting"* — the moral register, which is also a reference to the framework's stated discipline. B's close lands the closed-habitat / open-Earth structural mirror correctly but lacks A's load-bearing compression. *"The math, finally done"* is the kind of phrase that Aeon editors specifically respond to.
  
  **Edge to A**, large. A's close is one of those load-bearing rhetorical moves that is the load-bearing reason to use this venue.

**¶3 winner: A, decisively.** This is where the gap between A and B is largest. A's *"any world — including this one"* and *"The math, finally done"* are doing argumentative and rhetorical work that B's close gestures toward but does not deliver.

### 1.6 Voice register / LLM-tic exposure

Stage 2 brief §3.4 named these LLM tics to avoid: rule of three; em-dash crutches; tidy parallels; hedge phrases.

**A.**
- Em-dashes: 4 in body. (*"closed habitat — Mars,"* / *"granted all your life — someone"* / *"is free again — all of it"* / *"any world — including this one"*) — used for parenthetical extension and punch, not as crutches.
- Rule-of-three: I find none. The habitat list is 5 items; *"heat, light, water and even the air"* is 4; *"to communities downstream, to atmospheres…, to ecosystems…"* is 3 but the rhythm is escalating-and-extending, not tidy-parallel.
- Hedge phrases: none. (*"This essay walks…"* is descriptive, not hedge.)

**B.**
- Em-dashes: ~2 in body. Light usage; not a crutch.
- Rule-of-three: at least 4 instances:
  - ¶1: *"Someone hauled in the lithium. Someone metered the water for your shower. The CO₂ scrubbers do not refill themselves."*
  - ¶1: *"The river is free. So is the soil. So, somehow, is the air."*
  - ¶1: *"people, places, and futures"*
  - ¶2: *"the people, ecosystems, or future generations"*
- Hedge phrase: *"I would like to propose an essay"* in the salutation block (sits in the *"I will argue that…"* family Stage 1 flagged).

**Winner: A, decisively.** B's voice has real strengths in concrete texture, but the LLM-tic exposure is exactly what Stage 1 §3.4 warned against. A reads more like Mulgan / Sandel / MacAskill (Aeon's house voice register, per Stage 1 §5); B reads slightly closer to a polished-LLM register.

### 1.8 Aeon editorial-brain fit (Haselby's "simple direct opener that states the argument followed by 200 words outlining how it will be made")

Neither pitch lands Haselby's literal prescription (simple direct opener stating the argument). Both make the deliberate trade of a scene-first opener.

Stage 1 §2 mapped Aeon's editorial brain as *"literary-philosophical, thought-experiment-tolerant, abstract-tolerant. Reads pitches in seconds. Wants: distinctive angle (not standard direction); clean philosophical argument; universalizability; voice; cross-domain reach."*

- **Reads pitches in seconds:** A's no-salutation opener wins this on first-second-read; B's salutation+intent-to-pitch costs ~1.5 seconds before the actual pitch begins.

**Winner: A.** Across the editorial-brain criteria, A is closer or equivalent on all dimensions and clearly closer on three (clean argument; universalizability; voice register).

## 3. What B does better (for meta-verdict synthesis materials)

The line is concise (24 words) and would slot into A's ¶2 without disrupting structure. **Possible carry-forward: insert this definition into A's ¶2.** Cost: ~24w over A's 300w budget unless something else is cut.

The CO₂ scrubbers do not refill themselves"* gives more material texture than A's airlock-only opener. The cost is rhetorical compression and an LLM-tic exposure. **Probably not carry-forwardable** into A without breaking A's compression.
