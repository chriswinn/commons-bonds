# The Mask of Abundance

Imagine you have just arrived at a sealed habitat. It could be on Mars, on the Moon, on a station in orbit, or at one of the real places on Earth where crews already rehearse this kind of life, like Devon Island in the Canadian Arctic or HI-SEAS on the side of a Hawaiian volcano. The location does not matter. What matters is the wall. On one side, air a body can live in. On the other, nothing a body can.

At the airlock, before they let you in, you are asked a question that would sound absurd almost anywhere on Earth. How will you be paying for your air?

And right there, you feel something you have taken for granted your whole life. The air was never free. It was only ever free to you.

Eventually, you come home. The air is free again. So is the heat, the light, the water. All of it, free again, all at once, the moment you are back. That first morning home, you laugh at how absurd the airlock question now sounds. Pay for air. Then the laugh fades, because the airlock has shown you something you cannot stop seeing. The air here is just as costly to keep breathable. The bill is just as real. It is only paid by someone else, somewhere else, or by someone not yet born.

That is the subject of this essay, in a sentence. The most valuable things we have are the ones we all share and no one pays for: the air, the fresh water, a stable, livable climate, the quiet work the planet does of taking back the carbon we put into the sky. Economists call these the commons. They carry no price, not because they are worth little, but because, for now, there is so much of them that no seller can find a shortage to charge for. The abundance hides the value. I will call that the mask of abundance. And the danger of a thing with no price is not that we treat it as cheap. It is that we cannot see it at all. It never reaches the ledger, so we spend it as though it were free, which here, for now, it appears to be.

A sealed habitat is useful precisely because it tears that mask off. Put a wall around the air and it is no longer abundant, and the moment it is no longer abundant it has a price, and the price reveals what the air was worth the entire time. We do not even have to imagine this. We have already built small sealed worlds, and in every one of them the free things of Earth became the most expensive things inside the wall.
