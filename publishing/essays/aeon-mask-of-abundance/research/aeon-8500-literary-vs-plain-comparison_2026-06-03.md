# The Mask of Abundance — Literary (A) vs. Plain-Language (B)

Both essays follow the same 12-beat arc, the same verified-facts brief, and the
same ~8,500w floor. They differ in register: A is the standard Aeon
literary-philosophical voice; B is a "plain language for a layman" redraft. B is
~2,330 words longer. What follows leads with the register transformation
(Section 2) and the content inventory (Section 1), then attributes the extra
length (Section 3) and gives a verdict (Section 4). All quotes are verbatim.

---

## SECTION 2 — How "plain language" was operationalized (the heart of it)

B is plainer through a consistent, identifiable toolkit. Each technique below is
backed by a paired example: A's version of an idea against B's version of the
SAME idea.

### Technique 1: Introduce the concept in plain words BEFORE naming it

This is B's single most pervasive move and the clearest signature of the "plain
language" overlay. A names a term of art and then unpacks it; B builds the
intuition first, *then* drops the label as a payoff.

**Cost severance.**

A names it almost immediately, then explains:
> "Call it cost severance. Someone creates a cost. Someone else absorbs it. And
> the second party has no way to send the bill back to the first."

B walks the reader through an unnamed example first, lands the name afterward,
and even glosses the metaphor inside the name:
> "Start with the simplest unfair thing in the world. Someone does something
> that creates a cost, and someone else ends up paying that cost, and the person
> who paid has no way to send the bill back to the person who caused it. A
> factory pours waste into a river... That is the shape of the thing... I am
> going to give that shape a name and use it for the rest of the essay. Call it
> cost severance. A cost is severed when it is cut loose from the person or the
> business that caused it, and floats off to land on someone who did not."

**Residual commons value.**

A names and defines in a single literary motion:
> "I will call this remainder the residual commons value. It is the part of the
> worth that the price did not reach, because price measures the margin and the
> margin is now, while the worth runs on into a future the margin cannot price."

B sets up the leftover first, names it, then explicitly glosses the word
"residual" as if to a reader who might not know it:
> "That leftover, the worth taken and never paid for, owed to people who were not
> at the table, I am going to call the residual commons value. Residual just
> means what is left over. It is the commons value that remains unaccounted-for
> after the sale is done."

That "Residual just means what is left over" is the plainest tell of the overlay
— a one-clause dictionary aside A would never include.

**Foreclosure / restitution bonds.**

A names them and trusts the reader to absorb the metaphor:
> "Run forward, against a loss not yet realized, the instrument is a foreclosure
> bond: you price the future loss before you cause it, and you set the money
> aside... Run backward, against an extraction already realized, the instrument
> is a restitution bond: you price what was taken and what was severed, after
> the fact, and you reckon what is owed."

B defines the verb inside the noun for both — teaching "foreclose" and
"restitution" as vocabulary before using them:
> "Run it forward... the instrument is a foreclosure bond. The word foreclose
> means to shut off a possibility, to close a door on a future... Run it
> backward... the instrument is a restitution bond. Restitution means making
> good on a wrong already committed, restoring something taken."

**Discount rate.**

A assumes the reader knows what a discount rate is and goes straight to the
combatants. B stops to define the term in-line before deploying it:
> B: "...the fight was over something called the discount rate, which is the rate
> at which you shrink the value of future costs to compare them with present
> ones."

A never pauses for that definition; it embeds the concept in the Stern/Nordhaus
contrast and the worked example ("a hundred dollars of benefit a century out is
worth only about twenty-five dollars today at Stern's rate").

### Technique 2: The social-cost-of-carbon arithmetic — assert vs. walk through

This is the most dramatic single divergence in the essay. A states the result
in roughly two sentences:
> "Burning a ton of coal releases roughly two and a half tons of carbon dioxide,
> and if you value that carbon dioxide at the kind of social cost that careful
> estimates now reach, on the order of a hundred and ninety dollars a ton, the
> carbon term alone comes to something near five hundred and ten dollars."

B turns the same computation into a numbered, step-by-step walkthrough, with the
term defined, the multiplication shown out loud, and a sourced figure:
> "Here is where the five hundred dollars comes from, in plain steps. When you
> burn a ton of this particular coal, a high-carbon coal from the Pocahontas
> seams under McDowell, it releases about two and a half tons of carbon dioxide.
> Two point six one tons, to be exact... To put a dollar figure on the damage
> one ton of that carbon dioxide does, economists use a number called the social
> cost of carbon, which is their best estimate of the total harm... In 2023 the
> United States Environmental Protection Agency put that number at around a
> hundred and ninety dollars per ton. Multiply it out. Two point six one tons of
> carbon dioxide, times a hundred and ninety dollars of damage per ton, comes to
> roughly four hundred and ninety-six dollars."

B spells out the multiplicand, the multiplier, the product, the EPA as source,
and the 2023 date. A states the inputs and the answer and moves on. This is the
clearest case of "walking the reader step by step" vs. asserting a conclusion.
(Note also B introduces a specific figure A lacks — "Pocahontas seams" and
"2.61 tons" — covered in Section 1.)

### Technique 3: Sentence length and one-idea-per-sentence structure

A's signature is the long, accreting, multi-clause sentence. The opening is the
purest example:
> A: "The transaction you completed a moment ago is the most reliable purchase
> you will ever make, and you made it for nothing, and you will make it again in
> a few seconds, and you will go on making it tens of thousands of times today
> without once thinking of it as a purchase at all."

B breaks the same opening beat into short declaratives:
> B: "The honest answer is nothing. You did not pay for the air. You will not be
> billed for it. No one tracks how much you use. There is no meter on your chest,
> no monthly statement, no late fee if you breathe more than your share."

Same content (no charge, no meter, habitual), but A runs it as one breath while
B chops it into seven sentences. Another pair, on the diamond/water intuition —
which B adds as a teaching scaffold A never uses:
> B: "The cheap stuff is the throwaway stuff. The expensive stuff is the precious
> stuff. That is how prices teach us to think. A diamond is dear because a
> diamond is rare. Water is cheap because water is everywhere."

A reaches the same point through compression and inversion:
> A: "Cheap is what a thing is when it is plentiful and somewhat valued, like
> sand or salt. The air is something else."

### Technique 4: De-Latinized / less ornate vocabulary

B systematically swaps abstract or elevated diction for concrete plain words.

- A: "the great free atmosphere" / "a bubble of human life with a hard shell
  between it and the void." → B: "a dome or a buried base, with a hard shell
  between the people inside and the dead vacuum outside."
- A: "narcotic under pressure, clouding the mind like wine; oxygen itself... turns
  toxic." → B: "the ordinary nitrogen in air starts to act like a drug, fogging
  the mind, and the oxygen itself... turns harmful." ("narcotic"→"act like a
  drug"; "toxic"→"harmful".)
- A: "the carrying-away of carbon" stays in both, but A pairs it with "the
  stability of the climate that lets a farmer plant in confidence"; B renders it
  "A climate stable enough to plant by, the kind of weather you can build a life
  and a calendar around."
- A: "the priceless has become the most expensive thing in the room." → B: "the
  priceless things become the most expensive things there are."
- A frequently uses "the margin" as a bare technical noun ("price measures
  scarcity at the margin"). B almost always renders margin as "edge": "there is
  no edge... no next-hard-to-get unit." The "edge" metaphor is B's plain-language
  substitute for the economist's "margin," carried consistently throughout.

### Technique 5: Asserting a conclusion (A) vs. walking the reasoning (B)

Beyond the carbon arithmetic, the pattern recurs at the price-is-scarcity move.

A asserts the proposition and trusts it to land:
> "But price does not measure worth. Price measures scarcity at the margin. It
> tells you what the next unit costs..."

B builds it up as a derivation with a visible mechanism ("the edge"):
> "A price is not a measure of how much something is worth to you. A price is a
> measure of scarcity. It is the number that appears at the edge, where the next
> unit is hard to come by and someone has to decide whether it is worth the
> trouble. When something is scarce, the edge is sharp, and the price is high.
> When something is everywhere, there is no edge... And so the price falls not
> to a low number but to nothing at all."

Same at the renewable/non-renewable beat. A: "A renewable resource poses a
question of rate." B: "With a renewable thing, the question honest accounting
asks is a question of rate. How fast are you taking, compared to how fast it
grows back?" — B poses the question explicitly as a question.

### Technique 6: Dropped flourishes, inversions, and rhetorical devices

B strips the literary apparatus A leans on:

- **Sentence inversions.** A: "Cheap is what a thing is when it is plentiful..."
  (predicate-fronted). B never inverts; it would write "A thing is cheap when..."
- **The assayer's-scale conceit.** A opens the price discussion with a historical
  metaphor: "We trust it the way an earlier age trusted the assayer's scale."
  B cuts the conceit entirely.
- **Aphoristic compression.** A: "The zero is not a verdict that the air is
  worthless. The zero is a confession that the instrument has failed." B
  preserves the idea but unspools it: "So the zero on the tag cannot be a verdict
  on its worth. The zero is saying something else entirely, and most of us have
  never stopped to ask what."
- **The mask/face image.** A states "the mask of abundance" as a named mechanism.
  B adds an explicit gloss that A trusts the reader to infer: "It wears a price
  of zero like a mask, and we mistake the mask for the face."
- **"a corpse who didn't know it yet."** A's sharpest line on the asteroid miner:
  "a miner who extracts every gram and keeps nothing back has not been efficient,
  he has been a corpse who didn't know it yet." B softens to functional prose:
  "To strip the ship of its reserves in order to carry a little more iron would
  be to win the extraction and die of it." (B keeps the idea, loses the blade.)

### Technique 7: Where B arguably goes TOO plain (honest read)

- **Over-glossing common words.** "Residual just means what is left over" and the
  in-line definitions of "foreclose" and "restitution" risk talking down to a
  reader who already knows them. For Aeon's readership these are condescending.
- **Repetition as reinforcement tips into padding.** B repeats "The hundred
  dollars buys the present its tank of gas. It does not buy the barrel back. Keep
  that line. We are going to need it." — a mnemonic device that an Aeon editor
  would likely flag as hand-holding. A states the same gap once, elegantly:
  "The price is struck for the present; the loss runs forever."
- **Lost cadence at the close.** A's ending earns its force through accumulation
  and rhythm ("the ones downstream and downwind and down the years, and an honest
  ledger is finally only the form that owing takes when a civilization decides to
  grow up and pay attention"). B's close is clear but flatter: "Take a breath. It
  is still free. Whether it stays that way is up to us." — effective, but it
  trades the literary register's resonance for a slogan.
- **Editorial throat-clearing.** B adds connective scaffolding A omits: "So now
  we have our instrument," "Let me build the idea up one piece at a time," "Keep
  that line. We are going to need it." These guide a lay reader but read as
  visible machinery in a literary venue.

### Content present in B but ABSENT (or only implicit) in A — what B ADDED

1. **The diamond/water price intuition.** B: "A diamond is dear because a diamond
   is rare. Water is cheap because water is everywhere." A has no diamond example;
   it uses "sand or salt" in a different framing.
2. **The Pocahontas-seam specificity in the carbon arithmetic.** B: "a high-carbon
   coal from the Pocahontas seams under McDowell... Two point six one tons, to be
   exact, which is a bit above the national average for coal because this seam
   runs especially rich in carbon." A says only "roughly two and a half tons."
   B's 2.61t is a specific figure A does not carry.
3. **The 2023 EPA attribution.** B sources the $190/ton figure to "the United
   States Environmental Protection Agency" in "2023." A says "the kind of social
   cost that careful estimates now reach, on the order of a hundred and ninety
   dollars a ton" — no agency, no year. (Verification flag: the author should
   confirm the 2.61t and the EPA-2023 attribution against the spec, since these
   are figures B introduces that A does not.)
4. **The explicit multiplication shown ("comes to roughly four hundred and
   ninety-six dollars").** A jumps to "near five hundred and ten dollars" without
   showing the product.
5. **The in-line definition of "discount rate."** Added in B, absent in A.
6. **The colony "fairness falls out of the engineering" passage.** B adds an
   entire reflective paragraph: "There is something almost gentle hidden in this
   hard arrangement... The colony is fair not because its people are better than
   us but because its walls will not let them be worse." A has nothing equivalent;
   A's colony section ends on "Abundance is the luxury of a planet with an
   atmosphere to spare, and the colony has spent that luxury at the airlock."
7. **The "Earth itself is the largest barrel" framing.** B: "The Earth itself is a
   non-renewable resource on a long enough clock... It is the largest barrel of
   all, and it does not grow back either." A reaches the same horizon (Sun
   brightening, 500M–1B yr, 7.5B yr red giant) but does NOT make the
   Earth-as-barrel identity explicit.
8. **Numerous connective/glossing asides** ("Residual just means what is left
   over"; "The word foreclose means..."; "So now we have our instrument").

### Content in A but trimmed/compressed in B — what B effectively CUT

1. **The assayer's-scale historical conceit** (A's price-as-instrument opener) —
   gone in B.
2. **The Weitzman fat-tail is in BOTH** — present in A ("the small but real chance
   of catastrophe so large that no ordinary cost-benefit sum can contain it") and
   B ("a fat tail of disaster that no average estimate captures"). Not cut.
3. **A's worked discount example with both rates** — A: "a hundred dollars of
   benefit a century out is worth only about twenty-five dollars today at Stern's
   rate and barely a dollar and a half at Nordhaus's." B keeps the conceptual
   point but DROPS this specific two-rate numerical illustration.
4. **The "corpse who didn't know it yet" line** and several of A's sharpest
   aphorisms (see Section 2, Technique 6) — softened or removed.
5. **A's Sackmann / Schröder-and-Smith attributions** for the solar-physics
   figures. A names the astronomers ("the astronomers Sackmann and her
   colleagues"; "as Schröder and Smith reckoned"). B drops the named citations,
   rendering it "ordinary stellar physics" without attribution.
6. **A's "fifteen audiences"-style compression of the saturation-diving cost into
   the reclaim-rig irony** ("grown engineers build machinery to keep it from
   being wasted") — B keeps the reclaim-rig fact but loses the ironic framing.

Net: B cuts very little *substance* (one numerical discount illustration, the
astronomer attributions, several flourishes). It cuts mostly *literary apparatus*
and *citation specificity*, while adding *teaching scaffolding*, *one reflective
passage* (colony fairness), and *more granular arithmetic*.

## SECTION 3 — Where the +2,330 words went

Attribution of the extra ~2,330 words, in rough descending order:

1. **Step-by-step reasoning overhead (~40%, ~900w).** The single largest
   contributor. B derives where A asserts: the price-is-scarcity build-up, the
   renewable/non-renewable "question of rate" derivation, the carbon-arithmetic
   walkthrough, the asteroid "two faces of one rule" unpacking. Each
   assert-vs-derive swap costs B several sentences.

2. **Term-introduction overhead (~20%, ~470w).** The "build intuition, then name,
   then gloss the name" pattern for cost severance, residual commons value,
   foreclosure bond, restitution bond, discount rate, value extraction. Each adds
   a setup clause and often a dictionary aside.

3. **Sentence-splitting / one-idea-per-sentence (~15%, ~350w).** Breaking A's long
   accreting sentences into short declaratives adds words through repeated
   subjects and connectives ("The honest answer is nothing. You did not pay for
   the air. You will not be billed for it." vs. A's single comma-spliced run).

4. **Added arithmetic granularity (~10%, ~230w).** The Pocahontas-seam detail,
   the 2.61t figure, the explicit multiplication, the EPA-2023 sourcing, the
   "$496" intermediate result.

5. **Net-new passages (~10%, ~230w).** The colony "fairness falls out of the
   engineering" paragraph and the "Earth is the largest barrel" framing are
   genuinely new content, not just re-explained A material.

6. **Connective scaffolding and reinforcement / mild repetition (~5%, ~120w).**
   "So now we have our instrument," "Keep that line. We are going to need it,"
   "Let me build the idea up one piece at a time." This is the only category that
   reads as padding rather than explanation — and it is small.

**Verdict on the length:** the +2,330w is overwhelmingly explanation, not
padding. ~90% is step-by-step reasoning, term-introduction, sentence-splitting,
and arithmetic detail — all legitimate plain-language work. Only the ~5%
reinforcement/connective category ("Keep that line") is arguably cuttable, and
even that serves a lay reader.

## SECTION 4 — Verdict

**For Aeon specifically (literary-philosophical venue): A wins, clearly.** Aeon's
readership expects and rewards the long accreting sentence, the earned aphorism,
the historical conceit (the assayer's scale), and citation specificity (named
astronomers). A's register *is* the house style. B's defining moves — "Residual
just means what is left over," "The word foreclose means to shut off a
possibility," "Keep that line. We are going to need it." — would read to an Aeon
editor as talking down. B also drops the astronomer attributions and the worked
two-rate discount example, both of which lend A the intellectual texture Aeon
prizes. Submitting B as-is to Aeon would underperform on the venue's own terms.

**For a general lay reader (or a trade-press / op-ed adaptation): B wins.** B is
genuinely more teachable. The carbon arithmetic is the decisive case: a lay
reader who would glide past A's "the carbon term alone comes to something near
five hundred and ten dollars" actually *follows* B's "Two point six one tons...
times a hundred and ninety dollars... comes to roughly four hundred and
ninety-six dollars." The "build intuition then name" pattern lands the four key
terms (cost severance, residual commons value, foreclosure/restitution bonds)
far more reliably for someone meeting them cold.

**Beat-level wins:**
- **McDowell carbon arithmetic (Beat 6): B wins outright,** even for Aeon. The
  step-by-step is more persuasive and more honest (it shows its work and sources
  the EPA figure). Recommend porting B's arithmetic walkthrough into A.
- **Open and close (Beats 1, 12): A wins.** A's breath-open and the "form that
  owing takes when a civilization decides to grow up" close carry literary force
  B cannot match. B's close ("Take a breath. It is still free. Whether it stays
  that way is up to us.") is clean but slogan-like.
- **Lineage of near-misses (Beat 5): A wins** on register; the discount-rate
  worked example and the philosophers' compression are stronger in A. But B's
  in-line "discount rate" gloss is worth borrowing for accessibility.
- **Colony (Beat 9): split.** A's "corpse who didn't know it yet" is sharper; B's
  "the colony is fair... because its walls will not let them be worse" is a
  genuine net-new idea worth keeping regardless of register.

**Recommended hybrid (strong candidate):** Keep A as the spine — its open, close,
lineage, and overall literary register suit Aeon. Then port in, from B, the
specific *mechanism and arithmetic* improvements that don't cost register:
1. B's carbon-arithmetic walkthrough (the 2.61t × $190 = $496 → ~$510 steps,
   with EPA-2023 sourcing) — this is a substance upgrade, not just a register one.
2. B's "the colony is fair because its walls will not let them be worse" reflection.
3. B's "Earth is the largest barrel" framing at the longest-horizon beat.
Discard B's dictionary-glosses ("Residual just means..."), the "Keep that line"
reinforcement, and the de-Latinized softenings that flatten A's best lines.

In short: **A is the Aeon submission; B is the source of three concrete
upgrades** (chiefly the carbon arithmetic). A plain-on-the-arithmetic,
literary-on-the-frame hybrid is the best of both — and the carbon-arithmetic port
is worth doing regardless of which register ships, since it strengthens the
essay's most quantitative claim.

**One flag for the author:** B introduces figures A lacks (2.61 tons CO2 per ton
of Pocahontas coal; $496 intermediate; EPA-2023 for $190/ton). Before porting,
confirm these against research/aeon-complete-essay-spec_2026-06-03.md — they are
present in B and absent in A, so one of the two drafts is carrying detail the
other's drafter either didn't have or chose to omit.
