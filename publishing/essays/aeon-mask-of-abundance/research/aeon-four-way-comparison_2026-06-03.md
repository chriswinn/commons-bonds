# The Mask of Abundance — Four-Way Comparative Read

Full-length read of all four complete drafts. Arms 1–3 are the economical
"Globally Inclusive Plain English" airlock-dive-in family. Arm 4 is the
older literary-philosophical benchmark (breath-open, richer register, and a
stale McDowell carbon figure). Quotations are verbatim.

---

## 1. OPENINGS, HEAD TO HEAD

### Arm 1 "seed-verbatim" (author-locked open)
> You are standing in an airlock, and the inner door will not open until you
> answer one question. How do you intend to pay for your air?
>
> The question is not cruel, and here it is not even strange. Inside the wall,
> the air is made. A machine splits it out of water, drawing on power that
> something had to generate, and pulls the carbon dioxide you breathe out back
> out of the room before it rises high enough to kill you. Every litre is
> counted, because every litre had to be produced. So before they let you into
> the only breathable space for a very long way in any direction, they need to
> know how your breathing will be paid for.
>
> You have no answer ready. You have never needed one. You have spent your
> whole life drawing breath with no meter and no bill and no thought, and
> somewhere along the way you came to believe that air is simply free. It is
> not. It never was.

### Arm 2 "empty-chamber" (self-generated open)
> The door behind you has already sealed. The door in front of you has not yet
> opened. You are standing in a small chamber with metal walls, and the only
> sound is the faint working of pumps somewhere below the floor. A panel beside
> the inner door is waiting for you. Before it will let you through, it asks you
> a single question, and the question is how you intend to pay for your air.
>
> You think, at first, that you have misheard. Then you understand that you
> have not. The habitat on the other side of that door has a fixed quantity of
> breathable atmosphere, manufactured at cost, scrubbed and recirculated and
> topped up by machines that run on power that someone has to generate. Every
> breath you take inside will be drawn from a shared supply, and every breath
> out will load that supply with carbon dioxide that other machines will have
> to strip away again before the next person can use it. None of this is free.
> None of it was ever going to be free. The chamber is simply the first place
> in your life that has bothered to tell you so.

### Arm 3 "clerk-and-queue" (self-generated open, payment tiers)
> The clerk at the airlock does not look up from the screen when you arrive.
> There is a queue behind you, and the door at your back has already sealed,
> and the door in front of you will not open until the question on the screen
> has an answer. The question is not your name. Your name is already on file.
> The question is how you intend to pay for your air.
>
> You have been on this station for three hours, half of them spent in transit,
> the rest spent learning that nothing here is the way you assumed it would be.
> You assumed air, for instance. You walked off the transfer craft breathing
> without thinking about it, the way you have breathed every minute of your
> life, and somewhere in a maintenance bay a compressor adjusted its output by
> a fraction of a percent to account for one more set of lungs. That adjustment
> has a number attached to it. The number is small, but it is not zero, and on
> this station nothing that is not zero is allowed to hide.

…and then the payment-tiers paragraph:
> You can pay by the breath, metered against an account, debited as you exhale.
> You can pay a flat residency rate that bundles your oxygen with your water
> and your share of the machines that pull carbon dioxide back out of the cabin
> atmosphere… You can, if you have arrived with nothing, sign an indenture
> against future labour and breathe on credit until the station decides whether
> to keep you.

### Arm 4 "literary benchmark" (breath-open)
> Breathe in. Now breathe out. You have just consumed something, and you have
> just emitted something, and no meter ran. No charge appeared. The transaction
> you completed a moment ago is the most reliable purchase you will ever make,
> and you made it for nothing, and you will make it again in a few seconds, and
> you will go on making it tens of thousands of times today without once
> thinking of it as a purchase at all. The air is free. We say this as if it
> were a fact about the air, the way we say that water is wet or that iron is
> heavy. It is not a fact about the air. It is a fact about us, about the only
> instrument we have built for telling worth, and about the strange blindness
> that instrument carries at its center.

### Which grabs hardest, and why

**Arm 1 grabs hardest** of the four for the Aeon-editor 3-second test. Two
short sentences, a hard stop, and a direct second-person question that is also
the essay's whole thesis in miniature: *"the inner door will not open until you
answer one question. How do you intend to pay for your air?"* There is no
scene-setting throat-clearing before the question lands; the question IS the
first beat. The author-locked phrasing is the most compressed and the most
arresting. The third paragraph's *"It is not. It never was."* is the strongest
single rhetorical snap in any of the four openings.

**Arm 4** is the most beautiful but the slowest to commit. *"Breathe in. Now
breathe out."* is a lovely somatic hook and the most "literary" opening, but it
spends its first three paragraphs on a meditation on *price as an instrument*
before any scene or stake arrives. For a long-form literary venue this is
earned; for a cold editor skim it asks for more patience than the airlock
openings. It opens on the abstraction (the instrument) and works toward the
image, where the airlock arms open on the image and work toward the abstraction.

**The three airlock dive-ins differ meaningfully in execution:**

- **Arm 1 (locked)** puts the question first and the mechanism second. It is
  the most economical and the most front-loaded. It trusts the reader to feel
  the strangeness without staging it. The cost: slightly less *scene* — you are
  "standing in an airlock" with no sensory texture around you.
- **Arm 2 (empty chamber)** stages the room before the question: *"The door
  behind you has already sealed… the only sound is the faint working of pumps
  somewhere below the floor."* This is more cinematic and gives the reader a
  body in a space, but it delays the thesis-question by four sentences. The
  *"You think, at first, that you have misheard"* beat is a nice human reaction
  that Arm 1 lacks. Net: warmer, slightly slower.
- **Arm 3 (clerk and queue)** is the most novelistic. It introduces a *person*
  (the clerk who does not look up), a *queue*, and the most concrete physical
  grounding of all — *"somewhere in a maintenance bay a compressor adjusted its
  output by a fraction of a percent to account for one more set of lungs."*
  That compressor detail is the single best image in any of the three openings:
  it makes the metering physically real and not merely asserted. But it pays
  for that richness with the payment-tiers paragraph (see below).

**On Arm 3's payment-tiers / indenture image specifically:**

This is the most consequential opening-level divergence. The tiers (*pay by the
breath / flat residency rate / sign an indenture against future labour*) are
**vivid and they do add a social dimension** — they make the airlock economy
feel like a real economy with classes of people in it, not a thought-experiment
abstraction. The "compressor adjusted its output for one more set of lungs"
detail is genuinely worth harvesting regardless of which base wins.

**But the indenture line is a real risk, and I judge it a net liability as
written.** The framework's actual subject is *honest accounting of severed
costs across time* — pricing the invisible, holding reserves. The indenture
image tilts the opening toward a *dystopia-of-inequality* theme (breathe on
credit, the station decides whether to keep you, the rich breathe and the poor
indenture) that the essay never actually develops or pays off. The body of Arm 3
does not return to indenture, class stratification, or who-pays-for-whose-air
as a distributive-justice problem in any developed way. So the opening writes a
check the essay does not cash: it primes the reader for a story about coercion
and inequality and then delivers a story about accounting and reserves. It also
risks the exact "stolen hours / villain framing" failure mode the project has
flagged before (the essay is explicitly *not a villain story* — see the Norway/
Niger Delta beat where every arm insists *"It is not a villain story"*). The
indenture image quietly imports a villain (the station that owns your breath)
into an opening for an essay whose whole discipline is no-villain architecture.

**Verdict on openings:** Arm 1's question-first compression is the strongest
spine. Arm 2's "misheard" human beat and Arm 3's compressor detail are the best
graftable fragments. Arm 3's tier-ladder, especially indenture, should be
dropped or radically softened. Arm 4's breath-open is the best *sensory* hook
and the best candidate for a one-line graft into a stronger base (see §6).

## 2. CLOSES, HEAD TO HEAD

### Arm 1 final paragraph
> Which brings you back to the air. Here, now, it is still free. No meter, no
> bill, no question at any door. That freedom is real and worth keeping, but it
> was never a law of nature. It is a condition we happen to enjoy, and
> conditions change. Whether the air stays free, whether the abundance holds
> long enough to go on hiding how much it is worth, is not written anywhere. It
> is still ours to decide. The mask comes off the moment we are willing to take
> it off, and the number underneath was always there.

The universality move in Arm 1 lives one paragraph earlier (the person/
community/corporation/government/colony ladder): *"A person can run it over a
single decision… A community can run it over the land and water it holds in
common. A corporation can run it over what it takes and what it leaves behind.
A government can run it over an entire national inheritance, as Norway has. And
a sealed colony on another world would have to run it every day, by the litre
and the breath… on this world or any other."* Then the final paragraph is a
clean, short breath tie-back. **This is the tightest close of the four** —
controlled, no overreach, lands on "the number underneath was always there,"
which rhymes with the opening's "It never was."

### Arm 2 final paragraph
> So breathe. The air is free, for now. The number was always there; the mask
> only kept us from reading it. Whether the freedom stays is not a fact about
> physics or a verdict already handed down. It is a thing we still get to
> decide, the way every honest reckoning is decided, by whether we are willing
> to see the cost while we still have the choice, and to keep the books before
> the airlock closes behind us and asks, too late, how we ever intended to pay.

Arm 2's universality ladder (person/community/corporation/government/colony) is
in the penultimate "Honest books" section and is the most fully written of the
three: *"A person can keep these books… A community can keep them… A corporation
can keep them… A government can keep them, as Norway did, setting aside the
value of a thing that will not return so that the future has a chair and a fund.
And a sealed colony on another world keeps them by necessity, billing every
breath."* The close itself is strong and brings the airlock back as a literal
threat (*"before the airlock closes behind us and asks, too late, how we ever
intended to pay"*) — the best *callback* close, tying the door image to the
stakes. Slightly longer and more rhetorically loaded than Arm 1.

### Arm 3 final paragraph
> Which returns us, at the very last, to the breath. Down here, today, the air
> is still free, in the only sense that matters, which is that no one charges
> you and no one could, because the supply is still so total that the worth has
> nowhere to show itself. The mask is still on. You can stand under the open sky
> and draw breath after breath and never once see the meter, because there is no
> wall and no vacuum and no clerk at any airlock asking how you intend to pay.
> That freedom is real, and it is precious, and it is the thing the whole long
> argument has been about. But it is free for now, and whether it stays free is
> not a fact of the universe. It is a thing we still get to decide, by whether
> we keep honest books on the air while we still can, or wait until a wall goes
> up somewhere and the bill arrives with every breath.

Arm 3's close brings back the *clerk* from its own opening (*"no clerk at any
airlock asking how you intend to pay"*), which is the most complete open-to-
close ring of the three. Its universality ladder ("A person, a community, a
corporation, a government, a colony on another world. One ledger, run at any
size, on this world or any other.") is the most *staccato* and quotable version
of the move. The close is a touch wordier than Arm 1's. The earlier sentence
*"It is not a movement and it has no enemies, no villains, only severed costs
and unkept books"* is a strong, harvestable line that explicitly defuses the
villain risk its own opening introduced.

### Arm 4 final paragraph
> And here is the part that is not in any ledger, the part that no instrument
> reads, which is that the writing-down is a choice. The costs are real whether
> we see them or not; the bill drifts downstream whether or not anyone catches
> it; the barrel is gone whether or not anyone priced its permanence. What is
> still open, what we still get to decide, is whether we keep flying blind or
> learn at last to keep honest books. Whether the freedom of the next breath,
> and the breath after that, and the breath of someone not yet born and far
> away, stays free, or quietly becomes a thing that was spent without anyone
> noticing the price. We owe that breath to people who are not at the table,
> across distance and across time, the ones downstream and downwind and down
> the years, and an honest ledger is finally only the form that owing takes when
> a civilization decides to grow up and pay attention. The mask can come off.
> Whether we take it off, and what we do once we can see, is still, for now,
> ours to choose.

**Arm 4 has the richest close** — *"an honest ledger is finally only the form
that owing takes when a civilization decides to grow up and pay attention"* is
the best single sentence in any of the four closes. It also carries the
"breath of someone not yet born and far away… downstream and downwind and down
the years" cadence, which is the most affecting articulation of the cross-time
obligation anywhere in the corpus. Its weakness is length and a faint
abstraction (it closes on "owing" and "grow up and pay attention" rather than
on the concrete air/meter image), and it lacks the airlock-door callback the
airlock arms get for free.

**Which close lands best:** For pure craft, **Arm 4**. For discipline-fit +
ring-composition + economy, **Arm 1** (tightest) and **Arm 2** (best door
callback) are essentially tied. Best build: keep Arm 1 or Arm 2's architecture
and graft Arm 4's "the form that owing takes when a civilization decides to
grow up and pay attention" sentence (see §6).

## 3. THE MIDDLE ARC — BEAT BY BEAT

The shared 12-beat spine is present and in the same order in all four. Beat-by-
beat, with the standout developer named and quoted.

### Beat: sealed-world anchors (divers / Biosphere 2 / ISS)
All four hit the three anchors with the same facts. **Arm 1 develops the
synthesis best** with its closing drumbeat:
> Three real sealed worlds, three different scales, one identical result. The
> diver's helium, the dome's vanished oxygen and dead bees, the station's
> twenty-one-thousand-dollar water. In every case a thing that is free on the
> open Earth becomes the most expensive thing there is the moment the planet
> stops providing it. The price was always there. We simply never had to look
> at it, because the bill went to the planet, and the planet does not send
> statements.

**Arm 4 has the best individual images** — the diver beat closes on *"the same
element that floats a child's balloon becomes, behind a seal and under
pressure, a metered utility with an hourly rate, and grown engineers build
machinery to keep it from being wasted."* That child's-balloon contrast is the
most vivid single rendering of the helium point in the corpus and is worth
harvesting. Arm 4's Biosphere line *"could not give its keepers the thing the
open Earth gives six billion people without a thought"* is also stronger than
the plain arms' equivalents. **Harvest flag:** Arm 4's "child's balloon" and
"buy back the one commodity it had taken most for granted" framings.

### Beat: cost-severance mechanism (cost severance / value extraction / RCV)
**Arm 4 develops this best**, and it has content the others lack: the
**two-directions-of-severance** distinction (space vs. time):
> The severance has two directions… There is severance across space, the
> factory and the fishermen… And then there is severance across time, which is
> far stranger and far harder to argue about, because the injured party is not
> present, has not been born, cannot point at anything, cannot sue, cannot vote,
> cannot even be named… the years have no courtroom.

That "the years have no courtroom" framing, and the explicit space/time split,
is a genuine **argument-step the economical arms do not make** and is worth
grafting. Arm 4 also names the externality-word critique most sharply: *"the
word externality has always been too mild, too geometric, as if the cost had
simply rolled to the outside of a tidy circle. It did not roll. It was cut."*
Arm 3 has a weaker version of the same move (*"economists have a tired phrase
for one corner of it, but the phrase has gone soft with use"*). **Harvest flag:**
Arm 4's space/time severance split + "It did not roll. It was cut."

### Beat: $100 barrel + renewable-vs-liquidated
All four are strong and nearly identical in logic. **Arm 4 adds a structural
argument the others only imply** — the explicit account of *why the market is
structurally unable* to find the number:
> The future cannot bid against the present for the barrel. It has no agent in
> the room, no representative at the margin, no dollar to put down… The market
> is not failing to do its job. It is doing its job exactly, which is to clear
> the present against itself, and the residual commons value is simply the worth
> that no present party was positioned to defend.

This "no agent in the room / clearing the present against itself" paragraph is
the single most analytically valuable passage unique to one arm. **Strong
harvest flag.** Arm 1's renewable/liquidated contrast has the best aphoristic
compression: *"A renewing resource taken too fast is a debt you can repay by
slowing down. A barrel of oil burned is a debt that cannot be repaid by anyone,
ever."* Arm 1 also uniquely carries the **option-value sub-beat** as its own
developed paragraph (*"the barrel has a worth simply in remaining a barrel… you
close every door it could have opened for everyone after you, and you close
them permanently"*), which Arms 2–4 fold away. **Harvest flag:** Arm 1's
explicit option-value paragraph.

### Beat: lineage of near-misses (Marx / Ostrom / Daly / Hartwick / Rawls /
Sandel / Parfit / Stern-Nordhaus-Weitzman)
All four run the full lineage. **Arm 4 develops the discount-rate close best**,
and uniquely supplies a **worked numerical example** of discounting:
> You can discount a future harm until it nearly vanishes, the way a hundred
> dollars of benefit a century out is worth only about twenty-five dollars
> today at Stern's rate and barely a dollar and a half at Nordhaus's. But you
> cannot discount away the present fact that the barrel is gone…

That concrete "$100 → ~$25 at Stern / ~$1.50 at Nordhaus" worked example makes
the abstract discount-rate point land for a lay reader, and **no economical arm
has it.** Strong harvest candidate (verify the arithmetic before grafting).
The economical arms' versions of the same point are excellent in prose but
abstract: Arm 2's *"You cannot make a permanently missing thing matter less by
pushing it further into the future, because it is missing at every point in the
future equally"* is the cleanest plain-language statement of the survives-the-
discount-rate idea. Arm 3 adds the memorable *"It priced the smoke. It did not
price the missing barrel."* Arm 1 adds Sandel's bridge sentence cleanly.
**Harvest flags:** Arm 4's worked discount-example; Arm 3's "priced the smoke /
missing barrel"; Arm 2's "missing at every point in the future equally."

### Beat: McDowell (run backward)
All four run the arithmetic. **Arm 4 develops the rhetoric best** with the
"loan against the future" framing the economical arms lack:
> The honest ledger says the coal was never four dollars. It was always
> something near five hundred, and the difference was not a discount. It was a
> loan, taken out silently against the future and against the lungs and the
> climate and the unborn, a loan that nobody signed for…

That "not a discount; a loan nobody signed for" image is a strong harvest — BUT
Arm 4 here carries the **stale figures** (carbon ~$510, total ~$524). The graft
must port the *rhetoric* onto the *corrected numbers* (carbon ~$496, total
~$510). Arm 1's plain version is disciplined and clean: *"the arithmetic is not
exotic. It is multiplication. The only difficulty was ever the willingness to
do it."* **Harvest flag:** Arm 4's "loan nobody signed for" framing, re-based
to corrected figures.

### Beat: foreclosure / restitution bonds + Darity
All four name both instruments and the Darity/Mullen restitution connection,
and all four observe the coercion-deferral discipline (the "line the framework
keeps on purpose"). **Arms 3 and 4 add Ta-Nehisi Coates** as the moral-
architecture companion to Darity's economics; **Arms 1 and 2 do not name
Coates.** Arm 1's statement of the discipline is the most quotable: *"Honest
accounting includes being honest about whose accounting it is not."* Arm 3's is
nearly as good: *"Honest accounting includes knowing which entries are not yours
to make."* **Harvest flag:** the Coates citation (verify it is wanted /
consent-clear) and Arm 1's "whose accounting it is not" line.

### Beat: Norway / Niger Delta (two architectures)
All four are strong and carry the same facts (Ekofisk 1969; fund 1990 → renamed
2006; 2001 fiscal rule ~3%; >$2T / >$390k per citizen; Stoltenberg/Bondevik/
Solberg/Støre; Saro-Wiwa + 8 executed 1995; UN Ogoniland ~$1B / 25–30 yrs). All
four explicitly disavow the villain reading. **Arm 1 develops the architecture-
not-program point best:**
> The lesson is not that oil is wrong. The lesson is that when the cost of an
> extraction outlives the sale, someone carries it, and the only question that
> matters is whether anyone decided in advance who that would be and set
> anything aside to meet it.

Arm 3's version (*"it is an argument about what you do with the residual value…
This is not an argument against extraction. Norway extracted enormous quantities
of oil"*) is equally clean. No arm has a unique fact the others lack here; this
beat is a wash on content, slight edge to Arm 1 on phrasing.

### Beat: off-world colony as working economy
This is the integration beat and the four diverge in placement. Arms 1–3 put it
late (after Norway), as designed; Arm 4 makes it a titled two-part section
("The colony economy" + "The asteroid miner"). **Arm 4 develops it best** and
has the most striking distributive line:
> Abundance is the luxury of a planet with an atmosphere to spare, and the
> colony has spent that luxury at the airlock.

But **Arm 1 has unique content the others lack and that is worth harvesting**:
it is the only arm that turns the colony beat into an explicit *social-contract*
argument:
> On Earth a poor person and a rich person breathe the same free air… Inside
> the seal that is no longer true… the question of who pays for whose breathing
> becomes a real question… It might answer it by giving every resident a
> baseline ration… It might answer it by metering everything… the colony's
> answer to it is, quite literally, its social contract, written in the units of
> air and water and energy that the planet used to provide for free. The mask of
> abundance… is the absence of the one luxury that let Earth's societies leave
> their deepest distributional questions permanently unasked.

This is the single richest *idea* unique to one arm in the whole middle — and
notably, it is where Arm 1 *earns honestly* the social dimension that Arm 3's
indenture opening only gestured at. **Strong harvest flag.** (It is also the
right home for any softened version of Arm 3's tier idea: the distributive
question belongs HERE, developed, not in the cold open.)

### Beat: asteroid miner / take-freely + hold-reserves
All four nail the surprise turn (extract freely where severance is zero; reserve
absolutely). **Arm 4's compression is best:**
> a miner who extracts every gram and keeps nothing back has not been
> efficient, he has been a corpse who didn't know it yet.

Arm 1's *"take all the iron you want and do not touch the last tank of air"* is
the cleanest plain rendering. Arm 3's *"The miner who takes everything and
reserves nothing is not free. The miner is dead. Honest accounting is the
difference."* is the most staccato. Content is equivalent; Arm 4 wins on the
line. **Harvest flag:** Arm 4's "corpse who didn't know it yet" (verify
register fit — it is the most colloquial line in Arm 4).

### Beat: whatever-economy (Raworth / Ostrom / granaries)
All four carry Raworth + Ostrom + ancient granaries (China ever-normal granary,
Egypt) and all four self-flag the no-invented-particulars discipline (*"I will
not invent particulars I do not have"*). Content is a wash. Arm 4's *"It is the
set of books underneath whatever flag you have already chosen"* is the best line.

### Beat: longest-horizon reserves (Sun brightening → off-world as deep reserve)
All four carry the ~500M–1B yr habitability loss and ~7.5B yr red-giant
engulfment. **Discipline divergence:** Arm 4 **attributes the figures to named
sources** — *"as the astronomers Sackmann and her colleagues worked out in the
early 1990s"* and *"as Schröder and Smith reckoned."* Arms 1–3 give the figures
unattributed. The attribution is a strength IF the citations are correct
(Sackmann et al. and Schröder & Smith are real sources for these figures), but
it is a fabrication-risk surface to verify before grafting. Arm 2's *"These are
not round numbers chosen for effect; they come from the work of astronomers
modeling the Sun's evolution"* is the safe middle path. **Harvest flag (with
verification):** Arm 4's source attributions, if confirmed accurate.

## 4. VOICE / REGISTER

### Paired renderings of the same idea

**The price-measures-scarcity-at-the-margin idea:**
- *Economical (Arm 2):* "A price is a measure of scarcity at the margin. Where
  there is no scarcity, there is no margin, and so there is no number, and the
  most valuable things we have post a permanent and misleading zero."
- *Literary (Arm 4):* "Price does not measure worth. Price measures scarcity at
  the margin… The trouble is that we have mistaken the reading for the thing
  read. We have come to believe that what has no price has no worth, when often
  the truth is the reverse: the thing has nothing but worth, distributed so
  completely, so far past any margin, that no market can find an edge to charge
  at."

**The helium / breath-cost idea:**
- *Economical (Arm 1):* "the helium component of a single hour of breathing can
  reach up to about two hundred dollars an hour for the helium alone… because
  letting it go is letting money go."
- *Literary (Arm 4):* "the same element that floats a child's balloon becomes,
  behind a seal and under pressure, a metered utility with an hourly rate, and
  grown engineers build machinery to keep it from being wasted."

**The discount-rate-survives idea:**
- *Economical (Arm 2):* "You cannot make a permanently missing thing matter less
  by pushing it further into the future, because it is missing at every point in
  the future equally."
- *Literary (Arm 4):* "an absence is not a stream of future harms to be
  discounted year by year toward zero; it is a fact about now, a hole in the
  present world's stock of the irreplaceable."

### What the literary register (Arm 4) does that the economical arms do not

1. **Concrete sensory anchors at analytical pivots** — the child's balloon, the
   "hole in the present world's stock," "grown engineers build machinery." The
   literary arm reliably gives the reader an image at the exact moment the
   argument turns abstract. The economical arms state the turn cleanly but
   without a handhold.
2. **Aphoristic closers on paragraphs** — "It did not roll. It was cut." / "the
   years have no courtroom." / "a corpse who didn't know it yet." / "the form
   that owing takes when a civilization decides to grow up and pay attention."
   These are the lines a reader quotes. The economical arms have a few
   (Arm 1's "the planet does not send statements"; Arm 3's "It priced the
   smoke") but fewer per page.
3. **Extra argument-steps** the economical arms compress away — the space/time
   severance split, the "no agent in the room" market-structure paragraph, the
   worked $100→$25→$1.50 discount example. These are not just prettier; they are
   *more complete reasoning*.

Is it worth wanting for Aeon? **Yes, selectively.** Aeon's house style rewards
exactly this kind of image-at-the-pivot and quotable-closer writing. The
economical arms risk reading, in stretches, like very lucid policy explanation
rather than an *essay*. The fix is not to switch base register — it is to graft
Arm 4's best images and argument-steps into an economical base (§6).

### What the economical voice gains

- **Clarity and inclusivity** — jargon-free, idiom-free, no regionalism, no
  cultural-reference cost. "Globally Inclusive Plain English" genuinely reads as
  translatable and as accessible to a non-native or non-specialist reader. Arm 4
  has idioms ("flying blind," "grow up," "corpse who didn't know it yet," "won
  his own Nobel") that are mild but are exactly the kind of thing the discipline
  targets.
- **Pace** — the economical arms move faster through the lineage and the two-
  architecture beat because they do not stop to render. For a 9,000-word essay
  that is real protection against mid-essay drag.
- **Front-loaded thesis** — Arm 1 especially gets the stakes in front of the
  reader in two sentences.

### Where the economical arms read flat / where Arm 4 reads overwritten

- **Flat:** The economical sealed-worlds synthesis ("Three real sealed worlds,
  three different scales, one identical result") is clean but the *individual*
  anchors in Arms 1–3 lack the balloon/six-billion-people lift Arm 4 gives them.
  The economical colony beat in Arms 2–3 ("This is not a dystopia and it is not

  operate") states its thesis twice in nearly identical words across the arms and
  reads slightly templated.
- **Overwritten (Arm 4):** the three-paragraph price-as-instrument prologue
  before any scene; "a creature living inside the biosphere"; occasional piling
  of clauses in the closes. The "corpse who didn't know it yet" line, while
  vivid, is tonally the loudest moment in the essay and may be a half-step too
  colloquial for the register elsewhere.
