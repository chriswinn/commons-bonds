# Globally Inclusive Plain English — voice-capture findings

## The voice standard (author's, articulated 2026-06-03)

Origin: 5.5 years as an IBM tech-sales consultant across North America + APAC, where the
house discipline was always to use **jargon-free, non-colloquial, non-regionally-specific**
language, so that everyone in a conversation understands without ever feeling awkward about
a word they do not know. It is now how the author speaks and writes by default. This is the
target voice for publisher-facing prose, and the seed standard for the book-chapter redrafts.

**Globally Inclusive Plain English = five rules:**
1. **Jargon-free** — every term of art introduced in plain words BEFORE it is named
   (build-then-name). e.g. "Someone creates a cost and someone else pays it, with no way
   to send the bill back. I will call this cost severance."
2. **Idiom-free** — no figure of speech whose meaning is not literal. Say the literal thing.
3. **Non-colloquial** — even, neutral register; no casual filler ("stuff," "kind of"
   [as filler], "sit with it"), no chatty reader-buttonholing.
4. **Non-regional / internationally legible** — no country-specific reference or unit
   assumed; SI-legible units. (Place-specific CASES — McDowell, Norway, Niger Delta — are
   subject matter and stay; the rule governs the prose's idiom/reference layer, not facts.)
5. **Plain but not dumbed-down** — short clear sentences, everyday words, reasoning walked
   step by step, but a calm professional register carrying sophisticated ideas. Clear, not
   chatty; inclusive, not simplistic.

NOT folksy or conversational. The earlier "explain it to a friend over coffee" framing
(the `plain-language-colony-*` arms) produced the jargon-free + build-then-name half
correctly but injected colloquialisms/idioms ("for the price of a sandwich," "flying blind,"
"sit with it," "the cheap stuff is the throwaway stuff") that violate rules 2-4. The
globally-inclusive target is more neutral and slightly more formal than folksy-plain, not
less.

Calibration point — RATIFIED 2026-06-03: **natural contractions are allowed** (they aid
readability and ESL clarity when unambiguous). The stricter no-contractions variant was
considered and declined. This is now part of the standard.

## Calibration 2 — TRUST THE READER + ECONOMY (author, 2026-06-03, after reading the draft)

The first draft hit the inclusivity target (rules 1-4) but overshot on a separate axis:
it **over-explains and over-narrates**. Two distinct corrections, now part of the standard:

- **Trust the reader.** Inclusivity = removing jargon / idiom / regional barriers. It does
  NOT mean explaining things a normal reader already knows from ordinary life and film
  (e.g. what an airlock is). Reserve build-then-name for GENUINE terms of art (cost
  severance, residual commons value, social cost of carbon); never gloss common words.
  Over-explaining familiar concepts reads as condescension, not inclusion.
- **Economy.** Make each point as efficiently as it can be made. Cut method-narration and
  throat-clearing: "the misunderstanding I want to take apart slowly," "I want it on the
  table from the start," "I want to be precise here." One strong sentence beats three
  careful ones. The author's anchor: the Biosphere 2 content was all valuable, "but it took
  a lot of writing to get there."

**Root-cause note (connects to the min-floor / no-max finding):** much of the inflation was
the **8,500 floor interacting with the plain overlay**. "Explain plainly enough to reach
8,500 words" incentivizes spelling-everything-out + connective filler. The min-floor mechanic
is right when richness is genuinely wanted, but for CAPTURING A VOICE — and for book chapters,
where the right DENSITY matters more than any word count — economy and a high floor pull in
opposite directions. Refined standard: globally inclusive **and economical**, substance-driven
in length (likely meaningfully SHORTER than 9,962w), not floor-driven.

Next step (afternoon 2026-06-03): a tightened globally-inclusive pass — reader-trusting,
throat-clearing removed, no high floor — to land the voice at its natural (shorter) density.

## Key meta-finding: idiom-free needs its OWN scan/gate

A careful drafter briefed explicitly to avoid idioms still left ~5 genuine residual idioms in
~10,000 words (95%+ idiom-free, far better than the folksy arms, but not zero), because the
most deep-baked idioms do not REGISTER as idioms to the writer producing them. Residuals found:

- L31 "I want it **on the table** from the start"
- L71 "**Money changed hands**"
- L83 "not to **settle scores**"
- L137 "No government, **of any color**" (idiom for political affiliation; also awkward near
  the race discussion) — FIXED 2026-06-03 to "of any party" per author.
- L187 "you are **deciding in the dark**" (one line after correctly replacing "flying blind"
  with the literal "moving without sight" — an inconsistency)
- (Borderline: L137 "raided the principal." The L173/177/181/211 "in the dark" are LITERAL —
  the asteroid — and fine.)

**Implication for the book-chapter redraft phase:** treat "globally inclusive" as a
first-class gate with a dedicated idiom scan (like the em-dash gate), not as a sub-clause of
general voice-polish. An idiom checklist + grep scan should run on every globally-inclusive
draft, because the writer cannot reliably self-catch the idioms most baked into their own
English. Candidate scan seeds: on the table, changed hands, settle scores, of any color/colour,
in the dark / deciding in the dark, flying blind, run off a cliff, at the end of the day,
the lion's share, boils down, hand in hand, down the road, a stone's throw, raided.
