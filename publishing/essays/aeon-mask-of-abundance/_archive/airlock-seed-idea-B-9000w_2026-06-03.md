# The Mask of Abundance

The clerk at the airlock does not look up from the screen when you arrive. There is a queue behind you, and the door at your back has already sealed, and the door in front of you will not open until the question on the screen has an answer. The question is not your name. Your name is already on file. The question is how you intend to pay for your air.

You have been on this station for three hours, half of them spent in transit, the rest spent learning that nothing here is the way you assumed it would be. You assumed air, for instance. You walked off the transfer craft breathing without thinking about it, the way you have breathed every minute of your life, and somewhere in a maintenance bay a compressor adjusted its output by a fraction of a percent to account for one more set of lungs. That adjustment has a number attached to it. The number is small, but it is not zero, and on this station nothing that is not zero is allowed to hide.

So the clerk waits. You can pay by the breath, metered against an account, debited as you exhale. You can pay a flat residency rate that bundles your oxygen with your water and your share of the machines that pull carbon dioxide back out of the cabin atmosphere before it climbs to a level that would make you slow and stupid and finally dead. You can, if you have arrived with nothing, sign an indenture against future labour and breathe on credit until the station decides whether to keep you. What you cannot do is the thing you have done every day until now, which is to take the air as a given, a background condition, a fact of the universe rather than a line on a ledger.

You choose the residency rate, because the alternative is to feel your own breathing as a slow withdrawal from an account, and no one can live that way for long. The door opens. You step through into a corridor that smells faintly of machine oil and growing things, and you understand, with a clarity that will not leave you for the rest of your stay, that the air you have stopped noticing your whole life was never the free thing you took it for. It was only ever uncounted. Down on the planet you came from, no one ever sent the bill, because the supply was so total that no one could find the edge of it to charge at. Out here the edge is the wall of the habitat, and the wall is very close, and the bill arrives with every breath.

You will get used to this. People do. Within a week you will not think about your breathing any more than you did at home, because the residency rate has folded the cost into a number you pay once a month and then forget. But you will never again be able to believe, in the old unexamined way, that the air was free. You have seen the meter. You know that the freedom you grew up inside was not the absence of cost. It was the presence of so much value, spread so evenly across so many lungs, that the cost fell below the threshold where anyone bothered to write it down.

Now come home.

Step off the craft on the planet you were born on and the first thing that happens is that no one asks you anything. You walk out under an open sky and the air is simply there, in quantities no meter could ever sensibly track, free in the only way that matters, which is that nobody charges you and nobody could. The airlock question, which felt so reasonable an hour into orbit, now seems absurd. Pay for your air? You might as well be charged for the light of the sun or the pull of the ground beneath your feet. The whole apparatus of the metered breath looks, from down here, like a cruelty that scarcity forced on people who had lost the luxury of not counting.

And then the harder thought arrives, the one the trip was really for. The cost did not vanish when you came home. It is still there. The machine that pulls your exhaled carbon dioxide out of the air still runs. It is simply that here the machine is the whole living surface of the planet, the forests and the oceans and the soil and the slow chemistry of rock, working without a meter and without an invoice, and so you never see the cost as a cost. You see it as nothing at all. And because you see it as nothing, you behave as though it were nothing, and you put more carbon into the air than the machine can take back, and the bill for that does not disappear either. It is paid. It is just paid by someone else, somewhere else, or by someone who is not yet born and cannot object.

That is the subject of this essay. Not the air on the station, which is only a way of seeing, but the air down here, and the fresh water, and the stable and livable climate, and the planet's patient unmetered work of taking back the carbon we breathe and burn into the sky. These are the commons. They carry no price. And the reason they carry no price is not that they are worthless but very nearly the opposite. They are so saturated with value, so completely distributed, that the instrument we use to measure worth cannot get a grip on them. A price is a measurement of scarcity at the margin. It tells you what one more unit costs when units are limited. The commons have no margin. There is no one more unit of breathable atmosphere to bid for, because the atmosphere is simply there, all of it, all at once, for everyone. So the price reads zero, and we misread the zero. We take it as a verdict on worth when it is only a verdict on abundance.

Call this the mask of abundance. A thing of immense value, made invisible by the very completeness of its supply. And the danger of the mask is not the one we usually fear. We worry about treating precious things as cheap, about underpaying for what we should value, the way a society underpays its nurses or undervalues its topsoil. But underpriced things are at least visible. They have a price, and a wrong price can be argued about and corrected. The commons are worse off than that. They have no price at all, and a thing with no price is not merely undervalued. It is invisible to the only instrument we have agreed to trust. We do not see it as cheap. We do not see it.

## The sealed worlds where the bill already comes due

The airlock is a thought experiment, a lens for seeing, not a forecast of where anyone will live. But it is built on a true mechanism, and the mechanism has already shown its face in real sealed worlds, places on this planet where human beings have closed the system far enough that the priceless commons suddenly acquired a price. Three of them are worth dwelling on, because each one reveals a different face of the same fact.

Start under the sea. A saturation diver working on a pipeline or a wellhead at real depth cannot breathe ordinary air. Down there the nitrogen that makes up most of the atmosphere turns narcotic, clouding judgment like strong drink, and at the pressures involved the oxygen we depend on every second turns toxic, attacking the body it normally sustains. So the diver breathes a manufactured mixture, mostly helium, with the oxygen carefully diluted to a fraction that is safe under pressure. Helium is not cheap and it does not stay put. It costs somewhere in the range of one dollar and seventy cents to two dollars and seventy-five cents per cubic foot, and a diver living for weeks under pressure goes through a great deal of it. The hourly cost of the helium alone can reach up to about two hundred dollars an hour. The number is large enough that the support ships run reclaim rigs, capturing the helium the divers exhale, scrubbing it, and feeding it back into the supply, because to let it escape into the sea would be to let money dissolve. Notice what has happened. The most ordinary act a body performs, the drawing of a breath, has become a metered expense significant enough to justify an entire recovery operation. The breath did not change. The seal changed. The pressure pushed the body to a place where the commons could not follow it for free, and the moment the commons could not follow, the price appeared.

Now come up out of the water and into the desert of Arizona, to a structure of glass and steel that in the early 1990s tried to do something no one had quite done before. Biosphere 2 was a sealed world of about three acres, an artificial wilderness under glass containing a miniature rainforest, a small ocean, a marsh, a savanna, a farm, and eight human beings who intended to live inside it for two years, growing their own food, drinking their own recycled water, breathing air regenerated by their own plants. It cost on the order of one hundred and fifty million dollars to build. It was, in its ambition, a beautiful thing, an attempt to prove that a human community could carry its own life support inside a closed shell. And it taught a lesson its designers had not meant to teach. Over sixteen months the oxygen in the sealed atmosphere fell from the normal twenty-one percent toward fourteen, a level at which the crew grew chronically tired and short of breath. The cause turned out to be partly the microbes in the rich soil they had brought in, respiring faster than the plants could compensate, and partly the carbon dioxide quietly binding into the fresh concrete of the structure itself, removing oxygen from the cycle in a way no one had modelled. In the end the managers had to pump oxygen in from outside, breaking the seal they had built the whole project to maintain. Of twenty-five small vertebrate species inside, nineteen died. Every pollinating insect died, so the crops had to be pollinated by hand. The lesson was not that the people were foolish. They were among the most careful and committed people imaginable. The lesson was that the work the open planet does for free, the balancing of a breathable atmosphere across an entire living surface, is so vast and so finely tuned that a serious, well-funded, scientifically advised attempt to reproduce a sliver of it inside three acres of glass failed inside a year and a half. The commons were doing something. You could measure exactly how much by watching what broke when they stopped.

And then look up, a few hundred kilometres straight up, to the International Space Station, the most thoroughly sealed human habitat that has ever sustained continuous life. There the accounting is exact and merciless, because every gram of everything had to be lifted out of the planet's gravity at staggering cost. Water, the most common substance on the surface of the Earth, costs roughly twenty-one thousand dollars per kilogram to deliver to orbit. A six-month supply for a crew, if it had to be lifted fresh, would run on the order of one hundred and thirty-five million dollars. So it is not lifted fresh. The station recycles around ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine back into drinking water, because at twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram the alternative is unthinkable. The oxygen the crew breathes is manufactured on board by running electricity through water to split it into its elements, a process that exists only because air, that thing of no price, becomes one of the most expensive commodities in the known universe the moment you put a wall around it and a vacuum outside the wall. On the station there is no mask of abundance, because there is no abundance. There is a wall, and outside the wall there is nothing, and inside the wall every commons the Earth gives away has a number on it, and the numbers are enormous.

Three sealed worlds, three different seals, one fact. Close the system, and the priceless commons become the most expensive things in it. The price was always the wrong reading. It was never that the air was worth nothing. It was that the supply was so complete that the worth had nowhere to show itself. Pressure, glass, vacuum, the medium does not matter. Any seal will do. Build a wall tight enough and the mask of abundance falls off, and underneath it you find a value that was there the whole time, waiting for scarcity to make it visible.

## The name of the thing

Once you have seen the mask come off in a sealed world, you can begin to name what is happening in the unsealed one, where the mask stays on and the costs go on being real anyway. The naming matters, because a thing without a name is hard to hold in the mind, and these costs have stayed invisible partly for want of a vocabulary precise enough to point at them.

Start with the simplest case, which is not actually the commons but its more ordinary cousin. Someone does a thing that creates a cost, and the cost lands on someone else, and the second person has no way to send the bill back to the first. A factory upstream fouls a river, and the people downstream pay in dirtied water and lost fish and illness, and nothing in the price of whatever the factory sells reflects what the river-dwellers lost. The cost has been severed from the act that caused it. Call this cost severance. It is the oldest trick in economic life, and economists have a tired phrase for one corner of it, but the phrase has gone soft with use and stopped pointing at the thing. Cost severance is sharper. It names the act of cutting the cost loose from its source so that someone downstream, downwind, or downstream in time absorbs it without recourse.

Cost severance has a companion, which is value extraction. When a barrel of oil is pumped and sold, the seller extracts the value of the oil as a product and pockets it. But the oil was more than a product. It was part of a finite endowment that belonged, in some hard-to-formalize but undeniable sense, to everyone who might have wanted some of that endowment later, including everyone not yet born. The seller extracts the present value and leaves the rest unaccounted. And that unaccounted remainder is the thing this essay most wants to name. Call it the residual commons value. It is the worth that remains attached to a commons after the private sale is done, the value that was never captured in any price because it is owed to people who were not at the table when the deal was struck, many of whom do not yet exist and so could not have been at the table even in principle.

Most honest pricing, the kind a careful regulator or a thoughtful firm already tries to do, is about cost severance. It is about re-attaching a severed cost to its source, making the factory pay for the river, charging the polluter for the pollution. That is good and necessary work and the framework this essay describes includes it. But the commons case is stranger and harder, because the residual commons value is not a severed cost waiting to be re-attached. It is a value so total and so evenly spread that it never acquired a price in the first place. There was no original transaction to correct. The atmosphere was never sold; it was simply used, by everyone, forever, until the using began to exceed what the planet could absorb. To price the residual commons value you cannot just find the missing invoice and re-issue it. You have to do something that no market mechanism does on its own. You have to look at a thing that has always been free and ask what it is actually worth, and then keep that worth on the books even though no one is demanding payment, because the people to whom the payment is owed cannot demand anything.

That is the whole of the framework this essay is building toward. It is a way of keeping honest books on the commons, a method for making the hidden and severed costs visible, so that any actor at any scale can see the real price of what they are doing. It is not a new economic system. It does not tell you who should own what or how markets should be arranged. It is an accounting discipline, and it works on this world and, as the airlock has already hinted, on any other.

## The hundred-dollar barrel

Take the clearest case, the one that has done more to mask its own true cost than any other, and run the framework over it. A barrel of oil, priced at, say, one hundred dollars. The number is illustrative, a round figure to think with rather than a quotation from any particular day's market, but it is in the right neighbourhood, and the exact value does not matter for the point.

What does the hundred dollars buy? It buys the present its tank of fuel. It pays for the labour of extraction, the capital of the rig, the transport, the refining, the profit of everyone along the chain, and a modest contribution to whatever taxes the relevant governments levy. Every one of those is a real cost, fairly priced, honestly paid. The hundred dollars is, as far as the present is concerned, a fair price. The trouble is that the thing being sold does not belong only to the present.

A barrel of oil is a piece of a finite endowment that took the planet tens of millions of years to lay down and that, once burned, is gone. Not depleted in the sense that it will grow back slowly. Gone, permanently, on any timescale a human being or a human civilization can use. And here a distinction does real work, the distinction between what renews and what does not. A renewable commons, a fishery or a forest or an aquifer fed by rain, poses a question of rate. The fish breed, the trees grow, the rain falls, and the only question honest accounting has to answer is whether you are taking less than the system replaces. Take less than it breeds and you can take forever. Take more and you draw down the stock toward collapse, but even then the error is one of rate, and a slower rate would have been sustainable. There exists, for a renewable commons, some pace of extraction slow enough to last.

Oil is not like that. Oil is liquidated. There is no rate slow enough, because the stock does not replenish on any usable timescale, and so every barrel taken is a barrel that will never be available to anyone again. The hundred dollars buys the present its tank of gas, and it does not buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after, because no amount of money can buy back a barrel that no longer exists. The residual commons value of an oil barrel is therefore not a flow that can be balanced against a rate of regeneration. It is the flat, permanent absence of a thing that will not return. And nothing in the hundred-dollar price reflects that absence, because the people who will live in the absence were not party to the sale.

This is the precise point at which the mask does its most expensive work. Because oil is, for now, abundant, its price reflects only the cost of getting the next barrel out of the ground, the scarcity at the margin. It says nothing about the total stock, nothing about permanence, nothing about the future people who will inherit a world with that much less of the endowment left. The abundance hides the depletion. The market, doing exactly what markets do, prices the margin and ignores the whole, and the whole is precisely where the residual commons value lives.

## The lineage of near-misses

If this were a wholly new idea, you would expect to find the empty plot of ground untouched, no foundations, no half-built walls. Instead you find the opposite. Several of the most powerful minds in the history of economics and moral philosophy walked right up to this plot, surveyed it, sometimes sank a footing, and then built their structure somewhere adjacent. The residual commons value is the building none of them quite put up, though many of them cleared the ground for it.

Karl Marx priced the unaccounted value better than anyone before him. The whole engine of his analysis in the first volume of Capital is that value is extracted from labour and not returned to it, that there is a surplus the worker creates and the owner keeps. He was right that value goes unaccounted and right that the unaccounting is the heart of the matter. But the axis of his concern was class, the relation between the worker and the owner in the present. He was not pricing time. The surplus he tracked was extracted from living people, not from the unborn, and the commons he might have noticed, the planet's finite endowment held in trust across generations, was not the surplus he was looking for.

Elinor Ostrom did something almost no economist had managed, which was to show with patient empirical care that communities can govern a shared resource without either privatizing it or handing it to the state. Her work on the commons, which won her the Nobel Prize in economics, demolished the lazy assumption that a shared resource must inevitably be ruined by its users. But the commons she studied and celebrated were renewable. They were fisheries and forests and irrigation systems and grazing lands, resources that pose the question of rate, where a community that takes less than the system breeds can sustain itself forever. Her genius was in the governance of the renewable commons. She did not turn her instruments on the liquidated one, the barrel that no governance can make last, because no rate of extraction is slow enough.

Herman Daly spent a career warning that an economy is a subsystem of a finite planet and cannot grow forever inside it. He gave the world the idea of throughput, the flow of matter and energy from the environment, through the economy, and back to the environment as waste, and he insisted that this flow has limits that the standard measures of growth conceal. He was pointing directly at the planetary ledger. But his warning was about scale and flow in general, about the whole metabolism of the economy, and it did not resolve into an invoice for any one foreclosed thing. He told us the ship was overloaded. He did not itemize the cargo.

John Hartwick came closest of all in one narrow respect. He proved a rule that bears his name, that a society extracting a finite resource can sustain its consumption forever if it reinvests the rents from the extraction into reproducible capital, so that as the resource runs down, the built and financial capital that replaces it keeps the total stock of wealth intact. This is the saving of the rent, and it is exactly the right shape of answer. But Hartwick saved the rent, the financial return on the resource, and not the barrel itself, not the residual commons value owed to the future for the permanent loss of a piece of the endowment. His rule keeps the books balanced in a particular accounting sense, but it does not by itself put a number on what the future is owed for a thing that will never come back.

So much for the economists. The moral philosophers came at the same plot from the other side, and they too circled it. John Rawls asked you to design a society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which person in it you would turn out to be, on the grounds that a just arrangement is one you would accept without knowing your own place in it. He meant the veil to hide your class, your talents, your race, your sex. But the veil widens naturally across time. If you do not know which person you will be, why should you know which generation you will be born into? Behind a veil thick enough to hide the date of your birth, you would not consent to an arrangement that lets the present liquidate the endowment of every future generation, because you might be one of them. Rawls did not press the veil that far, but the logic of his own device pushes toward the residual commons value.

Michael Sandel asked what money should not be allowed to buy, and showed that there are goods a market corrupts merely by pricing them, that turning certain things into commodities degrades them. He was warning against the market's reach. The commons sit at a strange angle to his warning. The danger there is not that the market prices them and corrupts them but that the market cannot price them at all and so renders them invisible. Sandel mapped the territory where pricing does harm. The commons live in the adjacent territory where the absence of pricing does the harm.

Derek Parfit, more than anyone, made the philosophy of obligation to future people rigorous. He showed how strange and how real our duties to those who do not yet exist are, how the very identity of future people depends on choices we make now, and how this tangles our intuitions without dissolving the obligation. He gave the moral weight of the unborn its sharpest formulation. But he was building a philosophy of personal identity and obligation, not an accounting method, and the bridge from his obligation to a number on a ledger he left for others to build.

And then there is the battleground where this argument has actually been fought in public, which is the fight over the discount rate. When economists try to price the future damage of carbon emissions, they have to decide how much to weight harms that fall a century from now against costs we bear today, and that weighting is the discount rate. Nicholas Stern, in his review for the British government, used a very low rate, on the order of one and a half percent, reasoning that we have little ethical right to count a future person's suffering as worth less than our own merely because it is distant in time. With that low rate the future damages loom large, and he concluded that strong, early action was worth roughly one percent of global output to avoid them. William Nordhaus, whose own climate modelling later won him the Nobel Prize, used a much higher rate, around four percent, drawn from the observed returns on capital, and reached far more modest conclusions about how fast to act. Martin Weitzman cut across both of them by pointing out that the real danger is the fat tail, the small but genuine chance of a catastrophe so large that no ordinary cost-benefit calculation captures it, and that the possibility of ruin should dominate the analysis.

It was a serious and important fight. But notice what every party to it was actually pricing. They were all pricing the damage flow from combustion, the harm that the released carbon does as it warms the world. They were arguing about how to value a stream of future damages. None of them was pricing the resource's absence, the permanent flat fact that the barrel is gone and will not return. And this is why the residual commons value survives even Stern's low rate, even Nordhaus's high one. A discount rate shrinks a future flow. A dollar of damage in a hundred years, discounted, is worth less today than a dollar of damage now, and reasonable people argue about how much less. But the residual commons value is not a discounted flow. It is not a stream of harms arriving year by year. It is the present absence of a thing that will not come back, and you cannot discount an absence into smallness, because it is not in the future. It is already true. The barrel is already gone the moment it is burned, and its absence is not less real next century than it is today. The discount-rate argument, for all its rigour, was conducted on the wrong quantity. It priced the smoke. It did not price the missing barrel.

## McDowell County, run backward

So far the framework has run forward, pricing a loss the future will inherit. But it runs just as well backward, over an extraction already realized, and the clearest place to run it backward is a particular county in the mountains of West Virginia.

McDowell County was, for a span of decades, one of the great coal-producing places on Earth. The seams there, the Pocahontas coal, were thick and rich and prized, and they were dug and shipped out by the millions of tons. At the mine mouth, around 1960, the coal sold for something on the order of four or five dollars a ton. That was the price. That was what the buyer paid. And by the standard of the time it may even have looked like a fair one, covering the wages of the miners, the cost of the equipment, the railroad, the profit of the operators.

Now run the framework over it. Add back the severed costs, the ones that the four or five dollars never reflected. The largest of these by far is the carbon. A ton of that particular coal, burned, releases about two and six-tenths tons of carbon dioxide, a figure specific to the high-carbon Pocahontas seams and higher than the national average of around two and one-third tons. Put against that the social cost of carbon, the dollar value of the damage each ton of carbon dioxide does, which the United States Environmental Protection Agency in 2023 estimated at around one hundred and ninety dollars per ton. Multiply the two and you get a carbon cost of roughly four hundred and ninety-six dollars for every ton of coal that left McDowell County at four or five dollars. Add the smaller severed costs and the honest price comes to more than five hundred dollars a ton, around five hundred and ten in total, nearly all of it carbon.

Consider that ratio for a moment without flinching from it. The coal that sold for four or five dollars carried a true cost more than a hundred times as large, and the gap between the two numbers did not vanish. It was severed. It went out into the atmosphere with the smoke and lodged in a future that had no vote at the mine mouth in 1960. The county itself was emptied of much of its wealth and many of its people, the value extracted and shipped north and east, the residual cost left behind in the warming air. The framework, run backward, does not produce a villain. The miners needed the wages. The country needed the power. It produces a number, a measurement of how much value was severed from its cost in a transaction that everyone at the time regarded as ordinary commerce. That number is the residual commons value of a realized extraction, and it is owed, not to the people who sold the coal, but to everyone downwind in time who will live in the world the carbon made.

## Forward and in reverse, and a line not crossed

The framework, then, has two directions, and each direction needs its own instrument. Forward, where the loss has not yet happened, the instrument is a foreclosure bond. You price the future loss that an extraction will impose, and you set that money aside now, before the deal closes, so that the value extracted is matched by a reserve held against the cost severed. The bond forecloses the option of pretending the future cost does not exist. It puts the residual commons value on the books at the moment of the transaction rather than leaving it to be discovered as catastrophe.

Backward, where the extraction is already realized and the cost already severed, the instrument is a restitution bond. You measure the value that was extracted and the cost that was left behind, as the McDowell calculation does, and you create an obligation to make restitution for it. The forward bond prevents a future severance. The backward bond repairs a past one.

The backward direction connects directly to a body of work that has thought harder than any other about the accounting of historical extraction. William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen, in their book From Here to Equality, lay out the economics of reparations for slavery and its long aftermath in the United States with a rigour that is itself a form of honest backward accounting. They trace the racial wealth gap back to its origins in 1619, and they show how a legacy of extraction registers even in something as concrete and measurable as the gap in life expectancy between Black and white Americans, a difference in years of life that is the residual, compounding cost of a severance that began four centuries ago. Their work is backward honest accounting at the scale of a nation's founding wrong, and the restitution bond, as an instrument, operationalizes the restitution component of the redress they describe. Ta-Nehisi Coates, in his essay making the case for reparations, built the moral architecture that their economics fills in.

But here the framework reaches a line it must be careful not to cross, and naming the line is part of keeping the books honest. There is a difference between reaching a coerced injustice through its measurable legacy effects, the wealth gap, the longevity gap, the things that can be counted in the present, and presuming to put a price on the coercion itself, the original violence borne by people whose descendants are still living with and still defining the meaning of that history. The first the framework can do, because legacy effects are measurable commons-style residuals. The second it should not do, at least not from where this argument stands, because putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group the author of this framework does not belong to is not the author's work to do. It belongs to those who carry that history forward and are continuing to define what redress means. So the framework reaches the coerced cases through their legacy effects, which it can measure, and defers the direct pricing of the coercion vector to those whose work it properly is. Honest accounting includes knowing which entries are not yours to make.

## Norway and the Niger Delta

The forward bond is not a fantasy. One country has built it at the scale of a sovereign state and held it, through changes of government and generations of politics, as a working institution. And another place is the photographic negative of it, the same physical act of pulling oil out of the ground arranged so that almost nothing was set aside and almost everyone downstream paid. Put them side by side, not as a story of a hero and a villain, but as two pieces of architecture, two answers to the same question.

Just before Christmas in 1969, after a string of disappointments had nearly persuaded the oil companies to give up on the Norwegian sector of the North Sea, a well at the field that would be called Ekofisk came in, and Norway found itself sitting on a great endowment of petroleum. What the country did with it over the following decades is the closest thing the world has to a worked example of honest forward accounting at sovereign scale. Rather than spend the oil money as it came in, Norway built a fund. The Government Petroleum Fund, established in 1990, received the state's petroleum revenues and invested them abroad. In 2006 it was reorganized and renamed the Government Pension Fund Global. And in 2001 the country bound itself with a fiscal rule of remarkable discipline, allowing the government to spend only the expected real return on the fund, on the order of three percent a year, while the capital itself remained inviolate. The principle is exactly Hartwick's rule made real at the scale of a nation. The finite resource is being converted, barrel by barrel, into a permanent financial endowment that will outlast the oil. Today that fund holds well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every citizen of the country.

The most striking thing about it is not the size but the durability. The fund has survived governments of the left and governments of the right, the long stewardship of leaders from Stoltenberg to Bondevik to Solberg to Støre, and through all of it the principal has not been raided. No government has broken the rule to buy itself an election. The forward bond, in Norway, became a permanent feature of the constitutional landscape, a reserve fund held against the day the oil runs out, which ties it directly to the theme of reserves that this essay keeps returning to. Norway took a liquidated commons, an endowment that would otherwise simply vanish into present consumption, and converted it into a held reserve for the future. It priced the absence and set the money aside.

Now look at the Niger Delta, where oil has been pumped since the 1950s and where hundreds of billions of dollars of revenue have been generated, and ask where the reserve is. There is no fund. The value was extracted and very little was set aside, and the costs, the oil spills that poisoned the creeks and the farmland, the gas flares that burned day and night over villages, the fisheries foreclosed by pollution, were borne by people who never sold the oil and had no way to send anyone a bill. In the 1990s the writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa led a movement of the Ogoni people protesting what the extraction had done to their land, and in 1995 he and eight others were executed, an act that drew the country's suspension from the Commonwealth and the condemnation of the world. Years later a United Nations environmental assessment of Ogoniland estimated that an initial cleanup would cost around one billion dollars and take twenty-five to thirty years, and that was only the beginning of what restoration would require.

Set the two cases against each other and the lesson is not about national character or the goodness of one people and the wickedness of another. It is about architecture. The same physical act, oil pulled from the ground and burned, was arranged two ways. In one, the residual commons value was measured, captured, and held in reserve against the future. In the other, it was severed and abandoned, and the cost fell on those least able to bear it and least responsible for the extraction. This is not an argument against extraction. Norway extracted enormous quantities of oil. It is an argument about what you do with the residual value, whether you set it aside or let it run off into a future that did not consent. When the cost outlives the sale, someone carries it. The only question is whether anything was set aside to help them, and the answer is a matter of architecture, not of villainy.

## Off-world: the framework as a working economy

Return now to the sealed habitat, but this time do not visit it as a measuring device. Live in it. The three real sealed worlds, the divers and Biosphere 2 and the station, were instruments for seeing the mask come off. The colony is something else. It is the framework not glimpsed in an experiment but running continuously, as the ordinary economy of a place. I am not predicting that anyone will live this way. It is a lens, a thought experiment held up to the light to see the framework's logic in motion. But it is a lens worth looking through at length, because a sealed world is the one place where honest commons accounting is not a moral aspiration laid over an economy but the economy itself, the only economy a sealed world can possibly run.

So imagine the colony as a home. There are people who were born there and people who came, schools and workshops and quarrels and festivals, an economy in which people earn and spend and save. And folded into the base of that economy, beneath every other transaction, is the metering of the commons. The air is metered. Not as a cruelty but as a simple fact, because the air is manufactured, by electrolysis and by the cultivation of plants under light, and the manufacture has a cost, and the cost must land somewhere. So every breath is, in the most literal sense, paid for, whether by the breather directly or by a residency rate that bundles it or by a public provision funded out of common revenue. Water is metered, because every litre is recycled at real expense, the way the station recycles its crew's sweat and urine, and clean water is too precious to waste and too costly to give away unaccounted. And the removal of carbon dioxide is metered, because the machines that scrub the exhaled carbon out of the cabin atmosphere, doing the work that on Earth the whole living planet does for free, run on power and parts and maintenance, and the carbon you breathe out is a cost you impose on the shared air that someone must pay to remove.

This is the airlock question turned into a way of life. At the door they asked how you would pay for your air, and inside, the answer is woven into everything. The colony has no choice but to keep honest books on its commons, because there is no mask of abundance to hide behind. Nothing is abundant. The wall is too close. Every commons the Earth gives away is, inside the seal, a manufactured good with a price, and the price is on the books because it has to be, because a colony that let its commons go unaccounted would deplete its oxygen the way Biosphere 2 did, would run its water down, would let its carbon climb until the people grew slow and sick. The honest ledger is not an ethical decoration in a sealed world. It is the life-support system expressed as economics.

And here is the thing to see clearly. This is the same ledger as McDowell County's and the same ledger as Norway's, only run forward, in real time, on a world that cannot afford to look away. McDowell's ledger was run backward, after the fact, to measure a severance already complete. Norway's was run forward but slowly, as a fiscal rule maintained across decades. The colony runs it continuously, second by second, breath by breath, because the consequence of not running it is not a distant catastrophe inherited by the unborn but an immediate one suffered by the living. On Earth the mask lets us defer the reckoning, push the cost downwind and downstream and down the years, because the planet is so large that the severance takes a long time to show. The colony has no such room. It is Earth with the deferral removed, the planetary ledger made visible and immediate by the simple fact of a wall and a vacuum. First we used the seal to see the value. Now, living inside it, we pay it, continuously, as the price of staying alive.

Now leave the colony and go further out, to where the logic of the framework produces a result that surprises people who assume that honest commons accounting must always mean restraint. Imagine an asteroid miner, alone or nearly so, working a body of metal-rich rock far from any community, far from any ecosystem, far from anyone who could be harmed by the work. Again, this is a lens and not a forecast. But run the framework over it honestly and see what it says.

On the asteroid there is no community to bear a severed cost, because there is no community. There is no ecosystem to foul, because there is no ecosystem. The rock is dead metal and silicate, and pulling iron and nickel and rare metals out of it harms no one and forecloses nothing that anyone alive or unborn had any use for, because the asteroid was never going to become anyone's home or anyone's fishery or anyone's air. The residual commons value of asteroid iron is, as near as makes no difference, zero. The cost severance is zero. And so the framework, the same framework that priced the McDowell coal at more than five hundred dollars a ton and demanded that Norway set aside its oil rents, says something that sounds at first like its opposite. It says: extract freely. Take the iron. If a comet swings briefly within reach and will not return for a thousand years, extract aggressively while you can, because there is no future claimant to consider and no cost to anyone in the taking. Honest accounting is not a synonym for restraint. Where the residual commons value is genuinely zero, honest accounting licenses extraction without limit, because there is nothing severed and no one to owe.

And yet. On the rock itself, in the miner's own habitat, the ledger does not relax for a moment. Out there you must hold reserves, and hold them with a discipline far stricter than anything Earth requires, because the consequence of an error is not hardship but death. You must keep enough fuel to be certain you can leave the rock and reach home or the next world. You must keep enough air and water and spare life-support to survive every plausible failure of the machines, because there is no rescue and no resupply within any useful time. The same ledger that says take freely says reserve fiercely, and there is no contradiction between them, because both are simply the truth about cost. The asteroid imposes no cost on anyone, so take all of it you like. But you, extracting, are extracting into a future, your own near future, and that future has a cost, and honest accounting means keeping enough back to survive the future you are extracting into. The miner who takes everything and reserves nothing is not free. The miner is dead. Honest accounting is the difference.

This is the deepest thing the off-world lens shows, and it is why the framework is not anti-extraction and not a politics of restraint. It is knowing the real cost of what you do, both the cost you impose on others and the cost you impose on your own future, and keeping enough in reserve to remain alive and able to act. On the asteroid, with no commons to protect, the framework says take. In the colony, with every commons manufactured and scarce, it says meter and pay. In McDowell, after the fact, it says measure what was severed. In Norway it says set the rents aside. It is one ledger, and it gives different answers in different places not because it is inconsistent but because it is honest, and the honest answer depends on the real costs, which differ.

## Whatever economy you choose

Step back from the rock and the colony and ask what kind of thing this framework actually is. It is tempting, when an idea reaches across a whole planet's economy and out into hypothetical worlds, to mistake it for a rival economic system, a third thing to set beside the market and the plan. It is not. It does not say who should own the means of production or whether prices should be set by supply and demand or by a ministry. It is the accounting that any economic system needs in order to see what it is doing, and it sits underneath the choice of system rather than competing with it.

Consider how many different traditions have reached, in their own vocabularies, for exactly this. Kate Raworth, in her work on what she calls doughnut economics, draws a picture of a safe operating space for humanity, bounded on the outside by the ecological ceiling the planet cannot exceed and on the inside by the social floor below which people should not fall, and argues that a healthy economy keeps itself within that band. That is a way of insisting that the economy see its real ecological costs, which is the same insistence this framework makes. Ostrom's communities governing their renewable commons were keeping honest books on a shared resource, measuring what they took against what the system could replace. And long before any of them, civilizations built reserve systems against scarcity, the ever-normal granary of ancient China that bought grain when it was plentiful and released it when it was scarce, the state granaries of ancient Egypt that held the surplus of fat years against the lean ones. Keep these references high, at the level of the idea, because the idea is the point. A civilization that builds a granary is doing the same thing in stored grain that Norway does in invested oil money and that the colony does in metered air. It is knowing its real costs and holding reserves against the scarcity it can foresee.

Capitalist, planned, ancient, or off-world, the requirement is the same. The economy has to be able to see its real costs, including the severed ones and the residual commons value owed to those not at the table, or it makes its decisions without the information it most needs, and an economy that cannot see its real costs cannot make good decisions about them, no matter how its ownership or its prices are arranged. The framework is not a competitor to any of these systems. It is the eyesight any of them requires.

## Reserves on the longest horizon

There is one more turn, and it is the longest. The framework keeps coming back to reserves, the held-aside value, the granary, the fund, the fuel on the asteroid kept against the journey home. So far the reserves have been for the next generation or the next century, horizons a human institution can almost imagine. But the logic does not stop at the human horizon, and following it to the end is what finally explains why the off-world colony, which has been a lens for most of this essay, is at the deepest level not a metaphor at all but a reserve held on the longest horizon there is.

The Sun is slowly brightening. It is a feature of how a star like ours ages, the steady increase in its output as it burns through its hydrogen, and the consequence for this planet is not in doubt even if its timing is approximate. On the order of five hundred million to one billion years from now, the rising solar luminosity will have made the Earth uninhabitable, the oceans driven off, the surface sterilized, long before anything dramatic happens to the Sun itself. And much later, on the order of seven and a half billion years out, the Sun will swell into a red giant and may engulf the planet entirely. These are not round numbers chosen for effect. They are the considered estimates of the people who model the lives of stars, and the gap between them, the difference between a world made uninhabitable in under a billion years and a world physically destroyed in seven and a half, is itself instructive. Life on Earth has a deadline far sooner than the death of the Sun.

On those horizons, the framework's insistence on reserves stops being merely prudent and becomes the only coherent long-term stance there is. A civilization that thinks honestly about its real costs, all of them, across the whole span of its possible future, must eventually price the loss of its only home, and the only reserve that answers that cost is the capacity to live somewhere else. The off-world habitat, which appeared in this essay first as a thought experiment for seeing the mask come off and then as a colony running the honest ledger as its daily economy, turns out at the longest horizon to be exactly what the framework has been describing all along. It is a reserve. It is option-preservation across deep time, the held-aside capacity that lets the future remain possible even when the present world cannot. The granary held grain against the lean year. The fund holds money against the day the oil runs out. The off-world habitat holds the possibility of life against the certainty that this world will not last forever. They are the same instrument at three scales of time, and the longest scale is not science fiction. It is honest accounting carried to its natural end.

## The honest books

So bring it home, all the way home, to the open sky and the free air and the planet doing its unmetered work beneath an unmetered atmosphere.

What this essay has built is not a politics. It does not tell you to vote one way or to dismantle one system and raise another. It is not a movement and it has no enemies, no villains, only severed costs and unkept books. It does not even tell you, finally, to extract less, because on the dead asteroid it tells you to extract all you can. What it tells you is something smaller and harder and more universal than any of those. It tells you to keep honest books. To find the value that abundance or distance or time has hidden, to put it on the ledger at its true worth even when no one is demanding payment, and to hold reserves against the costs you can foresee.

And the remarkable thing about honest books is that they scale. A single person can keep them, asking what the real cost of a choice is and who will pay it and whether anything has been set aside. A community can keep them, measuring what it takes from its shared resources against what those resources can replace. A corporation can keep them, pricing the residual commons value of what it extracts instead of severing the cost and walking away. A government can keep them, as Norway did, converting a liquidated endowment into a permanent reserve held inviolate across the changing of administrations. And a sealed colony on another world must keep them, continuously, because the wall is close and the mask of abundance cannot survive inside it. The same ledger, the same discipline, the same single question asked at every scale. What is the real cost, and who carries it, and what have we set aside. A person, a community, a corporation, a government, a colony on another world. One ledger, run at any size, on this world or any other.

And anyone who looks at it honestly, here or anywhere, arrives at the same place, because honest accounting is not the property of any ideology or any system or any country. It is just the truth about cost, and the truth does not change with the politics of whoever is reading the books. It is not a politics and it is not a system. It is honest books, and that is all it ever has to be.

Which returns us, at the very last, to the breath. Down here, today, the air is still free, in the only sense that matters, which is that no one charges you and no one could, because the supply is still so total that the worth has nowhere to show itself. The mask is still on. You can stand under the open sky and draw breath after breath and never once see the meter, because there is no wall and no vacuum and no clerk at any airlock asking how you intend to pay. That freedom is real, and it is precious, and it is the thing the whole long argument has been about. But it is free for now, and whether it stays free is not a fact of the universe. It is a thing we still get to decide, by whether we keep honest books on the air while we still can, or wait until a wall goes up somewhere and the bill arrives with every breath.
