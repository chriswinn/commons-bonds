# The Mask of Abundance

Take a breath. Now think about what it cost you.

The honest answer is that it cost you nothing. The air around you is free. You did not pay for it when you woke up this morning, and you will not pay for it tonight. No one sends you a bill for breathing. In most parts of the world, for most of human history, this has been true, and we treat it as one of the few certainties we can rely on. Air is the thing that is always there, the thing so ordinary that we forget it is there at all.

It is tempting to read this zero price as a verdict on value. We are trained, all of us, to read prices this way. A thing that costs a great deal must be precious. A thing that costs little must be ordinary. A thing that costs nothing must be worth nothing. The price tag, we assume, is a kind of honest report on how much a thing matters.

But this is a misunderstanding, and it is the misunderstanding I want to take apart slowly, because almost everything else in this essay follows from it.

A price does not measure how much a thing is worth. A price measures something much narrower. It measures scarcity at the edge, the point where one more unit of a thing can be bought or sold. When something is rare, the edge is sharp, and the price climbs. When something is common, the edge softens, and the price falls. When something is everywhere, available to everyone at once, with no shortage anywhere that anyone can find, there is no edge at all. The market has nothing to grip. It cannot find a place to charge.

So the air posts a price of zero. Not because it has little value. Because it has nothing but value, spread so completely and so evenly across the surface of the planet that no seller can find a corner of scarcity to stand in and demand payment. The zero is not a measure of worthlessness. The zero is the signature of an abundance so total that price, our most trusted instrument, simply fails to register it.

I will call this the mask of abundance. When a thing is abundant enough, its value disappears from the one instrument we use to see value. The thing does not become less valuable. It becomes invisible. And we, looking only at the instrument, mistake the silence of the instrument for the silence of the thing.

This matters far beyond the air. There is a whole class of things that the planet gives to all of us at once, and gives so freely that none of them carry a price. The water that cycles through the rivers and the rain. The capacity of the oceans and the forests to absorb the carbon dioxide we produce. A climate that stays within the narrow band that allows farming, and cities, and ordinary life. These are not luxuries. They are the conditions for everything else. And they are all, every one of them, wearing the mask. They are not underpriced. They have no price at all.

I want to be precise here, because there is a difference that the rest of the argument depends on. Some valuable things are merely underpriced. A nurse is paid less than the work is worth to the people she keeps alive. The topsoil that feeds us is treated as cheaper than it is. These are real problems, but they are problems of the wrong number, not the absence of a number. The nurse has a wage. The topsoil has a market. We can argue that the figure is too low, and in arguing we are at least pointing at a figure.

The commons are different. The air, the carbon-absorbing capacity of the planet, the stable climate, these do not have a figure that is too low. They have no figure. And a thing with no figure is not visible to the only instrument we collectively trust to tell us what matters. We can feel its value. We cannot see it on the ledger. And what we cannot see on the ledger, we tend, in the end, to spend as though it were free.

So here is the problem in its plainest form. The most valuable things we hold in common are precisely the things our accounting cannot see, and they are invisible for the same reason they are valuable, which is that there is, for now, enough of them to go around. The mask is made of abundance itself. And the question this essay sets out to answer is simple to state and hard to live with. How do we put a true value on the things that are too abundant to have a price, before the abundance runs out and the price arrives on its own, all at once, far too late to plan for?

Let me say plainly what this essay is trying to do, because I want it on the table from the start. I am trying to build a way of doing honest accounting. Not a new economic system. Not a political program. An accounting method, a way of seeing the real costs of what we do, including the costs that are currently hidden, the costs that no bill ever reaches. I want a ledger that works on this world, and, as I will argue near the end, on any other world we might ever live on. The whole of the claim is this. We can make the hidden costs visible, and once they are visible we can decide honestly what to do about them. That is all. But it turns out to be a great deal.

## The sealed room

To make a hidden value visible, you have to break the abundance that hides it. And there is a clean way to do that, at least in the imagination. You seal the system.

Imagine taking a room, a building, a small piece of the world, and closing it off completely from the planet outside. No air comes in. No water comes in. Nothing the planet gives away for free is allowed to leak across the boundary. Everything inside the sealed space now has to be produced, or stored, or recycled, inside that space, by the people living in it, at a cost they can count.

The moment you do this, the mask comes off. The air, which cost nothing a second ago, now has to be made, or carried in, or scrubbed clean of the carbon dioxide that the people inside keep breathing out. The water has to be filtered and used again. The capacity to carry away waste, which the open planet supplies for free, now has to be built and maintained by hand. The commons that were invisible because they were abundant become, inside the seal, the most expensive and most carefully watched things in the entire economy. Their value did not change. Only the abundance changed. And with the abundance gone, the value finally appears on the ledger, often with a shock.

I am not only imagining this. Human beings have built sealed systems, and in every one of them the priceless free commons of the open planet turned, the moment they were sealed in, into line items with real and often startling prices. There are three of these that are worth holding in mind, because they are real, and because each one shows the mask coming off in a different way.

The first is the world of deep-sea diving. When people work at great depth, under enormous pressure, for many hours or many days, they cannot breathe ordinary air. At depth, the nitrogen that makes up most of our atmosphere stops being harmless and begins to act on the nervous system in dangerous ways, and the oxygen that keeps us alive at the surface becomes toxic in the wrong amounts. So saturation divers breathe a carefully mixed gas, mostly helium and oxygen, blended to keep them alive in conditions where ordinary air would harm them. And helium is not free. It costs somewhere in the range of one dollar and seventy cents to two dollars and seventy five cents for each cubic foot, which works out to roughly eighty to two hundred dollars an hour for the helium alone, for a single diver. The amounts involved are large enough that the support ships run machines to capture and reclaim the helium the divers breathe out, so that none of it is wasted. On the open surface of the planet, the gas you breathe is free and beneath your notice. A few hundred metres down, sealed inside a pressurised system, the gas you breathe is metered by the hour and reclaimed by machine.

The second is an experiment from the early nineteen nineties called Biosphere 2, in which eight people lived for two years inside a sealed structure of glass and steel covering about three acres, built at a cost of around one hundred and fifty million dollars, designed to be a small, closed, self-sustaining world. The plan was that the plants inside would supply the oxygen, that the water would cycle, that the system would balance itself the way the planet does on a vast scale. It did not balance. Over sixteen months, the oxygen inside the sealed structure fell from the ordinary level of about twenty one percent of the air down to about fourteen percent, low enough to leave the crew exhausted and unwell. The oxygen was being consumed faster than the plants could replace it, partly by microbes in the rich soil and partly by chemical binding into the freshly poured concrete of the building itself. In the end the managers had to pump oxygen in from outside, which quietly conceded that the sealed world could not, in fact, supply its own air. Of the twenty five small vertebrate animals brought inside, nineteen died. Every one of the pollinating insects died, so that the crops inside had to be pollinated by hand. The lesson was not that the people failed. The lesson was how staggeringly difficult, and how staggeringly expensive, it is to reproduce by hand even a small piece of what the open planet does for free, everywhere, all the time, at no charge to anyone.

The third is the International Space Station, which is the most completely sealed human habitat that has ever been operated, and which is therefore the place where the mask is most fully off. Up there, a few hundred kilometres above the planet, nothing is free. Water has to be lifted from the ground at a cost of roughly twenty one thousand dollars for each kilogram, so that a six month supply of water for the crew represents a value on the order of one hundred and thirty five million dollars. Because that is the cost, the station recycles about ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine back into clean drinking water, and makes its oxygen by passing an electric current through water to split it. On the planet below, the same water falls from the sky and runs in the rivers, free. In orbit, it is among the most expensive substances a human being can possess, and it is guarded and recycled accordingly.

Hold these three together. A diver's breath, metered by the hour. A sealed glass world that could not, in the end, make its own air. Drinking water in orbit worth more by weight than most things people buy on the ground. In each case nothing about the substance itself changed. Air is still air. Water is still water. What changed was the abundance. Take away the planet's free and overflowing supply, seal the system so that the supply has to come from inside, and the priceless free commons become the single most expensive and most carefully accounted things in the whole economy. The seal is a machine for removing the mask. It lets us see, in hard numbers, the value that abundance had been hiding all along.

## Naming the mechanism

Now I want to slow down and name the underlying thing carefully, because we are going to need it for the rest of the argument, and because naming it precisely is the only way to keep it from sliding back into vagueness.

Start with the most common case, the case that is easiest to see. Someone does something that creates a cost. The cost is real. But the person who created it does not pay it. Someone else pays it, someone downstream, someone who had no part in the decision and who has no way to send the bill back to the person who caused the harm. The cost has been cut loose from the person who created it and has landed on someone who did not. Let me give this a name. I will call it cost severance. A cost is severed when the person who creates it and the person who pays it are not the same person, and there is no path for the bill to travel back.

A factory that pours its waste into a river creates a cost. The cost is real and it is large. But the factory does not pay it. The people downstream pay it, in poisoned water and lost fishing and illness, and they have no way to invoice the factory for what they have lost. That is cost severance in its ordinary form. The cost exists. It is simply attached to the wrong people.

There is a second idea that travels with the first. When something is sold, the sale captures part of its value, the part the buyer and seller can agree on at the moment of the exchange. But there is often value left over that the sale does not capture, value that belongs to people who were not part of the deal and could not have been, because some of them are not yet born. When a barrel of oil is burned, the people who pump it and the people who buy it settle a price between them. But the absence of that oil, and the carbon now permanently added to the atmosphere, are costs that fall on people far away in space and far away in time, people who never had a seat at the negotiation. I will call this leftover, this worth that the sale does not capture, the residual commons value. It is the value that remains after the transaction is finished, owed to people who were not at the table, across distance and across time.

Most of the time, honest pricing means finding a severed cost and re-attaching it. You find the bill that landed on the wrong person, and you route it back to the person who caused the harm. That is the ordinary work of environmental accounting, and it is hard enough.

But the commons case is stranger than that, and I want to be careful about the difference. The air, the carbon-absorbing capacity of the planet, the stable climate, these were never given a price that we can correct. They were so abundant that they never acquired a price at all. There is no wrong number to fix, because there was never a number. The value was total and invisible at the same time, and for the same reason. It only becomes visible when the abundance fails, when scarcity finally arrives and the seal closes, whether the seal is a diving system, a glass dome, an orbiting station, or the slow closing of the whole planet's capacity to absorb what we put into it. So we are dealing with two related but distinct problems. One is a cost attached to the wrong person, which we must re-attach. The other is a value so complete that it never acquired a price, which we must now learn to see before the scarcity that would reveal it arrives on its own.

## The hundred-dollar barrel

Let me bring this down to a single object that almost everyone in the world has some relationship with, directly or indirectly. A barrel of oil.

Suppose a barrel of oil sells for one hundred dollars. I am using this figure as an illustration, not as a current market quote; the exact price moves constantly and the argument does not depend on the precise number. What I want to ask is what that one hundred dollars actually pays for, and what it does not.

The hundred dollars pays for the present. It pays for the labour of pumping the oil out of the ground, the work of refining it, the cost of moving it to where it is used, and a profit for the people who did all that. It buys the person at the fuel pump the fuel for the journey they are about to take today. In terms of the present moment, the transaction is complete and fair. Money changed hands and a useful thing was delivered.

But the thing that was sold does not belong only to the present. The barrel of oil took an immense span of time to form, and once it is burned it is gone, permanently, on any timescale that matters to human beings. The carbon that was locked inside it is released into the atmosphere, where it stays and acts on the climate for a very long time. The hundred dollars bought the present its fuel. It did not buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after. The future does not get a refund. It gets the absence of the oil and the presence of the carbon, and it pays for both, and it had no voice in the sale.

Here a careful distinction does a great deal of work, and it is worth slowing down over. There are two kinds of resource, and the difference between them is not a detail. Some resources renew themselves. A forest grows back. A fish population breeds and replaces itself. A field of crops returns each season. For these, the honest question is a question of rate. How fast does the resource renew itself, and are we taking less than that, so that the stock holds steady, or more than that, so that the stock declines toward nothing? A renewable resource can be used forever, as long as the taking stays below the rate of renewal. The question is one of speed, of balance, of not taking faster than the thing can replace itself.

Oil is not like this. Oil does not renew on any human timescale. When a barrel is burned, it is not drawn down faster than it replaces itself. It is simply gone, removed from the total stock that will ever exist, permanently. There is no rate of extraction slow enough to make it sustainable, because the stock does not refill. Every barrel burned is a barrel that will never be available to anyone, ever again. This is what it means to say that oil is liquidated rather than harvested. A renewable resource is income. A non-renewable resource is capital, and burning it is spending the capital itself, not living off what it produces.

So the hundred-dollar barrel is priced for the present and consumed in the present, but the thing being consumed extends to permanence. The price is honest about today and silent about forever. And the residual commons value, the worth that the sale did not capture, is the permanent absence of that barrel, owed to everyone who comes after, none of whom were at the table when the price was set.

## A field that several thinkers walked past

I want to say honestly that I did not invent any of the pieces of this. Several traditions of thought walked right up to the edge of this empty field, looked across it, and then turned away to work on something nearby. It is worth naming a few of them quickly, not to settle scores but to be clear about what has and has not been done.

The philosopher and economist Karl Marx, in his great work *Capital*, put a price on the value that workers create but do not receive, the surplus that is taken from their labour. It was a profound act of accounting, finding a hidden value and naming who it was taken from. But the axis he worked on was class, the relationship between those who own and those who work. He was not pricing the cost handed forward to people not yet born. His ledger ran between groups alive at the same time, not across the generations.

The political economist Elinor Ostrom, who won the Nobel Prize in economics in 2009 for her book *Governing the Commons*, showed that communities can manage shared resources well, without either private ownership or government control, when they build the right rules among themselves. This was a deep and hopeful finding. But the commons she studied were renewable ones, fisheries and forests and irrigation systems, the kind of resource where the question is rate, where good rules can keep the taking below the rate of renewal forever. She was governing the resources that breed back. She was not pricing the one that is liquidated and gone.

The ecological economist Herman Daly, who died in 2022, warned for decades that an economy cannot grow forever inside a finite planet, that the throughput of materials and energy has physical limits. He was right, and he was early, and he was largely ignored. But a warning about the total scale of throughput is not the same as an invoice for one specific foreclosed thing. He told us the whole system was running past its limits. He did not write the bill for the single burned barrel.

The economist John Hartwick, in a paper from 1977, worked out a rule that comes very close. If you are going to use up a non-renewable resource, he showed, you should take the rent, the pure profit from the resource itself, and reinvest it, so that the wealth the resource represented is preserved in another form for the future. This is genuinely an act of honest accounting across time, and we will see a country that has come closer to following it than any other. But Hartwick's rule is about saving the financial rent. It is not quite about pricing the full absence of the barrel itself, the loss to everyone who will never have access to it.

Then there is a group of moral philosophers who, working on a different question entirely, built much of the foundation that an honest cross-generational ledger would need. The philosopher John Rawls asked us to imagine choosing the rules of a society from behind what he called a veil of ignorance, not knowing who we would turn out to be within it, rich or poor, strong or weak. Choose fairly, he argued, as if you might be anyone. Later thinkers widened that veil across time, asking us to choose as if we might be anyone in any generation, including those not yet born. The philosopher Michael Sandel, in *What Money Can't Buy*, asked which things are corrupted when we put a price on them, and which are not. And the philosopher Derek Parfit, in *Reasons and Persons*, thought harder than almost anyone about what we owe to future people, people whose very existence depends on choices we make now. Between them they built much of the moral case for taking the future seriously. They did not, however, build the accounting tool that would turn that moral case into a number on a ledger.

Finally there is the long argument among economists about how to price the damage that carbon does, and it turns on a single technical idea that deserves a plain explanation. When a harm will fall in the future, economists usually shrink its present value, on the reasoning that a cost a hundred years away should weigh less in today's decisions than a cost today. The rate at which they shrink it is called the discount rate, and the choice of that rate is enormously consequential. The economist Nicholas Stern, in a major review for the British government, used a very low discount rate, which means he let the far future weigh almost as heavily as the present, and he concluded that strong action on climate change was urgently worth its cost. The economist William Nordhaus, who won the Nobel Prize in 2018, used a higher discount rate, which means he let the future weigh less, and he concluded that a slower, more gradual response made more sense. The economist Martin Weitzman pointed at a different problem, the small but real chance of a catastrophe so large that no ordinary calculation captures it. A simple illustration shows how much the rate matters. A cost of twenty five dollars a century from now might be worth only about one dollar and fifty cents in today's money at a high discount rate, and a great deal more at a low one.

But notice what all of these economists are pricing. They are pricing the flow of damage that comes from burning the carbon, the harm the released carbon does as it warms the world. They are not pricing the absence of the resource itself. And here is the point I most want to make about this whole lineage. The residual commons value, the permanent absence of the barrel, survives even the lowest discount rate, even Stern's. It survives because it is not a future flow of damage to be discounted at all. It is a present fact. The barrel is gone now. The absence exists now, fully, the moment the oil is burned. You cannot discount it away, because it is not in the future. The future merely inherits an absence that is already complete.

Every one of these thinkers did real and valuable work. None of them quite stood in the empty field and built the ledger that prices the thing itself, present and total, across all of time. That is the field I am trying to build in.

## McDowell County

Let me run the whole idea backward now, on something that has already happened, in a real place.

McDowell County, in the Appalachian mountains of the eastern United States, sat on top of a particular kind of coal. For generations the coal was dug out and sold at the mouth of the mine for a few dollars a ton; around 1960 the figure was roughly four to five dollars a ton. That was the price. That was what the transaction recorded. The coal went out, the money came in, and by the ordinary accounting of the time the deal was complete and the books balanced.

But the ordinary accounting recorded only the severed, visible part of the cost. It left out the part that was cut loose and handed to others. So let me add the severed costs back, and arrive at what I will call the honest price, the price that would have appeared if every cost, including the ones no bill ever reached, had been counted at the moment of the sale.

The largest severed cost, by far, is the carbon. When a ton of this coal is burned, it releases carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, where it stays and warms the climate for a very long time, and the cost of that warming falls on people across the whole world and across many future generations, none of whom were paid anything for bearing it. Economists have a name and a number for this cost. They call it the social cost of carbon, the dollar value of the harm done by releasing one ton of carbon dioxide. The Environmental Protection Agency of the United States, in an estimate published in 2023, put that figure at roughly one hundred and ninety dollars for each ton of carbon dioxide.

This particular coal, the Pocahontas coal of McDowell County, is unusually carbon-dense. Burning one ton of it releases about two point six one tons of carbon dioxide, higher than the national average of around two point three three tons. So the carbon cost of a single ton of McDowell coal is roughly two point six one tons of carbon dioxide multiplied by one hundred and ninety dollars per ton, which comes to about four hundred and ninety six dollars.

Add the remaining severed costs, the local harms to land and water and health, and the honest price of a ton of McDowell coal, the price that would have included everything, comes to more than five hundred dollars a ton, somewhere around five hundred and ten dollars in total. Nearly all of that, around four hundred and ninety six of the five hundred and ten dollars, is the carbon alone. The coal that sold for four or five dollars a ton carried a true cost more than a hundred times larger, and almost the entire gap was the carbon cost, severed from the sale and handed forward to the world and the future.

This is the framework run backward, on an extraction that has already happened, in a real place. We are not predicting a future loss. We are pricing, after the fact, what a past sale actually cost once we count the bills that were never sent. The price was always there. The accounting simply never looked at it.

## Forward and backward

So the ledger runs in two directions, and it helps to give each direction its own name and its own instrument.

Run forward, the ledger looks at an extraction that is about to happen and asks what it will cost the future, then sets that cost aside before the extraction begins. The instrument for this I will call a foreclosure bond. The word foreclose here means to close off a possibility, to take an option away from someone who comes later. Before you extract, you price the option you are about to close off, the resource that will be permanently gone, and you set aside a real reserve of value to answer for it. The bond is not a permission to extract without cost. It is a requirement to count and set aside the cost first.

Run backward, the ledger looks at an extraction that has already happened and asks what it cost, then asks what is owed because of it. The instrument for this I will call a restitution bond. Restitution means restoring or making good a loss that has already occurred. The McDowell calculation is the first half of this; the second half is the question of what is owed, and to whom, now that the true cost is visible.

The backward direction has a difficult and important application, and I want to handle it carefully. There is a great deal of extracted value in human history that was taken not through a sale at all but through force. The clearest case, in the country where McDowell County sits, is the value taken from enslaved people and their descendants across centuries. The economists William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen, in their book *From Here to Equality*, published in 2020, work out the accounting of this in careful detail. They trace the gap in wealth between Black and white Americans back to its origins in slavery beginning in 1619, and they show how that gap was built and maintained across generations. They also point to measurable effects that persist into the present, including a gap in life expectancy between Black and white Americans, a difference in how long people live that can be read as one legacy of the original extraction. Their work is honest accounting run backward, pricing an extraction already realized and asking what restitution it requires. The restitution bond is, in their terms, a way of operationalizing the restitution component of redress.

But I want to mark a clear limit on what this framework does here, because there is a kind of work that is not mine to do. Putting a price on the suffering of coercion itself, the direct cost of force imposed on a group of people, is not something I can responsibly attempt from the outside, as someone who does not belong to the group that bore it. That pricing belongs to the people who carry that history and who are continuing the work of accounting for it. What the framework can do is reach these cases through their measurable legacy effects, the wealth gap, the longevity gap, the inherited material consequences that can be counted. It defers the direct pricing of the coercion itself to those whose work it properly is. The ledger can be honest about what it can count and honest about what it must leave to others.

## Two countries, one act

Now I want to set two real places side by side, because between them they show that the forward ledger is not a fantasy. One country has come closer to running it than any other, and one country shows what happens when no one runs it at all. The same physical act, pumping oil out of the ground, took two completely different shapes in these two places, and the difference is entirely a difference of accounting.

The first place is Norway. Just before Christmas in 1969, a large oil field was confirmed beneath the North Sea, off the Norwegian coast, a field that came to be called Ekofisk. Norway was suddenly going to become wealthy from oil. And Norway made a decision that almost no other country has fully made. It decided that the oil wealth did not belong only to the Norwegians alive at the moment it was pumped. It belonged also to Norwegians not yet born, to the future of the country itself. The oil was a one-time inheritance, a stock of capital that could be spent only once, and to spend all of it in the present would be to take it away from everyone who came later.

So Norway built an instrument to hold it. In 1990 it created a fund, originally called the Government Petroleum Fund, later renamed the Government Pension Fund Global in 2006, into which the proceeds of the oil would be placed and invested. And in 2001 the country adopted a fiscal rule, a rule about spending, that is the heart of the whole design. The rule says that the government may spend, in any year, only the expected real return on the fund, on the order of three percent, the income the invested capital produces. The capital itself, the original wealth from the oil, is to be left untouched, inviolate, never spent down. The country lives off the income from the inheritance and never touches the inheritance itself.

The result is one of the most remarkable acts of long-term accounting any nation has performed. The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars, which is more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every Norwegian citizen. And here is the part that matters most. This discipline has held across the entire political spectrum, through governments of very different parties and philosophies. No government, of any party, has raided the principal. What Norway built is, in effect, the rule that the economist Hartwick described, applied at the scale of an entire nation across generations. It is the forward ledger made real. The oil was extracted, but the value was not consumed in the present and handed as an absence to the future. It was set aside, held in reserve, and made to last. It is also, worth noting, a reserve fund in the most literal sense, a store of value held back against the future, which is a theme we will return to.

The second place is the Niger Delta, in Nigeria. Oil has been pumped there since the nineteen fifties, and the revenue from it has run to hundreds of billions of dollars. But the costs of that extraction, the oil spilled across the land and water, the gas burned off in flares, the fishing grounds and farmland poisoned and foreclosed, fell on the people who lived in the delta, people who did not sell the oil, who saw little of the revenue, and who had no one to whom they could send the bill for what they had lost. The cost was severed completely from the sale.

In 1995, the writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa, who had led a movement of the Ogoni people against the environmental destruction of their homeland, was executed by the Nigerian government along with eight others, a killing that drew condemnation around the world and led to Nigeria's temporary suspension from the Commonwealth of Nations. Years later, an assessment by the United Nations of the environmental damage in Ogoniland concluded that simply beginning the cleanup would cost on the order of one billion dollars over an initial period of twenty five to thirty years, and that the full restoration would take far longer.

Set the two places beside each other. The same physical act, oil pumped from the ground for sale. In one place the future was priced and set aside, and the wealth was made to last across generations and across the whole political spectrum. In the other place the costs were severed, handed to people who never sold anything, and nothing was set aside at all, so that decades later the bill arrived anyway, in poisoned ground and a billion-dollar cleanup and a debt to the dead.

I want to be careful about what this comparison is and is not. It is not a story of villains and heroes. It is not an argument that oil should never have been extracted. It is not a political program. It is a comparison of two architectures of accounting. When the cost of an extraction outlives the sale that produced it, the question is always the same. Who carries that cost, and was anything set aside to answer for it? Norway answered the question. The Niger Delta was never allowed to ask it. That is the whole of the difference, and it is a difference of accounting, not of geology.

## Off-world: the ledger in operation

Everything so far has used the seal as a measuring device. We sealed a diving system, a glass dome, an orbiting station, in order to see the value that abundance hides. Now I want to do something different. I want to imagine actually living inside the seal, not as a measurement but as a home, with a working everyday economy, and to watch the honest ledger run forward, in real time, as the ordinary operating accounts of a world.

I want to be completely clear about what this is. I am not predicting that human beings will live in sealed habitats on other worlds. I am not forecasting anything. I am using an imagined situation as a lens, a way of seeing the framework in operation under conditions so extreme that nothing can hide. On the open planet, abundance lets value hide. On a sealed world, there is nowhere for value to hide, and so the ledger we have been building becomes simply the way the place keeps its books. The thought-experiment is a tool, not a forecast. With that said, let me build the picture one piece at a time.

### The colony and the airlock

Imagine a settlement on another world, a sealed habitat under a dome or beneath the surface, with several thousand people living inside it. Outside the seal there is no air a person can breathe, no water a person can drink, no climate a body can survive. Everything that the open Earth gives away for free, every commons that wears the mask of abundance at home, has to be manufactured, stored, recycled, and maintained inside the seal, by the people of the colony, at a cost that everyone can see and that no one can pretend is zero.

Now imagine arriving at this colony from outside, coming in through the airlock, the sealed double door that separates the survivable inside from the lethal outside. And imagine that at the airlock, before you are admitted, you are asked a question that would be absurd anywhere on Earth and is perfectly reasonable here. You are asked how you intend to pay for your air.

On Earth the question is meaningless. Air is free. It is the thing so abundant it has no price. But inside the colony, air is not abundant. Air is made, at a cost, by machines that take energy and water and produce breathable oxygen, machines that have to be built and maintained and powered, by people whose labour is also part of the cost. The carbon dioxide you breathe out has to be removed from the shared atmosphere of the colony, scrubbed out by other machines, or it will accumulate until it poisons everyone. So the air you breathe is genuinely an economic good inside the colony, as real and as costly as food or fuel, and the question at the airlock is simply an honest question about how you will cover the cost of the commons you are about to start consuming the moment you step inside.

Imagine, then, that inside the colony every one of these commons is metered and billed by how much of it you use, the way water and electricity are metered and billed in many cities on Earth today. There is a meter on the air, counting the oxygen you breathe. There is a meter on the water, counting each litre of recycled water you drink or wash with. There is a charge for the removal of the carbon dioxide you exhale, because that removal is a real service performed by real machines at a real cost. None of this is punishment and none of it is strange, inside the seal. It is simply honest accounting applied to the things that, on Earth, abundance had allowed everyone to ignore. The colony cannot afford the mask. So the mask is never put on in the first place, and the value that the mask would have hidden is simply on the ledger, every day, as the ordinary operating cost of being alive in that place.

Notice what this colony's books actually are. They are the same ledger we built for McDowell County and for Norway, run forward, in real time, as a daily economy rather than as an after-the-fact reckoning or a far-sighted reserve. In McDowell County we ran the ledger backward, pricing carbon that had already been released. In Norway we ran it forward across decades, setting aside the value of oil so the future would not be robbed. In the colony we run it forward in real time, hour by hour, breath by breath, because the seal makes any other accounting immediately fatal. It is one ledger, the same ledger, and the only thing that changes from McDowell to Norway to the colony is the scale and the direction in which it runs.

And here the argument closes a circle that it opened at the very beginning. We began by sealing a system in the imagination in order to see the value that abundance hides. The seal was a measuring instrument, a way to make the invisible visible, to take the mask off and read the true price. Now we have stepped inside the seal and made it a home. And inside the home, the value we used the seal to see has become the value we actually pay, every day, by the litre and the breath. First the seal let us see the hidden value. Now we live inside the seal and we pay it. The measuring device has become the economy. That is what it means for the ledger to be honest. The number was always there. On Earth, abundance let us look away from it. Inside the colony, there is nowhere to look but at it.

There is something almost clarifying in this picture, for all that the conditions imagined are harsh. On Earth, a person can spend an entire life without once being asked to account for the air, the water, the carbon, the climate, all the commons that keep them alive, because abundance keeps the bill hidden. Inside the colony, the bill is never hidden, and so a person inside the colony understands, in a way that is difficult to hold onto at home, exactly how much they depend on the commons and exactly what those commons cost to maintain. The colonist is not poorer in understanding than the person on Earth. The colonist is richer in understanding, because the colonist can see the whole ledger, while the person on Earth sees only the part that abundance has not hidden.

### The asteroid miner

Now move further out, away from the colony, away from any settlement at all, into the empty space between worlds, to a single small ship working alone among the asteroids. Here the picture changes, and the change is exactly what shows that this framework is not a blanket objection to extraction. It is something more careful than that.

Imagine a miner on a small asteroid, a lump of rock and metal drifting in the dark, with no community living on it, no ecosystem, no people downstream, no fishery to poison, no neighbours to harm, no future generation tied to this particular rock. The asteroid is just metal and stone. If you extract iron from it, who bears the cost? Almost no one. There is no severed cost here, or very nearly none, because there is no community and no living system to absorb a harm that gets cut loose from the sale. The cost severance that defined every case on Earth falls, out here, very nearly to zero.

And so the honest ledger, the same ledger that priced McDowell's carbon and built Norway's fund and meters the colony's air, says something here that might at first seem surprising. It says: extract freely. Take the iron. There is no one to harm and no cost to sever, so the careful accounting that would slow you down on a living world has almost nothing to weigh here. If a comet swings briefly through reach, carrying water or metal, and will not pass this way again for a very long time, the ledger says take what you can while you can, aggressively, because nothing is being foreclosed for anyone who would otherwise have had it. The framework is not opposed to extraction. Where the real cost is genuinely near zero, the framework says so plainly, and it says to go ahead.

But the same ledger says something else at the same time, and the two things together are the whole point. On the rock itself, alone in the dark, the miner must hold reserves. Fuel to get off the asteroid and back home, or on to the next world. Air to breathe for the whole journey, with a margin for failure. Water and food and the working life-support that keeps a person alive in a place that is trying, every second, to kill them. The miner cannot extract every gram of capacity and use every drop of fuel and breathe every litre of air, because then there would be nothing held back, and the first failure, the first delay, the first thing that goes wrong, would be fatal. To survive, the miner must keep enough in reserve to be sure of getting home, or of reaching the next place that can sustain life.

So the very same ledger that says "extract freely here" says, in the same breath, "hold reserves there." And this is the heart of what honest accounting actually is. It is not a rule that says extraction is wrong. It is not a rule that says extraction is right. It is a method for knowing the real cost of what you do, including the costs that are hidden, and for keeping enough held back to survive the future you are extracting your way into. On the asteroid the real cost of taking the iron is near zero, so the ledger says take it. But the real cost of leaving yourself without fuel or air is your life, so the ledger says hold those in reserve. The framework does not love extraction or hate it. It insists only on the truth about the cost, and on keeping back enough to live.

This is the same discipline that Norway followed when it spent only the income from its oil fund and never the capital. Norway held the principal in reserve so that the future would not be stranded. The asteroid miner holds fuel and air in reserve so that the journey home is not stranded. The scale is wildly different, a nation across centuries on one hand and a single ship across a single voyage on the other. The discipline is identical. Know the real cost. Keep enough back to survive the future you are heading into. That is the ledger, run forward, on a rock in the dark, by a single person whose life depends on getting the accounting right.

## Whatever economy you choose

I want to step back now and say clearly what kind of thing this framework is and what kind of thing it is not, because it would be easy to mistake it for a rival to the economic systems people already argue about.

It is not a rival system. It is not capitalism and it is not its opposite. It is not a plan for how an economy should be owned or organised or governed. It is something underneath all of those choices, something that any economy needs no matter how it is organised. It is the accounting. It is the requirement that the books be honest, that the real costs be visible, that the value abundance hides be put on the ledger before it is spent. You can run a market economy or a planned economy or a mixed economy or a small community economy, and whichever you choose, you still have to know your real costs, or you are deciding in the dark.

Consider how many different traditions, across the whole span of human history and human politics, turn out to be attempts at exactly this. The economist Kate Raworth, in her work on what she calls doughnut economics, draws a picture of a safe operating space for humanity, bounded on the outside by the ecological limits of the planet and on the inside by the basic needs every person must have met. It is a way of drawing the real costs and the real limits so that they can be seen and respected. Elinor Ostrom, whom we met earlier, spent a career documenting how communities build rules to manage shared resources within their limits, which is to say how they keep honest books on a commons. And the impulse is far older than any modern economist. Ancient civilizations built systems to hold grain in reserve against the years of scarcity that everyone knew would eventually come. Ancient China maintained, over long stretches of its history, a system of state granaries designed to stabilise the supply of grain across good years and bad. Ancient Egypt likewise stored grain on a large scale against future need. I keep these references deliberately at a high level, because the detailed history is not mine to embroider, and the point does not depend on the particulars. The point is only this. Across thousands of years, in radically different societies, organised in radically different ways, human beings keep arriving at the same underlying move: know the real costs, and hold reserves against the scarcity you can see coming.

Capitalist or planned, ancient or modern, on this world or on a sealed world far from this one, the requirement is the same. The ledger has to be honest, or the economy is making its largest decisions without being able to see the costs of them. An economy with a dishonest ledger is not steering toward anything. It is moving without sight, and the things it cannot see are precisely the commons, the most valuable things it holds, hidden behind the mask of their own abundance.

## Reserves on the longest horizon

I have used the word reserves several times now, and I want to follow it as far as it goes, because it goes much further than the next year, or the next generation, or even the next few centuries.

A finite resource, once it is gone, is gone for everyone who comes after, forever. We have said this about a barrel of oil. But the principle does not stop at the next generation. It extends across the whole future of the species, across spans of time so long that they are difficult even to imagine. If we take the idea of holding reserves seriously, and the whole framework insists that we should, then the horizon over which we owe reserves is not a hundred years or a thousand years. It is the entire future during which human beings, or their descendants, might exist.

That future has real outer limits, and it is worth naming them plainly, because they change the way the off-world thought-experiment should be read. Our Sun is slowly growing brighter as it ages. On the order of five hundred million to one billion years from now, the Sun's rising brightness will have warmed the Earth so much that the planet will no longer be able to support life as we know it, long before anything dramatic happens to the Sun itself. Much further out, on the order of seven and a half billion years from now, the Sun will swell into a red giant and is expected to engulf the Earth entirely. These are not predictions about anything that concerns a human lifetime, or a civilization's lifetime, or anything we will ever witness. They are the outer boundary conditions of the whole human project, the furthest edges of the field on which everything we do is played.

But they change how we should read the picture of a sealed habitat on another world. Seen against the next election or the next business quarter, a colony on another world looks like science fiction, a fantasy disconnected from any real concern. Seen against the actual lifespan of the planet, it looks like something else. It looks like the longest-horizon form of holding reserves, the preservation of an option, the keeping-open of a possibility for descendants so distant we cannot picture them, on a timescale where the Earth itself is not a permanent home. The off-world habitat is not, in this light, an escape from the framework's logic. It is the framework's logic carried to its farthest conclusion. If holding reserves against future scarcity is the right thing to do across a year, and across a generation, and across a century, then it is the right thing to do across the longest horizon there is, and the longest horizon there is eventually requires somewhere other than this single world to hold the reserves in.

I do not raise this to frighten anyone, and the timescales are so vast that fright would be misplaced. I raise it only to show that the framework is consistent all the way down, or rather all the way out. The same logic that meters a colonist's air and holds an asteroid miner's fuel and protects Norway's oil capital and prices McDowell's carbon also, at the limit, makes the preservation of options across deep time a coherent and even an obligatory concern. One ledger, run forward, on every horizon, from the next breath to the last billion years.

## The mask comes off

Let me come back, at the end, to the breath I asked you to take at the beginning.

It still costs you nothing. The air is still free. And now we can see why. Not because it is worthless, but because it is so completely valuable, so evenly spread across the whole planet, that the only instrument we trust to measure value cannot find an edge to grip and so reports a zero. The zero was never a verdict on the air. It was a confession by the instrument. The mask of abundance is made of the abundance itself, and it hides the most valuable things we have precisely because, for now, there is enough of them to go around.

The whole of this essay has been a single move, repeated at different scales and in different directions. Find the value that abundance, or distance, or time has hidden, and put it honestly on the ledger. We did it to a breath, and to a barrel of oil, and to a county that sold its coal too cheaply, and to two countries that pumped the same oil into two completely different futures, and to an imagined colony that meters its air, and to an imagined miner alone on a rock in the dark. Each time it was the same ledger. The only things that changed were the scale, from a single breath to a sovereign fund to the longest horizon there is, and the direction, sometimes run forward to price a future loss and set it aside, sometimes run backward to price a loss already realized and ask what is owed.

This honest accounting is not the property of any one kind of economy, or any one political program, or any one country. It is something an individual can run, in deciding what they owe to the people who will come after them. It is something a community can run, in deciding how to hold its shared resources in trust. It is something a government can run, as Norway has shown a government can. And it would be something a colony on another world would have to run, every day, just to stay alive, because a sealed world cannot afford the mask. One ledger, on any scale, on any world, run in either direction.

The deepest reason to do this is not contained in any number, though the numbers matter and I have tried to be honest about them. The deepest reason is that the costs we cannot see are not borne by no one. They are borne by people. By people far away from us in space, downstream of the river or downwind of the flare. And by people far away from us in time, not yet born, who will inherit the absence of what we spent and had no voice in the spending. The hidden costs are owed to people who were never at the table, across distance and across time. To make those costs visible is, in the end, to acknowledge that those people exist, and that we owe them something, and that what we owe them can be counted and set aside before it is too late to set anything aside at all.

The freedom not to think about the air, the freedom to take the breath without being asked at any airlock how you intend to pay for it, is a real freedom, and it is worth wanting to keep. Whether we keep it is not decided for us. It is a thing we still get to decide, while there is still abundance enough to hide the value, and time enough to choose to see it anyway. The mask can come off whenever we are willing to take it off. The number was always there underneath it, waiting, true, and ours to read.
