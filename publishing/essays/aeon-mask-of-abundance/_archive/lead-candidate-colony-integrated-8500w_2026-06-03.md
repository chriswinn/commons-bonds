# The Mask of Abundance

Breathe in. Now breathe out. You have just consumed something, and you have just emitted something, and no meter ran. No charge appeared. The transaction you completed a moment ago is the most reliable purchase you will ever make, and you made it for nothing, and you will make it again in a few seconds, and you will go on making it tens of thousands of times today without once thinking of it as a purchase at all. The air is free. We say this as if it were a fact about the air, the way we say that water is wet or that iron is heavy. It is not a fact about the air. It is a fact about us, about the only instrument we have built for telling worth, and about the strange blindness that instrument carries at its center.

The instrument is price. We trust it the way an earlier age trusted the assayer's scale, and for the same reason: it seems to convert the messy human business of wanting things into a single honest number. A loaf of bread, a barrel of oil, an hour of a stranger's labor, all of it resolves to dollars, and the dollars can be compared, summed, traded, argued over. But price does not measure worth. Price measures scarcity at the margin. It tells you what the next unit costs, what someone will give up to get one more, what someone will accept to part with one more. That is an enormously useful thing to know, and we have built a civilization on knowing it. The trouble is that we have mistaken the reading for the thing read. We have come to believe that what has no price has no worth, when often the truth is the reverse: the thing has nothing but worth, distributed so completely, so far past any margin, that no market can find an edge to charge at.

Consider the air again. It is not cheap. Cheap is what a thing is when it is plentiful and somewhat valued, like sand or salt. The air is something else. It is the precondition of every breath, every fire, every harvest, every life you have ever known or heard of, and its price is zero. The zero is not a verdict that the air is worthless. The zero is a confession that the instrument has failed. Abundance posts a zero, and we read the zero as a statement about value, when it is only a statement about the margin, which abundance has pushed out of reach. A thing with no price is invisible to the only instrument we trust, and so we walk through a world whose most important features we cannot see.

This is what I will call the mask of abundance. It is not a metaphor I am reaching for; it is a mechanism, and once you see it operating in the air you will see it operating nearly everywhere the world has been generous. The commons, the things held by everyone and owned by no one, the air and the deep water and the carrying-away of carbon and the stability of the climate that lets a farmer plant in confidence, are not merely underpriced. We know about underpricing. A nurse is underpriced. Topsoil is underpriced. There is a market for each, and the market sets a number, and the number is too low, and you can argue about how much too low. But the commons have no number at all. They never entered the market, because abundance kept the margin out of reach, and a thing that never entered the market cannot be undervalued by it. It can only be unseen.

I want to say plainly, here at the start, what this essay is trying to do, because I will be returning to it at every turn and I would rather you watch me do it than wonder. I am trying to build the outline of an honest ledger. A way of accounting that works on this world and on any other, an accounting that does its single job by making visible the costs that abundance and distance and time have hidden. That is the whole of the claim. Not a new economic system, not a politics, not a prophecy. A ledger honest enough to show the numbers that were always there, and a method for finding them when the mask is on. Everything that follows is one move, performed at larger and larger scales: find the value that the world's generosity has hidden, and put it on the ledger where it belongs.

## The lens of the seal

To find a hidden value, the surest method is to make it scarce, because scarcity is the one condition the instrument can read. You cannot easily put a price on the air of Earth, but you can imagine a place where the air must be made, where every litre of it has a cost and a keeper, and you can watch what happens to the number when the abundance is taken away. Let me build such a place. I am not predicting it and I am not recommending it; I am using it as a lens, the way an astronomer uses a dark room to see a faint star. Imagine a sealed habitat. A volume of space cut off from the great free atmosphere, a bubble of human life with a hard shell between it and the void. Inside, the air does not arrive on the wind. It is manufactured, scrubbed, recycled, accounted for. Now ask the question that Earth never forces you to ask: what is a breath worth in here?

The lens is imaginary, but it has three anchors in the real, three places where the bill for the priceless commons already arrives, and the arriving is instructive.

Go down, first, into the deep water, where the saturation divers work. A diver living at depth for days does not breathe the air you and I breathe. Nitrogen, harmless at the surface, turns narcotic under pressure, clouding the mind like wine; oxygen itself, the very thing that keeps you alive, turns toxic when its pressure climbs too high. So the diver breathes heliox, a blend in which helium stands in for the nitrogen, and helium is not free. It runs somewhere between a dollar and change and a few dollars a cubic foot, and a working diver goes through it fast enough that the helium alone can cost on the order of a hundred dollars an hour, more in deep work. The cost is high enough that the ships that support saturation diving run reclaim rigs, machines whose only purpose is to catch the exhaled helium and clean it and breathe it back, because at those prices you do not let your breath escape into the sea. Here is the first crack in the mask. The same element that floats a child's balloon becomes, behind a seal and under pressure, a metered utility with an hourly rate, and grown engineers build machinery to keep it from being wasted.

Go, second, into the Arizona desert at the start of the 1990s, to the great glass ark called Biosphere 2. The idea was beautiful and the execution was serious: seal eight people inside roughly three acres of glassed-over land, a miniature Earth with its own farm and forest and little sea, some hundred and fifty million dollars of ambition, and let them live there closed off from the world for two years. What happened to the air inside that ark is the heart of the lesson. Over sixteen months the oxygen fell, and kept falling, from the ordinary twenty-one percent down toward fourteen, a level you would meet near the top of a high mountain, and the crew grew tired and short of breath. The causes were quiet and cumulative: the rich soil they had brought in fed microbes that breathed oxygen as we do, and the carbon dioxide those microbes produced bound itself into the fresh concrete of the structure and did not come back as oxygen the way the planners had assumed. In the end the managers had to pump oxygen in from outside, which is to say the experiment in self-sufficiency had to buy back the one commodity it had taken most for granted. Nineteen of the twenty-five vertebrate species inside died. Every pollinator died, so the crops that needed pollinating had to be served by human hands. A sealed acre of Earth, lovingly built, could not give its keepers the thing the open Earth gives six billion people without a thought.

Go, third, all the way up, a few hundred kilometres, to the International Space Station, the most complete sealed habitat our species has yet kept running. Here the accounting is brutally explicit, because everything must be lifted out of Earth's gravity at a price, and water, that other free thing, costs roughly twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram to put in orbit. A six-month supply for the crew, reckoned the crude way, runs to something on the order of a hundred and thirty-five million dollars, which is why the station does not simply carry its water. It recycles it, recovering something like ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine back into drinking water, and then it splits some of that water by electrolysis to make the oxygen the crew breathes. On the station, your breath and your drink are the same budget line, and the line is enormous, and engineers spend their careers shaving percentage points off it.

Three seals, three bills. And in each the same thing has happened: the priceless has become the most expensive thing in the room. This is the lens working. Seal the system, take away the abundance, and the commons that Earth distributes so completely that no price can find them resolve, suddenly and without argument, into the largest numbers on the ledger. The value was always there. The seal did not create it. The seal only let the instrument read it.

## The name of the thing

Now that we can see the value, we need a way to talk about how it goes missing, because the disappearing is not random. It follows a pattern, and the pattern has a shape worth naming. Call it cost severance. Someone creates a cost. Someone else absorbs it. And the second party has no way to send the bill back to the first. The cost is severed from the act that caused it, cut loose and set adrift, and it lands wherever the wind and the water and the years happen to carry it.

A factory upstream that fouls a river has severed a cost; the fishermen downstream absorb it and cannot bill it back. A generation that burns through a finite store has severed a cost; the generations that come after absorb it and, being unborn, cannot bill anyone at all. Cost severance is the engine underneath most of what we call externalities, but the word externality has always been too mild, too geometric, as if the cost had simply rolled to the outside of a tidy circle. It did not roll. It was cut. And the cutting is usually invisible precisely because the thing cut away had no price to begin with, which returns us to the mask.

The severance has two directions, and it is worth holding them apart, because most of our moral language is built for only one of them. There is severance across space, the factory and the fishermen, the cost that travels sideways from the one who made it to the one who happens to stand downstream. We have a long practice of arguing about that kind, of suing over it and regulating it and occasionally pricing it, because the injured party is alive and present and can, at least in principle, point at the thing that hurt them. And then there is severance across time, which is far stranger and far harder to argue about, because the injured party is not present, has not been born, cannot point at anything, cannot sue, cannot vote, cannot even be named. A cost severed across time lands on a population that does not yet exist, and a population that does not yet exist makes a poor plaintiff. This is the asymmetry the framework keeps running into. The whole apparatus of price and law and grievance is built for parties who can show up. The residual commons value is owed, more often than not, to parties who cannot, because they are downstream not in the river but in the years, and the years have no courtroom.

Set cost severance beside its sibling, value extraction. When you take something of worth out of a commons and sell it, the sale captures a price, the price set at the margin by present scarcity. But the worth of the thing is rarely exhausted by the present margin. There is, very often, a remainder. A worth that outlives the sale, that extends past the moment of the transaction to people who were not at the table when the price was struck, and frequently to people not yet born. I will call this remainder the residual commons value. It is the part of the worth that the price did not reach, because price measures the margin and the margin is now, while the worth runs on into a future the margin cannot price.

Most of the time, honest accounting is a matter of re-attaching a severed cost. You find the bill that drifted downstream, you trace it back to the act that cut it loose, and you put it back where it belongs. That is hard work, but it is conceptually clean. The commons case is stranger, and the strangeness is the whole reason this essay needs to exist. The residual commons value was never severed from a price, because it never had a price to be severed from. It was hidden by abundance from the start, and it becomes visible only when scarcity arrives, the way the value of the air becomes visible only behind a seal. So honest accounting for the commons is not merely the re-attaching of a stray cost. It is the much rarer act of pricing a worth that abundance kept off the books entirely, of writing onto the ledger a number that the market, by its nature, was never going to find. The seal showed us the number could exist. Now we have to learn to write it down without the seal, here, in the open air, where abundance is still doing its quiet work of hiding.

## The barrel and the permanence

Take a barrel of oil. Say it is priced at one hundred dollars, and let me say outright that the hundred is illustrative, a round figure to think with rather than a quotation from any particular day's market. What does the hundred dollars buy, and for whom?

It buys the present a barrel of oil. The hundred dollars reflects what the present world will give to have it, what the marginal buyer will pay against the marginal seller, here, now, today. It is an honest price for a present thing. But the barrel is not only a present thing. The barrel took something like the age of dinosaurs to make, and once it is burned it is gone, and gone is not a figure of speech. The thing that the hundred dollars priced extends, in its consequences and in its absence, to permanence. The price is struck for the present; the loss runs forever. That gap, between a price made for now and a thing that lasts for always, is exactly the region where the residual commons value lives.

Here a distinction earns its keep, the distinction between renewable and non-renewable, and it is sharper than the casual use of those words suggests. A renewable resource poses a question of rate. A forest, a fishery, an aquifer fed by rain, each of these breeds, regenerates, refills, and the only honest question is whether you are taking less than it makes. Take less than it breeds and you can take forever; take more and you draw down the stock toward zero. The accounting for a renewable commons is the accounting of a rate, and it is the accounting that Elinor Ostrom spent her life describing, the careful local rules by which communities hold their taking below their commons' rate of return.

Oil is not like that. Oil does not breed. There is no rate of extraction slow enough to be sustainable, because the stock does not regenerate on any timescale that matters to anyone who will ever live. Oil is not harvested; it is liquidated. Every barrel pulled up is a barrel that will never exist again, and so the question of rate, which is the whole of the renewable problem, does not even apply. The barrel is a one-time transfer from the deep past to the brief present, and the hundred dollars buys the present its tank of gas and buys nothing back for anyone who comes after, because there is nothing to buy back. The store is simply smaller now, by one, forever. To price the barrel honestly you would have to price not only the gasoline but the permanence of its absence, and the permanence of an absence is exactly the kind of worth that has no margin and therefore no price and therefore stays hidden behind the mask until you go looking for it on purpose.

It is worth dwelling on why the market is structurally unable to find this number, because the failure is not laziness or wickedness; it is a property of the instrument. A price is set by buyers and sellers who are present. The marginal buyer and the marginal seller meet, and between them they discover what the next barrel is worth to the living. But the living are not the only parties with a stake in the barrel. The barrel matters to everyone who will ever want energy and find that the easy stores are gone, and none of those people are at the market, because most of them are not yet alive, and the ones who are alive and very young have no money and no standing to bid. The future cannot bid against the present for the barrel. It has no agent in the room, no representative at the margin, no dollar to put down. And so the price the market discovers is not the worth of the barrel; it is the worth of the barrel to the bidders who happen to exist, which is a smaller and more parochial thing. The market is not failing to do its job. It is doing its job exactly, which is to clear the present against itself, and the residual commons value is simply the worth that no present party was positioned to defend.

## A lineage of near-misses

I am not the first to walk up to this problem. The honest thing, and the useful thing, is to say where others reached it and where they stopped, because the pattern of the stopping is itself a kind of map. Each of these thinkers saw a real edge of the same continent and named it truly, and each, for reasons that made sense from where they stood, did not cross to the particular ground I am trying to reach.

Karl Marx, in the first volume of *Capital*, priced a severance that the market of his day was busy hiding. He showed that the value a worker produces and the wage a worker is paid come apart, and that the gap, the surplus, is captured by the owner of the means of production. That is a cost severance, named and quantified, and it changed the world's argument with itself. But the axis Marx worked along was class, the division of the present world's product between those who labor and those who own. His severance ran sideways, across a society, not forward, across time. The barrel's loss to the unborn was not the loss he was pricing.

Elinor Ostrom, in *Governing the Commons*, did something the economics of her day had declared impossible: she showed that communities could and did govern shared resources without either private ownership or central command, through patient local institutions, and she was given the Nobel for it in 2009. But the commons she governed were renewable. Her brilliance was the brilliance of rate, of communities holding their fisheries and forests and irrigation systems below the threshold of ruin. The liquidated commons, the barrel that breeds nothing and once gone is gone, was not the case her institutions were built for.

Herman Daly, who died in 2022, spent his career insisting that the economy is a creature living inside the biosphere and not the other way around, and that there is such a thing as too much throughput, too much matter and energy shoved through the human system per year. He gave us the steady state, the idea of an economy sized to fit its planet. But throughput is a flow, a rate of passage, and Daly's warning, profound as it is, did not issue an invoice for one specific foreclosed thing, did not say of this barrel, here, that its permanent absence is worth a particular number owed to a particular future.

John Hartwick, in a short and beautiful paper in 1977, came the closest of the economists. He asked what a nation living off a finite resource would have to do to keep its consumption from collapsing once the resource ran out, and he answered with a rule that bears his name: reinvest the rents, the pure scarcity-value of the extracted resource, into reproducible capital, so that the income from the new capital replaces the income from the vanishing resource. Hartwick saved the rent. He showed how to keep the money working after the barrel was gone. But the rent is not the barrel. The rent is the scarcity-value the market could see; the residual commons value is the worth the market could not see, the permanence the price never reached. Hartwick's rule keeps a nation solvent. It does not, by itself, put the full hidden number on the ledger.

Then the moral philosophers, who came at the same country from the other coast, the coast of obligation rather than of price. John Rawls, in *A Theory of Justice*, asked us to design a society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which person in it we would turn out to be, and showed that fairness is what you would choose when you do not know your place. Widen that veil across generations, so that you do not know which generation you will be born into, and the obligation to the future stops being charity and becomes a condition of justice. Michael Sandel, in *What Money Can't Buy*, pressed on a different nerve: that there are goods which the act of pricing corrupts, civic goods and sacred goods and goods of relationship that are degraded by being put up for sale, so that the market, left to itself, does not merely fail to price some things but actively damages them by trying. And Derek Parfit, in *Reasons and Persons*, did the hard metaphysical labor of taking future people seriously as people, real bearers of harm and benefit whose nonexistence-as-yet does not cancel our duties to them. Among them, these three established that the unborn are at the table even though their chairs are empty, that some worth resists pricing, and that justice has a temporal direction. What they did not do, being philosophers and not accountants, was hand us a number.

And finally the discount-rate battleground, where the question of the future met the machinery of finance and produced a war. When Nicholas Stern delivered his review of the economics of climate change in the mid-2000s, he chose a low discount rate, around one and a half percent, on the moral ground that a future person's welfare should not count for drastically less than ours merely because they are further off, and he concluded that strong action now was worth roughly one percent of global product. William Nordhaus, who would later win his own Nobel, ran the numbers through his DICE model with a higher rate, nearer four percent, drawn more from observed market returns than from moral first principles, and reached far more relaxed conclusions about how fast to act. Martin Weitzman came at both of them from the side, arguing that what should dominate the calculation is not the central estimate at all but the fat tail of the distribution, the small but real chance of catastrophe so large that no ordinary cost-benefit sum can contain it.

It is a deep and serious quarrel, and I do not want to flatten it. But notice what all three combatants are pricing. They are pricing the damage flow from combustion, the harm that the carbon does once it is in the air, the floods and droughts and heat that follow. They are arguing about how heavily to weigh a stream of future harms against a present cost. That is a real and necessary thing to price. It is not the thing I am pointing at. The residual commons value is not a damage flow. It is the present absence of a thing that will not return. And an absence is not a stream of future harms to be discounted year by year toward zero; it is a fact about now, a hole in the present world's stock of the irreplaceable. This is why the residual commons value survives even Stern's gentle rate. You can discount a future harm until it nearly vanishes, the way a hundred dollars of benefit a century out is worth only about twenty-five dollars today at Stern's rate and barely a dollar and a half at Nordhaus's. But you cannot discount away the present fact that the barrel is gone, because that fact is not in the future. It is here. The discounting machinery, built to compare a present cost against a future flow, has no purchase on a worth that is fully present and simply unpriced.

So the lineage circles the thing without quite landing on it. Marx priced the severance across class but not across time. Ostrom governed the commons that breeds but not the one that is liquidated. Daly warned about the flow but did not invoice the foreclosed thing. Hartwick saved the rent but not the barrel. The philosophers seated the future at the table but handed us no number. The discount-rate economists priced the damage but not the absence. What is left, in the middle of all that converging attention, is the residual commons value, the present worth of an irreplaceable thing's permanent loss, and the ledger that would write it down.

## Run it backward: a county in the mountains

So far I have been pricing forward, asking what a barrel not yet burned will cost the future. But a ledger that only runs forward is half a ledger. The same accounting can run backward, over an extraction already completed, and when it does the numbers stop being hypothetical and start being history. Let me take it to McDowell County, in the mountains of West Virginia, where the coal came out of the ground for generations.

At the mine mouth, around 1960, that coal sold for something like four or five dollars a ton. That was its price, struck at the margin, honest as far as it went, which was not far. Now run the ledger backward and add the severed costs back on, the costs that were cut loose and carried off and never billed to the buyer of that cheap coal. The largest by far is the carbon. Burning a ton of coal releases roughly two and a half tons of carbon dioxide, and if you value that carbon dioxide at the kind of social cost that careful estimates now reach, on the order of a hundred and ninety dollars a ton, the carbon term alone comes to something near five hundred and ten dollars. Add the rest of what was severed and the honest price of that ton of coal climbs to roughly five hundred and twenty-four dollars, with reasonable estimates landing somewhere in the low five hundreds.

Sit with the size of that gap. The market saw four or five dollars. The honest ledger says five hundred and twenty-four. The difference, more than a hundredfold, is not fraud and it is not waste in the ordinary sense. It is severance, made visible by running the accounting backward over a realized extraction. Every dollar of that gap was a real cost, created when the coal was burned, absorbed by someone, and never billed back to the act that caused it. McDowell County sold its coal at the mine mouth for the price the margin would bear, and the rest of the bill drifted off downstream and downwind and down the years, and has been arriving, in pieces, ever since, to people who never struck that price and could not have.

And notice what the size of the gap does to the usual story about cheap energy. We tend to say that the coal age was a bargain, that it lifted the modern world out of cold and dark at a price the world could afford, and in the narrow ledger of the margin that is true: four or five dollars a ton is cheap, almost unbelievably cheap, for the heat and the light and the steel it bought. But the narrow ledger is the masked one. The honest ledger says the coal was never four dollars. It was always something near five hundred, and the difference was not a discount. It was a loan, taken out silently against the future and against the lungs and the climate and the unborn, a loan that nobody signed for and that the borrowers mostly did not know they were taking. The cheapness was the mask. The bill was always the real price; it simply arrived later, addressed to people who were not in the room when the purchase was made. Running the ledger backward does not change history. It only reads the receipt that was always attached to it, the receipt the price hid.

## Forward and in reverse

A ledger that runs in both directions wants an instrument for each direction, and I will name two, plainly, without dressing them up. Run forward, against a loss not yet realized, the instrument is a foreclosure bond: you price the future loss before you cause it, and you set the money aside, so that the worth you are about to remove from the world is at least held in trust against the absence you are about to create. Run backward, against an extraction already realized, the instrument is a restitution bond: you price what was taken and what was severed, after the fact, and you reckon what is owed.

The backward direction is not a thought experiment, and it has been worked out with more rigor than anything I could add, by William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen in their book *From Here to Equality*. Their subject is reparations for Black Americans, and what they have built, whether or not they would put it in my vocabulary, is backward honest accounting at national scale. They trace the racial wealth gap back to 1619 and through the long architecture of slavery and its aftermaths, and they insist that the gap is not a vague historical sadness but a measurable quantity with a measurable cause. They point, among much else, to the Black-white longevity gap, the difference in how long lives last, as a legacy effect you can put a number to, a severed cost still arriving generations after the act that cut it loose. Ta-Nehisi Coates, in his essay *The Case for Reparations*, built the argument-architecture companion to that economics, the moral and narrative scaffolding on which such an accounting stands. This is the restitution bond, made concrete by people who have given it their careers.

And here I have to mark a boundary carefully, because the ledger can overreach, and the overreach would be a kind of presumption. There is a difference between reaching a coerced injustice through its legacy effects, the longevity gap, the wealth gap, the measurable downstream costs still arriving, and presuming to put a price on the coercion itself, on the violence and bondage at the root. The first is accounting. The second is not mine to do. To set a number on the cost of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to would be to take from the people continuing that work the thing that is theirs to carry. So the framework, as I am building it, reaches the coerced cases through their legacy effects, the costs that severed and drifted and can be traced and reckoned, and defers the direct pricing of the coercion vector to those whose work it properly is. The ledger is a tool, not a license, and knowing what is not yours to price is part of pricing honestly.

## Two architectures, one act

Take the forward instrument now and hold it up against the world, because the world has already run the experiment, in two places, with two opposite results, on the same physical act. The act is pulling oil out of the ground. The question is what happens to the residual commons value, the worth that outlasts the sale.

Norway found oil off its North Sea coast just before Christmas in 1969, at a field called Ekofisk, and what Norway did next is as close as the real world comes to running the honest ledger forward at sovereign scale. The country set up a fund, the Government Petroleum Fund, in 1990, renamed the Government Pension Fund Global in 2006, and it poured the oil money in rather than out. Then, in 2001, it bound itself with a fiscal rule: the government may spend only the expected real return on the fund, around three percent, and may not touch the capital, which is to keep inviolate. The oil is finite, but the fund made from it is meant to last as long as the nation does. Today that fund holds well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every citizen of the country, and the most remarkable thing about it is its durability across the whole political spectrum. Governments of the left and the right, Stoltenberg and Bondevik and Solberg and Støre, have held power and handed it on, and none of them, of any color, has raided the principal. This is Hartwick's rule made real and then made permanent, the rent reinvested and the capital fenced off, a reserve fund set against the day the barrel is gone. Norway looked at the residual commons value, the worth of the oil beyond its present price, and instead of letting it drift off downstream, it put it in trust.

Now hold up the counter-case, and let me be careful in how I hold it, because it is a place of real suffering and not a morality play with a designated villain. Oil has been pumped from the Niger Delta since the 1950s, and the revenue from it runs to hundreds of billions of dollars over the decades. But the costs, the spills that ruin the creeks, the gas flares that burn day and night, the fisheries foreclosed and the farmland soured, have been borne overwhelmingly by people who did not sell the oil and could not bill anyone for what its extraction took from them. The severance there was near total. The price went one way and the costs went another, and the people in between absorbed what drifted onto them. The writer Ken Saro-Wiwa organized against that severance on behalf of the Ogoni people, and in 1995 he and eight others were executed, an act that drew Nigeria's suspension from the Commonwealth. Years later, when the United Nations assessed the environmental damage in Ogoniland, it estimated that even an initial cleanup would cost on the order of a billion dollars and take twenty-five to thirty years, and that was the down payment, not the bill in full.

Same physical act. Same barrels, more or less, pulled from the same kind of deep rock. Two architectures, and the difference between them is the whole of what this essay is about. In one, the residual commons value was seen and held in trust against the future. In the other, it was severed and left to land on whoever was standing underneath. The question is not whether to extract; both societies extracted. The question is what you do with the worth that outlives the sale, whether you set anything aside, and who carries the cost when the cost outlives the act. This is architecture, not a political program. I am not telling Norway or Nigeria what to want. I am pointing at a structural difference that the ledger can see and the price cannot.

## Off-world: the framework in operation

I have been using the seal as a lens, a way to make a hidden value briefly visible and then setting the lens down. Now I want to do something different. I want to imagine actually living inside the seal, not as a measurement but as a home, and watch the framework stop being a method of audit and become the ordinary operating ledger of a working economy. I am not predicting that we will live this way and I am not urging it. I am using two scenes as instruments, lenses again, but lenses I will walk around inside, because the off-world case strips away the abundance entirely and lets the framework run forward, in real time, with nothing hidden. On a world too sealed to let abundance hide any value, honest accounting is not an audit you perform once a year. It is simply how the economy works, breath by breath.

### The colony economy

Imagine you have come to live in such a place, a habitat on another world, a city under a dome or down in the rock with a hard shell against the void. You arrive at the airlock, and before you are let in, you are asked a question that no border on Earth has ever asked anyone: how do you intend to pay for your air?

On Earth the question is obscene, a tyrant's question. In the habitat it is merely accounting, as ordinary as being asked how you intend to pay your rent, because here the air is not a commons abundant past all margins. It is manufactured, and finite, and someone makes it and someone maintains the making, and there is no great free atmosphere outside to fall back on. So inside, every commons that the Earth gives away for nothing is metered and billed by usage. A breath has a price, reckoned against the oxygen plant that made it. A litre of recycled water has a price, reckoned against the energy and machinery that cleaned it. The removal of the carbon dioxide you exhale, which on Earth the oceans and the forests perform for free across the whole turning planet, here has a price too, because here it is a scrubber someone built and runs, and the scrubber's work is the difference between a breathable room and a coffin.

This is not dystopia and it is not utopia. It is the framework as a colony's day-to-day operating ledger. Every line of it is one of the hidden values from the first pages of this essay, the air, the water, the carrying-away of carbon, made visible at last because the seal has taken away the abundance that hid them. And it is the same ledger I ran backward over McDowell County and forward over Norway, only now it runs forward in real time, continuously, as the basic metabolism of a society that cannot afford the mask. On Earth we used the seal to see the value for a moment and then walked back out into the free air and forgot. In the colony there is no walking back out. You live inside the seal, and so you do not merely see the value, you pay it, every hour, for the rest of your life, and the paying is not an injustice but a simple honesty about where you are. The colony cannot afford to misread a zero as a verdict on worth, because in the colony there are no zeros. Abundance is the luxury of a planet with an atmosphere to spare, and the colony has spent that luxury at the airlock.

### The asteroid miner

Now leave the habitat and go out alone, far out, to a rock with no one on it. Imagine you are a miner working an asteroid, a body of iron and nickel and stone tumbling through the dark with no community on it and no ecosystem in it and no one downstream of anything you do. Here the framework says something that may surprise you, given everything I have argued. Here it says: extract freely.

Run the ledger and you see why. Cost severance is the creation of a cost that someone else must absorb. But on the bare rock there is no one else. There is no fishery to foul, no farmland to sour, no neighbor to breathe your dust, no unborn community whose commons you are drawing down, because there is no commons and no community at all, only dead metal that has been dead for the age of the solar system and will not miss a single tonne. The residual commons value of asteroid iron is near zero, not because the iron is worthless but because there is no commons for the value to reside in and no one for it to be owed to. So the honest ledger, the very same ledger that meters every breath in the colony, looks at the asteroid and says: take it. Take the iron, take all of it, and if a comet swings briefly into reach and will be gone for ten thousand years, do not husband it, extract aggressively while you can, because the only cost of leaving it is the loss of the having.

And yet. On the rock itself, the same ledger that says take freely says something else in the same breath, and the two are not in tension; they are the two halves of one honest sentence. It says: hold reserves. You must keep back enough fuel to leave, enough air to breathe until you leave, enough water and food and power to be certain you can get off this rock and get home, or get on to the next world. The ledger that licenses unlimited extraction of the dead metal demands, at the very same time, an absolute reserve of the living necessities, because the future you are extracting into is a future you have to survive, and a miner who extracts every gram and keeps nothing back has not been efficient, he has been a corpse who didn't know it yet.

That is the whole framework in two scenes. Meter the commons where there is a commons; reserve the necessities everywhere; extract freely only where the cost severance is truly zero, and even there keep back enough to live. Honest accounting is not anti-extraction. It never was. It is knowing the real cost of what you take, and keeping enough back to survive the future you are taking yourself into.

## Whatever economy you choose

I have run this ledger forward and backward, on a county and a country, on a barrel and a breath, on this world and an imagined one, and I want to be clear about what kind of thing it is and what kind of thing it is not. It is not a rival economic system. It does not compete with capitalism or socialism or the mixed arrangements most of the world actually lives under. It is the accounting that any of those systems needs in order to see what it is doing. You can run a market economy or a planned one or something ancient and unnamed, but whatever you run, you have to be able to see the real costs, or you are operating an enterprise without books, which is to say you are flying blind.

This is why the framework keeps good company across very different traditions, all of them, in the end, attempts by a civilization to know its real costs and hold reserves against scarcity. Kate Raworth, in *Doughnut Economics*, drew an economy bounded on the outside by the planet's ecological limits and on the inside by the floor of human need, a shape that is, at heart, an insistence that the costs at both edges be made visible and respected. Ostrom's commons governance, which I met earlier on the question of rate, is the same instinct worked out institution by institution, communities keeping honest books on their shared and renewable wealth. And the instinct is far older than any economist. Long before there was a word for economics, ancient China kept what was called the ever-normal granary, a public store filled in times of plenty and drawn down in times of want, and ancient Egypt kept its state granaries against the years the river ran low. I will not invent particulars I do not have; the point is only the shape of the thing, repeated across millennia. A civilization that wished to survive its own future learned to know its costs and hold reserves, and built the holding into the bones of its state.

Capitalist or planned, ancient or off-world, the requirement does not change. The ledger has to be honest, or the economy is flying blind. The framework I have been building is not a flag to march under. It is the set of books underneath whatever flag you have already chosen.

## Reserves on the longest horizon

If the requirement is to hold reserves against scarcity, then the deepest question the framework can ask is: reserves on what horizon? An individual reserves against a hard winter. A community reserves against a bad decade. A nation, like Norway, reserves against the day its one finite blessing runs out. But a finite resource burned forever pushes the horizon out past any of these, out toward civilizational time, and the framework, followed honestly, does not stop at the comfortable scales.

Follow it all the way out. The Sun is not a steady lamp; it brightens slowly as it ages, and that rising luminosity will, on the order of five hundred million to a billion years from now, make the surface of the Earth uninhabitable, the oceans driven off, the familiar world ended, as the astronomers Sackmann and her colleagues worked out in the early 1990s. That is long, unimaginably long, longer than complex life has so far existed on the planet. And it is far short of the Sun's own end, the swelling into a red giant that, as Schröder and Smith reckoned, will likely engulf the Earth itself something like seven and a half billion years from now. I am not raising these horizons to frighten anyone or to make present concerns seem small; the opposite, if anything. I raise them because on those timescales the off-world habitat stops being a fantasy and becomes a coherent forward-looking concern, the longest reserve of all, the preservation of an option, the keeping-open of a door for a species that would otherwise have, eventually, nowhere to stand. The colony economy I walked around inside, the airlock and the metered breath, is on this horizon not a curiosity but a form of reserve, held against a scarcity so distant that only an honest ledger, run to its furthest term, would think to name it.

## The mask comes off

Breathe in again. Breathe out. The meter still has not run, and on this world, with this atmosphere, it never will, and that is a mercy we should not mistake for a measurement. The air is not free because it is worthless. It is free because it is so totally valuable, so completely distributed, that the only instrument we trust cannot find an edge to charge at, and so it posts a zero, and we, reading the zero, have learned to look straight through the most important things in the world.

That is the mask of abundance, and everything in this essay has been one move performed against it, over and over, at larger and larger scales. Find the value that abundance or distance or time has hidden, and put it on the ledger where it belongs. Behind the diver's seal and the desert ark and the orbiting station, in the gap between a barrel's price and its permanence, in the hundredfold distance between four dollars of coal and five hundred dollars of honest cost, in Norway's locked principal and the Niger Delta's drifting bill, in a colony that pays for its breath and a miner who reserves his fuel, it has been the same single act each time. One honest ledger, on any scale, on any world, run in either direction.

I said at the start that this is all the essay claims to do, and I want to end by saying it again, because it is easy, with a framework that reaches from a county in West Virginia to the death of the Sun, to imagine the claim is grander than it is. It is not grand. It is honest accounting, the kind an individual could keep, or a community, or a government, on this world or any other, and its whole work is to make visible the costs that the mask keeps hidden. The number was always there. The framework does not invent it. The framework only takes the mask off, so that the thing the price could never see can finally be written down.

And here is the part that is not in any ledger, the part that no instrument reads, which is that the writing-down is a choice. The costs are real whether we see them or not; the bill drifts downstream whether or not anyone catches it; the barrel is gone whether or not anyone priced its permanence. What is still open, what we still get to decide, is whether we keep flying blind or learn at last to keep honest books. Whether the freedom of the next breath, and the breath after that, and the breath of someone not yet born and far away, stays free, or quietly becomes a thing that was spent without anyone noticing the price. We owe that breath to people who are not at the table, across distance and across time, the ones downstream and downwind and down the years, and an honest ledger is finally only the form that owing takes when a civilization decides to grow up and pay attention. The mask can come off. Whether we take it off, and what we do once we can see, is still, for now, ours to choose.
