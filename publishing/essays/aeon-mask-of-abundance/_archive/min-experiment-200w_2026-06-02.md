# The Mask of Abundance

Ask anyone what air costs and the question barely registers. Nothing, of course. You breathe; the bill never comes. This is not because air has no value. It is because air has nothing but value, distributed so completely through the world that no market can find an edge to charge at. The price the market posts for the things we cannot live a single minute without is approximately zero, and we read that zero as a verdict on their worth. It is the opposite. The zero is a measure of how much there is, not how little it matters. Abundance is the mask. It hides what is most valuable behind the very fact that it is everywhere.

Pull the mask off and the face beneath is startling. Seal the system, cut off the free and inexhaustible supply, and the priceless commons become the most expensive things there are. We tend to think of honest pricing as the work of adding back a cost the market quietly severed, the carbon a smokestack never paid for, the river it fouled for free. But there is a stranger case, almost the inverse. Here nothing was severed, because nothing was ever attached. The value was so total and so ambient that it never acquired a price at all. Only scarcity can reveal it, and on Earth, for air and water and the patient carrying-away of our own exhaled carbon, scarcity has never arrived.

Consider, then, a thought-experiment. Picture a sealed habitat somewhere off the Earth, a closed life-support system on no particular world, built for no particular date. I do not offer it as a forecast. It is a lens, a way of seeing by changing the lighting. Inside this habitat a resident pays, by usage, for exactly the things the Earth supplies for nothing. A metered breath. A litre of recycled water. The removal of the carbon dioxide leaving her lungs, gas that on Earth a leaf would simply take. The habitat is not a prophecy of how we will live. It is a moral X-ray of how we already do, showing in hard outline what the Earth-commons would cost if the bill were ever itemised and sent.

The unsettling part is that the experiment is not, for everyone, hypothetical. Some people already live inside the sealed version, and for them the mask is already off.

Go down with the saturation divers, the ones who work for days at depths where ordinary air would poison them. They breathe heliox, a blend of helium and oxygen, and they buy it by the hour. Helium runs on the order of a couple of dollars a cubic foot; technical divers reckon the helium alone at something like eighty to two hundred dollars an hour. The cost is steep enough that the ships running deep saturation work install reclaim systems to scavenge the exhaled gas and use it again. A breath, down there, is a metered commodity with a running meter. The thing you and I forget we are doing every few seconds is, for a diver, a line item.

Or step inside Biosphere 2, the great sealed glass world built in the Arizona desert in the early 1990s, three and a bit acres enclosed against the open atmosphere, a small crew sealed in to live off what the system could regenerate. It cost something near a hundred and fifty million dollars. Within sixteen months the oxygen had fallen from the ordinary twenty-one per cent of the air to fourteen, low enough to leave the crew gasping, the soil microbes and the curing concrete between them having quietly eaten the difference. Managers pumped oxygen in. Nineteen of the twenty-five vertebrate species enclosed went extinct. Every pollinator died, so the crops had to be pollinated by hand. The lesson was not that the engineers had failed. The lesson was how staggeringly hard, and how expensive, it is to reproduce by deliberate effort the ecological services that the open Earth performs without invoice and without ceasing.

Or look up at the International Space Station, where the costliest things aboard are the two we treat as free. Water runs about twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram simply to lift it out of the gravity well; a half-year's drinking supply works out to something on the order of a hundred and thirty-five million dollars. So the station does what the Earth's water cycle does, only by machine and at ruinous cost, reclaiming roughly nine-tenths of the crew's sweat and urine and making the breathable oxygen by splitting water with electricity. In orbit the rivers do not flow and the forests do not exhale, and the price of standing in for them is written plainly in the accounts.

The diver, the Biosphere crew, the astronaut are not living in a different physics from the rest of us. They are living in the version where the supply was cut and the bill arrived. What they pay in dollars and engineering and anxious oxygen readings is the true and previously hidden price of the commons that the Earth, for now, still gives away. The mask of abundance is not a comforting illusion. It is the precise reason we under-protect the things we most depend on, because a value that never had to name itself is a value we find it almost impossible to defend. We guard what is scarce and priced. We squander what is plentiful and free, mistaking the absence of a price tag for the absence of worth, right up until the moment the system seals and the mask comes off in our hands.
