# The Mask of Abundance

Take a breath. Right now, without thinking about it, you just did something extraordinary, and it cost you nothing. You pulled in a lungful of air. About a fifth of it was oxygen. Your blood took the oxygen, carried it to every cell in your body, and let you keep being alive for a few more seconds. Then you breathed out, and the air carried away the carbon dioxide your body made as waste. You did not pay for any of this. You did not sign a contract. No bill will arrive at the end of the month.

We tend to read that price of nothing as a verdict. If air costs nothing, the reasoning goes, then air must be worth nothing, or close to it. Cheap things are common. Common things are cheap. We do not stop to admire the air the way we admire a diamond, and the reason seems obvious: there is so much of it. It is everywhere. You cannot run out.

But sit with the breath for a moment longer and the reasoning starts to wobble. Air is not worth nothing. Air is worth everything. Stop the supply for four minutes and you are dead. There is no good in the world you would not trade for the next breath if it were the last one available to you. A thing that is worth your entire life, every single life, every animal that has ever drawn breath, is not a thing of little value. It is a thing of almost unimaginable value. So why does it cost nothing?

Here is the answer, and it is the seed of everything that follows in this essay. A price is not a measure of how much something is worth. A price is a measure of scarcity. It is the number that appears at the edge, the margin, where supply runs short and someone has to decide who gets the last unit and who goes without. When a thing is so abundant that there is no edge, no last unit, no shortage to ration, there is nothing for a price to grab onto. The market reaches for something to charge for and its hand closes on empty space. So it posts a zero.

We then make a mistake. We see the zero and we read it as a statement about worth. We think the air is free because it is not worth much. The truth is the reverse. The air is free because it is so completely, so totally worth everything, and so completely available to everyone at once, that no market can find a place to stand and charge a fee. The value is not low. The value is so high and so evenly spread that it becomes invisible. It hides in plain sight, in front of your face, in your lungs, twelve times a minute.

Call this the mask of abundance. Abundance puts a mask over enormous value and the mask is a price of zero. We look at the mask and think we are looking at the thing, and we conclude the thing is worth nothing. We are wrong. We are looking at a mask.

This essay is about taking the mask off. I want to build something with you, step by step, over the next many pages, and I want to be plain about what it is from the start. It is a way of keeping honest accounts. It is a way of seeing the costs and the values that our ordinary accounting cannot see, because abundance has hidden them, or because distance has hidden them, or because time has hidden them. The claim is simple to state and large in its reach. There is one honest ledger, and it works on this world and on any other world, and you can run it forwards into the future or backwards into the past. That is all the essay sets out to do: to show you that ledger and to convince you it is real. Once you can see the costs that are currently invisible, you can decide what to do about them. But you cannot decide about a thing you cannot see. First the seeing.

And the fastest way to see what abundance has hidden is to take the abundance away.

## What happens when you seal the room

I want you to imagine being sealed inside a habitat on another world. Not as a thought about the far future, and not as a prediction. I am not telling you we will live this way. I am offering it as a lens, a way of forcing the hidden value out into the open where you can look at it. Bear with the device. It will earn its keep.

Picture a sealed dome on the Moon, or on Mars, or a station drifting in orbit. The thing that defines it is the seal. Nothing comes in for free and nothing leaves for free. Inside the seal there is a fixed amount of air, a fixed amount of water, and a fixed capacity for taking the carbon dioxide back out of the air after people have breathed it. On Earth, the planet does all of this for you, silently, for nothing. Inside the seal, somebody has to do it, and doing it costs energy, machinery, and constant attention.

The moment you draw that seal, something strange happens to the prices. The breath that was free on Earth is now the most expensive thing in the room. Why? Because now it is scarce. Now there is an edge. Now there is a last unit of oxygen, and someone has to decide what happens when it runs low. The mask comes off. The enormous value of the air, which was always there, suddenly has a place to attach a price, and the price is high, because the value was always high. Nothing about the air changed. What changed is that the abundance went away, and with it the mask.

You do not have to travel to another world to see this. The bill already arrives, today, in three real places on this Earth, every one of them a place where human beings have sealed themselves off from the planet's free gifts and had to start paying for them by hand.

The first place is the deep sea. When divers go down far enough to do industrial work on oil rigs and pipelines, they cannot breathe ordinary air. Deep underwater, under pressure, the nitrogen in normal air starts to act like a drug and dulls the mind, and the oxygen, which keeps you alive at the surface, becomes poisonous in too large a dose. So saturation divers breathe a special mix, mostly helium with a carefully controlled amount of oxygen, called heliox. Helium is not free. It runs somewhere around one dollar seventy to nearly three dollars per cubic foot, and a working diver can burn through enough of it that the helium alone costs roughly eighty to two hundred dollars an hour. The cost is high enough that the ships running these operations install special rigs to capture the breathed-out helium and reclaim it, scrubbing it clean to use again, because letting a breath escape is letting money escape. On Earth, in the open air, the idea of metering a person's breath and reclaiming the gas would be absurd. Underwater, sealed off from the free atmosphere, it is simply good bookkeeping. The breath got a price the moment the abundance went away.

The second place is a glass building in the Arizona desert called Biosphere 2. In the early 1990s, eight people sealed themselves inside about three acres of glass-roofed enclosure, an artificial little world built at a cost of around a hundred and fifty million dollars, and tried to live there for two years growing their own food and recycling their own air and water. It is one of the most instructive experiments anyone has ever run, precisely because of how it went wrong. The oxygen inside the sealed world began to fall. It started near the normal level of about twenty-one percent and over sixteen months it sank to around fourteen percent, which is roughly what you would breathe high up a mountain, and the crew grew tired and short of breath. The oxygen was disappearing partly into the soil, where microbes were consuming it, and partly into the concrete of the structure itself, which was binding up the carbon dioxide in a way nobody had fully planned for. In the end the managers had to pump oxygen in from outside to keep the crew safe. The little world could not balance its own air. And it was not only the air. Of twenty-five kinds of small vertebrate animals brought in, nineteen died. Every pollinating insect died, which meant the crops had to be pollinated by hand. Everything the Earth does for free, quietly, perfectly, at a scale we never notice, had to be done by hand inside the glass, and much of it could not be done well at all. The free services turned out to be doing an enormous amount of work. You only saw the work when you sealed it off and watched it fail.

The third place is the most sealed environment human beings routinely live in: the International Space Station, a few hundred kilometres above your head. Up there, nothing is free and the prices are written in the brutal arithmetic of the rocket. To lift one kilogram of water up to orbit costs on the order of twenty-one thousand dollars. A six-month supply of water for the crew, if you simply launched it all from the ground, would run to something like a hundred and thirty-five million dollars. That number is so large that it forces a different way of living. The station recycles roughly ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine back into clean drinking water, because throwing that water away would mean launching its replacement at twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram. The oxygen the crew breathes is not shipped up in tanks. It is made on board, by running an electric current through water to split it into hydrogen and oxygen. Up there, a litre of water and a lungful of oxygen are precious, metered, accounted for, fought over. Down here, the same litre and the same lungful are beneath our notice. The water and the air did not change between the ground and the orbit. The abundance changed. And when the abundance went away, the value came out from behind the mask.

So now look back at the breath you took at the start, and look at it through the seal. On Earth, the air, the water, and the planet's quiet work of taking the carbon dioxide back out of the atmosphere are all priced at zero, and we read the zero as worthlessness. Seal the system and the same things become the most expensive things there are. The value was always there. The seal did not create it. The seal only revealed it, by taking away the abundance that had been hiding it.

That is the mask of abundance, and that is the way in. Now I want to name the machinery underneath, because once you have a name for the pattern you will start to see it everywhere.

## Naming the broken bill

Let me start with the most ordinary kind of hidden cost, the kind that has nothing to do with abundance at all, just so we have the simpler case in hand before we tackle the stranger one.

Suppose a factory makes something useful and sells it at a fair-looking price, and in the process it dumps its waste into the river that runs past the town downstream. The people downstream get sick, or their water gets fouled, or their fishery dies. They never agreed to this. They never sold anything to the factory. They have no way to send the factory a bill for what they have lost. The factory made a profit, the buyer got a cheap product, and a real cost, a genuine loss of health and livelihood, landed on people who had no part in the deal and no way to charge for the damage.

Someone created a cost. Someone else absorbed it. And the second party had no way to bill it back to the first. Call that cost severance. The cost got severed, cut loose, from the transaction that caused it, and floated downstream to land on whoever happened to be there. Severance is the heart of the matter. A market price is supposed to gather up all the costs of making a thing and hand them to the person who buys it, so that the buyer feels the true weight of what they are asking the world to do. When a cost gets severed, the price lies. It tells the buyer the thing is cheaper than it really is, because part of the true cost has been quietly shoved onto someone who is not at the table.

Honest pricing, in this ordinary case, means re-attaching the severed cost. It means finding the bill that floated downstream and pinning it back onto the transaction that created it, so the price tells the truth again. Economists have a dry word for the severed cost. They call it an externality, a cost that lands outside the deal. I will mostly call it a severed cost, because that is what it is, and the plain word keeps the broken-bill image in front of us.

Now here is the stranger case, the one this essay is really about, and it is stranger precisely because of abundance.

Go back to the commons, the air and the water and the planet's work of carrying carbon away. These were never just underpriced, the way a nurse is underpaid or good topsoil is undervalued. An underpaid nurse still has a wage. Undervalued topsoil still has a price per acre. They are priced too low, and you could in principle argue the number up to where it should be. The commons are different. They have no price at all. They never acquired one, because abundance never let a price form. There was never an edge, never a last unit, never a shortage to ration, so no number ever attached. And a thing with no number is invisible to the only instrument our economy fully trusts, which is the price. We can argue a nurse's wage up. We cannot argue a number up from a thing that has no number to begin with. It is not on the books. It is not even in the language of the books.

So there is a value sitting there, enormous, real, completely unpriced, and when someone comes along and extracts from it, sells off a piece of it, the value does not vanish. Some of it goes to the buyer and the seller in the form of the price they agree on. But a great deal of it is left over, unaccounted for, owed to everyone the deal left out. I will call that leftover the residual commons value. Residual, because it is what remains after the sale is done. Commons, because it belonged to everyone and no one. Value, because it is real worth, not a bookkeeping fiction. It is the worth that was extracted from a shared inheritance and never paid for, because there was no one standing there with a bill, and no number on the books to write the bill in.

And who is owed this residual value? The people not at the table. The downstream town, yes, but also people far away who never heard of the deal, and most of all people who do not yet exist, the ones who will inherit a world with less of the commons in it because we spent it and did not set anything aside. The residual commons value is owed to people across distance and across time. It is a debt to those not present, including those not yet born. Hold onto that phrase. It will matter all the way to the end.

So we have two different jobs, and it is worth keeping them apart. In the ordinary case, honest pricing means re-attaching a cost that got severed and floated to the wrong place. In the commons case, honest pricing means something harder. It means putting a first price on a value so total and so evenly spread that it never acquired a price at all, and that only becomes visible when the abundance runs out and the scarcity finally arrives. The first job is repair. The second job is seeing, for the first time, a value that was always there and always invisible. The seal in the thought experiment was a machine for doing the second job. It manufactured the scarcity that forced the hidden value into view.

Now let us take this naming and walk it out of the laboratory of the sealed room and into the messy world, starting with the single most important commons we are spending without a bill: a stable climate, and the planet's free service of carrying our carbon away.

## The hundred-dollar barrel

Picture a barrel of oil. To make the arithmetic clean, give it a round price. Say it sells for a hundred dollars. I am picking that number to think with, not reporting today's market. A hundred dollars is what a barrel might fetch, and it is enough to show what I want to show.

What does that hundred dollars pay for? It pays for finding the oil and drilling for it and pumping it up and shipping it and refining it into fuel, and it pays a profit to the people who did all that work, and it pays for the oil itself as a thing you can buy and burn today. Every part of that hundred dollars is a present-tense number. It is the cost of getting the oil to you now, and the value of the oil to you now. You buy it, you burn it, your car runs, the present is served. The transaction, as far as the present is concerned, is complete and fair.

But the thing you just bought does not belong only to the present. It belongs to permanence. When you burn that barrel, two things happen that the hundred dollars never paid for, and both of them reach far beyond today.

The first is the carbon. Burning the barrel puts carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, and a stable climate is one of those total, unpriced commons, worth everything, owned by everyone, billed to no one. The planet has, for all of human history, carried that carbon away and kept the climate in the narrow band that lets us live. Burning the barrel uses up a piece of that free service and leaves the bill for the future, in the form of a hotter, more violent, less reliable world. That cost is severed. It does not come back to the buyer. It floats downstream in time, onto people not yet born, who never bought the barrel and cannot send anyone an invoice.

The second is subtler and it is worth slowing down for, because it is the part most discussions of climate miss entirely. It is the difference between a resource that grows back and a resource that does not.

Some of the things we take from the Earth renew themselves. A forest grows back. A fishery breeds new fish. A field of grass returns next spring. For these, the honest question is one of rate. How fast are you taking, compared to how fast the thing replaces itself? Take less than it breeds and you can go on forever, drawing an income off a living principal that never shrinks. Take more than it breeds and you eat into the principal, and eventually it collapses. The whole problem for a renewable resource is matching your rate of taking to its rate of growing. Get the rate right and the resource is, in effect, eternal.

Oil is not like that. Oil does not breed. It was made over tens of millions of years by a planet pressing dead life under heat and weight, and on any timescale that matters to a human civilization, it is not being made anymore. When you pump a barrel and burn it, that barrel is gone. Not depleted, not reduced, not running low. Gone, permanently, for everyone who will ever live after you. There is no rate of taking slow enough to make it sustainable, because the resource does not replace itself at any rate at all. Every barrel burned is a barrel that the entire future will never have. You are not drawing income off a principal. You are spending the principal itself, and the principal does not come back.

So look again at the hundred dollars. It bought the present its tank of gas, fair and square. It did not buy back the barrel for anyone who comes after. The barrel is simply gone from their inheritance, and they did not get a vote, and they did not get a payment, and there is no number anywhere on the books that records what they lost. That loss, the permanent absence of a thing that will never return, is the residual commons value of the barrel. It is real. It is large. And our ordinary accounting cannot see a penny of it.

This distinction between what grows back and what does not is going to do double duty later, when we go off-world, so let me set it down clearly here. For a renewable commons, honest accounting is about rate: take less than it breeds. For a non-renewable commons, honest accounting is about reserves: because every unit taken is gone forever, the only honest response is to hold something back, to set something aside against the future you are spending into. Rate for the renewable. Reserves for the finite. Keep both in mind.

Now, you might think I am the first person to notice that the barrel has a hidden bill attached. I am not, and the honest thing is to say so plainly. Many serious thinkers have walked right up to the edge of this empty plot of ground and looked at it and, for one reason or another, built their house somewhere just beside it. It is worth a quick tour of the near-misses, because seeing what they each saw, and what each one stopped just short of, sharpens what the empty plot actually contains.

## The ones who walked up to the edge

In the nineteenth century, the philosopher and economist Karl Marx wrote a vast study of how value is made and who captures it, and he put his finger on something real: that the worth a worker creates can be greater than the wage the worker is paid, and that the difference, the surplus, gets pocketed by the owner. That is a genuine severed value, a real gap between what is created and what is paid for. But Marx's gap runs between classes of people alive at the same time, the worker and the owner, here and now. It is a story about who, today, captures the value. It is not a story about time, about the value owed to people who do not yet exist. He priced the surplus, but he priced it across class, not across the generations.

A century later, the political scientist Elinor Ostrom did something remarkable. She went out and studied how real communities, all over the world, manage shared resources, the fishing grounds and grazing lands and irrigation systems that everyone uses and no one owns. The conventional wisdom said such commons were doomed, that everyone would overuse them until they collapsed. Ostrom showed, with patient fieldwork that eventually won her a Nobel Prize, that communities again and again invent rules to govern their commons well and sustainably. But notice what kind of commons she studied. They were the renewable ones, the grazing land and the fishery, the ones whose whole problem is matching the rate of taking to the rate of growing. She solved the rate problem, beautifully. She did not take up the liquidated commons, the barrel that does not grow back at any rate and cannot be governed into sustainability because there is no sustainable rate to find.

Around the same time, the economist Herman Daly spent his career warning that an economy cannot grow forever on a finite planet, that there is a physical throughput of stuff and energy that the planet cannot endlessly supply or endlessly absorb. He argued for a steady-state economy that lived within those limits. He was right about the limits. But his warning was about the whole flow of throughput in general. It did not produce an invoice for any one specific foreclosed thing, a price tag you could pin on a single barrel for the single permanent loss it represents. He saw the wall the economy was driving toward. He did not itemize the bill.

Closest of all, in a way, came the economist John Hartwick, who in the 1970s worked out a precise and elegant rule. If you are going to extract a finite resource and sell it, he showed, you should take the special profit you earn from it, the rent, the part that is pure windfall from owning something scarce, and reinvest it in something lasting, so that future generations inherit capital that produces income even after the resource is gone. Save the rent, and you can keep the future as well off as the present. It is a genuinely beautiful idea and we will see it actually used at the scale of a nation before this essay is done. But notice the boundary of it. Hartwick saved the rent, the financial windfall from the sale. He did not price the barrel itself, the physical thing that is gone forever from the world's stock. His rule keeps the money working. It does not record the loss of the resource as such.

So that is one cluster of near-misses, the economists, each of whom saw a true thing and built just to one side of the empty plot. There is a second cluster, the moral philosophers, who came at the same ground from the other direction, asking not how value is made but to whom we owe what.

The philosopher John Rawls asked us to imagine designing the rules of a society from behind what he called a veil of ignorance, not knowing who we would turn out to be in it, rich or poor, lucky or unlucky. From behind that veil, he argued, we would choose rules that protected even the worst-off, because we might be the worst-off. It is one of the great moral thought experiments. And some who came after him saw that the veil could be widened. If you do not know who you will be, why should you assume you will be alive now at all? Behind a wide enough veil you might be born in any generation, including one far in the future that inherits whatever we leave behind. Widen the veil across time and it starts to ask exactly our question: what do we owe to people who do not yet exist?

The philosopher Michael Sandel pressed a different but related point, that there are things money should not buy, that putting a market price on certain goods corrupts or degrades them, changes what they are. He was warning that the reach of pricing can be a kind of damage. The puzzle of the commons turns his worry inside out. Here the problem is not that we have priced something we should have left unpriced. The problem is that we have failed to price something we are busy spending, and the failure to price it is precisely what lets us spend it blind.

And the philosopher Derek Parfit thought harder than almost anyone about our obligations to future people, about the strange puzzles that arise when our choices today determine not only how well-off future people are but which future people exist at all. He showed that our common-sense moral intuitions get tangled and contradictory when we reason across deep time. He mapped the difficulty. He did not hand us a ledger for it.

Then there is the most active battlefield of all, the argument among economists about how to price climate damage, and it turns on a single technical question that is worth understanding in plain terms, because the residual commons value survives it.

The question is called the discount rate, and it is just this: how much do we care, in today's money, about a cost that lands a long time from now? If a hundred dollars of damage will happen a century out, what is that worth to us today? If you use a high discount rate, you decide it is worth very little today, because the future is far away and people then will probably be richer and can cope. At a high rate, that century-out hundred dollars of damage might be worth only a dollar and a half to us now, hardly worth acting on. If you use a low discount rate, you decide the future matters nearly as much as the present, and the same future damage might be worth twenty-five dollars to us today, well worth acting on now.

This is not a small or academic disagreement. The economist Nicholas Stern led a major British review of climate change in the mid-2000s that used a low discount rate, treated future generations as nearly as important as our own, and concluded that we should spend a meaningful slice of our wealth now, on the order of one percent of the world's output, to head off the damage. The economist William Nordhaus, who later won a Nobel Prize for his climate-economics modelling, used a higher discount rate, concluded the future could partly fend for itself, and recommended a slower, cheaper response. A third economist, Martin Weitzman, pointed out that the real danger might not be the most likely outcome at all but the small chance of a true catastrophe, a fat tail of disaster, which ought to make us cautious no matter what discount rate we pick.

Here is the crucial thing for our purposes, and it is easy to miss in the noise of that argument. Every side of the discount-rate debate is pricing the same thing: the flow of damage that comes from burning the barrel, the floods and droughts and heat that the carbon will cause down the line. They differ on how heavily to weigh that future damage, but they are all pricing the damage from combustion. None of them is pricing the residual commons value, the simple permanent absence of the barrel itself, the fact that a finite thing was removed from the world's stock and will never be there again for anyone.

And that absence does not behave like a discounted flow of future damage. You cannot shrink it with a discount rate, because it is not a stream of harms spread out over coming centuries. It is a present fact. The barrel is gone now. The forecloseit-forever happened the moment it was burned. Even Stern's low discount rate, which weighs the future heavily, is still a rate applied to a flow. The residual commons value is not a flow. It is the present, permanent absence of a thing that will not return, and so it survives the whole discount-rate war untouched, because that war was never fighting over it. The economists were measuring the damage the smoke would do. They were not measuring the hole where the barrel used to be.

So that is the empty plot, surrounded by the houses of people who each saw part of it. The surplus across class. The renewable commons governed well. The throughput warning. The saved rent. The widening veil. The corruption of pricing. The obligations to future people. The discounted damage flow. Every one of them is a real and valuable thing, and not one of them is the same as putting an honest price on the permanent loss of a finite commons, owed to people not at the table, across distance and across time. That is the empty plot. Now let me show you the ledger running on a real place, on real ground, on a thing that has already been dug up and burned.

## McDowell County

There is a county in southern West Virginia called McDowell County, and for a long stretch of the twentieth century it was one of the great coal-producing places in America. The coal under McDowell was rich and high in carbon, and it was dug out by the ton and sold. Around 1960, at the mouth of the mine, that coal sold for something like four or five dollars a ton. That was the price. That was what the world recorded as the value of a ton of McDowell coal.

Now run the honest ledger on it. Take that four-or-five-dollar ton of coal and add back the costs that the price left out, the costs that got severed and floated downstream in time onto everyone who would breathe the changed atmosphere afterward. When you do that, the honest price of a ton of McDowell coal is not four or five dollars. It is more than five hundred dollars. The full honest figure comes to around five hundred and ten dollars a ton, and nearly all of that, almost the entire amount, is carbon.

Let me show you where the big number comes from, because it is built from two pieces of arithmetic you can check, and seeing it built makes it real rather than rhetorical. A ton of this particular McDowell coal, when burned, releases about two and six-tenths tons of carbon dioxide. That is higher than the national average for coal, which runs closer to two and a third tons, because the McDowell seam was unusually rich and carbon-heavy. So: about two and six-tenths tons of carbon dioxide per ton of coal.

The second piece is what one ton of carbon dioxide actually costs the world, the damage it does once it is in the atmosphere, the severed bill for it. The United States Environmental Protection Agency, in a 2023 estimate, put that figure at around a hundred and ninety dollars per ton of carbon dioxide. This is the government's own best accounting of the harm a ton of carbon does, the social cost of carbon, as it is called.

Multiply the two. About two and six-tenths tons of carbon dioxide, at around a hundred and ninety dollars of damage per ton, comes to roughly four hundred and ninety-six dollars. That is the carbon term alone. Add the rest of the severed costs and you reach a bit over five hundred dollars. So a ton of coal that sold for the price of a cheap lunch carried, hidden inside it, more than five hundred dollars of real cost that nobody paid, almost all of it in the form of carbon poured into a sky that had always carried it away for free.

That is the mask of abundance, made of coal and arithmetic instead of breath. The four-or-five-dollar price was not wrong as a price. It accurately measured the present-tense scarcity of coal at the mine mouth in 1960. It was wrong as a statement of the true cost, because it was missing more than five hundred dollars of severed value, owed to people not at the table, across distance and across time. The price told the truth about scarcity and lied about cost, and we read it as if it had told the truth about both.

Notice the direction we just ran the ledger. McDowell coal is not a future loss we are trying to prevent. It is a past extraction already finished. The coal is dug, sold, burned, gone. So here we are running the honest ledger backwards, onto a thing already done, asking what the true cost was, after the fact. That is a different motion from the barrel, where we were looking forward and trying to price a loss before it happened. The ledger, it turns out, runs in both directions. And that is the next thing to make plain.

## Forwards and in reverse

The honest ledger is a single instrument, but it can be pointed at the future or at the past, and the two directions call for two different kinds of action.

Point it forward, at a barrel not yet pumped or a seam not yet mined, and the honest response is to price the future loss before you cause it, and to set the money aside. You look ahead, you reckon what permanent loss the extraction will impose on people not yet born, you put a number on it, and you bank that number, holding it in reserve against the future you are spending into. Think of it as a bond posted against a foreclosure you are about to commit on the future. A foreclosure bond, set aside before the act, so that something real is held back for the people the act will cost. The forward direction is about prevention and reserves: see the loss coming, price it, hold something back.

Point the same ledger backward, at an extraction already finished, like McDowell's coal, and the honest response is different. The loss already happened. You cannot prevent it. What you can do is reckon honestly what it cost, who bore the cost, and what is owed in repair. This is the ledger as restitution, the accounting of a debt already incurred. A restitution bond, you might call it, the backward twin of the foreclosure bond: not money set aside before the act, but a debt acknowledged and paid after it.

And once you can run the ledger backward to price extractions already realized, you arrive at a body of work that has been doing exactly this kind of honest backward accounting, with great care, for a long time. The economist William Darity Jr. and the writer A. Kirsten Mullen, in their 2020 book From Here to Equality, lay out the case for reparations to Black Americans for slavery and its long aftermath as, in effect, a rigorous backward accounting. They trace a debt that runs from 1619 forward through generations, and they show it is not a vague moral sentiment but a measurable quantity, visible today in the enormous gap in accumulated wealth between Black and white American families, a gap that did not arise from nowhere but is the present-day residue of value extracted and never paid for. The debt even shows up written into people's bodies, in the measurable gap in how long Black and white Americans live, a difference in lifespan that is itself a legacy effect of a long history of extracted and uncompensated worth. That is the honest ledger run backward at the scale of a nation's founding wrong: a severed cost, traced from its origin to its present residue, named as a debt and quantified.

Here I have to be careful and plain about a boundary, because the ledger has an edge it should not cross uninvited. There is a difference between the legacy effects of a coerced extraction, which show up as measurable gaps in wealth and health that anyone can count, and the direct pricing of the coercion itself, the violence and the unfreedom, the human suffering of being owned and worked under the whip. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group the author of this framework does not belong to is not the author's work to do, and the framework does not presume to do it. The framework reaches the coerced cases through their legacy effects, the countable residue, and it defers the direct pricing of the coercion itself to the people whose history it is and who are continuing that work. The ledger names the debt it can honestly measure and stops, deliberately, at the line where measuring would become presuming. That restraint is part of the discipline, not a gap in it.

So the ledger runs forward and backward, prevention and restitution, foreclosure bond and restitution bond, two directions of one honest accounting. Now let me show you two real nations that, between them, prove the forward direction is not a fantasy and that the backward debt is not a hypothetical. One of them actually did the honest forward accounting. The other did not, and you can read the difference in the lives of the people who live there.

## Norway and the Niger Delta

Just before Christmas in 1969, after years of fruitless searching, an oil company confirmed to the Norwegian authorities that there was a very large field of oil under the floor of the North Sea, off Norway's coast. It came to be called Ekofisk. Norway was about to become an oil nation, and it faced the choice every oil nation faces: what to do with the windfall.

Most places that strike oil treat it as income. The money flows in, and it gets spent, and for a while everyone feels rich, and then the oil runs down and the money is gone and very often the country is no better off, sometimes worse, having spent a one-time inheritance as if it were a salary. Norway did something different, and what it did is, in plain terms, the honest forward ledger run at the scale of an entire nation.

Norway decided that the oil was not income. It was the liquidation of a finite inheritance, a thing being permanently removed from the country's stock, and so the honest response was not to spend it but to hold it in reserve. In 1990 the country set up a fund to receive the oil money, originally called the Government Petroleum Fund and later renamed the Government Pension Fund Global, and it began pouring the oil revenue in rather than spending it. Then, in 2001, it added a rule of remarkable discipline. The government would be allowed to spend only the expected real return on the fund, the income the invested money was expected to throw off each year after inflation, originally set at around three percent. The principal itself, the great pile of capital built from the oil, was to be left untouched, inviolate, forever. The country would live off the interest and never eat the seed.

The result, decades on, is staggering. The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars. Spread across Norway's population, that is more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every single citizen, held in common, in reserve, against the future. And here is the part that proves it is a genuine national commitment and not the fad of one administration: across decades and across the entire political spectrum, through governments of the left and the right and the centre, no Norwegian government of any color has raided the principal. They have all honored the rule. The fund has become a fact of the nation, above politics, a reserve held in trust across generations.

Look at what Norway actually did, in the language of this essay. It recognized that the oil was a finite commons, gone once spent. It recognized that spending it as income would mean handing the future a country stripped of its inheritance with nothing set aside. So it priced the loss and held the value in reserve, exactly the forward motion of the honest ledger, the foreclosure bond posted against the future at the scale of a sovereign state. It is, in fact, the old economist Hartwick's rule come to life: take the windfall from the finite resource and turn it into lasting capital, so the future inherits an income even after the resource is gone. Norway is the proof, in the world, that the future can be priced and reserved in practice, not just in theory. And notice that the fund is exactly that, a reserve, which is the same theme we met with the non-renewable barrel and which will return when we go off-world.

Now set beside Norway a place where the same physical act, pumping oil out of the ground, was done under a completely different architecture, and read the difference in human terms.

In the Niger Delta, in Nigeria, oil has been pumped since the 1950s, and over the decades it has generated hundreds of billions of dollars of revenue. But the costs of that extraction, the oil spills that fouled the water and the land, the gas flares that burned day and night, the fisheries that were foreclosed and the farmland that was poisoned, fell on the people who lived in the Delta, people who had not sold the oil, who saw little of the money, and who had no way to bill anyone for what they lost. The cost was severed from the revenue and dumped on the bystanders, at the scale of a region and across generations. In 1995 the writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa, who had led a movement protesting what the oil industry was doing to his people's land, was executed by the Nigerian government along with eight others, an act so widely condemned that Nigeria was suspended from the Commonwealth of Nations. Years later, a United Nations environmental assessment of one part of the Delta, Ogoniland, estimated that simply beginning to clean up the damage would cost on the order of a billion dollars as an initial sum, and would take twenty-five to thirty years even to start to set right.

Two nations. The same physical act in each: pump the oil, sell it, burn it. But two completely different architectures of accounting. Norway treated the oil as a finite commons and set the value aside in reserve for the future. The Niger Delta's oil was extracted with the costs severed and left to fall on people who never sold it and could not bill anyone, and nothing was set aside, and the bill came due in fouled water and shortened lives and a cleanup measured in decades and billions.

I want to be careful here about what this comparison is and is not. It is not a hero-and-villain story. It is not a claim that Norwegians are virtuous and Nigerians are not, which would be both false and ugly. It is not even, at bottom, anti-extraction. Both countries extracted. The point is narrower and harder and more useful than a morality tale. The point is architecture. When you extract a finite commons, the cost outlives the sale, and the only question that finally matters is who carries that cost and whether anything is set aside against it. Norway answered the question one way. The Niger Delta's arrangement answered it another. The honest ledger does not tell you whether to extract. It tells you the real cost of extracting, and it insists that something be held back for the people the extraction will outlive. What you do with that knowledge is a political and moral choice. The ledger only makes the choice an honest one, by refusing to let the cost stay invisible.

So we have run the honest ledger backward on a coal county, and we have seen it run forward at the scale of a nation, and we have seen what happens when it is not run at all. Everything so far has been about this Earth, this history, these real places and real numbers. Now I want to do the thing I promised at the beginning. I want to take the same single ledger and carry it off the planet, and watch it run forward, in real time, as the everyday operating system of a world that is too sealed to let any abundance hide any value at all. This is where the lens we used at the start, the sealed room, stops being only a measuring device and becomes a place to live.

## Off-world: the ledger as a way of life

I want to be plain again about the register here, because it matters. What follows is a thought experiment. I am not predicting that we will live this way, and I am not forecasting colonies and asteroid mines. I am using an imagined future as a lens, the same way I used the sealed room at the start, to see the honest ledger doing in full daylight what on Earth it does in the dark. If imagining the future helps you see the present more clearly, the imagining has done its job. Treat everything in this section as a lens, not a claim.

At the beginning of this essay, we sealed a room in order to see the value that abundance was hiding. We used the seal as an instrument, a way of forcing the breath and the litre of water to show their true worth. Now I want to do something different with the seal. I want us to live inside it. Not to measure with it but to dwell in it, to imagine an off-world habitat not as a laboratory but as a home, with a working economy, with people getting up in the morning and going about their lives. And I want to show that the honest ledger, the very same ledger we ran on McDowell's coal and Norway's oil, is simply the everyday economics of such a place. On a world sealed tight enough, honest accounting is not a reform you argue for. It is the air you breathe, billed by the litre.

### The colony economy, and the question at the airlock

Imagine you arrive at a sealed colony on another world, a city under a dome or inside a mountain or floating in orbit, fully self-contained, cut off from any free atmosphere. And imagine that at the airlock, before they let you in, someone asks you a question that on Earth would be insane and here is merely sensible. How, they ask, do you intend to pay for your air?

On Earth the question is absurd because air is abundant and the abundance wears the mask of worthlessness. In the colony there is no abundance to hide behind. The air inside the seal is a fixed and precious stock, made and cleaned and circulated by machinery that runs on energy that someone has to generate, and every breath drawn is a real draw on a real and limited supply. So of course they ask how you will pay for your air. It would be reckless not to. The breath, which on Earth is the very image of a free gift, is here a metered utility, like electricity, like water, like anything else that costs something to provide and runs out if you are careless with it.

And it is not only the breath. Step inside the colony and look at how the economy works, and you find that every single commons the Earth gives away for free is, inside the seal, a metered and billed service. The air you breathe is metered. The litre of water you drink, recycled at great expense from every drop the colony can recover, is metered and billed by usage, because every litre lost has to be replaced at enormous cost, the way the space station replaces its water at twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram. Even the carbon dioxide you breathe out is on the ledger, because somebody, some machine running on some quantity of energy, has to pull that carbon dioxide back out of the shared air before it builds up and poisons everyone, and that removal is a real and costly service, the colony's own private version of the planetary work that Earth does for nothing. On Earth you breathe out and the world carries the carbon away for free. In the colony you breathe out and the bill arrives, because here the carrying-away is a job that costs energy and money and has to be paid for.

Look at what this is, in the language we have built. Every item on the colony's utility bill, the air in, the water through, the carbon out, is a commons that on Earth is priced at zero because of abundance, and that inside the seal acquires an honest price because the abundance is gone. The colony's day-to-day economy, its ordinary household accounting, is nothing more or less than the honest ledger of this essay, run forward, in real time, as a way of life. It is the same ledger as McDowell's coal and Norway's oil fund. The only difference is that on Earth we have to work to see the hidden value, because abundance keeps masking it, and in the colony there is no mask to lift, because the seal never lets the value hide in the first place. The colony has to keep honest accounts the way you and I have to breathe. It is not a virtue there. It is survival.

And here is where the off-world lens reconnects to the breath we started with, and closes a circle. At the beginning of this essay, we used the seal to see the hidden value of the commons. We sealed a room and watched the breath become expensive, and we said, look, the value was there all along, abundance was only hiding it. Now we have gone one step further. Now we are not visitors using the seal as a measuring stick. We are residents, living inside the seal, and we are paying the value, not just seeing it. First the seal let us see. Now the seal makes us pay. And the strange, clarifying thing the colony teaches is that the honest ledger, which on Earth feels like a moral demand, a reform we have to fight for against the comfortable lie of free air, is in the colony simply the meter on the wall. It is not idealism there. It is the utility bill. The mask of abundance is a luxury of a planet so generous it can afford to let enormous value go unpriced. A sealed world cannot afford the mask, and so on a sealed world the honest ledger is not a cause. It is the economy itself.

There is one more thing the colony teaches, and it is easy to miss. The colony's honest accounting is not anti-consumption. The people there breathe, drink, live, work, and consume the commons constantly. The ledger does not forbid them their breath. It simply makes the breath honest, by attaching to it the true cost of providing it, so that the colony as a whole never spends its air faster than it can make it, never runs its water stock down past the point of no return, never lets the carbon build past what its scrubbers can remove. The honest price is not a ban on living. It is what makes living sustainable inside a seal. And that is exactly what the honest ledger is on Earth too: not a ban on extraction, not a vow of poverty, but the accounting that keeps a system from spending itself blind into collapse. The colony just makes it impossible to pretend otherwise.

### The asteroid miner, and the discipline of reserves

Now leave the colony and go out into the emptiness between worlds, because there is a second scene the off-world lens can show us, and it teaches the opposite-seeming lesson, which turns out to be the same lesson wearing different clothes.

Imagine a miner working an asteroid, far from any colony, far from any living thing, alone with a machine on a lump of metal and rock drifting through the dark. The asteroid is made partly of iron, useful iron, and the miner is there to take it. Now run the honest ledger on this act of extraction, and watch what it says, because it says something that might surprise you given everything that has come before.

Out here there is no community to bear the cost of the mining. There is no town downstream, no fishery to foul, no atmosphere to poison, no ecosystem to wreck, no future generation whose inheritance is being spent, because the asteroid was never anyone's inheritance and supports no life and never will. The whole apparatus of severed cost, the bill that floats downstream onto people not at the table, has nobody to land on. There is nobody at the table and nobody away from it either. The cost severance, the thing the entire essay has been about, falls to nearly zero out here, because there is no one and nothing to absorb a severed cost. And so the honest ledger, the very same ledger that on Earth insists you price the hidden harm of the barrel and the coal, says something blunt and freeing about the asteroid: take it. Extract freely. Mine the iron and use it. There is no hidden bill here, because there is no one to send it to. If a comet swings briefly within reach, carrying water or metal, and will not pass this close again for a lifetime, then extract aggressively while you can, because the only loss in failing to take it is the loss of a resource that will otherwise simply drift away unused into the dark, helping no one.

This is worth dwelling on, because it corrects a misunderstanding that could otherwise grow up around everything I have said. The honest ledger is not a doctrine that extraction is bad. It is not a green sentiment that taking from the world is a sin. The ledger is indifferent to extraction as such. What it cares about is the cost, and specifically the severed cost that lands on people who did not consent and cannot bill. Where that severed cost is large, as it is with a barrel of oil burned into a shared atmosphere, the ledger demands you account for it and hold something back. Where that severed cost is near zero, as it is with an asteroid mined in the empty dark, the ledger says go ahead, take all you can use, there is nothing hidden here to pay for. Honest accounting is honest about cheapness as much as about costliness. It does not invent a cost where there is none. It tells the truth in both directions.

And yet. Even out on the asteroid, where the ledger says extract freely, there is a second column on the page, and it says something the miner ignores at the cost of his life. On the rock, alone in the dark, the miner must hold reserves. Fuel to get off the asteroid and back home, or on to the next world. Air to breathe for the journey. Water and food and the working life-support that keeps a human being alive in a place that is trying every second to kill him. The miner can extract the iron as freely as he likes, but he cannot spend his reserves freely, because the reserves are the difference between a future and no future. If he burns his fuel mining one more ton of iron and finds he hasn't enough left to break orbit, the iron is worthless and so is he. He must keep enough back to be sure, not hopeful but sure, that he can survive the future he is extracting into.

Now hold the two columns side by side, because their pairing is the deepest thing in this essay. The same honest ledger, on the same asteroid, in the same breath, says two things that sound like opposites. It says extract freely, because the severed cost is near zero. And it says hold reserves, because the future is real and you have to survive into it. Take freely here. Reserve there. And these are not in tension once you see what each is about. The first column is about cost severance: who bears the cost of the act, and out here, no one, so take. The second column is about reserves against a finite future: what must be held back so that a future remains possible, and out here, plenty, so keep. One ledger, two columns, and the colony and the asteroid are the two columns made into places.

This is the whole of it, really, stated as plainly as I can manage. Honest accounting is not anti-extraction. It is two things held together. It is knowing the real cost of what you take, the severed cost that lands on others, and pricing it honestly, high where it is high and low where it is low. And it is keeping enough back, reserves against the finite future, to survive the world you are extracting into. The colony pays for its commons because the commons are scarce and the cost is real. The miner takes the asteroid freely because the cost is nearly nothing, and holds his reserves fiercely because the future is everything. Norway held its oil money in reserve. McDowell's coal was extracted with the cost severed and nothing held back. It is all one ledger, run forward and backward, on this world and off it, and the off-world scenes only let us see, with no mask in the way, the two things the ledger has always been doing: price the real cost, and hold enough back to survive.

## Whatever economy you choose

I have been careful, all through this essay, not to tell you what kind of economy to have. I want to say plainly now why I have been careful, because it is the point.

The honest ledger is not a rival economic system. It is not capitalism and it is not socialism and it is not some third named thing competing with those for your allegiance. It is something underneath all of them, something any of them needs in order to work, the way any business needs honest books no matter what it sells. It is the accounting. And honest accounting is not a politics. It is a precondition for any politics being able to see what it is doing.

You can run a free market with honest accounting, and then the market's prices tell the truth about cost, including the hidden costs of the commons, and the market can actually do the wonderful thing markets are supposed to do, which is guide a society's choices toward what is genuinely valuable. You can run a planned economy with honest accounting, and then the planners can plan against real costs rather than fictional ones. You can run an ancient kingdom or a modern republic or a sealed colony on another world, and in every single case the same thing is true: either the books are honest or the economy is flying blind, spending values it cannot see, accumulating debts it does not record, until the unseen costs come due all at once and the system is shocked by a bill it never knew it was running up.

You can see this same impulse, the impulse toward honest books and held reserves, wherever you look in the long history of human economies, dressed in the clothes of its time. In our own era, the economist Kate Raworth has drawn what she calls Doughnut Economics, a picture of an economy that has to stay above a floor of human need and below a ceiling of what the planet can bear, a way of insisting that the real limits and the real needs both get onto the books. Elinor Ostrom, whom we met earlier, spent her life documenting how communities write rules to keep their shared resources honest and intact. And long before any of them, civilizations were doing the crude, essential version of the same thing. Ancient China is remembered for what came to be called an ever-normal granary system, a way the state held grain in reserve in good years to release in bad ones, smoothing the difference between feast and famine. Ancient Egypt is remembered for great state granaries that held reserves against the years the harvest failed. I am keeping those references deliberately high and plain, because the particulars are not the point and I will not pretend to a detail I cannot verify. The point is only this: across thousands of years and utterly different economies, you find the same two moves that the off-world ledger made explicit. Know the real cost. Hold reserves against scarcity. Capitalist or planned, ancient or off-world, the honest ledger is not a system. It is what every system needs in order not to fly blind.

So when I say this essay builds a framework for honest accounting that works on this world and any other, I mean it almost mechanically. The framework does not ask you to adopt a new economy. It asks you to keep honest books in whatever economy you have, by making visible the costs that abundance and distance and time have hidden. That is the whole of the claim. It steals nothing from your politics. It only insists your politics be able to see.

## Reserves across deep time

There is one more turn to make, and it is the longest view in the essay, and it brings the reserves theme to its outer limit.

I have talked about holding reserves for the next generation, the way Norway holds its oil fund, the way the asteroid miner holds his fuel. But finite resources, taken seriously, demand reserves on a horizon far longer than a generation, longer even than recorded history, because the future a finite resource is being spent into is not merely the future of our grandchildren. It is the future of the species, and the future of the species runs into spans of time that are hard to hold in the mind.

Consider the Sun, which gives us everything and will, in the fullness of time, take it all back. The Sun is slowly getting brighter. This is not a danger in any human timescale, but on the scale of the planet's life it is decisive. Astronomers who have modelled the Sun's future estimate that its rising brightness will, somewhere in the range of five hundred million to a billion years from now, make the Earth too hot to live on, boiling away the conditions that life needs, long before the Sun's final act. And the final act comes much later still. In roughly seven and a half billion years, the Sun is expected to swell into a red giant and very likely swallow the Earth entirely. I give you both numbers, the uninhabitable date and the engulfment date, because they are different events and the first one is the one that matters: the Earth becomes unlivable not in seven and a half billion years but in something like five hundred million to a billion, an order of magnitude sooner.

Now, those numbers are so vast that they can feel like a reason not to think at all, a kind of cosmic shrug. But hold them next to everything this essay has built and they do the opposite. They turn the off-world habitat, which I introduced as a thought experiment, a lens, into something that is, on the longest view, simply a coherent forward-looking concern. If the human story is going to run on a timescale anywhere near the timescale of the planet, then at some point the only honest reserve, the only foreclosure bond posted against the truly long future, is the option to live somewhere other than a single planet with an expiration date written into its star. I am not predicting that we will. I am saying that the reserves theme, followed honestly to its end, does not stop at the next generation or the next century. It runs out to deep time, and on that horizon, holding back the option of survival elsewhere is not science fiction. It is the longest entry on the honest ledger, the reserve held against the largest scarcity of all, which is the eventual scarcity of the only world we have. The colony at the airlock and the miner with his fuel and the granary against the famine and Norway with its fund are all, in the end, the same act at different scales: hold something back, because the future is finite and real, and the only sin in the ledger is to spend it all and keep nothing in reserve.

## The mask comes off

Let me bring it home, because we have come a long way from the breath, and the breath is where it ends.

I promised at the start that this essay would do one thing. It would build a way of keeping honest accounts, a single ledger that makes visible the costs that abundance and distance and time have hidden, the costs our ordinary prices cannot see. That is all it set out to do, and I hope by now you can see the whole of it, because the whole of it is simple, even though the world it accounts for is not.

There is one ledger. It finds the value that abundance has hidden, the worth so total and so evenly spread that no price ever attached to it, and it puts that value honestly on the books. It finds the cost that severance has hidden, the bill that floated downstream onto people not at the table, and it pins the bill back where it belongs. It finds the loss that time has hidden, the finite thing spent permanently out of the future's inheritance, and it records the loss and demands that something be held in reserve. It runs forward, pricing a loss before you cause it and setting the value aside, the way Norway did with its oil. It runs backward, pricing an extraction already done and naming the debt, the way the honest accounting of McDowell's coal and the long backward reckoning of a founding wrong both do. It runs on this world, on a coal county and an oil nation and a fouled delta. And it runs on any other world, as the everyday utility bill of a sealed colony and the survival arithmetic of a miner alone on a rock.

And the best news in it is that you do not have to be a government to run it. The honest ledger is not the property of nations or economists. An individual can run it, in the choices of a single life. A community can run it, in how it governs the things it holds in common. A government can run it, at the scale of an oil fund worth more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars a citizen. It is the same ledger at every scale, because honesty about cost does not change its nature when the numbers get bigger. It only gets harder to ignore.

The values were always there. The breath was always worth everything. The barrel was always going to be gone forever. The coal always carried five hundred dollars of severed cost into a sky we treated as a free dump. The future was always inheriting whatever we left it, and was always owed whatever we took. None of these facts is new. None of them was invented by the ledger. The ledger does not create the costs. It only takes off the mask, the comfortable mask of abundance that let us look at the most valuable things in the world and read their price of zero as a verdict that they were worth nothing. Take the mask off and the number was there all along. It was only ever hidden.

So take a breath again, the way you did at the start. It still costs you nothing. The abundance is still real, and the gift is still free, and that is a genuine grace of living on a planet generous enough to give its greatest treasures away. But now you know that the price of zero is a mask, and you know what is behind it, and you know that there are people not at the table, across distance and across time, to whom something is owed every time we spend a commons as if it were worth nothing. Whether we keep accounts honestly enough to honor that debt, whether the freedom of the free breath stays free for the ones who come after us, is not written in the stars or fixed by the price of oil. It is a thing we still get to decide. The mask is off. The number was always there. What we do with it now is up to us.
