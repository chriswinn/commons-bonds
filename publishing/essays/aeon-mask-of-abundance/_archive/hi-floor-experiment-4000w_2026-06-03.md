# The Mask of Abundance

Take a breath. It cost you nothing. Not nearly nothing, the way a glass of tap water is nearly nothing, but actually nothing, a transaction so far below the threshold of accounting that no ledger anywhere recorded it. You will take another twenty thousand or so today and not one of them will appear on any bill. And from this fact we have drawn, almost without noticing, a conclusion that is exactly wrong: that the air is worth nothing.

Consider what a price actually measures. A price is the answer to a question asked at the margin, the question of what someone will give up for one more unit of a thing given how much of it there already is. Diamonds are dear and water is cheap, the old puzzle goes, even though you can live without diamonds and you cannot live a week without water. The resolution is that price tracks scarcity at the edge, not worth at the core. The hundredth glass of water in a well-watered city is worth almost nothing to you, so water is cheap, even though the absence of all water would be the absence of your life. Price is a marginal instrument. It tells you about the next unit, not the whole.

Now push that logic to its limit. Air is not merely abundant. It is abundant past the point where any margin can be found. It surrounds every buyer and every seller equally, everywhere, all the time, so completely that there is no edge at which a market could position itself to charge. You cannot corner it. You cannot withhold it. You cannot meter it. And so it posts a price of zero, and we read that zero as a verdict on its value, when in truth the air has nothing but value. It is the one thing in the economy that is all worth and no scarcity, and our only instrument for sensing worth is calibrated to scarcity, so the instrument reads nothing and we believe it.

This is the mask of abundance. It is not that the commons are underpriced. Nurses are underpriced, and topsoil is underpriced, and care work is underpriced, and these are real and serious problems, but they are problems of degree. A wrong price is still a price, still a thread you can pull. The commons are stranger than that. Clean air, drinkable water, the carrying-away of carbon, a climate that stays roughly where civilization grew up inside it: these have no price at all. And a thing with no price is not undervalued. It is invisible. It does not appear in the model. It is not a number that is too small; it is the absence of a number, and the only language our economy speaks fluently is the language of numbers.

I want to follow this absence all the way down, and then all the way out, because I think the same correction works on every scale, on this world and on any other, run forward into the future or backward into the past. There is one honest ledger. Every move in this essay is the same move: find the value that abundance or distance or time has hidden, and put it on the ledger where it belongs.

## Seal the room

Let me offer you a lens, and let me be clear that it is only a lens. Imagine sealing a system. Not as a prediction, not as a forecast of where we are headed, but as a way of making the invisible visible by removing the abundance that hides it. Take a volume of the world and close it off, and watch what happens to the things that used to be free.

We do not have to imagine this entirely, because in three real places the bill already arrives.

Go down with the saturation divers, the ones who live for weeks under pressure to work on the seabed. They cannot breathe ordinary air down there. Under enough pressure the nitrogen that is harmless at the surface turns narcotic, clouding the mind, and the oxygen that keeps you alive turns toxic. So they breathe heliox, oxygen cut with helium, and helium is not free. It runs somewhere around two dollars a cubic foot, which works out to roughly eighty to two hundred dollars an hour for the gas alone, per diver, which is why the support ships run reclaim rigs to scavenge and recycle every breath exhaled. At the surface, helium is a party-balloon afterthought. Drop a few hundred feet of water on top of a person and it becomes one of the most carefully metered substances in their world. Nothing about the helium changed. What changed is that the abundance was stripped away, and the value that abundance had hidden came due by the hour.

Or take Biosphere 2, the great sealed glasshouse in the Arizona desert. In the early nineteen-nineties, eight people closed themselves inside roughly three acres of sealed structure, built at a cost on the order of a hundred and fifty million dollars, to live off an enclosed world. And the enclosed world began, quietly, to suffocate them. Over sixteen months the oxygen in the air fell from the ordinary twenty-one percent down toward fourteen, because the soil teemed with microbes that breathed it in faster than the plants could replace it, and because the carbon dioxide they produced was binding into the freshly cured concrete and vanishing from the cycle. The managers eventually had to pump oxygen in from outside. Inside, the small world was coming apart in other ways. Most of the vertebrate species kept inside died. Every pollinator died, so the crops had to be hand-pollinated by people. The air, the bees, the quiet labor of unseen organisms, all the things that on Earth are so abundant they have never needed a name on an invoice, turned out to be the load-bearing walls of the enterprise.

Or go up, to the International Space Station, a few hundred kilometers overhead, where the accounting is brutally explicit because every gram had to be lifted out of Earth's gravity. Water, the thing that falls from the sky for free, costs something like twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram to put into orbit. A six-month supply for the crew runs to a figure on the order of a hundred and thirty-five million dollars, which is why the station recycles around ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine back into drinking water, and makes its oxygen by splitting that hard-won water with electricity. Up there nobody is confused about what air and water are worth. The mask is off because the abundance is gone.

Seal the system, and the priceless commons become the most expensive things there are. They did not become valuable. They were always valuable. The seal merely revealed it.

## The name of the thing

So what is the mechanism? Why does the honest number go missing in the first place?

In most cases it is what I will call cost severance. Someone creates a cost, and someone else absorbs it, and there is no channel by which the second party can bill it back to the first. A factory pours its effluent into a river, and the people downstream pay in fouled water and lost fish, but no invoice travels upstream. The cost has been severed from the act that caused it. Honest pricing, in these ordinary cases, means re-attaching the severed cost, sending the bill back to where the harm began.

But the commons case is stranger than severance, and this is the heart of it. The value of the air was never severed from a price, because it never had a price to begin with. It is a value so total and so evenly spread that no market ever found an edge to charge at, so the worth simply sat there, unpriced, until scarcity arrived and revealed it. Call it the residual commons value: the worth that remains, unpriced, after every sale is done, owed to people who were not at the table when the deal was struck, and very often to people not yet born. When you burn a tank of gas, the sale that put it in your tank settled a price for the present moment. It did not settle anything for the residual, the part of the value that belongs to a stable climate and a livable future, because that part never had a price for the sale to settle.

This is the distinction the whole framework turns on. Sometimes honest accounting means re-attaching a cost that was severed. Sometimes it means discovering a value so complete it never became a price at all, and was only ever going to show itself when the abundance ran out.

## The hundred-dollar barrel

Put a number on it. Say a barrel of oil sells for a hundred dollars, and let that number be illustrative, a round figure to think with rather than a market quote. What does the hundred dollars actually buy?

It buys the present its barrel. It pays for the lifting and the refining and the shipping and the margin and the tax, all the real costs of getting that oil out of the ground and into your tank today. It is a fair price for the present moment's use. What it does not do, what no market price of a barrel has ever done, is buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after. Because the barrel does not come back.

Here the difference between renewable and non-renewable resources stops being a slogan and becomes precise. A renewable resource poses a question of rate. A forest, a fishery, an aquifer fed by rain: these breed, regrow, refill, and the only honest discipline is to take less than the rate at which they replenish. Take at or below that rate and you can take forever. Overshoot it and you draw down the stock, but even then the question is one of speed, of rate against rate.

Oil is not like that. Oil is not drawn down faster than it breeds, because it does not breed. On any human timescale it is simply liquidated, converted once from a stock that took the planet hundreds of millions of years to lay down into heat and motion and carbon in the sky, and then it is gone, permanently, with no rate of extraction slow enough to make it sustainable, because there is no replenishment to keep pace with. There is no take-less-than-it-breeds for a thing that does not breed. The hundred dollars buys the present its tank of gas. The barrel itself, and everything downstream of having burned it, is charged to a future that did not get a vote.

## The empty plot

It would be strange if no one had noticed this before, and the truth is that several of the deepest traditions in economics and moral philosophy walked right up to the empty plot of ground, looked at it, and built next door.

Karl Marx, in the first volume of Capital, priced the surplus that labor produces and the owner takes. It is a profound accounting of who captures the value, but it is an accounting of class, of the division of the spoils among the living, not of time and the claims of those who do not yet exist. Elinor Ostrom, whose Governing the Commons earned her a Nobel, showed the world that communities can and do manage shared resources without either privatizing them or having the state seize them, but the commons she governed so brilliantly were renewable ones, fisheries and pastures and irrigation systems, the question of rate rather than the question of permanent loss. Herman Daly spent a career warning that an economy treating throughput as costless was an economy headed for a wall, and he was right, but the warning was a general alarm, not an invoice for one specific foreclosed thing. John Hartwick, in a brief and beautiful argument, showed that if you reinvest the rents from a depleting resource into reproducible capital you can hold consumption steady forever, which is the right instinct exactly, except that it saves the rent rather than reckoning with the barrel.

Then the moral philosophers circled the same ground from the other side. John Rawls asked us to choose the rules of society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing who we would turn out to be, and others have widened that veil until it covers not just every living person but every generation, including those who cannot be at the table because they do not yet exist. Michael Sandel, in What Money Can't Buy, mapped the way that putting a price on certain things corrupts them, which is the mask of abundance seen from its mirror image. Derek Parfit, in Reasons and Persons, worked out with painful care what we owe to future people and how badly our ordinary intuitions fail when the future is involved.

And then the economists who took climate seriously fought a long and bitter war over a single number, the discount rate, the rate at which we shrink the future to compare it with the present. The Stern Review used a low rate, close to one and a half percent, and concluded the damages were enormous and urgent, around one percent of global output to address. William Nordhaus, whose DICE model also won a Nobel, used a rate closer to four percent and concluded for a far more gradual response. Martin Weitzman argued that the whole fight missed the point, that the real danger lived in the fat tail of catastrophic, low-probability outcomes that no central estimate captures. You can see how much the rate matters in a single illustration: a hundred dollars of benefit a century from now is worth about twenty-five dollars today at Stern's rate, and only about a dollar and a half at Nordhaus's.

But notice what every one of these brilliant fighters was pricing. They were all pricing the damage flow, the stream of harms that combustion sends into the future, the floods and droughts and failed harvests, discounted back to a present value. They were arguing about how heavily to weigh a flow of future damage. None of them was pricing the resource's absence, the simple fact that the barrel is gone and will not return. And the residual commons value survives even Stern's generous low rate, because it is not a discounted flow at all. It is not a stream of future harms to be summed and shrunk. It is a present absence, here and now, of a thing that will not come back, and you cannot discount a hole the way you discount a payment, because the hole is already total and already permanent on the day the barrel is burned.

## McDowell County, running backward

Let the abstraction touch ground. In McDowell County, in the southern coalfields of West Virginia, coal came out of the mine mouth around 1960 for something like four or five dollars a ton. That was the price. That was what the present paid, and it was, for the present, a fair-seeming number.

Run the honest ledger across it. Add back the severed costs, the ones that traveled downstream and downwind and down the years to people who never signed the sale. The carbon term alone, roughly two and a half tons of carbon dioxide for every ton of coal burned, multiplied by a social cost of carbon in the neighborhood of a hundred and ninety dollars a ton, comes to something on the order of five hundred and ten dollars. Sum the rest and the honest price of that ton of coal lands somewhere around five hundred and twenty-four dollars, with a defensible range a little above and below. The mine mouth charged four. The world was owed five hundred and twenty-four.

This is the framework run backward, on an extraction already realized, the ton already burned, the difference between what was charged and what was owed now scattered across the atmosphere and the people under it. The hundred-dollar barrel was the same arithmetic pointed forward, at a sale not yet made. McDowell County is the arithmetic pointed back, at a sale long since settled, with the residual unpaid and overdue.

## Forward and in reverse

The ledger runs in both directions, and each direction wants its own instrument.

Run it forward and you get something I will call a Foreclosure Bond: before the extraction, price the future loss the act will cause, and set that money aside, so that what is being foreclosed on the future is at least paid for rather than simply taken. Run it backward and you get a Restitution Bond: for an extraction already realized, price what was taken and never paid for, and reckon it now.

The backward direction is not a hypothetical. It is the architecture of the case that William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen make in From Here to Equality, the most rigorous economic case for reparations yet written. Read carefully, reparations economics is backward honest-accounting. It prices a residual that was severed from its sale and never paid: the wealth extracted across the centuries since 1619, compounded into a racial wealth gap that is still measurable today, and into legacy effects as stark as the gap in longevity between Black and white Americans, the difference in years of life itself. Ta-Nehisi Coates, in The Case for Reparations, built the argument's moral architecture; Darity and Mullen built the ledger.

Here I want to be careful about a line I will not cross. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not mine to do. That pricing belongs to the people living inside that history and continuing the work, and I will defer to it rather than presume to perform it. What the framework can honestly do from the outside is reach the coerced cases through their legacy effects, the measurable downstream consequences that show up in wealth and in lifespan, and leave the direct pricing of the coercion itself to those whose work it is. The ledger is honest precisely when it knows the limits of what it is entitled to total.

## Two architectures, one act

Place two countries side by side. They did the same physical thing, pulling oil out of the ground and selling it. They built opposite ledgers around the act, and the difference is the whole argument.

Norway is the existence-proof that the future can in fact be priced, not in theory but in practice, by an actual government answerable to actual voters. Oil was confirmed off Norway's stretch of the North Sea just before Christmas in 1969, at the Ekofisk field. Rather than spend the windfall as it came, Norway built an institution. In 1990 it created the Government Petroleum Fund, renamed the Government Pension Fund Global in 2006, and into it the oil revenue flowed. In 2001 the country adopted a fiscal rule of disarming simplicity: the government may spend only the fund's expected real return, around three percent, while the capital itself stays inviolate. The principal is not to be touched. Today the fund holds well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every citizen of the country, and what is most remarkable is that no government of any political color has raided it. Through administrations of the left and the right, Stoltenberg and Bondevik and Solberg and Støre, the principal has held. That is Hartwick's rule realized at sovereign scale, the rent reinvested rather than consumed, and it is also, fittingly, a reserve, money held back against the day the oil is gone.

The Niger Delta is the counter-case, and I want to name it as architecture rather than as a villain story, because the point is not that one people are wicked and another virtuous but that the same act built two different ledgers. Oil has been pumped from the Delta since the nineteen-fifties, generating hundreds of billions of dollars in revenue. But the costs, the spilled crude soaking into the creeks, the gas flared off into the sky, the fisheries foreclosed for the people who lived on them, were borne by communities who did not sell the oil and could bill no one for the loss. When Ken Saro-Wiwa led a movement to demand that accounting, he and eight others were executed in 1995, and Nigeria was suspended from the Commonwealth in the aftermath. Years later a United Nations assessment of just one part of the region, Ogoniland, estimated that the cleanup would require an initial billion dollars and run for twenty-five to thirty years, an invoice for a residual that was never set aside while the revenue flowed.

Same physical act. Two architectures. One question runs through both: when the cost outlives the sale, who carries it, and was anything set aside? This is not an argument against extraction. It is not a political program. It is a question about whether the ledger is honest, and Norway answered it one way and the Delta was answered another.

## The asteroid miner

Now let the lens travel as far as it can go, and again let me be explicit that this is a lens and not a prophecy. Imagine, only to think with, a miner working an asteroid.

Out there, in the dark between the planets, there is no community to drink the fouled water and no ecosystem to lose its fisheries, because there is no water and no fishery and no one downstream at all. The asteroid is a rock. When you take iron from it, the cost severance, the harm that travels to some uncompensated party, is close to zero, because there is no party. And so the honest ledger, the very same ledger that totaled five hundred and twenty-four dollars against a ton of McDowell coal, says something that might sound surprising coming from an argument so concerned with restraint: extract freely. Take the iron. If a comet swings only briefly into reach, mine it hard while you can, because there is no future community whose inheritance you are foreclosing.

And yet the same ledger, in the same breath, imposes an absolute discipline, and it is a discipline of reserves. On the asteroid or the comet itself, you must hold back fuel, and air, and water, and life-support, enough to be certain you can get off the rock and get home, or get on to the next world. The ledger that says take freely here says, with equal force, reserve there. The two are not in tension; they are the same honesty. Knowing the real cost means knowing both that the iron is nearly free to take and that the air in your tanks is the most valuable thing for a hundred million miles, and that running it down to nothing to grab one more load is not boldness but a failure of accounting. Honest extraction is not anti-extraction. It is knowing the true cost, and keeping enough back to survive the future you are extracting into.

## Whatever economy you choose

Step back and notice what the framework is not. It is not a rival economic system. It is not capitalism's replacement or socialism's vindication or some third doctrine to be adopted whole. It is the accounting that any system whatsoever needs in order to see what it is doing.

Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics, with its inner ring of human need and outer ring of planetary limit, is one attempt to draw the real boundaries on the map. Ostrom's patient documentation of how communities govern their commons is another. And the impulse is far older than any modern name for it. Long before economics existed as a discipline, civilizations built reserve systems against scarcity. Ancient China maintained granaries meant to even out the years of plenty and famine. Ancient Egypt kept state granaries against the lean seasons. I will keep these at the level of the gesture, because the particulars are not mine to embroider, but the gesture itself is unmistakable and ancient: a society trying to know its real costs and hold something back against the future. Capitalist or planned, ancient or off-world, the ledger has to be honest or the economy is flying blind, mistaking the zero on the air for a verdict on its worth.

## Reserves on the longest horizon

There is one more horizon, and it is the longest one there is, and it changes how seriously to take the off-world habitat that the asteroid lens conjured.

Finite resources demand reserves not only for the next generation but across civilizational time, and the deepest reason is written into the sky. The Sun is slowly brightening. As it ages it will grow more luminous, and on the order of five hundred million to a billion years from now, by the reckoning of Sackmann and her colleagues, that rising luminosity will make the Earth's surface uninhabitable, long before the Sun ever swells into the red giant that, by the work of Schröder and Smith, may engulf the planet around seven and a half billion years from now. The end of the world as a place for life is not the dramatic distant fireball. It is the quiet brightening, far sooner.

On those horizons the sealed habitat stops being science fiction and becomes a coherent forward-looking concern, the longest-dated entry on the ledger, option-preservation across deep time. To keep open the possibility that there is somewhere else for life to go is itself a reserve, held back not for the next generation but for the species, against a scarcity so far off that no market will ever price it, which is exactly why no market will, and exactly why the honest ledger must.

## The mask comes off

So take another breath. It still costs you nothing, and you can still hear, underneath that nothing, the whole argument. The price was always zero and the value was always total, and the only thing standing between those two facts was the abundance that hid one behind the other.

The framework is not a system to be installed. It is honest accounting that an individual can run on a tank of gas, that a community can run on a hillside of coal, that a government can run on an oil field, on this world or any other, forward into the future or backward into the past. One ledger, run honestly, finding the value that distance and time and abundance had hidden and writing it down where it belongs.

The number was always there. The mask only made it look like a zero. And whether the freedom to take the next breath without thinking about its cost stays free, whether the air stays abundant enough to keep its value hidden, is not written into the sky. It is one of the few things at this scale that we still get to decide.
