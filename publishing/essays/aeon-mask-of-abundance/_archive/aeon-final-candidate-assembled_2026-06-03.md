# The Mask of Abundance

You are standing in an airlock, and the inner door will not open until you answer one question. How do you intend to pay for your air?

The question is not cruel, and here it is not even strange. Inside the wall, the air is made. A machine splits it out of water, drawing on power that something had to generate, and pulls the carbon dioxide you breathe out back out of the room before it rises high enough to kill you. Every litre is counted, because every litre had to be produced. So before they let you into the only breathable space for a very long way in any direction, they need to know how your breathing will be paid for.

You have no answer ready. You have never needed one. You have spent your whole life drawing breath with no meter and no bill and no thought, and somewhere along the way you came to believe that air is simply free. It is not. It never was. On Earth the cost was real the entire time; it was only ever paid by something else, by the forests and the oceans and the slow chemistry of a living planet, quietly, on your behalf, with no invoice ever sent to you. Here, behind the wall, there is no planet to carry that cost, so it comes to you, by the hour, on a meter you can read.

None of this is a prediction. The airlock is a way of seeing, not a forecast of where you will live. But it is not pure invention either. We have already built small sealed worlds and lived inside them, on Devon Island in the Canadian Arctic and in a dome on the side of a Hawaiian volcano, and in every one of them the free things of Earth became the most expensive things inside the wall.

Now leave, and come home. The air is free again, and the heat and the light and the water with it, all at once, the moment you are back. For a while the airlock question seems absurd, a piece of off-world paperwork with no bearing on real life. Then the harder thought arrives. The cost here is just as real as it was behind the wall. It is only paid by someone else: by a forest on another continent, by an ocean turning slowly more acidic, by a person not yet born who will inherit a thinner margin than the one you were handed. The bill was never cancelled. It was sent somewhere you could not see, to someone who could not send it back.

That is the whole subject of this essay. The most valuable things we have are the ones we all share and never pay for: the air, the fresh water, a stable and livable climate, the planet's quiet work of taking back the carbon we burn. We are trained to read a price of zero as a verdict that a thing is worth little. The truth is the reverse. These things carry no price because they are so abundant that no market can find an edge to charge at, and that abundance hides their value so completely that we do not even undervalue them. We do not see them at all. Call that the mask of abundance. The danger is not that we treat these things as cheap. It is that a thing with no price is invisible to a civilization that decides what to protect by reading prices.

Start with the places where the mask has already slipped. They are real, and there are not many of them, and in each one a thing the rest of us take for granted carries a number you can read.

Go down far enough under the sea, in a diving suit or a pressurized vessel, and the ordinary air you have breathed your whole life turns into a slow poison. Under pressure, the nitrogen that makes up most of the atmosphere stops being inert and starts to act on the nervous system, dulling judgment the way alcohol does, until a working diver cannot be trusted with the work. Push deeper still and even the oxygen, the part of air we think of as the part that keeps us alive, becomes toxic at the wrong pressure and begins to do harm. So divers who work at depth for hours at a time do not breathe air. They breathe a mixture in which most of the nitrogen has been replaced by helium, which does not turn narcotic the same way. The helium is not free, and the clearest proof of that is not a price tag but a behavior: the ships running these operations install rigs to capture the helium the divers breathe out and reclaim it, scrubbing it clean to use again rather than letting it escape. The modern systems recover more than 80 percent of the exhaled helium, and the newer ones reach into the 90s. People do not build machines to recapture a breathed-out gas unless losing it genuinely costs them. Helium runs only a few dollars a cubic foot, a small enough number on its own, but at working depth a diver moves so much of it that letting the exhaled gas go is letting real money go, and so the gas is caught and cleaned and breathed again. None of this is the cost of being alive. It is the cost of one specific gas in the breath, broken out and reclaimed, in the one place on Earth where ordinary air will kill you and the substitute has a real price behind the seal.

That is the first crack in the mask. Here is a wider one.

In the Arizona desert at the start of the 1990s, a group of people sealed themselves inside a structure of glass and steel that covered about 3 acres and tried to live there as if it were a small separate Earth. The project was called Biosphere 2, on the idea that the first biosphere was the planet itself. Eight people went in. It cost roughly $150 million to build, and it was meant to be closed, a world with its own air and water and food, recycling everything, taking nothing in. Inside were small versions of the planet's great systems, a patch of rainforest, a stretch of ocean, a desert, farmland to feed the crew. The intention was that the thing the open Earth does without anyone noticing, the endless turning of breath and rot and growth into breathable air and fertile soil, would happen inside the glass as well, on its own.

It did not happen on its own. Over 16 months the oxygen inside fell from the normal share of the air, about 21 percent, down to about 14 percent, a level you would meet near the top of a very high mountain, low enough to leave the crew exhausted and unwell. The oxygen was not vanishing into nothing. The soil they had brought in to grow food was rich with microbes, and those microbes breathed, consuming oxygen and giving off carbon dioxide, faster than the plants could turn it back. And the carbon dioxide did not simply build up to be measured; much of it reacted with the fresh concrete of the structure and bound itself into the walls, so the carbon went out of the air without the oxygen coming back. In the end the managers had to do the thing a closed world is not supposed to need. They pumped oxygen in from outside. The little planet could not keep its own air. Meanwhile the small living systems collapsed in their own ways. Of 25 kinds of vertebrate animals brought in, 19 died. Every pollinating insect died, which meant the crops had to be pollinated by hand, person by person, flower by flower, doing the unpaid work that bees do for the open world for nothing.

Biosphere 2 is sometimes told as a story about failure, and as an experiment in self-sufficiency it did fail. But that is not the lesson that matters here. The lesson is what the failure priced. Outside the glass, the turning of carbon dioxide back into oxygen, the pollination of flowering plants, the maintenance of a breathable atmosphere at all, costs nothing and is noticed by no one. Seal eight people inside 3 acres of glass and those same services suddenly demand $150 million of infrastructure, a supply of bottled oxygen trucked in from the outside, and human hands doing the work of vanished bees. The services did not change. What changed is that the planet was no longer there to provide them for free, and the moment the free provider was gone, the bill arrived, itemized.

The third crack is the most expensive of all, and it is in orbit right now. The International Space Station circles a few hundred kilometres above us, and everyone aboard breathes, drinks, and washes, and every gram of what they use had to be lifted up out of Earth's gravity at enormous cost. Putting water into orbit runs to something like $21,000 per kilogram. A six-month supply of water for a crew, if you simply shipped it up, would cost on the order of $135 million. Water, the cheapest thing in the world to most of the world, the thing that falls from the sky for nothing, becomes one of the most expensive substances a human being can possess once you have to carry it off the planet. So the station does not ship it all up. It recycles. The life-support systems reclaim about 90 percent of the crew's sweat and urine, cleaning it back into drinking water, because the alternative is to keep paying $21,000 a kilogram. The oxygen they breathe is made on board, split out of water by running electricity through it, the same trick the airlock in the opening runs, because hauling up enough bottled oxygen to keep people alive for months would cost more than anyone wants to pay.

Three real sealed worlds, three different scales, one identical result. The diver's reclaimed helium, the dome's vanished oxygen and dead bees, the station's $21,000 water. In every case a thing that is free on the open Earth becomes the most expensive thing there is the moment the planet stops providing it. The price was always there. We simply never had to look at it, because the bill went to the planet, and the planet does not send statements.

Now I can name the thing that connects all of this, the mechanism that has been running quietly under the whole story so far. I want to build it in plain words first, because the plain words are the whole of it and the name adds nothing but a handle.

When you do almost anything, you create costs. Some of those costs you pay yourself, in money or effort, at the time, and those are the ones the market sees and prices. But many costs do not land on you. They land on someone else, somewhere else, sometimes much later, and that someone else has no way to send the bill back to you. You burn fuel and the warming lands on a farmer two continents away and on a child not yet born. You drink from a river and the depletion lands on the town downstream. You buy something cheap and the cheapness was paid for by a forest that was cleared, or by a community whose water was fouled, none of whom you will ever meet and none of whom can charge you. The cost did not disappear. It was cut loose from the person who caused it and set adrift, to be absorbed by people who had no part in the decision and no power to object to it. Call this cost severance. A cost is created, it is severed from its cause, and it comes to rest on someone who cannot send it back.

The severance has two directions, and it is worth holding them apart, because most of our moral language is built for only one of them. There is severance across distance, the burned fuel and the farmer two continents away, the cost that travels sideways from the one who caused it to the one who happens to stand downstream. There is at least, in principle, a party there who could be billed, someone alive and present who can point at the thing that harmed them. And then there is severance across time, which is stranger and harder, because the injured party is not present, has not been born, cannot point at anything, cannot send a bill at all. A cost severed across time comes to rest on people who do not yet exist, and people who do not yet exist make poor plaintiffs. The future's loss did not roll away on its own. It was cut. The whole apparatus of price and law and grievance is built for parties who can show up, and the deepest severed costs are owed to parties who cannot, because they are downstream not in the river but in the years, and the years have no representative who can present the bill.

Most of the costs that get severed this way are attached to an act of taking. You take something of value out of the shared world, and you sell it, and you keep the money. Call that taking value extraction, in the simple sense that you have extracted a value and turned it into a price you can pocket. The trouble is that the price almost never captures everything the taking destroyed. When a forest is cut and sold for timber, the price covers the wood. It does not cover the flood control the forest was quietly doing, the carbon it was holding, the species it sheltered, the water it cleaned for the people downstream. Those values were real, and the sale destroyed them, and no one paid for them, because no market had a way to charge for them. What is left over after the sale is done, the worth that was destroyed but never priced and never compensated, is the residual commons value. It is the part of what you took that belonged to everyone and was paid to no one. It is owed, but there is no one to collect it, because the people it is owed to are scattered across distance and across time, many of them not yet born, none of them at the table when the deal was struck.

This gives us a way to define honest pricing precisely. An honest price is one that has re-attached the severed costs, that has added back the residual commons value, so that the number on the tag is the whole of what the thing actually cost the world and not just the part that happened to land on the seller. In most cases honest pricing is exactly this act of re-attachment, taking a cost that was cut loose and reconnecting it to the price that should have carried it.

But the commons are stranger than that, and the airlock has already shown us how. The air, the water, the climate, the planet's work of taking carbon back, were never severed from a price, because they never had a price to be severed from. They are not underpriced the way a nurse's labor is underpriced or topsoil is underpriced, where there is a price and it is simply too low. They have no price at all. They are so abundant, so completely available to everyone everywhere, that no market could ever find an edge to stand at and charge. You cannot sell someone the next breath of air when the air is everywhere and free. The value is total and the price is zero, and the zero is not a measure of low worth but a measure of an abundance so complete that the worth never had to be expressed as a number. This is the mask of abundance stated exactly. The residual commons value of the air is not a cost that slipped through the cracks of the market. It is a value so large and so evenly spread that the market never reached it at all, and we will only ever see it priced in the one place where the abundance ends, which is inside the seal.

Hold those three ideas together, because the whole of the rest follows from them. Cost severance: a cost cut loose from its cause and dropped on someone who cannot bill it back. Value extraction: the taking that creates the severed cost. Residual commons value: the worth that the taking destroyed, owed to people not at the table, often not yet born, and never paid. Once you can see these three, you can run the same honest accounting on any decision, at any scale, on this world or any other, and the framework that follows is nothing more than running that accounting carefully and refusing to look away from the part of the bill that someone else is paying.

Take the clearest case there is. Imagine a barrel of oil priced at $100. I am using the round number as an illustration, not as today's market quote; the exact figure does not matter to the argument. A hundred dollars is what the present is willing to pay to have that barrel now, and as a price for the present it may be perfectly accurate. The buyer wants fuel this week, the seller wants money this week, and the two of them agree on $100, and the trade is fair to both of them. The problem is not that the price is wrong for them. The problem is what the price is silent about.

The barrel does not belong only to the present. The oil took tens of millions of years to form, and once it is burned it is gone, not for a season but for the entire future of the species. The $100 buys the present its tank of fuel. It does not buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after, because there is no buying it back. The value that was extracted reaches forward across all the time there is, and the price reaches forward exactly one transaction, from a willing seller to a willing buyer, both of them alive now, neither of them speaking for anyone who is not. Everyone downstream in time, everyone who might have wanted that carbon left in the ground, or wanted the warming it causes not to happen, or simply wanted the option of that resource still being there, is a party to the transaction who was never asked and cannot be paid. That is the residual commons value of the barrel, and the $100 does not touch it.
This is the place to be precise about a distinction that does a great deal of work, the difference between things that renew and things that do not. A forest, a fishery, an aquifer that refills, a herd of animals: these renew. They breed, they regrow, they refill. With a renewing resource the honest question is not whether to take but how fast. There is a rate at which you can take and the resource replaces what you took, and stay under that rate and you can harvest the same thing forever, generation after generation, taking nothing from anyone who comes later. The whole problem with a renewing resource is a problem of rate, of taking less than it breeds. Go over the rate and you draw the stock down; stay under it and you can take indefinitely. The accounting is real but it is solvable, because the resource is willing to give you a harvest every year if you let it.

Oil is not like this. Oil does not breed. There is no rate slow enough to make a barrel of oil sustainable, because the barrel does not come back at any rate at all. Every barrel burned is a barrel that the entire future will now never have, and no patience, no restraint, no careful management of the harvest brings it back, because there is no harvest, only a liquidation. A renewing resource taken too fast is a debt you can repay by slowing down. A barrel of oil burned is a debt that cannot be repaid by anyone, ever, because the thing that was owed was the barrel itself, and it is gone. This is why the $100 is so misleading. It is a price built for a renewing world, a price that says here is a fair exchange between two willing parties, when the thing being exchanged is not renewing at all but is a piece of a fixed and shrinking inheritance that belongs as much to the future as to the present. The $100 buys the present its tank of gas. It does not buy the barrel back for anyone after, because the barrel is not for sale to them. It was only ever for sale to us, and we are spending it.

There is a further part of the barrel's value that the $100 cannot see, and it is worth naming because it is the part most easily missed. Even setting aside the warming the burning causes, the barrel has a worth simply in remaining a barrel, in staying available, unspent, an option held open for whoever might want it. A future generation might find a use for that oil that we cannot imagine, as a feedstock for something, as a material rather than a fuel, or might simply value having the choice we are removing. When you burn the barrel you do not only consume its energy; you close every door it could have opened for everyone after you, and you close them permanently. That closed option is real and it is valuable, and it appears nowhere in the $100, because the buyer and the seller are pricing a use, here and now, and an option held open for an unborn person is not a use anyone can buy or sell today. The present cannot purchase the option from the future, because the future is not present to sell it, and so the option is simply taken, quietly, at no charge, the way the diver's helium was once free until the sea made it expensive. The barrel's full residual commons value is the warming avoided plus the option preserved plus the resource itself kept whole, and the $100 is silent on all three.

It would be strange if no one had ever noticed any of this, and no one has the empty plot to themselves. Several great traditions of thought walked right up to the edge of it and built something nearby, and it is worth seeing exactly how close each came and exactly where each turned away, because the near-misses map the shape of the thing better than any direct description could.

Karl Marx, in the first volume of *Capital*, built an entire theory around a value that is created and then taken by someone who did not create it. The worker produces more than the worker is paid, and the surplus is extracted by the owner of capital. This is value extraction described with enormous care, and Marx saw the moral force of it clearly. But the party he was concerned with was a class, the workers, alive at the same time as the owners, present in the same economy, capable in principle of organizing and of speaking for themselves. His severed cost ran across the line between classes, not across the line between generations. He was not thinking about the barrel of oil whose true owner has not been born. He priced the surplus, but he priced it as a matter of class, not of time.

Elinor Ostrom, who won the Nobel in economics in 2009 for the work in her book *Governing the Commons*, did something the standard theory had said was impossible. She showed, with case after real case, that communities can and do manage shared resources well, without either private ownership or top-down state control, through their own agreed rules. Fisheries, forests, irrigation systems, grazing lands: people who depend on a common resource can govern it sustainably among themselves. This is one of the most important findings in the social sciences, and it is directly about the commons. But look at what kind of commons. Ostrom's commons are renewing commons, the fishery and the forest and the irrigation water, the resources where the whole game is getting the rate right so the thing keeps providing. Her communities are communities of people who all use the resource and all live now and can sit in the same room and make rules together. She solved the problem of governing the renewing commons among the living. She did not turn to the liquidated commons, the barrel that does not come back, owed to people who cannot be in the room because they do not yet exist.

Herman Daly, the economist who spent his life arguing for what he called a steady-state economy and who died in 2022, came at it from the side of scale. His central warning was about throughput, about the sheer physical volume of stuff a growing economy pulls out of the Earth and pushes back into it as waste, and his insistence was that this throughput cannot grow forever on a finite planet. He saw, more clearly than almost anyone in his profession, that the economy is a subsystem of a finite biosphere and not the other way around. But his concern was the aggregate, the total flow, the overall scale pressing against planetary limits. He did not write an invoice for one specific foreclosed thing, did not take a single barrel and price the residual commons value owed by it to the future. He warned about the size of the river. He did not bill a single drop.

John Hartwick came closest of all, and his near-miss is the most instructive. In a short and famous result published in 1977 in the *American Economic Review*, Hartwick worked out a rule for what a society should do if it is going to deplete a non-renewing resource and still treat the future fairly. The rule, now named after him, is this: take the rents you earn from the depletion, the pure profit beyond the cost of extraction, and reinvest them, so that the productive capital you build up replaces the natural capital you are using up, and the total stock available to future generations does not fall. This is genuinely about the non-renewing resource. It is genuinely about fairness across time. It is the closest any economist came to the barrel. But notice what Hartwick's rule saves. It saves the rents, the money. It says reinvest the proceeds so that the future inherits as much wealth as you did. It does not save the barrel. It assumes the substitution works, that built capital can stand in for the oil and the climate and the lost option, that as long as the future is as rich it does not matter that the specific irreplaceable thing is gone. For many resources the substitution does work. For the deepest commons it may not, because there are things a future cannot buy back at any price, and a stable climate may be one of them. Hartwick saved the rent. He did not, and his rule does not, account for the possibility that the barrel itself was the thing that mattered and that no amount of reinvested money brings it back.

The moral philosophers came at the same empty plot from the other side, from the question of what we owe rather than the question of what things cost, and three of them in particular widened the circle in the right direction. John Rawls, in *A Theory of Justice* in 1971, asked us to design the rules of a society from behind what he called a veil of ignorance, not knowing which person in that society we would turn out to be, so that we could not rig the rules in favor of our own position. The device forces fairness by stripping away the knowledge of where you stand. Rawls applied it mostly across the people alive in a society at one time, but the device has an obvious and powerful extension: design the rules without knowing which generation you will be born into, the one that burns the barrel or the one that inherits the world after it is gone. Behind a veil that thick, no one would write the rule we actually live by, the rule that lets the present sell the future's inheritance and keep all the money.

Michael Sandel, in *What Money Can't Buy* in 2012, pressed on a different nerve, the question of which things should be for sale at all. His argument is that putting a price on some things does not just measure them, it corrupts them, changes what they are, degrades a value that depended on not being bought and sold. He was mostly concerned with goods inside a society, the buying of medical queue positions, of votes, of human organs, the way the market reaches into spheres where it does not belong and damages them by its mere presence. But the deepest of all the things that money cannot rightly buy is the one the airlock named, the stable climate and the breathable air that no generation made and none should be able to sell out from under the rest. Sandel showed that some values are corrupted by being priced. He did not extend the thought to the value that was never priced at all because it was too abundant to reach, but the bridge is short and the structure is the same.

Derek Parfit, in *Reasons and Persons* in 1984, came nearest of the three. He worked directly and rigorously on what we owe to people who do not yet exist, on the strange logical puzzles that arise when our choices today determine not only how good future lives will be but which future people will exist at all. He took the obligation to the future seriously as a problem in moral philosophy, with all the difficulty it deserves. What he did not do, because it was not his project, was turn that obligation into a number, into a price, into something you could put on a barrel of oil and add to the $100. He established that the obligation is real and that it binds us. He left the accounting to someone else.

And the economists who did try to put a number on the future fought a long war over a single figure, and that war is the last and most revealing near-miss. The question was how to compare a cost that lands today against a benefit, or a harm, that lands a century or more from now, and the tool for the comparison is the discount rate, the rate at which future value is converted into present value. A high discount rate says the far future barely counts in today's decisions; a low one says it counts almost as much as the present. Nicholas Stern, in the review he led for the British government published in 2006 and 2007, used a very low discount rate, on the order of 1.5 percent, on the moral argument that a person's welfare should not count for less simply because they happen to be born later. With that low rate the future weighs heavily, and the conclusion was that the world should spend on the order of 1 percent of its output now to prevent the climate damage coming later, because the later damage, barely discounted, is enormous. William Nordhaus, who won the Nobel in 2018 for building the standard economic models of climate change, used a higher rate, closer to 4 percent, drawn from the actual returns available in markets, and with that higher rate the far future weighs much less and the case for spending heavily now is correspondingly weaker. Martin Weitzman pushed from a third direction entirely, arguing that the real danger is not the most likely outcome but the small chance of catastrophe, the fat tail of the probability distribution, the low-odds, high-harm possibilities that a focus on the average completely misses.

This argument has run for decades, and people treat it as the whole of the matter, as if once you settle the discount rate you have settled what we owe the future. But notice what every side of it is actually pricing. Stern, Nordhaus, and Weitzman are all pricing the same thing: the flow of damage that combustion causes, the floods and the failed harvests and the rising seas, discounted back to the present at one rate or another. They are arguing about how much a future harm is worth in today's money. And here is the point that survives the whole argument. The residual commons value of the barrel is not a discounted flow of future damage. It is the present absence of a thing that will not return. The barrel is already gone. The oil is already burned. The loss is not a stream of harms arriving in installments over the coming century that we can discount more or less heavily. It is a fact, now, in the present, that a piece of a finite and unrepeatable inheritance has been permanently removed from the total that everyone after us will ever have. You do not discount the absence of a thing the way you discount a future cash flow, because the absence is not in the future. It is here, now, the moment the barrel is burned, and no choice of discount rate makes the barrel come back. This is why the residual commons value survives even Stern's low rate, and would survive a rate lower still. It is not in the part of the calculation that discounting touches. The discount-rate war is a real argument about a real thing, the future flow of damage, but it is fought on a battlefield that does not contain the deepest cost, which is the permanent subtraction itself.

So the lineage is a row of near-misses, each of which saw a true part of the thing and built next to the empty plot rather than on it. Marx priced the surplus but bounded it by class. Ostrom governed the renewing commons but not the liquidated one. Daly warned about the throughput but never billed the single barrel. Hartwick saved the rent but not the barrel. Rawls, Sandel, and Parfit widened the circle of moral concern across time but left the accounting undone. Stern and Nordhaus and Weitzman priced the damage flow but never the permanent absence. None of this was failure. Each of them was answering a question that was genuinely urgent and genuinely theirs, and the residual commons value of a liquidated resource was, for each, just outside the frame. You can stand at the edge of a thing for a century, each generation seeing a different true sliver of it, and still leave the center unbuilt, not from carelessness but from the angle of approach. Put their work together and you have nearly all the materials. What is missing is the act of running the honest accounting on a single real thing, forward into the future and backward into the past, and refusing to stop at the part of the bill that landed conveniently on someone else.

Run it backward first, on something that already happened, because the backward run is the easiest to check.

In McDowell County, in the coalfields of southern West Virginia, coal came out of the ground for decades and was sold at the mine mouth for a few dollars a ton. Around 1960 the figure was on the order of $4 or $5 a ton. That was the price the present paid, and like the $100 barrel it may have been an accurate price for the transaction it described, a fair exchange between the company that dug the coal and the buyer who burned it. Now add back the severed costs, the residual commons value the price never touched, and run the honest accounting.

A note on the numbers before the arithmetic, because it governs every figure in this essay. Every number here is the low end of its range, and anything genuinely contested I leave out. The goal is not a ceiling but a floor, the smallest honest number the framework can defend. If the conservative number is already this large, the true number is only larger.

The largest severed cost by far is the carbon. Burning a ton of this particular coal, the high-carbon Pocahontas coal of that region, releases about 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide, somewhat more than the national average of about 2.33, because the coal is unusually rich in carbon. Each ton of that carbon dioxide does damage to the climate that someone, somewhere, across distance and across time, has to bear, and the United States Environmental Protection Agency in 2023 estimated that damage at roughly $190 per ton of carbon dioxide. Now do the multiplication the framework has been promising. Two point six one tons of carbon dioxide, times $190 of damage for each ton, is about $496. That figure alone, just the carbon, just the one severed cost that the mine-mouth price ignored entirely, is around a hundred times the few dollars the coal sold for. Add the rest of the residual commons value, the streams that were fouled, the air that sickened the people who lived nearby, the land that was taken apart, and the honest price of a ton of that coal comes to more than $500, on the order of $510, nearly all of it carbon. The coal sold for $4 or $5. The honest price, with the severed costs re-attached, is more than $500. The difference, more than a hundredfold, is the residual commons value, the part of the true cost that was cut loose and dropped on the climate, on the neighbors, on the future, on everyone who was not party to the sale and could not send the bill back. This is the framework run backward on a realized extraction, and the arithmetic is not exotic. It is multiplication. The only difficulty was ever the willingness to do it.

The framework runs in both directions, and it has a named instrument for each. Run it forward, before the extraction, and the instrument is a foreclosure bond. You are about to take something that forecloses an option for the future, that removes a thing the future would have wanted to keep. Before you take it, price the foreclosure, the residual commons value of what you are about to remove, and set that money aside in a fund held against the loss, so that the future inherits not the barrel, which cannot be saved, but at least the value of the barrel, preserved and growing rather than spent. Run it backward, after an extraction already realized, and the instrument is a restitution bond. The taking is done, the costs were severed and borne, and the question now is what is owed to those who bore them and what can be set right.

The backward run connects to a body of work building it in earnest, in a domain far from oil and coal. In their 2020 book *From Here to Equality*, the economist William Darity Jr. and the writer A. Kirsten Mullen make the economic case for reparations to the descendants of enslaved people in the United States, which in the terms of this essay is backward honest accounting on the largest scale: value extracted across centuries from 1619 forward, the costs severed and borne, the racial wealth gap that persists today as the compounded residue, a debt that Darity and Mullen, updating the reckoning as the gap has widened, put by 2023 at about $14 trillion. And the cost is not only money. A 2022 study in *JAMA Network Open*, with Darity among its authors, found that the gap in survival between Black and white Americans was largely explained by the gap in wealth, so that once wealth is accounted for, the racial difference in how long people live largely closes. The severed cost is paid, in the end, not only in dollars but in years of life. The backward run also keeps a discipline. Some of those severed costs were the costs of coercion done to a group the author does not belong to, and so determining those numbers is respectfully left to the people in that field carrying that work forward. The framework reaches such cases through their measurable legacy effects, the wealth gap and the longevity gap among them, and it offers the ledger. The numbers on it are theirs to enter.

Now run the forward instrument at the largest scale anyone has actually run it, because one country has, and the proof that the future can be priced in practice is not a theory but a sovereign fund holding more than $2 trillion.

Just before Christmas in 1969, after a long run of dry holes that had nearly convinced everyone there was nothing to find, an oil field was confirmed under the North Sea off the coast of Norway. The field was Ekofisk, and it was the beginning of an enormous flow of petroleum wealth into a small country. What Norway did with that wealth is the forward run of the honest accounting made real. The Norwegians understood, early and as a matter of national policy, that the oil was a non-renewing inheritance, a barrel that does not come back, and that to spend the proceeds as ordinary income would be to let the present generation consume an inheritance that belonged equally to every Norwegian who would ever be born. So they built a fund. The Government Petroleum Fund, established in 1990 and renamed the Government Pension Fund Global in 2006, takes the revenue from the oil and invests it, and in 2001 the country adopted a fiscal rule that allows the government to spend only the expected real return on the fund, on the order of 3 percent a year, while leaving the capital itself untouched and inviolate. The principal is not to be spent. Only the harvest of the principal, the return it earns, is to be spent, which means the fund can in principle fund the country forever, the way a renewing resource can, even though the oil that filled it does not renew at all.

The fund now holds well over $2 trillion, which is more than $390,000 for every citizen of the country. And the most important fact about it, the fact that makes it an existence proof rather than a lucky accident, is that it has held across the entire political spectrum. Government after government, of the left and of the right, under Stoltenberg and Bondevik and Solberg and Støre, has left the principal alone. No government has raided the capital. The discipline has outlasted changes of party and changes of policy and the ordinary temptation of any government to spend what is in front of it, because the rule was set up to make the inheritance visible and to make spending it down a thing a government would have to do openly and against the law it had agreed to. This is John Hartwick's rule operating at the scale of a nation, the rents from the non-renewing resource reinvested so that the future inherits the value even though it cannot inherit the barrel. And it is a reserve fund, money held back against the future, which connects it to a theme that runs to the very end of this essay. Norway looked at a barrel that does not come back and decided to convert it into something that does, a permanent fund, and then had the discipline to leave the fund alone. The future was priced, set aside, and kept.

Set against Norway is a place where the same physical act, pumping oil out of the ground and selling it, was run with no such architecture, and the contrast is the whole argument in two countries. In the Niger Delta of Nigeria, oil has been pumped since the 1950s, and hundreds of billions of dollars of revenue have flowed out of the region. But the residual commons value, the severed costs, were borne by the people who lived there and did not sell the oil and could not bill anyone for what it cost them. The spills fouled the water and the land. The flaring of gas burned in the sky day and night. The fisheries and farmland that families had depended on were foreclosed, poisoned, made unproductive, and the people who lost them were not parties to the sale and received no part of the price. When Ken Saro-Wiwa led a movement protesting what the extraction was doing to the Ogoni people and their land, he and eight others were executed in 1995, an act that led to Nigeria's suspension from the Commonwealth. Years later, a United Nations assessment of the environmental damage in Ogoniland estimated that cleaning it up would take an initial $1 billion and 25 to 30 years of work, and that was for one part of the Delta. The wealth left. The costs stayed. Nothing was set aside.

I want to be careful about what this contrast is and is not. It is not a villain story, and it is not an argument against extraction. Norway extracted oil and so did Nigeria; the difference between them is not that one took and the other refrained. The difference is architecture. Norway built an instrument that priced the future and set the value aside, and held the discipline to leave it alone. In the Niger Delta no such instrument was built, and the costs that were severed simply landed where they fell, on the people least able to refuse them, with nothing held in reserve to set anything right. The same physical act, the same barrels coming out of the ground, ran through two completely different architectures and produced two completely different distributions of cost and benefit across distance and time. The lesson is not that oil is wrong. The lesson is that when the cost of an extraction outlives the sale, someone carries it, and the only question that matters is whether anyone decided in advance who that would be and set anything aside to meet it. This is architecture, not a political program. It does not tell a country whether to drill. It tells a country that if it drills, the bill is coming due somewhere, and honest accounting is simply the decision to know where, and to whom, and to hold something in reserve.

This is the heart of why the framework travels from one world to the next. It is not a fixed list of prices. It is a way of holding variables whose values depend entirely on where you are. The cost of a commons is not a property of the thing. It is a property of the quantity of the thing in its setting. Air is the clearest case. On the surface of the Earth, air is so abundant that its scarcity is effectively zero, so its honest cost is effectively zero, and the framework agrees: charge nothing, because nothing here is scarce. Put that same air behind the wall of a sealed habitat and its scarcity is no longer zero. It is now among the scarcest things in the system, and the framework, run with exactly the same logic, returns a cost that is nonzero. Nothing about the method changed. Only the setting changed, and with it the value of the variables. That is why the same ledger that prices a ton of coal in West Virginia can price a single breath on another world. It is not a set of Earth prices carried outward. It is a way of reading the real scarcity of each thing in each place and returning the honest cost of it there. Change the world and you do not need a new framework. You only get new numbers.

Now go back through the airlock, because the strongest way to see the framework whole is to watch it run not backward on history and not forward in a fund but in real time, every day, as the ordinary working economy of a place that cannot afford the mask at all. I am not predicting that we will live this way. The sealed world is a lens, a way of seeing the framework in operation by removing the planet that hides it. But it is a lens ground from real glass, because we have already built the small sealed worlds and watched the bills arrive.

Imagine, then, not visiting a sealed habitat but living in one, on the Moon or on Mars or in a station between worlds, as your home, with a job and neighbors and an ordinary daily life. In that place the opening question is not a piece of strange paperwork at a single airlock. It is the basic structure of the economy, because every commons that the Earth gives away for free is, inside the seal, a thing that had to be made and therefore a thing that is metered and billed. Your air is produced by machines that split it out of water using power that something had to generate, and so your breathing is metered, the way electricity is metered in a house, and it appears on a bill. The water you drink and wash with is recycled from every drop the colony can recover, cleaned and returned and used again, and because the recycling costs energy and equipment and maintenance, the water is metered too, by the litre. The carbon dioxide you breathe out has to be pulled back out of the shared air before it builds up to a level that would kill everyone, and pulling it out costs energy, so the removal of your own exhaled breath is a service the colony provides and meters and charges for. Living inside the seal means living inside the bill. There is no planet underneath to absorb any of it, so all of it is visible, all of it is priced, and the priceless commons of Earth are, inside the wall, simply the line items of ordinary life.

Notice what kind of work becomes valuable inside the seal, because it overturns the order we are used to. On Earth the labor that keeps the commons going is the labor we pay least and notice least, the maintenance, the cleaning of water, the tending of the systems that turn waste back into use. We can ignore that work because the planet does most of it for free, invisibly, and the part humans do we treat as low and pay as low, the same way a nurse's labor and the slow building of topsoil are underpriced because abundance lets us look past them. Inside the seal there is no looking past it. The people who maintain the air plant and the water recycling and the carbon scrubbers are doing the work that keeps everyone alive from one hour to the next, and an economy that meters every breath cannot pretend that the keeping of the breathable world is menial. Commons-keeping becomes the most valuable work there is, because it is the work that the seal will not let anyone take for granted. The colony revalues, out of sheer necessity, exactly the labor that Earth's abundance trains us to underpay.

Consider what such an economy would actually feel like to live inside, because the strangeness of it is instructive, and because the obvious objection to the whole picture lives here. A world that meters air could become a place where the poor cannot afford to breathe, a debtor's prison with an atmosphere, and it is right to name that fear plainly rather than let it sit unspoken. On Earth a poor person and a rich person breathe the same free air, and whatever else divides them, the act of drawing breath is not one of the things money touches. Inside the seal that is no longer automatically true, because the air now has a cost of production, and a cost of production has to be paid by someone, and the question of who pays for whose breathing becomes a real question the colony cannot avoid. But here the framework's discipline matters most, and it is important to be exact about what honest accounting does and does not do. Honest accounting lays the cost bare. It does not, by itself, decide who pays it. Those are two different acts, and collapsing them is the root of the dystopia fear. Knowing that a breath costs something to produce tells you nothing about whether breathing should be a right paid for collectively or a metered purchase paid for by each person alone. That second question is moral and political, and it stays moral and political; the ledger does not answer it and does not pretend to. What the ledger does is make the choice visible, which is the one thing that makes a just choice possible. A colony might give every resident a baseline ration of air and water and carbon removal as a right of citizenship, paid for collectively, the way some societies treat basic healthcare, so that no one's breathing depends on their ability to pay. It might meter everything and let people buy what they use. It might land anywhere between. But it cannot make that choice well while the cost is hidden, and it cannot make it at all while pretending there is no cost. Seeing the number honestly is not the dystopia. It is the precondition for avoiding one. On Earth the abundance of air lets us never decide how breathing should be paid for, because the planet pays invisibly and the question never arises. Inside the seal the question arises every day, and the colony's answer to it is, quite literally, its social contract, written in the units of air and water and energy that the planet used to provide for free. The mask of abundance is not merely an accounting error inside the seal. It is the absence of the one luxury that let Earth's societies leave their deepest distributional questions permanently unasked.

McDowell County and Norway had to choose to run the framework, because on an open world the planet keeps paying the hidden costs whether anyone accounts for them or not. Inside the seal there is no such mercy. The colony cannot let the mask stay on, because there is no abundance to hide behind and no planet to send the bill to, so it runs the honest accounting every day, by the litre and by the breath, not as a moral achievement but as the only way to stay alive, because the alternative is that the air runs out. First we used the seal to see the value, in the diver's helium and the dome's dead bees and the station's costly water. Now, living inside it, we pay the value, every day, on a meter we can read, which is exactly what the airlock asked of you at the very start.

And then the lens shows something that at first seems to contradict the whole argument, until you see that it confirms it. Go further out, away from any colony and any community and any living thing, to a single miner working an asteroid or a passing comet, alone in a place with no ecosystem to harm and no neighbors to bear any cost. Out there, when you break iron out of an asteroid, there is no forest you are foreclosing, no fishery you are poisoning, no community downstream, no future generation that wanted that particular rock kept whole. The cost severance is near zero, because there is almost no one for a cost to be severed onto. And so the honest accounting, run carefully, says something that surprises people who expected a framework about hidden costs to be a framework against taking. It says extract freely. Take the iron. If a comet is only briefly within reach before it swings away for a thousand years, extract aggressively while you can, because the resource is genuinely there for the taking and almost no one is harmed by your taking it. Honest accounting is not the same as restraint. Where the severed costs are real, it counts them and makes you carry them. Where they are genuinely near zero, it says take, and take freely, and feel no guilt about it, because the guilt was always supposed to attach to the severed cost and here there almost is none.

And yet the same lens, on the same rock, says one more thing, and the two things together are the whole framework in miniature. On the asteroid itself, alone, far from any help, you must hold reserves. You must keep back enough fuel and enough air and enough of whatever your life-support needs to be certain you can get off the rock and get home, or get on to the next world. The same honest accounting that told you to extract the iron freely tells you, in the same breath, to reserve the air absolutely, because the iron belongs to no one but the air belongs to your own future self, the one who has to survive the extraction. Take freely here; reserve there. That is not a contradiction. It is the framework seeing clearly in both directions at once. Honest accounting was never an argument against extraction. It is the practice of knowing the real cost of what you take, charging it to whoever truly bears it, and keeping enough back to survive the future you are extracting into. On the asteroid the framework says take all the iron you want and do not touch the last tank of air, and a person who understands why those two instructions belong together understands the whole of it.

Step back from the seal and the rock, and a larger point comes into view, because someone will want to know which economic system this framework belongs to, whether it is an argument for markets or against them, for planning or for property, and the honest answer is that it is none of those things and underneath all of them. The framework is not a rival economic system. It is the accounting that any economic system needs in order to know what it is actually doing. Whatever economy you choose to run, the ledger underneath it has to be honest, or the economy is making its choices without being able to see what those choices cost.
There is one more horizon, the longest one there is, and on it the theme of reserves stops being a matter of the next generation or the next century and becomes a matter of the survival of everything we are. The argument so far has been about holding something back for the people who come after us, which usually means the next few generations, the people who will inherit the climate and the resources we leave. But finite resources and a finite world demand reserves on a scale much longer than that.

The Sun is slowly getting brighter. As it ages it burns hotter, and its rising output will, on its own, make the Earth uninhabitable on the order of 500 million to 1 billion years from now, as the oceans are driven off and the surface becomes too hot for life as we know it. That is long before the more famous ending, when the Sun, around 7.5 billion years from now, swells into a red giant large enough to engulf the inner planets, the Earth quite possibly among them. The first of these is the one that matters for any sense of deep reserves, because it is the one that ends the habitability of our only world while the world is still here. On a horizon like that, the off-world habitat is not a fantasy or an escape. It is the longest-horizon reserve there is, the preservation of the option for life and for everything we have made and learned to continue somewhere when this world can no longer hold it. The same impulse that built the granary against the lean year and the fund against the spent barrel, scaled up to the longest time there is, says keep something back, keep the option open, hold a reserve against the failure of the only home we have. The asteroid miner who holds the last tank of air against the trip home is doing, in miniature, what a species holding open the possibility of life beyond one world would be doing across deep time. Reserves all the way down, and all the way out, against scarcities near and unimaginably far.

Strip away the worlds and the centuries and the framework is smaller than it sounds. It is honest accounting, the kind anyone can keep. A person can run it over a single decision, asking what the choice truly costs and who will pay. A community can run it over the land and water it holds in common. A corporation can run it over what it takes and what it leaves behind. A government can run it over an entire national inheritance, as Norway has. And a sealed colony on another world would have to run it every day, by the litre and the breath, simply to stay alive. Anyone who looks at it honestly, at any scale, on this world or any other, recognizes the same thing: not a politics and not a system, just an honest set of books.

Which brings you back to the air. Here, now, it is still free. No meter, no bill, no question at any door. That freedom is real and worth keeping, but it was never a law of nature. It is a condition we happen to enjoy, and conditions change. Whether the air stays free, whether the abundance holds long enough to go on hiding how much it is worth, is not written anywhere. It is still ours to decide. The mask comes off the moment we are willing to take it off, and the number underneath was always there.
