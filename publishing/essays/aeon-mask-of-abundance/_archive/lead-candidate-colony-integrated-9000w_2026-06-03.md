# The Mask of Abundance

Take a breath. Hold it a moment, then let it go. Whatever else is true of your life right now, that exchange just happened for nothing. No meter spun. No subscription lapsed. No one will send you a bill for the litre or so of air you just borrowed from the room and gave back warmer and spent. You did it without deciding to, the way you have done it every few seconds since before you were born, and you will do it a few hundred million more times before you stop. It is, by any sane reckoning, the single most valuable transaction in your life, repeated more often than any other, and its price is zero.

We are trained to read that zero as a verdict. A thing that costs nothing, we assume, is a thing worth nothing, or close to it. Water from a tap, sunlight on a field, the wind that turns a sail, the slow work the oceans do hauling carbon out of the sky, the strange grace of a climate that has stayed inside the narrow band where wheat will grow and rivers will run. These come to us free or nearly so, and because they come free we file them somewhere below the things we pay for. A bottle of branded water sits higher in our accounting than the river it was drawn from. A tank of fuel registers as a cost; the air it burns and the sky it fouls register as nothing at all.

This is the first lie I want to take apart, because every other honest accounting depends on seeing through it. Air costs almost nothing not because it has little value but because it has nothing but value. It is so completely distributed, so utterly without scarcity at the point where any of us touches it, that no market can find an edge to charge at. A price is not a measure of worth. A price is a measure of scarcity at the margin, the going rate for the next unit when there is a next unit somebody can withhold. Where there is no margin, where the next unit is simply everywhere, the price falls to zero and stays there, and the worth of the thing goes dark to the only instrument we have taught ourselves to trust.

So let me say plainly, here at the start, what this essay is trying to do, because I will be saying it again at the end and I want you to be able to hold me to it. I want to build a framework for honest accounting. Not a new economic system, not a manifesto, not a politics. An accounting. A way of writing down the full price of things that works on this world and, if we ever get there, on any other. The whole of the trick is this: to find the costs that abundance, or distance, or time has hidden from the ledger, and to put them back on it where they belong. The mask of abundance is the place we start, because the breath is the clearest case of a thing whose value is total and whose price is zero. But the mask is only the way in. What it hides is a whole architecture of costs we have severed from ourselves and handed, unbilled, to people who are not in the room. Some of them are across an ocean. Some of them are not yet born. The framework is simply the discipline of finding those costs and naming them.

## The cheapest way to learn the price of air is to run out of it

Here is the thing about a value that hides behind abundance: you can make it appear instantly, and brutally, by taking the abundance away. Seal the system. Cut it off from the great free commons of Earth, the atmosphere and the water cycle and the soil and the patient chemistry of a living planet, and watch what happens to the price of a breath.

I want to be careful about the register I am writing in now, because for the rest of this essay I am going to lean on imagined cases, and I do not want you to mistake a lens for a prediction. When I describe a sealed habitat, or a colony with a metered airlock, or a miner working an asteroid, I am not forecasting any of it. I am building instruments for seeing. Hold the picture up to the light, look through it at the world you already live in, and then set it down. That is all these are for.

But before the imagined cases, the real ones, because the bill for sealing a system already arrives, today, in places where people have actually done it.

Go down far enough into the sea and ordinary air turns against you. Under pressure the nitrogen that makes up most of what you breathe seeps into your nerves and dulls them, a drunkenness that thickens as you descend, and the oxygen that keeps you alive crosses, past a certain depth, into something that poisons. So saturation divers, the people who live for weeks at the pressure of the deep to weld and inspect and repair the hidden infrastructure of the offshore world, do not breathe air. They breathe a blend of helium and oxygen, mixed to keep them sane and alive at depth. And helium is not free. It runs somewhere around two dollars a cubic foot, give or take, which means the gas alone can cost a working diver on the order of a hundred dollars an hour just to keep breathing. The ships that support this work run reclaim rigs, scrubbers and compressors whose entire job is to catch the exhaled helium and clean it and send it back down, because at those prices you do not let a breath escape into the sea. You recover it. You bill it. The most ordinary act there is, the one you and I perform for nothing, has a meter on it down there, and the meter runs all day.

Now seal a larger system, and seal it longer. In the Arizona desert at the start of the 1990s, a project called Biosphere 2 put eight people inside roughly three acres of closed glass, an entire miniature world of rainforest and savanna and ocean and farm, sealed against the outside, and tried to run it for two years on nothing but sunlight and its own internal cycles. It cost something like a hundred and fifty million dollars to build. And the priceless commons began, almost at once, to misbehave. The oxygen inside the glass started falling. Over sixteen months it slid from the ordinary twenty-one percent down toward fourteen, a level you would find near the top of a very high mountain, as the rich soils they had brought in to grow food breathed harder than anyone had modelled and the fresh concrete of the structure quietly drank carbon dioxide out of the air and locked it away. In the end the managers had to pump oxygen in from outside to keep the crew functioning. Nineteen of the twenty-five small vertebrate species inside died. Every pollinator died, which meant the crops had to be pollinated by hand. The free services of a living planet, the gases balanced and the flowers visited and the air kept breathable, turned out, once sealed inside three acres of glass, to be the most expensive and least reliable things in the whole experiment.

And then the system humans have actually sealed and kept running, day after day, for a quarter of a century: the International Space Station, a few hundred kilometres over your head right now. Up there nothing is free, because nothing arrives for free. Lifting water to orbit costs on the order of twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram, which makes a mere six-month supply of water for the crew a sum in the neighbourhood of a hundred and thirty-five million dollars, if you were foolish enough to ship it all up rather than recycle. So they recycle. The life-support systems aboard the station reclaim something like ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine back into drinking water, and they make their oxygen by splitting that water with electricity. The astronauts drink yesterday's breath and yesterday's body, again and again, because the alternative is a budget no nation will pay. The free commons of Earth, sealed out, becomes a line item measured in the hundreds of millions.

These three cases are not metaphors. They are receipts. They are what it actually costs to do, inside a sealed boundary, the thing the open Earth does for everyone for nothing. And they tell you that the zero on the price tag of air was never a statement about air. It was a statement about abundance. Take the abundance away and the value that was always there comes roaring into view, denominated, suddenly, in dollars per hour and dollars per kilogram and the deaths of nineteen small animals behind glass.

## The name for the thing the mask hides

If a value that total can hide behind a zero, then so can a cost. This is the mechanism at the centre of everything that follows, and it deserves a plain name. I call it cost severance.

Cost severance is what happens when someone creates a cost, someone else absorbs it, and there is no channel through which the second party can bill it back to the first. A factory pours its waste into a river. The factory captures the value of cheap disposal. The town downstream absorbs the cost of poisoned water, and absorbs it with no invoice to send, no counter, no till at which to ring up what was taken from them. The cost has been severed from the act that caused it, cleanly, the way you might sever a rope, and it floats away downstream to land on people who never agreed to carry it. The transaction that happened at the factory looks, on the factory's books, like a clean profit. The cost did not vanish. It was simply moved to a ledger no one is keeping.

This is the dark twin of the breath. With the breath, a value so total it never found a price. With the river, a cost so easily exported it never found a payer. Both are invisible to the market for the same reason: the market only sees what passes across a price, and these never do. Economists have a tidy word for the river case, externality, and the word is true as far as it goes, but it is a word that lets you keep the cost at arm's length, an external thing, a footnote to the real economy. I want a word that puts it back in the centre. The cost was not external to anything. It was severed, deliberately or carelessly, from the party who caused it, and lodged in the body of someone who did not.

And there is a residue, even after the most honest sale you can imagine. Picture a transaction with no fraud in it at all. A fair price, a willing buyer, a willing seller, everything above board. Even then, very often, there is a worth left over that the price did not capture and could not capture, because it is owed to people who were not at the table. The villagers downstream. The fishery that will not recover for thirty years. The child not yet conceived who will inherit a thinner soil and a hotter sky. Call it the residual commons value: the worth that remains unpriced after the sale is done, because the only parties a price can speak for are the ones present to haggle, and the commons has no seat at the table. It belongs to everyone, which in the grammar of markets means it belongs to no one, which means no one is there to defend it when the deal is struck.

It is worth pausing on why the second kind of party, the one who absorbs the severed cost, so rarely manages to bill it back, because the failure is structural and not merely a matter of bad luck or weak law. To send an invoice you need three things: you need to know that a cost has landed on you, you need to be able to trace it to the party who caused it, and you need a forum in which the claim can be heard and enforced. The genius of cost severance, if you can call it genius, is that it tends to defeat all three at once. The town downstream may take years to learn that its rising rates of illness trace to the water. Even once it knows, the harm may come from a hundred upstream sources at once, no single one of which can be cleanly blamed. And the people who will bear the worst of it, the ones a generation or two downstream in time, are not present to file any claim at all, because they do not yet exist, and a claimant who does not yet exist is the easiest creditor in the world to ignore. The market is not built to hear from them. It is built to hear from whoever is standing at the till with money in hand, and the future never stands at the till.

Honest pricing, most of the time, is the work of re-attaching a severed cost. You find the river the factory was using as a free sewer, you put a price on the harm, and you make the factory carry it, so that its books finally tell the truth about what its product costs the world. But the commons case, the breath case, is stranger and harder than that, and I want to mark the difference clearly because it is the hinge of the whole argument. The carbon-fouled sky is not a cost that was severed from a price it once had. It is a value so total, so abundant, that it never acquired a price in the first place. It was free the way the breath is free, everywhere and for everyone, until the moment scarcity arrived and revealed, all at once, what it had been worth the entire time. The honest ledger has to handle both: the costs that were cut loose from their causes, and the values that were never priced at all because abundance kept them hidden. The mask of abundance hides them both. The framework is the discipline of pulling the mask off and writing down what was always underneath.

## A hundred dollars buys the present a tank of gas

Let me put a number on a barrel of oil and then show you everything the number leaves out. Say a barrel sells for a hundred dollars. I am using that figure as an illustration, not a quotation; the precise price swings week to week and does not matter for the point. A hundred dollars, then. What does that price actually price?

It prices the present. It prices the cost of finding the oil, drilling for it, pumping it, refining it, shipping it, and a margin on top, all denominated in the conditions of right now, this quarter, this market. It is an extraordinarily precise instrument for answering one question: what will it take to get this barrel out of the ground and into your tank today, and what is the most someone will pay for it before they go to a competitor. At that question the price is a marvel. It coordinates the labour of millions of people who will never meet, across every ocean, with a fidelity no planner could match. I am not here to insult the price. The price is doing exactly what it was built to do.

But the thing being priced does not live only in the present. The barrel of oil is a deposit of sunlight that fell on the Earth a hundred million years ago, pressed and cooked under rock through a span of time that makes all of recorded history a rounding error. It took geology that long to make, and once you burn it, it is gone. Not depleted, not strained, not in need of a rest. Gone. The carbon it held goes up into the sky, where it will alter the chemistry of the atmosphere for centuries before the slow sinks of the ocean and the rock draw it back down. The barrel reaches, in both directions, into permanence. And the price reaches only to the end of the quarter.

This is where a distinction usually blurred turns out to be decisive, and I want to be exact about it. There are renewable commons and there are non-renewable ones, and they pose entirely different questions to an honest ledger. A forest, a fishery, an aquifer fed by rain, the fertility of a living soil: these renew themselves, on their own schedule, if you let them. The question they pose is a question of rate. Take less than the thing breeds, and you can take forever; the commons replenishes behind you and your grandchildren inherit it intact. Take more than it breeds, and you are eating the principal, and the only argument is about how fast you are doing it. Elinor Ostrom spent her life on exactly this question, how real communities, fishers and herders and irrigators, have governed renewable commons for centuries without either privatising them or letting them collapse, and she earned a Nobel for showing that the choice between the market and the state was a false one, that people can and do write their own durable rules for sharing a thing that renews.

But oil is not a renewable commons, and no rule about rates can save it, because there is no rate slow enough. The forest you cut at the right pace is still there next year. The barrel you burn at any pace at all is simply gone, and the next barrel comes from a finite store that is one barrel smaller for the rest of time. You are not eating the interest. There is no interest. You are liquidating the principal, and the only question a rate can answer is how long it takes you to finish. When you sell that barrel for a hundred dollars, the hundred dollars buys the present its tank of gas. It does not buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after, because nothing can. The future does not get a slower-burning version. It gets the absence.

## A lineage of people who saw part of it

I am not the first to feel the wrongness in that price, and I would be a fool to pretend otherwise. There is a long lineage of thinkers who walked up to this same severed cost and saw a real part of it, and named that part, and then, for reasons particular to each, stopped just short of the part I am after. It is worth tracing the near-misses quickly, because the shape of what they each saw, and missed, is the shape of the thing itself.

Karl Marx, in the first volume of Capital, found a cost severed from a price and called it surplus value. The worker produces more than the wage returns to him; the difference is captured by the owner of the means of production; the cost is borne by the body and the hours and the life of the labourer who has no way to bill it back. That is cost severance, exactly, and Marx saw it with a clarity that still burns. He understood, better than almost anyone before him, that a transaction can be perfectly voluntary on its face and still hide a theft inside it, that the wage can be freely agreed to and still fail to capture what was actually taken. That insight, that a fair-looking price can conceal a severed cost, is the seed of everything I am doing here. But the axis Marx saw it along was class. The party absorbing the severed cost, for him, was the working class of the present, the living men and women at the factory gate, and the remedy he reached for was a rebalancing of power between people who were all alive at the same time. He was not looking down the axis of time. The future generation, the one that inherits an exhausted seam or a poisoned sky, was not, for Marx, the unbilled party. He had found the mechanism and pointed it in one direction. I am pointing the same mechanism down a different axis, the axis along which the unbilled party has not been born yet.

Ostrom, as I said, governed the renewable commons, and governed it superbly, but the commons she governed renews. The liquidated commons, the barrel that does not come back, the deposit that is simply spent, sits outside the frame of her great achievement, which is about sustaining a thing that sustains itself if you are wise.

Herman Daly, who spent his life arguing that an economy is a subsystem of a finite biosphere and cannot grow forever inside it, came closer than almost anyone to the throughput problem, the sheer physical flow of matter and energy through the economic machine and out the other side as waste. Daly warned, and warned, and was largely ignored by a profession committed to growth as a law of nature. But a warning about throughput in general is not an invoice for one specific foreclosed thing. Daly told us the machine was too big for the planet. He did not hand us the bill for the particular barrel.

John Hartwick, in a short and brilliant paper in the late 1970s, came closest of the economists to the mechanism. He asked: if you are going to draw down a non-renewable resource, what must you do to keep future generations as well off as the present? And he answered with a rule that bears his name. Take the rents, the pure profit from the exhaustion of the resource, and reinvest them in reproducible capital, so that the productive capacity handed forward does not shrink even as the oil runs out. Hartwick saved the rent. It is a genuine insight and we will see it work, at sovereign scale, in a moment. But saving the rent is not the same as pricing the barrel. Hartwick's rule keeps the books of the seller balanced across generations; it does not put a number on the thing the buyer is destroying. It is a discipline for the proceeds, not a price for the loss.

Around the same problem, from a different direction, the moral philosophers were widening the circle of who counts. John Rawls, in A Theory of Justice, asked you to choose the rules of society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which life inside it would be yours, and later thinkers reached for that veil and widened it across time, asking you to choose not knowing which generation you would be born into. From behind that wider veil, the barrel sold cheap to the present and stolen from the future looks very different, because the future might be you. Michael Sandel, in What Money Can't Buy, pressed on the question of which goods are corrupted by being priced at all, which things lose their meaning the moment a market touches them, and a stable climate owed to people not yet born sits very near the centre of that worry. And Derek Parfit, in Reasons and Persons, did the hardest philosophical work of all, taking seriously our obligations to future people, including people whose very existence depends on the choices we make, and showing how badly our ordinary moral intuitions break down across deep time. The philosophers saw the unbilled party. They saw that the future is a who, not a what, and that we owe it something. What they did not do, because it was not their trade, was build the ledger.

And then there are the economists who did try to price the future of the climate, and who fell to arguing, bitterly and for years, about a single number: the discount rate. How much less should we weigh a harm that lands a century from now than the same harm landing today? Nicholas Stern, in his review for the British government, argued for a very low rate, on the order of a percent and a half, on the moral ground that a future person's suffering counts very nearly as much as a present person's, and concluded that we should spend something like one percent of global output now to head off the damage. William Nordhaus, who built the most influential economic model of the climate and won a Nobel for it, argued for a much higher rate, around four percent, closer to what markets actually pay, and concluded that we should do considerably less, considerably more slowly. Martin Weitzman cut across both of them with a different worry entirely, arguing that the real danger lies in the fat tail of the probability distribution, the small but not negligible chance of catastrophe so total that no ordinary cost-benefit arithmetic can hold it, because the loss is effectively infinite.

The discount rate sounds like a technicality, a knob in a model, but it is one of the most morally loaded numbers a human being can choose, because it is a precise statement of how much you are willing to weigh a stranger in the future against yourself today. Set it high and you are saying that a harm landing on your great-grandchildren counts for almost nothing now, that the future is cheap. Set it low and you are saying that a person not yet born is very nearly as real, as deserving of consideration, as the person reading this sentence. Stern's low rate was, at bottom, a moral commitment dressed as an economic parameter, the claim that we have no good reason to value a future person's suffering much less than our own merely because they arrive after us. Nordhaus's higher rate was, in its way, also a moral claim, though it presented itself as mere realism, the observation that this is roughly the rate at which markets actually trade present against future, and that a policy should reckon with the world as it is. Weitzman's intervention was to say that both of them were arguing about the wrong part of the curve, that the action is not in the expected case at all but in the tail, the low-probability, unbounded-loss outcomes that no discount rate can tame because no finite number times a small probability captures a catastrophe that has no ceiling.

You can lose years in that argument, and many good people have. But step back and notice what every party to it shares. Stern, Nordhaus, Weitzman, all of them are pricing the same thing: the damage flow from combustion. The droughts, the floods, the failed harvests, the drowned coasts, the future stream of harm that follows from putting the carbon up. They differ on how heavily to weigh that future stream, but they agree on what is being weighed. And that is not the thing I am after. The residual commons value of the barrel is not a discounted flow of future damages. It is the present absence of a thing that will not return. The discount rate is an instrument for shrinking the future. You apply it to a stream of harms stretched out over coming centuries, and a high rate squeezes that stream down toward nothing. A benefit of a hundred dollars a century from now is worth about twenty-five dollars today at Stern's gentle rate, and only about a dollar and a half at Nordhaus's harsher one. But you cannot discount an absence that is already here. The barrel is gone the moment it is burned. Its non-existence is not a future event to be discounted back to the present; it is a present fact, fully realised, the instant the match is struck. The residual commons value survives even Stern's low rate, because it is not waiting in the future to be diminished by distance. It is standing in the room.

## Reading the ledger backward: a county in West Virginia

So far I have run the framework forward, pricing a barrel still in the ground, a sky not yet fouled. But the ledger reads in both directions, and there is a kind of brutal clarity in running it backward, over an extraction already complete, where the costs are not projected but realised, lying on the ground where you can walk among them.

Go to McDowell County, in the southern coalfields of West Virginia, where the mountains gave up some of the richest seams of coal on the continent. For a great deal of the twentieth century the coal came out of the mine mouth there at prices that now seem impossible to believe, a few dollars a ton in the middle of the century, four or five dollars, the price of a sandwich for a thing that would burn for hours and warm a house and run a turbine and put carbon into the sky for a century. That was the price the market saw. It was, in its narrow way, an accurate price. It captured the cost of getting the coal out and the most the buyer would pay.

Now run the honest ledger over that same ton of coal, and add back the severed costs. A ton of coal, burned, puts something like two and a half tonnes of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. Put a price on that carbon at a serious estimate of its true social cost, the harm it does as it warms the world over its long residence in the sky, and the carbon term alone comes to something on the order of five hundred and ten dollars. Add the other severed costs, the streams run orange with acid drainage, the black lung carried in the chests of men who are no longer alive to be compensated, the valleys filled and the water spoiled, and the honest price of that ton of coal climbs to somewhere around five hundred and twenty-four dollars. A few dollars at the mine mouth. Five hundred and twenty-four when the books are made to tell the truth. The difference did not disappear. It was severed and exported, across space to the people downstream and downwind, and across time to all of us now living under the sky those tons helped to load. McDowell County sold its mountains for a few dollars a ton and remains one of the poorest counties in the United States, which is what cost severance looks like from the inside, when you are the party that absorbed the cost and the value went somewhere else.

## A bond run forward, a bond run backward

If the ledger runs in both directions, then there is an instrument for each direction, and naming them makes the symmetry concrete.

Run the ledger forward, over a barrel still in the ground or a seam not yet cut, and the instrument is what I will call a foreclosure bond. You price the future loss before you cause it. You take the residual commons value, the worth of the thing you are about to remove from the world forever, and you set that sum aside, locked away, held against the absence you are creating. You are foreclosing on a piece of the future, and an honest accounting requires that you fund the foreclosure rather than hand it, unpaid, to whoever comes after. The bond is not a fine and not a tax. It is the price of the thing, paid to the only account that can hold a debt owed to the future, which is a reserve set aside and not spent.

Run the ledger backward, over an extraction already realised, the coal already burned, the mountains already sold, and the instrument is what I will call a restitution bond. Here the loss is not a projection. It happened. The costs are lying on the ground, in the orange streams and the scarred lungs and the gap between what a place was worth and what it was paid. Pricing them is not forecasting; it is reckoning. And this backward direction of the framework does not run on my own thinking. It builds directly on the work of William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen, whose book From Here to Equality is the most rigorous account I know of reparations as a matter of accounting rather than apology. Darity and Mullen run the ledger backward over the wealth extracted from Black Americans across the long arc since 1619, the unpaid labour, the land withheld, the property destroyed, the doors closed, and they show that the racial wealth gap of the present is not a vague historical residue but a measurable sum, a debt with a number on it. They point, too, at the legacy effects that outlive the original extraction, the gap in longevity itself, the years of life that did not get lived, carried forward as a measurable cost into bodies still breathing. That is the restitution bond at its fullest: the severed cost, priced, attributed, and set against the account that owes it. Ta-Nehisi Coates, in his essay The Case for Reparations, built the moral architecture of the same argument; Darity and Mullen built the ledger underneath it.

I want to be careful here, and the care is itself part of the discipline, so I will state it plainly. There is a kind of cost I am deliberately not pricing in this framework, and it would be dishonest to leave the omission silent. When the cost borne by a group is the cost of coercion itself, the violence done, the freedom taken, the human being treated as property, the pricing of that cost is not mine to do. I do not belong to the group that bore it, and there is a presumption in a stranger walking up to that wound with a calculator that I will not enact. What the framework can do, and what Darity and Mullen do so carefully, is reach the coerced case through its legacy effects, the wealth that can be traced, the longevity that can be measured, the gap that can be costed. The direct pricing of the coercion vector, the worth of the stolen freedom as such, I leave to those who are continuing that work and to whom it rightly belongs. The ledger reaches what it can reach honestly and stops where honesty requires it to stop.

## Two architectures, one physical act

Here is the argument's quiet centre. The framework is not against extraction. It is not a villain story with oil companies in the role of the villain. It is a question of architecture, and the cleanest way to see that it is architecture and not morality theatre is to set two real countries side by side, each of which did the same physical thing, pumping oil out of the ground and selling it, and watch how completely the outcomes diverge depending on whether anyone kept an honest ledger and set anything aside.

Start with the existence proof, because it is easy to believe that honest accounting at this scale is a fantasy until you see a country actually do it. Just before Christmas in 1969, drillers confirmed a vast field of oil under the North Sea off the coast of Norway, the Ekofisk field, and with it the prospect of more wealth than a small northern fishing nation had ever imagined. Norway might have done what nearly everyone does with a windfall, which is to spend it, to let the money flood in and the currency rise and the rest of the economy wither, and to leave the future to fend for itself once the field ran dry. Instead, slowly and with a discipline that is genuinely strange to behold, Norway built a ledger. It established a fund in 1990, the Government Petroleum Fund, later renamed the Government Pension Fund Global, and into it went the proceeds of the oil. And in 2001 the country adopted a fiscal rule with a single iron principle at its heart: the government may spend only the expected real return on the fund, something on the order of three percent a year, and the capital itself, the principal, the converted body of the oil, is inviolate. You may eat the interest. You may not touch the corpus.

That is Hartwick's rule made flesh at the scale of a nation, the rents of an exhausting resource reinvested rather than consumed, plus something Hartwick did not quite name, a reserve, a corpus held against the day the oil is gone. The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every Norwegian alive, and the most remarkable thing about it is the boredom of its politics. Governments of the left and the right have come and gone, Stoltenberg and Bondevik and Solberg and Støre, the whole spectrum, and not one of them, of any colour, has raided the principal. The discipline outlasted the politicians who made it. Norway took a non-renewable commons, priced it honestly, and set the value aside for the people not yet at the table, including the ones not yet born. The barrel is still gone. But its worth was not.

Now the counter-case, and I offer it not to point a finger but to show what the same physical act looks like with no honest ledger underneath it. Oil has been pumped from the Niger Delta since the 1950s, and the revenue that has flowed from it runs into the hundreds of billions of dollars. But the costs, the spills that blacken the creeks, the gas flares that burn day and night and turn the air rancid, the fisheries foreclosed and the farmland poisoned, fell on people who did not sell the oil and could not bill anyone for what it took from them. The value went out; the cost stayed and was severed from the value as cleanly as anything in this essay. When Ken Saro-Wiwa and eight others organised and spoke against what the extraction was doing to the Ogoni people and their land, they were executed, in 1995, an act so stark that the Commonwealth suspended Nigeria over it. Years later, a United Nations assessment of the damage to Ogoniland alone estimated that simply beginning to clean it up would cost on the order of a billion dollars and take twenty-five to thirty years, an initial instalment on a debt that had been accumulating, unbilled, for half a century.

Same physical act. Same oil, the same molecules of ancient sunlight pulled from under the ground and sold. Two architectures. In one, a ledger was kept and a reserve was set aside and the value survived the loss of the thing. In the other, the value was extracted and the cost was severed and left lying on the land and in the lungs and in the foreclosed futures of people who never had a seat at the table. The question the two cases pose together is not whether to extract. It is the only question this framework finally cares about: when the cost outlives the sale, who carries it, and is anything set aside? Norway answered yes. The Delta was given no chance to answer at all. That is architecture, not politics, and it is the architecture an honest ledger is built to make visible.

## The framework in operation, on another world

Now I want to take the framework off this planet entirely, and run it forward, in real time, on a world that cannot afford the mask of abundance for a single hour. Let me say again, clearly, that I am not predicting any of this. I do not know whether human beings will ever live sealed inside other worlds, and I am not claiming they will. I am holding up a lens. Look through it at the breath you are taking right now, and you will see your own world differently. That is the only use I am putting it to.

### The colony with a meter on the air

Imagine, then, not measuring a sealed system from outside, the way Biosphere 2 was measured, but living inside one. A home. A town. An economy, with shops and wages and arguments about the budget, sealed inside a hull on a world where stepping outside without a suit would kill you in under a minute. Picture arriving there for the first time, your ship docked, your bags in your hand, and the question they ask you at the airlock before they let you in.

They ask you how you intend to pay for your air.

It is not a cruel question. It is simply an honest one, asked by a society that cannot pretend, as ours can, that air is free, because in that place air is manufactured, by machines, out of split water and scrubbed carbon, at a real and constant cost in energy and parts and labour. Inside the colony, every commons that Earth hands out for nothing has a meter on it. A breath is metered. A litre of recycled water is metered. The removal of the carbon dioxide you exhale, the scrubbing of your own breath back out of the shared air so that it does not slowly poison everyone, is metered and billed by usage, the way we bill electricity, because someone built the scrubber and someone runs it and someone has to pay for the power it draws. The free commons of Earth becomes, inside that hull, the visible, priced, daily economy of survival.

And here is the thing I want you to see. That metered airlock is not a dystopia and it is not a different framework. It is exactly the same ledger I have been describing all along, the McDowell ledger, the Norway ledger, run forward and made literal, on a world too sealed to let abundance hide a single value. On Earth we argue for years about whether the carbon you exhale into the sky has a cost, because the sky is so vast that the cost hides in it. In the colony there is no vastness to hide in. The hull is the boundary, the air inside it is finite and made, and so the cost of your breath is not a philosophical claim to be debated by economists with rival discount rates. It is a line on your bill. The colony does honestly, by physical necessity, what Earth lets us avoid doing by the grace of abundance. It writes down the real price of the commons, because it has no other choice.

Notice what has happened across the arc of this essay. At the start we sealed a system in order to see the value of the breath, to make the hidden worth flash into view by taking the abundance away. The diver, the glass dome, the station: instruments for seeing. Now we have gone one step further. We have moved into the sealed system and made it our home, and what was an instrument for seeing has become the ordinary economy of a place where people live and work and raise children. First we used the seal to see the value. Now we live inside it and pay it. The mask is simply gone, because on that world it was never available to wear.

### The miner who takes everything and reserves everything

But run the same ledger somewhere else off the Earth and watch it produce, honestly, the opposite instruction, because honest accounting is not a single command to take less. It is the discipline of knowing the real cost, whatever the real cost turns out to be.

Imagine a miner working an asteroid, far out, where there is no community to bear a severed cost because there is no community at all, no ecosystem to foul because there is no ecosystem, no downstream village and no fishery and no one yet unborn whose inheritance you are spending, because the rock is dead and has been dead since the solar system was young. Run the framework here and the residual commons value falls to nearly nothing. There is no commons to speak of. The iron in that asteroid is worth more to a living human civilisation, turned into ships and stations and tools, than it is worth as one more lump of metal tumbling forever through the dark. So the ledger says, plainly: take it. Take the iron, all of it. If a comet swings briefly into reach, carrying water and volatiles that will be gone again for a thousand years once its orbit carries it back out, then extract aggressively, take everything you can while the window is open, because the cost of extraction here, the cost severed onto others, is as close to zero as the universe allows. The same framework that priced a McDowell ton at five hundred and twenty-four dollars prices this iron at almost nothing above the cost of getting it, and so it says: go.

And yet. On the rock itself, the very same ledger turns stern again, in a different register. Because while you owe nothing to the dead asteroid, you owe everything to your own future, and your own future out there is measured in the fuel and the air and the water and the spare parts you have brought with you and not yet spent. So the ledger that says take freely says, in the same breath, reserve absolutely. Hold back enough fuel to be certain you can leave. Hold back enough air and water and life-support that you are sure, not hopeful, sure, that you can get off this rock and home, or on to the next world. Mine the asteroid to nothing if you like, but never, ever spend the reserve that gets you out, because the one cost out there that can never be severed onto anyone else, the one cost that lands entirely and only on you, is the cost of not having kept enough back.

That is the whole framework in a single figure. Take freely where the cost is truly near zero. Hold reserves against the future you are extracting into, always, everywhere, without exception. Honest accounting is not anti-extraction and it never was. It is knowing the real cost of the thing, which is sometimes high and sometimes nearly nothing, and keeping enough back to survive the future you are reaching into. The asteroid miner who strips the rock bare and keeps a full tank is running exactly the same ledger as Norway, which pumped its oil and kept its corpus. The miner who strips the rock and spends the reserve to grab a little more is running the same failed ledger as the Delta, where everything was extracted and nothing was set aside, except that out there the bill comes due not in thirty years and not to someone downstream, but in a few hours, to you, in the silence after the air runs out.

## Whatever economy you choose, the ledger has to be honest

I have been careful, all along, to insist that this is not a rival economic system, and now I can say why with the full weight of the cases behind me. The framework is not capitalism and not socialism and not anything that competes with them on the same shelf. It is the accounting that any of them needs in order not to lie to itself. It sits underneath the economy you choose, whatever you choose, the way double-entry bookkeeping sits underneath every business regardless of what the business sells.

Look at what the serious attempts of our own moment are reaching for and you will see the same instinct everywhere. Kate Raworth, in Doughnut Economics, draws an economy bounded on the outside by ecological ceilings we must not breach and on the inside by social foundations we must not fall below, and the whole of that picture is an attempt to make a civilisation see its real costs, the ones our standard accounting leaves off the page. Ostrom's commons governance, which I praised earlier for the renewable case, is at bottom the same instinct, communities writing rules so that the true cost of overuse becomes visible and bindable before the commons collapses. And the instinct is far older than any of our names for it. Long before economics was a discipline, civilisations built granaries and held grain back in the fat years against the lean ones to come. Ancient China ran what was called an ever-normal granary, a public store meant to steady the price of grain and feed the people when the harvests failed. Ancient Egypt kept great state granaries against the years the river would not rise. I will not pretend to particulars I cannot verify, and the details of those systems are not the point. The point is that the impulse is among the oldest civilised acts there is: to know your real costs and hold reserves against scarcity. Capitalist, planned, ancient, off-world, it does not matter. The economy that does not keep an honest ledger and does not hold reserves is flying blind, and a thing flying blind eventually hits something.

## Reserves on the longest horizon there is

And if reserves are the discipline, then there is a horizon on which the discipline becomes almost vertiginous, and it is worth standing at its edge for a moment because it reframes the off-world habitat from a fantasy into something more sober, an option held open across deep time.

Our Sun is slowly brightening. It is not a stable lamp; it burns hotter as it ages, and the careful work of stellar physicists, Sackmann and her colleagues among them, puts the moment when that rising luminosity will have made the Earth uninhabitable, the oceans driven off, the surface sterilised, somewhere on the order of five hundred million to a billion years from now. That is long before the far more famous ending, the swelling of the Sun into a red giant that Schröder and Smith estimate will reach out and consume the inner planets around seven and a half billion years from now. The death of the surface comes first, and it comes, in cosmic terms, not unimaginably far off. Life on Earth is older than a billion years. We are, quite possibly, past the midpoint of the time this planet has to offer.

I raise this not to frighten anyone, and certainly not to claim we should be packing for Mars. The horizon is so vast that nothing we do this century turns on it directly. I raise it because it completes the logic of reserves at the largest scale the logic can run. Finite resources demand reserves across the time we can foresee using them, and the time a civilisation might foresee using anything at all is bounded, at the very outside, by the habitability of its world. On that horizon the off-world habitat stops being a boy's adventure and becomes what it always was inside this framework, a reserve, an option held open against a scarcity so distant it is hard to feel but so total that an honest ledger cannot quite leave it off the page. The granary against the lean year, scaled up to the granary against the dying of the light. It is the same move. It was always the same move.

## The number was always there

So let me come back, at the end, to the breath, and to the plain statement I promised to repeat.

This essay has tried to build a framework for honest accounting, the kind an individual could run over a household, a community could run over a watershed, a government could run over a coalfield or an oil field, and a colony could run, by sheer necessity, over the air itself. Honest accounting that works on this world and on any other, because its single discipline does not depend on where you stand. Find the cost that abundance, or distance, or time has hidden from the ledger. Put it back on. Price the thing that has no price because it has nothing but value. Reserve against the future you are reaching into. That is the whole of what I have claimed to do, and I want it held to that and nothing grander. Not a new economy. An honest set of books for whatever economy you keep.

The mask of abundance was never lying about the worth of the things behind it. It was only telling the truth about scarcity and letting us mistake that truth for a verdict on value. The breath costs nothing because it is everywhere, and we read the nothing as a measure of the breath, when all along it was a measure of the abundance. Take the abundance away, in a diving bell, behind glass, in orbit, inside a hull on a dead world, and the number comes roaring back, and you realise it was there the whole time, written in a ledger no one was keeping. The framework does not invent the number. It only keeps the ledger that was always owed.

There is one more thing the breath teaches, and I will let it stand without quite naming it. The reason the air is free is that it is shared so completely that no edge exists at which to charge. It belongs to everyone, which is the same as saying that everyone, including the ones across the ocean and the ones not yet born, holds a quiet claim on how it is kept. The costs we sever do not vanish; they travel, across distance and across time, and land on people who never sat at our table and cannot send us a bill. To keep an honest ledger is, in the end, to take seriously what is owed to them, the ones downstream and downwind and downstream in time, who are bound up in the consequences of what we extract whether they consented or not. That tie is real even when no market can see it. It is, perhaps, the realest thing on the ledger.

And here is the part that is not on any ledger, the part we still get to decide. The freedom of the breath, that astonishing gift of taking what you need without asking and without paying, is not guaranteed to last. It lasts only as long as the abundance does, and the abundance lasts only as long as we keep the books honestly enough to protect it. The colony pays for its air because it has no choice. We do not pay for ours because, so far, we do not have to. Whether that stays true is not written in the stars or in the price of anything. It is a thing we still get to decide, and we will decide it, one way or another, by whether we ever bother to write down the price that was there all along. Take a breath. It is free. Keeping it that way is the only bill that ever really comes due.
