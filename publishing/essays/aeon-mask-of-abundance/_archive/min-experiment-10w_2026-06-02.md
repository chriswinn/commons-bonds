# The Mask of Abundance

Air has no price. You took a breath while reading that sentence and another while reading this one, and the market posted no charge for either. The same is true of the water cycling through the clouds, of the slow work by which the planet carries away the carbon dioxide you exhale, of the climate that has stayed, for the whole of human history, roughly within the band a body can live in. These are the most valuable things you will ever use. They are also, by every meter the market keeps, worth nothing at all.

We are tempted to read that zero as a verdict. If something is free, surely it is cheap, surely it is the kind of thing a civilization can spend without counting. The verdict runs the wrong way. The price of air is zero not because air is worthless but because air is everywhere, and a thing that is everywhere never has to argue for itself in a market. Abundance does not announce value. It hides it. The price tag is a measure of scarcity, and where there is no scarcity there is no tag, and where there is no tag we mistake the silence for cheapness. Call this the mask of abundance: the suspicion that the very fact of plenty is what keeps us from seeing the worth of the things we most depend on.

The way to test a mask is to take it off. And the way to take this one off is to imagine a place where abundance has been withdrawn. So imagine, as a thought-experiment and nothing more, a sealed habitat somewhere off the Earth, a closed life-support system with a wall between its inside and the vacuum beyond. Imagine that inside it a person pays, by usage, for the things the Earth hands out for free. A metered breath. A litre of water that has been used and cleaned and used again. The carrying-away of the carbon dioxide that would otherwise gather and thicken until it stopped a heart. This is not a forecast. No one is claiming people will live this way, or should. It is a lens, a moral X-ray, a way of holding the Earth-commons up to a light that shows what they would cost if anyone ever sent the bill. On Earth the bill never comes, because the supply is effectively infinite and the value, though total, goes unnamed. The habitat is simply the version of the world in which the bill arrives.

The strange thing is that the habitat is not entirely imaginary. For some people the bill already comes.

Go down far enough into the sea and the air you would breathe on the surface becomes a poison, so commercial and saturation divers breathe heliox, a blend of helium and oxygen, and they buy it by the hour. Helium runs something on the order of $1.70 to $2.75 per cubic foot. Technical divers reckon the helium alone can cost them eighty to two hundred dollars an hour. The figure is high enough that ships running saturation systems install reclaim rigs whose whole purpose is to recapture the gas after it has been breathed, because a breath, once you have to pay for it, is far too valuable to let drift away. There, at the bottom of the working ocean, the mask is already off. A breath has a price, and the price is steep, and everyone who depends on it knows the number.

Or consider a smaller experiment that an earlier generation actually ran. Between 1991 and 1993 a crew sealed themselves inside Biosphere 2, a glass world of just over three acres in the Arizona desert, built at a cost of around $150 million to see whether human beings could close the loop and live inside an ecosystem of their own making. The loop did not close. Oxygen, which sits at about 21 per cent of the air you are breathing now, fell inside the dome to 14 per cent over sixteen months, drawn down by the respiration of soil microbes and by carbon dioxide quietly binding into the building's own concrete. The crew began to suffer, and in the end the managers did the only thing they could, which was to pump oxygen in from outside. Nineteen of the twenty-five vertebrate species in the enclosure went extinct. Every pollinator died, and the crops had to be pollinated by hand. What the experiment proved, at considerable expense, was how hard and how costly it is to reproduce even a fraction of the services the Earth performs for nothing, continuously, without thanks or invoice, on the far side of the glass.

Lift the question off the planet entirely and the numbers only sharpen. Aboard the International Space Station, water is the most expensive substance in the building, because every kilogram of it has to be hauled up out of Earth's gravity at a cost of roughly $21,000. A six-month supply, at something like thirty litres a day, runs on the order of $135 million. So the station does not waste it. Its life-support system recycles around 90 per cent of the crew's sweat and urine back into drinking water, and it makes the oxygen the crew breathes by running an electric current through that hard-won water to split it. In orbit, air and water are not background conditions. They are the budget. They are the thing the engineers think about most.

The diver, the Biosphere crew, the astronaut. None of them is living in a different universe from the rest of us. They are living in the same universe with the mask removed, in the small sealed pockets where abundance has been switched off and the true price of the commons has come due. And the price, it turns out, is enormous. The cheapest thing on Earth is the costliest thing in space, and the only difference between the two is a wall and a supply.

This is what makes the mask dangerous, not merely curious. Honest pricing, in the ordinary case, means adding back a cost the market quietly severed and left for someone else to carry, the soot in a downwind lung, the warming in a distant decade. Here the work is stranger and harder, because there is no severed cost to recover. There is only a value so complete that it never had to acquire a price in the first place. Abundance did the hiding, and it hid the worth of exactly those things, the breathable air and the drinkable water and the stable climate, that no amount of money could replace once they were gone. We under-protect them not in spite of their value but because of the very plenty that makes them valuable, since plenty is precisely what keeps the number off the tag.

The habitat was never a prophecy. It was a way of seeing. The point of imagining the metered breath is to walk back out into the unmetered one and feel, for a moment, the weight of what you are getting for free. The air around you is not cheap. It has simply never sent you the bill. Whether that remains true is, for once, a thing we get to decide.
