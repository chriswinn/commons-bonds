# The Mask of Abundance

Take a breath. Hold it for a second, then let it go.

That breath cost you nothing. No one billed you for it. No meter ticked. You did not check your balance before you inhaled, and you will not get an invoice at the end of the month for the air you used today. This is so ordinary that it feels silly to point out. Of course air is free. Air is everywhere.

But stay with the strangeness for a moment, because there is something hiding inside that ordinariness. We usually assume that things are cheap when they are not worth much. A free thing, we tell ourselves, must be a thing of little value. That is the lesson the price tag teaches us all day long. The expensive thing is the precious thing. The cheap thing is the throwaway. And the free thing is barely a thing at all.

Now hold that up against your own breath. Without air, you have a few minutes to live. There is almost nothing on Earth you need more urgently or more often. By any honest measure of worth, air is among the most valuable things in your life. And yet its price is zero.

So which is it? Is air priceless, or is it worthless? The price says one thing and your lungs say the opposite, and they cannot both be right.

Here is the resolution, and it is the seed of everything that follows in this essay. A price does not measure how much a thing is worth. A price measures how scarce a thing is at the edges, where the last unit changes hands. When something is plentiful enough that no one ever has to go without, there is no edge to charge at. The seller has no leverage, because the buyer can always step three feet to the left and breathe for free. So the price falls to zero. Not because the thing has little value. Because it has so much value, spread so completely and so evenly across everyone, that no market can find a place to stand and ask for money.

Air is not cheap because it is worthless. Air is cheap because it is worth everything, to everyone, all the time, and that total abundance is exactly what makes its price collapse to nothing. The zero on the tag is not a verdict on its worth. It is a verdict on its abundance. We have been reading the two as the same number for so long that we forgot they were ever different.

Call this the mask of abundance. When a thing is abundant enough, its price drops to zero, and that zero acts as a mask. It hides the value underneath. We look at the free thing and our trained eye reports back: nothing here, move along. The instrument we trust most, the price, has gone blind in exactly the place where the value is greatest.

And it is not only air. Think about the things that surround you and keep you alive that carry no price at all. Clean water falling out of the sky. A climate stable enough that crops grow on the same schedule your grandparents planted by. The quiet work of the oceans and forests pulling carbon out of the air you just exhaled and tucking it away. The web of living things that pollinates the food you eat. None of these arrive with a bill. None of them show up in any ledger. They are the commons, the shared inheritance that no one made and everyone uses, and the market treats every one of them as if it were free, which is to say, as if it were worth nothing.

Now, plenty of valuable things in our economy are underpriced. A nurse is underpriced for the value she creates. Good topsoil is underpriced for what it does. Teachers, caregivers, the people who keep a city running at three in the morning. We can argue about all of them, and the argument has a shape: the thing has a price, and we think the price is too low. We can point at the number and say it should be higher.

The commons are stranger than that. They are not merely underpriced. They have no price at all. There is no number to argue about. And a thing with no number is not just cheap in our accounting. It is invisible to it. Our entire economic machinery runs on prices. It is how we decide what to make, what to save, what to protect, what to spend. A thing that never enters the price system never enters the calculation. It is not weighed and found light. It is simply never placed on the scale.

That is the problem this essay is about. And here is what the essay is trying to do, stated plainly, so you know where we are headed. I want to build a way of accounting that is honest about the things abundance has hidden. A way of putting the invisible costs and the invisible values back onto the ledger where they belong, so that any person, any community, or any government can see what things actually cost before deciding what to do. That is the whole of it. Not a new economic system. An honest set of books that any system needs. And by the end I want to show you that the same set of books works here on Earth and works on any other world we might ever reach, and can be run forwards into the future or backwards into the past, and is always, underneath, the same single move.

So let us begin where the mask is thickest, and find a way to make it slip.

## Sealing the Room

If abundance is what hides the value, then the way to reveal it is to take the abundance away. Not in theory. In fact. Put people somewhere the air is no longer everywhere, and watch what happens to the price.

I am going to use an imaginary sealed habitat as a kind of thinking tool through this essay, and I want to be honest about that from the start. I am not predicting that we will all live in glass domes. The sealed habitat is a lens, not a forecast. It is a way of seeing something about Earth that is hard to see while we are standing in the middle of it, breathing for free. But before I lean on the imaginary version, I want to show you that the real version already exists, in three different places, and that in every one of them the bill for the commons arrives the moment the seal closes.

Start at the bottom of the sea. When divers work at great depth, on oil rigs or salvage or deep construction, they cannot breathe ordinary air. The pressure does strange and dangerous things to the gases we normally take for nothing. Down deep, the nitrogen that makes up most of our air starts to act like a drug, dulling the mind right when a diver needs it sharpest. And the oxygen that keeps us alive at the surface becomes poisonous under enough pressure. So deep divers breathe a special mix, mostly helium with a measured amount of oxygen, and they buy it.

Helium is not free. It runs somewhere in the range of one dollar and seventy cents to two dollars and seventy-five cents per cubic foot, and a working diver goes through enough of it that the helium alone can cost on the order of eighty to two hundred dollars an hour. The cost is high enough that ships running these operations install machines to capture the divers' exhaled breath and recover the helium so they can use it again. Think about what that means. Out in the open air, your breath is so worthless that the idea of saving it is absurd. Down there, behind the seal of pressure and depth, your breath is valuable enough that someone built a machine to catch it. The value did not change. The abundance did.

Now go up, to a glass dome in the Arizona desert. In the early 1990s, a project called Biosphere 2 sealed eight people inside about three acres of enclosed glass, a tiny artificial Earth, to see whether they could live off the air and food the enclosed system produced. It cost roughly a hundred and fifty million dollars to build. And it taught a hard lesson about the commons. Over sixteen months, the oxygen inside slowly fell, from the normal twenty-one percent of the air down to about fourteen percent, low enough that the crew suffered for it. The oxygen was disappearing into the soil, eaten by microbes, and into the concrete of the structure itself, which bound up carbon as it cured. Eventually the managers gave up and pumped oxygen in from outside. The life inside struggled too. Of twenty-five kinds of small backboned animals brought in, nineteen died. Every pollinating insect died, so the crops had to be pollinated by hand. Out here, the air refreshes itself, the bees do their work, and no one pays for any of it. Inside the seal, the free services of the living world stopped being free and started being matters of life and death.

Now go up much further, to the International Space Station, a few hundred kilometers above your head. Everything that is free on the ground is precious up there, and the prices are staggering, because everything has to be lifted out of Earth's gravity to get there. Carrying water up to orbit costs something like twenty-one thousand dollars per kilogram. A six-month supply of water for the crew, if you simply shipped it all up, would run on the order of a hundred and thirty-five million dollars. So they do not simply ship it. The station runs a recycling system that recovers around ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine and turns it back into drinking water. The oxygen they breathe is made by running electricity through water to split it apart. Up there, a liter of water and a lungful of oxygen are among the most expensive things human beings have ever handled. Down here, the same liter and the same lungful are free.

Three sealed systems, three depths and heights, one lesson. The moment you seal the room, the priceless commons become the most expensive things in it. The value was always there. Sealing the room did not create it. Sealing the room only stripped away the abundance that had been hiding it, and the bill, which had been there all along, finally arrived in a form we could read.

That is the whole trick of this essay, performed three times in the real world. Now we know the value is real, because we have watched it appear the instant abundance was removed. The next question is the important one. Where does the bill go when the room is not sealed? When we are all standing out in the open air, breathing for free, who is actually paying for the commons we use? Because the strange truth is that someone always is. The bill does not vanish. It just gets sent somewhere we have trouble seeing.

## Naming the Trick

Let me name the thing that happens, in plain words first, and then give it a short label so we can carry it through the rest of the essay.

Here is the plain version. You do something, and that something creates a cost. But the cost does not land on you. It lands on someone else. And that someone else has no way to send the bill back to you. You created it; they pay it; and the wire that should carry the charge from you to them has been cut. The cost is real, the payer is real, but the connection between them is severed.

Call that cost severance. A cost is created, a cost is paid, and the two are torn apart so that the person who made the cost never feels it and the person who feels it never agreed to it.

When you burn a gallon of fuel, you pay for the gallon. You do not pay for the carbon that goes up the tailpipe and stays in the sky, warming the climate for everyone, including people who will not be born for a hundred years. They will pay for that. You will not. The bill has been severed from you and sent forward in time and outward across the world to people who never sat at the table when the sale was made. That is cost severance, and once you have the phrase, you start to see it everywhere.

Most of the time, when people talk about fixing this, they mean re-attaching the severed cost. Find the bill that got sent to the wrong place, figure out who really created it, and route the charge back to them. That is the ordinary work of honest pricing, and it is good work. If your factory dumps poison in a river and a town downstream pays for the cleanup, honest accounting says the bill belongs to the factory. Re-attach the cost. Send it back.

But the commons case I have been describing is stranger than that, and I want to be careful about the difference, because the difference is the heart of this essay.

When you re-attach a severed cost, you are dealing with a thing that had a price, or at least could have had one. The cleanup has a price. The poison has a known harm. You are moving an existing bill to its rightful owner.

The commons are different, because they never had a price to begin with. The air was never sold. The stable climate was never on any market. There is no existing bill to move. What we are doing is not re-attaching a cost that wandered off. We are discovering a value that was so total, so completely abundant, that it never acquired a price in the first place, and then watching that value suddenly become visible the moment scarcity arrives. It is the mask of abundance again, seen from the cost side. The value was hidden by its own plenty, and only the seal, or the damage, or the running-out, reveals it.

So there are two pieces here, and I want both of them clearly in hand. The first is the value itself. After a sale is done, after the oil is pumped and the coal is burned and the money has changed hands, there is a worth that was never paid for and never can be paid for to the people who made the deal, because the people it is owed to were not at the table. Some of them are strangers across the world. Many of them are not yet born. That leftover, unpriced worth, the value that survives after the transaction is complete and is owed to people who had no voice in it, I will call the residual commons value. The word residual just means what is left over. It is the part of the worth that the sale did not capture and could not capture, because the only ones who could have charged for it do not exist yet or had no seat.

The second piece is the act that creates the gap. Value extraction. Something of shared worth is pulled out of the commons and turned into private money, and the worth that is owed back to everyone else is simply not paid, because there is no mechanism to pay it. The value is extracted; the residual value is left stranded; the cost is severed and sent to strangers and to the unborn.

Now we have the three plain ideas we need, and none of them require any economics to understand. A cost can be cut loose from the person who made it and sent to someone who never agreed to pay it. That is cost severance. A thing can be so abundant that its enormous value never gets a price, and is revealed only when the abundance ends. And after extraction, a leftover worth remains, owed to people not in the room, often not yet alive. That is the residual commons value. Honest accounting, the thing this essay is building, is just the discipline of putting these back on the books. Of refusing to let the mask of abundance, and the cut wire of severance, keep the real numbers off the page.

Let us test the idea on the single most important commons we extract from the ground.

## A Hundred Dollars a Barrel

Picture a barrel of oil with a price on it. I will use a round number, a hundred dollars a barrel, and I want to be clear that the exact figure does not matter to the argument. It is an illustration, not a market quote. A hundred dollars is just a clean number to think with.

What does that hundred dollars actually buy, and what does it leave unpaid?

The hundred dollars buys the present its tank of gas. It covers the cost of getting the oil out of the ground, refining it, shipping it, and a profit for everyone along the way. It clears the market today. A buyer hands over the money, a seller hands over the barrel, and as far as the transaction is concerned, the books are balanced. Today's accounts close clean.

But the thing being sold does not belong only to today. When you burn that barrel, the carbon goes into the sky and stays there, warming the world for people who will not be born for generations. That is the severed cost, sent forward in time. And there is something else, something even harder to see, which is the whole reason oil is a special case.

The barrel is gone. Not used, not borrowed, gone. Burned into the air and never coming back. There is a fixed amount of oil under the Earth, laid down over hundreds of millions of years, and every barrel we burn is one barrel fewer that will ever exist again. The hundred dollars bought the present its fuel. It did not, and could not, buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after. Their share of that finite inheritance was sold out from under them, and they got nothing, because they were not at the table. They could not be. They do not exist yet.

This is where it helps to draw a line that the price system never draws, between two completely different kinds of natural wealth. Some of nature renews itself. A forest grows back. A fishery breeds. A field of grass returns each spring. With these renewable things, the honest question is a question of rate. How fast can you take, before you take faster than the thing can replace itself? Take less than it breeds and you can take forever. Take more and you grind it down. The whole problem is finding the right speed.

Oil is not like that. Oil does not breed. There is no rate slow enough to make it sustainable, because it does not renew at any rate at all that matters to us. It took the planet hundreds of millions of years to make, and on any human timescale the supply is simply fixed. When you burn a barrel, you have not borrowed it. You have liquidated it. You have turned a piece of a finite, never-to-be-repeated inheritance into smoke. The word liquidated is exact here. You are not living off the interest. You are spending down the principal, permanently, and handing the empty account to your descendants.

So the hundred dollars is honest about today and silent about forever. It prices the present and says nothing about the permanence of what is being destroyed. The residual commons value of that barrel, the worth owed to all the people who will never get to use it because we used it up, sits there on no ledger anywhere. The mask of abundance is doing its work. Oil has felt abundant for a century, so its price has reflected only the cost of pulling it up, never the cost of its absence afterward. And by the time the abundance ends and the scarcity finally arrives, the people who would have to pay the bill will be the very people who got nothing, the ones we sold their inheritance out from under.

The barrel, then, is the breath again, only running the other way. The breath showed us a value so abundant it had no price. The barrel shows us a value so finite that no price could ever be enough, because no amount of money paid today can put the barrel back tomorrow. Both are the mask of abundance. Both are values the market cannot see. And in both, the people owed are people not at the table, scattered across distance and across time.

## A Lot of People Walked Near This

I want to be honest with you about something. The idea I am describing is not entirely new, and it would be a kind of dishonesty, in an essay about honest accounting, to pretend that no one ever thought along these lines before. The truth is that a great many serious thinkers walked right up to the edge of this empty plot of ground, looked at it, and then built their houses somewhere nearby instead. I will name a few of them quickly, not to give a full tour of their work, but to show you the shape of what they each saw and what they each missed. The pattern in the near-misses is itself revealing.

Start with the people who saw that someone was getting cheated. More than a century and a half ago, the philosopher and economist Karl Marx looked hard at the gap between what workers produced and what they were paid, and named the leftover that the owner kept. He had found a real severed value. But the cut he was looking at ran between classes of people alive at the same time, between the worker and the boss. He was not looking at the cut that runs across time, between the people alive now and the people not yet born. He priced a surplus, but it was the wrong axis for our problem.

Then there are the people who studied how communities share what nature gives. The political scientist Elinor Ostrom spent a career showing that ordinary people, left to govern their own shared pastures and fisheries and forests, often manage them wisely, without either private owners or distant governments forcing them to. She won a Nobel for it. But the commons she studied were the renewable kind, the pasture that grows back, the fishery that breeds. She solved the problem of rate, of taking no faster than the thing replenishes. She was not studying the liquidated commons, the barrel that never comes back. Her tools assume there is something left to govern next year.

Then there are the people who warned that the whole machine was running too hot. The economist Herman Daly argued for decades that an economy cannot grow forever inside a finite world, that there is only so much stuff and energy you can push through the system before you wreck it. He was right about the throughput, about the total flow. But a warning about the total flow does not write the invoice for one specific foreclosed thing. It tells you the river is too high. It does not hand you the bill for the single field that drowned.

Closest of all, in a way, was the economist John Hartwick. Back in the 1970s he worked out a clean rule. If you are living off a resource that will run out, do not spend all the money it brings in. Take the rents, the pure profit from the resource itself, and reinvest them, so that when the resource is gone you have built something lasting in its place. That is a genuinely deep idea, and we will see a whole country live by it later in this essay. But notice what Hartwick was saving. He was saving the money. He wanted you to reinvest the rent so your economy survives the resource running out. He was not pricing the barrel itself, the absence of the thing, the loss to the people who will never get to use it. He was protecting the seller's future. He was not paying the people not at the table.

So that is one whole tradition, the economists, each of whom saw a real piece of the picture and built on the piece next door to ours.

There is a second tradition, the moral philosophers, who came at it from the question of what we owe to one another, and they got remarkably close from that direction.

The philosopher John Rawls asked us to imagine designing the rules of a society without knowing in advance who we would be in it. Rich or poor, strong or weak, lucky or unlucky, you do not get to know, so you have to design rules that are fair no matter where you land. He called this thinking from behind a veil of ignorance. It is a powerful tool for fairness among people alive together. And later thinkers widened the veil to cover not just who you might be but when you might be born. If you did not know which generation you would land in, the one that burns the oil or the one that inherits the empty ground, what rules would you write? That widening is exactly our axis, the axis across time, and it points straight at the empty plot.

The philosopher Michael Sandel asked a different and sharper question. He argued that there are some things money should not buy, because the act of putting a price on them corrupts them, changes what they are. Friendship, a place in line, certain kinds of honor. He was warning that the market does not just measure value, it can degrade it. That is a crucial caution for anyone trying to put prices on the commons, and we will have to hold it carefully, because the last thing honest accounting wants is to cheapen the things it is trying to protect.

And the philosopher Derek Parfit thought longer and more carefully than almost anyone about what we owe to people who do not yet exist. He showed that our ordinary moral instincts get tangled and strange when we start thinking about future generations, and that the strangeness is real and has to be worked through, not waved away. The people not at the table, the ones the residual commons value is owed to, are in large part Parfit's future people. He gave us the tools to take their claims seriously.

So the moral philosophers built the case that we owe something across time. The economists built the tools for pricing scarce and severed things. The two traditions were standing on opposite sides of the same empty lot and never quite built the house on it together.

There is a third group I should mention, because they have been fighting about this very thing for years, and their fight reveals something important. These are the economists who try to put a number on the damage that carbon does. When you burn fuel and warm the world, that warming causes harm for a long time. Floods, failed crops, heat, rising seas, all of it stretching out over centuries. The hard question is how to count harm that happens far in the future against money spent today.

The crux is something called the discount rate, and you do not need any math to grasp the fight. The question is simply how much you should care about a dollar of harm a hundred years from now compared to a dollar of harm today. One famous review of the problem, led by the economist Nicholas Stern, argued that we should care almost as much about the future as the present, that a harm to our descendants is nearly as weighty as a harm to us. Run the numbers that way and the cost of carbon is enormous and we should act hard and fast. Another economist, William Nordhaus, who also won a Nobel, argued the opposite, that future harms should be discounted more steeply, the way money in a savings account grows, so that a distant harm is worth much less in today's terms. Run it his way and the urgency softens. A third thinker, Martin Weitzman, pointed out that the real danger might be the small chance of an utter catastrophe, a fat tail of possibility so bad that it should dominate the whole calculation regardless of the discount rate.

That fight has gone on for years, and the two sides can be a hundred times apart. A dollar of distant harm might be worth twenty-five dollars in one accounting and a dollar and a half in another, just depending on how steeply you discount. But here is the thing I most want you to see about that whole long battle. Every side of it is pricing the same thing. They are all pricing the flow of damage that comes out of burning the carbon. The floods, the heat, the failed harvests. They are arguing about how to count the harm the smoke does.

None of them is pricing the absence of the barrel itself.

The residual commons value I have been describing is not a flow of future damage. It is the present fact that a finite, never-returning thing is now simply gone, and the people it belonged to will never get it. That is not a harm that unfolds over time and can be discounted back to today. It is a loss that is already total the instant the barrel is burned. You cannot discount it away by saying the future matters less, because it is not a future harm. It is a present absence. Even Stern's gentle discount rate, the one most generous to the future, is answering a different question than the one I am asking. The whole discount-rate war is fought over the damage the carbon does. The residual commons value is about the thing the carbon was. It survives the entire argument, because it was never a discounted flow in the first place. It is the present loss of a thing that will not return.

So a lot of people walked near this. The shape of what they missed is consistent. The economists priced flows and surpluses and savings. The philosophers established the debt across time but did not price it. And the climate economists priced the damage from the smoke but never the loss of the thing burned. The empty plot is the residual commons value, run across time, owed to people not at the table. Now let us put a real number on it.

## McDowell County

Go to McDowell County, in the coalfields of southern West Virginia. For more than a century, men went underground there and brought up coal, and the coal was sold. Around 1960, at the mouth of the mine, that coal sold for something like four or five dollars a ton. That was the price. That was what cleared the market. A buyer paid it, a seller took it, and the books of the day closed clean, exactly like our barrel of oil.

Now run honest accounting over that same ton of coal. Add back the severed costs, the bills that got cut loose and sent to strangers and to the unborn. What does the ton actually cost, once you stop letting the mask of abundance hide the value?

The honest price comes out to more than five hundred dollars a ton. The coal that sold for four or five dollars carries a true cost of around five hundred and ten. And here is the part that should stop you. Almost the entire honest price is carbon. The wages, the equipment, the haulage, the danger, all the things the four or five dollars was paying for, those are a tiny sliver of the real cost. The overwhelming bulk of it, around four hundred and ninety-six dollars of the five hundred and ten, is the cost of the carbon that coal puts into the sky when it burns.

Let me show you where that number comes from, because it is built from honest, checkable pieces and not from any rhetorical flourish. A ton of coal from those particular McDowell seams releases about two and six-tenths tons of carbon dioxide when it is burned, a bit more than the national average because of the kind of coal it is. And the cost of putting a ton of carbon dioxide into the air, the harm it does to everyone over the long run, has been estimated by the Environmental Protection Agency, in its 2023 figure, at about a hundred and ninety dollars. Multiply the two together. Two and six-tenths tons of carbon dioxide, at a hundred and ninety dollars a ton, comes to roughly four hundred and ninety-six dollars. Add the rest of the severed costs and you reach more than five hundred.

So the coal that sold for four or five dollars truly cost more than five hundred, and more than ninety-five percent of that real cost was simply never on the bill. It was severed, cut loose, sent up the chimney and out across the world and forward across the generations to everyone who would live under that carbon. The mine-mouth price was not wrong about what it measured. It honestly measured the cost of getting the coal out and to market in 1960. It was silent about everything else, and everything else was almost the whole story.

This is the framework run backwards, over an extraction that already happened, on real ground, with real numbers. We took a sale that closed clean a lifetime ago and we asked what it actually cost, and we found that the true price was a hundredfold higher than the paid price, and that nearly all of the missing money was a severed cost owed to people not at the table. That is not a metaphor. That is just addition, performed honestly, on a ledger that had been left dishonest.

And once you can run the framework backwards over the coal of West Virginia, you start to see that the same single tool can be pointed in either direction in time. Which is the next thing to understand.

## Forwards and Backwards

The honest ledger runs both ways, and it helps to give each direction a name, so you can see that they are two faces of one idea.

Run it forwards, before the extraction, and you are pricing a loss that has not happened yet. You are about to take something finite out of the ground, and you can see the future absence it will leave. So you price that future loss now, today, and you set the money aside, locked away, so that when the scarcity arrives there is something held back to meet it. Think of it as a bond against foreclosure on the future. Call it a foreclosure bond. You name the future loss, you put a number on it, and you reserve against it before you act.

Run it backwards, after the extraction, and you are pricing a loss that already happened. The coal is burned, the oil is gone, the bill was severed long ago and never paid. Honest accounting backwards means going back to the realized extraction, putting a number on what was taken and never paid for, and making it right where you still can. Think of it as a bond of restitution, of paying back. Call it a restitution bond. You name the past loss, you put a number on it, and you restore what can be restored.

Forwards, you reserve against a loss to come. Backwards, you restore a loss already taken. One tool, two directions, the same honest question in both: what is the real cost, and who is it owed to.

The backwards direction has been worked out most carefully, I think, by people thinking about a specific historical debt, and I want to name them, because the framework I am describing builds directly on their work. The economist William Darity Jr. and the writer A. Kirsten Mullen, in their book From Here to Equality, lay out the case for reparations to Black Americans with a rigor that is, underneath, exactly this backwards accounting. They trace a severed cost across more than four centuries, from 1619 forward, a vast extraction of labor and life and wealth whose bill was cut loose and never paid to the people it was owed to. They show the gap it left, the difference in wealth between Black and white Americans that persists to this day, and even the difference in how long people live, a measurable shadow of an old extraction still falling on the present. Their work is honest accounting run backwards over a realized extraction, and the restitution bond I described is, in their language, a way of operationalizing the restitution part of redress. The journalist Ta-Nehisi Coates made the moral architecture of the same case in his essay The Case for Reparations a few years earlier. Together they are the clearest example I know of the ledger run in reverse.

But I have to be careful and honest about a limit here, and I want to state it plainly rather than slide past it. There is a difference between pricing the leftover legacy of an old extraction, the wealth gap, the longevity gap, the measurable effects you can still count today, and trying to put a dollar figure directly on the suffering of coercion itself, on the violence done to people. The first is something honest accounting can reach, because it deals in effects you can measure. The second is not mine to do. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not my work to claim, and I will not pretend the framework reaches it. That pricing belongs to the people living inside that history and continuing the work of it, not to an outside framework reaching in. So the honest ledger, run backwards, reaches the coerced cases through their legacy effects, the things that can be counted, and defers the direct pricing of the coercion itself to those whose work it properly is. An honest accounting has to be honest about what it cannot honestly do.

So we have a tool that runs in both directions, and a real example of each. Now I want to show you the forwards direction not as a theory but as something a whole nation actually did, and right beside it, the same act done without the ledger, so you can see the difference the accounting makes.

## Norway and the Niger Delta

Just before Christmas in 1969, after years of fruitless drilling that had nearly convinced everyone there was nothing to find, an oil company confirmed to the Norwegian authorities that it had struck a major field under the North Sea. The field was called Ekofisk. Norway, a country of a few million people, suddenly held an enormous finite resource under its waters. The question every resource-rich place faces was now Norway's question. What do you do with a fortune you can only spend once?

Norway answered it, over the following decades, with something very close to honest accounting run forwards. In 1990 the country set up a fund to hold the oil money, the Government Petroleum Fund, which was later renamed the Government Pension Fund Global. And in 2001 it adopted a rule of remarkable discipline. The principal, the actual oil wealth itself, would be kept inviolate, never spent. The government could spend only the expected real return on the fund, an amount set at around three percent a year, the interest and growth, while the underlying capital was left untouched to keep earning for the future.

Think about what that rule does in the terms of this essay. The oil is a finite thing, a barrel that does not come back. Norway looked at the future absence that pumping it would create, priced it, and set the money aside, locked away from the present's appetite. It did not spend the inheritance. It reserved it, and lived off only what the reserve threw off, so that the wealth under the sea was converted into a permanent wealth held for everyone who would come after. That is the foreclosure bond, the forwards ledger, run at the scale of a sovereign nation. It is also, almost exactly, the old Hartwick rule we met earlier, the one about reinvesting the rents instead of spending them, made real and made law.

The result is staggering, and it is checkable. The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars. Spread across the population, that is more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every citizen of Norway, held in common, the throw-off available to the present, the principal preserved for the future. And here is the part that matters most for whether honest accounting is a fantasy or a practical reality. The discipline has held across the entire political spectrum. Governments of the left and governments of the right have come and gone, prime ministers of every stripe, and not one of them has raided the principal. The rule survived the politics. The reserve held. That is the existence proof. The future can be priced and set aside, in practice, by real people in a real democracy, and it can last.

Now set beside Norway a second place where almost the same physical act was performed, and the ledger was never run at all.

In the Niger Delta, in Nigeria, oil has been pumped since the 1950s. The same finite resource, the same liquidation of a thing that does not come back, the same enormous flow of money. Over the decades the revenue has run to hundreds of billions of dollars. But the architecture around it was the opposite of Norway's. The severed costs, the spills that poisoned the water, the gas flares that burned day and night, the fisheries foreclosed and the farmland fouled, fell on the people who lived there. They did not sell the oil. They could not bill anyone for what it cost them. The wire was cut, and the bill landed on them, and there was no one to send it back to. When the writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa led a movement of his people, the Ogoni, to protest what was being done to their land, he and eight others were executed in 1995, an act so stark that Nigeria was suspended from the Commonwealth of Nations over it. Years later, a United Nations assessment of just one part of that region, Ogoniland, estimated that an initial cleanup would cost on the order of a billion dollars and take twenty-five to thirty years, and that was only the beginning of the bill.

Hold the two places side by side. Same physical act. Oil, finite, pulled from the ground, turned into money. Two completely different architectures of accounting. In Norway, the future loss was priced and reserved, and the wealth was held in common across time. In the Niger Delta, the cost was severed and dumped on the people who happened to live above the oil, and nothing was set aside, and the bill is still arriving, decades later, on people who never agreed to pay it.

I want to be careful about what this comparison is and is not. It is not a villain story. It is not an argument that oil should never have been pumped. It is not a political program for any particular party. And it is not anti-extraction, a point I will return to in a moment because it matters more than it might seem. What the comparison shows is something narrower and, I think, more useful than any of those. It shows that the same act can be done with the ledger or without it, and that the difference between an honest ledger and a dishonest one is the difference between a fortune held in common for the future and a wound that takes generations to close. The question is not whether to extract. The question, when the cost outlives the sale, is who carries it, and whether anything was set aside.

Norway proves the forwards ledger can be run. The Niger Delta shows what happens when it is not. And both of them are on this Earth, where the air is still free and the mask of abundance is still mostly on. To see the framework running not as a fix applied after the fact, but as the ordinary day-to-day economy of a whole society, I have to take you somewhere the mask has been removed entirely. I have to take you off the world.

## Off the World

Everything so far has been about Earth, where abundance still hides most of the value, and honest accounting is something we have to work hard to impose on a system that resists it. I want to spend real time now somewhere different, a place where the mask of abundance cannot exist at all, because there is no abundance to wear it. I want to imagine living off the world, inside a sealed habitat on another planet or moon, and I want to be completely honest about what kind of thing this is. I am not predicting that we will live this way. I am not forecasting colonies and asteroid mines. This is a lens, openly imaginary, a thought experiment, and nothing more. But it is the most revealing lens I have, because off the world, the honest ledger is not a reform you have to fight for. It is simply the only way to stay alive, which means that off the world, you get to watch the framework run forwards, in real time, as the plain operating economy of daily life. We used the seal earlier to see the hidden value. Now I want to live inside the seal and pay it.

### The Colony and the Airlock

Imagine you have arrived at a settlement on another world. Not as a visitor on a quick mission, but to live there, to make a home, to be part of a working economy. The first thing you come to is the airlock. And at the airlock, someone asks you a question that no one on Earth has ever been asked at a door.

How do you intend to pay for your air?

On Earth that question is nonsense. Air is free, the most abundant thing there is, and to ask a newcomer how they will pay for it would be either a cruelty or a joke. But here, inside the seal, the question is the most reasonable one imaginable. The air in this habitat did not fall from any sky. There is no sky. Every breathable molecule was made, by machines running on power that someone generated, scrubbed of the carbon dioxide that the people inside keep exhaling, balanced and maintained and replaced. The air is a product. It costs real energy and real labor to produce, every hour of every day. And so, inside, the air has a price, because here the air is scarce, and scarcity is the one thing that gives the priceless commons a price.

So this is a place where you pay for your breathing. Not as a punishment, not as some dystopia of the gasping poor, but simply because here the breath is a manufactured good and the books have to balance or everyone dies. And it is not only the air. Walk into this settlement and look at the everyday economy of it. Water does not pour out of any cloud. It is recycled, every drop, captured and filtered and used again, and metered by the liter, because making new water is enormously expensive and losing old water is a slow path to death. Even your waste is on the ledger. The carbon dioxide you breathe out, which on Earth the oceans and forests quietly carry away for nothing, here has to be actively pulled from the air by machines, or it builds up and poisons everyone, exactly as the oxygen fell and the carbon climbed inside that glass dome in Arizona. So the removal of your exhaled carbon is a service, and the service has a cost, and the cost shows up on someone's books.

Look at what has happened here. Every single commons that the Earth gives away for free, the breath, the water, the carrying-away of the carbon, the stable balance of gases that keeps you alive, has become a metered, billed, priced part of the daily economy. The exact things the mask of abundance hides on Earth are, inside this seal, the most carefully accounted things there are. Not because the people inside are greedy, but because they cannot afford the mask. A world too sealed to let abundance hide any value forces the value into the open, onto the ledger, into the price of an ordinary day.

And now look at what this settlement actually is, in the terms we have been building all along. It is the honest ledger, run forwards, in real time, as a working economy. It is the same ledger as McDowell County's coal, where we ran honest accounting backwards over what was taken. It is the same ledger as Norway's fund, where the future loss was priced and reserved. The only difference is that here it is not a backwards reckoning or a forwards reserve. It is the present-tense, hour-by-hour operating economy of a place that simply cannot pretend the commons are free, because here they manifestly are not.

This is why I called the sealed habitat the most revealing lens. On Earth, honest accounting feels like an imposition, an extra discipline we have to argue ourselves into, against the grain of a price system that keeps reporting zero for the things that matter most. Inside the seal, honest accounting is just arithmetic you cannot escape. The commons have prices because the commons are scarce, and the scarcity is total and obvious and a matter of survival. There is no mask to remove, because there was never any abundance to wear it. And so the settlement shows you, plainly, what Earth's economy would look like if we ever managed to take the mask off. The airlock asks how you will pay for your air because, underneath, on every world, the air was always something that had to be paid for. Earth has simply been generous enough, so far, to send the bill somewhere we could not see.

Reconnect this to where we started. At the beginning, I asked you to take a breath, and I used the seal as a way to see the value that abundance hides. Three real sealed systems, the divers, the dome, the station, showed us the value appearing the moment the seal closed. Now we have walked all the way inside the seal and set up a home there, and the value we glimpsed before is now the literal price of living through an ordinary day. First we used the seal to see the value. Now we live inside the seal and pay it. It is the same value, the same ledger, the same honest accounting, carried from a thought experiment at the start of the essay to the operating economy of a whole imagined world. One ledger, run forwards, in real time, on a world that cannot afford to lie about what things cost.

### The Asteroid Miner

Now leave the settlement and go further out, into the emptiness between worlds, alone on a small ship, to mine an asteroid. And here the same honest ledger says something that might surprise you, because it sounds, at first, like the opposite of everything I have been arguing. The honest ledger, out here, says extract freely.

Let me walk you to why, because it is not a contradiction. It is the same logic, applied honestly to a completely different situation.

Remember what cost severance is. A cost gets created, and it lands on someone who did not agree to pay it, some community downstream, some ecosystem, some future generation, some people not at the table. That is what makes extraction on Earth so fraught. The Earth is full of people and full of living systems, and almost anything you pull out of it sends a severed cost to someone. There is always a table you are not letting people sit at.

Out here, on a dead rock drifting between the planets, there is no one. There is no community whose water you will poison. There is no ecosystem to foreclose. There are no descendants of the asteroid waiting to inherit it, because there is nothing living on it and nothing that depends on it. The whole machinery of cost severance needs a someone to send the bill to, and out here there is no someone. So the severed cost, honestly accounted, falls to nearly zero. There is no table, so there is no one being kept from it.

And so the honest ledger, the very same ledger that ran the careful sums in McDowell County and held back Norway's fortune, looks at the asteroid and says: take the iron, freely. If a comet swings briefly into reach, a thing that will not come past again for a lifetime, take it aggressively while you can, because there is no one it is being taken from. Honest accounting is not a rule that says extraction is wrong. It never was. It is a rule that says know the real cost, including the costs you have been hiding. And when the real cost, honestly counted, is near zero because there is genuinely no one to bear it, then the honest answer is to extract. This is the same framework that condemned the dishonest pumping of the Niger Delta, and it does not condemn the asteroid, for the simple reason that the asteroid has no one to cheat. The framework was never against taking. It was against taking dishonestly, against pretending the bill did not exist while sending it to people who could not refuse it. Where there is no bill, there is nothing to be dishonest about.

But the ledger is not finished with you, even out here, even alone with a lifeless rock and no one to wrong. Because there is one more cost, and it is the one that ties this whole essay together. On that rock, far from any help, you must hold reserves. You must keep back fuel, and air, and water, and the supplies that keep your life-support running. Not a little. Enough. Enough to be certain you can get off the rock and make it home, or make it on to the next world. You do not burn your last reserve of fuel chasing the last vein of iron. You do not breathe down your last tank of air finishing the job. You hold back enough to survive the journey out of the very situation your extraction has put you in.

Look at what the single ledger has just done. To the same miner, about the same rock, in the same breath, it has said two things that sound opposite and are not. It has said take freely, because here the severed cost is near zero. And it has said hold reserves, because your survival into the future is itself a cost you must account for honestly. Take freely here. Reserve there. One ledger, both instructions, no contradiction, because honest accounting was never about whether to take. It was always about seeing the whole real cost, and the whole real cost includes the future you are extracting yourself into. The reserves you keep on the asteroid are the same idea as Norway's untouchable principal, the same idea as the foreclosure bond, the same idea as setting something aside before you act. The miner alone on the rock and the nation rich with oil are running the identical ledger. Take what honestly costs nothing. Hold back enough to survive what comes next.

And that is the deepest thing the off-world lens reveals. Honest accounting is not anti-extraction. It is not a brake on doing things or a sermon against using the world. It is the discipline of knowing what something truly costs, including the cost of the future, and keeping enough back to live in the future you are making. On a rock with no one to cheat, that means take all the iron and still hold your air. On a planet full of people and living things, it means count every severed cost and reserve against every finite loss. Same ledger. Same honest question, asked in every place a human being might ever stand. What does this really cost, and have I kept back enough to survive having done it?

## Whatever Economy You Choose

I have been careful, all through this essay, not to call honest accounting an economic system. It is worth saying plainly why, because it changes what the whole argument is asking of you.

Honest accounting is not a rival to capitalism, or to a planned economy, or to anything else. It does not tell you who should own the mines or how the prices should be set or whether the state or the market should run things. It is something underneath all of that. It is the set of books any economy needs in order to know what it is actually doing. You can run a market economy with honest books or dishonest books. You can run a planned economy with honest books or dishonest books. The choice of economic system is one question. Whether your books tell the truth about your real costs is a completely different question, and it is the only question this essay is asking.

You can see this in the fact that the same honest impulse keeps appearing in wildly different systems, across thousands of years, wearing whatever clothes the local economy gave it.

In our own time, the economist Kate Raworth has drawn a picture she calls the doughnut, a way of seeing the economy as something that has to stay inside two boundaries at once, not falling below a floor where people lack what they need to live, and not pushing past a ceiling where it wrecks the living systems it depends on. That is, underneath, an attempt to put the real costs and the real limits onto the books, to make visible the boundaries the ordinary price system cannot see. It is honest accounting wearing the clothes of modern ecological economics.

Elinor Ostrom's communities, whom we met earlier governing their shared pastures and fisheries, were doing the same thing in a much older and more local form. A village that knows it can take only so many fish before the fishery collapses, and that enforces that limit on itself, is keeping an honest ledger of a renewable commons. They never wrote down a social cost of carbon. But they knew the real cost of overtaking, and they reserved against it, by rule and by custom, generation after generation.

And the impulse is far older than any of that. Long before modern economics, large and complex civilizations built systems to hold reserves against scarcity. Ancient China kept what was sometimes called an ever-normal granary, a public store of grain meant to be filled in fat years and drawn down in lean ones, so that a harvest failure would not become a famine. Ancient Egypt's state granaries did something similar, storing grain against the years the river would not provide. I am keeping these references deliberately high and general, because the details are not mine to embroider, and the point does not need them. The point is only this. Civilizations separated by thousands of years and oceans of difference all reached, independently, for the same basic move. Know that scarcity is coming. Hold something back against it. Keep an honest reckoning of what you have and what you will need. That is the same instinct as Norway's fund and the same instinct as the asteroid miner's reserves and the same instinct as the doughnut's outer ring. It is the ledger, appearing again and again, because every society that lasts has had to learn, in its own language, that the books must be honest or the people starve.

So whatever economy you choose, on this world or any other, capitalist or planned, ancient or off-world, the requirement is the same and it is prior to the choice. You have to be able to see your real costs. You have to know the value of the commons you are drawing down, the severed costs you are sending to others, the finite things you are liquidating, the reserves you must hold against the future. An economy that cannot see those things is flying blind, no matter how it is organized, no matter whose name is on the deed. The honest ledger does not tell you where to fly. It just insists that you not fly with your eyes shut.

## The Longest Horizon

I want to push the idea of reserves out to its furthest edge, because it is at the furthest edge that the off-world lens stops looking like science fiction and starts looking like plain prudence.

We hold reserves for the next generation. That is hard enough, as the whole struggle over carbon and oil shows. But finite things, honestly accounted, demand reserves on a horizon far longer than a generation. They demand that we think about the survival of the whole human project across spans of time so vast they are difficult to hold in the mind.

Here is a fact that does that work of stretching the mind. Our Sun is slowly getting brighter. Not on any timescale that should worry you or your descendants for an immense stretch of the future, but inexorably. As the Sun ages and brightens, it will eventually heat the Earth past the point where life as we know it can survive, and the best estimates put that point somewhere on the order of five hundred million to one billion years from now. That is the deadline for Earth as a habitable home, and it arrives long, long before the more famous ending, the one where the Sun swells into a red giant and may swallow the Earth entirely, which lies something like seven and a half billion years out. Earth becomes uninhabitable not at the dramatic end, but far earlier, in the slow brightening.

I raise this not to alarm anyone. On a human scale these numbers are simply incomprehensible, and nothing you do this century turns on them. I raise it because it changes what kind of thing the off-world lens is. If you take seriously that the human story, if it continues, runs across timescales like these, then the sealed habitat on another world is not a fantasy bolted onto the end of the argument. It is the logical far end of the very thing we have been discussing the whole time, which is holding reserves against a future you can foresee. The asteroid miner holds back fuel to survive the trip home. Norway holds back its principal to survive the end of the oil. And the human species, on the longest horizon, holds back the option of living somewhere other than a single planet, against the certainty that this planet will not remain habitable forever. That is not a different idea from the granary and the oil fund. It is the same idea, the reserve, the thing held back against a foreseeable scarcity, drawn out to the longest horizon there is.

So the off-world habitat earns its place in this essay twice over. Once as the lens that lets us see honest accounting running as a live economy, where the breath is billed and the commons cannot hide. And once as the literal far end of the reserves principle, the option-of-last-resort that a species thinking honestly about its own deep future would want to keep open. On those horizons, the airlock that asks how you will pay for your air is not science fiction. It is just prudence, played out at the scale the universe actually operates on.

## The Number Was Always There

Let me bring the whole argument back to the breath, where it started, and say plainly, one more time, what the essay has tried to do.

I set out to build a way of accounting that is honest about the things abundance, distance, and time have hidden. Just that. Not a new economic order, not a political program, not a doctrine for or against any particular way of running the world. An honest set of books that any person, any community, or any government can keep, on this world or any other, so that we can see what things actually cost before we decide what to do with them.

The whole essay has been one move, made over and over, on widening ground. We began with a breath, and saw that its price of zero was not a verdict on its worth but a mask hung over it by sheer abundance. We sealed the room, three times in the real world, and watched the value appear the instant the abundance was removed. We named the trick: costs cut loose from the people who make them, sent to people not at the table; and a residual worth, owed across distance and across time, that no sale can capture. We followed a barrel of oil and saw that some commons are not renewable but liquidated, gone forever, owed to people who will never get them back. We watched a long line of brilliant thinkers walk near this empty ground and build next to it. We ran the ledger backwards over a ton of West Virginia coal and found a true cost a hundred times the paid price, almost all of it carbon. We gave the two directions their names, the reserve against a future loss and the restitution of a past one, and we found the backwards direction already being worked out, with care, by people pricing an old and terrible extraction through the effects it still leaves. We set Norway beside the Niger Delta and saw the same act done with the ledger and without it. And then we went off the world, and lived inside the seal, and watched the honest ledger become the ordinary economy of a place that cannot afford the mask, and watched the same ledger tell a lone miner to take freely and to hold reserves in a single breath. And we saw, finally, that whatever economy you choose, ancient or modern, here or elsewhere, the books have to be honest or the whole thing flies blind.

It is one ledger. It works on any scale, from a single person to a whole civilization. It works on any world, sealed or open, full of people or empty of them. It runs forwards, pricing the loss to come and reserving against it, and it runs backwards, pricing the loss already taken and restoring what can be restored. And underneath every version of it is the same honest question, asked at the airlock and at the mine mouth and at the edge of the asteroid field alike. What does this really cost, and who is it owed to, and have I kept back enough to survive having done it?

The mask of abundance made us think the answer to that question was nothing. The breath is free, the air is worthless, there is nothing here to count. But the moment we sealed the room, the moment we ran the numbers honestly, the moment we asked who would pay and who was owed, the value was there. It was always there. We did not invent it by counting it. We only stopped letting the abundance hide it. The number on the honest ledger was never created by the accounting. It was uncovered. It had been sitting under the mask the whole time, owed to people not in the room, scattered across the distance and down through the years, waiting for someone to take the mask off and read it.

So take a breath again. It is still free. That is still a gift, the gift of a world generous enough to give away the thing you need most, every few seconds, without a bill. But now you know what the zero on the tag really means. Not that the breath is worthless. That the breath is worth everything, to everyone, all the time, and that somewhere, on some ledger we have only just begun to keep honestly, that worth has always been written down. Whether we choose to read it, whether we keep the books honest while the air is still free and the choice is still ours to make, is one of the few things on the longest horizon that is genuinely up to us. The number was always there. What we do once we can finally see it is not yet written.
