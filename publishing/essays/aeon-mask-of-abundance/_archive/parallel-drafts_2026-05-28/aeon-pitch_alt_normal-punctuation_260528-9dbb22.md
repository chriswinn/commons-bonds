## Pitch body (293w)

You arrive at a closed habitat: Mars, the Moon, an orbital station, an Earth-side analog like Devon Island or HI-SEAS. Before your name, before your business, you are asked how you will be paying for your air. You understand viscerally, then and there, what you have been taking for granted your whole life. Someone else has been paying.

Then you come home. The heat, the light, the water, the air. All free again. All of it suddenly costless. You laugh at the absurdity. Then you notice, slowly, that the costs are still being paid. Just not by you.

The closed habitat is the limiting case that exposes Earth's open system. What follows is a pricing framework, built outside the academy across a decade. It names the patterns by which costs get hidden: geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry, power asymmetry, normative routinization, abundance masking. The patterns route the costs that stopped at the airlock to communities downstream, to atmospheres a generation has not yet been born to breathe, to ecosystems that cannot send a bill. The framework is not anti-extraction. Asteroid iron, with no community or ecosystem to bear the cost: extract freely. Coal in McDowell County in 1960: severely mispriced. A self-imposed commute lease whose hours of life were never computed: same framework, no external bad actor.

The claim that follows is structural, not political: this is a pricing framework first and an analytical lens second. Most discussions of resource pricing land in policy debate. This one lands in arithmetic. If the framework is right, the price tag on commons is many multiples of what we have been paying as a species. The math, finally done, is what a thoughtful reader on any world — including this one — would recognize as honest accounting.

## Phase E — comparison note

### What changed structurally between Version C and this alt

The two-sentence framework-introduction pattern was restructured. Version C threads the framework introduction through a single connective sentence ("This essay walks a pricing framework through what you discover") and then drops the bio-anchor as a participial phrase mid-paragraph ("Built outside the academy across a decade, the framework names patterns…"). The alt promotes the philosophical-anchor sentence ("The closed habitat is the limiting case that exposes Earth's open system") to the top of ¶2 and uses a clean "What follows is a pricing framework, built outside the academy across a decade" anchor — splitting the bio-attribution from the patterns enumeration. The anchor sentence does more rhetorical work at top-of-paragraph than buried mid-paragraph. The trade-off: Version C's ordering puts the patterns and cases first and lets the philosophical anchor land as the universalizing move that justifies the case-walks; the alt's ordering puts the philosophical claim first and lets the patterns and cases do illustrative work that follows from it. Both are defensible; the alt prefers the front-loaded philosophical claim.

The come-home moment was also restructured. Version C uses single-sentence ambient framing ("The abundant heat, light, water and even the air is free again. All of it suddenly costless."). The alt uses sentence-fragment percussion: a four-item list as fragment, then two short declaratives ("The heat, the light, the water, the air. All free again. All of it suddenly costless."). The percussive cadence does the felt-inversion work that Version B's billed/freed parallel did before the Version C compression — different mechanism, similar function. The alt also cuts "That first morning back," which Version C uses as a temporal anchor; the alt's terser "You laugh at the absurdity." is more concise but loses the explicit "first morning" framing. The central-claim ¶3 substitutes "Most discussions of resource pricing land in policy debate. This one lands in arithmetic." for Version C's "Most discussions of resource pricing are political. This one is structural." Same substantive distinction, more concrete metaphor (policy-debate vs. arithmetic as two places where claims can land).

### Where the em-dash calibration drove sentence-level choices

The calibration's "default to commas/periods/restructure unless em-dash earns its place" rule fired multiple times in drafting. Six candidate em-dash sites were considered and restructured:

1. **Habitat-list introduction.** Version C uses an em-dash ("a closed habitat — Mars, the Moon..."). The alt uses a colon ("a closed habitat: Mars, the Moon..."). Both work; colon is grammatically cleaner and reads less em-dash-habit.
2. **Reveal pivot.** Version C uses an em-dash for held-breath pivot ("...all your life — someone else has been paying."). The alt uses a period ("...your whole life. Someone else has been paying."). The period creates declarative finality on the reveal rather than syntactic compression — a different rhythmic choice with the same payload.
3. **Come-home appositive.** Version C uses an em-dash ("...is free again — all of it suddenly costless.") before Pass 3.2 F-V-AeonC-2 Option E converted to period+fragment ("...is free again. All of it suddenly costless."). The alt's fragment structure ("All free again. All of it suddenly costless.") goes further in the same direction.
4. **Framework-intro compound.** A draft tried "...framework — built outside the academy across a decade — applied to..." with paired em-dashes around the bio-anchor; this was restructured to two sentences ("...Earth's open system. What follows is a pricing framework, built outside the academy across a decade.") because the two-sentence form reads cleaner and the paired em-dashes were doing parenthetical work that comma + sentence boundary did equally well.
5. **Six-patterns introduction.** A draft tried "...by which costs get hidden — geographic distance, temporal asymmetry..." with em-dash list-introducer; this was changed to colon ("hidden: geographic distance, temporal asymmetry...") because colon is the conventional list-introducer and reads cleaner.
6. **Central-claim compound.** Version C uses a colon at "...universal: a pricing framework first, an analytical lens second" (Pass 3.2 F-V-AeonC-2 Option B). The alt also uses a colon ("...structural, not political: this is a pricing framework first..."). Both pitch versions converge on colon here.

**One em-dash survived the calibration.** The paired em-dashes around "including this one" at the universalizability bridge ("any world — including this one — would recognize as honest accounting") earn their place. The bridge is the pitch's load-bearing rhetorical pivot — the move from "any world" hypothetical to "this one" actual that makes the framework's universalizability land on Earth rather than remain abstract. A comma pair ("on any world, including this one, would recognize") smooths the bridge into a routine confirming aside and loses the emphasis. Parentheses ("on any world (including this one) would recognize") read more academic and weaken the punch. Restructuring into two sentences ("...on any world would recognize as honest accounting. Including this one.") breaks the single-sentence universalizability cadence and creates an unnecessary fragment. Em-dashes are the right tool here.

### Honest read — which feels stronger and why

Version C is tighter on the seconds-of-attention editor first-screen. The "Imagine landing at a closed habitat" opener is more cinematic than the alt's "You arrive at a closed habitat" — "imagine" recruits the reader's imaginative work explicitly; "you arrive" is more declarative. Version C's "Then you come home. The abundant heat, light, water and even the air is free again." has a deliberate collective-singular literary move that Pass 3.2 F-V-AeonC-1 specifically ratified as on-register for the Mulgan/Sandel/MacAskill cluster. The alt's "The heat, the light, the water, the air. All free again." is rhythmically interesting but loses that signature literary-philosophical move and substitutes a more journalistic percussive cadence.

The alt is stronger on em-dash hygiene and on front-loading the philosophical anchor sentence. The "What follows is a pricing framework, built outside the academy across a decade" intro reads cleaner than Version C's "This essay walks a pricing framework through what you discover" because it doesn't smuggle the meta-frame ("this essay") into the substantive claim — the alt lets the pitch's rhetorical move (offering a framework) stand without the editorial commentary. The alt's "Most discussions of resource pricing land in policy debate. This one lands in arithmetic." is more concrete than Version C's "Most discussions of resource pricing are political. This one is structural." — "lands in arithmetic" gives the reader a place the claim goes rather than an abstract adjective.

Net: Version C is stronger on opener cinematism + collective-singular literary signature; the alt is stronger on philosophical-anchor positioning + em-dash hygiene + the structural/political contrast made concrete. For Aeon specifically — a literary-philosophical editorial brain that already ratified Version C's voice register decisively at Pass 3.3 — the alt's gains may not outweigh what it gives up. The alt is most useful as a data point for the em-dash discipline question: it demonstrates that the load-bearing universalizability-bridge em-dash is the genuinely irreducible one, and that the other five em-dash sites in Version C (most of which Pass 3.2 already restructured to colons or periods) are restructurable without prose damage. The em-dash-forbidden parallel chip will show whether even the universalizability-bridge em-dash can be cleanly replaced; if it can, the alt's calibration was too lenient; if it can't, the alt's single-em-dash discipline is the empirically-defensible floor.
