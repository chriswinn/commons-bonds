## Pitch body (310 words)

Imagine arriving at a closed habitat: Mars, the Moon, an orbital station, an Earth-side analog like Devon Island or HI-SEAS. At the airlock the question is direct. How will you be paying for your air? You stand a second longer than expected. Something becomes visible that had been invisible all your life. Back on Earth you have always breathed without paying. That does not mean the air was free. Someone else has been paying.

Then you come home. The heat, the light, the water, the air: all of it free again. You laugh, that first morning back, at how strange the airlock question now feels. The laugh fades when you notice the costs are still being paid. Just not by you.

This pitch walks a pricing framework through what you find. Built outside the academy across a decade, it names the patterns by which costs get hidden: geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry, power asymmetry, normative routinization, and abundance masking. The closed habitat is the limiting case that exposes Earth's open system. These patterns route the airlock costs to communities downstream, to atmospheres a generation has not been born to breathe, to ecosystems that cannot send a bill. The framework is not anti-extraction. Asteroid iron, no community or ecosystem to bear the cost: extract freely. Coal in McDowell County in 1960: severely mispriced. A self-imposed commute lease whose hours of life were never computed: same framework, no external bad actor.

The central claim is that the framework is universal: a pricing framework first, an analytical lens second. Most discussions of resource pricing are political. This one is structural. If the framework is right, the price tag on commons is many multiples of what we have been paying as a species. The math, finally done, is what a thoughtful reader on any world (including this one) would recognize as honest accounting.
