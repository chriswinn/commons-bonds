# The Mask of Abundance

The door behind you has already sealed. The door in front of you has not yet opened. You are standing in a small chamber with metal walls, and the only sound is the faint working of pumps somewhere below the floor. A panel beside the inner door is waiting for you. Before it will let you through, it asks you a single question, and the question is how you intend to pay for your air.

You think, at first, that you have misheard. Then you understand that you have not. The habitat on the other side of that door has a fixed quantity of breathable atmosphere, manufactured at cost, scrubbed and recirculated and topped up by machines that run on power that someone has to generate. Every breath you take inside will be drawn from a shared supply, and every breath out will load that supply with carbon dioxide that other machines will have to strip away again before the next person can use it. None of this is free. None of it was ever going to be free. The chamber is simply the first place in your life that has bothered to tell you so.

So you answer. Perhaps you have an account, a stipend, a contract that covers a daily ration. Perhaps you are a visitor and your host has agreed to carry the cost. The panel accepts your answer and the inner door slides open and you step through into a corridor full of people who breathe the same metered air without thinking about it, the way you used to breathe without thinking about it, because for them the question was settled long ago and the meter simply runs. You join them. You become someone who pays for air.

It feels absurd for about a week. Then it stops feeling like anything at all, which is stranger. You stop noticing the meter the way the people around you have stopped noticing it. And one evening, watching the figure tick over on a wall display, you find yourself thinking a thought you could not have thought on Earth, because on Earth there was nothing to prompt it. The thought is that the air was never free. It was only ever unbilled. Out here a machine puts a number on it because out here a machine has to. Back home no machine ever had to, and so no number ever appeared, and the absence of the number was mistaken, by everyone, for the absence of a cost.

I should say plainly that I am not predicting any of this. The sealed habitat is a way of seeing, not a forecast. But the seeing has a real basis, because human beings have already built places that work like this, places where the priceless suddenly carries a price. There is a research station called Devon Island in the Canadian Arctic, on a cold and almost lifeless rock that crews use to rehearse the discipline of living somewhere that gives you nothing. There is a habitat called HI-SEAS on the side of a Hawaiian volcano where small teams seal themselves in for months and ration what they have. In those places the lesson arrives early and it does not leave: the things you assumed were the background of life turn out to be the most carefully managed quantities in the building.

Then you come home. You step off whatever brought you back, and the first thing you do, before you have even decided to do it, is breathe. The air is free again. No panel asks you anything. No meter runs. The whole machinery of accounting that governed every lungful out there has simply vanished, and you stand in the open with your chest rising and falling and the absurd little question from the airlock seems exactly that, absurd, a peculiarity of a peculiar place, the kind of story you tell at dinner. How will you pay for your air. As if anyone would ever.

And then the harder thought arrives, the one that does not leave either. The air here costs something too. It always did. The difference is not that the cost is absent. The difference is only that no one hands you the bill. The work of keeping this air breathable is done by oceans and forests and the slow chemistry of a living planet, and it is paid for, constantly, by something or someone. When that work is overwhelmed, the cost does not disappear. It moves. It lands on a community downwind, on a fishery that will not recover, on a coastline that floods, on a child not yet born who will inherit an atmosphere we are still busy changing. The bill is real. It is simply addressed to someone else, somewhere else, or to someone who cannot yet object.

This is the subject I want to put in front of you. There is a category of things on which all life depends and for which nobody is ever charged. The air. Fresh water. A climate stable enough to plant by and live in. The patient work the planet does of taking our carbon back out of the sky. These things carry no price, and we have made the oldest mistake in the world about them. We have read the absence of a price as the absence of value, when in truth it is the opposite. They have no price precisely because they are worth everything, distributed so completely and so evenly that no market can find an edge to charge at. A price is a measure of scarcity at the margin. Where there is no scarcity, there is no margin, and so there is no number, and the most valuable things we have post a permanent and misleading zero.

Call it the mask of abundance. When something is abundant enough, its value disappears from view, not because the value is small but because there is so much of the thing that the value never gets counted anywhere. The danger this creates is not the one we usually worry about. The danger is not mainly that we treat these things as cheap. It is that we cannot see them at all. A thing with no price is invisible to the only instrument we have all agreed to trust. We can ruin what we cannot see, and feel, while we do it, that we are getting something for nothing.

What follows is an attempt to take the mask off. The method is not a new economic system and it is not a politics. It is something smaller and, I think, harder to argue with. It is honest accounting: a way of finding the value that abundance, or distance, or time has hidden, and writing it back onto the books where it belongs. The claim of this essay is only that such accounting is possible, that it works on any scale and on any world, and that the first step is always the same. You have to make the hidden cost visible before you can do anything about it at all.

## Three sealed worlds

The fastest way to see a hidden cost is to remove the abundance that hides it. Seal a system, cut it off from the planet's free and limitless supply, and watch what happens to the price of air and water. We do not have to imagine this. We have done it, more than once, in different ways, and each time the result has been the same. The cheapest things on Earth become the most expensive things in the room.

Consider first the saturation diver. To work for days at the bottom of the sea, on a pipeline or a wellhead far below the surface, a diver cannot breathe ordinary air. Under that much pressure the nitrogen in normal air turns narcotic, clouding judgment like a drug, and oxygen itself, at the concentration we breathe at the surface, becomes toxic. So the diver breathes a manufactured mixture in which most of the nitrogen is replaced by helium, a gas that does neither of those things. Helium is not cheap. It runs somewhere between about a dollar seventy and two dollars seventy-five for a cubic foot, and a diver living under pressure goes through a great deal of it. The cost of the helium alone can reach up to about two hundred dollars an hour. The figure is large enough that the ships running these operations install reclaim systems to capture the gas a diver exhales, clean it, and feed it back, because at those prices you do not let your breathing gas escape into the sea. On the surface, the diver breathed for nothing. A few hundred metres down, breathing has a meter on it, and the meter runs fast.

Consider next Biosphere 2, the great glass experiment built in the Arizona desert at the start of the 1990s. The idea was beautiful and the ambition was total. Eight people would seal themselves inside roughly three acres of enclosed glassed-in landscape, complete with a miniature ocean and a rainforest and a farm, and they would live for two years on what the sealed world produced, breathing air that the sealed world's own plants renewed. It cost on the order of a hundred and fifty million dollars to build. And the air, the one thing everyone had assumed would take care of itself, did not take care of itself. Over sixteen months the oxygen inside fell from the normal twenty-one percent down toward fourteen, a level closer to what you would find high on a mountain, until the crew were laboring for breath. The cause turned out to be subtle: the rich soils the designers had used were alive with microbes that consumed oxygen as they worked, and the carbon dioxide they produced was quietly binding into the concrete of the structure itself, so the oxygen did not come back. In the end the managers had to pump pure oxygen in from outside to keep the crew safe, which is to say the sealed world failed at the one task we never think to ask of a world, the renewal of the air. Inside, the experiment lost most of its animal life. Of twenty-five species of vertebrate, nineteen died. Every pollinator died, so the crops that needed pollinating had to be pollinated by hand. A planet's worth of free services, withdrawn, and eight people discovered exactly how much those services had been worth all along.

Consider finally the International Space Station, the most expensive sealed habitat human beings have yet built, orbiting a few hundred kilometres above our heads. Up there nothing about life is free, and the accounting is exact because it has to be. Lifting a single kilogram of water to orbit costs in the neighborhood of twenty-one thousand dollars. A six-month supply of water for the crew, if you simply shipped it up, would run on the order of a hundred and thirty-five million dollars, which is why the station does not simply ship it up. Instead it runs one of the most sophisticated recycling systems ever engineered, reclaiming around ninety percent of the moisture in the crew's sweat and even their urine, purifying it back into drinking water, because at twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram you cannot afford to throw water away. The oxygen the crew breathe is manufactured on board by splitting water with electricity into its elements. Every breath up there is an industrial product with a cost attached. The astronauts know to the gram what their life costs to sustain. They are living, in the most literal sense, on an honest ledger, because the alternative to an honest ledger, at that altitude, is death.

These three are not thought experiments. The diver, the glass biosphere, the orbiting station: in all three the seal does the same work. It strips away the abundance that lets value hide, and the moment it does, the priceless reveals itself as the most expensive thing in the system. The air you are not charged for on Earth is not free. It is subsidized, completely and silently, by a living planet, and the subsidy is so total and so reliable that we have built an entire civilization on the assumption that it will never appear on any invoice. Seal the planet away, even a tiny corner of it, and the invoice appears at once.

## The mechanism, named

It helps to give the underlying thing a name, because once you can name it you start to see it everywhere. What the seal reveals, and what abundance ordinarily hides, is a particular failure in the way costs travel.

Most of the time, when we talk about a cost being hidden, we mean something fairly simple. Someone does a thing that imposes a burden on someone else, and the person doing the thing is not the one who pays. A factory pours waste into a river and the cost lands on the people downstream. A buyer and a seller agree on a price for a barrel of oil and shake hands, satisfied, and the agreement is honest as far as it goes, but it leaves something out. The two of them have settled the cost between themselves and ignored a third party who was not in the room and could not bargain, who absorbs a real cost and has no way to send anyone a bill. Call this cost severance: one party creates a cost, a second party bears it, and the line that should connect them, the line along which a bill would travel, has been cut. The cost is real and it is being paid. It is simply not being paid by the person who caused it.

When a sale goes through, the seller extracts a value and pockets a price. But the price covers only what the two parties could see and bargain over. There is very often a residue left behind, a value taken out of the world that the price did not account for, and that residue is owed to people who were not at the table. Sometimes they were not at the table because they lived downwind or downstream. Sometimes they were not at the table because they had not yet been born. Call that leftover the residual commons value: the worth that was drawn out of a shared inheritance and never paid back into it, because the only people who could have been paid did not exist yet, or had no standing, or simply were not asked.

Honest accounting, in the ordinary case, means finding a severed cost and re-attaching it. You trace the line back from the person bearing the burden to the person who caused it, and you make the second pay the first, or at least you make the cost visible so that a decision can be made with open eyes. That is hard enough, but it is familiar work, the work of pollution taxes and liability law and a hundred other instruments we already half understand.

The commons case is stranger, and it is the case this essay is really about. Here the problem is not that a cost was created and shifted onto someone else. It is that a value so complete and so evenly spread never acquired a price in the first place. The air did not become expensive because someone polluted it. The air was always beyond price, and we only ever discovered what it was worth at the moment scarcity arrived and the seal closed. The residual commons value of the atmosphere is not a damaged flow we can trace back to a culprit. It is the standing worth of a thing we have all been drawing down without anyone keeping the books. To put it on the ledger we cannot simply re-attach a severed line. We have to do something we have almost never done, which is to write a number where there has never been a number, and to write it not because the thing became scarce but because we can finally see that it was always priceless.

## The hundred-dollar barrel

Take a barrel of oil and put a price on it. Say the price is a hundred dollars. I choose the figure for the sake of the argument, not because it is today's quotation; treat it as a round illustrative number. That hundred dollars is a real and carefully arrived at sum. It reflects the cost of finding the oil and lifting it, the risk the producer took, the demand of the buyer, the going rate of the market on the day. It is an honest price for one thing. The trouble is that it is an honest price for the wrong duration.

The hundred dollars is a price for the present. It buys the present its tank of fuel, its plastic, its heat, its electricity, and in that exchange it is fair. But the thing being sold does not belong only to the present. The barrel was laid down over geological time and, once burned, it is gone for as long as a human being can meaningfully count. The price was negotiated by people alive now, on behalf of people alive now, for a thing whose absence will be felt by everyone who comes after. Nobody from the future was at the table. Nobody could be. And so the price, however fairly the living arrived at it, leaves out the entire population on whose behalf no one spoke.

There is a precision here worth being careful about, because it does real work in the argument. There is a difference between a renewable resource and a non-renewable one, and the difference is not about whether you should use the thing. It is about what kind of question its use poses. A renewable resource, a forest or a fishery or an aquifer that recharges, poses a question of rate. You may take from it forever, as long as you take less than it renews. The whole problem reduces to a comparison between two speeds: the speed at which you withdraw and the speed at which the thing replenishes. Get the rate right and you can draw on the resource for as long as the civilization lasts and never diminish it. This is the kind of commons that careful societies have learned, sometimes, to manage.

Oil is not that kind of thing. Oil does not renew on any schedule that matters to us. When you burn a barrel you do not draw it down faster than it refills, because it does not refill. You liquidate it. It converts from a stock that exists to a stock that does not, permanently, with no rate of extraction slow enough to make it sustainable, because sustainability is a statement about rates and there is no rate here, only a total. The hundred dollars buys the present its use of the barrel. It does not, and cannot, buy the barrel back for anyone who comes later, because there is no later barrel to buy. The transaction is complete and final in a way that no transaction over a renewable thing ever is. What the present pays, the future cannot be repaid, because the thing itself is gone.

This is why the honest price of a barrel is not a fact about the oil market. It is a fact about time. To price the barrel honestly you would have to include, somehow, the standing value of a thing that will not return, owed to people who do not yet exist and so cannot bargain for it. The hundred dollars sees only the living. An honest number would have to see the unborn as well.

## A lineage of near-misses

This is not a new thought, and I do not want to pretend that it is. The strange thing is not that the idea is original. The strange thing is how many powerful traditions have circled the empty plot of ground where this idea should stand, looked at it, named a piece of it, and then built their houses somewhere nearby. The pattern is consistent enough to be worth tracing, because seeing where each tradition stopped tells you where the unbuilt thing is.

Begin with Karl Marx, who in the first volume of Capital built an entire account of value extracted and not paid for. His surplus is precisely a value taken from one party and pocketed by another without a fair return. He saw the severed line clearly. But the line he traced ran between classes of people alive at the same time, between the worker and the owner, and he priced the extraction across the boundary of class. He did not price it across the boundary of time. The unpaid party in his account is the laborer in the factory, not the descendant three centuries downstream who inherits a drawn-down world.

Then there is Elinor Ostrom, who spent a career showing, against the conventional wisdom of her field, that ordinary people can and do govern a shared resource without either privatizing it or handing it to the state. Her book Governing the Commons, and the Nobel Prize that eventually recognized it, established that communities can sustain a fishery or a forest or an irrigation system across generations through their own rules. But the commons she studied so well were the renewable kind, the ones that pose a question of rate. Her genius was in solving the rate problem. The liquidated commons, the barrel that does not come back, was not the thing she built her tools for.

Then there is Herman Daly, who argued for decades that an economy is a physical thing inside a finite planet, that it has a throughput of matter and energy, and that this throughput cannot grow without limit on a world that does not grow. He warned, more clearly than almost anyone, that we were treating the planet's capacity as if it were infinite. But the warning was about scale and pace, about the danger of an economy too large for its host. It did not produce an invoice for a single foreclosed thing, a number you could write next to a particular barrel.

And there is John Hartwick, who in the late 1970s wrote down a rule with a quiet elegance to it. If you are going to deplete a non-renewable resource, he showed, you can keep your society's capacity constant across time by taking the rent you earn from the resource, the pure scarcity value, and reinvesting it permanently in something durable, so that what you draw down in one form you build back up in another. Hartwick saw the time problem more sharply than most. But his rule is about saving the rent, the scarcity premium, and reinvesting it. It is not quite an instruction to price the barrel itself, the whole standing value of the thing that will not return. He saved the interest, in a sense, and let the principal go.

So four traditions, each of which got close. Marx priced the extraction but across class, not time. Ostrom governed the commons but the renewable one. Daly warned about the throughput but wrote no invoice. Hartwick saved the rent but not the resource. Each circled the plot. None built the house.

Alongside the economists, a line of moral philosophers walked toward the same empty ground from a different direction, and got, if anything, closer. John Rawls, in A Theory of Justice, asked us to choose the rules of a society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which person in that society we would turn out to be. The device was meant to strip away the special pleading of self-interest. And Rawls saw that the veil could widen across generations as well as across the living, that a just arrangement has to account for people in times not our own, that we owe something to the future precisely because, behind the veil, we might have been born into it. Michael Sandel, in What Money Can't Buy, pressed on a related nerve, asking what happens to goods when we put them up for sale, how the act of pricing certain things corrupts or crowds out the values that ought to govern them. He was asking which things should have a price at all, which is the near neighbor of the question of what we lose when we price a thing for the present and forget the future it belongs to. And Derek Parfit, in Reasons and Persons, worked out with great care the strange logic of our obligations to future people, people whose very existence and identity depend on the choices we make now, and showed that the difficulty of owing something to someone not yet real does not dissolve the obligation. Three philosophers, walking straight toward the unbilled future. They named the obligation. They did not write the number.

There is a third group, and they are the ones who came closest of all, because they fought specifically about how to price the future, and the shape of their fight tells you exactly what they were and were not pricing. This is the long argument over the discount rate in climate economics. When Nicholas Stern produced his review of the economics of climate change, he used a very low discount rate, which is to say he treated harms to future generations as nearly as important as harms to us, and concluded that we should spend substantially now to prevent them. William Nordhaus, who would later win a Nobel for his work modeling the climate and the economy together, used a higher rate, closer to what markets actually pay, and concluded that we should move more gradually. Martin Weitzman complicated both positions by pointing out that the real danger might lie in the fat tail of the distribution, the small chance of a catastrophe so large that no ordinary cost-benefit arithmetic could contain it.

That argument has run for years and it matters enormously. But notice what all three sides are arguing about. They are arguing about how to value a flow of damage. They are pricing the harm that combustion causes: the floods, the heat, the failed harvests, the storms, the losses that pile up over the coming century because we put carbon into the air. They are debating how heavily to weigh those future harms against present costs. And that is a real and necessary debate. But it is a debate about the damage flow, not about the resource's absence. It prices what the burning does to the world. It does not price the simple fact that the barrel itself is gone, that a thing which existed no longer exists, that a stock has been converted to nothing and cannot be recovered at any rate.

This is the crucial point, and it is the reason the residual commons value survives even Stern's low discount rate. A discount rate works on a flow. It takes a stream of future costs and benefits and translates them into present terms, weighting the distant ones less. You can argue about how much less. But the absence of the barrel is not a flow. It is a standing fact about the present and every moment after it. The barrel is gone now, and gone in a thousand years, and gone in a million, equally and permanently. There is no future date at which its absence is discounted away, because its absence is not a future event to be discounted. It is a present and permanent condition. You cannot make a permanently missing thing matter less by pushing it further into the future, because it is missing at every point in the future equally. The discount rate, however you set it, slides off it. That is why the value the barrel's absence represents is not captured by any of the discount-rate combatants, however carefully they argue. They are pricing the wrong thing, not because they are careless but because the thing they are pricing, the damage flow, genuinely behaves like a flow, while the thing they are missing, the permanent absence of the resource, does not.

## McDowell County, run backward

So far the framework has faced forward, toward unborn people and undrilled barrels. But it runs just as well in reverse, on extraction already complete, and there is no better place to run it than a real one. Consider McDowell County, in the coalfields of southern West Virginia.

For a long stretch of the twentieth century McDowell County produced enormous quantities of coal, the high-grade Pocahontas coal that fed the furnaces and the steel mills and the power plants of an industrializing nation. At the mine mouth, around 1960, that coal sold for something like four or five dollars a ton. That was the price. Two parties agreed on it, the operator and the buyer, and by the standards of the transaction it was honest. The miner was paid, the coal was shipped, the furnace was fed. The county sent its wealth out into the country in railcars and got a few dollars a ton in return.

Now run the framework backward across that transaction and ask what the honest price would have been, once the severed costs are added back. The largest severed cost, by far, is carbon. Burning a ton of that Pocahontas coal releases on the order of two and a half tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, a higher figure than the national average because of the particular chemistry of that coal, about two point six one tons per ton burned against a national average closer to two point three three. To put a value on each of those tons of carbon dioxide we can use the social cost of carbon, the government's own best estimate of the damage each ton does, which in the Environmental Protection Agency's 2023 estimate runs around a hundred and ninety dollars a ton. Multiply it out and the carbon term alone comes to roughly four hundred and ninety-six dollars for every ton of coal. Add the rest and the honest price of that coal, the price that would have included the costs the original sale severed and shoved onto the future, is more than five hundred dollars a ton, around five hundred and ten dollars, nearly all of it carbon.

Consider that comparison plainly. Coal that sold for four or five dollars a ton carried a true cost above five hundred. The price the market saw was off, not by a margin, but by two orders of magnitude. And the gap was not fraud and it was not stupidity. The people buying and selling that coal were behaving rationally inside the only accounting they had. The cost was severed cleanly and sent forward in time, onto a world that would warm because of what those furnaces burned, and the bill for it is arriving now, addressed to no one in particular and so paid by everyone. McDowell County did not get rich. The wealth left in railcars and the costs stayed behind in other forms, and the largest cost of all left invisibly, in the smoke, to be paid a century later by people who never saw a lump of that coal.

That is the framework run backward: take a completed extraction, add back the costs the original price severed, and read out the honest number. It is the same operation as pricing the unburned barrel, only pointed at the past instead of the future.

## Forward and in reverse

Once you see that the accounting runs in both directions, it is natural to give each direction an instrument, because the two jobs, though they share a logic, are not the same job.

Facing forward, the instrument is what I will call a foreclosure bond. Before you extract something that will not return, you price the future loss, the residual commons value owed to the people who come after, and you set that sum aside, bonded and held, so that the value drawn out of the shared inheritance is paid back into it rather than simply vanishing into a present transaction. The bond is the mechanism by which the future, which cannot attend the sale, nonetheless gets represented at it. You cannot bring the unborn to the table, but you can leave a chair, and a fund, for them.

Facing backward, the instrument is a restitution bond: you take an extraction already realized, you compute the honest price the original sale left out, and you build a mechanism to pay it back to those who bore the severed cost. This reverse operation is not abstract. It connects directly to one of the most carefully worked-out programs of backward accounting in American economic thought, the case for reparations developed by William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen in their book From Here to Equality. Darity and Mullen trace, with great precision, the wealth extracted from Black Americans across the long arc from 1619 onward, and the way that extraction compounds into the racial wealth gap that persists today. They show that the legacy is measurable, that you can read it not only in dollars of foregone wealth but in harder quantities, in the gap in longevity between Black and white Americans, a difference in years of life that is itself a measurable effect of a history of extraction. Their work is honest accounting pointed at the past, a restitution computed and made legible, and the restitution bond is simply the name for the instrument that would carry such a computation into practice.

There is a discipline I want to be careful to observe here, because it bears on what this framework can and cannot do. There is a difference between the costs that flow from extraction and the costs that flow from coercion, from violence done directly to a group of people. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not work that is mine to do, and I will not pretend to do it. That accounting belongs to the people who are continuing it, who carry the history in their own families and their own communities, and who have far better standing to set its terms than I could ever have. What the framework I am describing can do is reach such cases through their legacy effects, through the measurable economic residue that a history leaves in wealth and in years of life, the quantities Darity and Mullen lay out. It defers the direct pricing of the coercion itself to those whose work it properly is. The line matters, and I want to keep it.

## Two architectures: Norway and the Niger Delta

It would be easy to mistake all of this for a counsel of despair, a demonstration that honest accounting is impossible and that the future is always robbed. So it is worth setting two real cases side by side, because they show that the same physical act, the extraction of oil from the ground, can be run through two completely different architectures, with two completely different results, and that the difference is not in the oil or the geology but in whether anyone kept honest books.

Begin with Norway. In the years after the Second World War, Norway was not a wealthy country by the standards of its neighbors. Then, just before Christmas in 1969, a company drilling in the Norwegian sector of the North Sea confirmed an enormous find, the field that would come to be called Ekofisk, and Norway discovered that it was sitting on a great deal of oil. What happened next is the part worth studying. Norway did not simply spend the windfall. It built, over the following decades, an architecture designed precisely to keep the future at the table. It established a fund, the Government Petroleum Fund, in 1990, which was later renamed the Government Pension Fund Global, and into that fund it directed the revenues from the oil. Then, in 2001, it adopted a fiscal rule of remarkable discipline: the government would spend, in any year, only the expected real return on the fund, on the order of three percent, and would leave the capital itself untouched, inviolate, so that the principal kept growing while the country lived on the income.

The result is one of the most striking facts in modern public finance. The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars, which works out to more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every citizen of Norway. And the most remarkable thing about it is not its size but its durability. Across decades, through governments of the left and the right, under prime ministers of every stripe, no administration has raided the principal. The discipline has held across the entire political spectrum, which tells you something important: this is not a partisan arrangement that one side imposed and the other resents. It is closer to a shared inheritance the whole country has agreed to protect. In effect Norway took the rent from a non-renewable resource and reinvested it permanently in a durable form, which is John Hartwick's rule made real at the scale of a nation. It is the foreclosure bond, the forward ledger, built and operated by a sovereign state. It is also, not incidentally, a reserve fund, a quantity held back against the future, which is a theme we will return to.

Now set beside it the Niger Delta. Oil has been pumped from the Niger Delta since the 1950s, and over the decades that extraction has generated hundreds of billions of dollars in revenue. But the architecture there was the opposite of Norway's. The revenue flowed out, to companies and to a distant central government, while the costs stayed behind and fell on the people of the Delta, who had not sold the oil and could not bill anyone for what its extraction did to them. The costs took the form of spilled crude in the creeks and the farmland, of gas flared off into the air around the clock, of fisheries and waterways foreclosed by contamination. The people bearing those costs had no fund, no bond, no chair at the table. When the writer Ken Saro-Wiwa led a movement of the Ogoni people to protest what was being done to their land, he and eight others were executed in 1995, an act that drew Nigeria's suspension from the Commonwealth and the condemnation of much of the world. Years later, a United Nations environmental assessment of Ogoniland found that simply beginning to clean up the damage would cost on the order of a billion dollars and take twenty-five to thirty years, an initial figure for an initial phase of a restoration whose full scope was larger still.

I want to be precise about what this comparison is and is not. It is not an argument against extracting oil. It is not a story with a villain and a hero, Norway good and someone else wicked. The point is architectural. The same physical act, lifting oil from beneath the ground, was run through two different sets of books. In one, the future and the bearers of cost were represented, a reserve was set aside, the principal was protected, and the wealth became a durable inheritance. In the other, the cost was severed completely from the revenue, borne by people who had no way to bill it back, and set aside in no fund at all. One question runs through both cases and it is the only question that matters: when the cost outlives the sale, who carries it, and is anything set aside to carry it with. That is a question about architecture, about how the books are kept, not about whether oil is good or bad. It is not a political program. It is an accounting standard.

## Off-world: the framework in operation

Now return to the airlock, and walk all the way through it this time. I am still not predicting anything. The sealed world is a lens, a way of seeing the framework run forward in real time, in a place stripped of the abundance that lets value hide on Earth. But it is a lens worth holding up to the eye for a while, because a place that cannot afford the mask of abundance shows you, with perfect clarity, what honest accounting looks like when it is simply the way an economy works.

Imagine, then, not measuring a sealed habitat but living in one. Imagine it as a home and a working economy, a settlement of some thousands of people on another world, sealed against a vacuum or a poisoned sky, going about the ordinary business of life. The first thing you would notice, the thing that would seem strange for a week and then disappear into the background, is that every commons the Earth gives away for free is here a metered quantity with a price. Air is billed by usage. A breath is the smallest unit of a real account. Water is recycled and the recycling has a cost, so a litre of clean water carries a charge that reflects the energy and the machinery that cleaned it. Even the removal of the carbon dioxide you breathe out is a service someone provides and someone pays for, because in a sealed volume your exhaled carbon dioxide is a pollutant that must be actively stripped from the air before it accumulates to a level that would harm you. The carbon you load into the shared atmosphere is not someone else's problem here. It is metered, billed, and scrubbed, and the bill comes to you.

This is not a dystopia and it is not a utopia. It is simply an economy in which the mask of abundance cannot operate, because there is no abundance to wear it. On Earth the planet's free services are so total that no meter ever runs and no number ever appears, and we mistake the silence for the absence of a cost. Inside the seal there is no silence. Every priceless thing has a price because every priceless thing is scarce, manufactured, and finite, and the books have to balance or people die. The colony's economy is the framework running forward, in real time, as the ordinary operating ledger of a world. It is McDowell County's books and Norway's fund and the unburned barrel's true price, all collapsed into a single daily reality where the residual commons value of air and water and a stable internal climate is not a philosophical abstraction but a line item you pay every day.

And here the lens closes a circle. At the start, we used the seal as an instrument, a device to see a value we could not otherwise see. We sealed a corner of the world and watched the priceless reveal its price. Now we are living inside the seal, and we are not merely seeing the value. We are paying it. The thought experiment has turned from a microscope into a home. The same fact that the diver and the biosphere and the orbiting station revealed to us as a surprise is, for the colonist, simply the weather, the unremarkable condition of being alive in a place too sealed to let abundance hide anything.

Now widen the lens to its other edge, far from any settlement, and consider an asteroid miner. Out beyond the worlds with people on them, in the empty volume between the planets, there is a rock made largely of metal, and someone has come to take the metal. Here something interesting happens to the framework, because here the conditions that make the framework demand a price are almost entirely absent. There is no community downstream of this rock to bear a severed cost. There is no ecosystem here to foreclose, no fishery to poison, no atmosphere to load with carbon, no unborn population whose inheritance this iron represents, because no one was ever going to inherit a nameless rock in the dark. The residual commons value of the asteroid's iron is, as near as makes no difference, zero. Cost severance requires someone on the far end of the severed line, and here there is no one.

So the framework, run honestly, does not say stop. It says extract freely. Take the iron. If a comet swings briefly into reach and will not return for a thousand years, do not husband it cautiously as though it were a renewable forest; extract it aggressively while it is there, because the entire logic of holding back, of taking less than the thing renews, of leaving value for those who come after, depends on there being a renewal, or a someone, and here there is neither. Honest accounting is not a reflex toward restraint. It is a reflex toward seeing the real cost, and where the real cost to others is genuinely zero, the honest answer is to take what you need without apology.

And yet. On the rock itself, in the very same operation, the framework says something that sounds like the opposite, and is not. You must hold reserves. The miner working that asteroid has to keep back enough fuel to leave it, enough air to breathe until departure, enough water and power and life-support to be certain of getting off the rock and home, or onward to the next world. The reserve is not held for some downstream community, because there is none. It is held for the miner's own future self, for the person who will need to leave and must not discover, too late, that the margin is gone. The very same ledger that says take the iron freely says hold the air in reserve, and there is no contradiction between the two, because both are simply statements of the real cost honestly seen. Where extraction costs others nothing, extract. Where survival depends on a reserve, reserve. Honest accounting is not against taking. It is against taking blindly, against the failure to know what the real cost is and to keep back enough of what you cannot do without.

## Whatever economy you choose

It is worth being clear about what this framework is, because it is easy to mistake it for a rival to the economic systems we already argue about, and it is not one. It does not tell you whether to organize an economy around private markets or public planning or some mixture of the two. It is not capitalism and it is not socialism and it is not a third system competing with those. It is the accounting that any of those systems needs in order to function without ruining the future, the books that have to balance honestly whatever the politics on top of them happens to be.

You can see this by noticing how many different traditions, with wildly different politics, have arrived at versions of the same underlying discipline. Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics asks an economy to operate within two boundaries at once, a floor below which human needs go unmet and a ceiling above which the planet's systems are overdrawn, and to find the safe space between them. That is a way of insisting that the real costs and the real limits stay visible in the accounting. Elinor Ostrom's work on the commons, which we met earlier, is another version of the same insistence, a set of rules by which a community keeps honest track of a shared resource so as not to exhaust it. And the impulse is far older than either. Long before modern economics, societies built systems to know their stores and hold reserves against scarcity. Ancient China maintained what was called an ever-normal granary, a public store of grain meant to buffer the years of failure against the years of plenty. Ancient Egypt kept great state granaries for the same essential purpose. I will keep these references at the level they deserve, as evidence that the impulse to know one's real stores and hold a reserve is among the oldest functions of organized society, without inventing particulars I do not have.

What all of these share, across thousands of years and every kind of political arrangement, is not an economic system. It is a discipline. Each is, in its own way, an attempt by a civilization to know its real costs and to hold something back against scarcity. Capitalist or planned, ancient or off-world, the requirement is the same and it is prior to the politics: the ledger has to be honest, or the economy cannot see where it is going. You may organize the ownership and the exchange however you like. You may have markets or not, prices set by supply and demand or by a planning board or by a colony's life-support computer. But underneath whatever system you choose, the costs are real, and if the books do not record them, the system is making its largest decisions with its eyes closed, and the costs it cannot see will still be paid, by someone, somewhere, or by someone not yet born.

## Reserves on the longest horizon

There is one more turn of the screw, and it is the one that makes the off-world habitat something other than a flourish. The logic of reserves, of holding back enough of what you cannot do without, does not stop at the next generation. It extends, in principle, across the whole future of a civilization, and the future of this particular civilization has an outer edge that is not a matter of speculation but of physics.

Our Sun is slowly brightening. It has been doing so for its entire life and it will continue, and the increase, though imperceptibly slow on any human timescale, is relentless. On the order of five hundred million to a billion years from now, the Sun's rising luminosity will have heated the Earth past the point where it can remain habitable, boiling away the conditions that make life on the surface possible, long before the Sun reaches the end of its own life. And the end of that life is itself a date with a number attached: roughly seven and a half billion years from now, the Sun will swell into a red giant and is expected to engulf the Earth entirely. These are not round figures chosen for effect; they come from the work of astronomers modeling the Sun's evolution, and the first of them, the loss of habitability, arrives far sooner than the dramatic finale, in the first percent or so of the time remaining before the Earth is physically destroyed.

On those horizons, the off-world habitat stops being science fiction and becomes something more sober: a reserve held on the longest possible timescale. If the logic of honest accounting is that you hold back enough of what you cannot do without, then a civilization that intends to persist across deep time has, as its outermost reserve, the capacity to live somewhere other than a single planet whose habitability has an expiry date. The sealed colony, which we entered as a thought experiment about pricing air, turns out to be the same instrument as Norway's fund and the ever-normal granary, scaled up to the largest timescale there is. It is option-preservation across deep time, the reserve of last resort, the held-back margin that lets a civilization be sure of getting off the rock when the rock can no longer sustain it. The asteroid miner's discipline, hold enough air to be certain of leaving, is the species' discipline too, only the rock is the Earth and the timescale is geological.

I do not raise this to make the near-term arguments seem trivial by comparison. The opposite. The point is that the same discipline runs continuously across every scale of time, from the daily breath of a colonist to the survival of life across billions of years, and it is one discipline, not many. Know the real cost. Hold enough back. The horizon changes; the books do not.

## Honest books

Come home one more time, and breathe. The air is free. No panel asks you anything, no meter runs, and the question from the airlock seems, here in the open, like a story from another world, which is exactly what it is.

But you know now what the freedom is and what it is not. It is not the absence of a cost. It is a cost so completely and so evenly paid by a living planet that no bill has ever reached you, and that silence, that absence of a number, was the thing that nearly let us lose it without noticing. The mask of abundance hid the value of the air, and the water, and the climate, and the planet's patient work of taking our carbon back, not by making them seem cheap but by making them seem free, which is worse, because a thing that seems free is a thing we forget to count at all.

The whole of what I have tried to show is a single thing, repeated at every scale. Honest accounting is not a system and it is not a politics. It is a set of books that anyone can keep, and that everyone, at every scale, has to keep if the future is going to be paid for rather than robbed. A person can keep these books, deciding what a choice really costs before making it. A community can keep them, knowing the value of the shared things it lives on and holding a reserve against the lean years. A corporation can keep them, pricing what its extraction takes from people not at the table. A government can keep them, as Norway did, setting aside the value of a thing that will not return so that the future has a chair and a fund. And a sealed colony on another world keeps them by necessity, billing every breath, because a world too sealed to let abundance hide anything cannot afford the mask for even a single day. Different scales, the same books. Anyone who looks at the situation honestly, on this world or any other, arrives at the same recognition, and it is not a system and it is not a politics. It is just honest books, kept by someone willing to write a number where there has never been a number, because they have understood at last that the value was always there.

So breathe. The air is free, for now. The number was always there; the mask only kept us from reading it. Whether the freedom stays is not a fact about physics or a verdict already handed down. It is a thing we still get to decide, and we will decide it the way every honest reckoning is decided, by whether we are willing to see the cost while we still have the choice, and to keep the books before the airlock closes behind us and asks, too late, how we ever intended to pay.
