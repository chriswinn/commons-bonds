# The Mask of Abundance

Take a breath. Now think about what it cost you.

The honest answer is nothing. You did not pay for the air. You will not be billed for it. No one tracks how much you use. There is no meter on your chest, no monthly statement, no late fee if you breathe more than your share. Air is free, and it is free for everyone, all the time, everywhere on Earth.

We tend to read that price, zero, as a verdict. If a thing costs nothing, we assume it is worth nothing, or close to it. The cheap stuff is the throwaway stuff. The expensive stuff is the precious stuff. That is how prices teach us to think. A diamond is dear because a diamond is rare. Water is cheap because water is everywhere. We trust the number on the tag to tell us what a thing is worth.

But the air is the thing this rule breaks on. Air costs nothing, and you would die in minutes without it. So the zero on the tag cannot be a verdict on its worth. The zero is saying something else entirely, and most of us have never stopped to ask what.

Here is what it is saying. A price is not a measure of how much something is worth to you. A price is a measure of scarcity. It is the number that appears at the edge, where the next unit is hard to come by and someone has to decide whether it is worth the trouble. When something is scarce, the edge is sharp, and the price is high. When something is everywhere, there is no edge. There is no next-hard-to-get unit. There is just more, in every direction, free for the taking. And so the price falls not to a low number but to nothing at all.

This is the trap. We think a low price means low value. Sometimes it does. But sometimes a price falls to zero for the opposite reason. Not because the thing has almost no value, but because it has almost nothing but value, spread so completely and so evenly across the whole world that no one can find an edge to stand on and charge at. The air is worth everything. That is exactly why it is free. Its value is so total and so universal that the market, which only knows how to price the edge, cannot see it at all.

I am going to call this the mask of abundance. When something is abundant enough, its abundance hides its value. It wears a price of zero like a mask, and we mistake the mask for the face.

The air is not alone behind that mask. Stand it next to a few of its neighbors. Fresh water in a place that has plenty of it. A climate stable enough to plant by, the kind of weather you can build a life and a calendar around. The quiet work the planet does of carrying away the carbon dioxide we exhale and the carbon we burn, soaking it into oceans and forests and rock so that it does not pile up in the sky. None of these has a price. You are not billed for a livable climate. No invoice arrives for the ocean's patient absorption of your carbon. These things are free in the same way air is free, and for the same reason. They are everywhere, or at least they have been, and so the market has never found an edge to charge at.

We have a phrase for things like this. We call them the commons. The commons are the things no one owns and everyone uses, the shared inheritance we draw on without paying and usually without noticing. The air, the water, the climate, the planet's slow machinery for cleaning up after us. The commons.

Now, it is worth being precise here, because there is a familiar complaint that sounds like what I am saying but is not. People often point out that some valuable things are underpriced. A nurse is paid less than the work is worth. Good topsoil is treated as cheap dirt until it washes away and you understand what you had. Those are real problems, and they are worth caring about. But they are problems of price being too low. The nurse has a wage. The topsoil has a market value, even if it is too small. There is a number, and the number is wrong, and you could in principle argue it upward.

The commons are stranger than that. The air does not have a price that is too low. The air has no price at all. The stable climate is not underpriced. It is unpriced. And a thing with no price is not merely cheap. It is invisible. It does not appear on any ledger, in any budget, in any calculation of profit or loss. The one instrument our whole economy trusts to tell us what matters, the price, simply returns nothing when you point it at the commons. Not a small number. Nothing. And so the most valuable things on Earth are, economically speaking, not there at all.

That is the problem this essay is about. And I want to name plainly, right at the start, what I am trying to do with it. I am going to build a way of keeping books honestly, a way of accounting that works on this world and would work on any other. The whole job of it is to take the costs and the values that are currently hidden, the ones no price ever captured, and make them visible. To put them on the ledger where they belong. That is all the essay claims to do. Make the invisible costs visible, so that whatever we decide to do next, we are at least deciding with our eyes open.

To do that, I need a way to make the mask come off. I need a way to force the hidden value of the commons to show itself. And it turns out there is one. You seal the system.

## What happens when you seal the system

Imagine, just as a thought, a sealed world. A habitat somewhere off the Earth, a dome or a buried base, with a hard shell between the people inside and the dead vacuum outside. Nothing comes in and nothing goes out unless someone hauls it there or makes it on the spot. I am not predicting this. I am not forecasting colonies. I am using the sealed world as a lens, a way of looking, because of one thing it does that nothing on Earth can do. It puts a wall around the commons.

On Earth the air has no edge. In a sealed habitat it does. There is exactly this much air, and when it is used up there is no more, not for free, not from anywhere, until someone makes more at real cost. The same goes for the water, which must be caught and cleaned and used again. The same goes for the carbon dioxide everyone breathes out, which on Earth the planet quietly carries away and which inside a sealed shell will poison everyone if it is not scrubbed from the air at the expense of power and machinery. Seal the system, and every one of those free Earth commons acquires the one thing it never had down here. An edge. A point where the next unit is genuinely hard to get. And the moment a thing has an edge, it has a price.

This is the trick. Seal the world and the priceless things become the most expensive things there are. The air, which costs nothing here, becomes the single biggest line on the budget there. Not because its value changed. Its value was always total. Sealing the world did not create the value. It revealed it, by giving abundance an edge it never had, and so making the mask fall off.

I said this was a thought experiment, and it is. But I want to anchor it in three real places where the seal already exists and the bill already arrives, so you can see this is not a fantasy. It is a thing we already know how to make happen, on small scales, when we have to.

The first is deep underwater. When divers work far down, on oil rigs and salvage and the like, they cannot breathe ordinary air. Go deep enough and the ordinary nitrogen in air starts to act like a drug, fogging the mind, and the oxygen itself, helpful as it is at the surface, turns harmful under that much pressure. So saturation divers breathe a special mix, mostly helium with a little oxygen, and helium is not cheap. It runs a couple of dollars a cubic foot, which adds up fast when you are breathing continuously, on the order of a hundred dollars an hour or more for the helium alone. The cost is real enough that the ships run reclaim rigs to capture the breathed-out helium and use it again, because letting a breath escape is letting money escape. Down there, sealed off from the free air of the surface, a breath has a price. You can read it off an invoice.

The second is a famous experiment from the early 1990s called Biosphere 2. Eight people sealed themselves inside about three acres of glassed-in, self-contained world that cost around a hundred and fifty million dollars to build, and tried to live for two years on what the sealed system could provide. The thing everyone remembers is what happened to the air. Over sixteen months the oxygen quietly fell, from the normal twenty-one percent of the air down toward fourteen, low enough that the crew were suffering for it. The microbes in the soil were breathing it, and the carbon dioxide they produced was binding chemically into the concrete of the structure and not coming back as oxygen the way it would in a natural cycle. In the end the managers gave up and pumped oxygen in from outside. Of the twenty-five small animal species, nineteen died. Every pollinating insect died, so the crops had to be pollinated by hand. Here was a sealed world built on purpose by serious people, and the free Earth commons it could not reproduce, breathable air first among them, nearly broke it. On Earth the air is free. Inside Biosphere 2 it was the thing that failed, and you could measure its value precisely by the desperation when it ran short.

The third is overhead right now. The International Space Station orbits a few hundred kilometers up, and it is the most thoroughly sealed human habitat we have ever run for long. Everything costs there in a way that is hard to imagine down here. Lifting a single kilogram of water up to orbit costs something like twenty-one thousand dollars. A six-month supply of water for the crew, if you simply shipped it all up, would run on the order of a hundred and thirty-five million dollars. So they do not simply ship it. They recycle about ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine back into clean water, because at twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram you cannot afford to throw a drop away. They make their oxygen by splitting water with electricity. On the station, water and air and the carrying-away of waste are not background facts of life. They are the central engineering problem and the central expense. The commons that Earth hands out for free are, up there, the whole budget.

Three sealed systems, three places where the mask is already off. In each one the lesson is identical. The value of the commons did not change when we sealed the world. It was always that high. What changed is that sealing the world gave it an edge, and the edge gave it a price, and the price let us finally see what was there all along.

So now we have our instrument. The seal is how we make hidden value visible. The next job is to describe, in plain terms, what exactly was being hidden, and how. Because once you can name the mechanism, you can look for it everywhere, sealed world or not.

## Naming the mechanism

Let me build the idea up one piece at a time, because the whole framework rests on it.

Start with the simplest unfair thing in the world. Someone does something that creates a cost, and someone else ends up paying that cost, and the person who paid has no way to send the bill back to the person who caused it. A factory pours waste into a river, and the people downstream lose their fishing and their clean water, and they cannot bill the factory for it. That is the shape of the thing. A cost gets created in one place and lands in another, and the connection between them is cut. The bill cannot find its way home.

I am going to give that shape a name and use it for the rest of the essay. Call it cost severance. A cost is severed when it is cut loose from the person or the business that caused it, and floats off to land on someone who did not. The factory got the benefit. Someone downstream got the cost. The line between them was severed, and so the price the factory charged for its goods never included the real cost of making them. The price lied, not on purpose necessarily, but lied all the same, because part of the true cost was off the books, carried by people who never agreed to carry it.

Now hold that next to a sale. When a barrel of oil is sold, or a ton of coal, the seller takes value out of the ground and out of the world and turns it into money. Call that value extraction, which is just the plain thing it sounds like. Value is pulled out and sold. There is nothing automatically wrong with that. We extract value all the time, and most of it is fine. But sometimes, when the extraction is severed from some of its costs, the sale leaves something behind. After the money has changed hands and the deal is closed, there is still some worth that was taken and never paid for. Worth that belonged, in some real sense, to people who were not in the room when the deal was struck. People downstream. People in the next county. People not yet born, who will inherit a world with that value already taken out of it.

That leftover, the worth taken and never paid for, owed to people who were not at the table, I am going to call the residual commons value. Residual just means what is left over. It is the commons value that remains unaccounted-for after the sale is done. The part of the real price that nobody collected and nobody set aside, because the only people it was owed to had no seat at the negotiation and, often, had not even been born.

Here is where the two halves of the problem meet, and where the air comes back. Most cost severance is a matter of re-attaching a bill that got cut loose. The factory should pay for the river. The cost was real and someone bore it, and honest accounting means routing that bill back to where it came from. That is the ordinary case, and it is hard enough.

But the commons case is stranger, and the breath shows you why. The value of the air was never severed from a price, because the air never had a price to be severed from. Its worth is so total and so evenly spread that no market ever attached a number to it in the first place. So when we burn fuel and load the sky with carbon and slowly erode the climate's stability, we are not failing to pay a bill that exists. We are drawing down a value so complete it never acquired a price, a value that stays invisible right up until the moment scarcity arrives and reveals it. The sealed habitat reveals it on purpose, with a wall. A changing climate reveals it the slow and involuntary way, by making the stable climate scarce. Either way, what gets unmasked is the same thing. A residual commons value that was always there, always enormous, and always, until the seal closed or the scarcity arrived, completely off the books.

So honest accounting has two jobs, not one. The familiar job is re-attaching severed costs, sending the river's bill back to the factory. The deeper job, the one the commons forces on us, is putting a number on a value so abundant it never had a number, before the loss of it teaches us the number the hard way. Hold both of those in mind. The rest of the essay is just these two jobs, run on bigger and bigger stages.

## The hundred-dollar barrel

Let me make this concrete with a single barrel of oil, and let me pick a price for it so we can do the arithmetic out loud. Say the barrel sells for a hundred dollars. I am choosing that number to think with, not reporting today's market. A hundred dollars, round and easy.

What does that hundred dollars actually buy, and what does it leave unpaid?

It buys the present its tank of gas. That is the honest description of it. The hundred dollars covers the cost of finding the oil, pumping it, refining it, shipping it, and a profit on top, and it delivers to whoever pays the use of that energy now, today, this year. For the present moment, the price is more or less fair. You pay a hundred, you get the energy, both sides walk away content.

But the thing you bought does not belong only to the present. The oil took the Earth tens of millions of years to make, pressing ancient life into rock under heat and time, and there is a fixed amount of it. When you burn that barrel, it is gone. Not gone like a harvested field that grows back next season. Gone like a word spoken into the air, gone forever, in any timescale that matters to a human being or a human civilization. The barrel you burned is subtracted from the total the Earth will ever have, and nothing you can do puts it back. The hundred dollars bought the present its energy. It did not, and could not, buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after. Those people, the ones downstream in time, paid nothing and received nothing and lost a barrel anyway. That loss is residual commons value, owed to people not at the table, and the hundred-dollar price never saw it.

This is the place to be careful about a distinction that does a lot of quiet work, so let me draw it slowly.

There are renewable things and there are non-renewable things, and the difference is not a slogan, it is a difference in what honest accounting even asks of you.

Take a forest, or a fishery, or healthy topsoil. These renew. The forest grows back, the fish breed, the soil rebuilds, as long as you do not take faster than they replenish. With a renewable thing, the question honest accounting asks is a question of rate. How fast are you taking, compared to how fast it grows back? Take less than it breeds and you can take forever, generation after generation, and never run the account down. There is a sustainable speed, and the whole game is staying under it. The resource is not the problem. The pace is.

Oil is not like that, and neither is coal, nor any of the things we dig up that took geological time to form. They do not renew on any human scale. There is no rate slow enough. If you take a single barrel, that barrel is gone, full stop, and the only question is whether you take the rest fast or slow. Slowing down does not make it sustainable. It only makes the ending take longer to arrive. A non-renewable resource is not being harvested. It is being liquidated, sold off out of a fixed inheritance that does not refill. And that changes what honest accounting must do. With the forest you ask about rate and you can in principle pass the forest on intact forever. With the barrel there is no rate that preserves it, because using it at all uses it up. So the residual commons value of the barrel cannot be protected by slowing down. It can only be acknowledged, priced, and somehow set aside, or it is simply taken from the future with nothing left in its place.

The hundred dollars buys the present its tank of gas. It does not buy the barrel back. Keep that line. We are going to need it.

## A field that several people walked across

I am not the first person to stand near this idea. It is worth a moment to say who else has, because the striking thing is how many serious thinkers walked right up to the edge of this empty plot of ground and, for one reason or another, did not build the particular thing on it that I am describing. I will keep this brief, a few names per cluster, because the point is the pattern, not a full tour.

Start with the people who saw that prices hide value. More than a century and a half ago Karl Marx argued, in his enormous book Capital, that the price of goods hides the unpaid value of the labor that made them, a surplus skimmed off the workers. He was pointing at hidden value cut loose from the people it was owed to, which is cost severance by another name. But the people he was concerned with were the living workers of his own time. His hidden value was about class, in the present. He was not looking down the corridor of time at people not yet born, and he was not looking at the barrel that does not grow back.

Come closer to our own day and you reach Elinor Ostrom, who won a Nobel for showing how real communities, fishers and herders and irrigators, actually govern shared resources without either privatizing them or having a government run them. Her commons were the renewable kind, the pasture and the fishery, the things you can keep forever if you manage the rate. She solved, beautifully, the problem of the renewable commons. She did not take up the liquidated one, the barrel of oil that no good management can make sustainable, because no rate saves it.

Around the same conversation stood Herman Daly, an economist who spent his career warning that an economy cannot grow forever inside a finite planet, that there is a throughput of material and energy that the Earth cannot indefinitely supply or absorb. He was right, and he was early, and he was largely ignored. But his warning was about the whole flow, the total throughput. He did not write the invoice for one specific foreclosed thing, one barrel taken from one future, in a way you could add up and set aside.

And there was John Hartwick, who in the 1970s wrote down a rule that comes closest of all the economists to what I am after. Hartwick's rule says, roughly, that if you are going to live off a non-renewable resource, you should not spend all the money you make from it. You should take the rent, the pure profit from selling off the inheritance, and reinvest it, so that when the resource is gone there is a lasting asset left in its place. Save the rent, not the resource. That is exactly the right instinct. But notice the gap. Hartwick saves the money. He does not, in the end, price the barrel itself, the residual value owed to the future for the simple fact of its absence. Saving the rent is the closest near-miss in the whole field, and it is still a near-miss.

Then there is a different group of people, not economists but moral philosophers, who circled the same ground from the side of fairness rather than the side of price.

The philosopher John Rawls asked us to imagine designing the rules of a society from behind what he called a veil of ignorance. Behind the veil you do not know who you will be in the society you are building, rich or poor, strong or weak, and so, he argued, you will choose rules that are fair to everyone, because any one of them might turn out to be you. It is a powerful way to find fair rules. And you can widen the veil. If behind it you also do not know which generation you will be born into, the present one that gets the cheap oil or some future one that inherits the emptied ground, then you would surely build rules that do not let the present strip the future bare. Rawls did not run his veil hard at the question of resources across time, but the tool is built for it.

Another philosopher, Michael Sandel, wrote a book about the kinds of things that money should not be able to buy, about how turning certain goods into commodities corrupts them or degrades them. He was pressing on the idea that the market is not a neutral measuring stick, that some values are damaged by being priced. That is cousin to the mask of abundance, the sense that the market's silence about a thing is not the same as the thing being worthless.

And the philosopher Derek Parfit, in a famously difficult book about reasons and persons, worked through the strange puzzles of what we owe to people who do not yet exist, future people whose very identities depend on what we do now. He took seriously, as carefully as anyone ever has, the idea that obligations reach across time to people not yet born. That is the exact population the residual commons value is owed to.

So the moral philosophers built the case that we owe something to the future, and the economists built the tools for pricing scarce resources, and somehow the two halves never quite clicked together into a single number you could put on a barrel and set aside.

Finally there is the one place where people did try to put a number on the future, and it became a battleground. The question was how to value future harm from burning carbon, and the fight was over something called the discount rate, which is the rate at which you shrink the value of future costs to compare them with present ones. The economist Nicholas Stern, in a major review for the British government, used a very low discount rate, which is to say he counted future harm as nearly as important as present harm, and concluded that strong action now was well worth the cost. The economist William Nordhaus, who later won a Nobel, used a higher rate, treating future harm as worth substantially less in today's terms, and concluded for slower, gentler action. A third economist, Martin Weitzman, argued that both were missing the point, that the real reason to act is the small but real chance of a genuine catastrophe, a fat tail of disaster that no average estimate captures.

That whole argument, fierce as it is, is about one thing. The damage flow. The stream of future harm that comes from the carbon, the floods and droughts and heat and loss that combustion sets in motion. They are pricing the harm the burning causes downstream. They are not pricing the barrel's own absence, the simple fact that the thing is gone and will not return. And this matters, because it means the residual commons value I am describing survives even Stern's low discount rate. You can argue about how much to shrink a future flow of damages. But the absence of the barrel is not a future flow you can discount. It is a present fact. The barrel is already gone, the moment it is burned, and no discount rate reaches back and refills the ground. The thing the future is missing is not a stream of payments arriving later. It is a hole, here and now, that stays a hole.

So the field has been walked across many times, from many directions, by people far cleverer than the present occasion requires. What has been missing is the simple combined move. Put a price on the residual commons value, the worth taken and owed to people not at the table across distance and time, and set it aside. Let me now show that move running, first backward on a real place, then in both directions at once.

## McDowell County

McDowell County sits in the southern coalfields of West Virginia, and for a long stretch of the twentieth century it produced enormous amounts of coal. I want to run the honest ledger over a single ton of that coal, backward, on an extraction that already happened, because it is the clearest illustration I know of how big the hidden number really is.

At the mine mouth, around 1960, that ton of coal sold for something like four or five dollars. That is the price the present paid, and by the logic of its moment it was a real price. The coal got dug, the buyer got energy, money changed hands.

Now add back the severed costs, the bills that were cut loose and never sent home, and see what the honest price comes to. When you do the arithmetic carefully, the honest price of that ton of coal is more than five hundred dollars. Around five hundred and ten, all in. And nearly the whole of that, the overwhelming bulk of it, is one thing. Carbon.

Here is where the five hundred dollars comes from, in plain steps. When you burn a ton of this particular coal, a high-carbon coal from the Pocahontas seams under McDowell, it releases about two and a half tons of carbon dioxide into the sky. Two point six one tons, to be exact, which is a bit above the national average for coal because this seam runs especially rich in carbon. That carbon dioxide goes up and stays up, adding to the slow loading of the sky that erodes the stable climate, the very commons we said the planet usually carries away for free.

To put a dollar figure on the damage one ton of that carbon dioxide does, economists use a number called the social cost of carbon, which is their best estimate of the total harm, spread across the whole world and far into the future, from releasing one ton of carbon dioxide. In 2023 the United States Environmental Protection Agency put that number at around a hundred and ninety dollars per ton. Multiply it out. Two point six one tons of carbon dioxide, times a hundred and ninety dollars of damage per ton, comes to roughly four hundred and ninety-six dollars. Add the smaller severed costs and you arrive at more than five hundred dollars for the honest price of a ton that sold for four or five.

Sit with the size of that gap. The coal sold for the price of a sandwich and carried a true cost of more than five hundred dollars, and almost all of the difference was the carbon, the loading of the sky, the residual damage owed to everyone downstream in space and time who had no say in the sale and got no share of the four or five dollars. The mask of abundance was on the whole time. The sky looked free. The climate looked free. The price of the coal said the carbon was worth nothing, because the sky had no edge and no price. And the honest number, run backward over the realized extraction, says the carbon was worth a hundred times the coal.

That is the framework run in reverse, on something that already happened. You take a finished extraction, add back every cost that was severed from it, and read off how much was really taken and never paid for. It is bookkeeping, in the end. Severe and unforgiving bookkeeping, but bookkeeping. The number was always there. We just never wrote it down.

## Forwards and in reverse

The McDowell example runs the ledger backward, over coal already burned. But the same ledger runs forward too, over extraction not yet done, and the framework has a distinct instrument for each direction. I will name them plainly, because the names carry the idea.

Run it forward, over a barrel or a ton about to be taken, and the instrument is a foreclosure bond. The word foreclose means to shut off a possibility, to close a door on a future. When you take a non-renewable resource, you foreclose on the future's use of it, you close that door for good. A foreclosure bond is simply the act of pricing that foreclosed future loss now, at the moment of extraction, and setting the money aside. You figure the residual commons value, the worth you are taking from people not at the table, and instead of pocketing it and walking away, you bond it. You hold it in reserve, dedicated to the future you are drawing down, so that something lasting stands in the place of the thing you took. It is Hartwick's instinct, save the rent, made into a real reserve and aimed at the real loss.

Run it backward, over extraction already done, and the instrument is a restitution bond. Restitution means making good on a wrong already committed, restoring something taken. A restitution bond prices an extraction that has already happened, the way the McDowell ton was priced, and sets aside the means to make good on what was taken from those who bore the cost. The McDowell ton is the toy version. The serious version is much larger.

The clearest and most rigorous work on the backward direction is not about coal at all. It is about people. The economist William Darity Jr. and the writer A. Kirsten Mullen, in their book From Here to Equality, lay out the case for reparations to the descendants of enslaved Black Americans as a matter of honest accounting. Their argument is, at its core, a restitution ledger. They trace the racial wealth gap, the vast and persistent difference in wealth between Black and white families in America, back to its roots in slavery beginning in 1619 and the centuries of severed costs that followed, value extracted from people who were owned, then excluded, then held back, with the costs landing on them and their descendants and the benefits landing elsewhere. They even point to measurable legacy effects that persist today, such as a gap in life expectancy between Black and white Americans, a difference you can put a number on, a present-day mark left by a severed history. Their book is honest accounting run backward at the scale of a nation's founding wrong, and the restitution bond, as an instrument, operationalizes the restitution piece of the broader redress they map out. The writer Ta-Nehisi Coates made the moral case for reparations in a widely read essay some years before; Darity and Mullen built the economic architecture under it.

I want to be careful and explicit here about a limit, because honesty about the limit is part of the honesty of the method. There is a kind of cost that this framework does not try to price directly, and I want to say plainly why. When the cost in question is the cost of coercion itself, of force and violence and bondage inflicted on a group, the direct pricing of that coercion is not mine to do. It belongs to the people who bore it and to those continuing that work, who have a standing in it that I do not have. So the framework reaches the coerced cases the way it reaches McDowell, through their legacy effects, the measurable marks left behind, the wealth gap and the longevity gap and the rest. It prices the legacy. It defers the direct pricing of the coercion to those whose work that is. That deferral is deliberate. A method that claimed to price everything, including the suffering of people it has no standing to speak for, would be dishonest in exactly the way the method is supposed to cure.

So the ledger runs both ways. Forward, a foreclosure bond, pricing the future loss before you take it and setting the reserve aside. Backward, a restitution bond, pricing an extraction already realized and setting aside the means to make it good. Same ledger, two directions, one underlying question, asked of every act of extraction. What was really taken, who was it taken from, and was anything set aside?

## Two countries, one act

Pricing the future sounds, when you first hear it, like a beautiful idea that no real government would ever actually do. So it is worth knowing that one of them did, and has stuck with it for a generation, across every change of leadership. The country is Norway, and it is the existence-proof that the future can be priced in practice and not just in essays.

Just before Christmas in 1969, after years of mostly disappointing wells, the people drilling beneath the North Sea confirmed an enormous oil field, later called Ekofisk, off the Norwegian coast. Norway had become, more or less overnight, an oil country. Now, the ordinary thing for an oil country to do is to pump the oil, spend the money, enjoy the boom, and discover a decade or two later that the easy years are over and there is not much to show for them. Norway, eventually, chose to do something else.

In 1990 the country set up a fund, the Government Petroleum Fund, to hold the oil money rather than spend it as it came in. In 2006 it was renamed the Government Pension Fund Global, which signals what it is really for, the long future of the whole population. And in 2001 Norway adopted a rule that is the heart of the whole thing. The rule says that in any given year the government may spend only the expected real return on the fund, on the order of three percent, the part that the invested money earns. The original capital, the money that came from selling the oil itself, is not to be touched. It stays whole, invested, growing, for good.

Look at what that rule does in the language we have been building. The oil is a non-renewable resource being liquidated, a barrel that does not grow back. The money from selling it is the rent, the value pulled out of a fixed inheritance. The fiscal rule takes that rent and refuses to consume it, holding the capital inviolate and spending only the harvest off the top. That is Hartwick's rule, save the rent, raised to the scale of an entire nation and made into law. It is a foreclosure bond at the level of a sovereign state. The present takes the oil, but it prices the future's stake in that oil and sets it aside, so that when the oil is gone the asset remains.

The result is hard to overstate. The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars. Divided among the people of Norway, that is more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every citizen, a reserve standing in for the oil that was sold. And the part I find most telling is the politics. Norway's governments have run across the whole political spectrum over these decades, left and right and center, one coalition after another, and not one of them has raided the principal. Not one has cracked open the capital to spend it. The discipline held through every change of government, which tells you the idea is not the property of one party or one ideology. It is just sound accounting that a whole country agreed to keep.

Now set beside Norway a place where the same physical act, pumping oil out of the ground, was run on the opposite architecture. The Niger Delta, in Nigeria, has had oil pumped from it since the 1950s, and the revenue over those decades runs to hundreds of billions of dollars. But the costs of that extraction, the oil spills across farmland and fishing waters, the gas flared off into the air, the foreclosed fisheries and ruined soil, fell on the people of the Delta, who did not sell the oil, did not see most of the money, and could not send the bill to anyone. The severance was near total. The value went out, the costs stayed behind, and nothing was set aside for the people who bore them.

When some of those people organized to protest, the response was brutal. The writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa, who led a movement among the Ogoni people of the Delta against the damage done to their land, was executed by the Nigerian government in 1995 along with eight others, an act that drew international condemnation and saw Nigeria suspended from the Commonwealth. Much later, an assessment by the United Nations of just one part of the Delta, Ogoniland, estimated that an initial cleanup would cost on the order of a billion dollars and take twenty-five to thirty years, and that was only the beginning of the bill, only one region, only the part that could still be cleaned.

Two countries, the same physical act, two completely different architectures, and one question hanging over both. When the cost of an extraction outlives the sale, when it reaches forward in time past the moment the money changed hands, who carries that cost, and was anything set aside to carry it with? Norway answered the question one way and built a reserve worth more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars a head. The Niger Delta had the question answered for it the other way and was left with the spills and the flares and a billion-dollar cleanup nobody set money aside for.

I want to be clear about what this comparison is and is not. It is not a villain story. It is not a claim that extraction is evil and should stop. Norway extracted oil too, enormous quantities of it, and the framework does not scold it for that. The framework is not anti-extraction and it is not a political program telling anyone which system to run. It is architecture. It is the difference between an extraction that prices its real costs and sets aside a reserve, and an extraction that severs its costs and walks away. Same act, two ledgers. One honest, one not.

## Off-world: the ledger running in real time

So far the ledger has run mostly over the past and over the Earth. Now I want to take it somewhere it has never been run, and watch it operate forward, in real time, on another world. I said near the beginning that I am not forecasting colonies. I hold to that. What follows is a lens, a thought experiment, a way of seeing the framework in motion by imagining a place where it would not be optional. But it is the most important section of this essay, because it is where the whole argument comes home. On a sealed world, honest accounting is not a reform you argue for. It is just how the lights stay on.

### The colony economy, and the airlock

Earlier I used the sealed habitat as a measuring device, a way to put a wall around the commons and force their hidden value to show. Now I want to stop using it as an instrument and start living inside it. Imagine the sealed habitat not as an experiment but as a home. A real off-world settlement, with people who live there, raise families there, work and trade and argue there, and run an economy among themselves the way any community does. Picture the day-to-day life of that place, and in particular picture its books, because a sealed world cannot afford to keep dishonest books for even a week.

You arrive at the airlock. And before you are let in, you are asked a question that on Earth would sound insane and on this world is simple practicality. How do you intend to pay for your air?

On Earth that question is meaningless, because the air has no edge and no price, and we have spent this whole essay establishing why. On the sealed world the question is the most natural thing imaginable, because the air there has the sharpest edge of all. There is exactly this much of it, and it is made and cleaned at real and continuous expense, and every breath you take is a breath someone had to provide. So at the airlock the mask of abundance is simply gone. There is no abundance to wear it. The value of the air is not hidden behind a zero. It is right there on the price list, because on this world the air finally has a price, and everyone living inside the seal pays it.

And it is not only the air. Inside the habitat, every single one of the commons that Earth gives away for free is metered and billed by usage, because every one of them is scarce behind the seal. A breath of air, drawn from a finite and carefully managed supply, is metered. A litre of water, caught and filtered and used again and again, is metered and billed. Even the removal of the carbon dioxide you breathe out, which on Earth the planet's forests and oceans do for nothing and which inside the seal must be scrubbed from the air by machinery that runs on power someone has to generate, even that is a service with a cost, metered and billed by the kilogram of carbon dioxide you produce. On Earth you are paid no mind for exhaling. On the sealed world, the carrying-away of your exhaled carbon is a line on your account, because behind the seal it is a real expense borne by the whole community, and an honest ledger sends that bill home to the person who created it.

Look closely at what that colony's economy is. It is not some exotic new system invented for space. It is exactly the honest ledger we have been building this whole time, turned into the ordinary day-to-day economy of a place. The same ledger that ran backward over the McDowell ton, that ran forward as Norway's reserve fund, that we kept calling honest accounting, is here simply the operating account of a town. The colony meters the air and the water and the carbon-scrubbing for the plainest possible reason. Behind the seal, those things are scarce, and a thing that is scarce and goes unpriced will be overused and run short and kill everyone, as the oxygen nearly did inside Biosphere 2. The sealed world cannot afford the mask of abundance, so it does not wear it. It prices the commons because it must, and in pricing them it runs, without ever calling it that, the exact framework this essay argues for.

And here is where the colony reconnects to the very first breath of the essay, and closes the loop. At the start, we used the seal as a way to see the hidden value of the commons. We sealed the world in our imagination so that abundance would acquire an edge and the mask would fall and we could finally glimpse what the air was worth. Now we are not looking at the seal from outside. We are living inside it. And the value we used the seal to see, we now simply pay, every day, breath by breath, litre by litre, as the ordinary cost of being alive in that place. First the seal let us see the value. Now the seal makes us pay it. It is the same value, the same ledger, the same air. The only thing that changed is which side of the wall we are standing on.

There is something almost gentle hidden in this hard arrangement, and it is worth naming. On the sealed world, no value is invisible, and so no cost is severed, and so no bill floats off to land on someone who did not cause it. The person who breathes pays for the breath. The person who fouls the air pays for its cleaning. The accounts are honest because they have no choice but to be honest, and a strange kind of fairness falls out of the engineering. The people not at the table on Earth, the downstream and the not-yet-born who pay for our severed costs, have, in the sealed world, a seat at the table, because the table is small enough that everyone is at it and the seal is tight enough that no cost can leave the room. The colony is fair not because its people are better than us but because its walls will not let them be worse.

### The asteroid miner

Now let me run the same ledger in what looks at first like the opposite direction, on a lone miner out among the asteroids, to show that honest accounting is not the same thing as taking less. This is the part that surprises people, and it is the part that proves the framework is about truth and not about restraint for its own sake.

Picture a miner working a metal-rich asteroid far out in the empty dark, alone, with no settlement nearby, no neighbors downstream, no ecosystem of any kind. Out there, who bears the cost of the extraction? Almost no one. There is no community to foul, because there is no community. There is no river to poison, no air to load with carbon, no fishery to foreclose, because there is nothing alive out there at all and nobody downstream to inherit a damaged anything. The asteroid is a dead lump of rock and metal in a vacuum. Pull the iron out of it and you have harmed no one, severed no cost from no one, because there was no one and nothing there to bear it.

Run the honest ledger over that, and the residual commons value comes out at very nearly zero. The cost severance is near zero, because there is no party to sever a cost onto. And so the framework, the very framework that priced a McDowell ton at five hundred dollars and built Norway a two-trillion-dollar reserve, says something that sounds shocking until you see why. It says, out here, extract freely. Take the asteroid iron, all of it. If a comet swings briefly into reach, a thing that will be gone again in a few years, take it aggressively while you can, because the window closes and nothing is lost to anyone by your taking it. The same accounting that demands a reserve against the future for a barrel of Earth oil demands almost nothing here, because here there is almost no severed cost and almost no one downstream to owe.

This is the proof that honest accounting is not anti-extraction and was never about taking less for its own sake. It is about taking the real cost into account, and where the real cost is near zero, the honest answer is to take freely. A framework that said take less everywhere, always, regardless of cost, would be a moral preference dressed up as accounting. The honest ledger is not that. It follows the real cost wherever it leads, and out at the lonely asteroid it leads to permission.

And yet. Even out there, with permission to take everything, the ledger does not fall silent. It changes what it is talking about. Because the miner, alone on that rock, must hold reserves. Not reserves against some downstream community, since there is none, but reserves for survival, for the simple brutal fact of being a fragile living thing in a place that wants you dead. The miner must keep back enough fuel to leave, enough air to breathe until the leaving, enough water and food and life-support to be sure, really sure, of getting off that rock and home, or on to the next world. To strip the ship of its reserves in order to carry a little more iron would be to win the extraction and die of it. So the same ledger that just said take the asteroid freely says, in the same breath, hold your reserves, because you have to survive the future you are extracting into.

Notice that these are not two different rules. They are one rule, the honest ledger, showing two faces because the situation has two parts. Take freely, because the cost to others is near zero. Hold reserves, because the cost to yourself, your future self, the self that has to make it home, is not zero at all and never will be. Honest accounting is not the rule take less. It is the rule know the real cost, all of it, the cost to others and the cost to your own future, and keep enough back to survive the thing you are doing. On a sealed colony that rule meters the air. On a dead asteroid that same rule frees the miner to take the iron and, in the next clause, forbids the miner to spend the last of the fuel. One ledger. It only looks like two because the world keeps changing the question.

## Whatever economy you choose, the ledger has to be honest

Step back now from the sealed worlds and the asteroids, because I want to be clear about what kind of thing this framework is, and what it is not.

It is not a rival economic system. It is not a plan to replace capitalism with something, or to replace planning with something else. It does not tell you whether markets or governments or communities should run an economy. It is more basic than any of those choices, and it sits underneath all of them. It is the accounting that any economic system, whichever one you pick, has to do if it does not want to fly blind. You can be a capitalist and keep honest books or dishonest ones. You can run a planned economy and keep honest books or dishonest ones. The framework does not care which economy you run. It cares only that the ledger tells the truth about the real costs, because an economy that cannot see its real costs is an economy that will eventually run itself off a cliff it never saw coming.

And once you see it that way, you start noticing that human civilizations have been groping toward this same honest ledger for a very long time, under many different names and in many different systems.

In our own moment, the economist Kate Raworth has proposed what she calls doughnut economics, a way of picturing the economy as living in a ring, a doughnut, with a floor below which people fall into deprivation and a ceiling above which we wreck the planet's systems, and the goal of staying in the safe and just space between. That is, at heart, an attempt to put the real costs and the real limits onto the picture, to make visible the floor and the ceiling that ordinary accounting leaves off the page. It is the honest ledger drawn as a diagram.

Elinor Ostrom, whom we met earlier, spent her career documenting how real communities keep honest books about their shared resources, tracking who takes what from the common pasture or the common fishery, and holding each other to a sustainable rate. That too is honest accounting, the renewable kind, done by communities for their commons.

And the impulse is far older than any modern economist. Long before there were economists at all, civilizations built systems to know their real position and to hold reserves against the lean years to come. Ancient China operated what was called an ever-normal granary system, a public store of grain meant to buy in times of plenty and release in times of scarcity, so that a bad harvest would not become a famine. Ancient Egypt ran great state granaries that gathered grain against the years when the harvest would fail. I will not pretend to know the fine particulars of those systems, and I will not invent any. The high-level point is enough and is solid. Across thousands of years and utterly different economies, the same instinct keeps recurring. Know your real costs. Hold reserves against scarcity. Do not let the abundance of the good years fool you into believing the lean years will not come.

Put them all in a row. Doughnut economics, commons governance, ancient granaries, a colony's air-meter, a sovereign oil fund. Capitalist, communal, ancient, modern, off-world. They are all, every one of them, the same thing wearing different clothes. They are all a civilization trying to know its real costs and hold reserves against the future. That is what the honest ledger is. It is not new and it is not mine. It is one of the oldest things people do, and the only thing this essay adds is the insistence on doing it completely, including for the commons that abundance has kept off the books, including across distances and across spans of time that no granary keeper ever had to reckon with. Whatever economy you choose, the ledger has to be honest, or the economy is flying blind.

## Reserves on the longest horizon

I have been talking about reserves for the next generation, and reserves for the people downstream, and reserves for a miner's trip home. I want to close the scale all the way out, because the logic of reserves does not stop at the next generation, and once you see how far it actually reaches, the off-world habitat stops looking like science fiction and starts looking like simple prudence on a very long clock.

Here is the longest horizon there is. The Sun, which makes all life on Earth possible, is slowly getting brighter as it ages. This is not a controversial claim or a distant guess; it is ordinary stellar physics. As the Sun brightens, the Earth gets hotter, and on the order of five hundred million to a billion years from now the Sun will have grown bright enough to make the Earth uninhabitable, the oceans boiling away, long before anything dramatic happens to the Sun itself. And much later still, around seven and a half billion years from now, the aging Sun will swell into a red giant and likely swallow the Earth entirely. Those are the two ends of the clock. Uninhabitable in something like a billion years. Gone in something like seven and a half.

Now, those numbers are so large that they slide right off the human mind, and it is tempting to wave them away as irrelevant to anything we should care about. But hold them next to everything we have said about reserves, and they stop being a curiosity and become the natural end of the argument. The whole framework is about not letting the abundance of the present blind us to the finiteness of what we are drawing down, about pricing what we take from the future and holding reserves against scarcity that has not arrived yet. Push that logic to its limit and you reach this. The Earth itself is a non-renewable resource on a long enough clock. It is a finite inheritance with an expiry date, abundant beyond all reckoning right now, which is exactly the condition under which the mask of abundance does its work. The planet looks infinite. It is not. It is the largest barrel of all, and it does not grow back either.

That is why the off-world habitat, which I have used all along as a thought experiment and a lens, is in the end not science fiction at all. It is the longest-horizon reserve there is. It is the species keeping enough back, holding open the option of survival, against a scarcity that is certain to come even if it is unimaginably far off. The miner holds reserves to survive the trip home. The colony meters its air to survive the week. The civilization that learns to live off Earth holds open the option of surviving the death of its only world. It is the same instinct, the same honest ledger, run on the longest clock the universe offers. Know the real cost. Hold reserves. Do not let abundance, even the abundance of a whole living planet, fool you into believing the lean times will never come.

## The mask comes off

Let me bring it back to where it started, to the breath.

Take one again, and notice that nothing about it has changed. It still costs you nothing. There is still no meter on your chest, no bill, no late fee. The air is still free, and on this world, behind no seal, it should be. That is not the problem. The problem was never that the air is free. The problem was that we read its price of zero as a verdict on its worth, and so we could not see it at all, and so we built an entire economy that treats the most valuable things in the world as if they were not there.

This whole essay has been one move, made over and over on bigger and bigger stages. Find the value that abundance or distance or time has hidden, and put it on the ledger honestly. We sealed a world to see the value of the air. We followed a hundred-dollar barrel and found the future's loss the price never paid for. We ran the ledger backward over a ton of McDowell coal and found five hundred dollars of carbon hiding behind four dollars of coal. We watched Norway price its future and build a reserve, and watched the Niger Delta have the same question answered the other way. We lived inside the seal and paid the value we had earlier only glimpsed. We followed a lone miner to a dead asteroid and learned that honest accounting can say take everything, and in the same breath say keep enough back to get home. And we ran the clock out to the death of the Earth itself and found the same rule waiting there. Know the real cost. Hold reserves. One ledger, on any scale, on any world, run in either direction.

And the thing I want you to carry away is how modest the framework actually is, underneath all the worlds and the centuries. It is not a new economic system. It is not a political program. It does not tell you to want less or take less or live smaller. It is just honest bookkeeping, the kind an individual could run over a single choice, the kind a community could run over its shared land, the kind a government could run over its oil, the kind a sealed colony would run over its air because it could not survive otherwise. The mask of abundance hides the number. Honest accounting takes the mask off. And the strange, almost reassuring thing is that the number was never invented in the taking-off. It was always there. The value of the air was always total. The future's stake in the barrel was always real. The carbon behind the coal was always worth a hundred times the coal. We did not create those numbers by writing them down. We only stopped pretending they were zero.

There is one more thing the breath reminds you of, and it is the only thing this essay leaves genuinely open. The air being free is not a fact handed down from nowhere. It is, in the end, a thing about the kind of world we keep. The freedom of the breath, the abundance that lets us take it without thought or charge, is not guaranteed forever. Biosphere 2 lost it in sixteen months. The Niger Delta lost a great deal of what it had. A changing climate is, slowly, putting an edge on commons that never had one. Whether the breath stays free, whether the abundance holds, whether we keep enough back of all the things that have no price, is not written in the stars or fixed by the market. It is a thing we still get to decide. We will decide it well or badly depending, more than anything, on whether we are willing to look at the real numbers, the ones the mask has been hiding all along, and finally write them down.

Take a breath. It is still free. Whether it stays that way is up to us.
