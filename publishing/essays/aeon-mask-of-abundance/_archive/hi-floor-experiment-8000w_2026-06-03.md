# The Mask of Abundance

Breathe in. Hold it a moment, then let it go.

You have just consumed something. Not metaphorically. Your blood has taken oxygen out of the air and handed back carbon dioxide, and you did it without thinking, without paying, without anyone billing you, the way you have done it every few seconds since the first breath you took and will keep doing until the last. In the time it takes to read this sentence you will do it again. The air is roughly a fifth oxygen, the rest mostly nitrogen, and it is everywhere you are. It is the most valuable thing you will use today, and it cost you nothing.

We are tempted to read that nothing as a verdict. A thing that costs nothing must be worth nothing, or close to it, or at least worth less than the things we pay for. The whole apparatus of modern life trains us in that inference. Price is the number we trust. It is the language an economy speaks to itself, the signal that tells a factory what to make and a farmer what to plant and a city where to build. We have built our civilization on the conviction that the price of a thing is, more or less, a measure of what it is worth.

But a price does not measure worth. A price measures scarcity at the margin. It measures how much someone will give up to get one more unit of a thing relative to how hard that one more unit is to supply. When the next unit is hard to come by, the price climbs. When the next unit is everywhere, the price falls toward zero, no matter how indispensable the thing is to your continued existence. Water is worth more to you than diamonds in any sense that matters to a living body, and you have known since school that diamonds cost more, and you have been told the resolution of the paradox is that water is abundant. That resolution is correct, and we draw the wrong lesson from it. We treat the low price as if it told us something about the value. It tells us nothing about the value. It tells us only that, for now, there is enough.

Air costs roughly zero not because it has little value but because it has nothing but value, distributed so completely that no market can find an edge to charge at. To charge for a thing you must be able to withhold it. You must own the next unit and be able to say: this is mine, and you may have it only on my terms. Nobody owns the next breath. Nobody can fence the atmosphere or meter the wind. The thing is too total, too evenly spread, too freely available to everyone at once for any owner to get a hand around its throat. So the market, which can only see things it can charge for, looks straight through the air and reports back that there is nothing there. Abundance posts a zero, and we misread the zero as a judgment.

This is the mask of abundance. The most valuable things we have are hidden from us precisely by how much of them there is. And the things behind the mask are not a short list. The air, yes. But also the water that falls from the sky and runs in the rivers. The capacity of the ocean and the forests and the soil to take carbon back out of the air and hold it, so that the climate stays inside the narrow band of conditions in which everything we have ever built was built. A stable climate itself, which is not a thing you can buy and is worth more than everything you can. These are the commons. They belong to everyone and so to no one, and they sit outside the price system not because we have failed to price them correctly but because they have never had a price at all.

It is worth being exact here, because the distinction is the hinge on which everything else turns. There are things that are underpriced, and there are things that have no price, and they are not the same kind of thing. A nurse is underpriced. The work she does is worth far more than what she is paid, and we know this, and we say it, and the market has a number for her labour even if the number is wrong. Topsoil is underpriced. There is a market in farmland, and the land is worth more than the price, but there is a price, and it can in principle be corrected. The commons are not underpriced. They are unpriced. There is no number to correct. A thing with a wrong price is a problem of calibration. A thing with no price is invisible to the only instrument we have agreed to trust. You cannot fix a measurement that was never taken.

So begin there. The mask of abundance is the way in. What it hides is a whole class of value that our accounting cannot see, and once you have learned to see it in the air, you can learn to see it everywhere, on any scale, run in either direction across time. The rest of this is an argument widening out from a single breath. It will go to the bottom of the sea and into a sealed glass dome and up into orbit. It will go down a coal mine in West Virginia and out to the North Sea and the Niger Delta. It will go, before it is done, to an asteroid and to the far end of the life of the Sun. But it is one argument the whole way, and the argument is this: an honest ledger, on any scale, on any world, run in either direction, has to find the value that abundance, distance, or time has hidden, and put it on the books.

## When the bill arrives

You cannot charge for the air because no one owns the next breath. But change the conditions, and ownership becomes possible, and the moment it does, the price appears, and it is enormous.

Imagine, as a lens and not a forecast, that you have sealed a system. You have drawn a boundary around some quantity of people and air and water and growing things, and outside the boundary is vacuum or poison or simply nothing, and nothing crosses the line that you do not carry across it. I am not predicting that we will live this way. I am asking you to seal the system in your mind so that the thing the mask hides becomes briefly visible, the way an eclipse lets you look at the corona of the Sun. Inside a sealed system, the next breath has an owner. Someone made that oxygen, or carried it, or paid to lift it, and the cost of it is now a line item. The priceless commons, the instant you seal the boundary, become the most expensive things there are.

You do not have to imagine this from nothing, because we have done it, in pieces, in three real places where the bill already arrives.

Go to the bottom of the sea first. A saturation diver, working at depth on a pipeline or a wellhead, cannot breathe ordinary air. Under that much pressure the nitrogen that makes up most of the air turns narcotic, clouding the mind like strong drink, and the oxygen, past a certain pressure, turns toxic. So the diver breathes heliox, a blend in which helium stands in for the nitrogen. Helium is not free. It runs somewhere between a dollar and change and a few dollars per cubic foot, and a working diver goes through enough of it that the helium alone can cost on the order of eighty to two hundred dollars an hour. The economics are punishing enough that the ships run reclaim rigs to capture the exhaled gas and use it again, because at those prices you do not simply vent your breath into the sea. Here is the air, the same air you breathe for free, except that the conditions have changed and now it has a price and the price is brutal. The value was always there. The seal made it visible.

Now go to the desert outside Tucson, to a structure of glass and steel that someone built in the early nineties to test whether eight people could live inside a closed world. Biosphere 2 enclosed about three acres under glass and cost something like a hundred and fifty million dollars, and it held eight crew members, and it was meant to be a small Earth, sealed, self-sustaining, a rehearsal. It did not stay sealed in the way intended, but the lesson it taught is the one that matters here. Over sixteen months the oxygen inside fell from the ordinary share of about twenty-one percent down to fourteen, low enough that the crew grew exhausted and short of breath, because the microbes in the rich soil were respiring faster than the plants could keep up and the carbon was binding into the fresh concrete of the structure and never coming back. In the end the managers pumped oxygen in from outside, which is to say the sealed world failed at the one thing the open world does for us without charge. Nineteen of the twenty-five vertebrate species inside died. Every pollinator died, so the crops had to be pollinated by hand. The air had stopped being a given. It had become a budget, and the budget did not balance.

Then go up. A few hundred kilometres over your head, the International Space Station is the most thoroughly sealed human dwelling ever built, and inside it the priceless commons carry their true price openly. Lifting a kilogram of water to orbit costs on the order of twenty-one thousand dollars, which means that a six-month supply for the crew, if you simply shipped it up, would run to something like a hundred and thirty-five million dollars, an order-of-magnitude figure that explains why nobody does it that way. Instead the station recycles. It reclaims roughly nine-tenths of the crew's sweat and urine back into drinking water, and it makes its oxygen by splitting that water with electricity. Everything your planet does for you silently, for free, behind the mask, the station does deliberately, expensively, and with constant attention, because the moment you seal the boundary the bill arrives, and the bill is the truth that the open air conceals.

Three sealed systems, one lesson. The value of the commons is not zero. It is total. The price of zero we read off the open air is an artifact of abundance, not a fact about worth. Seal the system and you see the number that was there all along.

## The mechanism

To see the commons clearly is to need a way of talking about how their value goes missing, and the language we need is a language of severance.

Start with the ordinary case, the one that does not involve the commons at all, because the commons case is stranger and the contrast sharpens it. A factory upstream makes a product and sells it, and in the course of making it, it puts something into the river, and the town downstream drinks the river. The factory has created a cost. The town absorbs it, in illness and in cleanup and in the loss of a thing that used to be clean. And here is the crucial structural fact: the town has no way to bill the factory back. The cost has been severed from the price. The person who created the cost and the person who pays it are different people, and there is no line connecting them on any ledger. The factory's books show a profit. The town's books, if the town kept books in this currency, would show a loss, and the two books never meet. This is cost severance, and it is the oldest trick in the economic world, older than economics. Someone creates a cost, someone else absorbs it, and the second party has no instrument to send the bill.

Most of what we mean by honest pricing is the work of re-attaching a severed cost. You find the cost that was pushed off the factory's books onto the town's, and you put it back, by a tax or a rule or a bond or a court, so that the price of the product finally tells the truth about what the product costs to make. That is the standard move, and it is a good move, and the framework I am describing does it. But the commons case is not quite this case, and the difference is worth slowing down for.

When a thing is sold, there is the price the buyer pays, and there is the value the buyer gets, and these are not equal. The buyer pays the price and keeps the difference, the value over and above the price, which is the whole reason to make the trade. Ordinary commerce runs on that gap, and there is nothing wrong with it. But sometimes the value of a thing is larger than the parties at the table, larger even than the buyer's surplus, because the thing carries worth that belongs to people who are not at the table and never will be, often people not yet born. Call that the residual commons value: the worth left over and unpriced after the sale is done, owed to those who had no seat at the negotiation.

In the factory case the cost was severed because the injured party could not bill back. In the commons case something deeper happens. The value was never severed, because it was never attached. A thing so total in its abundance that it never acquired a price cannot have its price corrected, because there is no price to start from. The residual commons value is revealed only when scarcity finally arrives, the way the value of the air in the sealed dome is revealed only when the oxygen starts to fall. Honest pricing in the ordinary case is re-attaching a cost that was pushed away. Honest pricing of the commons is stranger and harder: it is putting a number, for the first time, on a value so complete that the market looked straight through it.

Hold those two operations side by side, because the rest of the argument needs both. Re-attaching a severed cost. Pricing a residual commons value that abundance hid. One honest ledger has room for both, and a great deal of what follows is the same ledger run on different things, at different scales, in different directions across time.

## The hundred-dollar barrel

Take a barrel of oil and suppose it sells for a hundred dollars. I am using the round number as an illustration, not a quotation; the real price wanders, and the argument does not depend on where it sits today. A hundred dollars buys you the barrel. What does the hundred dollars actually pay for?

It pays for the present. It pays for the labour and the steel and the risk and the profit of getting the barrel out of the ground and to you, and it pays for the willingness of the next buyer to take the next barrel at roughly that price, and it clears the market today. The hundred dollars is a price set by present scarcity at the present margin, and as a present price it may be perfectly accurate. But the thing the hundred dollars buys does not belong only to the present. It extends to permanence. When the barrel is burned, it is gone. Not used up in the way a renewable thing is used up, with the possibility of return, but liquidated, converted to heat and carbon dioxide and scattered beyond any gathering. The hundred dollars buys the present its tank of gas. It does not, and cannot, buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after.

This is where a distinction that sounds technical turns out to be the whole moral architecture. A renewable resource and a non-renewable one pose different questions, and confusing them confuses everything. A renewable resource, a fishery, a forest, an aquifer that recharges, poses a question of rate. The thing breeds, or grows, or refills, and the honest discipline is to take less than it breeds, to live off the interest and leave the capital, so that the flow continues forever. There is a sustainable rate, and the work is to find it and stay under it. A non-renewable resource poses no question of rate, because there is no rate slow enough. Oil does not breed. The barrel you burn is not replaced on any timescale that means anything to a person or a nation or a species. It is not interest. It is capital, and burning it is not living off the estate but selling the house and spending the proceeds and calling the proceeds income.

So the hundred-dollar barrel hides a residual commons value the way the open air hides the value of oxygen. The present scarcity is on the ledger. The permanence is not. Somewhere in the gap between what the barrel is worth to the present and what it is worth to everyone who will never have it is a number that no market has ever written down, because no market is built to write it. The buyer pays the price and keeps the surplus, and the people not at the table, the ones who will inherit a world with that barrel already gone, are owed something for which there is no invoice.

## A lineage of near-misses

I am not the first to stand at the edge of this empty plot. Several great traditions of thought have circled it, and each saw something true, and none of them, as far as I can tell, built the particular thing the plot is shaped for. It is worth naming the near-misses, briefly, because the shape of what is missing comes clear from the shapes that surround it.

Marx priced the surplus. In the first volume of Capital he showed how value created by labour is captured by the owner of capital, the gap between what the worker produces and what the worker is paid, and built from that gap an entire account of class and exploitation. It is a theory about who takes the value and who makes it. But it is a theory about people alive at the same time, owner and worker in the same factory in the same year. It prices a severance across class. It does not price a severance across time, the debt the present owes the future for a thing burned and gone. Different axis.

Elinor Ostrom governed the commons, and won the Nobel for it, and her work in Governing the Commons is one of the few genuinely hopeful bodies of social science, because it showed by patient fieldwork that communities can and do manage shared resources without either privatizing them or having the state seize them. But the commons she studied are renewable. They are the grazing meadow and the fishery and the irrigation system, things that pose the question of rate, things you can govern toward a sustainable harvest. She solved the renewable problem with great beauty. The liquidated commons, the barrel that does not come back, is a different animal, and her tools, built for the flow, do not quite fit the thing that is simply gone.

Herman Daly warned about throughput. His ecological economics, his vision of a steady-state economy that does not grow its physical scale past what the planet can bear, named the deepest error of conventional economics, which is to treat the economy as a thing that can expand forever inside a finite world. He was right, and he spent a career being right against the grain. But his was a warning about the whole flow of matter and energy through the economy, the aggregate throughput, not an invoice for any single foreclosed thing. He told us the bath was overflowing. He did not write the bill for the one barrel.

John Hartwick saved the rent. In a spare and elegant argument he showed that a nation living off a non-renewable resource could keep its consumption constant forever if it took the rents from the resource and reinvested them in other capital, so that as the oil ran down the factories and schools and savings ran up to replace it. Hartwick's rule is real and it works and we will see it work. But notice what it saves. It saves the rent, the financial return on the resource, reinvested. It does not, on its own, price the barrel as a thing owed to the future in its own right. It is a discipline for converting a non-renewable asset into a permanent one, which is close to what we want and is not quite it.

Then the moral philosophers, who came at it from the other side, not from the ledger but from the question of who counts. John Rawls asked us to choose the rules of a society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which person in it we would turn out to be, and his veil, widened, slides naturally across generations: choose the rules not knowing when you would be born, and you will not let the present plunder the future. Michael Sandel, in What Money Can't Buy, pressed on the things that markets corrupt by pricing, the goods that are degraded the moment they are bought and sold, and taught a generation to be suspicious of the claim that everything has, or should have, a price. Derek Parfit, in Reasons and Persons, worked out with terrible patience the structure of our obligations to future people, including people whose very existence depends on what we choose now. They mapped the moral territory. They were not, mostly, in the business of writing the number down.

And then the economists who did try to write the number down, and fought bitterly over it. Nicholas Stern, in the review he led for the British government, used a low discount rate, treating the welfare of future people as nearly as weighty as our own, and concluded that the cost of acting on climate was small and the cost of not acting was large, on the order of a percent of global output to head off something far worse. William Nordhaus, whose DICE model also won a Nobel, used a higher rate, closer to the market's, and concluded that we should act more slowly. Martin Weitzman pointed past both of them to the fat tail, the small but real chance of catastrophe so large that ordinary cost-benefit arithmetic breaks down in its presence. The fight between them is the famous fight, and the discount rate is the famous battleground, and a great deal of intelligence has been spent there.

But look at what all of them are pricing. They are pricing the damage flow from combustion. The heat, the storms, the floods, the drought, the harm that comes from putting the carbon in the air. That is a real cost and re-attaching it is real work and I do not wish away a word of it. Yet it is not the thing the hundred-dollar barrel hides. The damage flow is the harm the carbon does on its way out. The residual commons value is the simple absence of the barrel, the fact that the thing is gone and will not return, owed to people who will never have it whether or not the carbon ever warmed a single degree.

And here is the point that the discount-rate war, for all its sophistication, slides past. A discounted flow shrinks as you push it into the future. That is what a discount rate does: a benefit a century out is worth a fraction of the same benefit today, and the fraction depends on the rate. At Stern's low rate, a hundred dollars of benefit a century from now is worth around twenty-five dollars today. At Nordhaus's higher rate, the same hundred dollars a century out is worth about a dollar and a half. The whole battle is a battle over how fast the future shrinks. But the residual commons value of the burned barrel is not a discounted flow. It is not a stream of benefits arriving year by year, to be summed and shrunk. It is a present absence. The barrel is gone now, and its being gone is a fact about the world from this moment forward, and it does not get smaller as you walk into the future, because it is not in the future. It is the standing condition of every future. It survives even Stern's low rate, because it is not the kind of thing a rate can touch.

That is the empty plot. Marx, Ostrom, Daly, Hartwick all built nearby. Rawls, Sandel, Parfit mapped the ground. Stern and Nordhaus and Weitzman priced the wrong thing with great care. The thing the plot is shaped for is a number on the residual commons value, the worth of the liquidated commons owed to the people not at the table, and the framework I am describing is an attempt, finally, to build there.

It is worth dwelling for a moment on why the plot stayed empty so long, because the answer is not that the thinkers were careless. It is that each of them was answering a question that was genuinely urgent and genuinely theirs, and the residual commons value of a liquidated resource was, for each, just out of frame. Marx was looking at the factory floor and the people on it now. Ostrom was looking at the meadow and the well that could, if governed wisely, feed people forever. Daly was looking at the whole metabolism of the economy and trying to slow it before it broke the world. Hartwick was looking at how a nation could keep itself whole as its oil ran dry. The moral philosophers were looking at the foundations of obligation, and the climate economists were looking at the bill for the heat. Not one of them was wrong to look where they looked. The empty plot is simply the place none of their questions happened to point, the intersection where a non-renewable thing, gone forever rather than merely flowing, meets a value so total it never acquired a price, owed to people who will exist but cannot speak. You can stand at the edge of a thing for a century, each generation seeing a different sliver of it, and still leave the centre unbuilt, not from failure but from the angle of approach.

## McDowell County

Bring the abstraction down to a real place, and run the ledger backward on something already done.

McDowell County sits in the coalfields of southern West Virginia, and for the better part of a century it sent its coal out into the world. In 1960 that coal sold at the mine mouth for something like four or five dollars a ton. That was the price. That was what the market said the coal was worth, and on the strength of that price the mountains were opened and the seams were worked and the coal was shipped and burned.

Now add back the severed costs and ask what the honest price was. The largest term by far is the carbon. Burning a ton of coal puts something like two and a half tons of carbon dioxide into the air, and if you price each ton of that carbon dioxide at a defensible social cost, in the neighbourhood of a hundred and ninety dollars a ton, the carbon term alone comes to around five hundred and ten dollars. Add the other severed costs, the things pushed off the mine's books and onto the lungs and the streams and the futures of people downwind and downstream and downtime, and the honest price of that ton of coal lands somewhere around five hundred and twenty-four dollars, in a range you could argue from the high five-hundreds to the low five-hundreds depending on your assumptions. Call it five hundred and twenty-four against a market price of four or five. The market saw a few dollars. The honest ledger sees more than five hundred.

That gap is cost severance and residual commons value made arithmetic. It is the framework run backward, on a realized extraction, on coal already burned, on a county that paid in poverty and illness and emptied hollows for a thing it sold for almost nothing. The honest number was always there. The market simply could not see it, the same way it cannot see the air.

## Forwards, and in reverse

Notice that the McDowell number was computed after the fact. The coal is gone. The ledger ran backward, from a realized extraction to the honest price it should have carried. But the hundred-dollar barrel ran the ledger forward, from a present sale to a future loss. The framework runs in both directions, and each direction wants its own instrument.

Run it forward and you get something I will call a Foreclosure Bond. Before you extract the non-renewable thing, you price the future loss, the residual commons value of the barrel that will not return, and you set that money aside, inviolate, so that the people not at the table are not simply forgotten but have something held in trust against the world you are foreclosing for them. You are pricing a loss that has not yet happened and bonding against it. That is the forward direction, and it is the direction of prevention.

Run it backward and you get a Restitution Bond. The extraction is already done, the coal already burned, the cost already borne, and you price it after the fact and you owe it, not to prevent a loss but to make good one already realized. The McDowell calculation is the raw material of a Restitution Bond: here is what the honest price was, here is the gap, here is the debt.

And the backward direction, pressed honestly, leads somewhere I want to be careful about. The clearest, most rigorous work on backward honest-accounting that I know is not about coal or oil at all. It is the reparations economics of William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen, whose book From Here to Equality is, read in this light, exactly a Restitution Bond worked out in full. They price the realized extraction of value across the long American history that begins in 1619, and they do it with the discipline the framework demands: the racial wealth gap is a measurable present quantity, the gap in wealth between Black and white Americans that did not fall from the sky but was produced by a specific history, and the gap in longevity is a measurable legacy effect, a difference in how long people live that is the downstream signature of that history written into bodies. They run the ledger backward on a realized extraction, and they put a number on it, and the number is owed.

I want to mark a line here and stand behind it, because it matters and because it is not mine to cross. There is a difference between pricing the legacy effects of coercion, the measurable wealth gap and longevity gap that persist into the present and can be counted, and pricing the coercion itself, the suffering of the people who were coerced, the direct cost of the violence done to them. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not my work to do. That pricing belongs to the people who carry that history and are continuing the work of accounting for it, and I defer to them on it entirely. The framework reaches the coerced cases through their legacy effects, the things that survive into the measurable present, and it stops at the coercion vector itself and leaves that pricing to those whose history it is. Darity and Mullen are doing that work. Ta-Nehisi Coates, in The Case for Reparations, built the argument's architecture in the public mind. My ledger learns its backward direction from theirs and knows the limit of where it may go.

So: one ledger, two directions, two instruments. Forward, the Foreclosure Bond, pricing the loss before it is realized and setting the money aside. Backward, the Restitution Bond, pricing the loss already realized and owing it. The same honest accounting, run with the arrow of time pointing either way.

## Two architectures, one act

The forward direction is not a fantasy. There is a country that has been doing a version of it for a generation, and there is another place that shows what the same physical act looks like with no honest ledger at all, and the contrast between them is the whole argument in two photographs.

Oil was confirmed off Norway's stretch of the North Sea just before Christmas in 1969, at the field called Ekofisk, and a poor, careful, fortunate country had to decide what to do with a non-renewable windfall. What it did, over the following decades, was build the closest thing the world has to a Foreclosure Bond at sovereign scale. It established a fund in 1990, the Government Petroleum Fund, later renamed the Government Pension Fund Global, and it began putting the proceeds of the oil into it rather than spending them. And then, in 2001, it bound itself with a fiscal rule of beautiful simplicity: the government may spend only the expected real return on the fund, around three percent a year, and may not touch the capital. The principal is inviolate. The oil, a non-renewable thing being liquidated, is converted as it is sold into a permanent financial asset, and the country lives off the interest and leaves the capital, which is to say it treats the liquidated commons with the discipline a renewable commons demands, taking less than it breeds.

The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every Norwegian citizen. And the part that should astonish anyone who has watched democracies handle money is that it has been durable across the entire political spectrum. Governments of the left and the right, the Stoltenberg governments and the Bondevik government and the Solberg government and the Støre government, every colour of Norwegian politics has come to power with this enormous accessible pile of money sitting there, and not one of them has raided the principal. They have argued about the three percent. They have not broken the capital. This is Hartwick's rule made real at the scale of a nation, the rents of a non-renewable resource reinvested into a permanent endowment, and it is also, fittingly, a reserve fund, a thing held back against the future, which is the theme this whole essay keeps returning to. Norway is the existence proof. The future can be priced in practice. A country can look at a barrel and see the permanence in it and set the money aside.

Now the counter-case, and let me be plain that it is not a villain story and not a brief against extraction. The Niger Delta has been pumping oil since the 1950s, and the revenue has run to hundreds of billions of dollars. But the architecture was different, and the difference is the whole point. The costs of the extraction, the spills that ruined the creeks, the gas flares that burned day and night, the fisheries foreclosed, the farmland poisoned, were borne by people who did not sell the oil and could not bill anyone for what it cost them. Cost severance at continental scale, with no Foreclosure Bond, no Restitution Bond, no fund, no rule, no capital set aside. When the writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa and eight others organized against what was being done to the Ogoni people and their land, they were executed in 1995, an act that drew Nigeria's suspension from the Commonwealth and the horror of the world. Years later a United Nations assessment of the pollution in Ogoniland estimated that the cleanup would take an initial billion dollars and twenty-five to thirty years, and that was only to begin. Hundreds of billions extracted; the costs left lying on the land and the people; a billion-dollar bill, decades long, handed to no one and paid by everyone who lives there.

Two architectures. Norway and the Delta. The same physical act, oil drawn from under the sea or the swamp and sold and burned. In one place the future was priced and the money was set aside and the principal was kept inviolate across every government for a generation. In the other the future was not priced, the costs were severed and abandoned, and the people who never sold the oil are still breathing the flares. The question the contrast asks is not whether to extract. It is the only question the framework ever asks: when the cost outlives the sale, who carries it, and is anything set aside? This is architecture, not a political program. It does not tell you to stop drilling. It tells you to keep an honest ledger while you do, and it shows you, in two real places, what honesty and its absence look like written across a generation.

## The asteroid miner

Now run the framework somewhere the mask of abundance has never even formed, because there is no one for the cost to be severed onto, and watch what honest accounting says.

I am going to send a miner to an asteroid, and again I am offering this as a lens and not a prediction. I do not know whether we will ever mine asteroids, and the argument does not need us to. I want the asteroid because it is a place stripped of the things that make cost severance possible, so that the ledger's logic stands out clean.

Out there, around a lump of iron and nickel drifting between the planets, there is no community to bear a cost. There is no ecosystem to foreclose. There is no downstream town, no fishery, no future generation that will want this particular rock for itself, no commons distributed so totally that abundance hides its value. When you cut the iron out of the asteroid and carry it away, whose cost are you severing? Nobody's. There is no one at the table because there is no table and there is no one. The residual commons value, the worth owed to people not present, is near zero, because there are no people, present or future, with a claim on a sterile rock in the dark. And so the honest ledger, the very same ledger that priced the coal at five hundred dollars and demanded Norway set its principal aside, says something that surprises people who expected it to be an anti-extraction creed: take it. Mine the iron. Extract freely. The honest accounting of this place returns a cost near zero, so there is nothing to set aside, and you should go ahead. If a comet swings briefly into reach and will not return for a thousand years, extract from it aggressively while you can, because the cost of taking is near nothing and the cost of not taking is a thing lost to no purpose.

And yet. On the asteroid itself, on the comet itself, there is a different kind of accounting that does not relax at all, and the same ledger that said take freely says it without contradiction. You are out there in the dark with your fuel and your air and your water and your life-support, and every one of those is a non-renewable resource in your sealed little world, your own Biosphere 2 with the vacuum pressing on the glass. And so you must hold reserves. You must keep back enough fuel to be sure you can leave, enough air to be sure you can breathe until you do, enough margin to get off this rock and home, or on to the next world. The ledger that says extract the iron without restraint says, in the same breath, reserve your own life-support without compromise, because the iron has no one to owe and your survival has a future to reach. Take freely here. Reserve there. There is no contradiction in it, because it is the same rule: know the real cost, and keep enough back to survive the future you are extracting into.

That is the whole shape of the thing, and the asteroid shows it bare. Honest accounting is not against extraction. It was never against extraction. It is the discipline of knowing what extraction truly costs, which on a sterile rock is nearly nothing, and on a living planet is a coal seam's worth of severed lungs, and in your own air tank is your life. You extract where the cost is low. You hold reserves against the future you cannot replace. The framework is not a faith. It is a ledger, and the ledger is honest, and an honest ledger gives different answers in different places because the costs really are different in different places.

## Whatever economy you choose

Step back far enough and the framework reveals what it is not. It is not a rival economic system. It does not compete with capitalism or socialism or the steady state or any other arrangement for who owns what and who decides. It is the accounting that any of those systems needs in order to work, the ledger underneath the ledger, the requirement that whatever you build, you build it knowing your real costs and holding reserves against scarcity.

Look at the people who have tried, in our time, to make economics see its real costs, and you find them building reserves and honest ledgers under different names. Kate Raworth, in Doughnut Economics, drew the picture that has done more than any other to make the costs visible: an inner ring below which people fall into deprivation and an outer ring beyond which the planet's systems break, and the safe and just space for humanity as the doughnut between them. That is a map of costs the market cannot price, the social floor and the ecological ceiling, drawn so a civilization can see what it is spending. Ostrom's governed commons are honest ledgers too, communities keeping track of a shared renewable resource and holding its harvest below the rate at which it breeds. These are not all the same project, and I do not claim they are; they are, among others, attempts by a civilization to know its real costs and to keep something back.

And the impulse is far older than any of them. Long before economics had a name, civilizations built reserve systems, granaries against the lean years, the ever-normal granary of ancient China that bought grain when it was cheap and released it when it was dear, the state granaries of ancient Egypt that held the harvest against famine. I will not invent the particulars of those systems, because the particulars are not mine to embroider, but the high outline is enough to make the point: the instinct to hold reserves against scarcity is one of the oldest things a civilization does, older than markets, older than money, as old as the first society that looked at a good year and understood that a bad one was coming.

So whatever economy you choose, capitalist or planned, ancient or off-world, the requirement does not change. You have to see the real costs. You have to keep an honest ledger. You have to hold reserves against scarcity. An economy that cannot see its real costs is not a different kind of economy. It is an economy flying blind, and it will fly blind straight into the thing it could not see, the way a market that cannot see the air will sell the climate for the price of a tank of gas.

## Reserves on the longest horizon

How long must the reserves be held? For the next generation, certainly. For the generation after that. But a finite resource on a finite world poses the question on a horizon longer than the human imagination is built for, and honesty, the kind this whole essay has been arguing for, does not get to stop where the imagination tires.

The Sun is not constant. It is slowly brightening, and the brightening is not a distant abstraction but a fact with a date, or at least an order of magnitude. The astrophysical work of Sackmann and colleagues, decades ago, put the time at which the rising luminosity of the Sun will make the Earth uninhabitable at something on the order of five hundred million to a billion years from now. Not the dramatic death of the Sun, not yet, just a star getting steadily hotter until the oceans cannot stay liquid and the planet cannot stay alive. The far more famous event, the swelling of the Sun into a red giant that will engulf the inner planets, comes much later, around seven and a half billion years out by the modelling of Schröder and Smith, and I name the larger number only to set it aside, because it is not the binding constraint. The binding constraint is the earlier one, the loss of habitability, half a billion to a billion years from now.

Those numbers are not an argument for despair and not, I think, for science fiction either. They are an argument for taking the idea of reserves seriously on its true horizon. If a civilization is going to keep an honest ledger of a finite world, the ledger does not close in a century or a millennium. The off-world habitat, the sealed system I asked you to imagine at the start as a lens for seeing the value of air, turns out to be more than a lens. On the longest horizon it is a coherent forward-looking concern, the preservation of an option across deep time, the reserve held back not for the next harvest but for the survival of the line itself when the home world's lease finally runs out. I am not predicting we will leave. I am saying that an honest ledger of a finite planet, run all the way out, has a line in it for the day the planet is no longer enough, and that line is not fantasy. It is just accounting on the only horizon that finally matters.

## The number was always there

Breathe in again. The same breath you started with, free, abundant, worth everything, costing nothing, hidden from the only instrument we trust by the very completeness of its abundance.

Everything in this essay has been one move, made over and over at widening scales. Find the value that abundance, or distance, or time has hidden, and put it on the ledger honestly. In the air, the value the mask hides. In the sealed dome and the diver's helium and the water lifted to orbit, the same value made visible by a boundary. In the hundred-dollar barrel, the residual commons value owed to people not at the table. In the McDowell coal, the honest five hundred dollars behind the market's four. In the Foreclosure Bond and the Restitution Bond, the ledger run forward and backward across time. In Norway and the Delta, two architectures for the same act. On the asteroid, the cost near zero and the reserve held all the same. In every economy anyone has ever built, the requirement to see real costs and keep something back. And on the longest horizon, the reserve held across deep time itself.

The framework is not a system to replace the systems we have. It is the honest accounting that an individual can run over a single decision, that a community can run over a shared resource, that a government can run over a windfall of oil. It works on this world and it works, in thought, on any other, because it is not made of any particular world's politics. It is made of the simple insistence that the ledger tell the truth.

The mask of abundance comes off the same way every time. You stop reading the zero as a verdict. You ask what the thing is worth and not only what it costs at the margin today. And the number you find was always there, behind the air, behind the barrel, behind the coal, waiting for someone to write it down.

What you do with the number is the part that is not accounting. The ledger can tell you the air is worth everything. It cannot make you keep enough of it. That much is still ours to decide, while the deciding is still free, which is to say now, while you can still breathe without being billed for it. Hold the breath a moment longer. Then choose what kind of accounting you will keep.
