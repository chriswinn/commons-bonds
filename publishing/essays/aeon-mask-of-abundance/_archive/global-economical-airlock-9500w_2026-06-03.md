# The Mask of Abundance

Picture yourself arriving at a sealed habitat that is not on Earth. You do not have to travel far in imagination to find one. People already live in places that come close. On Devon Island in the Canadian Arctic, researchers spend their summers inside a station built to rehearse what life on Mars might ask of them. On the slope of a Hawaiian volcano, at a site called HI-SEAS, small crews have lived for months inside a dome, eating stored food, going outside only in suits, keeping logs of what breaks and what holds. These are not science fiction. They are rehearsals, and they are useful to think with precisely because they take an ordinary thing and make it strange. So treat the habitat ahead of you as a lens, not a forecast. I am not predicting that you will live there. I am asking you to stand at its door for a moment, because of what the door reveals.

You reach the airlock. Before it will open, someone asks you a question that would sound absurd in almost any city, village, or open field on Earth. They ask how you intend to pay for your air.

The question is not a joke and it is not cruelty. Inside the wall, air is manufactured. Oxygen is split out of water by running current through it. The carbon dioxide you exhale has to be scrubbed back out of the shared atmosphere before it builds to a level that would dull your thinking and then stop your heart. Every breath you take is drawn from a supply that someone built, that someone maintains, and that will run short if more is taken than is made. So the question is exact. How will you pay for your air? You have never been asked it before. You have no ready answer. You have spent your whole life breathing for free, and you assumed, without ever putting the assumption into words, that air was simply free, the way the number zero is simply true.

Standing at that door, you begin to understand something. The air was never free. It was only ever free to you. On Earth a vast machine had always been running on your behalf, with no meter and no invoice, and because no bill ever arrived you mistook the absence of a bill for the absence of a cost. Plants and plankton split water and rock and sunlight into the oxygen you breathe. Oceans and forests pull your exhaled carbon back out of the sky. The work was real. The cost of doing it was real. It simply was not charged to you, and a cost that is never charged is a cost most people never learn to see.

Now imagine your stay ends and you come home. You step off the ship and the first thing you do is breathe, and nobody asks you to pay. The air is free again. Heat from the Sun is free. Light is free. Water falls out of the sky and runs in the rivers and nobody hands you a meter for it. For a while the airlock question seems like the strangest thing you have ever heard, a piece of off-world bureaucracy with no bearing on real life. Then a second understanding arrives, slower and more disturbing than the first. The cost here is just as real as the cost behind the wall. It is simply paid by someone else. It is paid by a forest on another continent, or by an ocean quietly turning more acidic, or by a person who has not been born yet and will inherit a thinner margin than the one you were handed. The bill did not vanish when you came home. It was sent somewhere you cannot see, to someone who cannot send it back.

This is the subject I want to open up. The most valuable things most of us will ever use are the ones we share and never pay for. Clean air. Fresh water. A climate stable enough to plant by and live in. The planet's slow, unpaid work of drawing our carbon back out of the sky. These things carry no price, and we are trained to read no price as a verdict on worth, to assume that what costs nothing is worth little. The truth is the reverse. They carry no price because they are so abundant that no market can find an edge to charge at, and that very abundance hides their value from us so completely that we do not undervalue them, which would at least mean we saw them. We do not see them at all. I will call this the mask of abundance, and the danger it poses is not that we treat these things as cheap. The danger is that we cannot perceive them as things at all, until the supply tightens and the mask slips and the value behind it, which was always total, suddenly acquires a price.

A sealed habitat is useful for exactly one reason. It strips the mask off. Inside a wall, the abundant becomes scarce, and the scarce acquires a price, and the price makes the value visible at last. And here is the part that should unsettle you. We do not have to imagine this from nothing. We have already built small sealed worlds, here on Earth, and inside every one of them the free things of the open planet became the most expensive things there were. So before we go off-world, let us look at the sealed worlds we have actually built, and at what the bill said when it finally arrived.

## The sealed worlds we have already built

Go down into the ocean, deep enough and for long enough, and ordinary air becomes a problem rather than a gift. Under pressure, the nitrogen that makes up most of the air you are breathing right now stops being inert and starts behaving like a drug, dulling and confusing the diver who breathes it. Push deeper and the oxygen itself, the part you cannot live without, turns toxic. So divers who must work at depth for days or weeks at a stretch do not breathe air. They breathe a manufactured mixture in which most of the nitrogen is replaced by helium, which does neither of those things. The helium is not cheap. It runs to something between roughly eighty and two hundred dollars an hour for the helium component alone, depending on the depth and the mixture, and that is only the gas, not the vessel or the crew or the decompression. The number is large enough that the support ships run reclaim rigs to capture the exhaled helium and use it again, because letting a breath escape into the sea is letting money escape into the sea.

Notice what has happened. The diver is breathing. Breathing is the most ordinary act there is, the one thing every human does without thought from the first second of life to the last. And here, a few hundred feet down, that ordinary act has acquired a price by the hour, because the conditions that made breathing free have been removed. The value of breathing did not change between the surface and the seabed. Only the price changed, and it changed because scarcity arrived.

Go the other direction and the same thing happens. In the Arizona desert, between 1991 and 1993, a group of eight people sealed themselves inside a structure of glass and steel that enclosed about three acres of built ecosystems, a small rainforest, a patch of ocean, a stretch of farmland, all under one airtight roof. It was called Biosphere 2, on the idea that Earth itself is Biosphere 1, and it cost on the order of a hundred and fifty million dollars to build. The plan was that the sealed world would balance itself, that the plants would make enough oxygen and the crops enough food and the whole closed system would breathe on its own. It did not. Over sixteen months the oxygen inside slid downward, from the twenty one percent that the open air holds toward fourteen percent, a level low enough to leave the crew exhausted and unwell, gasping at the top of a flight of stairs. The cause turned out to be quiet and almost invisible. Microbes in the rich soil were respiring faster than expected, consuming oxygen, and a great deal of the carbon dioxide they produced was being absorbed into the fresh concrete of the structure rather than recaptured by plants, so the oxygen never came back. In the end the managers did the only thing they could. They pumped oxygen in from outside, which was an admission that the sealed world could not make its own air. The living things fared worse than the people. Nineteen of the twenty five small vertebrate species inside died. Every pollinating insect died, which meant the crops had to be pollinated by hand, by human beings doing with paintbrushes the unpaid work that bees do everywhere on Earth for free.

That last detail is worth holding onto. Pollination is one of the great unpriced services of the living planet. No one is billed for it. It happens, abundantly, everywhere flowers grow, and because it is abundant it is invisible. Seal a world and let the pollinators die, and pollination instantly acquires a cost, measured in human hours bent over crops with a brush. The work was always being done. The seal simply moved it from the invisible side of the ledger to the visible one.

Now go up instead of down. A few hundred kilometres above your head, the International Space Station has been continuously occupied for more than two decades, and it is the most uncompromising sealed world we have built, because there is nothing outside it at all. Everything must be carried up or made on board. Lifting a kilogram of water to that orbit has cost on the order of twenty one thousand dollars. A six month supply of water for the crew, lifted from the ground, would run to something like a hundred and thirty five million dollars, which is a useful order of magnitude even if the exact figure shifts with the launch vehicle. At those prices you do not throw water away. The station recycles close to ninety percent of everything the crew sweats and urinates, cleaning it back into drinking water, because on the ground water is the cheapest thing imaginable and in orbit it is among the most expensive. And the oxygen the crew breathes is made the same way the habitat at the start of this essay made it, by running electric current through water to split off the oxygen, which means that up there, water and air are nearly the same commodity, and both are dear.

Three sealed worlds, three depths and altitudes, one lesson. Seal the system, and the priceless commons become the most expensive things inside the wall. Breathing, drinking, the recycling of waste, the pollination of crops, the maintenance of a breathable atmosphere, all the work the open planet does for free and out of sight, becomes metered, costed, and paid for the instant the abundance that hid it is taken away. The value was always there. The seal does not create it. The seal reveals it, by removing the abundance that had been the mask.

## What the seal reveals: the mechanism with no invoice

If the value was always there, where was it hiding, and why does no bill ever arrive on the open planet? The answer is a mechanism that operates quietly under almost everything we buy and sell, and it deserves a plain name before I give it a technical one.

Here is the mechanism. Someone takes an action that creates a cost. The cost is real, but it does not fall on the person who created it. It falls on someone else, somewhere else, or at some other time. And the person who bears it has no way to send the bill back to the person who caused it. The cost is severed from its cause. I will call this cost severance, and once you have the idea you will see it nearly everywhere.

When a factory makes a product and releases something harmful into the river behind it, the buyer of the product pays the factory's price, but the people downstream pay a second price that never appears on any receipt, in fouled water and lost fish and illness. The factory created that cost. The downstream community absorbs it. And there is, ordinarily, no mechanism by which the community can bill the factory for what it carries. That is cost severance in its everyday form, and economists have a related term for it, the externality, the cost pushed outside the transaction. But the externality framing tends to treat the severed cost as an exception, a leak from an otherwise sound system, a thing to be corrected at the edges. I want to treat severance as the central fact and the thing we can see and price as the exception, because once you take the commons seriously that is what it becomes.

There is a second idea I need, and it is stranger than the first. When something is sold, the sale settles a price between a buyer and a seller, both of whom are present and both of whom consent. But some of the value of the thing sold may belong to people who are not present and did not consent, and may not even be born. Think of a forest sold for its timber. The buyer pays the seller. But the forest was also pulling carbon out of the sky, holding soil against the rain, sheltering species, cooling the air for miles around, and the people who depended on that work, including everyone who will live downwind of that ground a century from now, were not at the sale and got no share of the price. There is a worth that remains after the transaction is complete, owed to people who were never party to it. I will call this the residual commons value, the value left over and unpriced after the sale is done, owed to people not at the table, often across great distance and often across long stretches of time.

Now I can say something precise about what honest pricing means, and about why the commons is a special and difficult case. Usually, honest pricing means re-attaching a severed cost. You find the cost that was pushed downstream, you measure it, and you put it back into the price where it belongs, so that the person who creates the cost is the person who pays it. That is hard, but it is conceptually clean. The commons case is harder and stranger than that, and the sealed worlds are what reveal why. The value of clean air or a stable climate was never severed from a price, because it never had a price to be severed from. It was so abundant, so completely distributed across everyone and everywhere, that no market could find a margin to charge at, and so it acquired a value so total that it remained, paradoxically, invisible. You cannot re-attach a price that never existed. You can only build one, after the fact, by imagining the seal, by asking what the thing would cost if its abundance were taken away. That is what the divers and Biosphere 2 and the space station do for us. They are not predictions. They are instruments for reading a value that the open planet keeps hidden behind the mask of abundance.

What I want to build, across the rest of this essay, is a single idea and a way of using it. The idea is that any economy on any world needs one honest ledger, a way of accounting that makes the severed costs and the residual value visible, so that the people deciding what to extract and what to keep can see the real costs and not only the priced ones. That is the whole of what I am claiming. It is not a new economic system to replace the ones we have. It is the accounting any system needs in order to know what it is actually doing. And the useful thing about the ledger is that it runs in both directions and on any scale, from a single barrel of oil to a sovereign state to a colony on another world. Let me show it running, and let me start with a single barrel.

## The hundred-dollar barrel

Put a price on a barrel of oil and call it a hundred dollars. The exact figure does not matter, and I am using it only to think with, so treat it as illustrative. What does that hundred dollars actually buy, and for whom?

It buys the present its tank of fuel. The hundred dollars covers the cost of finding the oil, lifting it, refining it, moving it, and a margin for everyone along the way, plus whatever the market will presently bear. It is a price set by the conditions of the present, by present scarcity, present demand, present technology. And for the present, it is more or less honest. The buyer wants the fuel now and pays for it now and drives away.

But the thing being priced does not belong only to the present. A barrel of oil is a piece of the planet's stored past, carbon laid down over a span of time so long it defeats the imagination, and once it is burned it is gone in a way that almost nothing else we buy is gone. The hundred dollars settles the present's claim on the barrel. It does nothing at all about the barrel's relationship to the future, because the future was not at the sale and could not bid. The hundred dollars does not buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after. It cannot, because the barrel is not there to be bought back. It has been turned into heat and motion and a permanent addition to the carbon in the sky.

This is the place to be precise about a distinction that does a great deal of work, the difference between a renewable resource and a non-renewable one. A renewable resource poses a question of rate. A forest, a fishery, an aquifer fed by rain, all of these will keep providing as long as you take less than they replace. The honest question for a renewable resource is whether your rate of taking is below its rate of renewing, and if it is, you can take forever, and the residual commons value is preserved because the thing itself persists. Manage the rate and you have managed the obligation.

Oil is not like that. Oil does not renew on any timescale that means anything to a human society. When you burn a barrel you have not borrowed it, to be repaid by next season's growth. You have liquidated it. It is converted, permanently, from a stock into a flow that does not come back. There is no rate of extraction slow enough to make oil renewable, because its rate of renewal is, for all human purposes, zero. So the hundred-dollar barrel is not just a present price on a thing with a future. It is a present price on a thing that is being removed from the future entirely, and the residual commons value, the worth owed to everyone who comes after and was not at the sale, is not being preserved by managing a rate. It is being extinguished. The mask of abundance hangs over oil too. There seems to be so much of it that the idea of a permanent loss feels unreal, right up until the moment a sealed accounting forces you to notice that every barrel burned is a barrel no one will ever have again.

## A lineage of near-misses

I am not the first person to stand near this empty plot of ground and feel that something belonged there. A number of serious traditions have circled the residual commons value, the unpriced worth owed across time, and each one saw a real part of it, and none of them built quite the structure the whole problem asks for. It is worth walking past their work, because the gaps are as instructive as the insights, and because honesty requires acknowledging how much of this has been seen before.

Karl Marx, writing in the first volume of Capital in the middle of the nineteenth century, was perhaps the most famous person ever to insist that the price of a thing conceals an uncounted cost. His uncounted cost was the surplus extracted from labor, the gap between what work produces and what the worker is paid. He was pricing a severance. But the severance he priced ran along a line of class, between owner and worker, both alive at the same time. He was not pricing the cost severed across generations, the bill sent forward to people not yet born. He found a hidden ledger and read one column of it.

Elinor Ostrom, who won the Nobel in economics in 2009 for the work gathered in her book Governing the Commons, did more than almost anyone to show that shared resources need not collapse, that communities can and do govern fisheries and forests and irrigation systems sustainably, without either privatizing them or handing them to a distant state. Her commons, though, were renewable. Her great subject was how a community manages the rate of taking from a resource that renews, how it keeps the herd within the carrying capacity of the pasture. She solved the rate problem with extraordinary care. She did not take up the liquidated commons, the thing extracted once and gone forever, because that is a different problem, and a community that governs a pasture beautifully has still said nothing about a barrel of oil that will never grow back.

Herman Daly, the ecological economist who died in 2022, spent his career warning that an economy cannot grow its physical throughput without limit on a finite planet, that the scale of what we pull from the Earth and push back into it as waste is the thing that matters and the thing conventional economics ignored. He was right, and he was early, and his warning was about the whole flow at once, the total burden of an economy on its planet. What he did not provide, because it was not his project, was an invoice for one specific foreclosed thing, a way to put a number on this barrel, this forest, this ton of coal, and say here is what was owed and to whom. He measured the river. He did not bill the drop.

John Hartwick, in an economics paper in the late nineteen seventies, came closest to the structure on the resource side. He asked what a society should do if it lives by extracting a non-renewable resource, and he gave an answer of real elegance. It should not consume all the proceeds. It should take the rents from the resource, the pure value of the resource itself beyond the cost of getting it, and reinvest them in some other lasting form of capital, so that as the physical resource is drawn down, a different and durable form of wealth is built up to replace it, and future generations inherit something instead of nothing. This is now called Hartwick's rule, and it is close to the heart of honest accounting across time. But Hartwick was saving the rents. He was making sure the proceeds of the sale were not simply consumed. He was not, in the form he gave it, pricing the full residual commons value of the barrel itself, the worth of the thing's permanent absence from the future, which can be larger than any rent the sale records. He saved the money from the sale. He did not price the hole left in the world.

These are the resource and economy traditions. Alongside them runs a tradition in moral philosophy that approached the same ground from a different side, by asking what we owe to people we will never meet. The philosopher John Rawls asked his readers, in his Theory of Justice at the start of the nineteen seventies, to imagine choosing the basic rules of a society from behind what he called a veil of ignorance, not knowing which person in that society you would turn out to be, rich or poor, powerful or powerless. The device forces fairness, because you will not rig the rules in favor of a position you might not occupy. Rawls applied it mostly across persons alive at once, but the veil widens naturally across time. If you did not know which generation you would be born into, the present one or one a century from now, what rules would you choose for the use of things that cannot be replaced? You would not choose to let the present burn the future's inheritance for a tank of fuel.

Michael Sandel, in his book on what money cannot buy, pressed a related question from another angle, asking which things are corrupted by being priced at all, which goods lose something essential when they are put up for sale. And the philosopher Derek Parfit, in his book Reasons and Persons in the middle of the nineteen eighties, thought harder than almost anyone about our obligations to future people, including the strange puzzles that arise because our choices today determine which people will exist at all. Each of these thinkers established, in their own terms, that obligations run forward in time, that the people not yet born have a claim on us even though they cannot press it. They built the moral foundation. They did not build the ledger that would put a number on the claim.

And then there is the tradition that did try to put a number on the future cost of burning carbon, the long argument over the discount rate. When you ask what a future harm is worth today, you have to decide how much to shrink it for the fact that it lies in the future, and that shrinking factor is the discount rate. Nicholas Stern, in a review commissioned by the British government in the middle of the last decade, used a very low discount rate, on the order of one and a half percent, which treats the welfare of future people as almost as important as our own, and concluded that strong action on climate change was worth a cost of around one percent of global output. William Nordhaus, who won his own Nobel in 2018 for the climate-economy models he built, used a substantially higher rate, closer to four percent, which discounts the far future more steeply and counsels a more gradual response. The gap between them is enormous in its policy consequences, and it turns almost entirely on that one ethical choice about the future's weight. A low rate values a harm a century out at something like twenty five dollars where a high rate values the same harm at something like a dollar and a half. Martin Weitzman added a further and disquieting point, that the real danger may lie in the small probability of catastrophic outcomes, the fat tail of the distribution, which ordinary cost-benefit reasoning handles badly.

But notice what all of these careful people were pricing. They were pricing the damage flow from combustion, the harm that the released carbon does as it warms the world. That is a real and important thing to price. It is not, however, the same as the residual commons value of the resource itself. The damage flow is what the burning does to the climate. The residual commons value is the worth of the thing's permanent absence, the barrel that will never be available to anyone again, the stock converted irreversibly to nothing. And here is the point that the whole lineage circles without quite landing on. The residual commons value survives even Stern's low discount rate, because it is not a discounted flow of future damages at all. It is a present absence. The barrel is gone now. The forest is cut now. The thing is not there now, and no discount rate touches a loss that is already total and already present. You can discount a future harm. You cannot discount away a thing that no longer exists.

So that is the empty plot, circled by Marx and Ostrom and Daly and Hartwick from the economic side, and by Rawls and Sandel and Parfit from the moral side, and by Stern and Nordhaus and Weitzman from the climate-pricing side, and never quite built upon, because each tradition held one piece. The mask of abundance is part of why. As long as the commons seemed infinite, no one needed a number for its loss, and the number stayed unbuilt. The sealed worlds take the mask off and show that the number was always there, waiting. Let me now put an actual number to an actual place.

## McDowell County

In the mountains of southern West Virginia, McDowell County was once one of the great coal counties of the United States, and the coal under it was among the best in the world, the dense Pocahontas seams that powered ships and steel. Around the middle of the twentieth century, coal came out of the McDowell mines and sold at the mine mouth for a few dollars a ton, something on the order of four or five dollars. That was the price the present paid, and like the hundred-dollar barrel it was, for the present, a roughly honest price. It covered the cost of mining the coal and a margin for the operator, and the buyer carried the coal away and burned it.

Now run the ledger the other way and ask what the honest price of that coal would have been, if the severed costs had been added back into it, the costs that fell not on the buyer but on people downwind and downstream and, above all, on people not yet born. The largest of those costs by far is the carbon. Burning a ton of that coal puts a quantity of carbon dioxide into the sky, and for the Pocahontas coal of McDowell, which is unusually dense and carbon-rich, that quantity is about two and six tenths of a ton of carbon dioxide for every ton of coal, higher than the national average, which sits closer to two and a third. To turn that into a cost you need a price for the carbon, a figure for the damage each ton of carbon dioxide does to the world over its long life in the atmosphere, and the United States Environmental Protection Agency, in its 2023 estimate, put that figure at roughly a hundred and ninety dollars per ton. Multiply the carbon released by the cost per ton, and the carbon term alone comes to nearly five hundred dollars, about four hundred and ninety six dollars, for a ton of coal that sold for four or five.

Add that back, along with the smaller severed costs, and the honest price of a ton of McDowell coal is more than five hundred dollars, on the order of five hundred and ten, and nearly all of that honest price is carbon, the cost of the climate damage that was severed from the sale and sent forward in time to people who never bought a lump of it and could never bill anyone for what they would carry. The mine-mouth price saw none of this. The mask of abundance hung over the whole transaction, because the sky seemed infinite and free, an unlimited place to put carbon at no charge, and so the carbon cost posted a zero that everyone read as the truth. The framework, run backward over a realized extraction, prices what the sale left out. It says that for every cheap ton that left those mountains, a cost more than a hundred times larger was created and sent downstream in space and forward in time, unrecorded, unbilled, and until recently unseen.

I want to be careful about what this does and does not claim. It is not a verdict that the coal should never have been mined, or that the people of McDowell County did something wrong by mining it. They were working, often in great hardship, inside an economy and a price system that showed them a number and told them it was honest. The point of running the ledger backward is not to assign blame to the people who answered the prices they were given. It is to show that the prices were not honest, that they had a severed cost folded into a zero, and that an economy which cannot see such costs is an economy operating without knowledge of what it is actually doing.

## Forwards and in reverse

The ledger I have been describing runs in two directions, and it has a distinct instrument for each, because pricing a future loss and pricing a past extraction are different acts even though they share an arithmetic.

Run it forward, before the extraction, and the instrument is what I will call a foreclosure bond. The word foreclosure here means the closing-off of a future, the permanent removal of an option that the people who come after would otherwise have had. Before you extract something that will not return, you price the residual commons value, the worth that the extraction forecloses, and you set that value aside, held against the future you are foreclosing. It is not a tax meant to stop the extraction. It is a reserve, money or durable wealth held back precisely because the thing being taken cannot be given back, so that the future inherits something in the place of what was removed. This is Hartwick's rule given a fuller object, not only saving the rents the sale records but pricing and reserving against the loss the sale creates.

Run the ledger backward, over an extraction already realized, and the instrument is what I will call a restitution bond. McDowell is the model. The coal is already burned, the carbon is already in the sky, the cost was already severed and sent forward. You cannot foreclose a future that is already foreclosed. What you can do is price what was taken and owed, and begin to make it good, which is what restitution means, restoring or repairing a value that was extracted without being paid for.

The backward direction is where this framework connects to a body of work it depends on and does not try to replace. The economists William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen, in their book From Here to Equality, published in 2020, set out the economic case for reparations to Black Americans for slavery and its long aftermath. Their argument is, at its core, an act of backward honest accounting on the largest scale. They trace the racial wealth gap back to its origins in 1619 and through the centuries since, and they show how an extraction of value, performed under coercion and never paid for, propagates forward through time as a measurable and persistent gap, including in something as fundamental as how long people live, the gap in longevity between Black and white Americans that stands as a legacy effect of costs severed long ago. Their work is the clearest demonstration I know that the ledger can be run in reverse, that a value extracted generations back can be priced in the present and made the basis of restitution. The restitution bond, as an instrument, operationalizes exactly the restitution component of the redress they describe.

I have to mark a line here carefully, and mark it as a limit on what this framework reaches and on what is mine to do. There is a cost in the cases Darity and Mullen address that is not the same as the climate cost of a burned barrel, and that is the cost of coercion itself, the human cost of the violence and unfreedom under which the extraction was carried out. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not my work to do, and I will not pretend to a standing I do not have. That pricing belongs to the people who are continuing it, who carry the history in a way I do not. What the framework can honestly reach, from where I stand, is the legacy effect, the measurable downstream consequence of a severed cost, the wealth gap and the longevity gap that can be priced as effects. The direct pricing of the coercion vector itself I leave deliberately to others, and the framework defers it rather than presuming to fold it in.

## Two architectures, one question

The forward direction is not a thought experiment. A country has run it, at the scale of a sovereign state, for more than thirty years, and another country has shown, over the same span, what happens when the ledger is never run at all. Set the two beside each other and the choice the framework describes becomes concrete.

Just before Christmas in 1969, a discovery was confirmed to the Norwegian authorities, a major field of oil under the North Sea, the field that came to be called Ekofisk. Norway was, at that point, not a wealthy country by the standards of its neighbors, and a sudden fortune in oil could have gone the way such fortunes often go, consumed in a generation, leaving little behind but the memory of a boom and the costs of the bust. Norway chose differently, and the choice was deliberate and patient. In 1990 the country established a fund, the Government Petroleum Fund, into which the proceeds of the oil would flow, and in 2006 it was renamed the Government Pension Fund Global. In 2001 the country adopted a fiscal rule of remarkable discipline. The government would spend, in any year, only the expected real return on the fund, on the order of three percent, leaving the capital itself untouched, inviolate, so that the fund would not be drawn down but would persist and grow and serve generations not yet born.

The result is among the most striking facts in modern economic history. The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars, which works out to more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every Norwegian citizen, a reserve built out of a resource that is being extracted and will one day be gone, held in a durable form against the future. And the discipline has held across the entire range of Norwegian politics, through governments led by the left and by the right, through the premierships of Stoltenberg and Bondevik and Solberg and Stoere. No government has raided the principal. This is Hartwick's rule realized at the scale of a nation, the rents of a non-renewable resource converted into a lasting form of wealth, and it is also, in the literal sense, a reserve fund, money held back against scarcity, which is a theme that will return when we leave this world.

Now set beside Norway the Niger Delta, in Nigeria, where oil has been extracted since the nineteen fifties and has generated revenue measured in hundreds of billions of dollars. The forward ledger was, in effect, never run. The costs of the extraction were severed and left to fall where they would, and they fell heavily on the people of the Delta, on communities whose fishing waters were fouled by spills and whose air was burned by the constant flaring of gas, costs borne by people who did not sell the oil and could not bill anyone for what they carried. When some of those people organized to protest what was being done to their land, the response was brutal. The writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa, who led a movement of the Ogoni people against the destruction of their region, was executed along with eight others in 1995, an act that drew worldwide condemnation and the suspension of Nigeria from the Commonwealth. Years later, an assessment by the United Nations of the environmental damage in just one part of the region, Ogoniland, estimated that an initial cleanup would cost on the order of a billion dollars and take twenty five to thirty years, and that was only the beginning of restoring what had been lost.

Same physical act, in both places. Oil lifted from the ground and sold. Two completely different architectures laid over that act. In one, the residual commons value was priced and reserved, held against the future in a fund that now belongs to everyone in the country. In the other, the costs were severed and abandoned, and the value flowed out while the bill stayed behind, unpaid, with the people least able to send it back. One question separates the two. When the cost outlives the sale, who carries it, and is anything set aside against it?

I want to be clear that this is not an argument against extracting oil, and it is not a story with a villain and a hero. It is an argument about architecture, about whether an economy runs an honest ledger or does not, and the same physical resource produced opposite outcomes not because one was extracted and the other was not, but because one was accounted for honestly across time and the other was not. The framework is not a political program. It is the accounting that any political program, of any kind, would need in order to know what it was actually doing.

## Off-world: the ledger running in real time

Now let me take the framework off this planet, and let me be plain about what I am doing. I am not predicting that human beings will live in sealed habitats on other worlds, or mine asteroids, or do any of the things I am about to describe. The off-world habitat is a lens, the same lens I used at the airlock and in the deep-sea diver and Biosphere 2 and the space station, and I am using it again here for the same reason, because a sealed world strips the mask off and lets us see the ledger running where on Earth it stays hidden. Treat what follows as an instrument for seeing, not a forecast of where we are going.

At the start of this essay you stood at an airlock and were asked how you would pay for your air, and the question revealed a value you had never seen. Now imagine living there, not visiting, not measuring, but making a home inside the seal, with a functioning economy around you, the way a person makes a home in any place where people have to get along and trade and keep accounts. Inside that home, every commons that the open Earth gives away for free is metered and billed by usage, because inside a seal there is no other honest way to run things. Your breath is drawn from a manufactured supply, so it is metered. The water you drink is recycled at real cost, so it is billed by the litre. The carbon dioxide you exhale has to be scrubbed out of the shared air, so the removal of your own exhaled carbon is a line on your account. None of this is punishment. It is simply what honest accounting looks like in a place where abundance cannot hide a single thing, where the mask has been removed not as an experiment but as a permanent condition of living there at all.

This is the framework as a colony's everyday economy, the same honest ledger I ran over McDowell County and over Norway, but run forward, in real time, as the operating system of a place, rather than backward over a history or forward as a policy a government chooses to adopt. On Earth the ledger is something we have to build deliberately against the pull of the mask, and we mostly do not. Inside the seal there is no choice, because the seal makes the costs visible whether anyone wants them visible or not, and a colony that pretended its air was free would simply suffocate. The airlock question that seemed absurd to you when you first heard it is, inside the seal, the most ordinary thing in the world, as ordinary as a price on bread. First we used the seal to see the value. Now we live inside it, and we pay it, and paying it is not a hardship but the plain shape of an honest economy in a place too sealed to let any abundance lie.

Notice that the colony's ledger has both a metering side and a reserve side, because the two always travel together. It is not enough to bill each breath and litre as it is used. A sealed habitat must also hold reserves, a margin of air and water and power kept back against failure, against the day a system breaks or a supply ship is late, because in a sealed world running the supply down to zero is not an inconvenience but a death. The reserve is the forward ledger made physical, value held back precisely because the future is uncertain and the thing cannot be conjured at need. Norway's fund and the colony's oxygen tank are the same instrument wearing different clothes, both of them a society keeping enough back to be sure of surviving the future it is moving into.

Now let me move the lens to a stranger case, which shows that honest accounting is not the same thing as being against extraction, and that the ledger can say take freely just as firmly as it says hold back. Imagine a miner working an asteroid, far out where there is no community, no ecosystem, no downstream village, no future generation that lives on that particular rock. The whole reason cost severance matters on Earth is that the cost lands on someone, on the people downstream, on the ones not yet born. Out on a barren asteroid there is no one for the cost to land on. The residual commons value of the iron in that rock is, as near as makes no difference, zero, because no one will ever depend on that asteroid's iron staying where it is. And so the framework, the same honest ledger that priced McDowell coal at more than five hundred dollars a ton, says something that might sound surprising coming from an essay about hidden costs. It says extract freely. Take the iron. If a comet swings briefly into reach and will be gone for a thousand years, extract aggressively while it is there, because there is no severed cost and no one to bear it, and leaving the resource untouched serves no one at all. Honest accounting is not a bias toward leaving things in the ground. It is a clear sight of the real costs, and where the real costs are genuinely near zero, it counsels taking.

And yet, on that same rock, the very same ledger imposes an absolute discipline of a different kind. You must hold reserves. You must keep back enough fuel and air and life-support to be certain you can leave the asteroid, get home, or reach the next world. Extract the iron as freely as you like, but never burn the reserve that gets you off the rock alive, because the one cost that is never zero, out there or anywhere, is the cost of failing to survive the future you are extracting into. So the same ledger, read on the same rock, says take freely and hold back, and there is no contradiction in that, because the two instructions answer two different questions. Take freely answers what does this extraction cost others, and the answer there is nothing. Hold back answers what do I owe to my own continued existence and to the next step of the work, and the answer there is everything I need to get out alive. Honest accounting is knowing the real cost of each thing, the one that is near zero and the one that is absolute, and keeping enough back to survive.

## Whatever economy you choose, you have to see the real costs

It would be easy to read everything so far as an argument for one kind of economy over another, but that is not the argument, and I want to be exact about what the framework is and is not. It is not a rival economic system. It does not tell you whether to organize a society around markets or around planning, around private ownership or common ownership. It is the accounting that any of those systems needs in order to function honestly, the ledger underneath the economy rather than a competitor to it. A market economy that cannot see its severed costs is operating without knowledge of what it is doing, and so is a planned one. The mask of abundance does not respect ideology. It hides the value of the commons from capitalist and planner alike.

Once you see the framework this way, as the accounting beneath any economy rather than as a particular economy, you start to recognize it in attempts that look very different from one another and from this one. The economist Kate Raworth, in her book Doughnut Economics, published in 2017, drew a picture of an economy bounded on the outside by ecological limits we must not exceed and on the inside by a social foundation we must not fall below, a doughnut-shaped safe space for human life between the two. That picture is, among other things, a way of insisting that the real costs and the real limits be made visible and kept in view, that an economy account for the boundaries it must live within. Ostrom's work on governing the commons, which I described earlier, is another such attempt, a careful account of how a community can see the real cost of overusing a shared resource and govern itself to avoid it. These are different projects with different vocabularies, and they are all, at bottom, efforts by people to make a civilization see its real costs and hold reserves against scarcity, which is the thing the mask of abundance prevents.

And the impulse is much older than any modern economics. Long before there was a discipline called economics, societies that understood the difference between a good year and a bad one built systems to hold reserves against the lean times to come. Ancient China maintained what was called an ever-normal granary, a store of grain bought up in times of plenty and released in times of scarcity, a public reserve held against the swings of the harvest. Ancient Egypt maintained great state granaries for the same purpose, storing the surplus of abundant years against the famine years that everyone knew would come. I will keep these references at the level of the general pattern, because the particulars are not mine to invent, and the pattern is what matters here. The pattern is that a civilization which understands scarcity does not consume everything in the good years. It sees the real cost of an empty store in a bad year, prices that cost into its present behavior, and holds a reserve. That is the same instinct as Norway's fund and the colony's oxygen tank and Hartwick's rule, reaching across thousands of years and across utterly different economies, the recognition that the future is real and uncertain and owed something, and that an economy which cannot see that owes its people an account it is not keeping.

Whatever economy you choose, then, the requirement is the same. You have to be able to see the real costs, the severed ones and the residual ones, the costs that the mask of abundance hides and the costs that distance and time push out of view. An economy that cannot see them is not made honest by being a market, and it is not made honest by being a plan. It is made honest by keeping a true ledger, and only by that.

## Reserves on the longest horizon

There is one more scale to reach, and it is the largest one there is, because the logic of reserves does not stop at the next generation or the next century. It runs to the limit of how long a planet can hold life at all, and that limit, though it lies almost unimaginably far ahead, is real and worth naming, because it changes how we should think about the sealed worlds I have been using as a lens.

The Sun is not a constant. It is slowly growing brighter as it ages, and that gradual increase in its output means that the Earth will not remain habitable forever even if we tend it perfectly. On the order of five hundred million to a billion years from now, the rising luminosity of the Sun will have warmed the Earth past the point where it can sustain the kind of life it now holds, boiling away the conditions that make the planet livable, and this is a finding from the study of stellar evolution rather than any claim about human action. That is long before the far more distant end, when the Sun swells into a red giant and, on the order of seven and a half billion years from now, may engulf the inner planets entirely. The nearer of these two horizons is the one that matters for the argument, because it means that the Earth's habitability is itself a finite resource, the largest commons of all, and one that will eventually be foreclosed by the Sun whatever we do.

On those horizons, the sealed habitat stops being science fiction and becomes a coherent forward-looking concern, the longest reach of the reserve logic that runs through this whole essay. If the habitability of a single planet is finite, then the preservation of the option to live somewhere, the holding-back of a capacity to continue across the deepest stretch of time, is the reserve discipline carried to its farthest scale. I am not saying we should be building off-world colonies now instead of tending the Earth, and I am not predicting that we will. I am saying that the same logic which tells a colony to hold back enough air to survive, and tells Norway to keep its capital inviolate, and told an ancient granary to store against famine, also tells a civilization on the longest horizon to preserve its options across deep time. The reserve is the recognition that the future is uncertain and that something must be kept back against it, and on the longest horizon the something is the bare option of continuing at all.

## The breath, returning

I want to end where the airlock began, with a breath, and with you on Earth again, home from the sealed world, breathing the open air.

Take a breath now. It costs you nothing. No meter ran, no account was charged, no one stood at a door and asked how you intended to pay for it. The air is free, the same air the airlock would have charged you for by the breath, and it is free for the same reason the divers' helium is not free and the colony's oxygen is not free, because here, for now, it is so abundant that no market can find an edge to charge at, and so its value, which is total, stays hidden behind the mask of abundance. The value was never zero. The price was zero, and we mistook the one for the other, the way we mistook the mine-mouth price of McDowell coal for its honest price, the way the present mistakes the hundred-dollar barrel for the whole cost of the barrel.

Everything in this essay has been one move, made on different scales and run in different directions. Find the value that abundance or distance or time has hidden. Put it on the ledger honestly. The diver and Biosphere 2 and the space station show that the value is real by sealing the world and watching the price appear. McDowell shows the ledger run backward over a county's burned coal. Norway and the Niger Delta show the ledger run forward, kept or abandoned, at the scale of a state. The colony and the asteroid miner show it run as the everyday economy of a sealed world that cannot afford the mask. And underneath all of them is the same recognition, that the most valuable things are not the things with the highest prices but the things with no price at all, the commons we share and never pay for, owed not only to one another but to people not at the table, across great distance and across long stretches of time.

Here is the part that should stay with you when the rest has faded. The air is still free. Not because it has no value, but because it has nothing but value, distributed so completely that no one has yet found the wall that would let them charge for it. Whether it stays free is not a fact about the world. It is a choice we are making, mostly without noticing that we are making it, every time we let a severed cost post a zero and read that zero as the truth. The mask of abundance is not a law of nature. It is a failure of accounting, and accounting can be corrected. The number was always there. We can decide to see it, and decide what to set aside, and decide what to keep back against the future we are breathing our way into. That decision is still ours. For now, the breath is free, and whether it stays free is something we still get to decide.
