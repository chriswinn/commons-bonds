# The Mask of Abundance

Take a breath. Whatever you paid for it was nothing, and you will go on paying nothing for every breath you take until the last one. This is not because the air is worthless. It is the opposite. The air is the one thing you cannot do without for more than a few minutes, the precondition of every other thing you value, and it costs you nothing precisely because it is everywhere, all at once, in such total supply that no one has ever found an edge to stand on and charge from. A price is a measurement taken at a margin. It asks how much you would give up for one more unit of a thing, given how much of it there already is. When the thing is scarce, the answer is large, and the price climbs. When the thing is abundant beyond any possibility of running short, the answer is zero, and the price vanishes. The air posts a zero. And we, who trust prices as our only honest instrument for telling worth, read that zero as a verdict. We think the air is free because it is cheap. It is free because it is priceless, and our instrument cannot tell the difference.

This is the mask of abundance. It hangs over a whole category of things, the things we hold in common and breathe without thinking: the air itself, the water that falls and runs and is drunk and returns, the slow work of forests and oceans carrying away the carbon we exhale and the far larger amounts we burn, the steadiness of a climate that has held within livable bounds for the whole of human history. These are not underpriced the way a nurse's labour is underpriced, or the way the topsoil that feeds us is underpriced. Underpriced things still have prices; you can argue the number should be higher and point to the number that exists. The commons have no number at all. They were never brought to a market, never metered, never billed, because for the whole run of human experience there was no scarcity to make a market possible. A thing with no price is not cheap. It is invisible. It does not appear on any ledger, and what does not appear on the ledger cannot be reasoned about, defended, or saved. We are running the largest enterprise in history on a set of books that omits its most important assets entirely, and we call the omission abundance.

I want to show you what is under the mask. Not by arguing it, at first, but by taking the air away.

## The sealed room

Imagine a sealed habitat. I am not predicting one; I am borrowing one as a lens. Suppose you wall off a volume of the world from the world, draw a hard boundary around some crew and their crops and their machines, and let nothing pass in or out that is not carried by hand or made inside. The moment you draw that line, every priceless thing on the far side of it acquires a price, and the prices are enormous. Air, which was free a moment ago because it was everywhere, is now the most expensive substance in the room, because there is only so much of it and the people breathing it are turning it, with every breath, into something they cannot breathe. Water becomes a budget. The carrying-away of carbon, which the open planet did for nothing, now needs a machine, and the machine needs power, and the power is finite too. Seal the system and the mask falls. What was abundant is revealed as merely undivided, and what looked like a verdict of worthlessness is exposed as the largest worth there is.

You do not have to imagine all of this. People already live on the far side of that line, and the bills already arrive.

Go down with the saturation divers, the ones who work for days at depths where ordinary air would kill them. Down there nitrogen turns narcotic and oxygen turns toxic, so they do not breathe air. They breathe heliox, a blend leaning on helium, and helium is not free. It runs somewhere between roughly a dollar and seventy cents and not quite three dollars per cubic foot, which adds up, breath after breath, to something on the order of eighty to two hundred dollars an hour for the helium alone. The cost is steep enough that the ships running these operations install reclaim rigs to capture the gas the divers exhale and use it again. Think about what that means. Up here, on the deck, exhaling is the most thoughtless thing a body does, the very emblem of something free. A few hundred feet down it is metered, captured, recycled, and accounted for to the cubic foot, because down there the abundance is gone and only the value remains.

Or take Biosphere 2, the great sealed glasshouse in the Arizona desert. In the early nineteen-nineties a crew of eight shut themselves inside roughly three acres of sealed glass, a project that cost something like a hundred and fifty million dollars, to see whether a slice of the living Earth could be made to close on itself. It could not, not cleanly. Over sixteen months the oxygen inside fell from the ordinary twenty-one percent toward fourteen, as the microbes in the soil breathed it down and the concrete of the structure quietly drank the carbon dioxide, and in the end the managers had to pump oxygen in from outside to keep the crew safe. Of the twenty-five vertebrate species sealed in, nineteen died. Every pollinator died, so the crops had to be pollinated by hand. The experiment is often told as a failure, and as an attempt to replace the biosphere it was. But as a measurement it succeeded perfectly. It put a number on what the open air gives away for nothing, and the number was that eight intelligent, well-funded, highly motivated people could not, across three acres and a hundred and fifty million dollars, reliably reproduce the single service you received for free the instant you woke this morning.

Or go all the way up, to the station a few hundred kilometres overhead. Up there water is so dear that the working figure for lifting it to orbit is around twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram, which makes a six-month supply for a crew a matter of well over a hundred million dollars, give or take an order of magnitude. No one ships water at that price if they can avoid it, so the station recycles it, recovering something like ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine and breathing it back through the system, and it makes its oxygen by splitting water with electricity. Every loop that the open planet runs for free, beneath notice, the station must run on purpose, at ruinous cost, with engineers watching the gauges. The lesson of the diver and the glasshouse and the station is one lesson. The commons are not cheap. They are the most valuable things there are, and the only reason you have never seen the price is that you have never been on the far side of the line.

## The name of the thing

Once you have seen it, you can name the mechanism, and naming it is what lets you put it on the ledger.

The first half of the mechanism is cost severance. One party creates a cost. A different party absorbs it. And the second party has no way to send the bill back to the first. A factory burns coal and the smoke settles on a town downwind; the factory keeps the profit and the town keeps the asthma, and there is no counter at which the town can present its lungs for payment. The cost was real, it was created by an identifiable act, and it was simply severed from the actor and left to land wherever gravity and wind and the law of the moment happened to drop it. Most of what we call pollution, most of what economists file dryly under externalities, is cost severance: a cost cut loose from the one who made it.

The second half is subtler, and it is the half the commons live in. Call it the residual commons value: the worth that is left unpriced after a sale is done, the value that does not belong to the buyer or the seller because it belongs to people who were not at the table, and who very often are not yet born. When a barrel of oil is sold, the buyer pays for the right to burn it and the seller pockets the money, and the transaction looks complete. But the barrel was part of a shared inheritance, a one-time endowment laid down over geological ages and held, until now, in common by everyone who might ever have wanted it. The sale transfers it out of that common hold and into a single combustion chamber. The people who were not party to the sale, the ones downwind and downstream and downtime, lose something they were never asked about and never compensated for. That loss is the residual commons value, and it does not show up anywhere, because the only number the transaction generated was the price, and the price measured present scarcity at the margin and nothing else.

Here the two halves diverge in an important way. Honest pricing of a severed cost is, in principle, a matter of re-attaching: you find the cost that was cut loose, you trace it back to the act that made it, and you put it back on the actor's bill. Hard to do, often resisted, but conceptually clean. The commons case is stranger and, I think, deeper. There the trouble is not a cost that was severed but a value so total and so evenly spread that it never acquired a price in the first place. You cannot re-attach a price that never existed. You can only recognise the value when scarcity finally arrives to reveal it, and decide, before the revelation is forced on you the hard way, to write it onto the ledger by an act of honesty rather than an act of catastrophe. That act of honest accounting, performed before the seal closes, is the whole of what I want to propose.

## The hundred-dollar barrel

Let me make it concrete with a barrel of oil, and let me put a price on it for the sake of the argument: a hundred dollars. I am not claiming that is today's price; I am using a round number as an instrument. A hundred dollars is what the present will pay for that barrel, and it is an honest enough measure of present scarcity. The trouble is that the barrel does not belong only to the present. The hundred dollars buys the present its tank of petrol, its afternoon of flight, its warm house for a week. It does not buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after, because there is no one to buy it back from. The barrel, once burned, is not somewhere else. It is nowhere. It has been converted into heat and carbon dioxide and motion, and no price on Earth will reassemble it.

This is where a distinction that sounds technical turns out to be the whole moral difference. Some resources are renewable, and some are not, and the honest ledger treats them in opposite ways. A renewable resource, a fishery, a forest, an aquifer that recharges, poses a question of rate. You may take from it forever, provided you take less than it breeds back, and the entire discipline is in finding that rate and staying under it. Elinor Ostrom spent her career documenting the communities that learned to do exactly this, that governed their shared pastures and fisheries and irrigation systems sustainably for generations by binding themselves to a sustainable rate, and she won a Nobel for showing that the commons need not end in tragedy. But oil is not renewable on any human horizon, nor on any horizon that matters. It is not being harvested; it is being liquidated. There is no rate slow enough to make the taking sustainable, because the stock does not refill. Every barrel burned is a barrel that is gone, finally and forever, subtracted from a total that only ever goes down. The hundred dollars is a fair price for a rate. It is no price at all for a liquidation, because a liquidation has no future units to be priced, only past ones being spent.

## A field that was almost ploughed

It would be strange if no one had noticed the empty plot in the ledger, and the truth is that many people noticed it, walked its edges, and built nearby without ever building on it.

Karl Marx, in the first volume of Capital, found a value the market had hidden and put it back: the surplus skimmed from labour. But his hidden value was a matter of class, of who took what from whom in the present, not of time, not of what the present takes from the people who come after. Ostrom found the renewable commons and showed how they could be governed, but the renewable commons are precisely the case with a sustainable rate; she was not pricing the liquidated one, the barrel that does not breed back. Herman Daly spent his life warning that an economy cannot grow its physical throughput forever on a finite planet, and he was right, but a warning about throughput in general issues no invoice for any one foreclosed thing. John Hartwick worked out, in the nineteen-seventies, the rule that bears his name: if you are going to draw down a non-renewable stock, reinvest the rents from it in lasting capital, so that what you leave behind is at least as productive as what you took. That is genuine wisdom, and we will see a whole nation live by it. But Hartwick saved the rent, the money the barrel earned. He did not price the barrel, the thing itself, the loss of the option to have it at all.

The moral philosophers came at the same plot from another direction and got closer to its centre. John Rawls asked, in A Theory of Justice, that we reason about the rules of a society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which person in it we would turn out to be, and others widened that veil until it covered not only every living person but the generations not yet born, the people whose only representation at any present table is our willingness to imagine ourselves into their place. Michael Sandel argued, in What Money Can't Buy, that there are goods a market corrupts merely by pricing them, that the act of putting some things up for sale degrades them, which is a warning the commons case needs to hear even as it insists that what cannot be sold must still be counted. Derek Parfit, in Reasons and Persons, worked out with terrible care that we do have obligations to future people, that the fact they do not yet exist does not dissolve our duties to them, even when our choices will determine which of them exist at all. These thinkers established the standing of the people not at the table. What they did not do, because it was not their trade, was hand us a number.

And then there is the fight over the discount rate, which is the closest economics has come, and which still misses. When Nicholas Stern wrote his review of the economics of climate change he used a very low discount rate, near one and a half percent, on the reasoning that we have no moral right to count a future person's welfare for much less than our own, and concluded that the cost of acting was around one percent of global output, small against the harm of waiting. William Nordhaus, who would win his own Nobel for the work, built his influential models on a much higher rate, around four percent, drawn from the returns capital actually earns, and concluded that we should act more slowly. Martin Weitzman cut across both by insisting that the real danger lies in the fat tail of the distribution, the small chance of catastrophe so large that no expected-value calculation at any rate captures it. You can see how much rides on the number when you notice that a benefit of a hundred dollars a century out is worth about twenty-five dollars today at Stern's rate and about a dollar and a half at Nordhaus's. But look at what all three are pricing. They are pricing the damage flow from combustion, the stream of harms that the carbon will cause as it warms the world. They are not pricing the resource's absence, the simple brute fact that the barrel is gone. And the residual commons value survives even Stern's generous low rate, because it is not a discounted flow of future damages at all. It is the present absence of a thing that will not return. You cannot discount an absence to a smaller absence. It is already, fully, here.

## The county that paid

So far I have run the ledger forward, toward losses not yet realised. Run it backward, over a loss already taken, and the number stops being theoretical.

In McDowell County, in the coalfields of southern West Virginia, coal came out of the mine mouth around 1960 for something like four or five dollars a ton. That was the price, and by the logic of price it was the whole story: a fair measure of what the present would pay for a ton of coal at the margin. But the price was a mask, and behind it stood every cost the sale had severed and left for someone else to absorb. The lungs of the miners. The streams and the ridgelines. And, dwarfing the rest, the carbon. A ton of coal burns to roughly two and a half tons of carbon dioxide, and at a social cost of carbon near a hundred and ninety dollars a ton, the carbon term alone comes to about five hundred and ten dollars. Add the other severed costs back and the honest price of that ton of coal lands somewhere around five hundred and twenty-four dollars, in a range from the high five hundreds and lower. It sold for four. The county kept the difference between four and five hundred and twenty-four, except that "kept" is the wrong word, because the county did not keep it. The county paid it, in poverty and illness and a hollowed-out future, while the value flowed elsewhere. The honest ledger does not change what happened in McDowell County. It only refuses, at last, to pretend the price was the truth.

## Two directions, two instruments

The framework, then, runs in both directions, and each direction wants its own instrument.

Run it forward, against a loss not yet taken, and the instrument is something we might call a Foreclosure Bond. Before the barrel is burned or the seam is cut, you price the future loss as honestly as you can, the residual commons value, the option you are about to foreclose for everyone downstream and downtime, and you set that amount aside. You do not pretend the loss is zero because the present cannot feel it. You write it on the ledger and you fund it.

Run it backward, against a loss already realised, and the instrument is a Restitution Bond. The extraction is done; the value has already flowed away from the people who bore the cost. Honest accounting here means naming what was taken, from whom, and beginning to set it right. And this backward direction is not my invention. It builds directly on the work of William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen, whose book From Here to Equality is, read in this light, a piece of backward honest accounting on the largest scale in American history. They trace the racial wealth gap that runs from 1619 to the present, the severed costs of slavery and the century of legal subordination that followed, costs created by some and absorbed, generation after generation, by others who could bill no one. They count, among other things, the Black-white longevity gap, the measurable difference in years of life that stands as a legacy effect of all that severance, a cost still being paid in the only currency that cannot be earned back. Reparations economics, in their hands, is the Restitution Bond made real: the ledger run backward over a realised extraction. Ta-Nehisi Coates, in The Case for Reparations, built the moral architecture that makes the case in public; Darity and Mullen built the accounting.

A discipline is required here, and I want to be plain about it. To put a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not my work to do. The framework reaches the coerced cases through their legacy effects, the longevity gap, the wealth gap, the measurable residues that can be counted from outside, and it defers the direct pricing of the coercion itself to those who are living it and continuing the work. That deferral is not an evasion. It is part of the honesty. An honest ledger knows the limits of who may hold the pen on which lines.

## One act, two architectures

Whether the future can actually be priced and held, rather than only described, is a fair question, and there is an existence proof. Its name is Norway.

Oil was confirmed off Norway's stretch of the North Sea just before Christmas in 1969, at the Ekofisk field, and a poor, fish-and-timber country on the northern edge of Europe found itself sitting on a fortune in liquidated barrels. The temptation, the one nearly every other oil state surrendered to, was to spend it as it came. Norway did something close to the opposite. In 1990 it established a fund to hold the petroleum revenue, the Government Petroleum Fund, renamed in 2006 the Government Pension Fund Global, and in 2001 it bound its own hands with a fiscal rule: the state may spend only the expected real return on the fund, around three percent a year, and must leave the capital itself inviolate. The principal is treated as what it is, the converted form of a one-time inheritance, not income to be consumed but a reserve to be kept. The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every Norwegian citizen, and the most remarkable thing about it is political rather than financial. Governments of the whole spectrum have come and gone, and none of any colour has raided the principal. This is Hartwick's rule lifted to sovereign scale, the rents reinvested and the capital kept whole, and it is also, fittingly, a reserve fund, money held back against a future that has not yet arrived. Norway looked at the mask of abundance, saw the future behind it, and decided to pay the future first.

Set beside it the Niger Delta, where the same physical act took a different shape. Oil has been pumped from the Delta since the nineteen-fifties, generating hundreds of billions of dollars in revenue across the decades. But the costs were severed and left where they fell: the spills that fouled the creeks, the flares that burned day and night, the fisheries foreclosed for the people who fished them. Those costs were borne by communities that had not sold the oil and could bill no one for what it took from them. When the writer Ken Saro-Wiwa led a movement to demand that the people of Ogoniland be counted in the ledger, he and eight others were executed in 1995, an act that drew Nigeria's suspension from the Commonwealth and the condemnation of the world. Years later a United Nations assessment of the contamination in Ogoniland alone put the initial cleanup at something like a billion dollars over twenty-five to thirty years, a number that begins to hint at the scale of the severed costs that were never on anyone's books.

I am not telling a villain story, and I am not arguing against extraction. The point is architectural. Norway and the Niger Delta performed the same physical act, the drawing of a finite resource out of the shared ground, and built two different machines around it. One priced the future and set a reserve aside. The other let the costs sever and fall where they would. The question the two cases pose between them is not whether to extract. It is the question the mask exists to hide: when the cost of an act outlives the sale that authorised it, who carries that cost, and has anything been set aside against it? That is not a political program. It is an accounting question, and either you answer it honestly or you do not.

## The miner and the reserve

Now let me run the lens the other way, out to where there is almost no cost to sever at all. Imagine an asteroid miner. Again, I am not predicting; I am borrowing the figure as an instrument. Out in the deep field, around a lump of metal and rock with no community living on it and no ecosystem woven through it, the whole apparatus of cost severance falls nearly to zero. There is no town downwind, because there is no wind and no town. There is no fishery to foreclose, no generation of asteroid-dwellers to inherit the loss. Take the iron. Take all of it. If a comet swings briefly into reach and will not return for a thousand years, take from it aggressively while it is there, because no one waits behind you for what you do not take. The honest ledger, applied here, does not whisper restraint. It says extract, and extract freely, because the residual commons value is genuinely close to nothing.

And yet the very same ledger, in the very same breath, imposes an absolute discipline, and it is the discipline the whole essay has been circling. On the asteroid, on the comet, in the deep field, you must hold reserves. Fuel enough to be certain you can leave. Air enough, water enough, life-support enough, to be sure you can get off the rock and home, or on to the next world. The ledger that says "take freely here" is the same ledger that says "reserve there," and they are not in tension; they are the same sentence read at two scales. Honest accounting was never anti-extraction. It is the practice of knowing the real cost of a thing, including the cost of the future you are extracting yourself into, and keeping enough back to survive that future. Strip away the community and the ecosystem and you do not strip away the discipline. You only reduce it to its irreducible core: do not spend the reserve you need to live.

## Whatever world, whatever economy

This is why the framework is not a rival economic system, and I want to be careful to say so. It is not capitalism or its replacement; it is not the plan against the market. It is the accounting that any of them needs, the honest ledger underneath whatever system is built on top, and the systems we admire most are best understood as different attempts to keep that ledger honestly.

Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics draws a safe and just space between a social floor no one should fall below and an ecological ceiling no one should breach, which is, at bottom, a way of writing the commons back onto the books, of refusing to let the priceless go uncounted. Ostrom's governed commons are communities binding themselves to a sustainable rate, which is the renewable half of the same honesty. And the impulse runs back far further than any modern theory. Civilisations have kept reserves against scarcity for as long as there have been civilisations to do it: ancient China maintained granaries to steady the price of grain across good years and bad, and ancient Egypt ran state granaries against the years the harvest failed. I will not invent the particulars, and I do not need to. The shape is the point. A society looks at its own abundance, declines to read the abundance as permission to spend everything, and holds something back against the lean time it knows will come. Capitalist or planned, ancient or off-world, the test is always the same. Either the ledger is honest, the real costs are seen and the reserves are kept, or the economy is flying blind, mistaking the mask for the face and the price for the worth.

## The longest horizon

If reserves are the discipline, then the question of how long a horizon to reserve against is not idle, and the honest answer is longer than we usually allow ourselves to think. We keep reserves for the next winter, and we have learned, slowly, to keep them for the next generation. But a finite resource, honestly accounted, asks to be reserved against civilisational time.

Consider that the Sun is slowly brightening. The work of Sackmann and colleagues, in the early nineteen-nineties, put the moment when the rising solar luminosity renders Earth uninhabitable on the order of five hundred million to a billion years from now, long before the far more famous ending, the red-giant Sun that Schröder and Smith calculated would swell to engulf the planet around seven and a half billion years out. I raise this not to be grand but to be exact about the length of the ledger. On those horizons, the sealed habitat I asked you to imagine at the start stops being science fiction and becomes something more sober: a coherent form of option-preservation across deep time, a reserve held not against the next bad harvest but against the eventual failure of the only home we have ever had. The asteroid miner, the off-world habitat, the reserve held on a rock you barely visited: read across the longest horizon, these are not adventures. They are the same prudence that fills a granary, extended to the scale on which a species, rather than a harvest, is the thing being kept.

## The number was always there

Take a breath again. It still costs nothing, and you can still feel, now, that the nothing was never a verdict on its worth. The whole framework, run forward as a Foreclosure Bond against losses not yet taken, run backward as a Restitution Bond against losses already realised, lifted to sovereign scale in a North Sea fund or abandoned in a fouled delta, carried out to an asteroid where the costs vanish but the reserve must not, traced back to granaries older than any economics, is finally a single, modest, demanding practice. It is honest accounting, of a kind an individual can keep, a community can keep, a government can keep, on this world or on any other. One ledger, run in either direction, on any scale.

The mask of abundance is only a mask. Behind it the value was always there, total and unpriced and waiting, the way the air is waiting in your lungs right now. We mistook the absence of a price for the absence of worth, and built our books around the mistake. Taking the mask off changes nothing about the world; the air was always priceless, the barrel was always finite, the future was always owed. It changes only what we are willing to see, and therefore what we are able to choose. Whether the breath stays free, whether the reserve gets kept, whether the future arrives to find that anything was set aside for it, is not written in the stars or the markets. It is, still, a thing we get to decide.
