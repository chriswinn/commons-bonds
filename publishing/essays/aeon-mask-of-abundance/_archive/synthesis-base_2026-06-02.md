# The Mask of Abundance

Stand outside on an ordinary morning and take a breath. Nothing happens. No meter clicks over, no balance is debited, no notice arrives at the end of the month. The air costs you nothing, and because it costs you nothing you do not think of it as worth anything in particular. This is the quiet trick that abundance plays. The things you most depend on, the things without which an afternoon would not be survivable, carry a price of approximately zero, and you read that price as a verdict on their worth. The verdict runs exactly backwards. The price of air is near zero not because air has little value but because air has nothing but value, distributed so completely through the world that no market can find an edge to charge at.

It helps to be precise about what a market price actually measures. It measures scarcity at the margin: what someone will give up to obtain the next unit of a thing that is not freely available to all. Air is freely available to all. The atmosphere does not run a checkout. So the price posted for a lungful of it is nil, and the nil is honest as far as it goes. What it conceals is that the value is not nil but total. A thing you cannot live ten minutes without is not worth little. It is worth everything. Abundance is the mask that lets a thing of total value wear the face of a thing worth nothing at all.

The concealment is not the ordinary kind. We are used to things that are underpriced, their value real but understated, the way the work of nurses or the topsoil under an industrial field carries a number that is simply too low. The great commons are stranger than that. Breathable air, drinkable water, the patient carrying-away of the carbon dioxide we exhale, a climate stable enough to plant by: these have no price at all, and a thing without a price is, to the only instrument modern societies trust for reckoning worth, not cheap but invisible. We guard what it would cost us something to lose. The mask makes sure the loss never registers as a cost until it is too late to be only a cost.

Most of the work of honest accounting runs the other way, and it is worth seeing how this case inverts it. When a factory fouls a river without paying for the harm, the task is to recover a cost the market managed to sever from a price, to make the polluter carry what the polluter offloaded onto the people downstream. The commons present the opposite problem. Here there is no severed cost waiting to be reattached. Here there is a value so complete and so evenly spread that it never had to acquire a price in the first place. The market never named it because the market never had to, and what is never named is easy to neglect. We under-protect the commons not in spite of their importance but because of it, because importance at that scale, thinned across every breathing thing, disappears into the background and stops reading as importance at all.

How would you ever see the value, then, if abundance is so good at hiding it? You would have to take the abundance away. Seal the system, cut it off from the free atmosphere that does the hiding, and watch what the prices do.

So consider, as a way of seeing the mask for what it is, a sealed world. Picture a closed habitat somewhere off the Earth, a station or a buried colony, where the air does not arrive for free because there is no atmosphere outside the wall to draw it from. I am not predicting this place. I am borrowing it as a lens, the way a physicist borrows a frictionless plane. Inside the seal, every commons the Earth supplies without a bill becomes a line item. You pay, by usage, for a metered breath. You pay for a litre of water that has been through someone's body and a reclamation loop before it reaches your cup. You pay for the carrying-away of the carbon dioxide you exhale, because in a sealed volume that gas does not disperse into an obliging sky; it gathers until something expensive removes it. The habitat is a moral X-ray. It shows the skeleton of value that the soft tissue of abundance keeps hidden. It does not change what these things are worth. It only strips off the mask and lets you read the number that was always underneath.

The lens is only a lens. But the thing it reveals is not hypothetical, because the sealed world already exists for some people, and the bills already arrive.

Go down far enough beneath the sea and you cannot breathe ordinary air; the nitrogen in it turns narcotic and the oxygen turns toxic under that much pressure. Commercial saturation divers breathe heliox instead, a blend of helium and oxygen, and the helium is not free. It runs somewhere in the range of $1.70 to $2.75 a cubic foot, and a technical diver running the mix can spend on the order of $80 to $200 an hour on the helium alone. The economics are tight enough that the support vessels run reclaim systems, plumbing built to recapture the exhaled gas rather than vent it overboard, because helium breathed out is money breathed out. Think about what that machinery means. Up here a breath is a thing you let go of without a thought, irretrievable and worthless. Down there a breath is dear enough that an industry has built equipment to catch it on the way out of the body.

Then there is Biosphere 2, the most literal version of the experiment anyone has built. Between 1991 and 1993, eight people sealed themselves inside a little over three acres of glassed-in world in the Arizona desert, a closed system meant to be self-sustaining, with farmland and rainforest and a miniature ocean under one roof. It cost something near $150 million to make. And the air, the part nobody is supposed to have to think about, began to fail. Oxygen drifted down from the ordinary 21 per cent toward 14 over sixteen months, far enough to leave the crew labouring on the stairs, as the microbes in the rich soil respired faster than anyone had modelled and the carbon dioxide they produced reacted with the curing concrete and vanished from the cycle. In the end the managers gave up on purity and pumped oxygen in from outside, conceding that the sealed world could not, unaided, make the one thing the open world makes for nothing. The living system frayed alongside the air. Nineteen of the twenty-five vertebrate species inside died out. Every pollinator died, so the crops had to be pollinated by hand. The lesson of Biosphere 2 was not that the engineers were incompetent. It was the opposite: how staggeringly hard, how expensive, and how prone to silent failure it is to reproduce by deliberate effort the services the Earth renders for nothing, around the clock, without a control room.

Lift the question off the planet entirely and the numbers only sharpen. Aboard the International Space Station, water is the costliest substance there is, because every kilogram lifted to that altitude costs on the order of $21,000 to put there, and a body wants its litres every day. A six-month supply at the rate a person needs runs into the realm of $135 million if you simply launch it, which is why the station does not. Its life-support system reclaims something close to 90 per cent of the crew's sweat and urine and turns it back into drinking water, and it makes the breathable oxygen by splitting that hard-won water with electricity. The two things that on Earth fall from the sky and pool in the ground and cost nothing are, a few hundred kilometres up, the most precious cargo aboard. Nothing about the water has changed. Only the seal has changed, and with it, what you can see.

The diver, the Biosphere crew, the astronaut are not living in a different physics from the rest of us. They are living in the same world with the mask removed, in the small sealed pockets where abundance has been switched off and the true price of the commons has come due. Their air and their water are not worth more than ours. Theirs is simply priced, and ours is not, and the gap between the price and the worth is the whole of what abundance conceals.

This is the uncomfortable thing the lens shows, and the reason the mask is dangerous rather than merely curious. We do not protect the commons in proportion to what they are worth, because the signal we usually trust, the price, is broken precisely where the value is greatest. The atmosphere posts a price of zero and we believe it. The water cycle posts a price of zero and we believe it. We pour our effort into guarding the things the market has bothered to name and let the unnamed essentials run down, not from greed alone and not from stupidity, but from a category error built into the only instrument we trust to tell us what matters. The instrument reads abundance as worthlessness. It cannot help it. So the air goes unguarded and the climate goes unpriced and the great cycling systems of the living planet are treated as a free input rather than the irreplaceable inheritance they are, until the supply tightens somewhere and the bill arrives, the way it arrived for the crew in the desert when the air went thin.

Take the breath again, knowing all this. It is still free. That was never the question. The question is what the freedom was hiding, and now you know. The sealed worlds have already filed the report, and the lesson they bring back is not that we should put a meter on the sky. It is that the sky was always the most valuable thing we owned, and that abundance was never proof of its cheapness. The air around you is not cheap. It has simply never sent you the bill. Whether it stays that way is, for once, a thing we still get to decide.
