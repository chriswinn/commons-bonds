# The Mask of Abundance

Stand outside on an ordinary morning and take a breath. Nothing happens. No meter clicks over, no balance is debited, no notice arrives at the end of the month. The air costs you nothing, and because it costs you nothing you do not think of it as worth anything in particular. This is the quiet trick that abundance plays. The things we most depend on, the things without which an afternoon would not be survivable, carry a price of approximately zero, and we read that price as a statement about their value. We have it exactly backwards.

Consider what a market price actually measures. It measures scarcity at the margin: what someone will give up to obtain the next unit of a thing that is not freely available to all. Air is freely available to all. The atmosphere does not run a checkout. So the price the market posts for a lungful of it is nil, and the nil is honest as far as it goes. What it conceals is that the value is not nil but total. A thing you cannot live ten minutes without is not worth little. It is worth everything. Abundance is the mask that lets a thing of total value wear the face of a thing worth nothing at all.

Most of the work of honest accounting goes the other way. When a factory dumps into a river without paying for the harm, the usual task is to recover a cost the market managed to sever from a price, to make the polluter carry what the polluter offloaded. The commons of breathable air, drinkable water, the patient carrying-away of our exhaled carbon, the slow self-correction of a stable climate, present the inverse problem. Here there is no severed cost waiting to be reattached. Here there is a value so complete and so evenly distributed that it never had to acquire a price in the first place. The market never named it because the market never had to. And what is never named is easy to neglect. We under-protect the commons not in spite of their importance but because of it, because importance at that scale, spread that thin across every breathing thing, disappears into the background and stops registering as importance at all.

How would you ever see the value, then, if abundance is so good at hiding it? You would have to take the abundance away. Seal the system. Cut it off from the free atmosphere that does the hiding, and watch what the prices do.

Suppose, for the sake of the experiment, that you live in a sealed habitat. Call it an off-world colony if you like, though the location is beside the point; what matters is the seal. Here the atmosphere does not arrive for free. Here a breath is manufactured and metered, a litre of water is reclaimed at cost from yesterday's sweat and urine, the removal of the carbon dioxide you exhale is a service somebody pays for by the hour. This is not a forecast. I am not telling you where we are going. The habitat is a lens, an instrument for seeing, a way of running cost severance in reverse so that the value abundance had concealed swings suddenly into view. Inside the seal, the cheapest things on Earth become the most expensive things there are. Air leads the bill. Water is a luxury good. The privilege of having your exhalations carried away, which the planet performs for you tirelessly and without invoice, turns into a line item. The habitat does not change what these things are worth. It only strips off the mask and lets you read the number that was always underneath.

The strange part is that the experiment is not hypothetical for everyone. Some people already live inside the seal, and the bill already arrives.

A saturation diver, working at depth on a commercial contract, does not breathe the air the rest of us breathe. The nitrogen in ordinary air turns narcotic and then toxic under that much pressure, so the diver breathes heliox, a blend of helium and oxygen, and the helium is not free. It runs somewhere in the range of $1.70 to $2.75 a cubic foot, and a technical diver running the mix can spend on the order of $80 to $200 an hour on the helium alone. The economics are tight enough that the support vessels run reclaim systems, plumbing to recapture the exhaled gas rather than vent it overboard, because helium breathed out is money breathed out. The diver inhabits, by necessity, the world the rest of us are insulated from: a world in which a breath is a metered quantity with a running cost, and you watch it the way you watch any expense.

Then there is Biosphere 2, the most literal version of the experiment anyone has built. Between 1991 and 1993, eight people sealed themselves inside a little over three acres of glassed-in world in the Arizona desert, a closed system meant to be self-sustaining, with farmland and rainforest and a miniature ocean under one roof. It cost something like $150 million to make. And the air, the part nobody is supposed to have to think about, began to fail. Oxygen drifted down from the ambient 21 per cent toward 14, falling far enough over sixteen months to leave the crew labouring and unwell, until managers gave up on purity and pumped oxygen in from outside. The microbes in the rich soil were respiring faster than anyone had modelled, and the carbon dioxide they produced was reacting with the concrete of the structure and vanishing from the cycle. The living world inside frayed alongside the air. Nineteen of twenty-five vertebrate species died out. Every pollinator died, which meant the crops had to be pollinated by hand. The lesson of Biosphere 2 was not that the engineers were incompetent. It was the opposite. It was how staggeringly hard, and how expensive, and how prone to silent failure it is to reproduce by deliberate effort the services that the Earth renders for nothing, around the clock, without a control room.

And in orbit, where the seal is absolute, the accounting is brutally explicit. Aboard the International Space Station, water is the costliest substance there is, because every kilogram lifted to that altitude costs on the order of $21,000 to put there; a six-month supply at the rate a body needs runs into the realm of $135 million. So the station does not waste it. Its life-support system reclaims something close to 90 per cent of the crew's sweat and urine and turns it back into drinking water, and it makes the crew's oxygen by splitting that water with electricity. The two things that on Earth fall from the sky and pool in the ground and cost nothing are, a few hundred kilometres up, the most precious cargo aboard. Nothing about the water has changed. Only the seal has changed, and with it, what you can see.

What the diver and the crew of Biosphere 2 and the astronaut all share is that they have stepped out from under the mask. They are not living in a different physics. They are living in the version of our own situation where the bill is itemised and presented, where the abundance that hides value at sea level has been deliberately, expensively withdrawn. Their air and their water are not worth more than ours. Theirs is simply priced, and ours is not, and the gap between the price and the worth is the whole of what abundance conceals.

This is the uncomfortable thing the lens shows. We do not protect the commons in proportion to what they are worth, because the signal we usually trust, the price, is broken precisely where the value is greatest. The atmosphere posts a price of zero and we believe it. The water cycle posts a price of zero and we believe it. We pour effort into guarding things the market has bothered to name and let the unnamed essentials run down, not out of stupidity but out of a reasonable habit of attending to what carries a number. The mask of abundance is not a metaphor for our ignorance. It is the actual mechanism of it. The free thing reads as the worthless thing, and the planet's most complete gift is the one we are likeliest to squander, because it never once handed us a bill, and we mistook the silence for a verdict on its value.

Take the breath again, knowing all this. It is still free. That was never the question. The question is what the freedom was hiding, and now you know. The seal is not somewhere we have to go to find out. The seal is the experiment that lets us read, while we are still on the open ground, the price tag that abundance has been keeping turned away from us all along.
