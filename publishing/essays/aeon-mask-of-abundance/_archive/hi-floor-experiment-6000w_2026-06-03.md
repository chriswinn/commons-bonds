# The Mask of Abundance

Take a breath. You did not pay for it, and you will not pay for the next one, and across the whole length of your life the running total for the air you have consumed will come to nothing at all. This is not because the air is worthless. It is the opposite. The air is the precondition of everything you value, the silent partner in every transaction you have ever made, the thing without which there is no you to do the valuing. It costs nothing because it is everything, distributed so completely and so evenly that no one can stand between you and it and charge a toll. A price is a measure of scarcity at the margin. It answers a narrow question: what would it cost to get a little more of this thing, or what would someone give up to keep from losing a little of it. Where there is no edge, where the next unit is as free as the last and the last as free as the first, the price falls to zero and stays there. The market has nothing to grip.

We have trained ourselves to read that zero as a verdict. Free, we say, as if the word meant unimportant. The air, the rain, the ocean's slow swallowing of the carbon we pour into the sky, the steadiness of a climate that has held still long enough for cities and harvests and the whole improbable architecture of settled life, all of these post a price of zero, and we have learned to treat the zero as the thing's assessment of its own worth. It is nothing of the kind. It is a measurement error built into the only instrument we trust. The price tag was designed to find scarcity, and where there is abundance it returns a blank, and we mistake the blank for a low number.

This is worth separating from a more familiar complaint. We often say that certain goods are underpriced. The nurse is underpriced, paid less than the work is worth to those she keeps alive. The topsoil is underpriced, its slow exhaustion invisible in the cost of a loaf. Those are real problems, and they share a structure: a price exists, and it is wrong, set too low by a market that cannot see the whole value or chooses not to. The commons are a stranger case. They are not underpriced. They have no price at all. The nurse's wage can in principle be raised; there is a number, and we could change it. The air has no number to change. It is not cheap. It is invisible to the instrument, and a thing that is invisible to the only instrument we trust may as well, for the purposes of every ledger we keep, not exist.

So begin there, with the mask. Abundance is the mask. It hides the value not by making it small but by making it total, and a total value, spread evenly across everyone, looks from inside the market exactly like nothing. The argument of this essay is that there is a way to take the mask off, and that the way is the same every time, on every scale, in either direction in time. Find the value that abundance, or distance, or sheer duration has hidden, and put it on the ledger honestly. That is the whole move. Everything that follows is that one move, repeated, widening.

## Seal the room

Here is a lens, and I want to be clear that it is a lens and not a forecast. I am not predicting that you will live in a sealed habitat. I am asking you to imagine one, because imagining it does something useful: it pulls the mask off the air by removing the abundance that holds the mask in place.

Picture a closed system. A station, a buried colony, a glass dome over a patch of desert. Nothing comes in, nothing goes out, except what you carry across the boundary at a cost you can read. Inside such a system, every one of the priceless commons acquires a price overnight, and the price is enormous, because now there is an edge. Now there is a margin. Now someone can stand between you and your next breath, not out of malice but out of arithmetic, because the next breath has become a unit that must be manufactured, and manufacturing has a cost. The air that was free because it was everywhere becomes, in the sealed room, the most expensive thing there is.

We do not have to imagine this entirely. The bill already arrives, in three real places, whenever we seal a system enough.

Go down with the saturation divers, the ones who work the deep for days at a stretch, living under pressure in steel chambers so they do not have to surface between shifts. They do not breathe air. At depth the ordinary mix turns against you: the nitrogen that is harmless at the surface grows narcotic in the blood under pressure, and the oxygen that keeps you alive becomes, past a certain partial pressure, a poison. So they breathe a blend leaning hard on helium, and helium is not cheap. It runs somewhere around a dollar seventy to two and three-quarters a cubic foot, and a working diver can burn through enough of it that the helium alone costs eighty to two hundred dollars an hour. The ships run reclaim rigs to capture and recirculate the exhaled gas, because at those rates you do not let the breath go. Down there, the thing that costs you nothing on the dock has an hourly meter running, and the meter is the truth that the surface hides.

Then there is Biosphere 2, the great sealed experiment of the early nineties: eight people closed inside roughly three acres of glass for two years, a small fortune, about a hundred and fifty million dollars, spent to wall off a piece of the world and run it as its own economy of air and water and food. The oxygen inside fell. Over sixteen months it dropped from the ordinary twenty-one percent toward fourteen, the level you would find on a high mountain, as the soil microbes respired faster than expected and the fresh concrete drank carbon dioxide out of the atmosphere and locked it away. The crew grew slow and short of breath. In the end the managers pumped oxygen in from outside, which is to say the sealed system could not, after all, keep its own books balanced, and had to be bailed out by the abundance beyond the glass. Nineteen of the twenty-five vertebrate species inside died. Every pollinator died, so the crops had to be pollinated by hand. The lesson was not that the project failed. The lesson was the price tag the failure exposed: inside the glass, a stable breathable atmosphere turned out to be a staggeringly expensive thing to produce and maintain, and outside the glass we get it for nothing and call it free.

And then there is the station overhead, a few hundred kilometers up, where the accounting is exact because everything has to be lifted there against gravity at a known rate. Water to orbit costs on the order of twenty-one thousand dollars a kilogram, so a six-month supply for a crew runs to something like a hundred and thirty-five million dollars, give or take an order of magnitude. The station cannot afford to drink water once and discard it, so it recovers roughly ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine and turns it back into something to drink, and it makes its oxygen by splitting water with electricity, the same water it worked so hard to lift. Up there, the air you breathe is a manufactured product with a supply chain, and water is treasure, and the only reason either is free for you, reading this on the ground, is that you live inside an abundance so vast that no edge has ever formed where someone could charge you.

Seal the room and the mask comes off. The priceless reveals itself as the most expensive thing there is. The work of the rest of this essay is to take the mask off without sealing the room, to read the true number while the abundance is still in place.

## The mechanism

To do that we need a name for what the mask is hiding, and the name has two parts.

The first part is cost severance. Most of the time, when economists worry about a price being wrong, the trouble is that a cost has been severed from the transaction that created it. Someone does a thing, the thing imposes a cost, and the cost lands on someone else who had no say and has no way to bill it back. The factory makes the widget and the river carries away the effluent; the buyer of the widget pays for the steel and the labor and the shipping but not for the river, because the river cannot send an invoice. The cost was created in one place and severed, and absorbed somewhere downstream by a party who never signed. Honest pricing, in these ordinary cases, means re-attaching the severed cost: finding the river's bill and adding it back to the widget.

The second part is stranger, and it is the part the commons forces on us. Call it the residual commons value. When a sale is done, when the price has been paid and the cost re-attached as best we can, there is sometimes a value left over that was never severed, because it never had a price to begin with. It is the worth that remains unpriced after the transaction closes, and it is owed to people who were not at the table. Often they are not at the table because they have no standing in markets. Often they are not at the table because they are not yet born. The residual commons value is what the market cannot reach not by accident but by construction, because the value was total and therefore invisible, and a sale conducted in the dark cannot account for what it could not see.

Most honest accounting is the first kind: re-attaching a severed cost. The commons case is the second kind, and it is the harder one, because there is no severed cost to find and reconnect. There is a value so complete that it never acquired a price, and it becomes visible only when scarcity finally arrives, which is to say only when it is too late to charge for it in the ordinary way. The whole problem is to charge for it before the scarcity, to read the bill while the abundance still holds. That is what putting the residual commons value on the ledger means.

## A barrel of oil

Let me make this concrete with a number, and let me flag that the number is illustrative. Say a barrel of oil sells for a hundred dollars. I am not arguing that a hundred is the right figure or the current one; it is a round number to think with.

What does the hundred dollars buy, and what does it leave out? It buys the present its tank of gas. It pays for the finding and the pumping and the refining and the shipping, the costs of bringing the barrel out of the ground and into the engine, and it pays the present's price for the present's use. Priced for the present, the hundred dollars is roughly fair.

But the thing being sold does not belong only to the present. The barrel is a piece of a stock that took a hundred million years to lay down and will not be laid down again on any timescale that means anything to us. When you burn it, it is gone, converted to motion and heat and a plume of carbon, and there is no slower rate at which you could have taken it that would let it grow back, because it does not grow. This is the distinction that the language of sustainability usually blurs, and it is worth keeping sharp. A renewable resource poses a question of rate. A forest, a fishery, an aquifer fed by rain: these reproduce, and the only honest question is whether you are taking less than the system breeds back, drawing the interest and leaving the principal. Take at the right rate and you can take forever. Oil poses no question of rate, because there is no rate slow enough. Every barrel burned is a barrel liquidated, subtracted permanently from a stock that does not replenish. The hundred dollars buys the present its tank of gas. It does not buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after, because nothing can.

So there is a residual commons value in the barrel that the hundred dollars does not touch: the value of the barrel's continued existence to everyone who will live after the present has burned it. The market cannot see this value, because the people it belongs to are not at the table, and most of them are not yet born. The barrel is priced for the present and consumed by the present, and the future, who owned a share of it too, is simply not invoiced. The mask here is not abundance exactly; it is duration. The value is hidden not because the thing is everywhere but because the claimants are distributed across time we cannot see into, and a market that prices at the margin in the present has no instrument that reaches them.

## The empty plot

Here is something that should give us pause. This is not a new thought. Several of the deepest traditions in economics and moral philosophy walked right up to the edge of this empty plot of ground, looked at it, and built their houses next door. It is worth naming them, briefly, because the pattern of the near-miss is itself instructive: each tradition saw a real thing and priced it precisely, and each priced something adjacent to the residual value of the liquidated commons rather than that value itself.

Marx, in Capital, priced the surplus. He showed how value is extracted from labor and accumulates as capital, and he built a whole architecture on the gap between what work produces and what work is paid. But the surplus he priced was a surplus across class, in the present, between owner and worker. He did not price the surplus across time, the share taken from the unborn. The axis was social, not temporal.

Elinor Ostrom, in Governing the Commons, did something that earned her a Nobel and deserved it: she showed that communities can manage shared resources without either privatizing them or handing them to the state, that the famous tragedy is not a law of nature but a failure of governance that real people, in real places, have repeatedly solved. But the commons she governed were renewable ones, the pastures and fisheries and irrigation systems that pose the question of rate. Her genius was in sustaining the stock that breeds back. The liquidated commons, the stock that does not, was not her subject.

Herman Daly spent a career warning that the economy is a subsystem of a finite biosphere and cannot grow through it forever, that throughput matters, that a steady state is the only honest long-run goal. He was right, and he was early, and he was largely ignored until the bills started coming due. But a warning about throughput in general is not an invoice for one foreclosed thing in particular. Daly told us the room was filling up. He did not hand us the bill for the specific barrel.

John Hartwick gave us a rule, elegant and exactly aimed: when you draw down a non-renewable resource, reinvest the rents, the pure scarcity value, into reproducible capital, so that the capacity to produce is sustained even as the stock is depleted. Save the rent and you can spend the resource without impoverishing the future. This is closer than any of the others, and we will see it built into stone later in this essay. But Hartwick saved the rent, the price the market already records, the scarcity premium baked into the sale. He did not reach past the rent to the residual value the market never recorded at all, the worth of the barrel's existence to those who will never get to choose.

Then the moral philosophers converged on the question from the other side. John Rawls asked us to choose the rules of a society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which life inside it would be ours, and others widened the veil until it covered not just which person you might be but which generation, so that justice between the born and the unborn became a question you could not dodge by being lucky in your birth year. Michael Sandel asked what money should not be allowed to buy, mapping the goods that markets corrupt simply by pricing them, and the commons sit squarely in that territory: things whose nature is changed, maybe degraded, the moment a price is stuck to them. Derek Parfit, in Reasons and Persons, worked out with terrible care what we owe to future people and how strange our obligations to them become, given that our choices determine not only how they live but whether those particular people exist at all. The philosophers built the moral case for the claim of the unborn. They did not, for the most part, hand it to the economists as a number.

And then the economists who did try to put a number on the future fought a war over the discount rate. How much is a benefit a century from now worth today? Nicholas Stern, in his review, argued for a low rate, around one and a half percent, on the grounds that we have no moral right to discount future people's welfare just because it is future, and concluded that strong action now was cheap insurance, on the order of one percent of global output. William Nordhaus, building the model that won him a Nobel, used a rate closer to four percent, drawn from how markets actually trade present against future, and concluded for a gentler, slower response. The gap between them is enormous when you run it out. A hundred dollars of benefit a century from now is worth about twenty-five dollars today at Stern's rate and about a dollar fifty at Nordhaus's. Martin Weitzman pulled the argument in a third direction, pointing at the fat tail, the small but real chance of catastrophe so large that no ordinary discounting captures it, and arguing that the possibility of the worst should dominate the calculation.

But notice what all three are pricing. They are pricing the damage flow from combustion: the heat, the storms, the floods, the harm that follows from putting carbon in the sky, discounted back to the present at whatever rate. They are not pricing the resource's absence. The residual commons value of the barrel is not a flow of future damages to be discounted. It is the present fact of a permanent subtraction, the simple, undiscounted truth that a thing which existed will not exist, that an option open today will be closed forever. And because it is a present absence rather than a future flow, it survives even Stern's low rate. You cannot discount it away, because there is no time-distance to discount across. The barrel is gone now. The option is foreclosed now. The discount-rate war was fought over the right ground but the wrong quantity, and the residual value walked through the battlefield untouched.

## Running it backward

So far I have run the framework forward, pricing a loss before it happens. But the same ledger runs in reverse, on extraction already done, and to see that, leave the abstractions and go to a place.

Go to McDowell County, in the southern coalfields of West Virginia, where the mountains were opened and the coal taken out across generations. At the mine mouth, around 1960, that coal sold for something like four or five dollars a ton. That was the price, and the price was honest in the narrow sense the market means: it covered the cost of getting the coal out and a margin besides, and buyer and seller agreed to it freely.

Now re-attach the severed costs. Add back what the four dollars left out, beginning with the carbon. Burning a ton of coal puts roughly two and a half tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, and if you price that carbon at the kind of figure the damage actually warrants, call it around a hundred and ninety dollars a ton of carbon dioxide, the carbon term alone comes to something near five hundred and ten dollars. Add the rest of the severed costs, the health and the water and the foreclosed futures of the place, and the honest price of that ton of coal lands somewhere around five hundred and twenty-four dollars, in a range from the high five hundreds down toward five hundred and eighteen, against a mine-mouth price of four or five. The coal was sold for a hundredth of what it cost. The other ninety-nine hundredths were severed and scattered across the atmosphere and the bodies and the water of people who never signed the contract, many of whom were not yet born when it was signed.

That is the framework run backward: take a realized extraction, find the severed costs and the residual value the sale never recorded, and total them honestly. The number is not a fine and not an accusation. It is a measurement. It says what the thing actually cost, as opposed to what it sold for, and the gap between the two is the size of the mask.

## Two directions, two instruments

The framework, then, runs in both directions in time, and each direction wants its own instrument.

Run it forward and you get something like a Foreclosure Bond. Before the extraction, price the future loss, the residual commons value about to be subtracted, and set that sum aside, held against the permanent absence you are about to create. You cannot give the barrel back, but you can hold, in trust, the measure of what its loss is worth, so that the future inherits the value even if it cannot inherit the thing.

Run it backward and you get something like a Restitution Bond. After the extraction, when the costs have already landed on people who could not bill them, total those costs honestly and set the total aside as what is owed. This is not a new idea either, and the backward direction builds directly on work that already exists. William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen, in From Here to Equality, make the economic case for reparations as exactly this kind of backward honest accounting. They trace the costs severed from Black Americans since 1619 and never billed back, and they show that the consequences are measurable in the present: a racial wealth gap that did not appear from nowhere, a gap in longevity between Black and white Americans that is itself a quantifiable legacy of costs imposed and never paid. Their book is the reverse direction at national scale, and Ta-Nehisi Coates's essay on the case for reparations is its argument-architecture companion, the moral frame around the economic ledger.

There is a discipline that goes with the backward direction, and I want to state it plainly because it constrains what I am claiming. The framework reaches coerced cases through their legacy effects: the measurable wealth gap, the measurable longevity gap, the residue of severed costs that shows up in the present and can be counted. It does not presume to price the coercion itself. To put a number on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not my work to do; that pricing belongs to the people living inside that history and continuing the accounting. The framework reaches such cases at one remove, through the legacy effects that are visible and countable, and defers the direct pricing of the coercion vector to those whose ledger it is.

## An existence proof and its shadow

Is any of this practical? Can a society actually price the future and set it aside, or is the Foreclosure Bond just a thought experiment dressed as policy?

It is practical, and the proof is Norway. Just before Christmas in 1969, oil was confirmed under the North Sea, at the field that became Ekofisk, and Norway faced the choice every resource-rich country faces: spend the windfall now, or hold it for the people not yet at the table. In 1990 the country created the Government Petroleum Fund, renamed the Government Pension Fund Global in 2006, into which the oil revenue would flow. Then, in 2001, it bound itself with a fiscal rule: the state could spend only the expected real return on the fund, around three percent, and the capital itself was to remain inviolate. The principal would not be touched. Only the interest on the saved rent would be spent.

The result is one of the most remarkable accumulations in the history of public finance. The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every citizen of the country, a share of the foreclosed barrel held in trust for everyone, including the unborn. And the most striking thing about it is the durability. Across the whole spectrum of Norwegian politics, through governments of the left and the right, under Stoltenberg and Bondevik and Solberg and Støre, no government of any color has raided the principal. The rule has held because the country decided that the future's share was not the present government's to spend. This is Hartwick's rule realized at sovereign scale: the rent from a depleting resource reinvested and held, so that the capacity to provide for the future outlives the resource itself. And it is, fittingly for the theme that runs through this essay, a reserve fund, a quantity of value held back against the day the abundance ends.

Now look at the shadow the proof casts. Run the same physical act, the pumping of oil, under a different architecture, and you get the Niger Delta. Oil has been extracted there since the 1950s, and the revenue has run into the hundreds of billions of dollars. But the architecture was the reverse of Norway's. The value left, and the costs stayed. The spills, the gas flares burning day and night, the fisheries foreclosed, the water fouled: these landed on people who did not sell the oil and could not bill anyone for what was done to the place they lived. When some of them organized to demand an accounting, the response was lethal. Ken Saro-Wiwa and eight others were executed in 1995, and Nigeria was suspended from the Commonwealth in the aftermath. Years later a United Nations assessment of just one part of the region, Ogoniland, estimated that cleaning it up would cost on the order of a billion dollars as an initial sum, over twenty-five to thirty years, an invoice for the severed costs arriving decades after they were severed, addressed to no one in particular.

The same physical act, two architectures, one question between them. When the cost of an extraction outlives the sale that authorized it, who carries the cost, and was anything set aside? Norway set something aside and held it. The Niger Delta set nothing aside, and the bill came due anyway, paid in spills and flares and a man's life. This is not an argument against extraction. Norway extracted; Norway pumped the oil. It is not a villain story, because the difference between the two cases is not that one had good people and the other bad. It is architecture. It is whether the ledger was honest, whether the residual value was named and held or left to scatter. Architecture, not a political program.

## Out where the cost is zero

Now let me run the framework somewhere it gives a surprising answer, and again I want to mark this clearly as a lens and not a forecast. I am not predicting an age of asteroid mining. I am using it to test whether the framework is really about honest accounting or just a dressed-up objection to taking things out of the ground.

Imagine a miner working an asteroid, far out where there is no community to harm and no ecosystem to disturb, a lump of iron and nickel orbiting in the dark. Run the ledger. Where are the severed costs? There is no river to foul, no air to poison, no people downwind, no unborn community whose commons you are liquidating, because there is no commons out there at all, only dead rock that has been dead since the solar system formed. Cost severance is near zero. And so the framework, the same framework that priced the McDowell coal at five hundred and twenty-four dollars and held Norway to its fiscal rule, says: extract freely. Take the iron. If a comet swings briefly into reach and will not return for ten thousand years, do not hold back; mine it aggressively while you can, because the cost of taking it is as close to nothing as the universe offers.

And yet. On the asteroid itself, on the comet itself, the framework says something else, and the two things it says are not in tension; they are the same sentence. You must hold reserves. Fuel to leave with. Air to breathe until you leave. Water, food, life-support, enough margin to be certain you can get off the rock and get home, or on to the next world, before your supplies run out. The same ledger that says take freely here says reserve there, and it says both for the same reason: it is the honest accounting of your real costs, and your real costs include the cost of survival in the future you are extracting into. Honest accounting is not anti-extraction. It never was. It is knowing what a thing actually costs, and keeping enough back to live through what comes after.

That is the whole framework in one image. Take freely where the cost is truly zero. Hold reserves where the future depends on what you keep. The asteroid miner who strips the rock and forgets to save fuel for the journey home has not been bold; he has kept a dishonest ledger, one that recorded the value of the iron and forgot the value of getting home alive. The barrel of oil and the asteroid are the same problem read in opposite conditions: in both, the only error is to mistake the price you can see for the cost that is really there.

## Whatever economy you choose

Step back and notice what the framework is not. It is not a rival economic system. It does not compete with capitalism or with planning; it does not tell you who should own the mine or set the wage. It is the accounting that any system needs in order to see what it is doing. You can run a market economy with an honest ledger or a dishonest one; you can run a planned economy with an honest ledger or a dishonest one. The question the framework asks sits underneath that choice: whatever economy you choose, can you see the real costs?

Seen this way, the framework has company across the whole history of civilization, because the impulse to know the real costs and hold reserves against scarcity is very old. Kate Raworth's Doughnut Economics is a recent and powerful version of it, an attempt to draw the floor below which a society must not let its people fall and the ceiling above which it must not push the planet, and to insist that an economy keep itself inside both. Ostrom's work on the commons, already mentioned, is the governance version of the same impulse: knowing the real cost of overuse and building the rules that hold a renewable stock against it. And far older than either are the reserve systems that ancient states built to carry themselves across lean years. Ancient China kept what came to be called the ever-normal granary, a public store meant to buy in plenty and release in dearth. Ancient Egypt built state granaries to hold grain against the years the river would not provide. I will not invent the particulars of those systems, because the particulars are not mine to invent, but the shape is unmistakable and ancient: a civilization, faced with the certainty that scarcity will come, holding a reserve against it, which is to say keeping an honest enough ledger to know that the abundance of this year is not the truth of every year.

Capitalist or planned, modern or ancient, on this world or off it, the requirement does not change. The ledger has to be honest or the economy is flying blind. An economy that cannot see its real costs is not a different kind of economy from one that can; it is the same kind, running without instruments, and it will fly into the mountain it cannot see.

## The longest horizon

There is one more turn, and it is the one that takes the reserve idea to its limit. If finite resources demand that we hold something back for the next generation, the logic does not stop at the next generation. It runs out along the whole length of the time a civilization might occupy, and that length is longer and stranger than we usually let ourselves think.

The Sun is slowly brightening. As it ages it grows more luminous, and the calculations of Sackmann and her colleagues put the point at which that rising luminosity makes Earth uninhabitable somewhere on the order of five hundred million to a billion years from now, as the oceans are driven off and the planet's chemistry turns against life. That is long before the far more famous ending, the swelling of the Sun into a red giant, which the work of Schröder and Smith places around seven and a half billion years out, when the expanding star may engulf the inner planets entirely. The habitable window closes not at the dramatic end but at the quiet one, an order of magnitude sooner.

On those horizons, the sealed habitat I asked you to imagine at the start stops being science fiction and becomes a coherent forward-looking concern, the longest-dated entry on the ledger. Holding reserves across deep time means preserving the option of life elsewhere, not because anyone alive will need it, but because the same honest accounting that holds Norway to its fiscal rule and tells the asteroid miner to save fuel for the journey home tells a civilization, on the longest horizon, to keep open the option of getting off the rock before the rock turns against it. Option-preservation across deep time is not a fantasy of escape. It is the reserve principle applied to the only stock that finally matters, which is the existence of anyone at all to keep a ledger.

## The number was always there

So take another breath. It still costs you nothing, and the running total of your life's air still comes to zero, and now you know what the zero means and what it does not. It does not mean the air is worthless. It means the air is so completely valuable, so evenly distributed, that the only instrument we trust returns a blank where the value should be, and we have spent a long time mistaking the blank for the verdict.

The mask of abundance is the way in, because the breath is where everyone can feel the gap between price and worth most directly. But the mask, once lifted, reveals something larger than the breath: a single move that works on any scale and runs in either direction. Find the value that abundance or distance or duration has hidden, and put it on the ledger honestly. Do it forward, and you price a loss before it happens and hold the measure of it in trust, as Norway did with the oil under its sea. Do it backward, and you total the costs already severed and name what is owed, as Darity and Mullen do with the costs severed since 1619. Do it out in the dark, and it tells you to take the dead rock freely and still save fuel for the journey home. Do it across deep time, and it tells you to keep a reserve against the day the Sun itself turns the abundance off.

The framework is not a system anyone has to adopt. It is honest accounting, the kind an individual can run over a single life, the kind a community can run over its shared ground, the kind a government can run over the resource under its territory, on this world or any other. One ledger, kept honestly, in either direction, on any scale.

The mask comes off, and there is no trick underneath. The number was always there. We could not see it only because the value was too large and too evenly shared to cast a price, and the price was the only thing we had taught ourselves to look at. Whether we put the number on the ledger now, before the room seals and the abundance ends, is not a fact about the world. It is a choice that is still ours to make, which is the one freedom the breath has been quietly funding all along, and whether that freedom stays is a thing we still get to decide.
