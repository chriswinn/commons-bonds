# The Mask of Abundance

Imagine you have come to live on another world. I am not predicting that you will; treat this as a lens, a way of seeing your own planet by stepping outside it for a while. We have already built the smaller version of what I am describing. On Devon Island in the Canadian Arctic, in a crater that resembles parts of Mars closely enough to rehearse there, researchers spend their summers in a sealed station and practise the discipline of a place where nothing outside the wall will keep you alive. On the slope of a Hawaiian volcano, in a dome called HI-SEAS, crews have lived for months at a time under the rules of an off-world mission, eating stored food, rationing water, going outside only in suits. These are not films. They are real places where real people learn what it costs to keep a human being breathing in a spot the Earth did not prepare for them. So the world I am asking you to imagine is an extension of something that exists, pushed a little further out.

You arrive at the airlock. Behind you is the dead ground, whatever it is on this world, regolith or ice or bare rock under a sky the wrong colour. In front of you is the inner door and the warmth and the company. Between the two doors stands the person whose job is to let you in, and before the inner door opens that person asks you a question you have never been asked in your life.

How do you intend to pay for your air?

The question is not hostile. It is administrative, the way a question about your luggage is administrative. The person asking it is not cruel and is not making a point. They simply need to know, because inside the wall the air is manufactured. Somewhere in the station a machine is splitting water into oxygen and hydrogen with electricity, and the electricity has a source, and the water had to be carried here or mined here, and every litre of it is counted. The carbon dioxide you will breathe out has to be scrubbed back out of the shared atmosphere by another machine that also runs on power and also wears out and also has to be paid for. None of this is free. None of it was ever going to be free. And so before they let you into the only breathable volume for a great distance in any direction, they need to know how the breathing will be accounted for.

You will not have an answer ready, because the question is absurd. It is absurd in the precise sense that it could not be asked almost anywhere on Earth. Walk up to a stranger in any city on your home planet and ask them how they intend to pay for their next breath and they will step away from you. The question has no meaning there. The air is simply there, for everyone, in such quantity that no one has ever found an edge of it to stand on and charge admission. That is the whole reason the airlock question lands so strangely. It takes something that has never once in your life carried a price and puts a price on it, and in doing so it tells you something true that the free version concealed.

The air was never free. It was only ever free to you.

Sit with that distinction for one moment, because the entire argument grows from it. The air on Earth has enormous value. It is the most valuable thing you will use today, more valuable than your house or your work or anything you own, because without it none of the rest lasts ten minutes. Its value is not low. Its price is low, and the two are not the same thing. A price is a number that appears when something is scarce at the margin, when there is a last unit that someone will compete for and someone else will sell. Air has no last unit on Earth. There is so much of it, so evenly spread, that no market has ever found the scarce margin where a price could form. So it shows a price of zero, and we read that zero as a verdict. We read it as a statement that the thing is worth nothing, when in fact it means the opposite, that the thing is worth so much and is so completely supplied that the instrument we use to measure worth cannot get a grip on it.

Now you go home. You leave the sealed world and return to Earth, and the air is free again, the way it always was. The heat is free, the planet's warmth holding you in a band of temperature you can survive without thinking about it. The light is free, falling out of the sky for nothing. The water runs and the climate stays roughly where your species has always known it. For a while the airlock question seems like a story you tell at dinner, a curiosity from a strange posting. How could anyone charge for air. What a place that must have been.

And then the second realization arrives, slower and heavier than the first. The cost did not disappear when you came home. It is still real here. It is exactly as real here as it was inside the wall. The only thing that changed is who pays it and when. On the sealed world the cost of your breathing landed on you, by the hour, on a meter you could read. On Earth the cost of what we take from the commons lands on someone else. It lands on people in another place who did not agree to it and cannot send you the bill. It lands on people who are not yet born and so cannot object. The bill is not cancelled. It is severed from the person who created it and slid sideways across distance, or forward across time, to settle on someone who was not at the table when the decision was made.

This is the subject of the essay. The most valuable things we have are the ones we share and never pay for. The air. Fresh water. A climate stable and mild enough to grow food and build cities in. The planet's vast and quiet work of pulling the carbon we burn back out of the atmosphere and storing it, year after year, for nothing, on a scale no factory could match. These are the commons, the things owned by everyone and no one, and they carry no price not because they are worthless but because their abundance hides their value from the only instrument we trust to tell us what things are worth. Call that concealment the mask of abundance. The danger the mask creates is not that we treat these things as cheap. Cheap things we at least notice; we haggle over them. The danger is that we cannot see them at all. A thing with no price is invisible to a civilization that decides what to protect by reading prices. What this essay is for is to build a way of accounting that can see these costs, the hidden ones, the severed ones, the ones owed to people not at the table across distance and across time, and to show that this honest ledger works on this world and on any other.

A sealed habitat is useful for exactly one reason. It strips the mask off. It takes the free things of Earth and forces them across a wall into a place where they are scarce, and the moment they become scarce they acquire a price, and the price reveals the value that was always there and was always hidden. We do not have to imagine this from nothing. We have built small sealed worlds already, by accident and on purpose, and in every one of them the free things of Earth became the most expensive things inside the wall. Before we go all the way out to another planet, we should look hard at the sealed worlds we have already made, because they have already taught the lesson.

## Three worlds with the mask removed

Go down, first, instead of up.

A saturation diver works at depths where ordinary air would kill in two separate ways. The nitrogen that makes up most of the air you are breathing right now turns narcotic under pressure, clouding judgement the way alcohol does, and the oxygen that keeps you alive at the surface turns toxic when the pressure forces too much of it into your blood. So divers who work deep, on pipelines and wellheads and the underwater structure of the oil industry, do not breathe air. They breathe a manufactured mixture, mostly helium with a measured amount of oxygen, blended to be survivable at the pressure of their work. They live for weeks at a time pressurised to that depth, in chambers on the surface between shifts, so their bodies need only make the slow and dangerous adjustment back to normal pressure once at the end of the job rather than every day.

Helium is not free. It runs from roughly one dollar and seventy cents to almost three dollars per cubic foot, and a working diver goes through it fast enough that the helium component alone can cost up to about two hundred dollars an hour. That figure, somewhere between eighty and two hundred dollars an hour, is for the helium only. It is not the cost to keep the diver alive, which includes the chamber and the gas blending and the surface crew and the ship. It is the price of one ingredient in the breathing mixture, the inert gas that on land you would never think to buy because the air gives you a working substitute for nothing. The companies that run these operations install reclaim systems on their ships, machinery whose whole purpose is to capture the exhaled helium and clean it and use it again, because at those prices letting it escape into the sea would be like letting money dissolve in water. On land no one builds a machine to recapture the air you breathe out. Under the sea, where the breathing mixture has been forced into scarcity, recapturing it becomes an obvious investment. The mask has come off the gas. Its value, always present, has finally found a price.

Go the other direction now, not down into the sea but into a sealed glass world built on purpose in the Arizona desert.

Between 1991 and 1993, eight people sealed themselves inside Biosphere 2, a structure of about three acres under glass that cost on the order of a hundred and fifty million dollars to build. The idea was beautiful and serious. Build a closed world, a miniature Earth with its own farmland and rainforest and tiny ocean, seal eight humans inside, and see whether the living systems could keep them alive with no input from outside but sunlight. For a while it held. And then, slowly, the air began to fail. Over sixteen months the oxygen in the sealed atmosphere fell from the ordinary share of about twenty one percent down toward fourteen percent, the kind of thinness you would meet near the top of a high mountain, and the crew grew tired and short of breath and could not understand why. The cause turned out to be hidden in the soil. The rich earth they had brought in to grow their food was full of microbes, and the microbes breathed, consuming oxygen and releasing carbon dioxide, and the carbon dioxide then bound chemically into the fresh concrete of the structure and was lost, so that the oxygen consumed never came back as anything the crew could breathe. The managers, watching their people decline, finally pumped oxygen in from outside. The closed world could not keep its humans breathing on its own.

The rest of the living balance failed alongside the air. Of the twenty five small vertebrate species placed inside, nineteen died. Every pollinating insect died, which meant the crops had to be pollinated by hand, the eight people inside doing with their fingers the work that bees and flies do across the entire surface of the Earth for nothing and unasked. This is the part worth holding onto. On the outside of that glass, the same week, the same year, the air renewed itself and the insects pollinated the crops and the carbon found its way through the cycle, all of it free, all of it invisible, none of it on anyone's books. Inside the glass the same processes had prices, and crew fatigue, and a near-disaster, and a bill of a hundred and fifty million dollars that bought a world that still could not breathe by itself. Biosphere 2 was a machine for removing the mask, and what it revealed was that the Earth performs, silently and at no charge, a quantity of life support that the cleverest sealed copy of it could not sustain for eight people for two years.

Now go up, all the way up, to the world we currently keep sealed and inhabited above our heads.

The International Space Station orbits a few hundred kilometres up, and everything inside it that a human needs to live had to be either carried there or manufactured there from things carried there. Lifting water to that orbit costs on the order of twenty one thousand dollars per kilogram. A six month supply of water for the crew, if you simply launched it, would run to something like a hundred and thirty five million dollars, an order-of-magnitude figure but a stunning one for a substance that falls out of the sky onto your home planet for free. So the station does not simply launch its water. It recycles it, recovering around ninety percent of the crew's sweat and urine and turning it back into drinking water, because at twenty one thousand dollars a kilogram throwing water away is not an option a sane engineer would choose. The oxygen the crew breathes is made on the station by running electricity through water to split it, the same chemistry as on our imagined off-world habitat, and the electricity comes from the solar panels, and every step of it is metered and accounted and engineered against waste. The station is the purest sealed world we have built, the one furthest from any free supply, and in it the price of the free things of Earth climbs to the highest numbers of all.

Three sealed worlds, three depths and altitudes, one lesson repeated in each. Seal a system off from the Earth's free commons and the priceless commons immediately become the most expensive things in the system. The value was always there. The seal does not create it. The seal only removes the mask of abundance that hid it, and lets the value find, at last, a price. This is why the sealed habitat is the right lens for the whole subject. It is the one place where the invisible costs of the commons are forced to become visible, and once you have seen them become visible there, you cannot unsee them at home.

## The mechanism with a name

What exactly happens, then, when a cost that is real does not appear on anyone's books. It helps to give the thing a plain description before giving it a name, because the name is only useful if you already understand the thing.

Here is the description. Someone takes an action that produces a cost. But the cost does not land on the person who took the action. It lands on someone else, in another place or another time, and that second person has no way to send the bill back to the first. The cost is real. It is paid in full. It is simply paid by the wrong party, and the connection between the action and the payment has been cut. Call that cutting cost severance. A cost is created, severed from its creator, and absorbed by someone who never agreed to absorb it and cannot bill anyone for having done so.

Cost severance is how the mask of abundance does its damage in practice. The commons are free to take from, so people take from them, and the taking produces a cost, and because the commons belong to everyone and no one the cost cannot be charged to the taker. It slides off onto the rest of the present world, or off the edge of the present entirely onto people who do not exist yet and so cannot defend themselves. The carbon you put into the air today will warm the climate for people not yet born, who cannot send you an invoice, and who would not even know your name. The cost is not gone. It is severed and deferred.

There is a second idea that sits beside the first, and it is the one that does the harder work. When something is sold, the sale settles a number of accounts. The buyer pays the seller, the seller pays the workers and the suppliers, and ordinary economics has a great deal to say about how that money should divide. But sometimes, after the sale is complete and everyone who showed up at the market has been paid, there is value left over that no one paid for, because no one who held a claim to it was present. Think of it as the worth that remains unpriced after the transaction is done, owed to people who were not at the table. Often those people are not yet born. Call that leftover worth the residual commons value, the value that belongs to the commons and to its future holders and that the sale, however fair to its participants, simply walked past.

These two ideas describe two different shapes of the same wound, and it is worth keeping them distinct. In the ordinary case, honest pricing means re-attaching a severed cost. You find the cost that slid sideways onto a neighbour or forward onto a descendant, and you bring it back and put it on the books of the person who created it, so the price tells the truth. A factory that fouls a river should pay for the fouling, and when it does, the price of what the factory makes finally includes what the factory actually costs the world. That is the familiar move, and a long tradition of economics has argued for it.

The commons case is stranger and has been seen less clearly. Here the problem is not that a cost was created and slid away. It is that a value was so total, so abundant, so completely supplied to everyone, that it never acquired a price in the first place. There was never a market in breathable air because there was never a scarce margin where one could form. So the value sat unmeasured, not because anyone hid it deliberately but because abundance itself is a kind of hiding. And then scarcity arrives, slowly, as the commons is drawn down, and the value that was always there begins at last to show. The diver pays for helium because the sea took the free substitute away. The carbon sink shows its worth as it fills and the climate begins to move. The mask comes off not because anyone removed it but because the abundance that was the mask is ending. Honest accounting for the commons is therefore not only the re-attaching of severed costs. It is the much harder act of pricing a value before its abundance ends, of seeing the worth of a thing while it is still apparently free, which is to say of reading the zero correctly, as a measurement failure and not a verdict.

## A barrel that is more expensive than it looks

Take a single barrel of oil and put a price on it. Say the price is a hundred dollars. I am using that number as an illustration, not as today's market quote; the argument does not depend on the exact figure. A hundred dollars buys the barrel, and the hundred dollars is honest about a great many things. It reflects the labour of pulling the oil up and refining it, the capital of the rigs and the pipelines, the competition of buyers and sellers, the scarcity of the moment. It is a real price doing real work.

But notice what the hundred dollars is a price for. It is a price for the present. It buys you, today, the energy in the barrel, the tank of fuel, the heat, the plastic, the motion. The transaction it settles is a transaction in the present tense. And yet the thing being sold does not belong only to the present. The barrel was the product of an unimaginable span of geological time, and once it is burned it is gone, not slowly, not at a rate, but completely and forever. The hundred dollars buys the present its use of the barrel. It does not buy the barrel back for anyone who comes after, because there is no buying it back. It is one of a fixed and dwindling stock, and every one burned is one that no future person will ever have.

This is where a distinction that sounds technical does real moral work, so it is worth drawing carefully. There are two kinds of natural resource, and they fail in two completely different ways. A renewable resource, a forest, a fishery, an aquifer fed by rain, poses a question of rate. The forest grows back. The fish breed. The question is only whether you take less than the resource replaces, in which case it lasts forever, or more than it replaces, in which case it shrinks toward nothing. With a renewable resource there is always, in principle, a rate of taking slow enough to be sustainable. You can get the arithmetic right or wrong, but the right answer exists.

Oil is not like that. Oil is non-renewable in the strict sense, which means it is liquidated rather than harvested. There is no rate of extraction slow enough to make it sustainable, because it does not replenish at any rate at all on any timescale that matters to a civilization. Every barrel burned is subtracted from a fixed total and is not coming back. So the question that applies to the forest, the question of whether you are taking less than it breeds, does not even arise for the barrel. The only honest question for the barrel is what is owed to the future for a thing the future will never get to have. The hundred dollars does not answer that question. It does not even ask it. The mask of abundance hangs over the oil as surely as over the air, and the price the market posts is honest about everything except the one thing that matters most across time, which is that the barrel is being permanently removed from a stock that belongs, in part, to people who do not yet exist.

## A plot of ground that several traditions walked past

It would be strange if no one had ever noticed any of this, and in fact a great deal of the noticing has been done. The history of economic and moral thought is full of people who walked right up to the edge of this idea, saw a piece of it clearly, named that piece, and then turned aside before building the whole thing. It is worth naming the near-misses, briefly, because seeing what each tradition saw and what it missed shows the shape of the empty plot they all circled.

Karl Marx, in the volumes of *Capital* he began publishing in 1867, priced a kind of value that the ordinary account of a sale walked past. He argued that there was a surplus in production that the worker created and the owner kept, value extracted and unpaid. That is a real insight about severed value, but the severance Marx tracked ran between classes in the present, between owner and worker, not across time between the present and the unborn. He found one of the empty seats at the table and named who should have sat in it, but the seat he found was a living person's seat, not a future person's.

Elinor Ostrom, whose *Governing the Commons* appeared in 1990 and who received the Nobel in economics in 2009, studied how real communities manage shared resources without either privatising them or having a government run them. Her work is the great corrective to the lazy assumption that a commons must always be destroyed. But the commons Ostrom governed so brilliantly were the renewable ones, the fisheries and pastures and irrigation systems, the resources with a rate, the ones where the question is how fast to take and the answer is a sustainable flow. She solved the governance of the resource that grows back. The liquidated resource, the barrel that does not grow back at any rate, was a different problem, and her tools were not built for it.

Herman Daly, who died in 2022 and who spent his career arguing for what he called a steady-state economy, warned as clearly as anyone that an economy cannot grow its physical throughput forever on a finite planet. He saw the wall coming. But a warning about the total scale of throughput is not the same as an invoice for one specific foreclosed thing. Daly told us the aggregate was unsustainable; he did not hand us a bill for this barrel, owed to these future people, in this amount.

John Hartwick, in a paper in the *American Economic Review* in 1977, came closer to the barrel than any of them on the economic side. He worked out a rule, which still carries his name, for what a society should do if it wants to live justly off a non-renewable resource. The rule is to take the rents from the resource, the pure profit beyond the costs of extraction, and reinvest them, so that as the natural wealth is drawn down an equivalent and lasting capital is built up in its place, and future generations inherit something of equal value even though the original resource is gone. This is a genuine answer to the problem of the liquidated resource, and we will see a country that has very nearly followed it. But Hartwick's rule saves the rent. It does not by itself price the full residual commons value of the barrel, the worth owed to the future for the permanent removal, and it does not address the cost that the burning of the barrel severs forward in time through the climate. It saves the money the sale produced. It does not measure the whole of what the sale walked past.

Then the moral philosophers, working the same ground from the side of obligation rather than the side of price. John Rawls, in *A Theory of Justice* in 1971, asked us to imagine choosing the rules of a society from behind a veil of ignorance, not knowing which person in it we would turn out to be, so that we would choose rules fair to everyone because any of them might be us. It is one of the most powerful moral instruments of the last century, and it was built to widen our circle of concern across the people alive around us. It can be widened further, across generations, to ask us to choose as if we did not know which generation we would be born into, and when it is widened that way it speaks directly to the barrel and the unborn. But Rawls aimed it mainly at the present society, and the generational widening was a possibility in his tool rather than the use he made of it.

Michael Sandel, in *What Money Can't Buy* in 2012, argued that there are goods that are corrupted by being priced at all, that putting some things up for sale degrades them, and that a society which prices everything has lost the ability to value some things properly. That insight matters here, because part of what the mask of abundance does is tempt us to think that anything without a price has no value, and Sandel reminds us that the relationship between price and value is fraught in both directions. Derek Parfit, in *Reasons and Persons* in 1984, thought harder than almost anyone about our obligations to future people, including the strange puzzles that arise because our choices today determine which future people will even exist. He gave the obligation to the future its sharpest philosophical examination. But the philosophers were building moral arguments, not ledgers. They told us with great rigour that we owe the future something. They did not put a number on the barrel.

Finally, the economists who did try to put a number on the future, in the long argument over the discount rate. The discount rate is the tool economists use to compare a cost today with a cost many years from now. The idea is simple enough. A dollar today is worth more than a dollar in fifty years, partly because you could invest today's dollar and have more than a dollar in fifty years, and partly because the future is uncertain. So future costs and benefits are discounted, reduced, when we compare them with present ones, and the rate at which we reduce them is the discount rate. The choice of rate turns out to dominate everything in climate economics, because a cost a century out, run through a high discount rate, shrinks almost to nothing, while the same cost run through a low rate stays large.

Nicholas Stern, in the review he led for the British government published in 2006 and 2007, chose a low discount rate, on the order of one and a half percent, on the moral argument that the welfare of a future person should not count for dramatically less than the welfare of a present one merely because they are born later. With that rate the costs of climate change loomed large in present terms, and the review concluded that strong early action was worth roughly one percent of global output. William Nordhaus, who received the Nobel in economics in 2018 for building the models that price climate damage, used a higher rate closer to four percent, drawn more from the observed returns in real markets, and his models accordingly counselled a more gradual response. Martin Weitzman added a further argument, that what should dominate our thinking is not the most likely amount of warming but the fat tail of the probability distribution, the small but real chance of a catastrophe so large that no discount rate makes it tolerable. The illustration that captures the whole quarrel is stark. A cost of a fixed size a century out might be worth around twenty five dollars in present terms under Stern's low rate and around a dollar and fifty cents under Nordhaus's high one. The same future cost, the same calculation, a difference of more than fifteen times, decided entirely by a choice of rate that is at bottom a moral choice about how much the future counts.

But here is the crucial point that the entire discount-rate quarrel misses. All of these economists, Stern and Nordhaus and Weitzman alike, are pricing the same thing as each other. They are pricing the damage flow that comes from burning the resource, the harm the carbon does as it warms the climate over time. They differ only on how heavily to discount that future flow of harm. None of them is pricing the absence of the resource itself, the simple fact that the barrel is gone and will never be available to anyone again. The residual commons value of the barrel is not a discounted flow of future harm. It is the present absence of a thing that will not return. And a present absence does not shrink when you discount it, because discounting reduces future quantities to their present worth, and this quantity is already present. The barrel is already gone. That is why the residual commons value survives even Stern's low rate and would survive a lower one still. It is not waiting in the future to be discounted back. It is here, now, in the empty place where the barrel used to be.

## A county where the bill is enormous and was never sent

Leave the thought experiments and the theory for a moment and look at a real place where the arithmetic can be done.

McDowell County sits in the coalfields of southern West Virginia, and for generations it sold coal. Around 1960 the coal left the mine mouth for something on the order of four or five dollars a ton. That was the price. That was what the transaction settled. The coal was dug, sold, shipped, and burned, and the money divided among the parties who were present at the sale, the operators and the railroads and, in far smaller measure, the people of the county who did the digging. By the ordinary accounting, the books balanced. The coal was worth a few dollars a ton and a few dollars a ton is what it fetched.

Now run the honest ledger. Add back the severed costs, the costs that the sale produced but slid off onto others, in other places and in later times. The largest of these by far is carbon. When that coal was burned it released carbon dioxide, and the carbon dioxide warms the climate, and the warming is a cost borne by everyone alive later and everywhere else, a cost severed completely from the four or five dollar transaction at the mine mouth. The coal from this particular region, the Pocahontas seams, is carbon-dense, releasing about two and six tenths of a tonne of carbon dioxide for every tonne of coal burned, somewhat above the national average of about two and a third. To price that carbon we need a figure for what a tonne of carbon dioxide actually costs the world, the social cost of carbon, and the United States Environmental Protection Agency in 2023 put that figure at around a hundred and ninety dollars a tonne. Multiply the carbon released by the cost of the carbon, two and six tenths tonnes by a hundred and ninety dollars, and you get a carbon cost of roughly four hundred and ninety six dollars for a single ton of this coal.

Add that to the few dollars at the mine mouth and the modest other severed costs, and the honest price of a ton of McDowell coal is more than five hundred dollars, something around five hundred and ten, and almost the whole of that is carbon. The price the market posted, the four or five dollars, captured less than one percent of what the coal actually cost the world. The other ninety nine percent and more was severed, slid forward across time onto people who were not born when the coal was burned and who never saw a cent of the four or five dollars. This is the framework run backward, on an extraction that already happened, on coal already burned and gone. It does not undo anything. The coal cannot be unburned. But it can be seen, and seeing it correctly changes what we think we know about the prosperity that the coal was supposed to have produced. A great deal of what looked like wealth was a cost severed and deferred, a bill written against the future and never sent.

## The same ledger, both directions

The honest ledger, it turns out, runs in two directions, and there is a named instrument for each.

Run it forward, before the extraction happens, and you are pricing a future loss in advance and setting the money aside to cover it. You look at what you are about to take, you calculate what it will cost the future, and you put that amount into a reserve before you take a cent of profit, so that the cost is covered rather than severed. Call the instrument for that a foreclosure bond, because you are pricing what the extraction forecloses, what it permanently removes from the options available to those who come after, and bonding yourself to make it good. Price the future loss, then set the money aside.

Run it backward, after the extraction has already happened, and you are pricing a loss that was realized in the past and was never accounted for, the way the McDowell coal was never accounted for. You calculate what the past extraction actually cost and what is owed for it, and you raise and direct the funds to make restitution. Call the instrument for that a restitution bond, because you are making restitution for an extraction already realized and never paid.

The backward direction, the restitution direction, has a deep and serious literature behind it, because the most important application of backward honest accounting is not to coal at all. It is to people. The economists William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen, in their 2020 book *From Here to Equality*, set out the economic case for reparations to Black Americans for slavery and its long aftermath. They trace the racial wealth gap back to 1619 and forward through the generations, and they show how a cost imposed in the past, never accounted for, compounds into measurable present effects, including a gap in wealth between Black and white Americans and a gap in longevity that can be read as a legacy effect of accumulated and uncompensated harm. This is backward honest accounting at its most demanding. It takes a severed cost imposed long ago, follows the severance forward to the present, and asks what is owed. The restitution bond, as an instrument, operationalizes precisely the restitution component of the redress that Darity and Mullen lay out. Ta-Nehisi Coates, in his 2014 essay *The Case for Reparations*, built the argument's moral architecture in a form that reached a wide public, and the two works sit together, the moral case and the economic one.

There is a discipline that has to be observed here, and the framework observes it deliberately. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group is not work that everyone is positioned to do. When the harm in question is the harm of coercion done to a people, the pricing of that coercion is properly the work of those who continue the struggle over it, not the work of an outsider who can reach the question only through a framework. So the framework reaches the coerced cases through their legacy effects, the wealth gap and the longevity gap and the other measurable inheritances that can be accounted for from the outside, and it defers the direct pricing of the coercion itself to those whose work that is. This is not an evasion. It is a recognition that some parts of the ledger are not the accountant's to fill in, and that honest accounting includes knowing which lines you are not the one to price.

## Two countries, one physical act, two architectures

To see the forward direction working in the real world, and to see what its absence looks like, set two oil-producing places beside each other. They did the same physical thing. They built completely different architectures around it, and the difference is the whole point.

Norway found oil in the North Sea at the end of the 1960s. The great Ekofisk field was confirmed to the authorities just before Christmas in 1969, and a country that might have simply spent the windfall as it arrived chose instead, over the following decades, to build an architecture for not severing the future. It established a petroleum fund in 1990, which became the Government Pension Fund Global in 2006, and into that fund it directed the state's oil revenues. Then, in 2001, it adopted a fiscal rule of remarkable discipline. The rule says that the government may spend only the expected real return on the fund, on the order of three percent a year, and may not touch the capital itself. The principal is treated as inviolate. The oil is finite and will run out, but if only the return is spent and the capital is preserved and reinvested, the wealth the oil represented becomes permanent, and future generations of Norwegians inherit it as a standing fund rather than a memory of a boom.

The fund now holds well over two trillion dollars, more than three hundred and ninety thousand dollars for every citizen of the country. And the most striking thing about it is its durability across politics. Governments of the left and the right have held power in Norway across these decades, under Stoltenberg and Bondevik and Solberg and Støre, and none of them has raided the principal. The discipline has held across the entire political spectrum, which tells you it has stopped being a policy and become an institution. What Norway built is, in effect, Hartwick's rule carried out at the scale of a nation. It takes the rents from a non-renewable resource and reinvests them so that an equivalent lasting wealth replaces the resource as it is drawn down. It is also, simply, a reserve. A great fund held back against the future, which is a theme we will meet again when we go off-world. Norway is the existence proof. The future can be priced and provided for in practice, by a real country, durably, for decades. It is not a fantasy of economists. It has been done.

Now set beside it the Niger Delta. Oil has been pumped from the Delta since the 1950s, and the revenues over those decades have run to hundreds of billions of dollars. But the architecture built around that extraction severed the costs rather than reserving against them. The oil money flowed largely to the central state and to the foreign companies, while the costs of the extraction, the spills that poisoned the water, the gas flares that burned day and night, the fisheries and farmland foreclosed by contamination, fell on the people of the Delta, who had not sold the oil and could not send anyone a bill for what it cost them. The writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa led a movement of the Ogoni people against this severance, and in 1995 he and eight others were executed by the Nigerian state, a killing that drew international condemnation and the suspension of Nigeria from the Commonwealth. Years later a United Nations environmental assessment of Ogoniland estimated that simply beginning to clean up the contamination would require something like a billion dollars over an initial period of twenty five to thirty years, and that was only the first phase of a restoration expected to take far longer.

Two places, the same physical act of pumping oil out of the ground and selling it. One built an architecture that priced the future and set the money aside, and now holds a fund of more than two trillion dollars that has survived every change of government. The other built an architecture that severed the costs onto the people of the place where the oil was, and produced poisoned water, a foreclosed livelihood, executions, and a cleanup bill that will outlast the people who incurred it. This is not a story about good countries and bad countries, and it is not an argument against pumping oil. It is an argument about architecture. When the cost of an extraction outlives the sale that produced it, the only questions that matter are who carries that cost and whether anything was set aside to meet it. Norway set something aside. The Delta did not. The framework is not a political program and it does not tell a country whether to drill. It tells the country, whatever it decides, what the drilling actually costs and whether the cost is being reserved against or severed onto someone who never agreed to bear it.

## Living inside the seal

Now go back out to the sealed world, because we left it too soon. At the airlock we used the seal as a measuring instrument, a device for seeing the value of the commons by forcing them into scarcity. But a colony is not a measuring instrument. It is a home. People would live there, and a home needs an economy, and the economy of a sealed world is the honest ledger run forward, in real time, as the ordinary business of staying alive. I am still not predicting any of this. It remains a lens. But it is a lens worth holding steady for a while, because the framework that looks abstract on Earth becomes simply the way the bills work on a world that cannot let abundance hide anything.

Imagine actually living there, not visiting, not measuring, but waking up inside the wall every day for years. The air you breathe is manufactured and metered. Every litre of water you use has been recovered and cleaned and is counted against you. The carbon dioxide you breathe out has to be scrubbed from the shared atmosphere by machinery that runs on power and wears out, and the scrubbing, like the breathing and the drinking, appears on your account. On Earth these are the free things, the commons that abundance keeps invisible. Inside the seal they are the daily economy. You do not pay for luxuries first and necessities as an afterthought. You pay, first and most, for exactly the things that are free on Earth, because those are the things the seal has made scarce, and scarcity is where price lives.

This is the airlock question made into a way of life. The person at the door asked how you would pay for your air because inside the wall air is a metered commodity, and now you see that the question was not strange at all. It was simply honest, in a place too sealed to allow the dishonesty that abundance permits. On Earth the same costs exist, exactly as real, but they are severed and deferred and hidden behind the mask, so no one is asked the question at any door. The sealed colony cannot afford the mask. Its atmosphere is too small to absorb a severed cost. Sever a cost there, let one person take more than their share of air or water or power without it appearing on the ledger, and the shared system fails for everyone, because there is no vast free Earth outside the wall to quietly absorb the difference. So the colony does the only thing a sealed world can do. It runs an honest ledger as its ordinary economy, pricing every commons by usage, because it has no choice.

Notice what this means. The colony's daily economy is the same ledger as McDowell's and Norway's, only run forward and in real time on a world that cannot hide anything. McDowell shows the ledger run backward, the carbon cost of burned coal made visible long after the fact. Norway shows the ledger run forward at the scale of a nation, the future loss priced and reserved against in advance. The colony shows the ledger run forward at the scale of a breath, the future loss priced and reserved against in the very moment of taking, because on a sealed world there is no gap between the taking and the consequence in which a cost could hide. First we used the seal to see the value. Now we live inside it and pay the value, in real time, by the litre and by the breath. The framework was never a special apparatus for special cases. It is what every economy does when it can no longer afford to let abundance lie.

Then take the lens further out, away from any colony, to a single miner working an asteroid. Here the same honest ledger gives an answer that surprises people who expect a framework about hidden costs to be a framework against taking things. Out on a bare rock in the empty dark there is no community to bear a severed cost, no ecosystem to poison, no neighbour and no descendant living downstream of the work, because there is no stream and no down. The cost severance is very nearly zero. There is almost no one and nothing for a cost to slide onto. And so the honest ledger says, plainly, extract freely. Take the iron. If a comet swings briefly into reach and will not return for a thousand years, do not ration your taking of it against some future that has no claim there; extract aggressively while it is in reach, because the residual commons value owed to others is, in that empty place, close to nothing. Honest accounting is not a religion of restraint. Where the cost is genuinely near zero, it says take.

And yet the same ledger, in the same breath, says something else to the miner. On the rock itself you must hold reserves. You must keep back enough fuel and air and life support to be certain you can leave the rock alive and reach the next world or get home. The taking of the asteroid's iron is free because no one bears its cost. The reserving of your own air is mandatory because you bear the cost of running out, and you bear it absolutely and soon. So the one ledger gives two instructions that only sound opposed. Take freely from the commons that has no claimant. Reserve rigorously against the future you yourself will have to live in. Honest accounting was never anti-extraction. It is the knowledge of what the real cost is, and the discipline of keeping enough back to survive the future you are extracting your way into. The miner who reserves nothing dies on a rock full of iron. The miner who reserves too cautiously and refuses the briefly-available comet wastes a windfall that harmed no one. The honest ledger steers between the two, and it is the same ledger Norway runs with its fund and McDowell never ran with its coal.

## Whatever economy you choose

It would be easy to mistake all of this for a proposal to replace one economic system with another, and it is not that. The honest ledger is not a rival to capitalism or to planning or to any other way of organizing production and exchange. It is the accounting that any of those systems needs in order to function without blinding itself. A capitalist economy can run the ledger or refuse to. A planned economy can run the ledger or refuse to. The ledger is upstream of the choice between systems, because every system, whatever else it does, has to know what its activity actually costs, and the mask of abundance hides that cost from all of them equally.

The point becomes clear when you notice how many different traditions have been, underneath their surface differences, attempts to run some version of this ledger. The economist Kate Raworth, in her 2017 book *Doughnut Economics*, proposes a picture of a safe and just space for humanity, bounded on the outside by the ecological ceilings we must not breach and on the inside by the social foundations no one should fall below. That is, at bottom, an effort to make the real costs and the real needs visible, to put on the books what an economy fixated on growth alone leaves off. Ostrom's study of how communities govern their renewable commons is another such effort, a demonstration that people can and do build institutions to account honestly for a shared resource and hold its use within sustainable bounds. And the impulse is far older than any modern economist. Across the ancient world, societies built systems of granaries, public stores of grain held against the years when the harvest would fail. Ancient China maintained what was called the ever-normal granary, buying grain when it was plentiful and cheap and releasing it when it was scarce and dear, both stabilizing the price and holding a reserve against famine. Ancient Egypt ran state granaries on a great scale for the same fundamental purpose. I will keep this at the level of the pattern rather than inventing particulars, but the pattern is unmistakable and very old. A civilization that wants to survive its own lean years builds a reserve, and to build a reserve it has to account honestly for what it has and what it will need.

That is the common thread running from the ancient granary to the Norwegian fund to the colony's metered air. Capitalist or planned, ancient or off-world, every economy that lasts has to know its real costs and hold reserves against the scarcity it can see coming. An economy that lets the mask of abundance hide its costs is not choosing a different and equally valid system. It is choosing to operate without knowing what it is doing, which no system survives for long. The honest ledger does not tell a society which economy to run. It tells the society that whichever economy it runs has to be able to see.

## Reserves on the longest horizon

There is one more turn of the screw, and it is the one that makes the off-world habitat something other than science fiction.

The reserves a society holds are usually imagined on a human timescale. Save for the next bad harvest, the next recession, the next generation. Norway's fund is built for the children and grandchildren of the Norwegians alive now, a horizon of a century or two at most. But finite resources and a finite home demand reserves on a far longer horizon than that, and the longest horizon is not a metaphor. It is written into the physics of the star we orbit.

The Sun is slowly growing more luminous as it ages. The increase is imperceptible across a human life or even across all of recorded history, but it is real and it is one-directional, and it sets a hard limit on how long the Earth can remain habitable. The astronomers who have modelled this find that the rising luminosity will render the Earth uninhabitable for complex life on the order of five hundred million to one billion years from now, as the oceans are driven off and the surface becomes too hot for the chemistry of life. That is long before the far more dramatic end, the swelling of the Sun into a red giant that will expand to engulf the inner planets around seven and a half billion years from now. The habitable window closes not at the spectacular end but at the quiet one, when a Sun only modestly brighter than today's makes the surface unlivable, ten times sooner than the red giant arrives.

These numbers are so large that they seem to belong to a different kind of conversation, a cosmological one with no bearing on economics or ethics. But hold them next to the honest ledger and they change character. They mean that the human story, if it is to continue, has a hard outer boundary, and that option-preservation across deep time is not a fantasy but a coherent and even a sober concern. On that horizon the sealed off-world habitat stops being a writer's lens for seeing the present, the use I have made of it through this whole essay, and becomes something more literal, the form a reserve must eventually take when the home planet itself reaches the end of its habitable life. The granary holds grain against the failed harvest. The fund holds capital against the spent oil. And on the longest horizon, the reserve a species would have to hold is the capacity to live somewhere other than the world that is ending. The off-world habitat is the granary of the deepest future. I am not predicting that we build it, and the timescales are so vast that no present policy turns on them. But the logic is continuous. It is the same logic of honest accounting and held reserves, followed out to the edge of the possible, and it shows that the framework does not break at any scale. It runs from a single breath to the lifetime of a planet, and the ledger reads the same all the way out.

## The breath, free for now

Come back, finally, to Earth, and to the air.

You are breathing it right now, the same air the airlock wanted payment for, and here it is free. You did not pay for the last breath and you will not pay for the next. The heat of the planet holds you in a livable band without a meter. Water runs and the climate, for now, stays roughly where your species has always known it, mild enough to grow the food and build the cities. All of it free, all of it the most valuable thing you will use today, all of it wearing the mask of abundance that makes its value invisible to the only instrument we trust.

But you have seen the mask come off now, three times in the sealed worlds we have already built, once on a county's worth of burned coal, once on the difference between two oil-producing nations, and once on an imagined world where the bill for a breath arrives at the door. So you know what the free air actually is. It is not worthless. It is a commons of immense value, supplied so completely and so evenly that no market has ever found the edge to charge at, and its freedom is not a fact of nature that will hold forever on its own. The sealed worlds prove that the value is real, because the moment the abundance ends the price appears. The county and the nations and the colony prove that the cost is real, because somewhere, in another place or another time, someone always pays it.

What the honest ledger offers is not a new economy and not a political program. It is a way of seeing the costs that the mask hides, the costs severed across distance onto people who were not at the table, and severed across time onto people not yet born, so that whatever economy we choose to run, on this world or eventually on another, can run with its eyes open. The number was always there. The mask only hid it. And the freedom of the air, the freedom that lets you breathe without being asked at any door how you intend to pay, is not a permanent gift and not an accident. Whether it stays free, for the people in other places who share this air with us now and for the people not yet born who will inherit it, is not a thing that nature will decide for us. It is a thing we still get to decide. That is the part the mask hides best of all, and it is the part worth seeing most clearly. The choice is still ours, and it is still open, and the ledger is finally honest enough to show us what the choice actually costs.
