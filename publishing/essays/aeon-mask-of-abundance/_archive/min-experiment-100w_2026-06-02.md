# The Mask of Abundance

Take a breath. Take another. You did not pay for either of them, and you will not pay for the millions that follow today, and this is the strangest fact about the most important thing you do. Breathing is the precondition of everything else you value, and its price is approximately nothing. We tend to read that zero as a sign of unimportance. It is the opposite. The zero is a mask.

The market posts a price for air of roughly nothing because air is everywhere. Scarcity is what lifts a price off the floor, and there is no scarcity of atmosphere, so the most valuable substance you will ever consume registers, on every ledger that governs how we behave, as worthless. The same goes for clean water falling as rain, for the slow oceanic cycling of carbon, for a climate stable enough to plant by. These are the commons we never price. They are priceless in the literal sense: no price has ever attached to them, not because they are worth little but because they are worth everything and have always been there. Abundance does the hiding. The more total the value, the more complete the disguise.

I want to call this the mask of abundance, and I want to be precise about what it conceals. It is not that we have undervalued these things in the way we undervalue, say, the work of nurses or the topsoil under an industrial field. Those have prices that are simply too low. The commons of air and water and a livable sky have no prices at all, and a thing without a price is, to the only instrument modern societies use for reckoning worth, invisible. We protect what costs us something to lose. The mask ensures that the loss never shows up as a cost until it is too late to be only a cost.

So consider, as a way of seeing the mask for what it is, a sealed world.

Imagine a closed habitat somewhere off Earth, a station or a buried colony, where the air does not arrive for free because there is no atmosphere outside the wall to draw it from. I am not predicting this place. I am borrowing it as a lens, the way a physicist borrows a frictionless plane. In the sealed world, every commons that Earth supplies without a bill becomes a line item. You pay, by usage, for a metered breath. You pay for a litre of water that has been through someone's body and a reclamation loop before it reaches your cup. You pay for the carrying-away of the carbon dioxide you exhale, because in a sealed volume that gas does not disperse into an obliging sky; it accumulates until something expensive removes it. The habitat is a moral X-ray. It shows the skeleton of value that the soft tissue of abundance keeps hidden. Run honest pricing in reverse and this is what you get: not a cost severed from a price and quietly reattached, but a value so complete it never had to acquire a price at all, suddenly itemised the moment the supply runs out.

The lens is only a lens. But the thing it reveals is not hypothetical, because the sealed world already exists for some people, and the bills already arrive.

Go down far enough beneath the sea and you cannot breathe ordinary air. Commercial saturation divers working at depth breathe heliox, a blend of helium and oxygen, and helium is neither cheap nor recoverable for free. At rough market figures it runs somewhere between $1.70 and $2.75 a cubic foot, and technical divers reckon the helium alone at $80 to $200 an hour of breathing. The cost is steep enough that saturation systems aboard ship run reclaim rigs whose entire purpose is to recapture exhaled gas and use it again. Picture that for a moment. Out here a breath is a thing you let go of without thought, irretrievable and worthless. Down there a breath is dear enough that an industry has built machinery to catch it on the way out of the body. The diver lives, by necessity, in the version of the world where the bill has already come.

So did the eight people sealed inside Biosphere 2 in the Arizona desert at the start of the 1990s. The structure was a glass world of a little over three acres, an attempt to enclose a working slice of Earth's life-support and keep humans alive inside it. The mask came off almost at once, in the one commodity nobody had thought to worry about. The oxygen began to fall. Over sixteen months it dropped from the ordinary 21 per cent of the air down toward 14, a thinning that left the crew gasping on stairs, as soil microbes respired faster than anyone had modelled and the freshly poured concrete drank carbon dioxide out of the cycle. In the end the managers did the only thing they could and pumped oxygen in from outside, which is to say they conceded that the sealed world could not, unaided, make the one thing the open world makes for nothing. Nineteen of the twenty-five small vertebrate species inside died. Every pollinator died, so the crops had to be pollinated by hand. The whole costly experiment, something near $150 million of it, amounted to an expensive demonstration of how hard it is to reproduce, on purpose, the services a planet renders for free.

And consider what those services cost when you carry them off the planet entirely. On the International Space Station, water is about the most precious cargo aboard, because lifting a kilogram of anything to orbit runs on the order of $21,000, and a human body wants its litres every day. A six-month supply of water at usual consumption would run to something like $135 million if you simply launched it, which is why the station does not. Its life-support system recovers around 90 per cent of the crew's sweat and urine and returns it to the water supply, and it makes breathable oxygen by splitting that reclaimed water with electricity. In orbit, air and water are not the background of life. They are the budget.

Here is the thing I keep turning over. The diver, the Biosphere crew, the astronaut are not living in a different physics from the rest of us. They are living in the same world with the mask removed. The air costs what air costs; it is only that down here the supply is so vast that the cost rounds to zero and the value vanishes behind the rounding. Honest pricing, in most of the places we apply it, means adding back a cost the market severed and pretended away, the carbon dumped, the aquifer drained, the harm exported downstream. The mask of abundance asks something different of us. It asks us to see a value the market never had to name, because there was always more of it than anyone could use, and a thing you cannot run out of is a thing you never learn to count.

That is precisely why we under-protect the commons we depend on most. Not from greed alone, and not from stupidity, but from a category error built into the only instrument we trust to tell us what matters. The instrument reads abundance as worthlessness. It cannot help it. So the air goes unguarded, and the climate goes unpriced, and the great cycling systems of the living planet are treated as a free input rather than the irreplaceable inheritance they are, until the supply tightens somewhere and the bill arrives, the way it arrived for the crew in the desert when the air went thin.

We do not have to seal a world to learn this. The sealed worlds have already filed the report. The lesson they bring back is not that we should put a meter on the sky. It is that the sky was always the most valuable thing we owned, and that abundance was never proof of its cheapness. It was only ever the mask.
