# What the Bay Knows About Us

## I.

The harbor at Old Point Comfort wakes a long time before the city does. I have been writing from a window above the slips for a few seasons now, and I have learned to listen for the order of the morning. The first sound is not a sound but the absence of one: the wind off the Roads dies just before dawn, and the water goes flat for a quarter hour, and the gulls go quiet because their argument is not yet useful. Then the diesel of the first boat clears its throat, and the second boat answers it from the other side of the breakwater, and the day is decided.

It is rarely the youngest man who leaves first. The man two slips over from where I sit has been doing this since before any of the others were born. He keeps his lines coiled in a particular way that none of the younger boats bother with anymore. He does not look at his watch. He does not need to. The water tells him.

I grew up on this bay. My father is from Fox Hill, which is a fishing village on the Virginia side of the Roads where his father worked at NASA Langley as one of the engineer-inventors who built the apparatus of a different America, and where Sunday afternoons were measured by who had come in full and who had come in empty. My family is one generation removed from the working life on the water and zero generations removed from the community of people who live it. That is the only credential the essay you are reading has. I have lived near this water long enough to have known three rough generations of the men who work it, and to have watched the green of the water change.

The bay looks, from a postcard, like one body of water. It is in fact a difficult body of water: the largest estuary in North America, about sixty-four thousand square miles of watershed reaching across six states and the District of Columbia, draining the eastern Appalachians and the Susquehanna and the James and the Rappahannock and the York and the Potomac into a single brackish sheet roughly two hundred miles long. The watermen who work it know what an outsider does not. They know that the wavelength of the chop on the Chesapeake is three or four seconds, sometimes less, while the wavelength of the ocean swell off the Atlantic is closer to twenty. A bay boat is built to the three. An ocean trawler will not survive a season in it. The body learns these intervals before the mind has a name for them. You watch men step off the dock, and you can tell in the first ten yards of the boat's run whether they grew up on this water or somewhere else.

That bodily knowledge is the watermen's analytical apparatus. The science they have is the science of the long observation. They have been on the same bars for forty years; they know what is there and what was there and what is not coming back. The scientists who come to study what they study respect them. The regulators who pass rules over their lives know what they are missing without that knowledge but cannot, by the architecture of their offices, incorporate it into a decision. The watermen carry the data. The regulatory system carries the authority. The two have, for forty years, been failing to find each other.

The first boats are out at the channel now. The men onboard are heading for the same bars their fathers worked. Most of them will come back with less than their fathers would have. Some will come back with nothing. The math of why is not a mystery to them, and it is not principally a story about the water.

It is a story about an accounting that does not balance, and about a community that has been absorbing the difference for longer than anyone has bothered to count.

## II.

The bay used to feed a country. That is not a romantic claim. It is a record.

In 1884, a Hampton oyster operation took the name J.S. Darling and Son when Frank Darling joined his father's existing business. At its peak the operation employed about a hundred and sixty shuckers and put two hundred thousand bushels of Chesapeake oysters into shipping every year. The shell mountains rose more than four stories high behind the packing houses; if you took an old photograph of the Hampton waterfront in the eighteen-nineties and held it up to the same view today, you would see those mountains where today there is parking. The Darlings also ran a menhaden reduction factory at Factory Point in Fox Hill, processing thousands of tons per year into fish meal for fertilizer and oil for industrial uses. The whole length of the Hampton waterfront was a working machine that fed northern cities, fertilized southern fields, and put pocket money into the hands of fishing families up and down the lower Bay.

Hampton was known up and down the coast as Crabtown in the nineteenth century. In the eighteen-seventies, a Hamptonian named James McMenamin patented a process for canning cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in airtight tins. The patent is one of those quiet artifacts that changes the shape of an entire industry without ever making it into a survey course. McMenamin's tins put Chesapeake crab into household pantries from Boston to New Orleans. There was a window of years in which a working family in lower Manhattan ate Hampton crab on Sunday afternoons because the local boatmen could not catch enough of their own.

Two pieces of arithmetic frame the rest of what follows. In the late eighteen-eighties, the Chesapeake oyster fishery produced about fifteen million bushels in a year. By the nineteen-nineties, it was producing under two hundred thousand. Two orders of magnitude in a century. About a ninety-nine-percent decline. That is not a soft figure. That is the figure.

There is a body of regional scholarship that has done the hard work of recording what was lost and what came after. William W. Warner's *Beautiful Swimmers* in 1976 is the canonical Chesapeake-fisheries narrative, and it won the 1977 Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction. Warner walked the bay, sat on the boats, kept his notebook out, and wrote a book that any working waterman from Crisfield to Reedville will still tell you got the work right. Anyone writing about the Bay since has had to write knowing Warner did it first. The thing I want to add here is what came after the decline Warner watched begin: what the architecture around the watermen has looked like through the four decades it has had a chance to respond, and what it has and has not been capable of.

What it has been capable of, you can see in the shoreline that is now condominiums. What it has not been capable of is the question.

## III.

There was a man called Biggie. He had the build of an oak: not tall, in the way some big men are not tall, but solid through the shoulders and back in a way that made the rest of him look smaller than it was. He worked oysters his whole life. His family had been doing it for generations. He died more than thirty years ago, before Virginia's commercial shad fishery closed, and what I know of him I know from my father, who watched him work as a boy.

He kept a six-pack of Coca-Cola on the boat, and that was lunch. He used to say, if you asked, that lunch was waiting in the water. Once the sun was high he would start eating: oysters one by one, off the side of the hull, opened on the deck rail with a wrist motion my father tried to keep count of and never could because the motion was faster than the count. He ate them at the rate his hands could free them. He kept working while he ate. He ate while he kept working. The work and the meal were not two things; they had been one thing for so long that there was no seam between them, the way a man who has done a thing for fifty years no longer separates the doing from himself.

He worked particular bars. They were the bars his father had worked. They were the bars his grandfather had worked. The boat was suited to the work, the way a tailored coat is suited to a body — the boat was old, and it was repaired the way old boats are repaired, with the patience of a man who does not throw things away because something he might need is in them. Biggie had a small dock on the Bay, and on the dock there were two coolers and a length of hose and a rope and a knife block and the few other things a man needs to receive what he has caught.

I never knew him. My father did. The witness is one generation removed. My father watched him as a boy; what comes through to me comes through my father's report. I am writing about a working community I am not myself a member of. My family is from it; I am one generation off. The men I name in the pages that follow are men whose work I have watched but not done. The science of the bay I have at second hand. What I have at first hand is the way the people I love talk about the water when they think no one outside the kitchen is listening.

Biggie's motion died with him. Not the work, which others took up, but the motion: the particular economy of a man's wrist over a particular shellfish on the deck rail of a particular boat in the morning fog of a particular bar in the lower Bay where the salinity sits at a certain band and the temperature in October falls into a certain range and the oysters come up sized a certain way. When he died, that combination went out of the world. The men who learned from him learned other versions of it; the work continues; the specific motion does not.

## IV.

Take the oyster first, because the arithmetic is the cleanest. From fifteen million bushels in the late eighteen-eighties to under two hundred thousand by the nineties is the headline. The cause structure has three waves. The first wave was overharvesting. The dredges scraped the reefs flat. By the eighteen-nineties the photographers were already publishing pictures of skipjacks coming in heavy because the operators were taking the bottom out from under the resource, and the reef structure, the cathedral of dead shell on which the next generation of oysters grows, was being broken faster than it could rebuild. The second wave was disease. MSX showed up in the late nineteen-fifties; Dermo followed; the oysters did not have any historical exposure to either parasite and the immune machinery was not there. The third wave is what is happening now, and it is what makes the bay's oyster problem inseparable from its agricultural problem: sediment from upstream runoff smothering the spat that does survive; nutrient loading driving algal blooms; dissolved oxygen falling in the deep water of the channel each summer to the point where the bottom of the central bay becomes uninhabitable to most of what lives in it.

Take the rockfish next, which is the bay's name for striped bass. Through the nineteen-seventies the rockfish collapsed in a way that registered in the recreational and commercial counts both. Maryland imposed a five-year moratorium in 1985. Virginia coordinated. Charter boats, commercial seiners, recreational anglers: all of them shut down. The federal government coordinated coastwide. It took until 1995 for the fishery to be declared restored, and there were a few years in the late nineties when the rockfish were thick enough on the bay that the working watermen who had taken the moratorium hardest were among the people most often quoted in the Bay-region papers about how restoration could in fact work.

Then the slow second decline began. The 2024 Maryland young-of-year index, the number of juvenile rockfish caught per sample at the seining stations the state runs every summer, was 2.0 fish, against a long-term average of 11.0 fish. That was the sixth consecutive below-average year.

Allison Colden, the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's Maryland executive director, told the public on the release of that index that "Maryland's striped bass surveys have made it clear; we're facing a looming disaster. Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future." She said in the same statement that "there are very few levers left to pull." She said, of the people who would carry the consequences, that "many people in Maryland rely on striped bass for their food, their businesses, and their futures. Without action, that will all be at risk." She said, of the climate stressors that compound the collapse, that "rising water temperatures and the low-oxygen periods of summer are pummeling [the species] during the months it can least afford to be pummeled."

Colden is forty-something and has the institutional patience of someone who has been making the same argument in slightly different words for a decade. Bill Goldsborough, who ran the same Foundation's fisheries portfolio through the rockfish-moratorium years and into the recovery, said to *Smithsonian Magazine* a few seasons back what is probably the single cleanest statement of the gap between the scientific time-horizon and the working time-horizon any conservation officer has put on the record. "Our objective is to try to restore the health of bay and aquatic life so that it can support viable fisheries," he said. "We see that task taking place over a longer term. But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment."

Take the shad. The American shad and the hickory shad are anadromous fish: they live most of their lives in the open Atlantic and run in the spring up the bay's tributary rivers to spawn in fresh water. In January of 1994, Virginia closed its commercial shad fishery in the bay's main stem. The runs did not come back. There is no single agreed-on explanation. The decline is multifactorial: turbines on the spawning rivers, predation on juveniles by the surging striped-bass population, water-quality changes in the spawning reaches, distant commercial bycatch in the open ocean. The science has not converged on a clean attribution. What it has converged on is the absence of a recovery. A working waterman who had built half his calendar around the spring shad run lost half his calendar in January of 1994, and has not gotten it back.

Take the crab. The blue crab is the most economically important species the bay still produces. The Maryland Department of Natural Resources and the Virginia Institute of Marine Science run a coordinated winter dredge survey every year to estimate the Bay-wide adult population. The 2025 estimate was about 238 million crabs, the second-lowest level since the survey began in 1990. The sixth consecutive year of below-average numbers. Colden's word for that, in the public statement that accompanied the release: "The red flags are flying for blue crabs." She wrote, of the regulatory architecture that has been trying to manage the resource against the running tally, that "with more than five years of below-average crab numbers, it is clear that changing conditions in the Bay are undermining the current management of this important species."

J.C. Hudgins runs the Virginia Watermen's Association and works the Piankatank in the middle Bay. He told *Maryland Matters* what the math at the dock looks like to a working crabber. "You're lucky if you can put $150 in your pocket per day. And it ain't no easy job. You're freezing your butt off. It's cold." He went on, of why the buyers cannot bear the prices the watermen need: "People aren't going to spend $20 for a pint of oysters. It's pretty expensive eating. I think people are feeding their families in other ways."

The institutional architecture that has been trying to manage the bay's fisheries for forty years has not been capable, on its own machinery, of acting on its own science. The science has been clear, in case after case, that the resource is in trouble. The recommendations from the scientific committees have run ahead of the actions the management boards have been politically capable of taking. The action has lagged, and the lag has compounded. The watermen, who carry the consequence, have watched this from inside the work and have been saying for thirty years, in their own vocabulary, what the scientific committees have been saying in another vocabulary. They have been saying it to whoever would listen. The state has held the hearings and recorded the testimony. The acting has not kept pace with the listening.

## V.

The case that shows the shape of the architecture most clearly is the menhaden case.

Menhaden are a small oily forage fish, sometimes called bunker in New Jersey, sometimes pogy in New England, that sits at the base of the bay's food web. The rockfish eat them. The osprey feed them to their fledglings. The bluefish chase them. The whales offshore depend on them. They are not what most readers would think of as a Chesapeake fishery, because they are not what gets ordered at the seafood counter. They are what gets harvested by industrial reduction vessels at Reedville, Virginia, at the mouth of the Great Wicomico River in Northumberland County, and what gets processed in the reduction plant there into fish meal and fish oil and omega-3 supplements and pet food and aquaculture feed. The end customer is an omega-3 capsule on a shelf in Indianapolis, a salmon farm in Chile, a forty-pound bag of dog food in a Phoenix grocery. The plant employs several hundred people in shifts that follow the menhaden runs.

The Bay-specific cap on the menhaden reduction fishery, the amount the industry is allowed to extract from the Chesapeake in a given year, was set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission in 2006 and reduced in 2017 to its current level of fifty-one thousand metric tons. The conservation side regards that figure as a floor, not a ceiling. The industry side regards it as a hard-fought concession. The number itself was not derived from any biological estimate of the menhaden population in the Bay; it was derived from historical landings.

The number was derived from historical landings because no one has the data to derive it from anything else.

"We don't know the abundance of menhaden in the bay at any given time relative to the coast," Genny Nesslage told the public-radio station WHRO a few seasons ago. Nesslage is an associate research professor at the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science, one of the small handful of menhaden scientists in the country, and the statement she made on the record is the kind of statement scientists do not make casually. Rob Latour at the Virginia Institute of Marine Science, also one of the country's menhaden authorities, said it the same way: "We have no data to really do this in a meaningful way, from a biological perspective." VIMS drafted, in 2023, a proposal for a three-million-dollar Bay-specific menhaden study that would have produced the missing biomass and recruitment data. The Virginia General Assembly declined to fund it. Three million dollars against a fishery worth tens of millions per year in plant revenue, several hundred jobs in a politically organized rural county, and the foodweb foundation of a multi-billion-dollar recreational fishery. The appropriation was not made.

In the same window in which Maryland's rockfish young-of-year index was running at two against an eleven baseline, the bay's reduction fishery was operating against a cap derived from historical extraction rates rather than from any current biological inventory. The ecological-impact pattern is what you would expect. Osprey fledging failure has been observed in several Bay tributaries and is suspected to track insufficient forage. Rockfish summer-heat stress and low-oxygen stress compound with insufficient forage. The bay-wide story is not separable from the menhaden story, and the menhaden story is operating without the data the participants in the conversation know it needs.

On the last day of 2025, a Virginia resident named Tonya O'Connor filed a 349-page petition with the Virginia Marine Resources Commission asking it to declare a temporary moratorium on the bay's reduction fishery until Bay-specific science was produced. The petition cited Virginia's public-trust doctrine, with the legal grounding worked through three Virginia cases: *Commonwealth v. City of Newport News* (1932), *Taylor v. Commonwealth* (1904), and *Bradford v. Nature Conservancy* (1982). Its central finding, in the petitioner's own words, was that the regulatory body "did not have — and still does not have — a Bay-wide estimate of menhaden biomass, age structure, spatial distribution, recruitment trends, or localized depletion risk."

The hearing was set for April 21, 2026. The Commission received 1,266 public comments on the petition between filing and hearing, in a state where most citizens have never heard of the Commission and most fisheries hearings draw double-digit comment counts. The vote, when it came, was six to two against. The moratorium did not move forward. The reduction fishery continued at its cap.

Colden said, of the broader question of how the science is supposed to land in the management architecture, that "just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should, we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that that's warranted." She has said versions of this for years. The line that lands most plainly is the one she gave to the public after the rockfish index: the Bay's regulatory architecture has very few levers left to pull. The menhaden case is what an architecture looks like when the levers it still has are pulled in the wrong direction because the data the levers are supposed to be pulled against does not exist, and the appropriation that would have produced the data was not made, and the petition that would have forced the question was voted down six to two.

This is the same shape coal had. The Bay's reduction fishery is an extractive operation taking a resource that other people in the system depend on, processing it into intermediate inputs sold to distant end-customers, and concentrating both the revenue and the political organization at the operator while dispersing the costs across a thousand miles of foodweb and forty years of bay-wide decline.

## VI.

A working community is not principally a count of jobs. It is a fabric. The fabric thins by one capacity at a time, and the loss is not legible until the fabric is no longer holding what it used to hold.

Tangier Island sits in the middle of the lower bay, roughly fifteen miles east-southeast of the mouth of the Potomac. It is roughly two square miles of low marsh and sand. It has been continuously inhabited since the seventeen-seventies; the people who live there speak with an accent that is, by some accounts, closer to colonial-era English than to anything else still spoken in North America. The island's economy has historically been crabs in the warm months and oysters in the cold months, with an eight-generation working-community fabric inside which the same families have done the same work for longer than the country has been a country. The shoreline is eroding at roughly fifteen feet a year. The projection on which the island's mayor and its elementary-school teachers and its waterman families operate is that most of Tangier will be uninhabitable within the lifetime of the children currently in school there.

James "Ooker" Eskridge has been the mayor of Tangier for two decades and a working waterman for more than fifty years. He spoke to the Thomson Reuters Foundation in plain words about what is at stake. "We're talking about saving a culture," he said, "the people, a way of life — the whole package." Cook Cannon worked Tangier waters before he took a seat at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation; the quote of his that *Smithsonian Magazine* carried, when the writer asked him about what he expected the next decade to do to the place he is from, was this: "I could cry to think about what's going to happen to Tangier." Cameron Evans, who grew up on Tangier and went to Virginia Wesleyan as one of the few in his cohort who left the island for the mainland, said to the same Thomson Reuters reporter the line that has been quoted around the bay since: "If you can't save an island a mile and a half long, how are you going to save the East Coast." Andy Harris, who represents Maryland's Eastern Shore in Congress, has framed the same arithmetic from the other side of the aisle. "Our watermen are pretty resilient," he has told *Maryland Matters*. "They're like our farmers. They have good years and bad years. But if you have a couple bad seasons in a row, then you're in trouble." The asks the island has put forward, for a seawall, for federal funding to slow the erosion, for the kind of attention coastal communities in other parts of the country have received, have mostly not been answered. Earl Swift's 2018 book *Chesapeake Requiem* walked the year on Tangier in the kind of detail that a working waterman can read and recognize, which is the test that matters. Anyone writing about Tangier since has been writing in Swift's wake.

Smith Island sits twelve miles north of Tangier, on the Maryland side of the state line that runs through the lower bay. The picking houses where the soft-shell crab is hand-prepared have been thinning for decades; what remains is mostly older women, working at long tables with the picking knives their mothers used. There is a saying on Smith Island, an older one, about how the mud between your toes never quite leaves once you have walked the back creeks as a child. The young people are leaving. Tom Horton's 1996 *An Island Out of Time* recorded the place at the period when the leaving had begun to accelerate, and his Chesapeake Bay journalism since has kept the record fresh. He has been on Smith with the same patience he has brought to the rest of the bay, and the people there know him as a writer who got it right.

Tilghman Island, on Maryland's Eastern Shore, has its own working presence. *Maryland Matters* published a photograph in 2023 of Pat Whewell culling oysters aboard Lonnie Gowe's boat in Broad Creek. Three years later, the same paper carried photos of Chumley Fisher and Chet Schwartz offloading oysters at Tilghman; the men were named in the captions because the paper had taken the time to name them. The thinning of working-watermen rolls there has run in step with what the rest of the bay's communities have seen.

Hampton, where I have been writing this, is its own slower version of the same arc. The marina I look down on is in the process of tipping from a working marina, slips occupied by crab boats, oyster boats, charter operators, to a recreational marina occupied by leisure boats. The tipping has been gradual. It has happened by one slip at a time. The supply chain around a working marina is what thins first: the welders who built the davits, the engine shops that kept the diesels running, the ice plants that kept the holds cold, the buyers who picked up the catch at the dock, the haulers who put it into the trucks to Baltimore or Philadelphia or New York. Lose one of those and the working operators have to drive further or wait longer. Lose two and some of them quit. Lose three and the slips start emptying. By the time you can see it from the road it has been happening for a generation.

What is being lost in Tangier and Smith and Tilghman and Hampton is something other than a list of jobs. The fabric is what it is — a way the people who live near working water know one another, the cousin-network and the church-network and the marina-network and the picking-house network through which a community looks after its own. When the picking houses close, the soft-shell crab keeps being eaten; it gets picked elsewhere, or by machine, or by hands that have not done it for three generations. The product is preserved; the practice is not. The product is the thing the market priced. The practice is the thing the market did not.

## VII.

The waterman who follows is not named. He is a man my father has long known, and the choice of anonymity is the discipline of writing about someone whose community knows him. He is a crabber and fisherman. He has been on the bay since he was a boy himself. He is from one of the same working-Bay communities the Tangier and Smith and Tilghman watermen come from. What is true of him is true, in slightly different shapes, of a great many men still working this water.

He still wakes between two and three in the morning to run his crab pots. He has been waking at that hour, for that purpose, for as long as he has had pots. He runs them the way he ran them when he was younger, with the same boat, the same lines, the same hands. The hands are older. The math at the end of the day is different. He comes back to the dock by mid-morning. He hoses down the boat. He goes to a mechanic's shop where he works on cars until evening. The shop is his. He built it on his own time and his own dime over the course of a couple of decades, while the water side of his work was slowly failing to support the family math by itself. He works the shop until five or six. Then he comes home.

What he calls this arrangement is between him and the people who know him. What it is, in any case, is two full working days inside one twenty-four-hour day, six days a week, fifty weeks a year, against a body that is no longer twenty-five. He has not quit the water. He could not quit the water. The water is what he knows. The shop is what he has built so he does not have to quit it.

The crab work, on its own, no longer pays for the family. The second job is the math of the gap. The boat payments, the slip fees, the fuel, the gear, the licenses, the freezer space, the trip out and the trip back — the math at the end of a season comes up short of what a family in this part of the country needs to live. The architecture around the work has not been pricing the work at its real cost for thirty years. The watermen are not idiots; they have seen the math drift; they have watched the buyer prices stall and the input prices climb and the catches thin. What they have done with the drift is what working people in every American extractive industry have done with the same drift: they have absorbed it. The body has been the cushion. The second job has been the cushion. The willingness to stay in the work for less than the work used to give back has been the cushion.

What did not come up in the tongs got left in the water. What got left in the water had to be carried by something. By the Bay, while the Bay could carry it. By the watermen, after the Bay could no longer carry it alone. The cost did not vanish because the price did not include it. The cost was paid. It was paid by people whose books did not record the payment in the column where it would be visible. It was paid in the second job and the long days and the body absorbing the difference and the family math continuing because the man at the center of it would not let it fail.

Maryland's oyster-restoration program is one of the most ambitious ecological restoration efforts of its kind in the country. Five tributary-scale sanctuaries; hundreds of acres of rebuilt reef; tens of millions of oysters per year added to the rebuilt structures; close to twenty years of coordinated state-and-federal work. The Maryland oyster population has roughly tripled since 2005. By any reasonable ecological measure, the program has worked. Colden has said so on the public record. "Large-scale oyster restoration is working," she said. "Our effort to bring oysters back from the brink in Chesapeake Bay is working." She has also been careful to say, in the same statements, that "continued recovery is not guaranteed."

In the same year that ecological success was being acknowledged, Maryland filed a federal disaster declaration request for its oyster fishery. The harvest had collapsed in volume and value. The watermen whose livelihood depended on the wild harvest were in financial crisis. The market for Chesapeake oysters had been hollowed out by Gulf Coast farm-raised aquaculture and by a sequence of extreme-weather seasons that disrupted the narrow harvest windows. Mitch Tarnowski, who leads the Maryland Department of Natural Resources oyster reef surveys, said it to *Maryland Matters* in twelve words. "There are plenty of oysters out there. They're just not being harvested." Jason Ruth, who owns Harris Seafood Co. in Grasonville and processes a substantial fraction of the Maryland wild-oyster harvest, said the structural piece. "While the demand for boutique aquaculture oysters has grown over the last decade, the shucked oyster sector — the larger part of the industry — has seen a sixty-year nationwide decline."

Colden's word on the disaster declaration, given in support of it, holds the paradox in a single sentence. "Despite a growing oyster population, Maryland's watermen are feeling the pinch of extreme weather and market competition." The recovery was real. The squeeze on the watermen was real. They were not separate processes. They were the same process: the population came back to a market the watermen were already losing, and into a working fabric that had been thinning while the restoration was succeeding, and the recovery did not come back to them.

Colden has been clear about the political stakes. "One of the most important ways people connect with the Chesapeake Bay is through eating seafood and fishing," she said. "Without watermen, there's certainly a lot fewer eyes on the water and a lot fewer people caring passionately about the Bay." The restoration program will not survive the absence of the working community that has been the bay's chief constituency for four hundred years. The fabric has been the political prerequisite for the science. Without the fabric, the science does not hold.

## VIII.

A few hundred miles west of the bay's headwaters, the same shape repeats. The water gives way to hills. The boats give way to mines.

McDowell County, West Virginia, sits at the bottom of a watershed of value that for a hundred years flowed out of it. The coal that came out of McDowell powered Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Detroit; it lit the houses of New York; it built the steel for the bridges that crossed every American river east of the Mississippi. The price the operators paid for the coal at the mine mouth went, very largely, to people who lived somewhere else. The costs the coal generated — the black lung that took the miners, the acid drainage that ran orange in the creeks for fifty miles, the schools and clinics that emptied as the operators decamped, the longevity gap that opened between McDowell residents and the American median — were paid in McDowell. They were paid by McDowell. The architecture that managed the relationship had no slot in its books for what McDowell was absorbing.

The Bay is at the bottom of a different kind of watershed. The value the bay produces in seafood, in tourism, in recreational fishing, in the cultural identity of an entire mid-Atlantic region — that value is captured by people scattered across six states and the country beyond, including the people who write essays like this one in apartments above marinas. The costs of producing the value — the bodies of the watermen, the thinning of the working communities, the loss of the motions, the architecture's failure to act on its own science — are paid in the working communities of the bay. They are paid by the watermen of Tangier and Smith and Tilghman and Fox Hill and the Piankatank and the lower Bay. The architecture that has been managing the relationship for forty years has not had a slot in its books for what the working communities have been absorbing.

That is the shape. It is the same shape, in different geography, with a different resource. The miner in McDowell and the waterman on the bay have been at the bottom of two different watersheds of value that have flowed somewhere else. Each of them has been carrying the cost of what the system did not, on its own books, pay for. Each of them has been told, in the political vocabulary available to him, a different story about who his enemy is. The miner has been told the waterman's coastal-environmentalist neighbors are the enemy. The waterman has been told the miner's energy-extractive neighbors are the enemy. Different states. Different news outlets. Different votes. The same mechanism, dressed in different clothes. Each of them has been given an enemy who is, in fact, the other one. The system that has been doing the extracting has been keeping the stories in different rooms because the stories make more sense to the people inside them than they would if they were told together in one room.

This essay's argument is structural, not partisan. The accounting that has failed the watermen is the same accounting that has failed the miners. The politics of how each community has been told a different story about the other is part of the accounting too. Naming the shape does not require choosing a side in the partisan fight; it requires noticing that the fight has been used to keep the shape hidden.

The first boats are out at the mouth now. The light has crossed the channel. The wind has come up off the Roads, the gulls are arguing again, and the day is beginning. The man two slips over from where I sit is on his way back already because his boat is faster and his bars are closer than the younger men's, and he runs his pots before the heat sets in. He will tie up at his usual cleat. He will hose down. He will put what came up in the right cooler in the right order. He will load the truck. He will drive to the shop, or to the buyer, or to whatever the next thing on his calendar is, because there is more than one thing on his calendar and there will be every day for as long as he can keep going.

It is not yet the day he could have had.
