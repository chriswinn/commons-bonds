# What the Bay Knows

The wind that works Hampton Roads all night usually gives out a little before first light. In the stillness it leaves behind, the harbor at Old Point Comfort begins to make its working sounds. A diesel turns over, runs rough for a moment, settles into itself. A line comes off a cleat. Somewhere down the row of slips a man asks a man on a boat a question in the voice he would use across a kitchen table, and the water carries it the length of the pier, because at this hour the water carries everything. The Fort is a black shape behind the masts. Out past the breakwater the mouth of the Bay opens gray into gray, and the channel marker blinks red on its own slow schedule, the only color anywhere in the world.

The man two slips over from the sailboat I live aboard has been going out at this hour since before most of the men on this dock were born. He does not hurry. Nothing in fifty years of mornings has ever been bought by hurrying. He checks his fuel, checks his lines, stands for a moment reading the water the way other men read a page of figures, and goes, and the sound of his engine moves out toward the channel until it is part of the gray. The harbor is quiet for a while after that. Then the next boat starts.

I grew up on this bay. My father is from Fox Hill, the old island fishing neighborhood on Hampton's eastern edge, where for most of the twentieth century nearly every house had at least one person on the water. My grandfather was the exception on his street, an engineer and an inventor at NASA Langley just up the road, building the apparatus of a different America in a different kind of building. The men on either side of him worked the water. The men who taught my father to handle a small boat, to read a channel, to know what the water means by its smell in late August, were watermen and the sons of watermen. My family is one generation removed from the working life. We are zero generations removed from the community.

Most days, at most hours, the Chesapeake photographs like a postcard: flat water, soft light, sails standing in the middle distance, the kind of bay you would put a child on. The watermen know a different body of water. The Bay is not the ocean, and the difference is specific enough to be counted in seconds. On the Atlantic, a five-foot swell is barely worth mentioning, because the crests run far apart; twenty seconds can pass between one and the next, a long slow breathing that big ships ride and small boats survive. The Bay does not give twenty seconds. The Bay gives three or four, because the water is shallow and the distances are short and the energy has nowhere to spread, so it stacks. A three-foot chop on a bad Bay afternoon will hammer a small boat in a way a much larger ocean swell never touches it. The men who work this water know which days you do not go out, which days you go out but stay close, and which days the sky is lying about what the water will be doing in three hours. They know it without consulting anything outside their own bodies. Forty years of mornings is the instrument.

It is worth being precise about what that instrument holds. A man who has worked the same water six days a week for four decades is carrying a continuous record of it: where the crabs ran in late October and the year they stopped running there, which bars sounded under the tongs and which fell silent, what the smell at the dock did, August over August. The record is as fine-grained as anything the state survey boats bring in, and it runs longer. The watermen carry the data. The regulatory system carries the authority. The two have been failing to find each other for a generation, and the men going out of this harbor in the dark have been living in the gap.

---

What this water used to carry is not a matter of memory or family legend. Hampton keeps the receipts. In the nineteenth century the city was known up and down the coast as Crabtown. In the 1870s a Hampton packer named James McMenamin patented a process for canning cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in airtight tins, and the Bay's blue crab went out to tables that had never seen the Bay. The firm of J.S. Darling and Son took that name in 1884, when Frank Darling joined his father's oyster business, and grew into one of the largest oyster-packing operations in the country. The Dictionary of Virginia Biography keeps the arithmetic: 160 shuckers at the peak, 200,000 bushels of Chesapeake oysters packed in a single banner year, some 50,000 gallons a year still being shucked as late as 1938. The empty shells went out the back of the plant onto piles that rose more than four stories, mountains of shell a town could navigate by. Down at Factory Point, on the Fox Hill shore, the Darlings ran a menhaden factory that rendered thousands of tons of fish a year into meal and oil. An operation at that scale is not a business in a town. It is closer to a town's metabolism, and Hampton's ran on the Bay.

The shells did not stay piled. Shell was cheaper than gravel, so it was sold, crushed, and spread into roads and driveways across the lower Bay, which is how the driveway of my father's family's house came to be paved with it. I spent parts of my childhood walking on the material oyster reefs are built from, ground up and rolled flat under a family's cars, and most of my life went by before it occurred to me that there was anything strange in that.

The scale of what was lost comes down to two numbers. In the late 1880s, the Chesapeake produced something on the order of 15 million bushels of oysters a year. By the 1990s, the harvest was under 200,000. Two orders of magnitude inside a century, a 99 percent decline, and a fishery that survived in name while almost everything the name referred to went away.

Anyone who writes about the Chesapeake writes knowing William W. Warner did it first. *Beautiful Swimmers*, his account of the watermen and the blue crab, won the Pulitzer in 1977 and has stood since as the book this Bay produced about itself. Warner caught the working Bay whole, the boats and the seasons and the men, rendered just as the long decline was gathering under all of it. What he could not write, because it had not happened yet, was the half century after: the decline running most of the way to the bottom, the institutions built to arrest it mostly failing to, the working communities thinning toward the edge of memory. His book permits an elegy reading, the long look back at a world that time and weather took. The record since permits no such reading. What took the world of *Beautiful Swimmers* was not weather. It was an accounting failure, and accounting failures produce bills, and the bill for this one is documented, payable, and currently being paid by nearly everyone except the parties who ran it up.

One of the oystermen my father knew, growing up on Fox Hill, was a man called Biggie. Biggie had the build of an oak: not tall, in the way some big men are not tall, but solid through the shoulders and the back, the body of a man who had been doing one specific kind of work since he was old enough to be useful in a boat. He worked oysters. The bars he worked were the bars his father and his grandfather had worked before him, and he knew them the way you know rooms of a house you were raised in. His boat was an old boat kept the way old boats are kept, repaired by the man who ran her, one season's need at a time, carrying nothing that had not earned its place aboard. He went out before the day. He came back when the bushels were whatever the bushels were going to be.

Every morning he went out, he carried a six-pack of Coca-Cola and no lunch. My father, who was a small boy then and honestly puzzled, asked him about that once, and Biggie told him his lunch was waiting for him in the water. When the sun got high and the work found its rhythm, he ate as he worked. He kept a spot on the boat near his body where the next oyster sat, so that eating cost him no reach and no pause, and the motion he opened them with was the smallest motion the work can be reduced to, a quarter turn of the wrist, the shell parting, the meat coming up clean. My father tried more than once to count how many oysters Biggie went through in a day on the water, and he never managed it. Not because there were too many to count, though there may have been. Because the eating never started and never stopped. It ran under the work as continuously as breath, and it drew about as much attention to itself.

I never saw any of this. Biggie has been dead for more than thirty years, and what I have is my father's witness, told often enough across my life that the scene plays in my head like memory. It is not memory. It is inheritance, one generation removed, and I am setting it down the way it was handed to me, because the inheritance is now most of what is left. The bars Biggie worked sounded under his tongs the way they had sounded under the tongs of the men before him. They do not sound that way now. And the quarter turn of the wrist, the motion a working life had polished down to almost nothing, is not being learned by anyone, because there is no younger man going out to bars that could teach it. Biggie's motion died with him.

---

How a bay loses 99 percent of its oysters is worth slowing down for, because nothing about it was mysterious, and most of it was understood while it was happening. The first wave was overharvesting: dredges scraping the reefs flat, season after season, faster than the reefs could rebuild. The second wave was disease, MSX arriving in the late 1950s and Dermo in the years that followed, parasites the weakened, low-density populations had no resilience against. The third wave was the land itself, sediment and agricultural runoff smothering the young oysters where they tried to set, nutrient loads feeding algal blooms that drew down the oxygen everything else needed. Each wave compounded the last. Reefs that colonial ships had to chart around were ground down to flat, silt-buried bottom.

The part of this that the watermen understood before anyone wrote it down is that an oyster reef is not a deposit, like coal, that can only be drawn down. A reef left standing is an engine. Take some oysters, leave the rest to spawn, come back next year, and the harvest holds across generations; the fishery makes more of itself. What happened on the Chesapeake is what happens when the rate of taking exceeds the rate of making, and then keeps going: the dredges did not just take the oysters, they took the reef the next oysters would have set on. The engine itself went up the culling board. After that, leaving the water alone stopped being an answer. You put the structure back, or what was lost stays lost. Nobody marks the day a fishery crosses that line. The boats go out the next morning the same way they went out the morning before. But something has been done that no decision available to the next morning's men can undo.

The striped bass, what the Bay calls rockfish, is the proof that the line can be seen coming and answered. By the late 1970s the population had collapsed badly enough that Maryland shut its fishery down entirely: a five-year moratorium beginning in 1985, Virginia and the federal government coordinating, the commercial fleet and the charter fleet and the recreational anglers all beached together. I was a boy at the marinas in those years, and I remember the talk without having understood the politics of it. What I understood, the way a child understands without being told, was that a thing had reached a point where the men whose living depended on it had agreed, with whatever reluctance, to stop, for years, so the thing could come back. Some of them never came back to the work. The fish did. By 1995 the fishery was declared restored, one of the few large fisheries anywhere brought back from that kind of bottom.

That memory is why the present numbers land the way they do on the men who lived through the first collapse. Maryland's juvenile survey, the young-of-year index the Department of Natural Resources has run for decades, came in at 2.0 fish per sample in 2024, against a long-term average of 11.0. It was the sixth consecutive below-average year. One bad year is weather. Six in a row is structure.

Allison Colden, who directs the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's Maryland office, has been watching the signal accumulate. "Maryland's striped bass surveys have made it clear; we're facing a looming disaster," she has said. "Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future." And, more bluntly: "there are very few levers left to pull." Many people in Maryland, she has said, "rely on striped bass for their food, their businesses, and their futures. Without action, that will all be at risk." Rising water temperatures and the low-oxygen stretches of summer, she says, are "pummeling" the fish "during the months it can least afford to be pummeled."

Her counterpart in Virginia, Chris Moore, has said the harder thing, responding to the 2024 stock assessment: "Despite recent management actions, striped bass continue to struggle. It is obvious that conditions have changed and striped bass will not have the same recovery they had in the 1980s. Warmer water from climate change, habitat loss, and invasive species are all making it very hard for striped bass to bounce back."

Sit with that sentence. The recovery the men on this water remember, the one they paid five years of their lives for, is not on the menu this time. The conditions that made it possible are gone. And the institutional response, so far, has been calibrated to the Bay that no longer exists: catch limits tightened in increments smaller than the science indicates, emergency actions debated longer than the species can spare. When the question on the table was raising the harvest, the committees acted with confidence. Now that the question is cutting it, they have discovered an unfamiliar caution.

Bill Goldsborough, who directed fisheries at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation through the moratorium years, described the two clocks to *Smithsonian Magazine*. "Our objective is to try to restore the health of bay and aquatic life so that it can support viable fisheries," he said. "We see that task taking place over a longer term [...]. But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment."

The rockfish at least got its rescue once. The shad did not. In January 1994, Virginia closed its commercial shad fishery, the spring run that had been one of the Bay's reliable harvests through most of the twentieth century. Shad spend most of their lives in the open Atlantic and come into the Bay each spring to spawn, running up the tributaries to fresh water; the Bay is the nursery for the shad of a whole region. The closure was the same instrument that had worked for the rockfish, a generation off so the runs could rebuild. The runs did not rebuild. The science on why has never converged on a single answer. What it has converged on is the absence of a recovery. There are not, in any working sense, shad in the Bay anymore. The tool that saved the rockfish arrived, for the shad, too late, and thirty years of waiting has not changed the verdict.

Now it is the blue crab's turn in the same queue. The 2025 winter dredge survey, run jointly by Maryland's Department of Natural Resources and the Virginia Institute of Marine Science, put the Bay-wide population at about 238 million crabs, the second-lowest count since the survey began in 1990, and the sixth consecutive below-average year. "The red flags are flying for blue crabs," Colden said. Her longer framing is the one worth holding: "with more than five years of below average crab numbers, it is clear that changing conditions in the Bay are undermining the current management of this important species." Read that carefully. The rules are not being broken. The rules are being outrun. The management regime was calibrated to a Bay that has since changed underneath it, and the committees that set the limits still meet on the old Bay's schedule. The crabs are no longer on that schedule.

The pattern repeating across the species is the thing the watermen saw first, every time, from the deck, years before the surveys confirmed it. They felt the rockfish thinning before the index recorded it, the way miners know what the dust is doing to them before the X-rays come back. And the pattern repeating across the institutions is the other thing they have learned to expect. The Chesapeake Bay Program, the federal-state partnership established in 1983 to clean the Bay, has been measuring and modeling and allocating nutrient reductions for more than forty years; its own science advisory committee's recent evaluation was as plain as such documents are allowed to be. The cleanup is not on track to meet its goals. Point-source pollution has come down in many places. The agricultural sector has not been required to do what the science said it needed to do, and the political will to require it has not formed. The watermen live at the bottom of a 64,000-square-mile watershed, six states and the District of Columbia draining past their boats, and everything the upstream economy declines to contain comes to them. Nothing they do reaches upstream at all.

What the arithmetic of all this feels like from inside a workboat, J.C. Hudgins can tell you. He runs the Virginia Watermen's Association and works the Piankatank River in the Middle Bay. "You're lucky if you can put $150 in your pocket per day," he told *Maryland Matters*. "And it ain't no easy job. You're freezing your butt off. It's cold." On the other end of the transaction: "People aren't going to spend $20 for a pint of oysters. It's pretty expensive eating. I think people are feeding their families in other ways."

There is a fish almost nobody reading this has ever eaten, and it sits at the center of the Bay's hardest fight. The menhaden is small, oily, and silver, and it runs in schools so dense their movement can flatten the surface of the water like a passing wind. New Jersey calls it bunker, New England calls it pogy. By weight it is one of the most important fish in the Atlantic, because it is the thing almost everything else eats: the forage base of the food web, the species the striped bass and the bluefish and the ospreys and the dolphins all come for.

It is also the target of the largest single fishery on the East Coast, an industrial reduction operation out of Reedville, Virginia, at the mouth of the Great Wicomico River in Northumberland County. The plant catches menhaden at scale and grinds them into fish meal, fish oil, omega-3 supplements, pet food, and aquaculture feed. The catch leaves the Bay in tank trucks and railcars and ships. Nobody eats it at a table. It is converted into inputs for industries somewhere else, and the end customers have no idea the Chesapeake is in their lives at all: the consumer of an omega-3 capsule in Indianapolis, the operator of a salmon farm in Chile, the buyer of a 40-pound bag of dog food in a Phoenix grocery. The value disperses across a continent and beyond it. Whatever the removal is doing to the Bay stays in the Bay. And the plant employs several hundred people, in shifts that follow the runs, in a county that needs the payroll; nothing in this story is simple where a payroll is involved.

Here is what the regulatory architecture knows about how much menhaden the Bay can spare: it does not know. The Bay-specific cap, 51,000 metric tons, was set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission under Amendment 3, adopted in 2017 and in force in 2018, a cut of roughly 41 percent from the 87,000 metric tons that preceded it. The motion to cut came from Colden herself, then a senior fisheries scientist on the foundation's Maryland staff. But the cap, before and after the cut, has been derived from the history of what the fleet landed, not from any measurement of what the Bay holds, because no such measurement exists. Genny Nesslage, an associate research professor at the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science, said it plainly to WHRO: "We don't know the abundance of menhaden in the bay at any given time relative to the coast." Rob Latour, who studies menhaden at the Virginia Institute of Marine Science, went further: "We have no data to really do this in a meaningful way, from a biological perspective." In 2023 his institute drafted a proposal for a Bay-specific menhaden study, about $3 million. The Virginia General Assembly declined to fund it.

So the fight runs on what each side can see. The ospreys in some tributaries have been failing to fledge their young, and the people who watch ospreys suspect the parents cannot find enough small oily fish for a growing brood; the rockfish take their summer heat stress with less to eat. The industry disputes the causal story, and the suspicion is not yet a settled attribution. What nobody disputes is the absence of the measurement. "Just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should," Colden has said, "we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that that's warranted." Last year the commission opened a new addendum that could cut the Bay cap by as much as half. The pattern of the last forty years suggests the cut that finally passes will be smaller than the one the science recommends, and later.

On the last day of 2025, a Virginia resident named Tanya O'Connor filed a 349-page petition with the Virginia Marine Resources Commission asking it to declare a temporary moratorium on the reduction fishery until the Bay-specific science existed. Petition 449 put the absence in regulatory language. The commission, its central finding read, "did not have, and still does not have, a Bay-wide estimate of menhaden biomass, age structure, spatial distribution, recruitment trends, or localized depletion risk." Her legal grounding reached back through a century of Virginia's public-trust doctrine, *Taylor v. Commonwealth* in 1904, *Commonwealth v. City of Newport News* in 1932, *Bradford v. Nature Conservancy* in 1982: the cases that say the Commonwealth holds its waters in trust for its people. The commission heard the petition on April 21, 2026, and received 1,266 public comments. It voted six to two against. The moratorium did not move forward, and the fleet fishes on, against a cap built from its own past landings, over a stock nobody has measured.

It is the same shape coal had. An extraction whose value disperses beyond the horizon, a cost that concentrates at home, a regulator that cannot fund the question, and a public asked to prove harm to the satisfaction of the parties profiting from the absence of proof.

Tangier Island sits in the middle of the lower Bay, a low spread of marsh and sand that has been continuously inhabited since the 1770s. The accent spoken there is closer, by some accounts, to colonial-era English than anything else still spoken in North America. The economy has been crabs in the warm months and oysters in the cold ones for eight generations. The island is also disappearing. Army Corps of Engineers scientists, writing in the journal *Scientific Reports* in 2015, put the loss at about two-thirds of Tangier's landmass since 1850, with roughly 1.2 square miles remaining; the figure that travels in the press, 15 feet of shoreline a year, describes the worst of the exposed western shore, while the peer-reviewed average runs closer to 1.7 feet a year. The average is the more frightening number, because it never takes a year off. The same study's projection gives the island a horizon inside the lifetime of children now in its school. The islanders have asked, for years, for the engineering that would buy them decades. The answers have mostly not come at the scale that would matter.

James "Ooker" Eskridge, Tangier's mayor for two decades and a working waterman for more than fifty years, told the Thomson Reuters Foundation what the stakes are: "We're talking about saving a culture, the people, (a) way of life, the whole package." Cook Cannon, a Tangier waterman before he joined the Chesapeake Bay Foundation, told *Smithsonian Magazine*, "I could cry to think about what's going to happen to Tangier." Cameron Evans, a Tangier native who was studying at Virginia Wesleyan when the Thomson Reuters Foundation found him, asked the question that hangs over every coastal community on the seaboard: "If you can't save an island a mile and a half long, how are you going to save the East Coast..."

Earl Swift went and lived among the islanders to write *Chesapeake Requiem*, and his 2018 account stands: part rising water, part political failure, all of it borne by people who answer the inevitability argument by going crabbing the next morning. What the requiem register leaves on the table is the ledger underneath it. An island is not only a place; it is eight generations of accumulated knowledge of one body of water, a working asset no one ever appraised, and when it goes under, the loss will be recorded nowhere, because no books exist that carry it.

Twelve miles north, on the Maryland side, Smith Island is running the same course at its own speed. The picking houses are thinning; for generations, mostly older women picked the meat from soft-shell crabs the men brought in, and the young people have been leaving for the mainland. There is a saying on Smith that you get mud between your toes when you spend time there, and the mud means the island never leaves you. The women who say it are mostly past the age when leaving was an option. The children who heard it have been on the mainland for a decade. Tom Horton wrote Smith Island's book, *An Island Out of Time*, in 1996, when the leaving was already underway; he recorded who was going and what they took with them. The question his record leaves standing is the one nobody on this coast asks out loud: when a working community is let go, who holds the bill for what it took three centuries to build?

The work itself has not stopped, and the record keeps catching it where it persists. On Tilghman Island, *Maryland Matters* photographed Pat Whewell culling oysters aboard Lonnie Gowe's boat in Broad Creek in 2023, and Chumley Fisher and Chet Schwartz offloading oysters on Tilghman three years later. The pictures show exactly what the same pictures would have shown in 1950: the boots, the piles, the gray water. What the pictures cannot show is the supply chain hollowing out behind the subjects. Hampton is not Tangier; a city that size absorbs its losses quietly. But I have watched the marina where I keep my own boat tip, year by year, from a place where working operators held most of the slips to a place where recreational owners do. The boatbuilders who built the wooden deadrises are mostly gone. The welders go, the engine shops, the ice plants, the buyers, the haulers, each closure making the next more likely, until the young person who might have stayed cannot find the shoreside work that would have made staying possible. They leave, and the waterfront thins by one more capacity, and the next year someone else faces the same choice with less holding them. You can still buy a Chesapeake oyster in Hampton. The product is preserved; the practice is not.

Andy Harris, the Republican who represents Maryland's Eastern Shore in Congress, put the working math in the plainest terms anyone has. "Our watermen are pretty resilient," he told *Maryland Matters*. "They're like our farmers. They have good years and bad years. But if you have a couple bad seasons in a row, then you're in trouble." Note who is saying that, and from which side of the aisle. The arithmetic of a couple bad seasons in a row does not check party registration on its way in the door.

There is a man on this water I have known of most of my life, and I am not going to name him. He is alive, and the question of how he wants to appear in print is not yet settled between us, and his name belongs to him. He is of the generation after Biggie's, and the distance between their two working lives is the distance the Bay has traveled in one lifetime.

He gets up at two or three in the morning and goes out to run his crab pots, the way he has all his working life. He is back at the dock by mid-morning, because the catch that once filled a day no longer fills one. Then he goes to his second working day: the mechanic shop he built for himself, on his own time and his own dime, across years of the same mornings, and he works it until five or six in the evening. The shop is not a sideline. The shop is the instrument that keeps the boat in the water: it carries the slip fee and the fuel and the license and the gap between what the Bay pays now and what a family costs now. Biggie's working day produced a living and a six-pack of Coca-Cola's worth of leisure inside the work itself. This man's working day is two working days, and the second one exists because the first one stopped being enough through no decision of his.

Nobody ordered him to do this. That is what makes it so easy not to see. The economy that eats the crab he catches has simply accepted, year after year, that the price of a Chesapeake crab does not need to cover the cost of the man who catches it, because the man will cover the difference himself, out of his own body, in hours the books do not record. He is the cushion between the price and the truth. Multiply him up and down this Bay, every waterman running a shop or a charter or a trade on the side of a trade that once stood on its own, and you are looking at the subsidy that keeps Chesapeake seafood on the menu: paid in person, recorded nowhere, renewed every morning at two or three.

And here is the paradox that should make the whole arrangement impossible to look away from. The oysters are coming back. Maryland's restoration program has rebuilt reefs across five tributary sanctuaries, hundreds of acres, tens of millions of oysters added a year, and the state's oyster population has roughly tripled since 2005. "Large-scale oyster restoration is working," Colden has said, and elsewhere, "Our effort to bring oysters back from the brink in Chesapeake Bay is working," with the caveat that "continued recovery is not guaranteed." Mitch Tarnowski, who leads the Department of Natural Resources oyster surveys, compressed the situation into twelve words for *Maryland Matters*: "There are plenty of oysters out there. They're just not being harvested."

In the same year the program was being celebrated as one of the largest ecosystem restorations on the continent, the State of Maryland filed for a federal disaster declaration for its oyster fishery. The harvest had collapsed in volume and in value; the watermen who depend on the wild bars were in financial crisis; the market was being hollowed out by Gulf Coast farm-raised competition and extreme-weather seasons that wrecked the harvest windows. "Despite a growing oyster population," Colden said in support of the disaster request, "Maryland's watermen are feeling the pinch of extreme weather and market competition." Jason Ruth, who owns Harris Seafood Co., an oyster processor in Grasonville, Maryland, gave the longer arc: "While the demand for boutique aquaculture oysters has grown over the last decade, the shucked oyster sector, the larger part of the industry, has seen a 60-year nationwide decline."

The species recovering and the fishery in federal disaster, in the same water, in the same year. The two facts coexist because the recovery is happening inside sanctuaries the watermen may not work, while the wild harvest outside them is undercut by competitors who never bore this Bay's costs. There is a reading of this that calls it market efficiency: aquaculture outcompeted wild harvest, consumers got cheaper oysters, the workforce should retrain. The reading fails on its own terms, because the recovery and the squeeze are not two markets doing two things. They are one process viewed from two sides of a ledger that was never built to carry what the watermen absorbed. The ecosystem is being asked to come back, the watermen are being asked to pay for its coming back, and the diner eating a recovered oyster pays for the oyster but not for the man, because the price was never constructed to carry him. "One of the most important ways people connect with the Chesapeake Bay is through eating seafood and fishing," Colden has said. Without watermen, "there's certainly a lot fewer eyes on the water and a lot fewer people caring passionately about the Bay." The longest-running watch the Bay has ever had is being priced off the water by the same accounting that is reviving the species it watched over.

This magazine has carried an argument of this shape before. In 2014, Ta-Nehisi Coates built "The Case for Reparations" the way an oyster builds a reef, accretion on accretion, case set on case, until the structure was too large to be dismissed as anecdote. What Coates established was that an unpaid ledger existed and that the refusal to examine it was itself the injury's continuation; he asked, in the end, not for a number but for the accounting. The ledger he documented is not one I would presume to total, and I will not try. But the Chesapeake offers something his subject could not: one line of this ledger is physical, documented, and priceable at the cost of construction. On that one line, the accounting can actually be run. So run it.

Price one thing only. Not the lost fishery, not the menhaden, not the watermen's foregone living, not the century of dispersed wealth; every one of those is real, and every one invites an argument that never ends. Price the thing that is concrete in both senses: the reef bottom itself, the hard foundation that was stripped out of this water and never put back. The acreage exists for the James River, the river system Hampton's bars belong to, and I use the James as the named stand-in for Hampton's own bars because the careful survey history exists for the James and not for Hampton Bar alone; better to name the proxy than to hide it. David Schulte of the Army Corps of Engineers, working in *Frontiers in Marine Science* in 2017 from bottom surveys taken in 1910 and again in 1981, put the loss at roughly 2,700 acres of high-quality oyster rock, close to 40 percent of what the river had held. Rebuilding an acre of reef on lower-Bay bottom, where young oysters still set on their own, has cost about $7,300 an acre in NOAA's accounting of the completed Great Wicomico work, $907,000 for 124 acres, and the Corps' forward estimate for the same river runs to about $13,500 an acre. Multiply, and the floor comes out at roughly $20 million to $37 million to rebuild the reef bottom foreclosed in this one river system. Price the same acreage instead at the Bay-wide average cost of completed restoration, about $77,000 an acre in NOAA's per-tributary accounting, and the figure runs to about $211 million; that is the robust center of the estimate, not the floor. I am standing on the floor. The smallest documented loss, priced at the cheapest documented rebuild, errs low on purpose. A floor that errs low cannot be knocked over.

Set the floor beside what the reef earned while it stood. There is no honest way to recover an 1890 price, so use today's: 200,000 bushels, the Darling peak year, at the roughly $35 a bushel Maryland's docks paid in the most recent seasons, comes to about $7 million. That was the labor's share, the part that stayed at the dock, and one year is all I will price, because no operation runs its peak for a century and the fastest way to lose this argument is to multiply as though one did. The same harvest, at the prices the far end of the supply chain pays, runs on the order of $120 million. Today's prices laid over yesterday's volume, an illustration of proportion and nothing more. But the proportion is the mechanism. Of the value the reef produced in a year, the dock kept a sliver, other regions' tables got the rest, and the cost of keeping the reef alive was kept by no one at all.

Now the fact that makes the case hard to argue with: the reef is being rebuilt right now, and the public is paying for all of it. In Maryland alone, by the state's own accounting as of August 2025, more than $92 million in combined state and federal money has gone into rebuilding about 1,300 acres of sanctuary reef across the five tributaries. Across the whole Bay, NOAA's itemized tally of the completed ten-tributary program comes to roughly $115 million of public money, $93.52 million on Maryland's side and $21.77 million on Virginia's. And in July 2020, on the Lafayette River shoreline of the Navy's own Hampton Roads installation, Navy personnel and federal and Virginia agency staff stacked concrete structures called oyster castles into the water to give young oysters something hard to set on, the Chesapeake Bay Program supplying the spat. No public dollar figure exists for that installation, and I am not going to invent one. Who paid is on the record: the Navy, the agencies, the taxpayers. Who has not paid is also on the record. The industry that pulled one of the world's great oyster operations' worth of value off these bottoms, and its successors and beneficiaries, have contributed to the rebuilding of the bottoms nothing at all. The industry's line in this column is zero. A century ago we crushed the reef's own material into roads and driveways because shell was cheaper than gravel; a generation later the public is casting concrete into the water to do what the shell would have done for free. My family's driveway sits at one end of that sentence. Everyone's taxes sit at the other.

What is missing here has a name, or deserves one. Call it a Restitution Bond: an instrument that looks backward at a commons that was drawn down, prices the physical cost of making it whole, and names who should hold the bill, the parties who drew the commons down and their successors, rather than the public the bill was dropped on. It would not cut a check to any waterman; the moment restitution becomes checks to individuals, the fight over which individuals and how much swallows the whole. It would pay into the reef. It would fund the foundation the next generations of oysters, and oystermen, would stand on. The machinery of such an instrument, who issues it, who holds it, how successorship is traced, is a longer argument than a magazine essay can carry, and I am building it elsewhere. The shape is what belongs here, because the shape is simple. The bill already exists. The public is already paying it. The only question an honest accounting leaves open is whether the bill ever moves to the column where it belonged. We know a great deal, as a civilization, about how to govern a commons so that it is not drawn down; that knowledge fills an honorable shelf. For the commons that has already been drawn down, the shelf is nearly empty. The forward problem has a literature. The backward problem has a bill.

A day's drive west of this harbor, in the coalfields of southern West Virginia, the same accounting ran on dry land. In 1960, by the federal Energy Information Administration's series, bituminous coal at the mine mouth sold for just under five dollars a ton. The value in that coal compounded far from the hollows that produced it, in the steel and the rails and the cities they built. The costs stayed home: the dust that settled in the miners' lungs, the towns that emptied as the companies wound down across the 1980s, the bill that landed, when it finally came due, on a federal trust fund, which is to say on everyone. McDowell County sits at the bottom of a watershed of value that flowed somewhere else. So does the Chesapeake waterman. The two communities have never thought of themselves in the same sentence, and almost everything in American public life is arranged to keep it that way.

The miner and the waterman live in different states and watch different news, and they vote, when they vote, for different people. Each has been handed a story about who his enemy is, and in each man's story the enemy is, roughly, the other man's side. The waterman has been told that the people who want his water clean are the people who want his harvest closed. The miner has been told that the people who want his air clean are the people who closed his mine. As long as each believes it, neither looks at what the two stories have in common: the same missing line in the same books, the value priced and sent away, the cost unpriced and left behind, the public picking up the difference decades later and calling it a disaster declaration, a trust fund, a restoration program.

None of this requires a reader to change parties, and none of it has a villain worth the name. The dredgers are dead. The packers are dead. The men who set the menhaden cap from landings instead of biomass were using the only numbers anyone had funded. The accounting that failed the watermen failed the miners under administrations of both parties, under regulators sincere and captured alike, in markets up and down. A villain would be a comfort; a villain could be sued. What exists instead is a missing instrument, and the fight over which side's villain to blame has functioned, for two generations, as the best cover the missing instrument ever had. Whoever creates a cost should bear it. That is not a left proposition or a right one. It is the oldest rule of honest bookkeeping there is, and the watermen, who have been keeping the Bay's books in their bodies for three hundred years, are the ones who can tell you exactly what it costs when nobody applies it.

The light is up over the Fort now. The channel marker has gone pale against the brightening water, and the boats that left at four are specks out toward the mouth. The man two slips over is somewhere among them. He will come back in the afternoon with whatever the day decides, tie up, hose down, walk to his truck, and be back at the slip tomorrow at the same hour, carrying the longest unbroken record anyone holds of what this water was and what it has become, a record no institution has figured out how to act on and no market has figured out how to pay for. The morning around him looks ordinary, and that is the hardest part to set down: how ordinary it all looks, a working man going to work on gray water. It is the day he has, on the Bay he has, under the accounting his country has so far agreed to do.

It is not yet the day he could have had.
