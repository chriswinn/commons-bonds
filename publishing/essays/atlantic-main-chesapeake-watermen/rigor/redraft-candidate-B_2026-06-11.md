# What the Bay Knows

## I.

The harbor at Old Point Comfort wakes a long time before the city does. I have learned the order of the morning the way you learn a house in the dark, by the sounds it makes when nobody is performing for you. The wind off the Roads dies first. It comes down out of the southwest all night and then, a quarter hour before any gray reaches the water, it quits, and the bay goes flat and the gulls stop their argument because at that hour the argument is not yet useful. Then a diesel coughs and settles somewhere along the breakwater, and a second engine answers it from the far slips, and the day is decided before there is light enough to read by.

It is not the youngest man who leaves first. The man two slips over from where I write has been doing this since before any of the others were born. He coils his lines a particular way that none of the younger boats bother with. He does not look at his watch. The water tells him, and he believes the water, which is the whole of his profession compressed into one habit.

I grew up on this bay, though I did not grow up on the water the way a waterman's son does. My father is from Fox Hill, a fishing neighborhood on the Virginia side of the Roads, where for most of the last century almost every house had at least one person who worked the water. His own father was the exception on his street. My grandfather went up the road to NASA Langley and built scale models in the wind tunnels, components of aircraft and spacecraft that did not yet exist, so that the tunnel would tell the truth when reality was run against the design. He held patents. He built the apparatus of a different America twelve miles from the docks where his neighbors built nothing and brought home everything. The men on either side of him were on the water. The men who taught my father to handle a small boat, to read the channel, to know what the marsh smelled like in late August when the tide was wrong, were the sons of watermen. My family is one generation removed from the working life. We are zero generations removed from the community. That is the only credential this essay has, and I am going to spend it carefully, because it is the only thing here that cannot be checked against a published source.

From a postcard the bay is one soft body of water, the kind you would put a child on. It is in fact a difficult thing, the largest estuary in North America, draining some sixty-four thousand square miles across six states and the District of Columbia into a single brackish sheet two hundred miles long. The watermen know what the postcard does not. The chop on the Chesapeake runs at a wavelength of three or four seconds, sometimes less, because the bay is shallow and finite and the energy in its waves stacks up steep and close. The swell off the open Atlantic runs closer to twenty seconds between crests, a long rocking that can put a man to sleep. A boat built for the one will not last a season in the other. The body learns these intervals before the mind has a word for them. You can watch a man step off the dock and tell in the first ten yards of his run whether he was raised on this water or came to it later.

That knowledge in the body is the watermen's instrument, the way they read the resource. They have been on the same bars for forty years; they know what is there and what was there and what is not coming back, and they know it the way you know your own hands. The scientists who study what they study respect them. The regulators who write the rules over their lives understand what they lose by not having that knowledge, and cannot, by the architecture of their offices, take it into a decision. The watermen carry the data. The regulatory system carries the authority. For forty years the two have been failing to find each other, and the failure is the subject of this essay.

The first boats are at the channel now, heading for the bars their fathers worked. Most will come back with less than their fathers brought home. Some will come back with nothing. The math of why is not a mystery to them, and it is not, at bottom, a story about the water. It is a story about an accounting that does not balance, and about a community that has been absorbing the difference for longer than anyone has bothered to count.

## II.

The bay used to feed a country, and I do not mean that the way people mean it when they are being sentimental about a place. I mean it as a record.

In 1884 a Hampton oyster operation took the name J.S. Darling and Son, when Frank Darling joined his father's existing business. At its peak the firm kept about a hundred and sixty shuckers at the tables and put two hundred thousand bushels of Chesapeake oysters into shipping in a single year, the meat going out in iced tubs to dining rooms in Baltimore and New York and farther. That figure, two hundred thousand bushels, is the operation in a good year, not a number you multiply across the decades; nobody packs his best year every year for a century, and the moment you try, a careful reader takes the claim apart. The shells stayed. They came off the conveyors and went onto piles behind the packing houses that rose more than four stories high, mountains a town could see and smell from the waterfront. The Darlings also ran a menhaden reduction factory at Factory Point in Fox Hill, rendering thousands of tons a year into fish meal for fertilizer and oil for industry. The whole length of the Hampton waterfront was a working machine. It fed northern cities, fertilized southern fields, and put money in the pockets of fishing families up and down the lower bay. Hampton was known along the coast as Crabtown. In the 1870s a Hamptonian named James McMenamin patented a process for sealing cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in airtight tins, and for a window of years a family in lower Manhattan could eat Hampton crab on a Sunday because the local boatmen could not catch enough of their own. All of this is in the Library of Virginia's record of the Darling family and on the marker that stands where the packing houses stood.

Two pieces of arithmetic frame everything that follows. In the late 1880s the Chesapeake oyster fishery produced something on the order of fifteen million bushels in a year. By the 1990s it was producing under two hundred thousand. Two orders of magnitude in a century. Roughly a ninety-nine percent decline. That is not a soft figure. That is the figure, and the historical record carries it for the oyster more clearly than for any other species the bay has lost.

William W. Warner wrote the book that any account of the working bay has had to write in the wake of. *Beautiful Swimmers* came out in 1976 and took the Pulitzer the next year. Warner walked the docks, rode the boats, kept his notebook out, and produced the portrait of the working Chesapeake that watermen from Crisfield to Reedville will still tell you got the work right, which is the only test that matters to them. Anyone writing about this bay since has had to write knowing Warner did it first. What I want to add is what came after the decline Warner watched begin: not the loss itself, which he rendered, but the four decades the architecture around the watermen has had to respond to it, and what that architecture has and has not proven capable of. Warner's bay can be read as an elegy, a beautiful thing passing the way beautiful things pass. I want to refuse the elegy, gently, because the loss was not weather and was not fate. It was an accounting failure, and an accounting failure leaves a bill, and a bill can be added up and handed to whoever should have been holding it. That is the part of the story that comes after the part Warner told.

You can see what the architecture was capable of in the shoreline that is now condominiums. What it was not capable of is the rest of this essay.

## III.

There was a man called Biggie. He had the build of an oak, not tall, in the way some big men are not tall, but solid through the shoulders and the back, the build of a man who has done one specific kind of work for most of his life. He worked oysters, and he had worked them since he was old enough to be useful in a boat with whoever first brought him into one. He died more than thirty years ago, before Virginia closed its commercial shad fishery in 1994, which is a date the men still on the water sometimes use to mark when things were still working. What I know of him I know from my father, who watched him as a boy. The witness here is one generation removed, and I am not going to pretend otherwise; I never knew him, and the intimacy in this passage is my father's, not mine.

He carried a six-pack of Coca-Cola onto the boat in a wire carrier, and that was his lunch. No cooler, no ice, no sandwich. The first time my father asked him about it, my father was a small boy and honestly puzzled, and Biggie told him his lunch was waiting for him in the water, as fresh as a lunch could be. Once the sun was high and the work had found its rhythm, he would start eating oysters as he worked, off a spot on the boat near his body where he set the one he was about to open, so that he never had to reach or lift his arm. The motion he shucked with was the motion of a man who had reduced the whole task to its smallest possible movement across a working life of doing it, a quarter turn of the wrist, the shell parting, the meat coming up clean. My father tried more than once to count how many Biggie put away in a day and never managed it, not because there were too many to count, though there may have been, but because the eating was so woven into the work that there was no moment when an oyster started or stopped being eaten. It was simply something he did alongside everything else he did, the way breathing is. My father has told that story so many times that I finish it for him now.

He worked particular bars, the bars his father had worked and his grandfather before that. The boat was suited to the work and old, repaired the way old boats are repaired, by a man who does not throw a thing away because something he might one day need is still in it. There is no documentary record of Biggie, no quote in a newspaper, no line in a survey. He is a private man my father knew, dead a long time, and I am naming him with the courtesy due a man whose family may still read this. I bring him here for one reason. The book this essay comes out of argues a structural case, and a structural case dies on the page unless it runs through people the reader can follow. Matthew Desmond ran the case for housing through the people he followed in *Evicted*; Sarah Smarsh ran the case for the rural working poor through her own family in *Heartland*. The discipline is the same, and the method does not care whether the commons is an apartment or an oyster bar. I am borrowing it here. Biggie is one life. The man who appears later in this essay is another, one generation downstream from Biggie on the same arc, and the distance between the two of them is the argument.

Biggie's motion died with him. Not the work, which others took up, but that particular economy of the wrist over that particular shellfish on the rail of that particular boat, on a bar where the salinity holds at a certain band and the October water falls into a certain range and the oysters come up sized a certain way. When he died, that combination went out of the world. The men who learned from him learned other versions. The work continued. The motion did not, and it is not being taught to any younger man at Fox Hill now, because no younger man at Fox Hill is going out to bars that would let him learn it.

## IV.

Take the oyster first, because the arithmetic is the cleanest. Fifteen million bushels in the late 1880s to under two hundred thousand by the 1990s is the headline, and the cause structure has three waves. The first was overharvesting. The dredges scraped the reefs flat, season after season, faster than the reefs could rebuild, and the reef is not incidental to the resource; it is the resource. An oyster needs something hard to set on, and what an oyster sets on is the cathedral of dead shell the oysters before it built. Grind that down and you have not only taken this year's oysters. You have taken the capacity of the bottom to make any oysters at all. The second wave was disease, MSX in the late 1950s and Dermo in the years that followed, parasites the weakened, low-density populations had no resilience against. The third wave is the one running now, and it is what makes the oyster's problem inseparable from the bay's agricultural problem: sediment from upstream runoff smothering the spat that does survive, nutrient loading feeding the algal blooms that pull the dissolved oxygen out of the deep channel each summer until the bottom of the central bay becomes uninhabitable to most of what lives in it.

This is worth being precise about, because there is a tidy story that says a fishery is just a renewable resource and a renewable resource cannot really be used up, only mismanaged for a while. The oyster refuses the tidy story. A coal seam is finite from the day it forms; you can take it fast or slow but you cannot take more than was laid down. An oyster reef is the other kind of thing, an instrument that makes more of itself every year as long as you do not damage the instrument. Take some, leave the rest to spawn, come back next year, and your son can do it, and his son after him. What happened in the Chesapeake is what happens when the rate of taking runs ahead of the rate of making, and what was taken, underneath the oysters, was the structure that produces the renewal. A renewable resource was turned non-renewable, one ground-down reef at a time, and from that point on, leaving the system alone is no longer the answer. You put something back, or you accept that what was lost stays lost.

Take the rockfish next, which is the bay's name for striped bass. Through the 1970s the rockfish collapsed badly enough that Maryland imposed a five-year moratorium in 1985, Virginia coordinated, the federal government coordinated coastwide, and everyone shut down: commercial seiners, the charter fleet, the recreational anglers. The species was given a generation off. It was a hard ask of the people whose income came off the fishery, and some of them never came back to the work. But the moratorium did the narrow thing it was built to do. The spawning population recovered, and by 1995 the fishery was declared restored, and the watermen who had given up five years saw a fishery brought back from a place very few fisheries get brought back from. That recovery is part of why what is happening now is hard for the men who remember it, because what is happening now to the rockfish is what was happening before the moratorium. The 2024 Maryland young-of-year index, the count of juvenile rockfish per sample at the state's summer seining stations, was 2.0 fish against a long-term average of 11.0. It was the sixth consecutive below-average year. Six in a row stops being weather and becomes structure.

Allison Colden, the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's Maryland executive director, put it to the public on the release of that index the way the watermen would recognize. "Maryland's striped bass surveys have made it clear; we're facing a looming disaster. Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future." She said, in the same window, that "there are very few levers left to pull." She said that many people in Maryland "rely on striped bass for their food, their businesses, and their futures. Without action, that will all be at risk." Of the warming that compounds the collapse, she said that rising water temperatures and the low-oxygen periods of summer are "pummeling" the species "during the months it can least afford to be pummeled."

Chris Moore, who runs the same Foundation's Virginia office, said the harder thing in response to the 2024 stock assessment, and it is the sentence I keep coming back to. "Despite recent management actions, striped bass continue to struggle. It is obvious that conditions have changed and striped bass will not have the same recovery they had in the 1980s. Warmer water from climate change, habitat loss, and invasive species are all making it very hard for striped bass to bounce back." The instrument that worked in 1985 may not work again, because the bay it worked on is not the bay there is now. Bill Goldsborough, who ran the Foundation's fisheries portfolio through the moratorium years and into the recovery, gave *Smithsonian Magazine* what is probably the cleanest statement on record of the gap between the scientific clock and the working one. "Our objective is to try to restore the health of bay and aquatic life so that it can support viable fisheries. We see that task taking place over a longer term. But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment."

Take the shad. The American shad and the hickory shad are anadromous, living most of their lives in the open Atlantic and running each spring up the bay's tributary rivers to spawn in fresh water. The bay is the spawning ground for the shad of an entire region, and when its rivers fail the shad, the shad fail in the ocean too. In January of 1994 Virginia closed its commercial shad fishery in the main stem. The men whose fathers had timed the run to the calendar could tell you the spring the nets in late March began coming up empty. The runs did not come back. The science has not converged on one explanation; the decline is multifactorial, the dams and the turbines and the bycatch and the water in the spawning reaches. What the science has converged on is the absence of a recovery. A waterman who built half his calendar around the spring run lost half his calendar that January, and has not gotten it back, and the same instrument that worked once for the rockfish was tried for the shad and arrived too late.

Take the crab, the species the bay still produces in commercial quantity and the one most working families have leaned on. The blue crab does not build a reef; its life cycle runs a year and a half or two, which means in principle it can rebound from a bad year quickly if the next year is good, and for a long time that made it the bay's most reliable seasonal income. The 2025 winter dredge survey, run jointly by the Maryland Department of Natural Resources and the Virginia Institute of Marine Science, put the bay-wide population at about 238 million crabs, the second-lowest level since the survey began in 1990, the sixth consecutive year below average. "The red flags are flying for blue crabs," Colden said. And the structural sentence, the one that carries more than it looks like it carries: "with more than five years of below average crab numbers, it is clear that changing conditions in the Bay are undermining the current management of this important species." The rules are not wrong so much as calibrated to a bay that no longer exists. The committees still meet on the previous bay's schedule. The crabs are not on that schedule, and neither is the water.

J.C. Hudgins runs the Virginia Watermen's Association and works the Piankatank in the middle bay, and he has done the math out loud. "You're lucky if you can put $150 in your pocket per day. And it ain't no easy job. You're freezing your butt off. It's cold." On why the buyers cannot bear the prices the watermen need: "People aren't going to spend $20 for a pint of oysters. It's pretty expensive eating. I think people are feeding their families in other ways." A hundred and fifty dollars for a day that starts in the dark and ends frozen, against a pint of oysters the customer has decided is a luxury. That is the arithmetic at the dock, and it is the arithmetic the architecture has not been able to change for thirty years.

Across all four species the pattern is the same and it is not principally a pattern about fish. It is that the institutional system built to manage the bay has not been able, on its own machinery, to act on its own science. The scientific committees have run ahead of the management boards in case after case. The recommendations have come in; the actions have lagged; the lag has compounded. The watermen, who carry the consequence in their incomes and their bodies, have been saying the same thing for thirty years in their own vocabulary that the scientists have been saying in another, and the state has held the hearings and recorded the testimony, and the acting has not kept pace with the listening. The men know what the dust is doing to them long before the X-rays come back. The institution acknowledges it last.

## V.

The case that shows the shape of the architecture most clearly is the menhaden case, because in it you can see the machinery operating with the cover pulled off.

Menhaden are a small, oily, silver forage fish, bunker on the New Jersey side, pogy in New England, just menhaden on the bay. They run in schools dense enough to flatten the surface of the water like a passing wind, and by weight they are one of the most important fish in the Atlantic. You do not eat one, because it is not pleasant to eat. What it is, instead, is the thing almost everything else eats, the base of the bay's food web, the fish the rockfish and the bluefish and the ospreys and the loons and the dolphins all come for. It is also the species harvested in the largest single fishery on the East Coast, by an industrial reduction operation out of Reedville, Virginia, at the mouth of the Great Wicomico River in Northumberland County, ground down into fish meal and fish oil and omega-3 supplements and pet food and aquaculture feed. The catch leaves the bay in tank trucks and railcars and on ships. People do not eat it. An industrial process converts it into inputs for industries somewhere else. The end customer is a man taking an omega-3 capsule in Indianapolis, a salmon farm in Chile, a forty-pound bag of dog food on a shelf in a Phoenix grocery. The plant employs several hundred people in shifts that follow the runs.

The Bay-specific cap on the reduction fishery, the amount the industry may pull out of the Chesapeake in a year, is 51,000 metric tons. It was set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission in its Amendment 3, adopted in 2017 and put into effect in 2018, a cut of roughly forty percent from the cap that preceded it, on a motion made by Colden herself when she was the Foundation's senior fisheries scientist. The conservation side regards the figure as a floor. The industry side regards it as a hard-fought concession. The number was not derived from any biological estimate of the menhaden population in the bay. It was derived from historical landings, because no one has the data to derive it from anything else.

"We don't know the abundance of menhaden in the bay at any given time relative to the coast," Genny Nesslage told the public-radio station WHRO. Nesslage is an associate research professor at the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science and one of a small handful of menhaden scientists in the country, and that is not a sentence a scientist says casually. Rob Latour, a menhaden researcher at the Virginia Institute of Marine Science, said it more bluntly to the same outlet: "We have no data to really do this in a meaningful way, from a biological perspective." In 2023 the Virginia Institute of Marine Science drafted a proposal for a three-million-dollar Bay-specific menhaden study that would have produced the missing biomass and recruitment data. The Virginia General Assembly declined to fund it. Three million dollars against a fishery worth tens of millions a year, several hundred jobs in a politically organized rural county, and the foodweb foundation of a multi-billion-dollar recreational fishery. The appropriation was not made.

What that absence does to the rest of the bay is what you would expect of a hole at the base of a food web. Osprey nests in several tributaries have been failing to fledge their young, and the suspicion among the people who watch ospreys is that the parent birds are not bringing in enough small oily fish to feed a growing brood; nobody is calling that settled, but it is being watched. The rockfish, heat-stressed and oxygen-starved in summer and trying to put on weight, do not have enough to eat in the months they can least afford to come up short. The bay-wide signal is sometimes called a hollowing: the bigger species look fine until you ask whether they are reproducing and whether their young are surviving, and then the picture changes under you. In the same window in which Maryland's rockfish index was running at two against a baseline of eleven, the reduction fishery was operating at a cap built from extraction history rather than from any living count.

On the last day of 2025 a Virginia resident named Tanya O'Connor filed a 349-page petition with the Virginia Marine Resources Commission, asking the agency to declare a temporary moratorium on the bay's reduction fishery until Bay-specific science was produced. She grounded it in Virginia's public-trust doctrine, working through three cases the Commonwealth's own courts decided: *Commonwealth v. City of Newport News*, 158 Va. 521 (1932); *Taylor v. Commonwealth*, 102 Va. 759 (1904); *Bradford v. Nature Conservancy*, 224 Va. 181 (1982). Her central finding, in her own words, was that the regulatory body "did not have — and still does not have — a Bay-wide estimate of menhaden biomass, age structure, spatial distribution, recruitment trends, or localized depletion risk." The Commission set the hearing for April 21, 2026. It received 1,266 public comments in a state where most fisheries hearings draw double-digit comment counts and most citizens have never heard of the agency. The vote, when it came, was six to two against. The moratorium did not move forward. The reduction fishery continued at its cap. In 2025 the Atlantic States commission began the process of considering a further cut to the bay cap, up to half, which is the system circling the question one more time around the same missing data.

"Just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should," Colden said of how the science is supposed to land in the management architecture, "we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that that's warranted." The menhaden case is what an architecture looks like when the levers it has left are pulled in the wrong direction, because the data the levers should be pulled against does not exist, and the appropriation that would have produced the data was not made, and the petition that would have forced the question was voted down six to two. It is the same shape coal had. A resource other people in the system depend on is taken at scale, processed into intermediate inputs sold to distant customers, and the revenue and the political organization concentrate at the operator while the costs disperse across a thousand miles of food web and forty years of decline. The shape does not care whether it is wearing coal or fish.

## VI.

A working community is not a count of jobs. It is a fabric, and a fabric thins one thread at a time, and the loss is not legible until the cloth no longer holds what it used to hold.

Tangier Island sits in the middle of the lower bay, two square miles of low marsh and sand, continuously inhabited since the 1770s. The accent the people speak there is, by some accounts, closer to a colonial-era English than to anything else still spoken in North America, eight generations of the same families doing the same work, crabs in the warm months and oysters in the cold ones, for longer than the country has been a country. The island is going under. How fast is a question worth getting right, because the figure that has circulated, fifteen feet of shoreline a year, is the maximum loss along the exposed western shore and not the rate for the island as a whole; the peer-reviewed study by Schulte and his colleagues at the Army Corps, published in *Scientific Reports* in 2015, put the long-term average closer to 1.7 feet a year and found that Tangier has lost about two-thirds of its landmass since 1850, with roughly a square mile of land left. The exposed shore gives way at fifteen feet; the island as a whole erodes more slowly; either way the projection stands, that most of Tangier will be uninhabitable within the lifetime of the children now in its school. The mayor and the men on the docks have asked, repeatedly, for the engineering that would buy them a few more decades. The asks have mostly not been answered at the scale that would matter.

James "Ooker" Eskridge has been the mayor of Tangier for two decades and a working waterman for more than fifty years, and he put it to the Thomson Reuters Foundation in plain words. "We're talking about saving a culture, the people, (a) way of life — the whole package." Cook Cannon worked Tangier waters before he took a seat at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation, and what *Smithsonian Magazine* carried from him, when the writer asked about the next decade, was this: "I could cry to think about what's going to happen to Tangier." Earl Swift walked an entire year on the island for *Chesapeake Requiem* in 2018, close enough that a working waterman can read it and recognize his own life, which is again the only test that counts. Swift's island is a place caught between a rising bay it cannot stop and a political system that has not chosen to help; that reading is right, and I want to extend it by one line. The water is doing what water does. The decision not to hold the bill for it is a decision, and the bill is the kind of thing that can be costed and assigned, which is the register Swift did not need to foreground and this essay does.

Smith Island sits twelve miles north of Tangier, across the line into Maryland, and is in roughly the same condition. The picking houses where the soft-shell crab is hand-prepared have been thinning for decades; what remains is mostly older women working long tables with the picking knives their mothers used. There is a saying on Smith that the mud between your toes never quite leaves once you have walked the back creeks as a child, which is a thing the women who say it can say because they stayed and the children who hear it cannot, because the children left. Tom Horton recorded Smith in *An Island Out of Time* in 1996, at the period when the leaving had begun to accelerate, and the people there know him as a writer who got it right. The record he kept is the record of the going; what this essay adds, here as everywhere, is the question of who holds the bill for it.

Up the bay on Tilghman Island the same work continues, and *Maryland Matters* has kept the names. The paper ran a photograph in 2023 of Pat Whewell culling oysters aboard Lonnie Gowe's boat in Broad Creek, and three years later carried Chumley Fisher and Chet Schwartz offloading oysters at Tilghman, the men named in the captions because the paper took the time to name them. Hampton, where I have been writing this, is its own slower version of the arc. The marina I look down on is tipping from a working marina, slips full of crab boats and oyster boats and charter operators, toward a recreational one full of leisure boats. It has happened one slip at a time. What thins first is the supply chain, the welders who built the davits and the engine shops that kept the diesels running and the ice plants that kept the holds cold and the buyers who took the catch at the dock and the haulers who put it on trucks to Baltimore and Philadelphia. Lose one and the working operators drive farther and wait longer. Lose two and some of them quit. Lose three and the slips start emptying, and by the time you can see it from the road it has been happening for a generation.

What is being lost in Tangier and Smith and Tilghman and Hampton is something other than a list of jobs. It is the way people who live near working water know one another, the cousin-network and the church-network and the picking-house network through which a community looks after its own, the three-in-the-morning arrival of pickups at the place where a man's nets need rebuilding before the season, because there is no industry of net-rebuilders to hire and the only thing that does the work is the community itself. When the picking houses close, the soft-shell crab keeps being eaten. It gets picked elsewhere, or by machine, or by hands that have not done it for three generations. The product is preserved. The practice is not. The product is what the market priced. The practice is what the market did not.

## VII.

The waterman who follows is not named. He is a man my father has long known, and the anonymity is the discipline of writing about someone whose community would know him on sight. He is a crabber and a fisherman, a large man, not tall, on the bay since he was a boy, from the same Fox Hill world Biggie was from, one generation downstream on the same arc. What is true of him is true, in slightly different shapes, of a great many men still working this water.

He still wakes between two and three in the morning to run his crab pots. He has been waking at that hour, for that purpose, for as long as he has had pots, and he runs them the way he ran them when he was younger, the same boat, the same lines, the same hands, the hands older. The math at the end of the day is different now. The catches have come back thinner, the costs of fuel and gear and the slip have moved the other way, and not all of the species he came up working are still running. None of that is particular to him; it is the condition every man still on this water has had to absorb. What is particular to him is what he has done with the absorption. He comes back to the dock by mid-morning. He hoses down the boat. He goes to a mechanic's shop he owns and works on cars until five or six in the evening, and then he comes home. What he calls that arrangement is between him and the people who know him. What it is, by the clock, is two full working days inside one twenty-four-hour day, six days a week, fifty weeks a year, against a body that is no longer twenty-five.

He did not quit the water. He could not. The water is what he knows. The shop is the thing he built so that he would not have to quit it, on his own time and his own dime, across a couple of decades, while the water side of his work was slowly failing to carry the family by itself. The crab work, on its own, no longer pays for the family. The second job is the math of the gap. The boat payments and the slip fees and the fuel and the gear and the licenses and the freezer space, the trip out and the trip back, the season's books come up short of what a family in this part of the country needs to live, and the architecture around the work has not been pricing the work at its real cost for thirty years. The watermen are not fools. They have watched the math drift, the buyer prices stall and the input prices climb and the catches thin, and what they have done with the drift is what working people in every American extractive industry have done with it. They have absorbed it. The body has been the cushion. The second job has been the cushion. The willingness to stay in the work for less than the work used to give back has been the cushion. The cost did not vanish because the price did not name it. The cost was paid. It was paid in a column the books do not keep, by a man who would not let the family math fail.

The same paradox runs through the oysters, and it is the sharpest illustration the bay has produced of the whole pattern. Maryland's oyster-restoration program is one of the most ambitious ecological efforts of its kind in the country, five tributary-scale sanctuaries, hundreds of acres of rebuilt reef, tens of millions of oysters a year added to the rebuilt structures, close to twenty years of coordinated state and federal work, and by the measure it set for itself it has worked. The Maryland oyster population has roughly tripled since 2005. "Large-scale oyster restoration is working," Colden has said. "Our effort to bring oysters back from the brink in Chesapeake Bay is working." She has been careful to add, in the same breath, that "continued recovery is not guaranteed." Mitch Tarnowski, who leads the Maryland Department of Natural Resources oyster reef surveys, summed it up in twelve words to *Maryland Matters*. "There are plenty of oysters out there. They're just not being harvested." He meant the wild bars outside the sanctuaries, where the watermen are allowed to work and are not, because the boats are tied up.

In the same year the program was being celebrated as one of the largest successful ecosystem-restoration efforts in North America, Maryland filed for a federal disaster declaration for its oyster fishery. The harvest had collapsed in volume and in value. The watermen who depend on the wild harvest were in financial crisis, the market hollowed out by Gulf Coast farm-raised aquaculture and by extreme-weather seasons that disrupted the narrow harvest windows. Jason Ruth, who owns Harris Seafood Co. in Grasonville and processes a substantial share of the Maryland wild harvest, named the longer arc. "While the demand for boutique aquaculture oysters has grown over the last decade, the shucked oyster sector — the larger part of the industry — has seen a 60-year nationwide decline." Colden's word on the disaster declaration holds the paradox in one sentence. "Despite a growing oyster population, Maryland's watermen are feeling the pinch of extreme weather and market competition." The recovery was real. The squeeze was real. They were not two processes. They were one process seen from the two sides of a ledger, the population coming back into a market the watermen were already losing and into a working fabric that had thinned while the restoration succeeded, and the recovery did not come back to them. "One of the most important ways people connect with the Chesapeake Bay is through eating seafood and fishing," Colden has said. "Without watermen, there's certainly a lot fewer eyes on the water and a lot fewer people caring passionately about the Bay." The people whose attention has held the bay accountable for four centuries are being squeezed out of the relationship by the same accounting that is, at the species level, beginning to recover what was lost.

So let me do for the oyster what this book refused to leave undone for the miner. Let me add up the column.

The instrument that does the adding is one I am going to name here and build properly in a later chapter. I will call it the Restitution Bond, and the idea is simple even where the machinery is not. It looks backward at a commons that was drawn down, prices the physical cost of making it whole, and names who should hold that bill. I am only going to run it once, here, on this reef, and take it to a floor I can defend against anyone who wants to fight me about it. The first discipline of the floor is what it leaves out. I am not going to price the lost fishery, or the menhaden, or the livelihoods of the watermen, or the value of all the oysters the bars would have grown if they had been left whole. Every one of those is a real cost, and every one invites an argument I do not need to have. I am going to price one thing, the thing that is physical and documented and beyond dispute: the reef bottom itself, the hard foundation that was stripped out of the water and never put back.

I am not going to build the number out of Darling's shell mountains, tempting as they are, because the mountains are the wrong measuring stick. Raw shell is cheap, and the dramatic figure you get by multiplying a peak year across a century is exactly the figure a careful critic takes apart in an afternoon. The four-story piles are the image of the severance. They are not the price of it. The price is built from acres. Scientists who study the James River, the river system Hampton's bars belong to, have measured how much high-quality oyster reef bottom was lost there across the twentieth century: Schulte, in *Frontiers in Marine Science* in 2017, reading the historical surveys of Moore in 1910 against Haven and his colleagues in 1981, found that the James lost about 2,738 acres of it between 1910 and 1981, a 38.9 percent areal loss. I use the James figure, and I tell you plainly that I am using it as the stand-in for the Hampton bars, because the careful historical acreage exists for the James and not for Hampton Bar alone, and I would rather name my proxy than hide it. Multiply those 2,738 acres of foreclosed reef by what it costs to rebuild an acre of reef on lower-bay bottom where oysters still set on their own, which the restoration record puts between about $7,300 and $13,500 an acre. The Great Wicomico project came in at about $7,300 an acre realized, by NOAA's accounting; the forward engineering estimate in the tributary plan runs to $13,500. That gives a floor of roughly $20 million to $37 million.

That is the conservative number, deliberately the smallest defensible figure, built from the smallest documented loss and the cheapest documented rebuild, because a floor that errs low is a floor nobody can knock over. Price the same acreage instead at the average cost of completed reef restoration across the whole bay, about $77,000 an acre, and the figure comes to roughly $211 million. That is the robust center of the estimate. I am not leading with it. I am leading with the floor, $20 million to $37 million to physically rebuild the reef bottom foreclosed in this one river system, and I am leaving the higher figure standing behind it so that you know which direction the truth runs.

Now set the floor next to what the reef was worth while it stood, because the gap between the two is the whole argument. The watermen who brought the oysters up were paid by the bushel at the dock. Price a Darling peak year at today's dockside rate, around $35 a bushel by Maryland's official figures, and two hundred thousand bushels comes to about $7 million, and I will not multiply that across the decades for the same reason I would not multiply the shells. Seven million dollars in a peak year, paid to the men who did the bringing up. But the dockside fee was never what the oyster was worth. By the time those oysters reached the tables they were sold to, at a few dollars an oyster on the plate, the same harvest was worth, in current terms, something on the order of $120 million a year, realized in the dining rooms of Baltimore and New York and beyond. I give that last figure as an illustration of where the value landed, not as a historical ledger, because no defensible Darling-era plate price exists and I am not going to invent one. The shape, though, is not in doubt. The watermen got the dockside fee. The cities got the oyster and the restaurants and the jobs and the tax base, the entire downstream economy of the bay's own abundance, built everywhere except the bay.

Hold the numbers together. The value the reef produced dispersed up and out to other regions' tables. The labor fee stayed at the dock. The cost, the $20 million to $37 million it takes to rebuild the foreclosed reef, stayed home and went unpaid and is coming due now. Value goes up and out. Cost stays and settles down.

And here is the fact that makes the case hard to argue with: the reef is already being rebuilt, and the public is paying for the whole of it. In Maryland alone, more than $92 million in combined state and federal money has gone into rebuilding oyster reef across the sanctuaries, about 1,300 acres of it, by the state's own accounting in 2025. Across the whole bay the public total comes to roughly $115 million, by NOAA's itemized table for the completed ten-tributary restoration, Maryland's share at $93.52 million and Virginia's at $21.77 million, paid by NOAA and the Army Corps and the two states' resource agencies, every dollar of it public. Out at the Navy's own installation on the Hampton Roads shoreline, in the summer of 2020, sailors and federal and state agencies stacked concrete blocks called oyster castles into the water to give young oysters something hard to cling to, the spat supplied by the Chesapeake Bay Program, the industry paying nothing. I cannot give you a dollar figure for that one installation, because none was published, and I am not going to invent one to make a point. But I can tell you exactly who paid and who did not. The Navy paid. The taxpayers paid. The industry that took a world's-largest oyster operation's worth of value out of this water and shipped it to other people's cities has paid nothing into the reef's return. Its line in this column is zero.

There is something I keep returning to in all this. My father's family driveway, on Fox Hill, was paved with oyster shell, the way most of the dirt lanes in the lower bay once were, shell being cheaper than gravel for keeping a road out of the mud. We paved with the thing that builds reefs, because the going price of the thing was nothing, and a generation later the Navy is casting concrete to do what the shell would have done for free. That is not a metaphor. It is the same severance, small enough to fit under a family's cars: the material was treated as worthless at the moment of the transaction, and the cost of having treated it as worthless came due later, in concrete, on the public's account.

What the Restitution Bond does is more modest than it sounds, and the modesty is the strength of it. It does not invent a new bill. The bill already exists, and we are already paying it, the $92 million in Maryland, the $115 million across the bay, the Navy crew in the Hampton Roads mud. The Bond does the one thing the present arrangement refuses to do. It names who should have been holding the bill all along, and it moves the cost back onto the value instead of leaving it severed onto the public. It does not pay a single waterman a single dollar, because the moment you start cutting checks to individuals you start an argument about which ones and how much that drowns the whole idea. It pays into the reef. It funds the foundation. It rebuilds the commons that was drawn down, and it asks the parties who drew it down, and their successors, to fund the rebuilding, rather than handing the invoice, one more time, to everyone. Twenty to thirty-seven million dollars. That is the floor. The real figure is higher, and the true social cost, the fishery and the menhaden and the watermen's lost living and the century of dispersed wealth, is higher still, and I have left all of it off the books on purpose, because the floor is the part nobody can take away from me.

## VIII.

A few hundred miles west of the bay's headwaters, the same shape repeats. The water gives way to hills. The boats give way to mines.

McDowell County, West Virginia, sits at the bottom of a watershed of value that for a hundred years flowed out of it. The coal that came out of McDowell sold at the mine mouth, in 1960, for just under five dollars a ton, and went on to power Pittsburgh and Cleveland and Detroit and to make the steel for the bridges over every American river east of the Mississippi. The price the operators got went, very largely, to people who lived somewhere else. The costs the coal generated stayed in McDowell. The black lung that took the miners has run to something like forty-four billion dollars in benefits through 2009, by the accounting of the Government Accountability Office and the Congressional Research Service, and that is a conservative floor, not a current total. The acid drainage ran orange in the creeks. The schools and clinics emptied as US Steel wound down its operations across the 1980s. The county, which once held tens of thousands of people, is down under twenty thousand now. The architecture that managed the relationship had no slot in its books for what McDowell was absorbing, and when the bill came due, it was the public trust fund and the public reclamation money that paid it, because the companies that had collected the value were gone or shielded or simply never on the hook.

The bay is at the bottom of a different watershed, and the same thing has happened in it. The value the bay produces, in seafood and tourism and recreational fishing and the cultural identity of an entire mid-Atlantic region, is captured by people scattered across six states and the country beyond, including the people who write essays like this one in apartments above marinas. The costs of producing that value, the bodies of the watermen and the thinning of the communities and the loss of the motions and the foreclosed reef, are paid in the working communities of the bay, and when the bill for the reef came due it was public money and Navy labor that paid it. The defendant is the same in both cases, and in both cases it is not in the room.

The miner in McDowell and the waterman on the bay would not, put in a room together, recognize each other at first. They live in different states. They watch different news. They vote, when they vote, for different people, and they have been told different stories about who is to blame for what has happened to them. The waterman has been told that the people who would clean up the runoff are the people who would shut down his harvest. The miner has been told that the people who would clean up the air are the people who would close his mine. Each of them has been handed an enemy who is, in fact, the other one. The system that has been doing the extracting is maintained, in part, by keeping the two of them in separate rooms with their separate stories, because the stories make more sense inside the rooms than they would if you told them together in one.

The argument I am making here is structural, not partisan, and I want to be exact about that before the essay closes, because the exactness is the point. The accounting that has failed the watermen is the accounting that has failed the miners. Whoever creates a cost should bear it is an accountability claim, not a progressive one or a conservative one; it reaches the center-right reader and the left reader by the same logic, because it is a claim about a missing instrument and not about bad people. There is no greedy individual at the center of this; there is a price that was never built to carry what the working communities absorbed, and a fight between the communities that has been useful to whoever was collecting the value. Naming the shape does not require choosing a side in that fight. It requires noticing that the fight has been used to keep the shape hidden.

The first boats are out at the mouth now. The light has crossed the channel and reached the top of the Fort. The wind has come up off the Roads, the gulls are arguing again, and the day is beginning. The man two slips over is already on his way back, because his boat is faster and his bars are closer than the younger men's, and he runs his pots before the heat sets in. He will tie up at his usual cleat. He will hose down. He will put what came up in the right cooler in the right order, the way a man does a thing he has done ten thousand times. He will load the truck and drive to the shop, because there is more than one thing on his calendar and there will be every day for as long as he can keep going. Within my father's lifetime, on this same water, a man stepped aboard at this hour with six bottles of Coca-Cola in a wire carrier and nothing else, because his lunch was waiting for him in the water, as fresh as it could be.

It is the day a waterman has, on the bay he has, with the accounting the country has agreed to do.

It is not yet the day he could have had.
