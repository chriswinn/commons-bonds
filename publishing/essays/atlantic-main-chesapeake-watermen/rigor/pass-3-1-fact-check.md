### F-3.1-1 (HIGH) — Atlantic wavelength figure drifts from chapter (factor-of-two)

**Location:** §I opening scene, line 29.

**Current draft text:**
> The watermen who work it know what an outsider does not. They know that the wavelength of the chop on the Chesapeake is about three seconds, while the wavelength of the ocean swell off the Atlantic is closer to nine. A bay boat is built to the three. An ocean trawler will not survive a season in it.

**Canonical source citation:** Brief §7 does NOT cover the wavelength figures (the brief's §4 §I row references "the three-second-wavelength bay-vs-ocean detail" but does not pin the Atlantic value). Pass 3.1 verification-allowed exception per Amendment A → consult Ch 3 line 24: "In the open ocean, a five-foot swell is almost not worth a comment, because the wavelength, the distance and the time between one peak and the next, is long. **Twenty seconds between waves on the Atlantic feels like a slow rocking.** The Bay does not give you twenty seconds. **The Bay's wavelength runs three or four seconds, sometimes less**, because the body of water producing the waves is shallow and finite..."

**Issue:** Two-figure drift from the chapter:

1. **Atlantic wavelength:** essay says **"closer to nine [seconds]"**; chapter says **"twenty seconds."** Factor-of-two-plus drift on a numerical claim.
2. **Bay wavelength:** essay says **"about three seconds"**; chapter says **"three or four seconds, sometimes less."** Range-precision compression (essay drops "or four").

The Atlantic figure is the load-bearing one. The chapter's "twenty seconds" between waves on the Atlantic is the canonical figure; the essay's "closer to nine" is a substantively different physical claim. The two figures cannot both be correct; one of them is wrong against the underlying oceanography of the Bay-vs-open-ocean wavelength contrast the watermen-as-instrument observation rests on.

A skeptical reader cross-checks: open-ocean swell *period* (the time between successive crests at a fixed point) is conventionally cited in the 8–14-second range for typical sea state; storm swell and Pacific cross-ocean swell can reach 18–22 seconds. The chapter's "twenty seconds" is at the high end of conventional oceanographic figures; the essay's "closer to nine" is in the middle of conventional figures. The chapter may have a real-physical-error; the essay may have silently "corrected" it; OR the essay introduced a fresh drift while attempting prose compression.

Per Pass 3.1 Path B discipline, the chapter is the verification source where §7 doesn't cover the fact, and the essay's drift from the chapter is the artifact under audit — the underlying physical-correctness question is downstream (Stage 5 academic-rigor sign-off, or a Ch 3 rigor-pass), not Pass 3.1's job.

A fact-checker at The Atlantic would catch this within the first three minutes of opening the piece, because (a) the figures are unusual-enough to test, and (b) the body-knowledge-as-instrument observation depends on the figure being right.

**Resolution (verified):** Ch 3 line 24 = "twenty seconds between waves on the Atlantic" + "The Bay's wavelength runs three or four seconds, sometimes less." No §7 entry on this. The chapter is the canonical source for Pass 3.1 purposes; the essay should align OR the discrepancy should be flagged for Ch 3 + essay simultaneous reconciliation outside Pass 3.1.

**Options:**

- **Option 1: Align essay with chapter — "the wavelength of the chop on the Chesapeake is three or four seconds, sometimes less, while the wavelength of the ocean swell off the Atlantic is closer to twenty."** Smallest text edit; preserves the wavelength-ratio contrast at the chapter's specified figures (20:3-4 ≈ 5:1, which is the cleanest contrast); aligns with chapter; defers the underlying physical-correctness question to a separate Ch 3 rigor-pass if author has reason to believe twenty-seconds is wrong.

- **Option 2: Replace numerical figures with general phrasing — "they know that the chop on the Chesapeake breaks at intervals an open-ocean swell never gives you. A bay boat is built to a short, steep chop. An ocean trawler is built to a long, slow rocking; it will not survive a season in the Bay."** Drops the numerical specificity entirely in favor of a qualitative bay-vs-ocean contrast; removes the fact-checker exposure surface; loses the watermen-have-arithmetic-in-their-bodies register that the specific figures earn. Cost: dilutes the "the body learns these intervals before the mind has a name for them" observation that the next sentence in the essay depends on.

- **Option 3: Verify against an authoritative oceanographic source (NOAA buoy data, Chesapeake Bay Observing System, or peer-reviewed wave-period literature) + reconcile chapter AND essay to verified figures.** Highest-rigor option; spawns a sub-session for figure verification + (probably) a separate Ch 3 rigor-pass to update chapter + then Pass 3.1 spot-fix to essay. Cost: highest token + workflow overhead; benefit: figure-precision sign-off at academic-rigor standard.

- **Option 4: Keep essay as-is at "three" / "nine" — accept the drift from chapter.** The essay's figures are more physically-conventional than the chapter's; an Atlantic reader unlikely to flag. Cost: leaves the chapter + essay numerically out-of-sync, which is a corpus-consistency violation; if the Ch 3 figures appear in any other essay-derivative (Ch 4 → Foreign Affairs may reference Bay physical-properties), the inconsistency propagates.

**Recommendation:** **Option 1** — align essay with chapter at "three or four seconds, sometimes less" / "closer to twenty."

**Reasoning:** Pass 3.1 is a corpus-consistency pass against canonical sources, not a physical-correctness pass; the chapter is the canonical source for this fact (§7 doesn't cover it), and the essay's job is to inherit the chapter's facts. The "twenty seconds" figure may be at the high end of conventional oceanographic ranges, but it is internally-consistent with the chapter's contrast-architecture (20:3-4 is a cleaner 5:1 ratio than 9:3 = 3:1, and the chapter's rhetorical structure leans on the bigness-of-the-contrast). If author has reason to believe twenty-seconds is wrong on physical grounds, the right discipline is a separate Ch 3 rigor-pass that reconciles chapter + the essay simultaneously to the verified value — not a silent essay-side correction that leaves the chapter outside-corpus. Option 2 sacrifices the load-bearing watermen-as-instrument register; Option 3 is right for an academic monograph but overkill for an Atlantic-features submission where the chapter's figure was already in the public-record substrate the chapter built on; Option 4 leaves a precision miss on the table. Recommend Option 1 with a side-note that the Stage 5 academic-rigor sign-off (or a pre-pub queue artifact) can flag the wavelength figures for external-reviewer verification if author wants belt-and-suspenders.

### F-3.1-2 (HIGH) — Goldsborough verbatim quote omits load-bearing clause + adds article

**Current draft text:**
> Bill Goldsborough, who ran the same Foundation's fisheries portfolio through the rockfish-moratorium years and into the recovery, said to *Smithsonian Magazine* a few seasons back what is probably the single cleanest statement of the gap between the scientific time-horizon and the working time-horizon any conservation officer has put on the record. **Issue:** Two divergences from the canonical verbatim:

1. **Material omission:** The clause **"so that it can support viable fisheries"** is dropped from the first quoted sentence. The brief preserves it; the chapter preserves it; the essay does not. This clause is the load-bearing conservation-goal context — it is the antecedent that makes the watermen's-time-horizon mismatch ("don't have the luxury of the long term") read as a mismatch in object (the fishery-restoration program's longer time-horizon vs the watermen's next-boat-payment time-horizon). Without it, the quote reads as a generic restoration-vs-economic-pressure statement; with it, the quote names the *content* of the longer-term goal that the watermen cannot afford to wait for. The essay's framing sentence ("the single cleanest statement of the gap between the scientific time-horizon and the working time-horizon") depends on the clause being in the quote to land.

2. **Article addition:** The essay reads "the health of **the** bay"; the brief + chapter read "the health of bay" (no definite article before "bay"). The article addition is small — possibly an unintended Path B drift-toward-grammatical-smoothness during audience-blind drafting — but for a verbatim direct quote attributed to a named public-record-exception subject, the addition introduces words the source did not use.

3. **Elision marker absence:** The brief preserves Goldsborough's spoken pause/transition between "longer term" and "But the watermen" with the convention `[...]`. The essay's split-with-"he said" handles this gracefully *if* the omitted clause is restored, but the current essay omits both the clause AND the elision marker, presenting a continuous-seeming quote that is not continuous in the source.

Per the public-record-exception named-subject discipline ([`tools/memory/feedback_named_subject_consent.md`](../memory/feedback_named_subject_consent.md)), Goldsborough is one of the 15 verbatim-attributed public-record-exception subjects (Stage 1 brief §1 Tier 2 #10); the citation-accuracy fidelity is load-bearing. **Options:**

- **Option 1: Restore the verbatim — "Our objective is to try to restore the health of bay and aquatic life so that it can support viable fisheries. We see that task taking place over a longer term [...]. But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment."** Smallest fidelity-restoring edit; preserves Goldsborough's exact words; preserves the load-bearing conservation-goal clause; preserves the elision convention. Single quoted block rather than split-with-"he said" — the original is one continuous statement. Cost: ~12 additional words; the prose-flow benefit of the split-with-"he said" is lost. Benefit: full citation-accuracy fidelity.

Cost: marginal expansion (~12 words); requires careful elision marker handling.

- **Option 3: Preserve the current essay structure but adopt explicit `[...]` elision — "Our objective is to try to restore the health of bay and aquatic life [...]. We see that task taking place over a longer term [...]. But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment."** Maintains the omission of the goal-clause but signals to the reader that material was elided; minimal-edit option. Cost: drops the load-bearing conservation-goal clause that the essay's own framing sentence depends on; introduces TWO `[...]` markers in a single quote, which is hard on the reader.

Option 1's single-quoted-block is more verbatim-fidelity-conservative but Option 2 preserves the prose-flow benefit the audience-blind draft sought; both are acceptable, and Option 2 is the lower-disturbance edit. Option 3 is the worst of both worlds: it signals elision without restoring the substantive content.
