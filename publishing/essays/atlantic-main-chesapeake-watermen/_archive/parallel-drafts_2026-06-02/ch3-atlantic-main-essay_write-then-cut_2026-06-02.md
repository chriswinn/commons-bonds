# What the Bay Knows About Us

## I.

The harbor at Old Point Comfort wakes a long time before the city does. I have been writing from a window above the slips for a few seasons now, and I have learned to listen for the order of the morning. The first sound is the absence of one. The wind off the Roads dies just before dawn, and the water goes flat for a quarter hour, and the gulls go quiet because their argument is not yet useful. Then a diesel clears its throat at the far end of the breakwater, and another diesel answers it from somewhere behind the fuel dock, and the day is decided. Lines come off cleats. Engines drop into idle. A man calls something to another man in a voice neither of them needs to raise, because at that hour, in a harbor that has not yet started moving, sound carries across the water as if the water itself were an instrument the men have agreed to share.

It is rarely the youngest man who leaves first. The man two slips over from where I sit has been doing this since before any of the others were born. His boat is older. His lines are coiled in a particular way that the younger men no longer bother with. He does not look at his watch. He does not need to. The water tells him what time it is, and the water has been telling him what time it is for fifty years, and the relationship between them has been one of the longer marriages a man can have with anything in this country.

I grew up on this bay. My father is from Fox Hill, which is a fishing village on the Virginia side of Hampton Roads where, for most of the twentieth century, almost every family in every house had at least one person on the water. His father was the exception in his block; he worked at NASA Langley, just up the road, building scale models in a different kind of building for a different kind of America. The men on either side of him were watermen, and the men my father grew up with as boys were the sons of watermen. My father did not become a waterman himself, but the men who taught him how to handle a small boat, where the fish were running, what the smell of the channel meant in late August, what the weather was about to do based on what the gulls were doing twenty minutes before, were men whose lives were the water. My family is one generation removed from the working life on the bay. We are zero generations removed from the community of people who live it. That is the only credential the essay you are reading has.

The bay looks, from a postcard, like one body of water. It is in fact a difficult body of water. The largest estuary in North America, about sixty-four thousand square miles of watershed reaching across six states and the District of Columbia, draining the eastern Appalachians and the Susquehanna and the James and the Rappahannock and the York and the Potomac into a single brackish sheet roughly two hundred miles long. The watermen who work it know what an outsider does not. They know that the wavelength of the chop on the Chesapeake runs three or four seconds, sometimes less, while the wavelength of an ocean swell off the Atlantic runs closer to twenty. A bay boat is built to the three. An ocean trawler will not survive a season in it. A three-foot chop on a bad afternoon in the lower Bay will roll a small craft in ways an oceangoing yacht in much larger seas does not get rolled, because the energy in those steep, short waves stacks differently than open-water energy, and there are days when the postcard surface goes from glass to a wall of standing water in twenty minutes and a man who does not know which days those are does not come home. The men who have worked this water for forty years know which days are the days you do not go out, and which days are the days you can go out but you stay close to home, and which days the sky is lying about what the water is going to do in three hours. They know all of it without consulting anything external to themselves. The water grants them this knowledge in exchange for the obligation never to mistake which day they are looking at.

That bodily knowledge is the watermen's analytical apparatus. The science they have is the science of the long observation. They have been on the same bars for forty years; they know what is there and what was there and what is not coming back. The scientists who come to study what they study respect them. The regulators who pass rules over their lives know what they are missing without that knowledge and cannot, by the architecture of their offices, incorporate it into a decision. The watermen carry the data. The regulatory system carries the authority. The two have, for forty years, been failing to find each other.

I have lived near this water long enough to have known three rough generations of the men who work it. The Bay that the grandfathers fished was not the Bay that the fathers fished, and the Bay that the fathers fished was not the Bay that the sons would have fished if there had been a Bay to fish. Across three generations, the resource has visibly thinned. Not in some places. Not in some seasons. Everywhere; every season. The clearest single number anyone has put on it is the oyster number. In the late eighteen-eighties, the Chesapeake produced something on the order of fifteen million bushels of oysters in a year. By the nineteen-nineties, that number had fallen to under two hundred thousand. Two orders of magnitude in a century. A ninety-nine-percent decline. There are not many fisheries in the world that have lost that much of themselves and remained recognizable as the same fishery. There is a real argument that the Chesapeake oyster fishery did not, in fact, remain recognizable; the thing the men were going out for in 1990 was a different thing from what their grandfathers had been going out for in 1890, even though the boats looked similar and the tongs looked similar and the dock at the end of the day looked similar.

The first boats are out at the channel now. The light has not yet reached the top of the Fort. Most of the men onboard are heading for the same bars their fathers worked. Some are heading for bars their fathers never worked because the bars their fathers worked are no longer there. Most of them will come back with less than their fathers would have. Some of them will come back with nothing. The math of why is not a mystery to them, and it is not principally a story about the water.

It is a story about an accounting that does not balance, and about a community that has been absorbing the difference for longer than anyone has bothered to count.

## II.

There is a fish you have probably never eaten. It is small. It is oily. It is silver. It runs in schools so dense that their movement can flatten the surface of the water like a passing wind. By weight, it is one of the most important fish in the Atlantic ecosystem.

The menhaden. Bunker, on the New Jersey side. Pogy, on the New England side. On the Bay, just menhaden.

You do not eat it because it is not pleasant to eat. What it is, instead, is the thing that almost everything else eats: the forage fish at the base of the Bay's food web, the species the striped bass and the bluefish and the ospreys and the loons and the dolphins all come for. It is also the species that is harvested, in the largest single fishery on the East Coast, by an industrial reduction operation out of Reedville, Virginia, at the mouth of the Great Wicomico River in Northumberland County. The Reedville plant catches menhaden at scale and grinds it down into fish meal, fish oil, omega-3 supplements, pet food, and aquaculture feed. The catch leaves the Bay in tank trucks and railcars and on ships. It does not get eaten by people on this coast. It gets converted, by an industrial process, into intermediate inputs for industries somewhere else. The end customer is the consumer of an omega-3 capsule on a shelf in Indianapolis, the owner of a salmon farm in Chile, the buyer of a forty-pound bag of dog food in a Phoenix grocery. The value disperses across a continent. The cost concentrates here, on the rockfish and the ospreys and the watermen who depend on a Bay that depends on the menhaden.

This is, structurally, an interesting case, and it is worth slowing down for, because what is at stake in it is not the menhaden. What is at stake in it is whether the architecture that has been built to manage the Bay is capable of acting on its own science. So far the answer has been no. The Bay-specific cap on the reduction fishery, the number of metric tons the industry is permitted to extract from the Chesapeake in a given year, was set by the Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission in 2006 and reduced in 2017 to its current level of fifty-one thousand metric tons. The conservation side regards that figure as a floor, not a ceiling. The industry side regards it as a hard-fought concession. The number itself was not derived from a biological estimate of the menhaden population in the Bay. It could not have been. The biological estimate does not exist.

The number was derived from historical landings because no one has the data to derive it from anything else.

"We don't know the abundance of menhaden in the bay at any given time relative to the coast," Genny Nesslage told the public-radio station WHRO a few seasons ago. Nesslage is an associate research professor at the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science, one of the small handful of menhaden scientists in this country, and the statement she made on the record is the kind of statement scientists do not make casually. Scientists are trained to say what they know with as much confidence as the evidence supports and to say what they do not know with the rest of the available oxygen. What Nesslage said is what she does not know, and what she said about what she does not know is that she does not know it about one of the most economically and ecologically important fish in the watershed she has spent her career inside. Rob Latour at the Virginia Institute of Marine Science, also one of the country's menhaden authorities, said the same thing to the same outlet, with the bluntness of a man who has run out of polite versions: "We have no data to really do this in a meaningful way, from a biological perspective."

VIMS drafted, in 2023, a proposal for a three-million-dollar Bay-specific menhaden study. The proposal would have produced the missing biomass numbers, the missing age-structure data, the missing spatial-distribution maps, the missing recruitment-trend curves. Three million dollars, against a Bay fishery whose plant revenue runs into the tens of millions per year, whose plant employs several hundred people in shifts that follow the runs, and whose foodweb-foundation underpins a multi-billion-dollar recreational fishery on the same coast. The Virginia General Assembly declined to fund the proposal. The fishery continued at its cap, set in 2006 against landings data and reduced in 2017 against pressure from a scientific committee that was operating, as it had been operating from the start, without the data the committee itself had said it needed.

The ecological-impact pattern is what you would expect when a base of a food web is being managed without an inventory of the base. In several Bay tributaries, osprey have begun to fail to fledge their young, and the suspicion among the biologists who watch ospreys is that the parent birds are not bringing in enough small oily fish to feed a growing brood. Rockfish in the Bay's summer waters are increasingly heat-stressed and oxygen-starved when they ought to be putting on weight, and the species' summer condition is compounded by the absence of the prey that summer condition has historically depended on. The bay-wide signal is sometimes described, by the people who like the phrase, as a hollowing. The bigger species look fine until you ask whether they are reproducing and whether their young are surviving, and then the picture shifts.

On the last day of 2025, a Virginia resident named Tonya O'Connor filed a 349-page petition with the Virginia Marine Resources Commission asking the Commission to declare a temporary moratorium on the Bay's reduction fishery until the missing Bay-specific science was produced. O'Connor was not an institution. She was a citizen, doing the work that, in the architecture as designed, the institutions were supposed to have done. Her petition's legal grounding was the public-trust doctrine. She cited three Virginia cases by name and by year and by reporter citation: *Commonwealth v. City of Newport News*, 158 Va. 521 (1932); *Taylor v. Commonwealth*, 102 Va. 759 (1904); *Bradford v. Nature Conservancy*, 224 Va. 181 (1982). She wrote in regulatory language; she had taken the time to learn the regulatory language. The central finding of the petition was put in the petitioner's own words: the Virginia Marine Resources Commission, she wrote, "did not have — and still does not have — a Bay-wide estimate of menhaden biomass, age structure, spatial distribution, recruitment trends, or localized depletion risk." The Commission held a hearing on the petition on April 21, 2026. The Commission received, between the petition's filing and the hearing, 1,266 public comments. In a state where most citizens have never heard of the Commission, and where most fisheries hearings draw double-digit comment counts, twelve hundred and sixty-six people sat down and wrote to a regulatory body about an oily silver fish they will never see in a restaurant. The vote, when it came, was six to two against. The moratorium did not move forward. The reduction fishery continued at its cap.

Allison Colden, the Chesapeake Bay Foundation's Maryland executive director, has put the structural question about Bay management in plain words on the public record for years. "Just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should," she has said, "we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that that's warranted." That sentence is doing a lot. It names, without raising its voice, the asymmetry that the architecture has spent four decades demonstrating. When the offer is to take more, the system acts at the speed of the appetite for taking more. When the offer is to take less, the system discovers an unfamiliar caution. The menhaden case is what an architecture looks like when its asymmetric machinery meets a citizen petition with three case citations and twelve hundred and sixty-six comments. The petition does not change the machinery. The vote was six to two before the hearing began.

What the menhaden case shows is that the architecture is not, on its own machinery, capable of acting on what its own scientists are saying. The scientists are on the record. The data they need is not in their hands. The appropriation that would have produced the data was not made. The petition that would have forced the question was voted down. The fishery continues. The osprey continue to fail to fledge. The rockfish continue to come into summer at insufficient weight. The cost continues to disperse across the foodweb that depends on the menhaden, and to concentrate at the watermen who depend on the foodweb. There is no missing step in this chain. Each step has been documented; each step has been put on the public record; each step has been observed in real time by a citizen who took the time to file three hundred and forty-nine pages of legal argument against it. The architecture saw all of it. The architecture voted six to two against.

What does not come up at the Reedville plant gets paid for somewhere else, eventually, by someone. The cost that does not appear in the price for the omega-3 capsule in Indianapolis or the salmon farm in Chile or the dog food bag in Phoenix is the cost that gets carried by the osprey nest and the summer rockfish and the working waterman whose income depends on what the rockfish do in summer. It is the same shape coal had.

## III.

There is a man on Fox Hill I am leaving unnamed here. He is alive, still on the water, and the question of how he wants to appear in a piece of writing is not yet settled between us. He is from the same world Biggie was from, and his work and his life have unfolded in the next stretch of the same arc.

He is a crabber. He has been a crabber his whole working life. He still gets up between two and three in the morning. He still goes out and pulls his pots. The boat is the boat he has had for years. The hands are his. The hands are older. The pots are in the same water they have been in for as long as he has been running them, which is longer than most of the regulatory architecture that governs his work has existed in its current form. He runs them the way he ran them when he was younger, with the same patience and the same economy of motion, except the math at the end of the day is different now than it was then. The crabs are smaller, on average, than the crabs his father brought in. The runs are not where they used to be. The bigger ones are gone. The price he is paid for what he brings to the dock has not kept up with what he is paying for fuel and gear and the slip and the licenses and the freezer space. He runs the same pots. He works the same water. The water gives back less, and the world charges him more.

So he comes back to the dock mid-morning. He hoses down the boat. He coils the lines. He walks up the ramp and gets in a truck and drives somewhere else, and then he begins his second day. He works the second day until five or six in the evening, six days a week, fifty weeks a year, against a body that is no longer twenty-five. He built the second day on his own time and his own dime, over the course of a couple of decades, while the water side of his work was slowly failing to support the family math by itself. The second day is what keeps the boat in the slip and the license current and the presence on the water continuing. He did not quit the water. He could not. The water is what he knows. The second day is what he has built so he does not have to quit.

What he calls this arrangement is between him and the people who know him. What it is, in any case, is two full working days inside one twenty-four-hour day, six days a week, against a man who has been doing the first day for forty years and is doing the second day because the first day no longer pays what it used to pay. He is one man. He is also a portrait. There are men like him up and down this Bay; there are men like him in every American working-extractive industry where the price the market is willing to pay has, over the course of a generation, ceased to be the price the work actually costs to do. The men have not stopped doing the work. They have absorbed the difference. The body has been the cushion. The second job has been the cushion. The willingness to stay in the work for less than the work used to give back has been the cushion.

He has not been compensated for the cushion. The market does not have a category for it. The price the market is willing to pay for the crab does not include the second day he is putting in to keep the first day viable. The price does not include the body absorbing what the body can absorb until the body cannot absorb it anymore. The price does not include the daughter who has gone to nursing school in Norfolk because there was no boat for her to come back to, and the granddaughter who will never have a grandfather standing on the deck of a boat at four in the morning teaching her where the crabs run in late October. The price does not include any of it. The price is for the crab.

What does not come up in the pots gets paid for somewhere. By the man on his second day. By the family that does not see him until after dark. By the working community whose supply chain thins by one welder and one engine shop and one buyer and one hauler each year, because the price the market is paying does not support a welder or an engine shop or a buyer or a hauler the way the price used to. By the daughters who do not come back. By the grandchildren who will never have a chance to learn the late-October crab runs, because by the time they would be old enough to learn the runs will no longer be running on a schedule anyone could teach. The price is for the crab. The cost is somewhere else.

The accounting around this man has been failing to price his work at its real cost for thirty years. The watermen are not idiots; they have seen the math drift. They have watched the buyer prices stall and the input prices climb and the catches thin. What they have done with the drift is what working people in every American extractive industry have done with the same drift: they have absorbed it. The body has been the cushion. The second job has been the cushion. The willingness to stay has been the cushion. The cost the market did not price did not vanish because the market did not price it. The cost was paid. It was paid by people whose books do not record the payment in the column where it would be visible to a future buyer of a future pint of Chesapeake oysters or a future order of soft-shell crab at a restaurant in a city upriver.

The man on Fox Hill is what the menhaden case looks like when you walk it from the reduction plant in Reedville to the deck of a small crab boat at four in the morning on a Tuesday in late August. The same architecture. The same asymmetry. The same answer when the question of who is paying for what arrives at the regulatory door.

## IV.

A working community is not principally a count of jobs. It is a fabric. The fabric thins by one capacity at a time, and the loss is not legible until the fabric is no longer holding what it used to hold.

Tangier Island sits in the middle of the lower bay, roughly fifteen miles east-southeast of the mouth of the Potomac. It is roughly two square miles of low marsh and sand. It has been continuously inhabited since the seventeen-seventies. The people who live there speak with an accent that is, by some accounts, closer to colonial-era English than to anything else still spoken in North America. The island's economy has historically been crabs in the warm months and oysters in the cold months, with an eight-generation working-community fabric inside which the same families have done the same work for longer than the country has been a country. There are women on Tangier whose grandmothers' grandmothers picked soft-shell crabmeat at long tables in the picking houses on the harbor. There are men on Tangier whose great-great-grandfathers ran skipjacks under sail before the diesel engine reached the lower Bay. The shoreline is eroding at roughly fifteen feet a year. The projection on which the island's mayor and its elementary-school teachers and its waterman families operate is that most of Tangier will be uninhabitable within the lifetime of the children currently in school there.

James "Ooker" Eskridge has been the mayor of Tangier for two decades and a working waterman for more than fifty years. He has spoken on this question for years to whoever will listen. He spoke to the Thomson Reuters Foundation, a few years back, in plain words about what is at stake when the question is whether a place like Tangier survives the next half-century or does not. "We're talking about saving a culture," he said, "the people, (a) way of life — the whole package." The whole package is the thing the language has the hardest time with. The whole package is not just the boats and the picking houses and the seafood-processing operations and the church on the harbor and the school on the only road. The whole package is the cousin-network and the marina-network and the picking-house network through which a community looks after its own. The whole package is the way the families on the island know one another across eight generations. The whole package is the accent the linguists come down to study because it preserves consonant patterns most of the English-speaking world has long since given up on. The whole package is what cannot be reconstructed by an engineering report or replicated by a relocation program in a strip-mall town on the mainland forty miles away.

The asks the island has put forward, for a seawall, for federal funding to slow the erosion, for the kind of sustained attention coastal communities in other parts of the country have received when their disappearance has been similarly imminent, have mostly not been answered at the scale that would matter. The reasons are familiar. The island is small. The population is small. The Congressional weight of two square miles of low marsh in the middle of the Bay is what it is. The numbers on which a national infrastructure decision gets made do not, on the standard arithmetic, justify the expenditure that would buy the island another few decades of habitable ground.

Cook Cannon worked Tangier's waters for most of his adult life before he took a seat at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation in the latter half of his career. He sat for an interview with *Smithsonian Magazine* a few seasons back. The writer asked him what he expected the next decade to do to the place he was from. The quote the magazine carried, given on a cold January night in his island home, was this: "I could cry to think about what's going to happen to Tangier." That is one of the more honest sentences any working waterman has put on the public record in this generation, and it does not lend itself to summary. A man who has spent fifty years working the same water and looking out the same windows is telling you what the loss feels like in advance of its happening, while there is still enough time for the loss to be averted and not enough political will to avert it.

Earl Swift, who wrote *Chesapeake Requiem* in 2018, walked the year on Tangier in the kind of detail that a working waterman can read and recognize, which is the test that matters when an outsider writes about an island. Anyone writing about Tangier since has been writing in Swift's wake. The thing this essay is trying to add, in the brief stop it can afford to make on the island, is the structural framing the menhaden case has already established. Tangier is not a freestanding climate-displacement story; it is the same accounting in a different geography. The Bay that is wearing down the island's shoreline is the same Bay whose foodweb is being managed without the data its own scientists say it needs, and the political will that has not formed to fund a three-million-dollar Bay-specific menhaden study is the same political will that has not formed to fund the seawall the island has been asking for. The architecture has been failing the island for the same reason the architecture has been failing the men on the small crab boats. The value that comes out of the Bay disperses upstream and inland, into cities and capsules and dog-food bags and seafood counters in distant restaurants; the costs concentrate on the people who live at the water's edge, and on the people who work the water, and on the island that is losing fifteen feet of shoreline a year and on the men whose great-grandfathers ran skipjacks under sail.

What the Bay's working communities have been asking for, when you compress all of it into a single ask, is not subsidy and not sympathy. It is honest accounting. It is for the value that flows out of the Bay to be priced in such a way that the cost of producing it is paid where the cost is incurred. The architecture has not been able to do that. The institutions have known they have not been able to do that. The watermen have known they have not been able to do that. The petitions have been filed. The hearings have been held. The science has been on the record. The votes have been six to two against. And the boats keep going out, in fewer numbers each year, run by men who are working their second jobs in the afternoons because the first jobs no longer pay what the work actually costs.

## V.

A few hundred miles west of the Bay's headwaters, the same shape repeats. The water gives way to hills. The boats give way to mines.

McDowell County, West Virginia, sits at the bottom of a watershed of value that for a hundred years flowed out of it. The coal that came out of McDowell powered Pittsburgh and Cleveland and Detroit. It lit the houses of New York. It built the steel for the bridges that crossed every American river east of the Mississippi. The price the operators paid for the coal at the mine mouth went, very largely, to people who lived somewhere else. The costs the coal generated, the black lung that took the miners, the acid drainage that ran orange in the creeks for fifty miles, the schools and clinics that emptied as the operators decamped, the longevity gap that opened up between McDowell residents and the American median, were paid in McDowell. They were paid by McDowell. The architecture that managed the relationship had no slot in its books for what McDowell was absorbing.

The Bay is at the bottom of a different kind of watershed. The value the bay produces in seafood and in tourism and in recreational fishing and in the cultural identity of an entire mid-Atlantic region is captured by people scattered across six states and the country beyond, including the people who write essays like this one in apartments above marinas. The costs of producing the value, the bodies of the watermen and the thinning of the working communities and the loss of the motions and the architecture's documented failure to act on its own science, are paid in the working communities of the Bay. They are paid by the watermen of Tangier and the lower Bay and by the men still going out before dawn from a slip in Hampton. The architecture that has been managing the Bay relationship for forty years has not had a slot in its books for what the working communities have been absorbing.

The miner and the waterman would not, if you put them in a room together, recognize each other immediately. They live in different states. They watch different news outlets. They vote, when they vote, for different people. They have been told different stories about who is to blame for what has happened to them. The waterman has been told that the people who would clean up the upstream runoff are the same people who would shut down his harvest. The miner has been told that the people who would clean up the air are the same people who would close his mine. Each of them has been given an enemy who is, in fact, the other one. The system has been extracting from them both, pricing the value of what they produce without pricing the cost of producing it, and the system has been maintained, in part, by keeping them in those different rooms with those different stories.

This essay's argument is structural, not partisan. The accounting that has failed the watermen is the same accounting that has failed the miners. The politics of how each community has been told a different story about the other is part of the accounting too. Naming the shape does not require choosing a side in the partisan fight; it requires noticing that the fight has been used to keep the shape hidden.

The first boats are out at the mouth now. The light has crossed the channel and reached the top of the Fort. The man two slips over from where I sit has come in early, because his boat is faster and his bars are closer than the younger men's, and he runs his pots before the heat sets in. He will tie up at his usual cleat. He will hose down. He will put what came up in the right cooler in the right order. He will load the truck. He will drive to the shop, or to the buyer, or to whatever the next thing on his calendar is, because there is more than one thing on his calendar, and there will be every day for as long as he can keep going.

It is not yet the day he could have had.
