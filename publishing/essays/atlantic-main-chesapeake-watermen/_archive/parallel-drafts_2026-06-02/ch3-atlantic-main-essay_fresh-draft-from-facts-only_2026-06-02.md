# What the Bay Is Carrying

## I.

The harbor at Old Point Comfort wakes a long time before the city does. The water at the mouth of Hampton Roads has a texture in the early dark that is hard to mistake for any other water on the East Coast: not the long ocean swells of the open Atlantic, not the slack of a river above the fall line, but a short, broken chop that runs at a wavelength of about three seconds, the wavelength a working bay has when wind has been pushing across its fetch all night against a tide coming in.

Watermen know this in their bodies before they have words for it. If you walk along the bulkhead at any of the working slips around the lower bay an hour before sunrise — at Hampton, at Poquoson, at Gloucester Point, at Reedville, at Crisfield, at Tilghman, at Smith Island, at Tangier — you will see men loading boats by the spill of dock lights and stepping aboard without saying very much. They will already know what the day's water is going to do. They will have read it walking down to the slip.

I grew up on this bay. My father is from Fox Hill, the old fishing community on the Virginia side of Hampton Roads, twelve miles from the wind-tunnel hangars at NASA Langley where his own father — a model maker who patented a tool bit for machining the resin-impregnated laminates that went into spacecraft components, and a zero-gravity liquid-waste feed system for crewed missions — worked the territory that fed the lunar program. My grandfather went by Pooh, which was the family name for him; in the patent record he is L. E. Winn. The men in the wind-tunnel shops measured to a thousandth of an inch because the wind tunnel had to tell the truth about full-size designs that did not yet exist. The men on the water, four miles east of the hangars in a straight line, measured what was in the tongs and what was in the pots and what was in the pound nets at the rate the bay was returning it. The work was different. The discipline was the same. Both communities understood that the world is unforgiving about the difference between what you measured and what the situation actually was.

I have lived near this water long enough to have known three rough generations of the men who work it. My father knew an oysterman called Biggie who has been dead more than thirty years. My father, watching as a boy, watched Biggie work. I have watched men of my father's generation work, and I have watched men of my own generation work, and I have watched the green of the water change. The water is not what it was when my father was small. It is not what it was when I was small. It is not what it was ten years ago.

This essay is the report of someone who has been watching a particular working community for a long time and who has been asking, with increasing precision, what the surrounding economy has been accepting from the men still on the water in exchange for a continued supply of the seafood the surrounding economy has not stopped wanting.

The short answer is: more than the surrounding economy has been admitting.

## II.

Hampton was known up and down the East Coast in the nineteenth century as Crabtown.

In 1884, when Frank Darling joined his father's existing oyster business, the operation took the name J.S. Darling and Son. At its peak the Darling operation employed about one hundred and sixty shuckers and handled approximately two hundred thousand bushels of Chesapeake oysters every year. The shell mountains rose more than four stories high above the working dock. The Darlings also ran a menhaden factory at Factory Point in Fox Hill, processing thousands of tons per year into fish meal for fertilizer and oil for industrial uses. In the 1870s a Hampton inventor named James McMenamin had patented a process for canning cooked Chesapeake crabmeat in airtight tins, and Hampton crab ran in McMenamin tins to grocers along the Atlantic seaboard for the next several decades. The harbor was a working city.

In the late 1880s the Chesapeake Bay was producing approximately fifteen million bushels of oysters per year. By the 1990s, after a hundred years of dredging the reefs flat and then watching the reefs not come back, the bay was producing under two hundred thousand bushels per year. Two orders of magnitude in a hundred years. Ninety-nine percent of the oyster fishery, gone.

That decline has at least three structural waves. The first was the harvest itself: the nineteenth-century dredges scraped the reefs flat and kept scraping them flat for decades after the rate at which the reefs were rebuilding had fallen below the rate at which the boats were taking them. The second was disease: a parasite called MSX surfaced in the late 1950s; another called Dermo followed; both have a higher mortality on stressed populations than on healthy ones. The third was the watershed: as the Chesapeake watershed industrialized and as the population in its sixty-four thousand square miles of drainage across six states and the District of Columbia kept growing, more sediment and more nutrient ran into the bay and more algae bloomed and more dissolved oxygen left the deeper water in summer. Oyster spat smothered. Reefs did not recover.

The shell mountains are gone. The McMenamin tins are antiques. The Darling operation is a name on a historical marker. What is left in Hampton is the marina, which is full of recreational boats, and a thinning supply chain — a welder here, an engine shop there, an ice plant — that still serves the working watermen who go out before light.

That is what the bay was, in the relevant past. What follows is what is on it now.

## III.

There was a waterman my father knew when my father was a boy on Fox Hill. He went by the name Biggie. He died more than thirty years ago, before Virginia closed its commercial shad fishery in January of 1994. He had the build of an oak — not tall, in the way that some big men are not tall, but solid through the shoulders and the back, with the kind of forearms a man develops by shucking oysters one at a time over the side of a wooden boat for forty winters.

He worked the oysters his entire life. His family had been doing this for generations. He had a boat suited to the work and bars he worked over and over for as long as the bars yielded what he was willing to come up the river for. My father, watching as a boy, watched Biggie step onto his boat at first light with nothing but a six-pack of Coca-Cola for the day. *Lunch,* Biggie would say if asked, *is waiting in the water.*

Once the sun was up he would shuck oysters one by one off the side of the hull as he worked. His wrist motion was so economized — so honed by decades of repetition — that my father, who tried to keep count, never could. He ate them at the rate his hands could free them. He kept working.

The economy of that motion is what I want to put down on the page before anything else, because the motion is the thing that does not come back. Biggie's wrist contained a piece of intelligence that took a working lifetime to deposit. He did not write it down. He could not have. It was the kind of knowledge that is held in a body and is unloaded into other bodies only by another working lifetime of being near it. When a man like Biggie dies, the intelligence dies with him unless someone is still on the water in the same way for long enough to have absorbed it. If there are no oysters worth coming up for, no one is on the water in the same way for long enough. The intelligence quietly walks out of the system.

This is a kind of loss the economic accounting does not name. There is no line item for the wrist motion. There is no depreciation schedule for the body knowledge. There is no ledger column for what does not get handed on. But when you watch the cumulative effect of fishery after fishery either closing or thinning or running so close to the floor that the next generation does not learn the way the older men learned, you are watching a quiet form of demolition.

Biggie was one man. The motion was his. But the motion was also the bay's, in the sense that the bay produced enough of a working oyster fishery for a long enough stretch of time that men like Biggie were what the bay routinely raised. The bay has stopped raising them at that rate.

## IV.

There is a man named J.C. Hudgins who runs the Virginia Watermen's Association and works the Piankatank River in the Middle Bay. The Piankatank empties into Mobjack Bay and Mobjack into the lower Chesapeake. The oysters there are good — when they are there.

In an interview with the Maryland-side news outlet *Maryland Matters*, Hudgins put a number on what an oysterman who is still doing this work can expect to bring home on a typical day under current conditions. "You're lucky if you can put $150 in your pocket per day," he said. "And it ain't no easy job. You're freezing your butt off. It's cold."

He went on, in the same interview: "People aren't going to spend $20 for a pint of oysters. It's pretty expensive eating. I think people are feeding their families in other ways."

You have to sit with the arithmetic for a moment. A hundred and fifty dollars in your pocket on a good day. Fuel out of that. Slip fees out of that. Insurance out of that. License renewals out of that. Boat maintenance out of that. The market price for the pint, on the consumer end, has moved up to a number that has stopped competing with chicken thighs and ground beef at the supermarket case. The captain freezes his fingers; the shopper feeds the family on chicken; the gap between them is what the economy has stopped sending back to the people who go out into the cold.

This is one of four cases I want to walk through, because the cases are not a generalization. They are particular, dated, specific, and they have specific named voices attached to them.

The first case is the oyster. The arithmetic is above. Ninety-nine percent decline over a century; a working day that returns one hundred and fifty dollars to a man freezing in a small boat at the mouth of a river his great-grandfather worked.

The second case is the striped bass — what watermen along this coast call rockfish. In the 1970s the rockfish stock collapsed for a complicated set of reasons that included overharvest, declining spawning success, and an early indicator of what we would later call hypoxia in the bay. In 1985 Maryland declared a moratorium on commercial and recreational rockfish for five years. Virginia coordinated. The federal Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission anchored the arrangement. Both commercial and recreational and charter all shut down. By 1995 the stock had been declared restored. For a stretch of years, into the 2010s, you could go out at dawn off Tilghman or off the mouth of the Patuxent and catch a striped bass on light tackle and bring it back to the dock and not feel that you were doing something the bay could not afford. The story was, for a while, the success story the bay's fisheries movement had to point to.

The story stopped being a success story.

The 2024 Maryland young-of-year survey — the standard method by which the state samples how many juvenile rockfish are surviving each year, where a long-term average around eleven fish per sample represents a healthy spawn — came back at 2.0 fish per sample. Two. Not eleven. Two. And not as a freak year. The 2024 survey was the sixth consecutive below-average year. Allison Colden, who runs the Maryland office of the Chesapeake Bay Foundation, named what this means in language the institutional system she works inside understands but rarely says out loud.

"Maryland's striped bass surveys have made it clear," Colden said. "We're facing a looming disaster. Without action now, the Chesapeake Bay won't have a striped bass fishery in the future."

She has gone further. "There are very few levers left to pull." Many people in Maryland, she said, rely on striped bass for their food, their businesses, and their futures. Without action, that will all be at risk. About the warming summers and the low-oxygen periods that compound the spawning failure, she said the rising temperatures are "pummeling the species during the months it can least afford to be pummeled."

When the executive director of the state-level chapter of the largest conservation organization on the bay uses the words *looming disaster* and *very few levers* and *pummeling*, those are not rhetorical flourishes. Those are the words of someone who has been watching the data come in for many years and who knows what the language of the institutional system she has to work inside will and will not register.

Bill Goldsborough — who ran the fisheries program at CBF through the rockfish moratorium years and who is one of the people whose work was responsible for the 1995 declaration of restoration — told *Smithsonian Magazine* the part the system itself has trouble holding. "Our objective," he said, "is to try to restore the health of the bay and aquatic life so that it can support viable fisheries. We see that task taking place over a longer term. But the watermen don't have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment."

The third case is the shad. American shad spend most of their lives in the open Atlantic and return in spring to spawn in the Bay's tributary rivers — freshwater spawners with a Chesapeake center of gravity. In January of 1994, Virginia closed its commercial shad fishery. The runs were not what they had been; the spawning recoveries were not what they had been; the science did not converge on a single cause. The closure was supposed to give the runs a chance to come back.

The runs did not come back. The science has not converged on a single explanation for why. What has converged on is the absence of a recovery. Thirty-two years later, no commercial shad fishery exists in Virginia. The fish that used to run up the Rappahannock and the York and the James in tonnages a community could plan a season around does not run that way anymore. The closure was a moratorium. The moratorium became, in practice, the end of the fishery.

The fourth case is the blue crab. The Chesapeake blue crab is what is left of the commercial bay fishery as a daily working presence — what the men in the small boats in the early morning are now mostly chasing. The 2025 winter dredge survey put the bay-wide blue crab population at approximately two hundred and thirty-eight million crabs. That number is the second-lowest the survey has recorded since the survey was started in 1990. It is the sixth consecutive year of below-average numbers.

Colden, again: "The red flags are flying for blue crabs." And: "With more than five years of below-average crab numbers, it is clear that changing conditions in the Bay are undermining the current management of this important species."

What I want the reader to hold, when they have read those four cases — oyster, rockfish, shad, crab — is that they are not, individually, an alarm. The bay's fisheries have always had bad years and good years. What they are, taken together, is a pattern. They are a pattern in which one fishery after another either has not recovered after a closure, or has been recovered and is now declining again, or is running at a fraction of its historical numbers, or is running at a number that yields a working day that puts one hundred and fifty dollars in a freezing waterman's pocket. The bay is not in the middle of a single specific crisis. The bay is in the middle of a chronic structural shift that is showing up in case after case.

The men on the water know this in the way Biggie knew the wrist motion. They have known it for some time. The accounting has not been catching up to what they know.

## V.

The case that shows most clearly why the accounting has not been catching up is the menhaden case. I want to slow down on this one because the structure of what has gone wrong in the menhaden case is the structure of what has gone wrong in many of the others.

A menhaden — called bunker in New Jersey, pogy in New England, menhaden along the mid-Atlantic — is a small oily forage fish that swims in dense schools. It is at the base of the food web. Striped bass eat menhaden. Bluefish eat menhaden. Ospreys eat menhaden. Whales eat menhaden in places where menhaden are plentiful. The bay's food web depends on a steady supply of them.

Menhaden are also, as a body, an industrial commodity. The single largest industrial operation processing them on the East Coast is in Reedville, Virginia, at the mouth of the Great Wicomico River in Northumberland County, where Omega Protein operates a reduction plant. A reduction plant catches menhaden by the boatload and reduces them, industrially, into fish meal and fish oil. The fish meal goes into pet food and into aquaculture feed; the fish oil goes into omega-3 capsules, into industrial uses, and into nutritional supplements. The end customers of a single boatload of menhaden caught at the mouth of the bay are dispersed across the global economy — an omega-3 capsule in a medicine cabinet in Indianapolis; a salmon farm in Chile feeding pellets containing fish meal from this run; a forty-pound bag of dog food in a Phoenix grocery store containing a different fraction of the same boatload's protein.

The plant in Reedville employs several hundred people in shifts that follow the menhaden runs. It is, locally, a real economic presence. I am not going to pretend it is not.

The point is that the value of what comes out of the bay in a menhaden boatload is realized somewhere else. The value is captured by the global supply chains the fish meal and fish oil enter. The cost of removing that volume of menhaden — the cost paid by the rockfish that have less to eat, by the ospreys whose chicks failed to fledge in tributaries where adult osprey could not find enough menhaden during the breeding window, by the watermen whose other fisheries are weakened by the thinning of the foundation of the food web — that cost is left in the bay.

The accounting, at the policy level, has been trying to wrestle with this for some time, and the wrestling has been losing.

The Atlantic States Marine Fisheries Commission, in 2006, set a bay-specific cap on the menhaden reduction harvest, at a number the bay's conservation community argued was a floor and the reduction industry argued was a ceiling that had been hard-fought. In 2017 the commission reduced that cap to its current level: fifty-one thousand metric tons. The conservation side regards that as a floor too generous to the industry. The industry side regards it as a concession it has paid a great deal to avoid. The scientific committee of the commission has recommended coastwide reductions, with bay-specific precautions. The management board, which makes the actual policy, has adopted reductions smaller than the scientific committee recommended and slower than the scientific committee proposed.

Underneath all of this is a fact that did not change: nobody has the bay-specific data the policy decisions would require to be well grounded.

"We don't know the abundance of menhaden in the bay at any given time relative to the coast," Genny Nesslage, an associate research professor at the University of Maryland Center for Environmental Science, told WHRO. She is one of the menhaden scientists actually working on the question. She would know if anybody had the data. She is saying nobody does.

Rob Latour, at the Virginia Institute of Marine Science, said the same thing more directly. "We have no data to really do this in a meaningful way, from a biological perspective."

In 2023, VIMS drafted a proposal for a three-million-dollar bay-specific menhaden study that would have started to fill the gap Nesslage and Latour are describing. The Virginia General Assembly declined to fund it.

This is the institutional architecture I want the reader to hold. There is a commercial harvest of an ecologically foundational species occurring in the bay under a cap set by an interstate commission. The commission's own scientific committee has recommended reductions. The commission's management board has adopted smaller reductions, slower. The scientists working on the question have publicly stated that no bay-specific biomass data exists. A bay-specific study to produce that data was proposed for an amount of money — three million dollars — that is small in the context of the state budget. The legislature declined to fund it.

What we have is a system that is being asked to manage a fishery without the data it has itself said it needs, and that has declined to authorize the study that would produce the data.

In December of 2025 a Virginia resident named Tonya O'Connor filed a petition with the Virginia Marine Resources Commission. She filed it on the last day of the year. The petition was three hundred and forty-nine pages long. It asked VMRC to declare a temporary moratorium on the bay's reduction fishery until bay-specific science was produced.

O'Connor's petition was not a vague public-comment letter. It was a structured legal filing. The central finding of the petition was stated in language that put the institutional gap on the record: the VMRC "did not have — and still does not have — a bay-wide estimate of menhaden biomass, age structure, spatial distribution, recruitment trends, or localized depletion risk."

The legal grounding was Virginia's public-trust doctrine — the centuries-old common-law principle that the waters of the commonwealth are held in trust by the state for the use of the public. The petition cited *Commonwealth v. City of Newport News*, 158 Va. 521 (1932). It cited *Taylor v. Commonwealth*, 102 Va. 759 (1904). It cited *Bradford v. Nature Conservancy*, 224 Va. 181 (1982). The argument the petition built was that the commission's regulatory posture — managing the fishery in the absence of bay-specific science — was inconsistent with the trustee obligation the public-trust doctrine places on the state.

VMRC scheduled a hearing on the petition for April 21, 2026. By the time of the hearing, the commission had received one thousand two hundred and sixty-six public comments — comments from watermen, from waterfront residents, from conservation organizations, from anglers, from recreational fishermen, from people up and down the bay who had taken the time to file something on the record.

On April 21, 2026, the commission voted six to two against the petition. The moratorium did not move forward. The fishery continues to operate at its current cap.

What I want the reader to do with that vote is not to read it as villainy. The commissioners who voted against the petition can give reasons for their votes, and some of those reasons will sound institutionally responsible — a moratorium is a large action, the data Latour described as inadequate is also inadequate to confirm immediate harm, the commercial fishery represents jobs and revenue, the existing cap is the existing cap and was itself the result of compromise. I am not saying those reasons are bad-faith. I am saying that, taken together, the architecture produces a result in which the bay continues to lose a foundational species under a management regime that the system itself has said it cannot scientifically defend.

That is the structure I want named. It is not that someone in the room is wicked. It is that the accounting that decides what counts, who pays, and what gets measured, is not capable of registering the costs that the bay itself is absorbing.

Bill Goldsborough said it more than once. The watermen do not have the luxury of the long term. They are concerned about making their next boat payment. Allison Colden has put the policy implication directly. "Just as we were confident in increasing the total allowable catch when the science says we should, we need to be as willing to take reductions when the science indicates that's warranted."

The reductions, in the menhaden case, have not been forthcoming.

## VI.

If you take Highway 13 down the Eastern Shore of Virginia and stay on the spine of the Delmarva peninsula until the road runs out at Cape Charles, and then take the ferry the rest of the way — there used to be regular ferry service; there is occasional service now — you will reach Tangier Island. Tangier sits in the lower bay, about twelve miles from the mainland in either direction. It is roughly two square miles of low-elevation marsh and sand. People have lived on it continuously since the 1770s. The accent is closer, by some accounts, to colonial-era English than to anything else still spoken in North America. The economy was crabs in the warm months and oysters in the cold months for eight generations of continuous working-community life.

The island is losing approximately fifteen feet of shoreline per year. The lower projections have most of it uninhabitable within the lifetime of children currently in the Tangier school.

James Eskridge — known on the island and in the press as Ooker — has been mayor of Tangier for years and has been a working waterman for more than fifty. He spoke to Thomson Reuters Foundation about what the island means and what the loss of it would be.

"We're talking about saving a culture," Eskridge said, "the people, a way of life — the whole package."

Cook Cannon is a former Tangier waterman who now works with the Chesapeake Bay Foundation. He spoke to *Smithsonian Magazine*.

"I could cry," Cannon said, "to think about what's going to happen to Tangier."

Twelve miles north of Tangier across the state line, Smith Island sits in Maryland waters. The picking houses on Smith Island, which have for generations processed soft-shell crabs by hand, are thinning. The work is still mostly done by older women picking the soft crabs as they shed; the saying on Smith Island, mud-between-toes, refers to the way you know you have lived a particular kind of life on a particular kind of island. The younger people are leaving for the mainland.

On Tilghman Island in Maryland, *Maryland Matters* photographed Pat Whewell culling oysters aboard Lonnie Gowe's boat in Broad Creek in 2023. Three years later the same publication photographed Chumley Fisher and Chet Schwartz offloading oysters on the Tilghman dock. The boats in the photographs are working boats. The men in the photographs are working men. The pictures are part of an ongoing reportage record of the contemporary Maryland oyster fishery as it operates — actual, daily — under conditions no Maryland watermen alive today are accustomed to.

A young man named Cameron Evans grew up on Tangier and went on to Virginia Wesleyan. Asked about the island and the bigger question that Tangier raises for the East Coast, Evans said, to Thomson Reuters: "If you can't save an island a mile and a half long, how are you going to save the East Coast?"

That is the question. And it is not only a question about climate. It is a question about whether the political and economic system that the bay sits inside has anything in it that can respond to the structural shift the bay is presenting.

Andy Harris, the Republican congressman from Maryland's First District on the Eastern Shore, framed his constituents' situation when *Maryland Matters* asked him about the bay's watermen.

"Our watermen," Harris said, "are pretty resilient. They're like our farmers. They have good years and bad years. But if you have a couple bad seasons in a row, then you're in trouble."

Harris is not a politician who shares the priors of the bay's conservation community. Eskridge is not a politician who shares the priors of the bay's conservation community. Cannon worked for years on the water before he came to CBF and would not describe himself as someone who arrived at his current position from an environmentalist starting point. Hudgins runs the watermen's association. O'Connor filed her petition on the basis of public-trust doctrine, not on the basis of an environmental-justice framing. The 1,266 commenters who filed on the menhaden petition were not, taken as a body, a single constituency.

I am pausing on this because it is a real structural fact about who has been speaking. The reporting on the bay's fisheries decline does not break down along the partisan lines the national political conversation breaks down along. It runs across them. The mayor of Tangier and the executive director of CBF Maryland and a Republican congressman and the head of the Virginia Watermen's Association are all, in their own languages and from their own starting points, telling versions of the same story.

The story is that what has been working for several generations is not working anymore, that the institutional system that is supposed to be managing the situation has not been doing so in a way that anyone working the water finds adequate, and that the cost of that inadequacy is being carried, day by day, by the people whose livelihoods are at the receiving end.

The marina in Hampton where I keep a sailboat has gone, in the years I have been keeping a boat there, from a mix of working watermen's slips and recreational slips to a marina where the recreational boats are the majority and the working boats are the minority. The slip next to mine, which used to belong to a charter captain, now belongs to a man who flies in from Charlotte on weekends. The slip on the other side belongs to a man who is restoring a sailboat that he intends, eventually, to live aboard. These are not bad people. They have not done anything to the bay. They are part of a slow demographic substitution that is occurring on a thinning supply chain — the welders and engine shops and ice plants and seafood buyers and haulers that used to serve the working boats are themselves thinning, because the working boats they served are thinning, and as they thin they raise the cost of staying in business for the working boats that are still there, which thin further.

This is not catastrophic on any given day. It is corrosive on every given day. It is the kind of slow erosion that does not show up in the morning paper because the morning paper covers events and this is not an event. It is a structure.

## VII.

There is a waterman I have watched for a long time who is in the same world Biggie was in — from the same working community, having known some of the same older men, having spent his entire working life on the same water. He is alive. He has been on the bay since he was a boy. He still wakes between two and three in the morning to run his crab pots. He comes off the water by mid-morning. He hoses down the boat.

Then he goes to a mechanic's shop he owns and runs and where he works on cars until evening. He fills the gap with the only thing he had left to fill it with: more of himself.

What he calls this is between him and the people who know him. What it does, in any case, is the work that keeps the family math balancing and the boat in the slip and the license current and the presence on the water continuing. He did not quit the water. He could not. He has spent decades building, on his own time and his own dime, the second economy that has allowed him to not quit it. The accounting that made the older men's lives possible — the accounting in which the crab and the boat and the long days were enough — does not work for him. He has filled the gap. The gap is being filled by the part of him that is not on the ledger.

I do not know what he would say if you asked him directly to put a name on what he has done. He might say he is glad to still be on the water. He might say he is grateful for the shop. Those things would be true. But what the surrounding economy has been accepting from him, and from many men like him up and down this bay, is not what his answer to the question would describe. What it has been accepting is a continued supply of crabmeat and oyster shucks and rockfish fillets in exchange for a working life that has been, in its accounting, a transfer.

The cost that the crab and the boat and the long days used to cover is now being covered by the man's body and the man's evenings and the man's chance to rest. The cost has been moved off the ledger and onto him.

If you stand back from the cumulative case — Biggie's generation, Hudgins's generation, the anonymized waterman's generation, the next generation that is not learning the way the older men learned because the bay does not produce enough working fishery for them to learn in — you can see what is going on. The accounting has been making the bay look like it is paying for itself, decade after decade, because the gap between what the bay can return and what a working life requires is being absorbed somewhere off the ledger. It has been absorbed by the bay, in the ecological costs that do not appear on a balance sheet. It has been absorbed by the watermen, in the second jobs and the early mornings and the deferred retirements and the bodies that wear out earlier than they would have if the original arrangement had still held. It has been absorbed by the communities — Tangier, Smith, Fox Hill, Tilghman, Hampton — that are losing population and culture and continuous working knowledge a generation at a time.

The recovery and the squeeze are the same process.

This is the part of the structure that the Maryland oyster restoration story makes vivid, and so I want to put the oyster restoration story on the page before I close.

The Maryland Department of Natural Resources, working with state and federal partners and with CBF and with a range of academic institutions and with private oyster restoration operators, has been engaged in a long-running effort to restore the wild oyster reefs in five tributary sanctuaries. The numbers, by any reasonable measure of restoration ecology, are encouraging. Hundreds of acres of rebuilt reef. Tens of millions of oysters per year added to the rebuilt reef structures. The bay's overall oyster population in Maryland waters has roughly tripled since 2005.

"Large-scale oyster restoration is working," Colden has said. "Our effort to bring oysters back from the brink in Chesapeake Bay is working." She has also said, with the careful caveat the work requires, "continued recovery is not guaranteed."

In the same year that the restoration program was being celebrated as the most successful coastal restoration effort in the country, the State of Maryland filed for a federal commercial-fisheries disaster declaration for the oyster fishery. The harvest had collapsed in both volume and value. Watermen who depended on the wild harvest were in financial crisis. The market for Chesapeake oysters had been hollowed out by Gulf Coast farm-raised competition and by extreme-weather seasons disrupting the harvest windows.

Mitch Tarnowski, who leads Maryland DNR's oyster reef surveys, put the contradiction in twelve words, to *Maryland Matters*: "There are plenty of oysters out there. They're just not being harvested."

Jason Ruth, who owns Harris Seafood Co. in Grasonville, Maryland and is one of the remaining processors of shucked Chesapeake oysters, told *Maryland Matters*: "While the demand for boutique aquaculture oysters has grown over the last decade, the shucked oyster sector — the larger part of the industry — has seen a 60-year nationwide decline."

Colden, in her statement supporting the disaster declaration: "Despite a growing oyster population, Maryland's watermen are feeling the pinch of extreme weather and market competition."

And, at the close: "One of the most important ways people connect with the Chesapeake Bay is through eating seafood and fishing." Without watermen, she went on, "there's certainly a lot fewer eyes on the water and a lot fewer people caring passionately about the Bay."

This is the contradiction at the center of the bay's current situation, named by the institutional voice with the longest continuous engagement with it. The restoration program is working. The watermen are in disaster. Both are true. They are true because the restoration is not, by itself, what produces a working fishery. The restoration produces oysters in the water. A working fishery requires a market structure, a price, a buyer, a processor, a season the weather has not destroyed, and a man in a small boat for whom the math at the end of the day comes out in his favor. The bay restoration program does its part. The market structure does not do its part. The man in the small boat takes the difference.

That is the part of the accounting the framework's vocabulary is built to name. The cost that gets paid by someone else. The difference that gets absorbed off the ledger. The recovery and the squeeze inside the same process.

The bay restoration is celebrated. The watermen file for disaster. The same year. The same people. The same water.

## VIII.

Several states away from the harbor at Old Point Comfort, the coal communities of southern West Virginia have been through something with the same shape.

I went back, in my mind, while I was working on this piece, to a trip I took to McDowell County a number of years ago. McDowell sits in the coalfields of the southern part of the state, in the country that produced more bituminous coal per square mile than almost any country in the United States for nearly a century. The coal that came out of McDowell heated New York apartments and Pittsburgh steel furnaces and built whole sections of the American twentieth-century economy. The coal that came out is gone. What is left in McDowell is a county whose population has fallen by more than three-quarters from its mid-century peak. The towns are emptier; the storefronts are darker; the people who stayed are people the system did not have a use for after the value had been extracted.

The miners and the watermen are not in the same industry. They do not in any obvious sense share a constituency. They are talked about in the national political conversation as occupants of different stories. The coal miner is a story the political conversation routinely codes one way; the waterman is a story the political conversation, when it remembers to mention him, codes another way. The stories are different. The states are different. The newspapers are different. The votes are different.

What is the same is the position. Both communities sit at the bottom of a watershed of value that flows somewhere else. The coal flowed out of McDowell and the miners are still there in what is left of McDowell. The crab and the oyster and the menhaden and the rockfish flow out of the bay and the watermen are still there in what is left of the bay communities. In both cases the value has been captured by a global supply chain — by the buyer of the omega-3 capsule in Indianapolis; by the salmon farm in Chile; by the steel works that long ago retooled or shut; by the apartment building whose furnace has not burned coal in fifty years — and the cost of producing the value has been absorbed by the place and by the people of the place.

The accounting the country uses to talk about this is not capable of holding both sides of it at once. The accounting is good at counting what got moved. It is not as good at counting what got left behind. It records the production. It does not record the depletion. It records the GDP contribution. It does not record the body that wears out at fifty.

What the miner and the waterman have in common is not their politics. What they have in common is that each of them has been given an enemy who is, in fact, the other one. The miner has been told that the waterman is the constituency of a politics that does not understand work; the waterman has been told that the miner is the constituency of a politics that does not understand the environment. Each has been told that his neighbor across the state line is the problem he is having. Neither has been told that the system that has been extracting from them both is the same system, and that the politics of how they have been told a different story about the other is itself part of the extraction.

That is the closing observation I want to leave the reader with. This essay's argument is structural, not partisan. The accounting that has failed the watermen is the same accounting that has failed the miners. The politics that has kept the two communities in different rooms is part of the accounting too. A country that knew how to count what was actually being paid, and by whom, would not have produced the result that McDowell and Tangier have both arrived at.

## IX.

It is light, now, on the water at the mouth of Hampton Roads. The boats that were out at dawn have crossed the channel by now and are working the bars and the pots and the runs and the spots that the men in the boats know in the kind of way Biggie knew the shucking motion. Some of them will come back with a good day. Some will come back with one hundred and fifty dollars in their pockets, before the costs come out. Some of them will come off the water and go to a second job somewhere, on the Peninsula or on the Eastern Shore or in a small inland town that thirty years ago was not a place a working waterman would have had to go.

The bay is still beautiful, in the way photographs of the bay are beautiful, in the morning light. The marsh grass at the edge of the water is green. The sky is enormous. The boats moving on the water are slow and orderly and ancient in their pattern. The picture, taken at any moment, is a postcard.

The picture is not the situation. The situation is what is moving inside the picture and what is being subtracted from the picture year by year. The situation is what the men on the boats know in their bodies, what Colden and Goldsborough and Nesslage and Latour have been saying as plainly as the institutional language they have to work inside will let them say, what O'Connor put on the record in three hundred and forty-nine pages of legal filing, what Tonya O'Connor's 1,266 fellow-commenters put on the record, what Eskridge and Cannon have been saying in two different forms about Tangier, what Harris has been saying in his Republican congressman's plain register about good years and bad years and a couple of seasons in a row, what Hudgins has been saying about a hundred and fifty dollars in a freezing pocket, what Tarnowski's twelve words have summarized, what Ruth has named about a sixty-year nationwide decline in the shucked oyster sector, what the anonymized waterman has been saying with his body for thirty years without ever having to put it into words.

It is not yet the day they could have had. It is the day the accounting has produced.

The accounting can be different. That is the point of putting it on a page. The accounting we have was built by people; another accounting can be built by people. The bay does not know what category of policy it is being run through. The bay registers what gets taken and what gets left. The men on the water register it the same way. The accounting we ship is the accounting we choose to use. We can choose to use one that counts.

For now, the boats are out at the mouth. The light has crossed the channel. The day is beginning. The men in the small boats are working with the wrist motion they have absorbed across however many seasons they have been on the water, and at the end of the day they will come back to the dock, and they will hose down the boat, and they will go on to whatever the second economy they have built for themselves requires of them next.

That is what the surrounding economy has been accepting from them.

It has not been a fair trade.
