## How the no-target + no-cap discipline felt in practice

Honest observation: the discipline mostly worked, with one wrinkle.

The freeing part was real. I knew, going in, that the floor was 5,000w and there was no cap. That meant I never asked the question "should I cut this paragraph to make room for something else?" while drafting. I asked, instead, "is there more here?" The §V menhaden case — which I let run to ~14 paragraphs and ~1,150w — is the clearest example. Under a target-word-count regime I would have been thinking about that section as 1,200w of an 8,000w budget; I would have measured each addition against whether I could afford it. Under this regime I let it land at the length its substance carried. The O'Connor petition material, the three legal-precedent citations, the 6-2 vote and the way I framed the institutional reading of that vote — those landed in their full breath because I never asked the budget question.

The §IV cumulative case-walk also benefited. I let each of the four cases get the breath the case warranted (rockfish, especially, gets two extended Colden quotation moments and a full Goldsborough breath because that case has the most-tested institutional voice in the §7 inventory). Under a target regime I would have been compressing one case to give breath to another. Here I let each one settle.

The §I opening also benefited — I let the NASA Langley grandfather + Pooh + L. E. Winn + wind-tunnel-discipline material breathe across a full paragraph rather than compressing it. That paragraph might be cut by a Pass 3.5 editor or by author judgment as not load-bearing, but I let it land at the length its substrate warranted.

The wrinkle is this. The lack of a target did not make the discipline of *what to include* easier — it made it harder in a different way. With no target I had to make every inclusion decision on its own substantive merits rather than against a budget. That was good for the prose. But it meant I had to be more vigilant about *every* paragraph carrying its own weight, because the floor + no-cap discipline can produce the failure mode of "include everything because there's no reason not to." I tried to push back against that — for instance, I deliberately did not develop the Bay Program 40-year arc as a standalone section even though §7.13 supports it, because the institutional observation lands through the menhaden case and a Bay Program section would have been redundant. I deliberately did not include Mazzucato + Harvey citations even though brief §3 names them as optional, because the Atlantic features register works without them. Those were calls made on cumulative-portrait-register grounds, not on budget grounds.

So: the discipline freed me from compression pressure but did not free me from selection pressure. Selection pressure is the right pressure to be under; compression pressure is the wrong one.

Did an implicit target creep in? I have to be honest that the Stage 1 brief's §4 word-target table (~8,700w body, ~900/700/800/1,500/1,200/1,200/1,400/1,000 per section) was in my context window before I started drafting. I tried to consciously ignore it. I think I did, mostly. The actual landing — approximately 6,300w in 9 sections — is meaningfully shorter than the brief's per-section table suggests, which is at least weak evidence that I was not targeting the brief's numbers. But I cannot claim total absence of implicit target; the brief's structure (eight-section table with per-section anchor scenes) was in my head as a backbone even if I was not optimizing per-section length against it.

The cleaner test would be a fresh session that has not seen the brief's §4 table at all and only sees §7 + §8 + voice register. That session is not this session. But the empirical observation from this session is: even with the §4 table in my head, removing the cutting/compression framing produced a draft that landed shorter than the table's targets, with prose that felt more breath-led than budget-led.

## Honest observations about the experiment itself

This was the first session in this essay's drafting history that operated under the no-target + no-cap discipline as a primary constraint. Prior sessions (V-D HYBRID; ALT-NO-EM; ALT-NORM-PUNCT; write-then-cut 2026-06-02) all had a word-target somewhere in their constraint set — either as an Atlantic-features-band target (~7,000-9,000w) or as a "write long then cut" target (an implicit cut-toward-band budget).

My honest observation: the discipline did produce different prose than I would have produced under a target. I cannot run the counterfactual to be sure, but the specific quality difference I noticed in drafting was at the paragraph-extension level. Under a target I would have ended paragraphs sooner. Under this regime I let some paragraphs land at their natural length — the §I grandfather paragraph; the §IV $150 arithmetic paragraph; the §V institutional-architecture paragraph at line 121; the §VII anonymized-waterman concluding observation. Some of those will look long to an editor or to a Pass 3.5 audit, and some of them genuinely are too long for the cumulative-portrait register and want compression. But the kind of compression they want is "this sentence can be cut without losing the observation" rather than "this paragraph is too long for the budget." The first kind of compression preserves voice; the second kind kills it.

If the author's hypothesis is that target-word-counts trigger compression pressure that flattens prose, this draft is at least weak supporting evidence. The prose here may not be better than V-D HYBRID's prose at the polished-published-register level; V-D HYBRID has been through Stage 3 audit and this draft has not. But this draft was written under different cognitive conditions than V-D HYBRID was, and the difference shows up in paragraph-extension and in willingness-to-let-a-section-breathe.

The cleanest empirical test would be a side-by-side Pass 3.3 acceptance + Pass 3.4 robustness audit comparing this draft to V-D HYBRID at the same stage. That audit is not this session's job.
