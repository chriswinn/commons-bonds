Dear Features Editorial Team,

Please consider *What the Bay Knows About Us*, an approximately 6,600-word feature submitted to *The Atlantic* on the Chesapeake's working watermen and the architecture that has failed them. The essay opens at a working marina at Old Point Comfort and proceeds through four named-fishery cases — oyster, rockfish, shad, blue crab — into the menhaden reduction-fishery case where a 2025 citizen petition with three Virginia public-trust-doctrine case citations was voted down six to two against a regulatory architecture operating without the Bay-specific biological data its own scientists say it lacks. The middle of the piece walks Tangier, Smith, Tilghman, and Hampton with named watermen and named photographs; the closing portrait reports a single anonymized crabber-and-mechanic whose two-jobs-in-one-day arrangement names what the architecture has been failing to price for thirty years. The essay closes by reading the bay's watershed of value against McDowell County, West Virginia's watershed of coal — different geography, different resource, the same accounting failure.

The essay sits in the tradition of *The Atlantic*'s long-form American-place reportage — Coates's *Case for Reparations* (2014) on the structural-economic-harm side; Wallace-Wells, McKibben, Kolbert, and Andersen on the environmental long-form side; Earl Swift's *Chesapeake Requiem* (2018), Tom Horton's *An Island Out of Time* (1996), and William W. Warner's *Beautiful Swimmers* (1976; Pulitzer 1977) on the Chesapeake regional-scholarship side. Fifteen named voices carry the analytical work at journalistic-record standard: Allison Colden of the Chesapeake Bay Foundation; Bill Goldsborough on the institutional time-horizon gap; J.C. Hudgins of the Virginia Watermen's Association; Genny Nesslage and Rob Latour on the menhaden-data absence; Tonya O'Connor on the petition; James "Ooker" Eskridge, Cook Cannon, Cameron Evans, and Andy Harris on Tangier; Pat Whewell, Lonnie Gowe, Chumley Fisher, and Chet Schwartz on Tilghman; Mitch Tarnowski and Jason Ruth on Maryland's oyster paradox. Each quote is attributed to a specific public-record source. The argument is structural rather than partisan; the cross-aisle pairing at the Tangier section and the closing return to McDowell County mean the piece reads the same way from a Niskanen reader as from a center-left reader.

I grew up on the Chesapeake. My father is from Fox Hill, a fishing village on the Virginia side of Hampton Roads where his father worked at NASA Langley as one of the engineer-inventors who built the apparatus of a different America. My family is one generation removed from the working life on the water and zero generations removed from the community of people who live it. I am a nursing student at Virginia Peninsula Community College and a former healthcare-IT executive whose career spanned the National Institutes of Health during the Human Genome Project, federal research and consulting, and the CEO seat of a healthcare-IT firm. My forthcoming book *Commons Bonds*, currently with agent submissions, develops a formal framework for pricing the intergenerational costs that resource-extractive systems systematically sever from the populations that bear them. The Chesapeake essay is the first of the book's case-chapters; the McDowell parallel that closes the essay is the cross-extraction structural anchor the book develops at chapter length. I live aboard a sailboat in Hampton, in the marina that the essay's opening scene watches.
