### F-N1 — HIGH — Christophers "profit, not price, is what reshapes the world" verbatim attribution

**Site.** V-D line 176 (§IV): *"The book's central formulation, which has acquired the kind of currency a precise phrase earns when it captures a structural truth, is that "profit, not price, is what reshapes the world." The line is not a slogan, though it has been used as one. It is the conclusion of an extended analysis."*

**Problem.** The exact verbatim phrase **"profit, not price, is what reshapes the world"** does NOT appear in either the Verso Books product page for *The Price Is Wrong* nor in the Berkeley Economy & Society Initiative summary of Christophers's talk. WebFetch returned the closest in-book formulation as:

> *"The problem is that investment is driven by profit, not price, and operating solar and wind farms remains a marginal business..."* (Verso Books / Berkeley summary, attributed to the book's argument)

The Verso product page features Isabella Weber's blurb-quote:

> *"Firms and financial market actors invest not because of relative prices but because of the anticipated profitability."* — Isabella Weber

Bloomberg's coverage headlines the argument as **"Profits Not Prices Drive Renewable Development, New Book Says"** (Bloomberg, 25 March 2024) — Bloomberg's editorial synthesis, not a verbatim Christophers sentence.

The precise V-D sentence "profit, not price, is what reshapes the world" appears to be a synthesizing distillation that has acquired circulation in book-marketing / review-blurb material, NOT a verbatim sentence in Christophers's book text confirmable from sources currently available via web consultation.

**Reputational risk.** NYRB fact-checker tradition would flag this as a verbatim-quote integrity issue. The V-D essay presents the phrase in direct quotation marks attributed to "the book's central formulation" (i.e., Christophers's verbatim). If the phrase is actually a synthesizing distillation (Bloomberg headline / blurb-copy) rather than Christophers's verbatim sentence, the attribution is misleading.

**V-D's existing partial mitigation.** V-D does hedge: *"The line is not a slogan, though it has been used as one."* This acknowledges the slogan-like circulation. But the quotation marks + "the book's central formulation" framing still asserts verbatim status.

**Recommended correction.** Two options ranked by preference:

- **Option A (recommended; preserves rhetorical force):** Open the actual book at the relevant chapter and verify whether this verbatim sentence appears. If yes, cite chapter and reframe with a chapter-locator note. If no, rewrite as paraphrase:

  > **Before:** *"The book's central formulation, which has acquired the kind of currency a precise phrase earns when it captures a structural truth, is that "profit, not price, is what reshapes the world.""*

  > **After (if not verbatim):** *"The book's central formulation, which has acquired the kind of currency a precise phrase earns when it captures a structural truth, is that profit, not price, is what reshapes the world."* (Drop quotation marks; phrase becomes the reviewer's paraphrase of the book's central argument.)

- **Option B (more conservative):** Replace the quoted phrase with a verbatim sentence from the book that IS verifiable. The "investment is driven by profit, not price" formulation is closer to verifiable in-book text per Verso/Berkeley summaries.

**Severity.** HIGH. NYRB fact-checker tradition requires verbatim-quote integrity; this finding is correctable pre-submission with a single chapter-locate-and-verify or rephrase-as-paraphrase pass.

**Reasoning.** The discipline that protects against this failure mode is the same one Pass 3.1 applies to Mazzucato's value-extraction verbatim (F-4 deferred to Stage 4 / Stage 5 source-check). The check was deferred; the external fact-checker now suggests the deferral may have masked a real correction need on the parallel Christophers quote. The Pass 3.1 verification noted the phrase "consistent with the book's signature formulation as captured in bibliography §13 line 541" — but bibliography signature-formulation capture does NOT guarantee that the precise sentence is the book's own verbatim wording rather than a blurb-distillation. ### F-N2 — HIGH — Pistor "six legal modules" enumeration

**Site.** V-D line 136 (§II): *"The book proceeds through a careful anatomy of six legal modules: property, collateral, trust, the corporate form, bankruptcy, and choice-of-law clauses."*

**Problem.** Pistor's actual book enumeration of legal modules, per the SSIR excerpt of the book's opening (consistent with the kortina.nyc reading notes on the book):

> *"Capital is coded in law, and, more specifically, in institutions of private law, including property, collateral, trust, corporate, bankruptcy law, and contract law."*

And later reinforced:

> *"The most important ones are contract law, property rights, collateral law, trust, corporate, and bankruptcy law. These are the modules from which capital is coded."*

Pistor's actual SIXTH module is **contract law**, NOT **choice-of-law clauses**. The V-D enumeration substitutes "choice-of-law clauses" for "contract law."

Choice-of-law clauses ARE central to Pistor's argument (Chapter 6: *A Code for the Globe*), but they operate as the META-MODULE that extends the OTHER modules' universality across jurisdictions — they are the mechanism by which the modules are projected globally, not one of the six core modules from which capital is coded.

**Reputational risk.** NYRB fact-checker tradition would flag this as a Pistor mis-enumeration. The V-D §II paragraph immediately following the enumeration (line 136) goes on to discuss "choice-of-law" extensively at line 140-142, which is consistent with the book's Chapter 6, but the OPENING ENUMERATION confuses the level-of-analysis: choice-of-law is named as one of the six core modules when it is actually the cross-jurisdictional mechanism that the other modules deploy.

This was correctly flagged at Pass 3.1 F-3 (MED; "Stage-5-defer"). The external fact-checker simulation converts the deferral to NEEDS CORRECTION based on primary-source verification against Pistor's own enumeration.

**Recommended correction.** Two options:

- **Option A (recommended; aligns with Pistor's enumeration):** Replace "choice-of-law clauses" with "contract law" in the six-module list. The subsequent V-D paragraph at line 140-142 can preserve its discussion of choice-of-law as the mechanism by which the modules acquire universality — this is consistent with Pistor's Ch 6 argument.

  > **Before:** *"six legal modules: property, collateral, trust, the corporate form, bankruptcy, and choice-of-law clauses."*

  > **After:** *"six legal modules: property, collateral, trust, the corporate form, bankruptcy, and contract law."*

- **Option B (more conservative; sidesteps the enumeration question):** Replace "six legal modules" with "the legal modules" (drop the strict count):

  > **After:** *"The book proceeds through a careful anatomy of the legal modules through which capital is coded: property, collateral, trust, the corporate form, bankruptcy, and the choice-of-law clauses that govern how the others are recognized across jurisdictions."*

Option A is the minimum-touch fact-check-faithful correction. Option B is the more permissive reviewer-organization framing.

**Severity.** HIGH. The Pass 3.1 deferral was correct caution; external fact-check confirms the discrepancy is real and substantive.

**Site.** V-D line 206 (§VI): *"Polanyi's account of the fictitious commodities of land, labor, and money..."*

**Problem.** Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* (1944) Chapter 6 title:

**Reputational risk.** Mild. Polanyi scholars often invoke the three commodities in varied orderings without it being load-bearing. But strict fact-check convention would align the ordering with Polanyi's chapter-title canon.

**Recommended correction.** Reorder to match Polanyi:

> **Before:** *"Polanyi's account of the fictitious commodities of land, labor, and money..."*

> **After:** *"Polanyi's account of the fictitious commodities of labor, land, and money..."*

**Severity.** MED. One-word edit; no substantive impact on the V-D §VI lineage paragraph's argument; restores Polanyi's canonical ordering.

### F-M3 — MED — Blankfein "doing God's work" framing

**Site.** V-D line 152 (§III): *"...the post-2008 spectacle of Goldman Sachs's then-chief executive announcing, with no apparent irony, that his firm was doing God's work."*

**Problem.** The actual 2009 Lloyd Blankfein interview with John Arlidge of the Sunday Times of London produced first-person quotes about Blankfein himself:

> *"I'm just a banker doing God's work."*

V-D rephrases this as **"his firm was doing God's work"** — extending the first-person self-characterization to a firm-level characterization. Substantively the connection is straightforward (Blankfein was the CEO; what he says about his work is interpretable as a claim about the firm's work) but the framing slightly elasticizes the verbatim attribution.

Additionally: Blankfein later said he intended the remark as a joke. V-D's "with no apparent irony" is editorial framing about the moment of utterance; defensible but not strictly factual (the irony question is interpretive).

**Reputational risk.** LOWER than F-N1/F-N2. The substantive accuracy holds; the elasticization is at the level of editorial framing rather than verbatim-quote integrity.

**Recommended action.** Editorial discretion. Either:

- **Keep as-is** (the firm-level reading is interpretively defensible; "with no apparent irony" frames the moment-of-utterance).
- **Tighten to verbatim:** *"...announcing, with no apparent irony, that he was doing God's work."* (First-person aligns with the actual quote.)

Option B is the more conservative fact-check-faithful framing. The V-D's existing framing is acceptable for an essayistic register but a strict NYRB fact-checker might flag it.

**Severity.** MED. Single-word edit if tightening; editorial discretion otherwise.

## §4. LOW-severity findings (editorial discretion)

### F-L1 — Polanyi "protective counter-movements" (plural)

**Site.** V-D line 206 (§VI): *"Polanyi's...analysis of the protective counter-movements that arise when the disembedding becomes politically untenable..."*

**Problem.** Polanyi's canonical phrasing uses the singular **"protective countermovement"** (or "the protective countermovement"), as part of the **"double movement"** framework — laissez-faire expansion paired with a single protective countermovement. V-D uses plural "protective counter-movements" — defensible as a generalization across multiple national contexts in the long-twentieth-century arc, but technically a paraphrase variation rather than Polanyi's canonical phrasing.

**Severity.** LOW. Editorial discretion. NYRB fact-check would likely leave the plural as defensible; strict fact-check would suggest singular to match Polanyi's canon.

### F-L2 — Harvey "from the late 1990s into the 2010s" timeline

**Site.** V-D line 206 (§VI): *"David Harvey's work on accumulation by dispossession, developed across his books from the late 1990s into the 2010s..."*

**Problem.** The term "accumulation by dispossession" was coined in *The New Imperialism* (Oxford UP, October 2003). Harvey's earlier work — *Limits to Capital* (1982), *The Condition of Postmodernity* (1989), *Spaces of Hope* (2000) — prefigures the concept but does not use the term. The "from the late 1990s" framing stretches the start-date slightly relative to the term-coinage.

**Severity.** LOW. Editorial discretion. The "late 1990s" claim is defensible on the grounds that the political-geographic work prefigured the term-coinage by several years; strict fact-check would suggest "early 2000s into the 2010s" to align with the term's actual emergence.

## §9. Methodological caveat

This is a SIMULATION of what an external fact-checker WOULD CATCH, not an actual external fact-check. Limitations:

- **Web-source-only verification.** All verifications drew on web sources (publisher pages, university directories, book-excerpt sites, news archives, academic indexes). A real NYRB fact-checker would have access to library copies of the actual books and could verify verbatim-quote integrity against the printed pages. The V-D ratification recommendation explicitly should NOT be read as "external fact-check passed" — only as "external fact-check simulation surfaced the following correctable items."

- **Selection bias on the verification queries.** The fact-checker simulation ran ~40 targeted WebSearch / WebFetch queries on the highest-priority items (verbatim quotes, biographical specifics, numerical case material, top-tier lineage attributions). A real NYRB fact-checker might also run additional checks the simulation did not run (e.g., Hartwick rule's operationalization in Norway; Ørsted/DONG Energy history; specific German Energiewende public-finance vehicles; Spanish solar buildout political-withdrawal timeline).

- **Conventional intellectual-history attributions (F-C25 through F-C30)** were verified at the level of conventional NYRB-fact-check acceptance, not exhaustive primary-source verification. A real fact-checker tightening these would likely confirm them all without correction.

- **Real NYRB submission also runs its own fact-check post-acceptance.** This simulation is supplementary to NYRB's editorial fact-check, not a substitute. Pre-submission correction of the HIGH findings is recommended discipline; the MED + LOW findings can be safely deferred to NYRB editorial without reputational risk.
