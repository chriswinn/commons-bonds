# The Architecture and Its Residue

<!-- structure-note (GATE R-1, title): drafted under the locked title "The Architecture and Its Residue." Alternative flagged for the promotion call: "The Engineered Coast" (2026-06-03 zero-risk pickup). Decided at promotion; not resolved here. -->

There is a stretch of coastline in the Gulf of Mexico where a spill, some years ago, became one of the largest environmental settlements in the history of corporate liability. The number ran to many billions of dollars. The cleanup is studied in business schools. The legal apparatus that produced the figure is itself a kind of monument, the work of years of litigation and the assembled weight of a national regulatory state pressing on a single defendant until it agreed to pay.

A few hundred miles south, on a different shore of the same body of water, oil from a comparable source reached a comparable coast, and the settlement that followed was smaller by two or three orders of magnitude per mile of beach. The water that carried the oil did not know which jurisdiction it was crossing into. The hydrocarbons were the same hydrocarbons. What differed was the architecture waiting on shore: the courts, the statutes, the standing to sue, the public agencies with the authority and the appetite to convert injury into a number and a number into a payment. On one shore the architecture existed and did its work. On the other it was thin or absent, and the cost of the spill settled where it landed, on the people nearest the water, and stayed there.

The water has not changed. The architecture around the water has.

The difference between the two shores is not a difference in the size of the harm. The oil did what oil does to a marsh, a fishery, a season of work, a family that lived off the season, and it did it on both coasts. The difference is in what the harm was converted into afterward, and for whom. On the northern shore the injury became a claim, the claim became a case, the case became a settlement, and the settlement, whatever its inadequacies, moved some portion of the cost back toward the party that produced it. On the southern shore the same injury remained an injury. It was absorbed where it fell, by the people who could least afford to absorb it, and it never became anything a ledger anywhere would record as a cost the producer owed. The gap between those two outcomes is the subject of this essay, and it is not, in the end, a gap about oil.

Four books published over the past several years circle this fact from four directions, and none of them was written with the others in mind. Katharina Pistor, a legal scholar at Columbia, asks how law turns ordinary assets into capital. Mariana Mazzucato, an economist who has advised governments on both sides of the Atlantic, asks how the modern economy distinguishes the creation of value from its extraction, and concludes that it largely does not. Brett Christophers, a geographer at Uppsala, asks why private capital has been so slow to build the renewable-energy infrastructure that everyone agrees the climate requires, and finds the answer not in prices but in profits. Daniel Susskind, an economist at Oxford and King's College London, asks what economic growth was ever supposed to be for, and what it has cost to make growth the unexamined objective of public life. Read singly, each is a strong book in its own argument. Read together, they describe the same coastline from four vantage points. They describe, with remarkable convergence and without coordination, an economy in which the machinery that captures value and the machinery that absorbs its costs have come apart, and have been engineered to stay apart.

## I. The Location

Pistor's *The Code of Capital* opens with a sentence that does most of the book's work before the argument has properly begun: capital, she writes, is coded in law. The claim is deceptively flat. Everyone knows that property is a legal institution, that contracts are enforced by courts, that corporations are creatures of statute. What Pistor does is take this commonplace and turn it until it becomes strange again. Capital, in her telling, is not the substance most people picture. The factory, the patent, the parcel of land, the bundle of loans are merely assets, and an asset on its own is inert. What converts an asset into capital, what gives it the capacity to store and multiply wealth across time and jurisdiction, is a specific legal treatment, applied deliberately, usually by private lawyers, and recognized by states that did not themselves choose to confer the privilege so much as inherit the obligation to honor it.

She identifies the legal techniques by which this coding is done, and the discipline of her book is that there are only a handful of them. Property law establishes who may exclude others from a resource. Collateral law allows a claim to be pledged and re-pledged, so that the same asset can support obligations many times its value. The law of trusts allows ownership to be split and shielded, separating the person who benefits from an asset from the person who appears to hold it. The corporate form supplies a veil, an entity that can own, owe, and be sued in place of the human beings who direct it, and that can shelter their personal wealth from the consequences of its failures. Bankruptcy law sets the terms on which obligations can be discharged, and decides who absorbs the loss when the claims exceed the assets. And the rules of choice-of-law allow the parties to a transaction to select, almost as a matter of shopping, which jurisdiction's law will govern their arrangement, so that an asset physically located in one country can be coded under the law of another.

These are the modules. Pistor's case material walks the reader through them with the patience of someone showing how a familiar machine actually works, lifting one panel at a time. Land becomes mortgageable property. A mortgage becomes a security. A security becomes a tranche. A tranche, coded through the right choice-of-law clause and held inside the right corporate veil, becomes an instrument that can be sold to a pension fund in a third country and that will, if it fails, distribute its failure across a geography its original parties never contemplated.

The case she returns to, and the one that lingers, is the long litigation that followed Argentina's sovereign default in 2001. When a country cannot pay its debts, most of its creditors negotiate. They accept partial repayment, a haircut, because a restructured bond that pays something is worth more than a defaulted bond that pays nothing, and because everyone understands that a sovereign cannot be liquidated the way a bankrupt firm can. In the Argentine case, most creditors did exactly this. A minority did not. They had bought the country's distressed debt at a steep discount, and rather than accept the restructuring they pursued full payment through the courts of New York, whose law the bonds had been coded under, on the strength of a clause requiring that all creditors be treated equally. Through the early 2010s the litigation ground through the Southern District of New York, and the holdouts, in the end, largely prevailed, extracting from a sovereign nation a return that the restructuring creditors had judged neither realistic nor available.

What makes Pistor's reading of this episode more than a morality tale is her refusal to supply a villain. The holdout creditors did nothing the law forbade; they read a clause, and the clause meant what it said. The court did nothing but enforce the coding the parties had chosen. The outcome was neither a story of judicial overreach nor a story of greedy financiers. It was a story of how a legal module, the *pari passu* clause operating through a particular choice-of-law selection, produced an outcome that no party to the original transaction had imagined and that fell, in the end, on Argentine citizens who had been nowhere near the drafting table. The capital had been coded to survive the country's default. The country's people had not been coded to survive the capital.

It is worth pausing on what kind of claim this is. Pistor is sometimes read alongside David Harvey, whose account of accumulation by dispossession covers adjacent ground, the steady conversion of common or public wealth into private claims. But the registers are different, and the difference matters. Harvey's dispossession is a project, something done by a class to a class. Pistor's coding is closer to a craft. The lawyers who draft the clauses are not conspirators; they are competent professionals serving clients, and the asymmetry they produce is the cumulative residue of a great many transactions, each of them locally reasonable, none of them intended to produce the aggregate that results. This is not a softening of the indictment. In some ways it is harder, because there is no one to stop. The modules are the location where wealth is made and where its costs are quietly assigned, and they are operating, in every case, exactly as designed.

There is a tradition that has thought hard about whether the people who bear the costs of such arrangements might govern them directly, and Pistor's book gestures toward it without entering it. Elinor Ostrom spent a career showing that communities can manage shared resources without either privatization or central command, through rules the users write and enforce themselves. But Ostrom's commons were bounded, and her users shared an interest in the resource's survival. The commons that Pistor describes are engineered across jurisdictions precisely so that the parties who benefit and the parties who bear the cost do not share a table, a polity, or in many cases a hemisphere. Self-governance presumes a homogeneity of interest that the legally coded commons is built to dissolve. The location is identified. Whether anything can be attached to it that the dispersed and unconsulted could use is a question Pistor's framing raises and her subject does not answer.

## II. The Activity

If Pistor locates where value is captured, Mazzucato asks what the modern economy has agreed to call its capture.

*The Value of Everything* opens, as it must, near the financial crisis, with a remark that has become a small artifact of the period. In the autumn of 2009, with the wreckage of the crash still being cleared and public money still flowing into the banks that had caused it, the chief executive of one of the most powerful investment firms in the world described his institution's work, to a newspaper interviewer, as doing God's work. The remark was widely taken as arrogance, and it was arrogant. But Mazzucato is interested in something the easy reading misses. The executive was not being ironic. He believed it. And the conditions under which a person in his position could believe it, and could say it to a journalist without expecting ridicule, are the real subject of her book.

Her central distinction is old and was once central to economics and has since been allowed to lapse: the distinction between making and taking, between the creation of value and its mere extraction. The classical economists, from the Physiocrats through Smith and Ricardo and into Marx, all maintained some version of a theory of value, a way of saying which activities added to the wealth of a society and which merely rearranged or skimmed it. The boundary moved, and the theories disagreed about where to draw it, but the project was understood: an economy needed to know the difference between the baker and the rentier. Over the twentieth century, Mazzucato argues, this project was abandoned. Value came to be defined, circularly, as whatever commanded a price in the market. If an activity earned income, it had by definition produced value equal to that income. The question of whether the income reflected something made or something merely taken became, formally, unaskable.

The consequence is that extraction now wears the costume of creation, and the national accounts cannot tell the two apart. Mazzucato's term for the activities in question is precise, and she has used it consistently: activities focused on moving around existing resources and outputs, and gaining disproportionately from the ensuing trade. The phrase is doing careful work. It does not say that finance produces nothing; intermediation is a real service, and an economy needs it. It says that a portion of what is captured is disproportionate to what is contributed, and that the prevailing theory of value has no way of registering the disproportion, because it has defined the disproportion out of existence.

Her cases are chosen to make the abstraction concrete, and the most forceful of them concerns a drug. In December 2013 a treatment for hepatitis C reached the American market at a list price of eighty-four thousand dollars for a twelve-week course, a thousand dollars a pill. The price provoked a Senate investigation and a national argument about pharmaceutical pricing that has not ended. Mazzucato's interest is in the accounting behind the figure. The science that made the drug possible did not originate, in her telling, inside the firm that priced it. It rested on a long chain of publicly funded basic research and on the work of a smaller, federally supported biotechnology firm whose program the larger company acquired. The public had borne a substantial share of the risk and the early cost. The private firm bore the late-stage development and the commercialization, which are real and not cheap, and then captured a return calibrated to what a desperate insurer or government would pay rather than to anything the firm had spent. The price was not, in her reading, the wage of innovation. It was the rent of a position, the position of holding the one thing a sick person and a public health system could not do without.

The same logic, traced into finance, produces her account of the fee. The asset-management industry charges, in its classic form, two and twenty: two percent of the assets under management each year, and twenty percent of the returns above some threshold. Mazzucato walks the reader through this structure with the patience of someone showing how a card trick is done, because the structure is in fact a kind of trick, though a perfectly legal one. The two percent is charged whether the fund gains or loses. The twenty percent is charged on gains that, over long horizons and across the industry, frequently fail to beat what a low-cost index fund would have returned for a fraction of the cost. Compounded across decades and across the enormous pools of pension and institutional money the industry manages, the transfer she describes runs, on her accounting, to hundreds of billions of dollars, money moved from the savers who bore the risk to the managers who bore the fee. The order of magnitude is the point, and the managers broke no rule in producing it. They occupied a position, and the position paid.

It would be easy, and it would be a misreading, to take all this as a story about the unusual venality of bankers and drug companies. Mazzucato is careful, and the carefulness is the substance of her argument. The point is not that the pharmaceutical industry is uniquely greedy or that fund managers are worse than other people. The point is that an economy which has lost the distinction between making and taking will reward the two identically, will record them identically in its accounts, and will therefore generate, as a matter of routine and without anyone deciding it should, a steady drift of reward away from contribution and toward position. The God's-work remark, read this way, was less a confession of cynicism than an accurate report of what the system tells the people at its commanding heights about themselves. They are told they are creating value, because the system has no category for what they are actually doing, and so the only word available is the wrong one.

## III. The Mechanism

Christophers narrows the lens to a single sector and, in narrowing it, sharpens the whole inquiry to a point.

The puzzle that animates *The Price Is Wrong* is one that ought to be impossible under the standard account of how markets work. The world needs to build an enormous quantity of renewable-energy infrastructure, and quickly. The technology exists and has become, by most measures, the cheapest new source of electricity in history. Solar and wind power are now, per unit of energy, less expensive to build than the fossil generation they would replace. Under the textbook account, this should be the end of the story. Cheap clean power should be flooding the grid, driven by nothing more than the profit motive and the price signal. It is not, or not fast enough, and Christophers is interested in why the textbook is wrong.

His answer is contained in a sentence that he is willing to stake the book on: profit, not price, is what reshapes the world. The price of renewable electricity has fallen dramatically. But a private investor does not deploy capital because a price is low. A private investor deploys capital because an expected profit clears a threshold, and the threshold is set not by the cost of the technology but by what that same capital could earn elsewhere, at what perceived risk, over what horizon. Renewable-energy projects, with their enormous upfront construction costs, their long payback periods, their exposure to shifting subsidy regimes and volatile wholesale power markets, generate returns that are real but modest and, crucially, uncertain. Measured against the returns available from other uses of the same money, and against the hurdle rates that decades of historical performance have trained institutional investors to expect, the modest and uncertain return of a wind farm simply does not clear. The electricity is cheap. The profit is thin. And it is the profit, not the price, that the capital is watching.

Christophers builds the case patiently, through the actual mechanics of how energy projects are financed, the spreadsheets and the discount rates and the contractual structures by which a flow of future electricity sales is converted into a present decision to build or not to build. The calibration that governs these decisions, he shows, was formed in a particular history. The return expectations that institutional capital brings to an energy project were set largely by the high-yield environment of the decades after 1980, and they do not bend easily to accommodate a technology whose social value vastly exceeds the private return it can be made to throw off. The fiduciary managing a pension fund is not free to accept a lower return because the project is good for the planet. He is bound, often legally, to seek the best risk-adjusted return for his beneficiaries, and a wind farm yielding less than the alternatives is, by that standard, a breach. The mechanism is not indifference. It is fidelity, fidelity to a calibration that was set before the climate became the binding constraint and that has no instruction for the case where the cheapest thing to build is the thing that will not quite pay.

It is worth dwelling on the figure of the fiduciary, because Christophers's argument turns on his being conscientious rather than corrupt. The manager allocating a pension fund's capital is the same kind of actor Mazzucato found at the other end of the asset-management chain, the one collecting two and twenty, except that here the structure has him on the right side of his beneficiaries rather than the wrong one. He is doing what he is supposed to do. The calibration he applies, the expected return below which an investment is not prudent, is not a personal preference he could relax out of conscience; it is the accumulated weight of decades of comparison, the benchmark against which his own performance will be judged, the legal standard of care he can be sued for failing. When that calibration declines the cheapest available remedy for the largest available problem, the failure belongs to no person in the chain. It sits in the calibration itself, which was built by a long history and which no individual is positioned to override. This is the same structural-not-intentional reading that Pistor applied to the holdout creditors and Mazzucato to the firm that priced the drug, arriving now in the gap between what a wind farm is worth to a society and what it can be made to yield to the capital that would have to build it.

What gives the book its force is the counter-case, the demonstration that the wall is not a law of nature. Where the renewable build-out has moved fastest, Christophers shows, it has frequently moved on public money or under public ownership. The Danish offshore-wind industry, now a global model, was built in significant part through a company that began in public hands, the firm once known as DONG Energy and now called Ørsted, developing the early offshore projects under a public mandate before the private market would touch them. The German energy transition, the Energiewende, leaned heavily on public-finance vehicles that absorbed the early risk the private market declined. Spanish solar advanced in the late 2000s on public-investment instruments that were later, when the political winds shifted, withdrawn, stranding part of what had been built. The pattern is consistent and it is the argument's spine: the infrastructure got built where an institution other than the profit-seeking investor was willing to bear the cost that the profit calibration would not, and it stalled, or reversed, where that institution was withdrawn.

This is the same shape Mazzucato found in the drug and Pistor found in the bond, arriving now from the direction of the future rather than the present. In Pistor the legal module assigns the cost of a default to people who never signed. In Mazzucato the lost theory of value lets a price be charged that bears no relation to what was made. In Christophers a return calibration formed in one era forecloses an investment that a later era desperately needs, and the cost of the foreclosure, a climate left to warm because the cheap remedy did not clear a hurdle rate, falls on a generation that is not yet at the table and cannot adjust the discount rate to include itself. The mechanism that governs the outcome is not the price. It never was. It is the structure of who can profit, on what terms, from bearing or shedding a cost, and the structure was built for a world that is no longer the world.

## IV. The Metric

Susskind stands the furthest back of the four, and asks the question that the other three leave implicit: what was all of this supposed to be for?

*Growth: A Reckoning* is a history before it is an argument, and the history is its strongest part. Economic growth, Susskind reminds the reader, is a young idea, younger than most of the institutions that now pursue it. For most of human history there was effectively no growth to speak of; living standards in 1700 were not meaningfully above living standards two thousand years earlier. The very concept of a national economy whose total output could be measured, tracked, and made to rise was an invention of the twentieth century, and Susskind tells the story of the invention with care. The national-income accounts that let a government speak of the size of its economy were assembled in the 1930s and 1940s, in significant part through the work of Simon Kuznets, who built the apparatus and then warned, presciently and in vain, against confusing the figure it produced with the welfare of a nation. The theoretical machinery that turned growth from a measurement into a policy objective came in the 1950s and 1960s, with the work that identified the residual portion of growth that capital and labor alone could not explain, the contribution attributed to technological progress, and with the consolidation of growth as the organizing aim of economic policy across the developed world.

Susskind's history is honest about the achievement, and the honesty is what makes the book trustworthy when it turns critical. The growth that this machinery measured and promoted was not a statistical illusion. The acceleration of living standards from the end of the Second World War into the early 1970s was real and was vast, the fastest broad improvement in material life that had ever occurred. And the story did not end there. Between 1980 and 2020, by the World Bank's poverty series, more than a billion people in East Asia alone moved out of extreme poverty, on the back of exactly the kind of growth-centered development the model commends. Child mortality fell across the same span by margins that, stated plainly, describe tens of millions of children alive who would not otherwise have lived. Susskind insists on these facts, and he is right to, because any reckoning with growth that cannot first say clearly what growth has done is not a reckoning but a posture.

The Kuznets warning is the hinge of the whole book, and Susskind gives it the weight it deserves. The man who assembled the apparatus that let nations measure their output told the body that commissioned the work that the welfare of a nation could scarcely be inferred from the measurement he had built. The warning was recorded and ignored, and the ignoring is the history Susskind is recounting. A number created to serve as one indicator among many was promoted, over the decades that followed, into the objective itself, the figure that governments would rise and fall by, the proxy that came to stand in for everything it was only ever a partial measure of. Susskind's point is not that the number is false but that a society which steers by a single proxy will reliably optimize the proxy at the expense of the thing the proxy was meant to track, and will do so without anyone deciding that it should, because the proxy is what the instruments report and the instruments are what the institutions watch.

Having credited the ledger's left side, he turns to the right, and his accounting of the costs is organized rather than merely indignant. There is the climate cost, the carbon-intensive character of the growth that lifted living standards and the warming it has bought. There is the inequality cost, the now-familiar divergence by which the gains of recent decades of growth have accrued disproportionately to those already holding capital. There is the cost to wellbeing, the accumulating evidence that beyond some threshold the rise in measured output stops tracking any rise in how people actually experience their lives. And there is a cost that Susskind is more willing than most economists to name, a cost to meaning, the hollowing of the kinds of work and attachment and purpose that a relentlessly growth-optimized economy treats as friction to be removed. The four costs are not presented as a brief for the prosecution. They are presented as items the metric does not capture, and the failure to capture them is the book's real charge against the metric.

It matters that Susskind does not draw the conclusion that the loudest critics of growth have drawn. The degrowth position holds that growth itself is the pathology and that a humane economy must aim at less. Susskind declines this. His case is for redirection, not abandonment. The objective should not be growth as such, measured by a number Kuznets warned against trusting, but the particular things growth was always a proxy for, and the work is to aim the economy at those things directly and to stop treating the proxy as the goal. This places him in company he half-acknowledges. Kate Raworth's doughnut, with its floor of human provision and its ceiling of ecological limit, and Herman Daly's older steady-state economics describe an economy bounded rather than maximized, and Susskind's redirection points toward the same territory from a more orthodox starting point. The disagreement he names with the degrowth position is real, but it is a disagreement among people who agree that the metric, left as the unexamined objective of public life, has become a poor servant pretending to be the master.

What Susskind shares with the other three, and what makes his book belong on the same shelf, is the recognition that the number does not capture the thing. Pistor's modules capture wealth without capturing its costs. Mazzucato's accounts capture income without distinguishing what was made from what was taken. Christophers's profit calibration captures private return without registering the social value it forecloses. And Susskind's growth metric captures aggregate output while remaining blind to the climate, the inequality, the wellbeing, and the meaning that the output was bought with. Four measurements, each of them serviceable for the thing it was built to count, and each of them silent about a cost that the thing it counts is generating on the other side of a wall the measurement cannot see across.

## V. The Negative Space

Set the four books beside one another and a single shape emerges that none of them draws alone.

Pistor supplies the location. The separation between value-capture and cost-bearing is not a metaphor or a moral failing; it has an address, and the address is the set of legal modules through which assets are coded into capital. Mazzucato supplies the activity. What happens at that location is extraction misrecognized as creation, rewarded as creation, accounted as creation, because the theory of value that could tell the difference has been allowed to lapse. Christophers supplies the mechanism. The thing that governs whether a cost is borne or shed is not the price but the profit, the calibration of return against which a cost-bearing project is accepted or refused. And Susskind supplies the metric. The instrument by which a society keeps score has no column for the costs that the other three describe, and so a polity steering by that instrument cannot see what it is doing even when it is doing it on a planetary scale.

Location, activity, mechanism, metric. Read as one diagnosis, the four books describe an economy in which value reliably comes apart from the costs of its production, in which the coming-apart is engineered rather than accidental, rewarded rather than penalized, governed by profit rather than price, and invisible to the metric by which the whole arrangement is judged. The convergence is the more striking for being unplanned. A legal scholar, two economists, and a geographer, working in different countries and different disciplines and different decades of training, have each pressed on the same structure from a different face and reported the same interior.

There is precedent for a convergence of this kind, and naming it locates the present moment. Between 1936 and 1948, in the space of barely more than a decade, three books appeared that between them reset the terms on which the relationship between markets and the societies that host them could be discussed. Keynes published *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* in February 1936, and the idea that an economy could settle into a stable equilibrium of mass unemployment, which the market would not on its own correct, entered the mainstream. In 1944 Karl Polanyi published *The Great Transformation*, with its argument that the attempt to organize society around self-regulating markets was not a natural development but a deliberate and violent project, and that society would inevitably generate a counter-movement to protect itself from the consequences. And in the same year Friedrich Hayek published *The Road to Serfdom*, with its warning that the planning proposed as the remedy carried its own road to tyranny. The three did not agree. Between them they staked out the poles of the argument that the second half of the twentieth century would conduct. But they shared a recognition that the prewar settlement was finished and that the relationship between the market and the society would have to be built again on new terms.

The quartet under review reads like the early signal of a comparable remaking. The diagnosis these four books share is the diagnosis that the late-twentieth-century settlement, the one that defined value as price and growth as the goal and the market as the presumptive default, has produced an architecture in which costs reliably escape the parties that generate them, and that this architecture is now the binding constraint on a list of problems, the climate first among them, that the settlement is structurally unable to address. The lineage runs directly through Polanyi. His disembedding, the tearing of the economy loose from the social relations that once contained it, is the long historical process whose late residue these books describe. His double movement, society's instinctive counter-pressure against the costs of disembedding, is the impulse one feels gathering behind all four of them. And his book pointed toward exactly the gap that this convergence inherits and does not close.

For here is what the four books, read together, do not do, and the not-doing is the most important thing about them.

Each names a cost that escapes. None supplies the instrument that would attach the cost back to its origin. Pistor identifies the modules through which capital is coded, with great precision, as the location where the separation is engineered, and the natural question, what would have to be attached at that location to make the coding carry its costs as well as its benefits, is not her question. Mazzucato distinguishes value created from value extracted with a clarity the discipline had lost, and the natural question, how an economy might account for extraction at the point and the moment it occurs rather than only deploring it in aggregate, is not hers. Christophers shows that profit and not price governs whether the climate-saving infrastructure gets built, and the natural question, what corrective could be attached to the profit calibration so that the foreclosed social value enters the private decision, sits just past the edge of his account. Susskind shows that the metric is blind to the costs that growth generates, and the natural question, what would have to be counted, and priced, for the metric to see them, is one his redirection gestures toward at the level of the national objective without descending to the level of the individual transaction where the cost is actually shed.

The gap is sharpest where the cost is borne by people who cannot be at the table because they do not yet exist. A spill, a default, an emission, a depleted aquifer transfers a cost forward across generations as surely as the legally coded bond transfers it across borders, and the generation that will bear it has, by construction, no standing in the decision that imposes it. The economics of this intergenerational transfer is not new. As long ago as 1977 the economist John Hartwick formulated a rule for it, a principle stating the condition under which a society drawing down a non-renewable resource could keep faith with the generations to come, by reinvesting the proceeds so that the productive base passed on undiminished. The rule has been operationalized in places, in the Norwegian sovereign-wealth architecture that holds the proceeds of the country's oil against the day the oil is gone, and in the bonded-coverage mechanisms by which some extractive and offshore-energy industries are required to set aside in advance the cost of the cleanup they will eventually owe. These are existence proofs. They show that the intergenerational cost can be given an instrument. They are also exceptions, narrow and contested, and the four books name the gap they address with one voice while leaving the instrument that closes it to an inference no single book draws.

This is not a deficiency in any of the four. It is the shape of a moment. Polanyi named the counter-movement and specified no pricing instrument; the gap these books inherit is the same gap, carried forward eight decades and made more urgent by the climate clock. The convergence of the four is the recognition that the diagnosis is essentially complete. The location is mapped, the activity is named, the mechanism is exposed, the metric is indicted. What the convergence also reveals, in the negative, is that the diagnosis does not carry its own remedy, and that the step from naming the separation to instrumenting its repair is the step none of these books takes, because it is a different kind of step, and because, perhaps, it is the work of a different decade.

## VI. The Architecture That Would Have to Exist

It is one thing to observe that the four books stop where they stop. It is another to say, with any precision, what would lie past the stopping point, and a review-essay is not the place to design what the books decline to design. But the negative space has a shape, and the shape can be described without being filled.

If the costs that the four books describe escape at a location, then a corrective would have to attach at that location rather than hovering above it as exhortation. This is the force of reading Pistor as the others' ground. A carbon tax, a disclosure rule, a fiduciary-duty reform, a sovereign-wealth fund: each of these existing instruments reaches some part of what the mechanism does, and each, reaching only part, leaves the rest to escape through the parts it does not reach. What the four books jointly imply, without any of them proposing it, is the question of whether a corrective could be attached at the per-transaction level, at the modules themselves, where the cost is generated, so that the bearing of the cost travels with the capturing of the value instead of being assigned, downstream and out of sight, to whoever happens to be standing where the cost comes to rest. The books name the location. They do not attach anything to it. The gap between those two sentences is the whole of the unfinished work.

The intergenerational version of the gap is the one that the existing political vocabulary is least equipped to name, and it is worth being exact about why. A cost shed across space, onto people in another jurisdiction, can at least in principle be addressed by a politics of solidarity among the living, by treaties, by transfers, by the slow construction of standing for the people the cost lands on. A cost shed across time, onto people not yet born, has no such recourse, because the bearers cannot organize, cannot vote, cannot sue, and cannot be compensated by any transfer that reaches them in time to matter. The discounting that governs the profit calibration Christophers describes is, among other things, a machine for converting the absence of future people from the negotiating table into a mathematically respectable reason to impose costs on them. The intergenerational pricing gap, the absence of any instrument that makes a present decision carry the future cost it generates, is named by implication in all four books and instrumented by none of them, and it is the part of the negative space where the stakes are highest and the existing tools thinnest.

There are traditions that have thought about pieces of this, and the honest thing is to inventory them as company rather than to enlist them as proof. The reparations economists, working from the premise that a backward-looking injury can be priced and that the pricing is a precondition of any honest reckoning, have built the most developed methodology anyone has for attaching a number to a cost shed in the past, and the methodology is a template for the more general problem even where its specific subject is not in view here. The civic-republican tradition of Pettit and Skinner, with its account of freedom as non-domination, supplies a language for why an arrangement that leaves some parties structurally unable to refuse the costs imposed on them is a failure of liberty and not merely of efficiency; an accountability architecture would be, in that tradition's terms, non-domination made institutional. The Public Choice scholars, Buchanan and Tullock foremost, who showed how concentrated interests shape the rules to their advantage, are usually read as a conservative caution against state action, but their analysis of rent-seeking-as-architecture-shaping sits on exactly the same side of the structural-mechanism question as everything in these four books; the rules are written by the parties who benefit from them, which is the problem these books describe, named in a different key. And the Ostromian tradition of self-governed commons supplies both a model and its limit, the model of users who write their own rules and the limit, the heterogeneity of interest, that the legally engineered commons is built to exploit.

None of these traditions, and none of the four books, resolves into a program, and the refusal to supply one is not evasion. The correction the diagnosis implies is structural, not partisan, and it holds whichever political route reaches it. It could be approached through regulatory caution, the careful extension of the rules so that costs are priced where they arise. It could be approached through market discipline, the design of instruments that make the bearing of a cost a tradable, priced obligation rather than an externality. It could be approached through the solidarity of workers and citizens insisting that the costs settling on them be carried by the value that generated them. These are not the same politics, and the four books do not adjudicate among them. What the books establish, jointly, is that the diagnosis no longer depends on which route one prefers. The separation of value from cost is visible from the right and from the left, from the lawyer's bench and the economist's spreadsheet, and the question it poses, what instrument attaches the cost to its origin, is prior to the question of who builds the instrument.

Return to the coastline.

The oil that reached the southern shore was the same oil that reached the northern one. What differed was everything that had been built on land to meet it: the law that could name the injury, the courts that could price it, the agencies that could collect, the standing that let the people nearest the water be counted as parties to their own damage rather than as its silent site. On one shore that architecture existed, and the cost of the spill was converted into a number and a payment and a deterrent. On the other the architecture was thin, and the cost stayed where it fell. The water did not choose. The architecture did the choosing, in advance, by being there or not being there.

The four books under review are, each in its own idiom, surveys of the architecture that exists. Pistor maps the modules through which value is coded and its costs assigned. Mazzucato shows how the accounts came to reward extraction as though it were creation. Christophers shows how the profit calibration decides which costs are borne and which are shed. Susskind shows how the metric keeps a society from seeing the costs at all. Together they have done the surveying with a thoroughness that leaves little of the existing structure unmapped. The separation of value from cost has become, in their hands, a described feature of a built environment, with a location, a vocabulary, a mechanism, and a measure, no longer a suspicion to be argued for but a structure to be reckoned with.

What the survey leaves, by the precision of its own completeness, is the outline of what has not been built. The architecture that exists is an architecture for capturing value and shedding cost. The architecture that does not yet exist is the one that would attach the cost back to the value at the point where they come apart, and would do it for the costs shed across time as well as the costs shed across space, for the generation not yet born as well as the people on the far shore. The four books name the residue that the existing architecture leaves behind, the cost that settles on whoever is standing nearest the water. They do not build the thing that would carry the residue back to its source, and no fault attaches to them for not building it. The diagnosis that completes itself in one decade leaves to the next the construction it has made newly impossible to ignore.

The spill on the southern shore was never really a story about oil. It was a story about what was waiting on land when the cost came in. On one coast the answer was an architecture, and the architecture, whatever its faults, did its work. On the other the answer was its absence, and the absence did its work too. The water has not changed. The water never changes. What can change is the architecture around it, and the question the four books leave on the shore, having named with such care everything that is already there, is the question of what is not there yet, and who will build it, and whether it will be built in time for the next contamination, on the next coastline, where the cost is even now settling on the people who happen to live where the value went away.
