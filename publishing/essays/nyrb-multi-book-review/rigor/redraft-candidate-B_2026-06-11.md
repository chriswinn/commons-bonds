# The Architecture and Its Residue

<!-- structure-note: title drafted under the locked default "The Architecture and Its Residue"; the live alternative for the promotion call is "The Engineered Coast" (flagged 2026-06-03 as a zero-risk pickup). -->

*The Code of Capital: How the Law Creates Wealth and Inequality*
by Katharina Pistor
Princeton University Press, 2019

*The Value of Everything: Making and Taking in the Global Economy*
by Mariana Mazzucato
PublicAffairs, 2018

*The Price Is Wrong: Why Capitalism Won't Save the Planet*
by Brett Christophers
Verso, 2024

*Growth: A Reckoning*
by Daniel Susskind
Allen Lane / Belknap Press of Harvard University Press, 2024

---

Consider a coastline. Not an emblem of one, a particular shore: marsh grass, barrier sand, a working harbor where the boats go out before light and the morning smells of diesel and brine. Some years ago, oil reached it. The oil kept coming for weeks. When the accounting was finally done, the companies responsible had agreed to settlements that ran to many billions of dollars. There were claims facilities. There were penalties assessed under statutes with names and histories. There were restoration funds with boards, budgets, and decades of obligated work ahead of them.

Now consider a second shore, south across the same water, reached in the relevant season by the same oil. The people there fished the same species from boats doing the same work, and the oil did to their marshes what oil does. Counted per mile of coastline, what reached the southern shore in recompense came to two or three orders of magnitude less.

The difference was never in the oil. It was in the architecture standing onshore.

On the northern shore the cost arrived and found institutions built to receive it: courts with jurisdiction, statutes with damage provisions, agencies with the standing to demand restoration and the staff to supervise it. On the southern shore the same cost arrived and found almost nothing built to receive it, and so it was received the way unreceived costs are always received: by bodies, by livelihoods, by the slow subtraction from a place of the things that made the place possible. There is a word for what a cost becomes when no architecture exists to catch it. It becomes residue.

Four recent books, written from four directions and never intended as a set, read together as one extended explanation of that difference. A legal scholar shows that capital itself is manufactured in law. An economist shows that the moving around of existing value is routinely booked as the creation of new value. A geographer of finance shows why prices alone reshape nothing. An economist of growth shows what the master metric counts and what it has never counted. None of the four mentions that coastline. Read in one another's company, they explain its two shores all the same.

Begin with the law, because the law was there first. Katharina Pistor teaches at Columbia Law School, and *The Code of Capital* rests on a sentence short enough to carry in a pocket: "Capital is coded in law." An object, a promise, an idea: none of these is capital on its own. Each becomes capital when legal devices confer on it attributes that ordinary things lack: priority over competing claims, durability across time, universality against strangers, convertibility into state money when the holder needs to run. Take the devices away and the asset reverts to being a field, an IOU, a thought.

The devices are old, several of them centuries old, and Pistor counts six doing most of the work: property, collateral, trust, the corporate veil, bankruptcy, and choice of law. The artisans are lawyers. The workshop is private. The product is wealth that carries the state's enforcement behind it wherever it travels, which is the entire point of the coding: a well-coded asset takes its privileges abroad the way a passport holder takes a citizenship.

The force of the book is in its reversal of figure and ground. What economics has tended to treat as a natural kind, arising wherever markets arise, Pistor treats as a manufactured one, assembled module by module on behalf of whoever can afford the assembly. Land becomes an asset; a debt becomes tradable; an idea becomes a monopoly with a term. Each conversion looks, afterward, like something that was simply true about the thing. Each was drafted.

Her most instructive case study runs through a Manhattan courtroom. In 2001 Argentina defaulted on its sovereign debt. Most of its creditors eventually accepted restructured bonds at a steep discount; a few did not. Among the holdouts was NML Capital, a fund that had bought Argentine bonds at distressed prices and then litigated for payment in full. The bonds had been written under New York law, the choice-of-law module doing its quiet work decades in advance, back when the clause had seemed a boilerplate comfort to lenders and a formality to everyone else.

In the early 2010s, in the Southern District of New York, the litigation found its lever in two Latin words. The bonds promised creditors treatment *pari passu*, on equal footing, and the court read the promise to mean that Argentina could not pay the creditors who had accepted the restructuring without also paying the holdouts in full. Payment channels froze. A sovereign nation found its obligations to tens of thousands of cooperating bondholders pinned in place by the interaction of an antique clause and a forum selected before some of the litigants were born.

In Pistor's telling, the episode is neither a story of judicial overreach nor a story of rapacious creditors. It is a story of a module performing exactly as engineered, in circumstances its engineers never imagined. The clause did what clauses do. The forum did what forums do. The outcome belonged to no one, which is what makes it worth staring at: the architecture produced a result that none of the original parties had intended or foreseen, and the architecture was the only author available.

What the map provides is an address. The separations the other three books describe at the level of sectors and national accounts happen, in the first instance, in documents: in the clause, the filing, the security agreement, the choice of forum. Pistor's legal-architecture diagnosis names the institutional location at which any instrument of accountability, whoever might someday build one, would have to attach. The modules that code an asset's privileges could in principle carry its obligations too; nothing in the coding runs in only one direction. The book does not pursue that thought, and was not written to; its work is the map, and the map is the precondition for everything else.

One tradition deserves naming here because it approaches the same question from below. Elinor Ostrom, in *Governing the Commons* (1990), documented communities that have governed shared fisheries and pastures and irrigation works for generations without much help from either state or market; her design principles assume a community that knows itself as one, neighbors with names for each other. The cost-bearers that coded capital produces are something else: strangers dispersed across jurisdictions and generations who will never meet and do not know they are members of anything. Ostrom mapped what self-governance can carry and marked its boundary honestly. The engineered, dispersed commons of modern capital lies past that boundary, in territory her cases were never asked to govern.

In the autumn of 2009, with the wreckage of the financial crisis still smoking, the chief executive of Goldman Sachs told a Sunday Times interviewer that his bank was doing God's work. The remark was received as arrogance, which let everyone off easily. Mariana Mazzucato holds it up to a colder light. Her subject in *The Value of Everything* is how such a sentence becomes sayable at all: by what accounting a firm at the center of a catastrophe could describe its activity, without embarrassment, as creation.

Her answer runs through the history of a line economists once drew with care and have since mislaid. The classical economists kept a production boundary: a working distinction between activity that creates value and activity that merely moves it around or collects tolls on its movement. Rent, in that older vocabulary, was income without creation. The marginalist revolution dissolved the boundary's independent footing. Value became whatever a buyer would pay; whatever fetches a price is, by construction, productive. From inside that definition, extraction is invisible. If the fee was paid, the service was worth it. If the price cleared, the price was right.

Against this she sets a definition with edges. Value extraction, in her published usage, means "activities focused on moving around existing resources and outputs, and gaining disproportionately from the ensuing trade." The definition would be merely polemical if the book did not then do the patient work of showing where the gaining-disproportionately lives, and the case material is where the book earns its keep.

Take the pill. In December 2013, Gilead Sciences brought to market a drug called Sovaldi, the first of a class that cures hepatitis C outright. The American launch price, as Mazzucato cites it, was $84,000 for a twelve-week course: $1,000 per pill, one pill each morning, a tablet the size of an aspirin priced like a used car. The figures sit in the public record independently of her; a Senate Finance Committee investigation took up the launch pricing in 2015. What a cure costs to the person who needs one is not an abstraction at that price. It is an actuarial decision made about a body, one body at a time.

What the price did not reflect, on Mazzucato's accounting, is the genealogy of the science. Basic research funded by the National Institutes of Health came first; then the essential development work at Pharmasset, a small biotechnology firm sustained in part by federal support; then Gilead's acquisition of Pharmasset; then the launch price. Gilead broke no law. Nothing in the sequence required villainy. The architecture permitted the price, and the price was charged, and the public that had funded the early decades of the science paid once in taxes and again at the counter.

Or take the fee. Mazzucato walks through the fee structure of asset management with the patience of someone showing how a card trick is done. The standard arrangement is two-and-twenty: 2 percent of assets under management, charged every year, in good years and bad, and 20 percent of any returns above an agreed threshold. The 2 percent does not depend on performance. It depends on the assets sitting still and being counted. By her accounting, the arrangement, compounded across the recent decades, has moved hundreds of billions of dollars from savers to managers; and the national accounts record the transfer, every year, as the value added of financial services.

The trick is in the booking. An economy that cannot distinguish making from taking will reward taking at whatever rate taking can negotiate for itself, and will report the proceeds as growth. This is the platform on which any cumulative reading of these four books has to stand, and Mazzucato built it. She shows that the modern accounts cannot tell making from taking; the question is then how to operationalize the corrective her diagnosis demands, and that question is larger than any single sector she audits. Her accounting is sectoral: pharmaceutical pricing, asset management, the financialization of the firm. The separation it documents happens transaction by transaction, which is a scale her ledgers point toward and do not pretend to reach.

The turbines off the Danish coast were not put there by the price mechanism. Ørsted, the company that built much of Europe's early offshore-wind capacity, did its pioneering work as DONG Energy, owned by the Danish state, putting steel into the seabed in years when private capital regarded the whole proposition as a charity case. Brett Christophers opens his argument in that water, and the argument is the most uncomfortable of the four for anyone who has spent the past decade repeating that solar and wind are now cheap.

They are cheap. Christophers does not dispute the cost declines; he asks why cheapness has not done what cheapness was supposed to do. The assumption embedded in most climate policy is that once renewable electricity costs less than the alternatives, capital will flood in and the buildout will take care of itself. *The Price Is Wrong* is an extended demonstration that this assumption misunderstands how capital decides. His formulation is the book in seven words: "profit, not price, is what reshapes the world."

The demonstration runs through rooms most climate writing never enters: the investment committee, the fund board, the model with the hurdle rate at the top of the page. An asset manager weighing a wind farm is not asking whether the electricity will be cheap. The manager is asking whether the return will clear the rate the institution requires, and the rate the institution requires was calibrated, as Christophers shows, on the returns investors learned to expect in the period since 1980. A wind farm's revenues arrive at the mercy of weather and wholesale markets. Its returns, in his accounting, are modest and uncertain beside that calibration. Capital, asked to choose, chooses elsewhere, and the electricity stays unbuilt at any price.

No malice is needed at any point in the sequence. The pension manager who passes on the wind farm is performing fiduciary duty as the law defines it, on behalf of the teachers and nurses whose retirements the fund exists to protect. Duty discharged conscientiously, a thousand times over, adds up to a planet that does not get the grid it has already invented. The book's discipline is to keep the analysis at that level, where the failure is structural and the participants are doing their jobs.

Where the buildout has happened at speed, Christophers shows, a public balance sheet has usually been standing under it. Ørsted under Danish ownership. The public-finance vehicles of the German Energiewende. Spain's public-investment instruments of the late 2000s, which drew private capital into solar on public terms and were then, when the politics turned, withdrawn; the buildout stalled, and capital filed the lesson away. Public money built what fiduciary calibration would not touch, and public money proved revocable. Both halves of that sentence are load-bearing, and the second half is the one the energy transition has not yet metabolized.

None of this is offered as scandal, and it would miss the book to read it that way. Christophers's price-failure argument supplies the mechanism-level evidence that any constructive correction would have to answer: profit calibrations, not price signals, govern what gets built, and the calibrations are institutional artifacts with histories, which means they are in principle revisable artifacts. Read beside Pistor and Mazzucato, the argument widens past energy. The same calibrations that decide which turbines get built decide, everywhere, which costs get counted and which get left on the southern shore. No one enforces the separation of who captures value from who bears cost. The arithmetic enforces it, in good faith, inside institutions doing exactly what they were designed to do.

In the early 1930s, a young economist named Simon Kuznets was set the task of counting the income of the United States, which in the fourth year of the Depression did not know how much poorer it had become. The report he delivered to the Senate in 1934 gave the country its first systematic national income accounts, and gave the world an early version of the number it now watches the way sailors watch barometers. Daniel Susskind opens the history there because the number's later career was not inevitable, and Kuznets himself warned in the report that a nation's welfare could scarcely be inferred from a measure of its income.

The warning did not survive contact with the uses the number was wanted for. War planning in the 1940s consolidated the accounts; what had been a measurement became an instrument of mobilization. In the 1950s, Robert Solow decomposed American growth and found that most of it could not be attributed to added labor or capital at all; the unexplained remainder, the residual, was christened technical progress, and the pursuit of it became a profession. By the 1960s, in Susskind's telling, growth had completed its ascent from measurement to objective, the thing governments were understood to be for. No parliament voted on the promotion. It happened the way deep assumptions happen, by accretion, until the metric was the horizon.

Susskind refuses the easy verdict on what the metric delivered, and the refusal is what makes the book serious. From 1945 to the early 1970s, living standards across the industrialized world rose at a pace no previous generation had experienced. By the World Bank's poverty series, more than a billion people in East Asia moved out of extreme poverty between 1980 and 2020. Lives lengthened. Childhoods stopped being spent in fields and mills. Anyone who writes growth off as ideology has to explain those decades away, and Susskind declines to.

Then he opens the other column. The climate bill, now arriving. Inequality at widths the metric was never asked to register. Measured wellbeing flat against rising income in country after country. And a quieter entry he calls meaning: what work is for, what places are for, the goods that appear in no account because no transaction marks their loss. The ledger has two sides, and the book's claim is that the metric was built to see only one of them.

His answer is redirection rather than abandonment, and he is unsparing about the alternative. A deliberate contraction would send its bill, he argues, first to the people least able to refuse it, which is the same delivery route every unpriced cost has always taken. Growth is to be steered, by prices and laws and the alternative measures his final chapters survey, toward the things worth growing. Nothing in the other three books argues otherwise; their quarrels are with the counting, not with the growing. Susskind's growth-redirection is the macro analog of a redirection that would have to happen far below the level of the national accounts, case by case, at the scale where Pistor's clauses and Christophers's calibrations actually operate. He redirects the objective. The objective's machinery lives downstairs.

Set side by side, the four books stop reading as four arguments. Pistor supplies the location: the legal modules where capital's privileges are coded. Mazzucato supplies the activity: extraction booked as creation. Christophers supplies the mechanism: profit calibrations deciding what is built and what is left. Susskind supplies the metric: the accounts inside which all of it nets out as growth. One diagnosis, four floors of the same building.

Together they describe a single arrangement: value captured at one address, costs delivered to another, and no instrument anywhere along the route requiring the two to meet. The arrangement needs no defenders because it does not look like an arrangement. It looks like the absence of one. It looks like prices, like fiduciary duty, like boilerplate, like growth. That is what the quartet, cumulatively, makes impossible to unsee: the natural-looking surface is drafted, calibrated, booked, and measured into place, and each book hands the reader one of the four tools.

Something like this moment has happened before. In the mid-century pivot of 1936 to 1948, the terms of the postwar political economy were set by books written under pressure: Keynes's *General Theory* in February 1936, then Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* and Hayek's *The Road to Serfdom*, both in 1944. The books disagreed about nearly everything except the scale of what had to be rethought, and the institutions that followed were built inside the space their disagreement defined. Read the quartet under review against that precedent and the resemblance is not in stature, which no one can assign in advance, and not in any shared program, which the four do not have. The resemblance is in the shape of the moment: diagnosis accumulating across hostile disciplinary borders, faster than any instrument is being built to answer it.

Polanyi stands nearest of the ancestors. *The Great Transformation* described market society disembedding itself from the social fabric that had contained it, and named the double movement, the counter-motion by which society protects itself from being priced to death. Polanyi gave the counter-movement its name; he did not give it an instrument. Eighty years on, the four books under review inherit exactly that estate: richer diagnosis, same empty toolbox.

David Harvey has worked the same territory under the name accumulation by dispossession, most directly in *The New Imperialism* (2003), and the kinship is real. The difference is register. Harvey's account runs on a project and a class to drive it. The cases assembled across these four books require neither: a boilerplate clause, a launch price, a hurdle rate, an accounting convention, each operated in good conscience. The quartet's force is that its mechanism runs without a dispossessor in sight, which is also why the mechanism survives every change of management.

Ostrom, met earlier, holds one boundary of this territory: what communities can govern for themselves, and where the governed commons ends. Kate Raworth's *Doughnut Economics* (2017) and Herman Daly's steady-state economics hold another, the ecological frame within which Susskind's redirection has somewhere to go. The four books sit inside a lineage they largely do not cite as one, and the best of what they do extends it.

And then there is what the lineage, including its four newest members, leaves standing open. The question the quartet cumulatively puts is not whether the extracting and the building and the growing should have happened. It is whether the people and places that bore the costs should have been made whole, and by what instrument. The four books assemble that question with a completeness none of them could manage alone. The assembling is the achievement. The question is the residue.

Two observations sit in the negative space the quartet outlines. First: nowhere in the four books, and nowhere in the law or market practice they survey, does there exist an instrument that prices the separation at the transaction where it happens, at the lease, the clause, the launch price, the hurdle rate. Liability arrives after harm, sometimes, on the lucky shore. Regulation arrives generally, by category, and lobbying arrives with it. Nothing attaches at the event. Second: each of the four implies, without quite saying, that the future is a counterparty to the present's transactions, the party on whom the longest costs are served. No architecture any of them examines gives that counterparty standing. The intergenerational gap is named everywhere by implication and instrumented by none, anywhere, yet.

What would an accounting equal to the diagnosis have to do? It would have to price the separation where it occurs, transaction by transaction, at the scale of the lease and the loan rather than the scale of the economy. It would have to attach at the location Pistor names, since the modules that code an asset's privileges are the natural place for its obligations to ride. It would have to meet the calibrations Christophers describes on their own ground, as enforceable terms in the arithmetic rather than exhortations outside it. And it would have to do for the single extraction event what Susskind proposes for the aggregate: count honestly first, and let redirection follow the count.

The materials are not nowhere. The reparations economists, William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen most systematically in *From Here to Equality* (2020), with Darrick Hamilton, Ta-Nehisi Coates, and Dalton Conley working adjacent ground, have built the most developed method in the literature for pricing harm backward across generations: evidence standards, valuation discipline, the rigor of an actual ledger. The civic-republican tradition, Philip Pettit's *Republicanism* (1997) and Quentin Skinner's histories behind it, names the underlying wrong with precision: to be subject to costs one cannot refuse is domination whatever the intentions of those imposing them, and accountability of this kind is non-domination made institutional. Public choice economics reads the same architecture from the other shore of politics: what Buchanan and Tullock called rent-seeking is the shaping of rules for private capture, and on the structural question that tradition sits on the same side as every book reviewed here. And John Hartwick formulated in 1977 the rule that bears his name, that the rents from non-renewable extraction be reinvested for the generations that inherit the depletion; the rule has been operationalized in the Norwegian sovereign-wealth architecture and in the bonded-coverage mechanisms used in the U.S. coal and offshore-energy industries. Pieces, scattered across traditions that rarely meet. No one has joined them at the scale of the event.

Whatever joined them would be structural rather than partisan, and the construction would hold from whichever direction it was reached: regulatory caution, market discipline, worker and citizen solidarity. Each of those routes has its own reasons to want costs borne by the parties who create them. None of them owns the question, which is precisely what the question has going for it.

The oil will come again. Somewhere there is a coastline that has not yet had its season, marsh grass and barrier sand and a harbor where the boats go out before light, and one morning the water will bring it something the price of the cargo never carried. Nothing about that morning will be decided on the water. It will have been decided onshore, years earlier, in documents: in whether a clause was drafted to meet it, whether a calibration had been made to answer for it, whether the accounts had a column ready to receive it, whether anyone had counted the people on the second shore.

The four books in this set have made the difference between the two shores legible from four directions at once, which no one of them, and no book before them, had done. They have named the architecture, and named what the architecture leaves behind. The naming is the part that books can do. Between the naming and the next tide lies the building, and the building has not begun.
