## §2. Editorial-brain map for NYRB review-essay specifically

**What NYRB editors want (distinct from Boston Review, Aeon, Atlantic Ideas, Harper's, $100 Barrel/Phenomenal World):**

- **Intellectual synthesis across multiple books.** NYRB review-essays are not catalogue-reviews; they are essays that use the reviewed books as the occasion to think through a larger question the books cumulatively raise. The reviewer's analytical position emerges through the reading — not declared in opening throat-clearing — and the four reviewed books cohere into a single intellectual landscape the essay surveys.
- **Reviewer-as-mind-of-the-essay.** The reviewer's authority comes from depth of engagement, not from apparatus-display. NYRB readers expect the reviewer to have intellectual depth that informs the synthesis — that depth IS what makes the review distinctive — but the depth shows up as reading-acuity (specific phrasings; close-textual engagement with the reviewed books; sentence-level attention to what each author actually argues), not as authorial-system-construction or institutional-credential display.
- **Negative-space construction.** Per Stage 0 §3.2 Calibration 1 — the essay constructs the "what's missing" punchline as a negative-space observation **across all four books cumulatively** (no one has yet operationalized the corrective at the per-extraction-event mechanism level) rather than as a positive-naming move. The reader infers that someone could build the corrective; the essay does not name the builder or the apparatus. **This is the discipline that holds reveal at concept-only.**
- **Reviewer-not-architect register.** Per Stage 0 §3.2 Calibration 2 — the essay's voice is reviewer-synthesizing-across-four-books, NOT framework-architect-summarizing-his-own-work. The first-person reviewer "I" or "the reviewer" or third-person impersonal essayistic register all work; what does NOT work is the author-of-an-architecture register that gestures at the architecture as if the reader should infer it.
- **Long-arc literary-critical voice.** NYRB essays carry the literary-essay tradition that publishes Joan Didion + Susan Sontag + Robert Hughes + Helen Vendler + Adam Shatz + Sue Halpern. Sentence-level care; varied rhythm; intelligence felt rather than announced; selective named-tradition deployment.
- **Cross-political-spectrum register.** NYRB is read across left + center + moderate-right by trade-nonfiction intellectuals. The essay's structural analysis must hold across this readership without slipping into left-coded register-by-association. Stern-Nordhaus is "the existing intellectual battleground," not a side-taking exercise. The framework's non-partisan punchline is FOREGROUNDED over any of the four reviewed authors' partisan-codable positions (per Condition NR-1 + Ch 2 umbrella-mechanism defense cross-partisan punchline).
- **Constructive close without manifesto.** NYRB review-essays often end with a gesture toward "what would be required" — but the gesture is at the level of observation, not call-to-action. "Manifesto register" is the load-bearing tell of an essay that has overreached. The essay's close gestures toward the negative-space "what no one has yet built across these four diagnoses cumulatively" without naming what the builder would build.

**What hurts NYRB submissions specifically:**

- **Catalogue-review register.** "Book 1 argues X. Book 2 argues Y. Book 3 argues Z. Book 4 argues W. These four arguments together suggest..." — NYRB editors reject this register on read-1. The reviewed books must cohere into a synthesis that emerges through the reading, not into a paragraph-per-book enumeration.
- **Apparatus jargon in body prose.** Greek letters, subscripts, formal three-component breakdowns, named-instrument acronyms — NYRB reader bails immediately. The full apparatus-exclusion list (see §8) is load-bearing.
- **Author-of-framework register slippage.** Phrases like "the framework's response is..." / "the framework shows that..." / "a more complete accounting would..." — these read as architect-of-the-system register, which the essay specifically EXCLUDES per Stage 0 §3.2 Calibration 2. The reviewer-not-architect discipline is load-bearing.
- **Op-ed throat-clearing.** "In our current political moment, the question of how we account for industrial extraction has become..." — NYRB essays do not begin this way. Begin in idea, scene, or close-textual engagement.
- **Manifesto register.** "It is time to..." / "We must..." / "The future demands..." — NYRB reads manifesto as evidence of weak argument.
- **Three-in-a-row declarative LLM cadence.** Particularly dangerous in long-form review-essay; cumulative cadence over 7,000+ words flattens. Vary sentence length aggressively.
- **Em-dash density.** Per [`feedback_em_dash_overuse.md`](../memory/feedback_em_dash_overuse.md) RATIFIED 2026-05-21: em-dashes require active justification; default to commas / periods / restructure for smoother prose.
- **Self-narration / meta-commentary.** "What the synthesis shows is..." / "It bears noting that..." / "What this means is..." Drop entirely.
- **Rule-of-three closes.** Vary rhythm at section closes; the rule-of-three is the load-bearing LLM-cadence-tell at this length.
- **Defensive author-register.** Long preemptive-rebuttal-of-objections passages. NYRB essays trust the reader; objection-handling happens briefly inside the prose's natural argumentative motion.
- **Critique-register Register B drift.** Per Condition NR-2: "Mazzucato shows X but stops short of Y" / "Pistor names the modules but doesn't price" / "Christophers names the failure but doesn't fix" / "Susskind names the metric but doesn't operationalize" — ALL BANNED phrase-patterns. Drift to this register forecloses blurb paths and reads as superior-judgment-of-the-reviewer over the reviewed authors. See §10 for the full discipline.
