# The Architecture and Its Residue

*A review of:*
*Katharina Pistor,* The Code of Capital: How the Law Creates Wealth and Inequality *(Princeton University Press, 2019)*
*Mariana Mazzucato,* The Value of Everything: Making and Taking in the Global Economy *(PublicAffairs, 2018)*
*Brett Christophers,* The Price Is Wrong: Why Capitalism Won't Save the Planet *(Verso, 2024)*
*Daniel Susskind,* Growth: A Reckoning *(Allen Lane / Belknap Press of Harvard University Press, 2024)*

On the last page of most commercial contracts there is a paragraph that almost no party to the contract reads with attention. The paragraph specifies which jurisdiction's courts will adjudicate any dispute arising under the agreement, and which body of law that adjudication will apply. It is often the work of a junior associate at a major commercial law firm, drawn from a short list of options the firm uses repeatedly, accepted by counterparties whose principals have already moved on to other questions. It looks, to the people signing, like housekeeping. It is, in practice, the moment at which the architecture of the deal is decided. The price that will eventually be paid for the deal's failure was set on the page no one read.

The clause is one of six legal instruments by which assets are made into capital and capital is made into a thing the law will enforce. The others are property, collateral, trust, the corporate form, and bankruptcy. Each instrument has a long and defensible jurisprudential history. Each was developed for purposes of social order, predictability, and exchange. Each operates, on inspection, as a kind of code that the courts will read and enforce when called upon. The combination of the six, applied to a given asset by lawyers who understand what they are doing, returns the legal properties the system will treat as constituting capital: priority over competing claims; durability across the passage of ownership and time; universality across jurisdictions; convertibility into legal-tender money when the holder requires liquidity. The combination, in other words, is the engineering by which the inequality of outcome is produced and the architecture by which the production is rendered invisible to the parties who bear the cost.

Four recent books, taken together, have made it newly difficult to ignore this architecture. The four are not in conversation with one another. They were written separately, by scholars trained in different traditions, addressed to overlapping but distinct readerships. Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* (2019) describes the legal coding directly, with the granularity of the legal scholar who has read the contracts. Mariana Mazzucato's *The Value of Everything* (2018) describes the operation the coded architecture supports, distinguishing what she calls the creation of value from what she calls its extraction, and arguing that the contemporary national accounts have lost the ability to tell one from the other. Brett Christophers's *The Price Is Wrong* (2024) describes the pricing mechanism the architecture relies upon, and the conditions under which the mechanism fails to deliver the public goods it nominally calls for. Daniel Susskind's *Growth: A Reckoning* (2024) describes the metric against which the entire arrangement has been judged for two centuries, and proposes the redirection that would, at the macro level, do what an honest pricing at the per-transaction level would do at the granular scale.

Each book is consequential on its own terms. Read together, in the order in which their respective questions present themselves, the four sketch the shape of an architecture the standard literature has lacked the vocabulary to name. The shape is engineered. It has been engineered for a long time. It can be engineered differently. The work of the reading that follows is to set out, in the form the four books cumulatively make available, what such a re-engineering would have to attend to and where, institutionally, the attention would have to attach.

The question the four books pose in common is the question of what kind of accounting would make legible, at the scale of the activity, what current accounting is structurally unable to register. The question is older than any of these four books. Karl Polanyi raised it in 1944, in the language of disembedded markets and the fictitious commodities of land, labor, and money. David Harvey has raised it across three decades, in the language of accumulation by dispossession. Elinor Ostrom raised it across a lifetime of work on the commons, asking under what conditions collective resources can be managed without enclosure. Kate Raworth has raised it more recently, in the language of doughnut economics; Herman Daly raised it earlier, in the language of steady-state economics. The four books reviewed here are descendants of those lineages. They extend the diagnostic vocabulary into the contemporary cases the past two decades of legal scholarship, financial-economic data, and political-economic case studies have made available. The lineage is honored not by paraphrase but by the analytical specificity each of the four books achieves on a particular face of the architecture the older traditions named in larger gestures.

The reading begins with Pistor, whose book makes the engineering most visible.

Pistor's central proposition is at once austere and radical. Capital is not a natural category. It is a legal artifact. The line between assets that count as capital, with the legal protections and economic privileges that designation confers, and the much larger universe of assets that do not, is a line drawn by lawyers, refined across centuries of jurisprudence, and maintained by a transnational infrastructure of private legal practice that operates with very little democratic oversight. "Capital," she writes, "is coded in law." The lawyers who do the coding are, in her account, the chief architects of the inequalities the economic literature has been content to attribute to luck, talent, market discipline, or the unfathomable workings of price.

The argument proceeds through a careful anatomy of the six legal modules. Each is independently legitimate. Each has a long and defensible jurisprudential history. Each was developed for purposes of social order, predictability, and exchange. Together they constitute, as Pistor borrows the term from computer science, a code: a layered set of instructions that, when applied to an asset, returns the properties the legal system will then enforce. Priority moves the coded asset ahead of competing claims in distributions. Durability survives the coded asset across changes in ownership and the passage of generations. Universality recognizes the coded asset across jurisdictions, not only in the venue where it was created. Convertibility converts the coded asset into legal-tender money when the holder requires liquidity, even when the underlying object would not naturally yield such a conversion. An asset with all four properties is capital. An asset lacking one or more is, by degrees, less so.

The reach of the diagnosis lies in what the coding has been applied to over the past four decades. Subprime mortgages were coded as capital and bundled into securities whose priority claims against the resulting cash flows were defined by the coding rather than by the underlying soundness of the loans. Patents on pharmaceutical molecules whose research had been substantially funded by the public were coded as private capital with durability across generations of public benefit. Sovereign-debt instruments were coded with universality such that the same paper, sold under the laws of distant jurisdictions, became enforceable against the issuing nation through those same distant courts. The *NML Capital v. Argentina* litigation that ran through the United States federal courts in the years after the 2001 Argentine sovereign default is one of Pistor's most legible cases. Argentine sovereign bonds issued under New York law contained a clause providing that the bonds would be treated *pari passu*, on equal footing, with one another. When Argentina restructured most of its debt at substantial haircuts, a small group of holdout creditors who had acquired the paper at deep discount declined the restructuring and sued in the Southern District of New York to enforce the *pari passu* clause. The litigation eventually produced an order that effectively prevented Argentina from paying the restructured bondholders until it had also paid the holdouts in full, on terms that recovered something close to face value for paper bought at fractional cost. Argentine fiscal policy on a different continent, and the welfare of the citizens whose taxes would service the debt, were governed by the operation of a clause selected, as a matter of contract drafting, in a private law office in another hemisphere.

Trusts were coded such that family wealth could be moved offshore and preserved across centuries while the political-economic activity that generated the wealth stayed onshore and was taxed there. The corporate form was refined such that a parent company could insulate itself from the obligations of its subsidiaries; subsidiaries could be designed for disposability when liabilities matured. Bankruptcy was structured such that financial claims survived the discharge of operational ones, with the result that the residual cost of decades of activity could be left to local public-health systems, federal cleanup funds, and the affected communities. Each of these modules is independently legitimate. Each, taken in combination with the others and with the choice-of-law clause that places them under the protective jurisdiction the parties have selected, produces outcomes that no legal scholar attempting an account of the individual module could have predicted.

The book's strongest pages are those that show how each module, taken in isolation, looks like a refinement of legal craft and, taken in combination, produces outcomes the legal craft cannot itself address. Pistor's treatment of the trust is exemplary. She traces the medieval origins of the trust through its early-modern English refinement, through its nineteenth-century commercial expansion, to its twentieth-century redeployment as the instrument of choice for cross-jurisdictional wealth preservation. The trust permits the legal separation of an asset's ownership from its beneficial enjoyment. It permits the holding of assets across generations under conditions specified by the original settlor. With the addition of choice-of-law clauses pointing to jurisdictions hospitable to the form, it permits the moving of those assets beyond the practical reach of the political-economic systems that produced them. The combined effect, documented with patient attention to the case material, is a system in which the descendants of the original wealth-generators continue to enjoy the returns to the assets while the political-economic systems in which the original generation took place have lost their ability to draw on those returns through taxation, regulation, or redistribution. The trust, like the choice-of-law clause, is independently defensible. The combination, scaled across generations and across jurisdictions, is the architecture by which intergenerational wealth concentration has become structurally insulated from the political processes that might, in another configuration, have addressed it.

What Pistor's diagnosis names, in language a reader who is not a lawyer can follow, is the institutional location at which any architecture of accountability would attach. The modules are the place where the work would be done. Property law would have to be reformed if the unprotected interests of those without title were to find a hearing. Collateral law would have to be reformed if the priority of secured creditors over unsecured cost-bearers were to be reconsidered. The trust would have to be reformed if intergenerational wealth was to be subject to intergenerational accounting. The corporate form would have to be reformed if liability was to follow the corporate person across reorganizations. Bankruptcy would have to be reformed if operational obligations were not to be discharged by financial maneuvers. Choice-of-law would have to be reformed if jurisdictions could no longer be selected for their cost-allocation indulgences. Pistor herself does not propose this list. She writes as a diagnostician, with the reserve of the legal scholar who knows how much depends on the granularity of the prescription. The institutional surface on which the work of any redesign would be done is now in view.

A scope the book leaves to further work, which Pistor would be the first to acknowledge, is that the legal-modules account does not specify the political composition of the constituency whose interests an alternative architecture would be designed to protect. The longer literature on the commons, from Elinor Ostrom forward, has been careful to specify the conditions under which self-governance succeeds and the conditions under which it does not; among those conditions, the homogeneity of interest among stakeholders has emerged as load-bearing. Where the cost-bearers of a particular extraction are heterogeneous, with different exposures, different access to legal apparatus, and different political weight, the question of which alternative architecture to build, and on whose behalf, is itself a contested space. Pistor's modules supply the institutional location. They do not supply the political composition. Her book is honest about that scope. The reader who takes the diagnosis seriously must also take seriously the further work of constituency-building it makes legible.

What *The Code of Capital* leaves the reader with, then, is the architecture as engineered object. Once the building has been described in this way, the next question presents itself almost mechanically. Who has been describing the activity that the building was designed to make extractable, and from what conceptual vantage have they been describing it?

Mariana Mazzucato has been describing that activity for more than a decade, in a sequence of books that constitute one of the more consequential interventions in political economy of the period. *The Value of Everything* opens with the post-2008 spectacle of Goldman Sachs's then chief executive announcing, with no apparent irony, that his firm was doing God's work. The line provides the book's animating question. If the financial sector's contribution to the production of value is, as the firm's leadership claimed, theological in its magnitude, why is it so difficult to specify what the value actually consists in? And if the difficulty of specifying it is not an accident but is built into the way the national accounts are structured, what work is the accounting doing on behalf of the activity it cannot quite describe?

Mazzucato's answer threads through two centuries of economic thought. She begins with the physiocrats, who held that value derived from the land and only the land. She works through the classical economists, Smith and Ricardo and Marx, who in their different ways drew lines between productive and unproductive labor. She traces the marginalist revolution of the late nineteenth century, in which the line between productive and unproductive activity dissolved into the more abstract proposition that value is what someone is willing to pay for. The contemporary national-income accounts inherit the marginalist settlement. They register, as contributions to gross domestic product, whatever activity passes through markets at a price. The question of whether the activity actually produced anything is left to the market to answer, by the circular logic that if it did not produce anything, no one would have paid for it.

The book's most important move is the rehabilitation of the distinction between value creation and value extraction. Value creation, in Mazzucato's lexicon, refers to activity that adds to the underlying productive capacity of the economy: research, manufacture, infrastructure, education, care. Value extraction refers to "activities focused on moving around existing resources and outputs, and gaining disproportionately from the ensuing trade." The phrase is precise. It is also, by Mazzucato's own demonstration, no longer fringe. The financial sector's share of corporate profits across the past four decades, the pharmaceutical industry's pricing of molecules developed through publicly funded basic research, the asset managers' fee structures that compound regardless of fund performance: these are her cases, and they are not curiosities. They are, in her reading, the architecture of contemporary capitalism in its most consequential form.

The chapter on the pharmaceutical industry is among the book's sharpest. Mazzucato shows that the molecule which became Sovaldi, Gilead's hepatitis-C drug, was developed through a chain of basic research conducted under National Institutes of Health funding and small-biotech work supported substantially by federal grants. Gilead acquired the compound through a corporate transaction and priced the resulting drug at $84,000 per twelve-week course of treatment in the United States. The pricing was justified, in industry communications, as reflecting the costs of research and the risks of development. Mazzucato's accounting shows that the public's contribution to the underlying research considerably exceeded Gilead's, and that the pricing reflected not the cost of value creation but the firm's capacity to extract a return on a coded asset. The point is not that the pharmaceutical industry is uniquely venal. The point is that the conventional accounting of value, which counts what passes through markets and not what underlies the activity that produced the priced thing, cannot distinguish a value creator from a value extractor. Both register as contributing to GDP. The distinction is invisible to the system that is supposed to be measuring economic activity.

The same accounting blindness applies, Mazzucato argues, to the financial sector. The national accounts count financial intermediation as productive activity in proportion to the spread between borrowing and lending costs, the volume of transactions executed, and the fees collected. The accounting assumes that the activity is, by its presence in the market, value-creating. The assumption survives the financial crisis of 2008 with a striking inelasticity. The same firms whose value-extracting activity produced the crisis, by the conventional accounting, also recovered the largest share of value as the crisis was being resolved, by the same accounting. The accounting could not distinguish the rescue from the activity that required it.

The chapter on the asset-management industry is, on its own terms, one of the book's quieter performances and one of its more devastating. Mazzucato walks through the fee structures of the major institutional asset managers with the patience of someone showing the reader how a card trick is done. The standard two-and-twenty arrangement of the private-equity and hedge-fund industries, two percent of assets under management plus twenty percent of returns above a threshold, compounds across decades into a structural transfer from the institutional investors whose savings are being managed to the management firms whose fees are being collected. The compounded effect, across the larger institutional investors, runs to the hundreds of billions of dollars over the recent decades. The fees are justified, in industry communications, as the price of expert management of the underlying assets. Mazzucato's accounting shows that the long-run performance of actively managed funds, net of fees, is on average no better than the performance of low-fee index funds; the value-creation claim is, on the available evidence, difficult to substantiate. What the fee structure does substantiate is the asset managers' capacity to convert position in the financial system into a durable claim on the returns generated by the underlying productive economy. The pattern is what Mazzucato means by value extraction.

The broader argument about productive and unproductive activity is the kind of intellectual contribution that becomes invisible once it has been absorbed into common discourse. Before *The Value of Everything,* the public conversation about whether activities like high-frequency trading, leveraged corporate buyouts, or pharmaceutical price arbitrage actually produced anything was conducted, when it was conducted at all, in moral rather than analytical registers. After the book, the same conversation has access to a more precise vocabulary, in which the question of whether an activity creates new productive capacity or merely repositions claims against existing productive capacity is operationalizable in language a policy audience can follow. The political-economic literature on rentier capitalism, the policy literature on platform monopolies, the regulatory discussion of share buybacks and corporate financialization, all draw on a vocabulary the book substantially established. The accomplishment is the rehabilitation of a distinction that the marginalist tradition had quietly retired from serious analytical use.

Mazzucato shows, persuasively, that the architecture of value extraction is the operative reality of the contemporary economy and that the architecture of value creation is its rhetorical complement. The question her diagnosis raises is then how to operationalize the corrective her diagnosis demands. The book gestures at the answer in its closing chapters, which call for what Mazzucato has elsewhere termed a "mission economy": public investment organized around large-scale objectives, with the state in the role of risk-taker and value-creator that conventional accounting has erased. The mission-economy frame is generative. It has produced policy programs in several jurisdictions; it has informed the design of the European Union's Recovery and Resilience Facility, among other instruments. The operationalization of the corrective at the transaction level, the question of how a particular fee structure or pricing arrangement would be flagged as extractive rather than creative in real time, of how the legal modules Pistor names would be adjusted to make the distinction enforceable, is a further task the frame opens. The diagnostic vocabulary has been built. The instrumentation that would operationalize the diagnosis at the per-transaction mechanism level is a further task, and Mazzucato's diagnosis is what makes that task legible as the work it would be.

A further note about the contribution belongs at this point in the reading. The phrase "value extraction" has now become close to common currency in serious political-economic writing. It appears in academic literature on rentier capitalism, in policy reports on platform monopolies, in commentary on private equity. Its passage from specialist coinage to common reference is itself an event in intellectual history. The diagnostic frame Mazzucato put into circulation is now load-bearing in a way that few books published in the past decade can claim. Subsequent work, including the books under review here and the work to which they point, builds on a vocabulary that *The Value of Everything* substantially established. The acknowledgment matters because the cumulative architecture an honest accounting would require depends on a vocabulary it did not have to invent.

If Pistor names the institutional location at which any accountability architecture would attach, and Mazzucato names the activity such an architecture would distinguish from value creation, Brett Christophers names the mechanism through which the architecture would have to operate, and the mechanism's current failure.

*The Price Is Wrong* announces its argument in its title. The book takes the renewable-energy transition as the case in which the failure is most visibly costly. By every measure that matters to climate policy, the world is failing to make the transition at the pace climate physics requires. The technology is no longer the constraint: the levelized cost of energy from solar photovoltaics and onshore wind has fallen to or below the cost of fossil-fuel generation in most major markets, and continues to fall. The denial-and-political-will narratives, which have organized much climate commentary, are also no longer sufficient explanations. Most governments have adopted some version of a net-zero target. Most large financial institutions have made some version of a climate commitment. The public in most developed and many developing economies broadly favors faster transition. And yet investment in renewable generation continues to lag the pace required, by the most accommodating accounting, by a factor of three or four against what would be needed to bring the trajectory back into compatibility with the warming targets set in Paris.

Christophers's argument is that the explanatory locus is pricing. Not pricing in the sense of consumer cost of energy, where the renewable case is in fact strong. Pricing in the sense of the rate-of-return calculations that determine whether private capital will, in fact, allocate to renewable projects at the scale required. The asset-manager firms whose decisions are the proximate cause of how capital actually moves, Brookfield, BlackRock, Macquarie, KKR, the large institutional pools, are evaluating renewable projects against a hurdle rate set by their fiduciary obligations to investors and against the alternative returns available in fossil-fuel projects, in private equity, in real estate, in technology. The renewable projects in many cases do not clear the hurdle. They do not clear it not because the underlying economics is bad but because the structure of returns available in renewables differs from the structure of returns available in fossil-fuel investment in ways the rate-of-return framework was not designed to handle. Renewable projects have higher capital costs paid upfront, lower operating costs that compound over time, and a return profile that depends on the regulatory and grid-infrastructure environment in which the project sits. Fossil-fuel projects have more familiar return profiles to which institutional capital is accustomed. The familiarity is part of the pricing.

The book's central formulation, which has acquired the kind of currency a precise phrase earns when it captures a structural truth, is that "profit, not price, is what reshapes the world." The line is not a slogan, though it has been used as one. It is the conclusion of an extended analysis. Price, in Christophers's account, is the signal markets use to communicate information about scarcity and demand. Profit is the signal capital uses to decide where to allocate. The two are not the same. A market can price something correctly, in the sense of reflecting its underlying scarcity and demand, and capital can still decline to deliver what the price calls for, because the structure of profit available does not justify the deployment. This is the mechanism of the renewable shortfall. The price is right. The profit is wrong.

Christophers's case material is detailed and disciplined. He walks the reader through the rate-of-return mathematics that asset managers actually use, with the specificity of someone who has read the prospectuses and modeled the structures. He shows how the financial-derivatives markets price renewable revenue streams in ways that incorporate regulatory risk premia not faced by fossil-fuel incumbents. He documents the role of public-purpose capital allocation in jurisdictions that have made faster transition progress: the Danish offshore-wind buildout, the public investment vehicles that anchored the early Spanish solar expansion, the development-finance institutions that financed renewables in markets where private capital found the risk profile uncongenial. His conclusion is unsparing. Capitalism, as currently constituted, will not finance the climate transition at the pace required, because the price signal does not translate into the profit signal at the magnitude and on the timeline the physical problem demands. The required complement is some combination of public ownership, public investment, public-purpose capital allocation, and regulatory restructuring that aligns the private profit calculation with the social-cost calculation in ways the private profit calculation does not, on its own, do.

The chapter that develops the asset-manager argument at greatest depth is among the most carefully constructed in the recent political-economy literature. Christophers shows that the firms now controlling the largest pools of capital available for global energy investment are not, in any straightforward sense, investors in the underlying assets. They are managers of pooled capital deployed against a structure of fiduciary obligations to the underlying investors, who are themselves substantially institutional: pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign-wealth funds, family offices, university endowments. The asset managers are intermediaries. Their fiduciary obligation is to deliver risk-adjusted returns at the level the underlying investors expect, and the levels the underlying investors expect have been calibrated against the historical returns available across the post-1980 period in fossil-fuel investment, real estate, and the broader equity markets. Renewable-energy returns, in their current configuration, sit below those calibrations. The asset managers respond accordingly. Christophers's case material is not the case material of bad faith. It is the case material of an incentive structure operating exactly as designed.

The treatment of the development-finance institutions provides the necessary counterpoint. The Danish state's involvement in the early offshore-wind buildout, through public ownership of Ørsted (formerly DONG Energy) in the years when the technology's commercial viability was still being established, is one of the cases in which a non-market actor absorbed the early-stage risk that the private-capital pricing model would not absorb. The German Energiewende's public-finance vehicles played a similar role. The Spanish solar buildout of the late 2000s benefited from public-investment instruments that, until their politically motivated withdrawal, anchored the early commercial deployment. The pattern Christophers traces is consistent. The cases of faster transition are cases in which public-purpose capital was present at the scale required. The cases of slower transition are cases in which the private-capital pricing model has been allowed to govern the allocation. The implication is structural rather than ideological. Where the architecture of capital allocation has included a public-purpose-capital component sized to the magnitude of the social-cost externalities the private pricing fails to register, the transition has proceeded at a different pace than where the architecture has not.

What Christophers's diagnosis names, then, is the mechanism-level evidence the constructive move would correct. The accountability architecture an honest accounting requires would have to operate at the level of the pricing mechanism, not only at the level of the legal architecture that produces priced assets. Pricing the cost severance that Pistor's modules make possible would be one of the things the architecture would do. Christophers's argument is the demonstration that the failure of pricing to do this work is not an oversight but a structural feature of how capital, under contemporary fiduciary and regulatory conditions, evaluates returns. The argument is, in the most exact sense, the mechanism Mazzucato's diagnostic frame predicts. It is the place where the pricing of value extraction either is or is not made structurally visible. In Christophers's accounting, it currently is not.

The book is less interested in the politics of resistance than in the political economy of the structural condition itself. There is little manifesto in *The Price Is Wrong*; the analytical posture is closer to engineering than to advocacy. The pages on the asset-manager industry are particularly cold-eyed. The asset managers are not portrayed as villains but as agents responding rationally to the incentive structure they face. The question the book leaves open, and that the cumulative reading of these four books makes legible, is what an alternative structure would consist in. The Norwegian sovereign-wealth fund's allocation patterns, which Christophers references, are one example of an institutional alternative. The development-finance institutions are another. The public-ownership models in the European utilities are a third. None of these, taken alone, constitutes the corrective the diagnosis demands. Taken cumulatively, they constitute a body of practice from which a pricing architecture different from the current one might be built.

The remaining diagnostic vocabulary belongs to Daniel Susskind, whose book operates at a different scale.

*Growth: A Reckoning* takes as its subject what the other three books have been describing without quite naming directly: the metric against which the architecture has been judged for two centuries. Susskind opens with a historical observation that turns out to be load-bearing. Economic growth, as the concept now operates in political and policy discourse, is younger than is usually assumed. The systematic measurement of national income dates to the work of Simon Kuznets and the development of the national accounts in the 1930s and 1940s. The deliberate orientation of economic policy toward growth as such, the policy commitment that any year of growth is a successful year and any year of decline is a failed one, emerges in the 1950s and 1960s with the work of Robert Solow on the residual and the institutional consolidation of Cold War development economics. Susskind's first thesis is that an idea this recent, in operation for not yet a century at the level of governing policy, should not be received as a fact of nature.

The book's second thesis is that growth has done substantially what its defenders claim it has done. The acceleration in living standards across the developed world from 1945 to the early 1970s, the lifting of more than a billion people out of extreme poverty in East Asia between 1980 and 2020, the corresponding declines in child mortality and the increases in life expectancy, are real, and they are causally connected, in the political-economy literature Susskind reads carefully, to growth as conventionally measured. The book does not dismiss growth. The book's tone, throughout, is the opposite of dismissive. It is the tone of a careful accounting, in which the magnitudes on both sides of the ledger are taken seriously.

What growth has also done, as Susskind documents at length, is generate a class of costs the conventional accounting has been unable or unwilling to register. Climate is the most legible of these. The cumulative emissions trajectory associated with twentieth-century growth has loaded the atmosphere with carbon at a pace that exceeds the natural absorption rate, and the resulting warming is already producing costs that will compound across the coming century at a magnitude the growth-as-success accounting did not include. Inequality is a second. The distribution of growth's benefits, particularly in the developed economies since 1980, has been concentrated in the upper tail of the income distribution, with stagnant or declining real wages for the middle and lower portions of the distribution and a corresponding accumulation of political-economic disaffection. Wellbeing is a third. The empirical literature on subjective wellbeing, which Susskind reviews with appropriate caution, suggests that the relationship between aggregate growth and reported life satisfaction weakens or flattens at the income levels the developed economies have reached, and the most powerful correlates of life satisfaction in those economies are not income-related but relate to social connection, meaning, and security. Meaning is the fourth and most difficult to operationalize. The displacement of forms of work and community that organized lives across previous generations has produced what Susskind, drawing on a wide literature, calls a crisis of significance, in which the affluence growth has delivered does not deliver the kinds of structured engagement with the world that the previous economic order, for all its material limitations, produced as a byproduct.

The book's central move is the redirection rather than the abandonment of growth. Susskind is explicit about this. The post-growth and degrowth literatures, which take the disaffection with growth-as-such as license to argue for an economic order organized around a different aggregate, are insufficiently attentive, in his account, to the material conditions of the developing economies that have not yet completed the growth-driven transition out of subsistence-level poverty. The argument that growth must be abandoned in the developed economies sits awkwardly alongside the argument that growth must continue in the developing ones, and the awkwardness has not been resolved by the existing literature. Susskind's alternative is to redirect growth: to specify, with the technical apparatus the national accounts already have available, the kinds of growth that compound human flourishing and the kinds that compound the costs the conventional accounting fails to register, and to organize economic policy around the former rather than the latter.

The chapters on the costs of growth are carefully sequenced. Susskind takes climate first, with the technical literature on the social cost of carbon and the cumulative-emissions accounting that the post-Paris policy discussion has substantially established. He takes inequality second, with the work of Piketty and Saez and Zucman on the distribution of income and wealth in the developed economies, the work of Branko Milanović on the global distribution and the so-called elephant curve, and the work of Anne Case and Angus Deaton on the mortality consequences of stagnant or declining real wages in the United States. He takes wellbeing third, drawing on the empirical literature that includes Richard Layard's foundational work on the economics of happiness, Daniel Kahneman's distinction between experienced and remembered wellbeing, and the more recent work on the Easterlin paradox at the level of nations and across time. He takes meaning fourth, the most difficult to operationalize but, in his careful handling, perhaps the most analytically generative. The displacement of forms of work that organized lives across previous generations, the corresponding decline of the institutions (religious, civic, occupational) that supplied the structuring narratives within which a life made sense, the rise of what Susskind calls the significance gap, all are documented with empirical care and connected, through the political-economy literature, to the same growth-as-conventionally-measured that produced the material gains.

The redirection is not, in Susskind's hands, a vague aspiration. He proposes specific reforms to the measurement apparatus: revised national accounts that distinguish productive from extractive activity, subjective-wellbeing indices that complement income measures, satellite accounts for environmental and social externalities, intergenerational accounting that registers the present's claims on the future as the obligations they are. He proposes specific reforms to the policy apparatus: tax structures that price the extractive forms of growth more accurately, public investment in the forms of growth that compound human flourishing, regulatory restructurings that align corporate incentives with the redirection. The policy chapters are, in their way, the most ambitious of any of the four books under review. They constitute a serious attempt to specify what the redirection would look like at the level of governing instruments.

Susskind's growth-redirection is the macro analog of the per-extraction-event redirection the cumulative reading of the four books makes legible. Where Pistor names the legal location, Mazzucato the diagnostic frame, and Christophers the pricing mechanism, Susskind names the aggregate metric against which the activity has been judged and proposes the reorientation that would, at the macro level, do what an accountability architecture at the per-transaction level would do at the granular scale. The two scales are not in tension. They are complementary. A redirected national accounting at the growth level, combined with a redesigned pricing mechanism at the transaction level, a corrected diagnostic frame for value, and an adjusted institutional location at the legal-modules level, is what the cumulative diagnostic vocabulary the four books supply would, taken together, enable.

What the four books help to see is the shape of an architecture: how it operates, where it attaches institutionally, what activity it produces, how that activity is priced or fails to be priced, and what aggregate measurement it produces or fails to produce. They are, taken together, a more substantial cumulative diagnosis of contemporary political economy than any single one of them alone provides. Pistor's legal-modules account would be less consequential without Mazzucato's diagnostic vocabulary for the activity the modules enable. Mazzucato's vocabulary would be less analytically grounded without Pistor's specification of the legal architecture that produces the priced assets. Christophers's pricing analysis would be less compelling without Mazzucato's prior work on the distinction between value creation and value extraction. Susskind's growth-redirection argument would be less specifiable without Christophers's mechanism-level diagnosis of how prices currently fail to align private and public returns. The four books, read together, do for contemporary political economy something close to what the canonical works of the 1936 to 1948 mid-century pivot did for the previous mid-century moment: they make a vocabulary available without which the next analytical step cannot be taken.

The lineage in which the four books sit reaches back through several earlier traditions that deserve acknowledgment at this point in the reading. Karl Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* (1944) supplied the original vocabulary of disembedded markets and the social costs of their disembedding. Polanyi's distinction between embedded and disembedded economies, his account of the fictitious commodities of land, labor, and money, and his analysis of the protective counter-movements that arise when the disembedding becomes politically untenable are all in the analytical bloodstream of the four books reviewed here. David Harvey's work on accumulation by dispossession, developed across his books from the late 1990s into the 2010s, supplied the political-geographic analysis of how value extraction operates across spatial scales; Mazzucato's value-extraction vocabulary is in productive dialogue with Harvey's, though it operates in a different register and addresses a different readership. Elinor Ostrom's lifetime of work on the commons, culminating in *Governing the Commons* (1990) and the subsequent collaborative literature, supplied the institutional analysis of how collective resources are managed under conditions of heterogeneity, scale, and external pressure; the question of where Ostromian self-governance succeeds and where the conditions for its success do not hold is implicit across the cases the four books reviewed here describe. Kate Raworth's *Doughnut Economics* (2017) and Herman Daly's earlier work on steady-state economics supplied the ecological-economic frame within which Susskind's redirection argument is most coherent. Amartya Sen's *Development as Freedom* (1999) supplied the capabilities-rather-than-income vocabulary on which Susskind's wellbeing chapter substantially rests. The four books under review do not displace these earlier traditions. They extend them into the contemporary case material, with the additional analytical specificity that the past two decades of legal scholarship, financial-economic case studies, and political-economic data have made available.

What the four books do not do, taken together, is operationalize the corrective the cumulative diagnosis demands at the per-extraction-event mechanism level. The architecture they cumulatively describe is the architecture by which value extraction is enabled, value creation is undercounted, prices fail to align private and social returns, and growth is measured against a metric that hides the costs of the activity it registers. The architecture is engineered. It can be engineered differently. The four books name the location, the activity, the mechanism, and the metric. The instrumentation that would, at the level of the individual transaction, price the cost severance that the location enables and the activity produces and the mechanism fails to register and the metric obscures, is the work the four diagnoses cumulatively make legible and that the four books, by the structure of their respective inquiries, do not undertake.

Two negative-space observations sharpen this point. First, none of the four books offers a per-transaction pricing instrument designed to attach at the legal-modules level Pistor names. Each, in its own register, gestures at the institutional question of what would be required, but the gesture is at the level of policy aspiration, not at the level of instrument specification. Second, none of the four books offers a treatment of the intergenerational pricing question that connects the present extraction to the future cost-bearer in a way that is operationalizable at the level of the individual transaction. Susskind's intergenerational accounting is at the macro level. Christophers's pricing analysis is at the rate-of-return level for the present-time investor. Mazzucato's distinction between making and taking is a frame, not a metric. Pistor's legal-modules account is a diagnosis of architecture, not a prescription for redesign. The intergenerational pricing gap, the gap between what the present transaction pays and what the future cost-bearer will be made to absorb, is named, by implication, across all four books. It is not, in any of them, given a specific instrument.

The reader who has followed the cumulative diagnosis through the four books is now in a position to ask the obvious next question. If the architecture is engineered, and if the four books cumulatively name the location, the activity, the mechanism, and the metric of the engineered architecture, what would the redesign consist in? What would the instruments that priced the cost severance at the per-transaction level have to do, what would they have to attach to, and how would they have to be sized?

The instruments would have to attach at the legal-modules location Pistor names. The pricing they performed would have to distinguish, in real time, between value creation and value extraction as Mazzucato describes them. The mechanism by which they operated would have to correct the failure Christophers diagnoses, in which private rate-of-return calculations fail to deliver the public goods the price signal nominally calls for. The aggregate measurement to which they contributed would have to register, in the national accounts Susskind proposes to redirect, the kinds of activity that compound human flourishing and the kinds that compound cost severance. The instruments would, in other words, be the mechanism by which the four diagnoses are operationalized as a single architecture of accountability rather than as four independent observations.

What such an architecture would not consist in is equally specifiable. It would not consist in a single regulatory program imposed by central decree, because the diagnostic literature is clear that centrally imposed corrections to mechanism-level failures rarely survive contact with the granularity of the cases they are required to address. It would not consist in a market-based solution unanchored to public-interest instruments, because the diagnostic literature is also clear that markets, left to the rate-of-return calculations Christophers documents, do not deliver the corrections the diagnosis demands. It would not consist in an abandonment of growth as such, because Susskind's work is clear that the redirection rather than the abandonment is the available path. It would not consist in a reparations program limited to historical wrongs already in the political conversation, though the methodological apparatus developed by reparations economics is one of the available templates for the backward-looking instrument an architecture of accountability would also require.

The corrective the four diagnoses point toward is structural, not partisan. It does not depend on any particular electoral coalition. It does not depend on any particular ideological commitment beyond a willingness to take the diagnostic vocabulary the four books supply seriously as a description of how contemporary capitalism actually works. The architecture of legal modules Pistor describes is in operation regardless of which party holds office. The value-extraction activity Mazzucato documents continues across electoral cycles. The price-failure mechanism Christophers diagnoses is structural to the current configuration of capital markets. The growth metric Susskind proposes to redirect is the operative measure of policy success across the developed-economy political spectrum. A corrective architecture would be structural in the same way. It would hold whether the architecture were reached from regulatory caution about the costs of unaddressed extraction, from market discipline that priced what the current pricing mechanism fails to price, or from worker and citizen solidarity that demanded the cost-bearing side of the ledger be honored. The construction the diagnoses cumulatively make available does not depend on the route by which it is reached.

Such an architecture would not require new philosophical foundations. The intellectual foundations have been built. The reparations-economics tradition has developed the methodological apparatus for pricing backward-looking obligations across cohorts. The civic-republican tradition, from Pettit and Skinner back to the early-modern English writers and the Roman law before them, has developed the normative apparatus for thinking about freedom as non-domination, and the intergenerational application of that tradition is well established. The Public Choice tradition, from Buchanan and Tullock forward, has developed the complementary analysis of how political and regulatory institutions are themselves arenas in which concentrated interests can capture returns at the expense of dispersed ones; that tradition's diagnosis of rent-seeking as architecture-shaping is on the same side of the structural-mechanism question as the four books reviewed here, not against it. The Hartwick rule, formulated in 1977 in a paper about non-renewable resources and intergenerational equity, has been operationalized in the Norwegian sovereign-wealth architecture and in the bonded-coverage mechanisms used in the U.S. coal and offshore-energy industries. The institutional templates exist. The legal modules Pistor describes are precisely the modules through which any new instrument would be coded. The pricing mechanism Christophers analyzes is the mechanism into which any corrective price signal would have to be introduced. The diagnostic frame Mazzucato provides is the frame within which any corrective accounting would be legible. The national-account redirection Susskind proposes is the macro complement to any per-transaction redirection at the mechanism level. The intellectual work has been done, in pieces, across the books these four authors have written and the traditions on which they draw.

What remains is the assembly. The instruments would be conventional in their components. What is new is the willingness to assemble the components into an architecture. The conditions for that assembly, the political-economic conditions and the institutional conditions and the intellectual conditions, are now substantially in place. The diagnostic vocabulary is available, after these four books, in a way that it was not available a decade ago. The institutional templates have been operated at industrial scale across three decades. The legal modules have been refined for centuries. The reparations-economics methodology has been developed at the level of detail required for the backward-looking instrument. The civic-republican tradition has been developed at the level of philosophical detail required for the forward-looking instrument. What remains is the work of construction itself, the granular, jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction, case-by-case work of building the instruments and seating them in the architecture the four books describe.

The work is not, by its nature, completed in a single book or a single program. It is the work of several decades. The four books reviewed here are, properly understood, the predicate for that work, not the work itself. Their authors have made the diagnostic vocabulary available. The further task they cumulatively make legible is the construction of the architecture the diagnoses describe.

Return now to the paragraph on the last page of the contract with which the reading began. The clause is still there. The lawyers are still drafting it. The jurisdiction whose courts will adjudicate the future dispute is still being selected from the firm's short list. The signing parties are still treating the page as housekeeping. The price that will eventually be paid for the deal's failure is still being decided on the page that the signing parties are not reading. The fact of the architecture's contingency is what the four books cumulatively make impossible to overlook. The fact of its engineering is what makes its redesign conceivable. The next sovereign default that prices Argentine fiscal policy in a courtroom on another continent, the next pharmaceutical molecule priced for extraction rather than dissemination, the next renewable-energy project that fails to clear the asset-manager hurdle rate, the next family trust that survives the political-economic system that produced its wealth, will pass through the same modules, register the same value-extraction signature, fail to be priced by the same mechanism, and disappear into the same aggregate metric that hides its cost. The diagnostic vocabulary for naming what is being lost is now available. The architecture for ensuring that what is named is also paid for is the work the next decade's political economy will be required to do.

The four books under review do not name themselves as a quartet. They were written separately and arrived at the same conclusion from different directions. The conclusion is not, in any of them, stated as the conclusion of an architecture-of-accountability program. It is stated, more modestly, as the conclusion of an inquiry into a specific facet of contemporary political economy. Read together, what the four conclusions add up to is something larger than any of them: a cumulative diagnosis of the architecture by which contemporary capitalism produces the costs it does not pay for, and the location at which the work of redesign would have to begin. The shape of what is missing, after these four books, is no longer a matter of speculation. It is a matter of construction.
