# The Account That Will Not Close

*A review of:*
*Mariana Mazzucato,* The Value of Everything: Making and Taking in the Global Economy *(PublicAffairs, 2018)*
*Katharina Pistor,* The Code of Capital: How the Law Creates Wealth and Inequality *(Princeton University Press, 2019)*
*Brett Christophers,* The Price Is Wrong: Why Capitalism Won't Save the Planet *(Verso, 2024)*
*Daniel Susskind,* Growth: A Reckoning *(Allen Lane / Belknap Press of Harvard University Press, 2024)*

There is a single word that does most of the work in contemporary capitalism, and the word is *value*. Quarterly earnings calls report it; asset managers promise to deliver it; pension funds count it on behalf of their members; sovereign-wealth instruments are measured by how much of it they preserve and pass forward. The word is treated, in these settings, as if it referred to something objective: a quantity that exists in the world, that can be increased or decreased, and that markets reliably register through prices. To ask what the quantity actually consists in, or whose activity produced it, or whose obligations have been left unrecorded against it, is to step out of the standard registers of business reportage and into a different conversation entirely. That conversation has, until recently, been carried on at the edges of the discipline that has the word in its title.

It was not always so. The classical economists, Adam Smith and David Ricardo and Karl Marx in their different ways, distinguished productive from unproductive activity. They disagreed, sometimes violently, on the line: Smith drew it between labor that produced an embodied surplus and labor that did not; Ricardo placed it at the margin of the land's yield; Marx located it inside the wage relation and the appropriation of surplus value. But the line itself was load-bearing for political-economic thinking. An economy could be assessed by what proportion of its activity produced an enduring contribution to its productive capacity, and what proportion merely repositioned claims against existing capacity. The line could be drawn imperfectly. It was not, in the classical mind, dispensable.

The marginalist revolution of the late nineteenth century quietly dispensed with it. The new orthodoxy, formalized by Jevons and Walras and Marshall, replaced the productive-unproductive distinction with a simpler proposition: value is whatever someone is willing to pay for. The line between making and taking disappeared into the consumer's preferences. The national-income accounts that arose in the 1930s and 1940s inherited the simplification. Anything that passed through a market at a price was registered as a contribution to gross domestic product. Whether the priced activity actually produced something new, or merely transferred something old, was a question the accounts no longer asked, because the underlying theory had stopped asking it.

Four recent books, taken together, have changed what it is possible to say about that simplification without sounding eccentric. Mariana Mazzucato's *The Value of Everything* (2018) rehabilitates the productive-unproductive distinction the marginalist tradition retired and rebuilds it, with empirical precision, for the contemporary economy. Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* (2019) shows that what the market is pricing is itself the output of a legal infrastructure whose modules are designed by lawyers and whose operations have been substantially invisible to the public conversation about how wealth is created. Brett Christophers's *The Price Is Wrong* (2024) demonstrates that even where markets succeed in pricing an underlying physical reality correctly, the structure of returns the same markets deliver to private capital does not align private incentive with public good, with the climate transition as its showcase. Daniel Susskind's *Growth: A Reckoning* (2024) returns to the aggregate, the metric of economic growth itself, and asks what the metric measures and what it has been allowed to leave out.

The four books were not in conversation when they were written. Their authors are trained in different disciplines: Mazzucato as an economist at University College London; Pistor as a legal scholar at Columbia Law School; Christophers as a geographer at Uppsala; Susskind as an economist at Oxford. The methodologies vary, the political registers vary along with them, and the readerships overlap only partially. Read together, the four books rhyme. Each names, from a different angle, the same underlying structure: a systematic separation between those who capture the value a transaction produces and those who bear the costs the transaction also produces, between what the accounts register and what the accounts cannot or will not see. The cumulative shape of what the four authors collectively make visible, considered as the description of a single phenomenon rather than four independent inquiries, is the work the reading that follows is meant to undertake.

The reading begins with Mazzucato, whose argument is the one that re-opens the question the marginalist tradition closed.

*The Value of Everything* opens with a scene from 2009. The chief executive of Goldman Sachs, called to testify before Congress in the aftermath of the financial crisis his firm had substantially contributed to, observed in an unguarded moment that the firm was doing God's work. The line has been quoted many times; it provides the book's animating question. If the financial sector's contribution to value creation is, as the firm's leadership claimed, theological in magnitude, why is it so difficult to specify what that contribution actually consists in? And if the difficulty of specifying it is not an accident but a structural feature of how the national accounts have been built, what work is the accounting doing on behalf of the activity it cannot quite describe?

Mazzucato's answer threads through two centuries of economic thought. She works through the physiocrats, who located value in the land. She traces Smith's distinction between productive and unproductive labor, and Ricardo's refinement of it at the margin. She follows Marx into the wage relation and the production of surplus value. She watches the marginalist revolution dissolve the line, and she follows the post-marginalist tradition into the contemporary national accounts that inherit the dissolution. Then she rebuilds the distinction for the contemporary case. Value creation, in her rebuilding, refers to activity that adds to the underlying productive capacity of the economy: research, manufacture, infrastructure, education, care. Value extraction, by contrast, refers to "activities focused on moving around existing resources and outputs, and gaining disproportionately from the ensuing trade." The phrase is precise. It is also, by the book's careful empirical demonstration, no longer fringe. The financial sector's outsized share of corporate profits across the past four decades; the pharmaceutical industry's pricing of molecules whose underlying research was substantially publicly funded; the asset managers' fee structures that compound regardless of fund performance: these are her cases, and they are not curiosities. They are the architecture of contemporary capitalism in its most consequential form.

The pharmaceutical chapter is among the book's sharpest. Mazzucato shows that the molecule that became Sovaldi, Gilead's hepatitis-C drug, was developed through a chain of basic research conducted under National Institutes of Health funding and small-biotech work supported substantially by federal grants, then acquired by Gilead through a corporate transaction, then priced at $84,000 per twelve-week course of treatment in the United States. The pricing was justified, in industry communications, as reflecting research costs and development risks. Mazzucato's accounting demonstrates that the public's contribution to the underlying research considerably exceeded Gilead's, and that the pricing reflected not the cost of value creation but the firm's capacity to extract a return on a coded asset. The point is not that the pharmaceutical industry is uniquely venal. The point is that the conventional accounting of value, which counts what passes through markets and not what underlies the activity producing the priced thing, cannot distinguish a value creator from a value extractor. Both register as contributing to GDP. The distinction is invisible to the system that is supposed to be measuring economic activity.

The same accounting blindness, Mazzucato argues, applies across the financial sector. The national accounts treat financial intermediation as productive activity proportional to the spread between borrowing and lending costs, the volume of transactions executed, and the fees collected. The accounting assumes the activity is, by its mere presence in the market, value-creating. The assumption survived the 2008 financial crisis with striking inelasticity: the same firms whose value-extracting activity had produced the crisis, by the conventional accounting, also recovered the largest share of value as the crisis was being resolved, by the same accounting. The accounting could not distinguish the rescue from the activity that required it.

The chapter on the asset-management industry is one of the book's quieter performances and one of its more devastating. Mazzucato walks through the fee structures of the major institutional asset managers with the patience of someone showing the reader how a card trick is done. The standard two-and-twenty arrangement of the private-equity and hedge-fund industries (two percent of assets under management plus twenty percent of returns above a threshold) compounds across decades into a structural transfer from the institutional investors whose savings are being managed to the management firms whose fees are being collected. The compounded effect across the larger institutional pools runs to the hundreds of billions of dollars over recent decades. The fees are justified, in industry communications, as the price of expert management. Mazzucato's accounting demonstrates that long-run performance of actively managed funds, net of fees, is on average no better than the performance of low-fee index funds; the value-creation claim is, on the available evidence, difficult to substantiate. What the fee structure does substantiate is the asset managers' capacity to convert position in the financial system into a durable claim on the returns generated by the underlying productive economy. That pattern is what Mazzucato means by value extraction.

The broader argument is the kind of intellectual contribution that becomes invisible once it has been absorbed into common discourse. Before *The Value of Everything*, the public conversation about whether activities like high-frequency trading, leveraged corporate buyouts, or pharmaceutical price arbitrage actually produced anything was conducted, when it was conducted at all, in moral rather than analytical registers. After the book, the same conversation has access to a more precise vocabulary, in which the question of whether an activity creates new productive capacity or merely repositions claims against existing productive capacity is operationalizable in language a policy audience can follow. The political-economic literature on rentier capitalism, the policy literature on platform monopolies, the regulatory discussion of share buybacks and corporate financialization, all draw on a vocabulary the book substantially established. The accomplishment is the rehabilitation of a distinction that the marginalist tradition had quietly retired from serious analytical use.

Mazzucato's diagnosis is also, by its own architecture, a diagnosis that points beyond itself. She shows persuasively that the structure of value extraction is the operative reality of the contemporary economy and the structure of value creation is its rhetorical complement. The further task her diagnosis makes legible is the operationalization of the corrective at the transaction level: how the kinds of activity she distinguishes would be priced and counted, of how a particular fee structure or pricing arrangement would be flagged as extractive rather than creative in real time, of how the institutional infrastructure that codes the assets in the first place would be adjusted to make the distinction enforceable. The book's closing chapters gesture toward what Mazzucato has elsewhere called a "mission economy," public investment organized around large-scale objectives with the state in the role of risk-taker and value-creator that conventional accounting has erased. The mission-economy frame is generative; it has produced policy programs in several jurisdictions; it has informed the design of the European Union's Recovery and Resilience Facility, among other instruments. The transaction-level instrumentation that would operationalize the diagnosis is a further task, and Mazzucato's diagnostic frame is what makes that task legible as the work it would be.

If Mazzucato names the activity an honest accounting would distinguish from value creation, Katharina Pistor names the architecture by which the priced asset comes into being in the first place. *The Code of Capital* is the book that supplies, in the language of legal scholarship, the institutional location at which the activity Mazzucato describes is engineered.

Pistor's central proposition is at once austere and radical: capital is not a natural category but a legal artifact. The line between assets that count as capital, with the legal protections and economic privileges that designation confers, and the much larger universe of assets that do not, is a line drawn by lawyers, refined over centuries of jurisprudence, and maintained by a transnational infrastructure of private legal practice that operates with very little democratic oversight. "Capital," she writes, "is coded in law." The lawyers who do the coding are not bystanders to the production of wealth; they are, in her account, the chief architects of the inequalities the economic literature has been content to attribute to luck, talent, market discipline, or the unfathomable workings of price.

The book proceeds through a careful anatomy of six legal modules: property, collateral, trust, the corporate form, bankruptcy, and choice-of-law clauses. Each is independently legitimate; each has a long and defensible jurisprudential history; each was developed for purposes of social order, predictability, and exchange. Together they constitute a code, in the technical sense Pistor borrows from computing: a layered set of instructions that, when applied to an asset, returns four properties the legal system will then enforce. Priority: the coded asset moves ahead of competing claims in distributions. Durability: the coded asset survives changes in ownership and the passage of generations. Universality: the coded asset's legal character is recognized across jurisdictions, not only in the venue where it was created. Convertibility: the coded asset can be converted into legal-tender money when the holder requires liquidity, even when the underlying object would not naturally yield such a conversion. An asset with all four properties is capital. An asset lacking one or more is, by degrees, less so.

The reach of the argument lies in what the coding has been applied to over the past four decades. Subprime mortgages were coded as capital and bundled into securities with priority claims against the resulting cash flows. Patents on pharmaceutical molecules whose underlying research was substantially publicly funded (the case Mazzucato has documented in her own register) were coded as private capital with durability across generations. Sovereign-debt instruments were coded with universality such that the same paper, sold in New York, became enforceable against the issuing nation through the courts of distant jurisdictions. The *NML Capital v. Argentina* litigation that ran through the United States federal courts in the early 2010s, with consequences for an Argentine government on a different continent, is one of Pistor's telling cases. Trusts were coded such that family wealth could be moved offshore and preserved across centuries while the political-economic activity that generated it stayed onshore and was taxed there. The corporate form was refined such that a parent company could insulate itself from the obligations of its subsidiaries; subsidiaries could be designed for disposability when liabilities matured. Bankruptcy was structured such that financial claims survived the discharge of operational ones; the residual cost of decades of activity could be left to local public-health systems, federal cleanup funds, and the affected communities.

The book's strongest pages are those that show how each module, taken in isolation, looks like a refinement of legal craft and, taken in combination, produces outcomes that the legal craft cannot itself address. Pistor's treatment of the choice-of-law clause is exemplary. A drafter at a major commercial law firm selects, almost as a routine matter, the jurisdiction whose courts will adjudicate disputes arising under a contract. The choice is presented as a technicality. It is, in practice, a sovereign decision: the parties to the transaction have selected the legal architecture under which their relationship will be governed and, by implication, the architecture under which the costs of the relationship's failure will be allocated.

Consider a coastline. From one shore the water runs to the other; the salt is the same; the storms cross the line without consulting it; the fish that feed there make no distinction. Now consider what happens to a barrel of oil spilled on either side. On the northern shore, the spill produces a federal investigation, a multi-billion-dollar settlement, a compensation fund, and a litigation history measured in volumes. On the southern shore, the same physical contamination is met by a settlement two or three orders of magnitude smaller per mile of damaged coast. The water has not changed. The architecture around the water has. That difference is a choice-of-law case in Pistor's sense exactly. The contamination did not respect the border. The legal architecture did.

Pistor's discussion of the *NML Capital* litigation, which threaded its way through the United States federal courts in the years after the 2001 Argentine sovereign default, is the case in which the choice-of-law module is most legible as the production of capital out of paper. Argentine sovereign bonds issued under New York law contained a clause providing that the bonds would be treated *pari passu*, on equal footing, with one another. When Argentina restructured most of its debt at substantial haircuts after the default, a small group of holdout creditors, led by NML Capital, declined the restructuring and sued in the Southern District of New York to enforce the *pari passu* clause. The litigation eventually produced an order that effectively prevented Argentina from paying the restructured bondholders until it had also paid the holdouts in full, on terms that recovered something close to face value for paper acquired at deep discount. In Pistor's telling, the case is neither a story of judicial overreach nor a story of greedy creditors. It is a story of how a legal module, selected by the parties to the original transaction as a matter of contract drafting, encountered a court whose obligations were defined by the same legal architecture, and produced an outcome no party to the original transaction had imagined. The architecture did what it was designed to do. The costs settled where they settled.

The treatment of the trust is, if anything, more sobering. Pistor traces the medieval origins of the legal trust through its early-modern English refinement, through its nineteenth-century commercial expansion, to its twentieth-century redeployment as the instrument of choice for cross-jurisdictional wealth preservation. The trust permits the legal separation of an asset's ownership from its beneficial enjoyment; it permits the holding of assets across generations under conditions specified by the original settlor; it permits, when combined with choice-of-law clauses pointing to hospitable jurisdictions, the moving of those assets beyond the practical reach of the political-economic systems that produced them. The combined effect, documented with patient attention to case material, is a system in which the descendants of the original wealth-generators continue to enjoy the returns to the assets while the political-economic systems in which the original generation took place have lost their ability to draw on those returns through taxation, regulation, or redistribution. The trust, like the choice-of-law clause, is independently defensible. The combination, scaled across generations and across jurisdictions, is the architecture by which intergenerational wealth concentration has become structurally insulated from the political processes that might, in another configuration, have addressed it.

What Pistor's diagnosis names, in language a reader who is not a lawyer can follow, is the institutional location at which any architecture of accountability would have to attach. The modules are the place where the work would be done. Property law would have to be reformed if the unprotected interests of those without title were to find a hearing; collateral law would have to be reformed if the priority of secured creditors over unsecured cost-bearers were to be reconsidered; the trust would have to be reformed if intergenerational wealth was to be subject to intergenerational accounting; the corporate form would have to be reformed if liability was to follow the corporate person across reorganizations; bankruptcy would have to be reformed if operational obligations were not to be discharged by financial maneuvers; choice-of-law would have to be reformed if jurisdictions could no longer be selected for their cost-allocation indulgences. Pistor herself does not propose this list. She writes as a diagnostician, with the reserve of the legal scholar who knows how much depends on the granularity of the prescription. But the location is named. The institutional surface on which any redesign would be done is now in view.

A scope the book leaves to further work, which Pistor would be the first to acknowledge, is that the legal-modules account assumes a relative homogeneity of interest among those for whom an alternative architecture might be devised. The longer literature on the commons, from Elinor Ostrom forward, has been careful to specify the conditions under which self-governance succeeds and the conditions under which it does not; among those conditions, the homogeneity of interest among stakeholders has emerged as load-bearing. Where the cost-bearers of a particular extraction are heterogeneous, with different exposures, different access to legal apparatus, and different political weight, the architecture's reform is itself a contested space. Pistor's modules supply the institutional location; they do not supply the political composition of the constituency that would mobilize the reform. Her book is honest about its own scope. The reader who takes the diagnosis seriously must also take seriously the further work of constituency-building it makes legible.

If Mazzucato names the activity and Pistor names the institutional location, Brett Christophers names the mechanism by which the architecture currently fails to deliver, in priced form, what the diagnoses would require it to deliver. *The Price Is Wrong* takes the renewable-energy transition as the case in which the failure is most visibly costly.

By every measure that matters to climate policy, the world is failing to make the transition at the pace climate physics requires. Technology is no longer the constraint: the levelized cost of energy from solar photovoltaics and onshore wind has fallen to or below the cost of fossil-fuel generation in most major markets, and continues to fall. The denial-and-political-will narratives that have organized much climate commentary are also no longer sufficient: most governments have adopted some version of a net-zero target; most large financial institutions have made some version of a climate commitment; the public in most developed and many developing economies broadly favors faster transition. And yet investment in renewable generation continues to lag the pace required, by the most accommodating accounting, by something like a factor of three or four against what would be needed to bring the trajectory back into compatibility with the warming targets set in Paris.

Christophers's argument is that the explanatory locus is pricing. Not pricing in the sense of consumer cost of energy, where the renewable case is now in fact strong. Pricing in the sense of the rate-of-return calculations that determine whether private capital will, in fact, allocate to renewable projects at the scale required. The asset-manager firms whose decisions are the proximate cause of how capital actually moves (Brookfield, BlackRock, Macquarie, KKR, the large institutional pools) are evaluating renewable projects against a hurdle rate set by their fiduciary obligations to investors, and against the alternative returns available in fossil-fuel projects, in private equity, in real estate, in technology. The renewable projects in many cases do not clear the hurdle. They do not clear the hurdle not because the underlying economics is bad but because the structure of returns available in renewables differs from the structure of returns available in fossil-fuel investment in ways the rate-of-return framework was not designed to handle. Renewable projects have higher capital costs paid upfront, lower operating costs that compound over time, and a return profile that depends on the regulatory and grid-infrastructure environment in which the project sits. Fossil-fuel projects have more familiar return profiles to which institutional capital is accustomed. The familiarity is part of the pricing.

The book's central formulation, which has acquired the kind of currency a precise phrase earns when it captures a structural truth, is that "profit, not price, is what reshapes the world." The line is not a slogan, though it has been used as one. It is the conclusion of an extended analysis. Price, in Christophers's account, is the signal markets use to communicate information about scarcity and demand. Profit is the signal capital uses to decide where to allocate. The two are not the same. A market can price something correctly, in the sense of reflecting its underlying scarcity and demand, and capital can still decline to deliver what the price calls for, because the structure of profit available does not justify the deployment. This is the mechanism of the renewable shortfall. The price is right. The profit is wrong.

The case material is detailed and disciplined. Christophers walks the reader through the rate-of-return mathematics that asset managers actually use, with the specificity of someone who has read the prospectuses and modeled the structures. He shows how the financial-derivatives markets price renewable revenue streams in ways that incorporate regulatory risk premia not faced by fossil-fuel incumbents. He documents the role of public-purpose-capital allocation in jurisdictions that have made faster transition progress: the Danish offshore-wind buildout, anchored by public ownership of Ørsted (formerly DONG Energy) in the years when the technology's commercial viability was still being established; the public-finance vehicles that supported the German Energiewende; the Spanish solar expansion of the late 2000s before its politically motivated withdrawal of public-investment instruments. His conclusion is unsparing: capitalism, as currently constituted, will not finance the climate transition at the pace required, because the price signal does not translate into the profit signal at the magnitude and on the timeline the physical problem demands. The required complement is some combination of public ownership, public investment, public-purpose capital allocation, and regulatory restructuring that aligns the private profit calculation with the social-cost calculation in a way the private profit calculation does not, on its own, do.

The asset-manager chapter is among the most carefully constructed in the recent political-economy literature. Christophers shows that the firms now controlling the largest pools of capital available for global energy investment are not, in any straightforward sense, investors in the underlying assets. They are managers of pooled capital deployed against a structure of fiduciary obligations to the underlying investors, who are themselves substantially institutional: pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign-wealth funds, family offices, university endowments. The asset managers are intermediaries. Their fiduciary obligation is to deliver risk-adjusted returns at the level the underlying investors expect, and the levels the underlying investors expect have been calibrated against the historical returns available across the post-1980 period in fossil-fuel investment, real estate, and the broader equity markets. Renewable-energy returns, in their current configuration, sit below those calibrations. The asset managers respond accordingly. Christophers's case material is not the case material of bad faith. It is the case material of an incentive structure operating exactly as designed.

The book is less interested in the politics of resistance than in the political economy of the structural condition itself. There is little manifesto in *The Price Is Wrong*; the analytical posture is closer to engineering than to advocacy. The pages on the asset-manager industry are particularly cold-eyed; the asset managers are not portrayed as villains but as agents responding rationally to the incentive structure they face. The question the book leaves open, and that the cumulative reading of these four books makes legible, is what an alternative structure would consist in. The Norwegian sovereign-wealth fund's allocation patterns, which Christophers references, are one example of an institutional alternative; the development-finance institutions are another; the public ownership models in the European utilities are a third. None of these, taken alone, constitutes the corrective Christophers's diagnosis demands. Taken cumulatively, they constitute a body of practice from which a pricing architecture different from the current one might be built. The mechanism the diagnosis names is the place where any such reconstruction would have to attach. In its absence, the price signal will keep failing to translate into the profit signal at the magnitude the diagnosis identifies.

The remaining diagnostic vocabulary belongs to Daniel Susskind, whose book operates at a different scale.

*Growth: A Reckoning* takes as its subject the metric against which the architecture has been judged for two centuries. Susskind opens with a historical observation that turns out to be load-bearing. Economic growth, as the concept now operates in political and policy discourse, is younger than is usually assumed. The systematic measurement of national income dates to the work of Simon Kuznets and the development of the national accounts in the 1930s and 1940s. The deliberate orientation of economic policy toward growth as such, the policy commitment that any year of growth is a successful year and any year of decline is a failed one, emerges in the 1950s and 1960s with Robert Solow's work on the residual and the institutional consolidation of Cold War development economics. Susskind's first thesis is that an idea this recent, in operation for not yet a century at the level of governing policy, should not be received as a fact of nature.

The book's second thesis is that growth has done substantially what its defenders claim it has done. The acceleration in living standards across the developed world from 1945 to the early 1970s; the lifting of more than a billion people out of extreme poverty in East Asia between 1980 and 2020; the corresponding declines in child mortality and the increases in life expectancy: these are real, and they are causally connected, in the political-economy literature Susskind reads carefully, to growth as conventionally measured. The book does not dismiss growth. Its tone, throughout, is the opposite of dismissive. It is the tone of a careful accounting, in which the magnitudes on both sides of the ledger are taken seriously.

What growth has also done, Susskind documents at length, is generate a class of costs the conventional accounting has been unable or unwilling to register. Climate is the most legible of these: the cumulative emissions trajectory associated with twentieth-century growth has loaded the atmosphere with carbon at a pace that exceeds the natural absorption rate, and the resulting warming is already producing costs that will compound across the coming century at a magnitude the growth-as-success accounting did not include. Inequality is a second: the distribution of growth's benefits, particularly in the developed economies since 1980, has been concentrated in the upper tail of the income distribution, with stagnant or declining real wages for the middle and lower portions of the distribution and a corresponding accumulation of political-economic disaffection. Wellbeing is a third: the empirical literature on subjective wellbeing, which Susskind reviews with appropriate caution, suggests that the relationship between aggregate growth and reported life satisfaction weakens or flattens at the income levels the developed economies have reached, and that the most powerful correlates of life satisfaction in those economies are not income-related but relate to social connection, meaning, and security. Meaning is the fourth, and the most difficult to operationalize: the displacement of forms of work and community that organized lives across previous generations has produced what Susskind, drawing on a wide literature, calls a crisis of significance, in which the affluence growth has delivered does not deliver the kinds of structured engagement with the world that the previous economic order, for all its material limitations, produced as a byproduct.

The book's central move is the redirection rather than the abandonment of growth. Susskind is explicit about this. The post-growth and degrowth literatures, which take the disaffection with growth-as-such as license to argue for an economic order organized around a different aggregate, are insufficiently attentive, in his account, to the material conditions of the developing economies that have not yet completed the growth-driven transition out of subsistence-level poverty. The argument that growth must be abandoned in the developed economies sits awkwardly alongside the argument that growth must continue in the developing ones, and the awkwardness has not been resolved by the existing literature. Susskind's alternative is to redirect growth: to specify, with the technical apparatus the national accounts already have available, the kinds of growth that compound human flourishing and the kinds that compound the costs the conventional accounting fails to register, and to organize economic policy around the former rather than the latter.

The chapters on the costs of growth are carefully sequenced. Susskind takes climate first, with the technical literature on the social cost of carbon and the cumulative-emissions accounting that the post-Paris policy discussion has substantially established. He takes inequality second, with the work of Piketty, Saez, and Zucman on the distribution of income and wealth in the developed economies, the work of Branko Milanović on the global distribution and the so-called elephant curve, and the work of Anne Case and Angus Deaton on the mortality consequences of stagnant or declining real wages in the United States. He takes wellbeing third, drawing on the empirical literature that includes Richard Layard's foundational work on the economics of happiness, Daniel Kahneman's distinction between experienced and remembered wellbeing, and the more recent work on the Easterlin paradox at the level of nations and across time. He takes meaning fourth, the most difficult to operationalize but, in Susskind's careful handling, perhaps the most analytically generative.

The redirection is not, in Susskind's hands, a vague aspiration. He proposes specific reforms to the measurement apparatus: revised national accounts that distinguish productive from extractive activity (the very distinction Mazzucato has been rehabilitating in a different register), subjective-wellbeing indices that complement income measures, satellite accounts for environmental and social externalities, intergenerational accounting that registers the present's claims on the future as the obligations they are. He proposes specific reforms to the policy apparatus: tax structures that price the extractive forms of growth more accurately, public investment in the forms of growth that compound human flourishing, regulatory restructurings that align corporate incentives with the redirection. The book's policy chapters are, in their way, the most ambitious of any of the four books under review. They constitute a serious attempt to specify what the redirection would look like at the level of governing instruments.

Susskind's growth-redirection is the macro analog of the per-extraction-event redirection the cumulative reading of the four books makes legible. Where Mazzucato names the activity and Pistor names the institutional location and Christophers names the pricing mechanism, Susskind names the aggregate metric against which the architecture has been judged and proposes the reorientation that would, at the macro level, do what an accountability architecture at the transaction level would do at the granular scale. The two scales are not in tension. They are complementary. A redirected national accounting at the growth level, combined with a redesigned pricing mechanism at the transaction level, a corrected diagnostic frame for value, and an adjusted institutional location at the legal-modules level, is what the cumulative diagnostic vocabulary the four books supply would, taken together, enable.

What the four books help to see, then, is the shape of an architecture: how it operates, where it attaches institutionally, what activity it produces, how that activity is priced or fails to be priced, and what aggregate measurement it produces or fails to produce. They are, taken together, a more substantial cumulative diagnosis of contemporary political economy than any single one of them alone provides. Mazzucato's diagnostic vocabulary for the activity would be less analytically grounded without Pistor's specification of the legal architecture that produces the priced assets; Pistor's legal-modules account would be less consequential without Mazzucato's diagnostic vocabulary for the activity the modules enable; Christophers's pricing analysis would be less compelling without Mazzucato's prior work on the distinction between value creation and value extraction; Susskind's growth-redirection argument would be less specifiable without Christophers's mechanism-level diagnosis of how prices currently fail to align private and public returns. The four books, read together, do for contemporary political economy something close to what the canonical works of the 1936–1948 mid-century pivot, *The General Theory*, *The Great Transformation*, *The Road to Serfdom*, did for the previous mid-century moment: they make a vocabulary available without which the next analytical step cannot be taken.

The lineage in which the four books sit reaches back through several earlier traditions that deserve acknowledgment at this point in the reading. Karl Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* (1944) supplied the original vocabulary of disembedded markets and the social costs of their disembedding; the four books reviewed here are, in different registers, descendants of the Polanyian diagnosis. David Harvey's work on accumulation by dispossession, developed across his books from the late 1990s into the 2010s, supplied the political-geographic analysis of how value extraction operates across spatial scales; Mazzucato's value-extraction vocabulary is in productive dialogue with Harvey's, though it operates in a different register and addresses a different readership. Elinor Ostrom's lifetime of work on the commons, culminating in *Governing the Commons* and the subsequent collaborative literature, supplied the institutional analysis of how collective resources are managed under conditions of heterogeneity, scale, and external pressure; the question of where Ostromian self-governance succeeds and where the conditions for its success do not hold is implicit across the cases the four books reviewed here describe. Kate Raworth's *Doughnut Economics* and Herman Daly's earlier work on steady-state economics supplied the ecological-economic frame within which Susskind's redirection argument is most coherent. The four books under review do not displace these earlier traditions. They extend them into the contemporary case material, with the additional analytical specificity that the past two decades of legal scholarship, financial-economic case studies, and political-economic data have made available.

What the four books do not do, taken together, is operationalize the corrective the cumulative diagnosis demands at the per-extraction-event mechanism level. The architecture they cumulatively describe is the architecture by which value extraction is enabled, value creation is undercounted, prices fail to align private and social returns, and growth is measured against a metric that hides the costs of the activity it registers. The architecture is engineered. It can be engineered differently. The four books name the activity, the institutional location, the pricing mechanism, and the aggregate metric. The instrumentation that would, at the level of the individual transaction, price the separation of who captures value from who bears the cost (the separation that the activity produces, that the location enables, that the mechanism fails to register, and that the metric obscures) is the work the four diagnoses cumulatively make legible and that the four books, by the structure of their respective inquiries, do not undertake.

Two negative-space observations sharpen this point. First, none of the four books offers a per-transaction pricing instrument designed to attach at the legal-modules location Pistor names. Each, in its own register, gestures at the institutional question of what would be required, but the gesture is at the level of policy aspiration, not at the level of instrument specification. Second, none of the four books offers a treatment of the intergenerational pricing question that connects the present extraction to the future cost-bearer in a way that is operationalizable at the level of the individual transaction. Susskind's intergenerational accounting is at the macro level; Christophers's pricing analysis is at the rate-of-return level for the present-time investor; Mazzucato's distinction between making and taking is a frame, not a metric; Pistor's legal-modules account is a diagnosis of architecture, not a prescription for redesign. The intergenerational pricing gap, the gap between what the present transaction pays and what the future cost-bearer will be made to absorb, is named, by implication, across all four books. It is not, in any of them, given a specific instrument.

The reader who has followed the cumulative diagnosis through the four books is now in a position to ask the obvious next question. If the architecture is engineered, and the four books cumulatively name the activity, the location, the mechanism, and the metric of the engineered architecture, what would the redesign consist in? What would the instruments that priced the cost-bearing separation at the per-transaction level have to do, what would they have to attach to, and how would they have to be sized?

The instruments would have to attach at the legal-modules location Pistor names. The pricing they performed would have to distinguish, in real time, between value creation and value extraction as Mazzucato describes them. The mechanism by which they operated would have to correct the failure Christophers diagnoses, in which private rate-of-return calculations fail to deliver the public goods the price signal nominally calls for. The aggregate measurement to which they contributed would have to register, in the national accounts Susskind proposes to redirect, the kinds of activity that compound human flourishing and the kinds that compound the cost-bearing separation. The instruments would, in other words, be the mechanism by which the four diagnoses are operationalized as a single architecture of accountability rather than as four independent observations.

What such an architecture would not consist in is equally specifiable. It would not consist in a single regulatory program imposed by central decree, because the diagnostic literature is clear that centrally imposed corrections to mechanism-level failures rarely survive contact with the granularity of the cases they are required to address. It would not consist in a market-based solution unanchored to public-interest instruments, because the diagnostic literature is also clear that markets, left to the rate-of-return calculations Christophers documents, do not deliver the corrections the diagnosis demands. It would not consist in an abandonment of growth as such, because Susskind's work is clear that the redirection rather than the abandonment is the available path. It would not consist in a reparations program limited to historical wrongs already in the political conversation, though the methodological apparatus developed by reparations economics is one of the available templates for the backward-looking component an architecture of accountability would also require.

The corrective the four diagnoses point toward is structural, not partisan. It does not depend on any particular electoral coalition. It does not depend on any particular ideological commitment beyond a willingness to take the diagnostic vocabulary the four books supply seriously as a description of how contemporary capitalism actually works. The activity Mazzucato describes continues across electoral cycles; the architecture of legal modules Pistor describes is in operation regardless of which party holds office; the price-failure mechanism Christophers diagnoses is structural to the current configuration of capital markets; the growth metric Susskind proposes to redirect is the operative measure of policy success across the developed-economy political spectrum. A corrective architecture would be structural in the same way. It would hold whether the architecture were reached from regulatory caution about the costs of unaddressed extraction, from market discipline that priced what the current pricing mechanism fails to price, or from worker and citizen solidarity that demanded the cost-bearing side of the ledger be honored. The construction the diagnoses cumulatively make available does not depend on the route by which it is reached.

Such an architecture would not require new philosophical foundations. The intellectual foundations have been built. The reparations-economics tradition has developed the methodological apparatus for pricing backward-looking obligations across cohorts. The civic-republican tradition, from Pettit and Skinner back to the early-modern English writers and the Roman law before them, has developed the normative apparatus for thinking about freedom as non-domination, and the intergenerational application of that tradition is well established. The Public Choice tradition, from Buchanan and Tullock forward, has developed the complementary analysis of how political and regulatory institutions are themselves arenas in which concentrated interests can capture returns at the expense of dispersed ones; that tradition's diagnosis of rent-seeking as architecture-shaping is on the same side of the structural-mechanism question as the four books reviewed here, not against it. The Hartwick rule, formulated in 1977 in a paper about non-renewable resources and intergenerational equity, has been operationalized in the Norwegian sovereign-wealth architecture and in the bonded-coverage mechanisms used in the United States coal and offshore-energy industries. The institutional templates exist. The legal modules Pistor describes are precisely the modules through which any new instrument would be coded. The pricing mechanism Christophers analyzes is the mechanism into which any corrective price signal would have to be introduced. The diagnostic frame Mazzucato provides is the frame within which any corrective accounting would be legible. The national-account redirection Susskind proposes is the macro complement to any per-transaction redirection at the mechanism level. The intellectual work has been done, in pieces, across the books these four authors have written and the traditions on which they draw.

What remains is the assembly. The instruments would be conventional in their components; what is new is the willingness to assemble the components into an architecture. The conditions for that assembly, the political-economic conditions and the institutional conditions and the intellectual conditions, are now substantially in place. The diagnostic vocabulary is available, after these four books, in a way it was not available a decade ago. The institutional templates have been operated at industrial scale across three decades. The legal modules have been refined for centuries. The reparations-economics methodology has been developed at the level of detail required for the backward-looking component. The civic-republican tradition has been developed at the level of philosophical detail required for the forward-looking component. What remains is the work of construction itself, the granular, jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction, case-by-case work of building the instruments and seating them in the architecture the four books describe.

The work is not, by its nature, completed in a single book or a single program. It is the work of several decades. The four books reviewed here are, properly understood, the predicate for that work, not the work itself. Their authors have made the diagnostic vocabulary available. The further task they cumulatively make legible is the construction of the architecture the diagnoses describe.

Return now to the distinction with which the reading began. The classical economists drew a line between productive and unproductive activity, and disagreed about where exactly the line should be drawn, but did not doubt that the line was the one a serious political economy would have to draw. The marginalist tradition dissolved the line and built an accounting system in which the question of whether an activity created new productive capacity or merely repositioned claims against existing capacity could no longer be raised inside the accounting itself. The four books reviewed here, taken together, have re-opened the question the marginalist tradition closed. They have done so without claiming to have settled it. Mazzucato has re-drawn the line for the contemporary case; Pistor has shown the legal architecture by which a priced asset comes into being on one side of the line or the other; Christophers has documented how the pricing mechanism currently fails to translate even an accurately priced underlying reality into a flow of capital that would deliver the public goods the underlying reality requires; Susskind has shown how the aggregate metric absorbs and conceals what the disaggregated reading of the cluster makes visible.

The account is, on these four books' showing, an account that will not close. It will not close at the level of the activity, which the conventional measurement cannot distinguish from value creation. It will not close at the level of the institutional location, which has been engineered to insulate the coded asset from the obligations the activity also produces. It will not close at the level of the pricing mechanism, which delivers profit to the structures of return that capital recognizes and not to the structures of return that the underlying physical and social reality requires. It will not close at the level of the aggregate metric, which counts what passes through markets and treats what does not pass through markets as if it had not happened. The diagnostic vocabulary for naming the unclosed account is now available. The architecture for ensuring that the account is finally closed is the work the next decade's political economy will be required to do. The classical line, between what an economy produces and what it merely repositions, has been re-opened. The accounting that would let the line carry its weight again has not yet been built.

The four books under review do not name themselves as a quartet. They were written separately and arrived at the same conclusion from different directions. The conclusion is not, in any of them, stated as the conclusion of an architecture-of-accountability program. It is stated, more modestly, as the conclusion of an inquiry into a specific facet of contemporary political economy. Read together, what the four conclusions add up to is something larger than any of them alone: a cumulative diagnosis of the architecture by which contemporary capitalism produces the costs it does not pay for, and the location at which the work of redesign would have to begin. The shape of what is missing, after these four books, is no longer a matter of speculation. It is a matter of construction.
