# The Engineered Coast

*A review of:*
*Katharina Pistor,* The Code of Capital: How the Law Creates Wealth and Inequality *(Princeton University Press, 2019)*
*Mariana Mazzucato,* The Value of Everything: Making and Taking in the Global Economy *(PublicAffairs, 2018)*
*Brett Christophers,* The Price Is Wrong: Why Capitalism Won't Save the Planet *(Verso, 2024)*
*Daniel Susskind,* Growth: A Reckoning *(Allen Lane / Belknap Press of Harvard University Press, 2024)*

Picture a single coastline divided by a national border. The water runs from one shore to the other without pausing at the line. The salt is the same on both sides; the storms cross without consulting the map; the fish that feed in the shallows recognize no jurisdiction. Now imagine a barrel of oil spilled into that water, and ask what follows on each shore. On the northern side the spill produces a federal investigation, a settlement measured in billions, a compensation fund, and a litigation record thick enough to fill shelves. On the southern side the identical contamination meets a settlement smaller by two or three orders of magnitude for every mile of fouled coast. Nothing about the water has changed between the two outcomes. What has changed is the architecture standing around the water.

There is a particular discomfort to this observation, which is that once it is made it cannot easily be unmade. Markets like to present themselves as facts of nature, the arithmetic that results when supply meets demand and a price emerges to clear them. But the outcomes a market delivers turn out, on inspection, to be shaped from top to bottom by an architecture of law that most participants take for granted and that most accounts of "the economy" treat as stage scenery rather than a working part of the machine. The price that clears is downstream of the coding of the asset. The asset's character was settled before the market ever opened, frequently centuries before, in the chambers where lawyers do their quiet work.

Four recent books, none of them written in conversation with the others, have together altered what one can say about that architecture without being taken for an eccentric. Each names a structural feature of contemporary political economy that ordinary economic discourse leaves unnamed. Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* names the law itself, the body of jurisprudence through which assets acquire the legal properties that turn them into capital. Mariana Mazzucato's *The Value of Everything* names the operation by which value accrues to those who arrange capital rather than to those who produce, a distinction between making and taking that conventional accounting has been either unwilling or unable to draw. Brett Christophers's *The Price Is Wrong* names the mechanism by which prices fail to align private return with the public goods those prices are supposed to summon into being, and takes the climate transition as its principal exhibit. Daniel Susskind's *Growth: A Reckoning* names the metric, economic growth itself, that has served for two centuries as the implicit criterion of success in political economy and that now demands to be reckoned against the costs it has cumulatively concealed.

The four authors come from different rooms of the academy. Pistor is a legal scholar at Columbia Law School; Mazzucato is an economist now at University College London; Christophers is a geographer at Uppsala; Susskind is an economist at Oxford. Their methods diverge, their vocabularies diverge with them, and their political registers diverge again. The books were written separately, for overlapping but not identical readerships, within a window of six years. And yet, read in one sitting, they rhyme. Each diagnoses, from its own angle of approach, the same underlying shape: a systematic separation between those who capture the value a transaction produces and those who absorb the costs the transaction also produces. Each gives that separation a different face. Read together as they were not written, the four diagnoses pose a single cumulative question, and it is the burden of what follows to draw it out.

This is not the only quartet one might assemble. Older books and adjacent traditions could be marshaled to similar effect: Polanyi, Harvey, Ostrom, Raworth, Sen, Daly. But these four, composed within so short a span and addressed to overlapping public audiences, amount to more than coincidence. They mark a moment in which the diagnostic vocabulary for an architecture of accountability is becoming available even though the architecture itself remains to be built. We may begin where the architecture is most plainly visible as engineered work, which is to say with Pistor.

Pistor's central proposition is at once austere and radical. Capital, she argues, is not a natural category. It is a legal artifact. The line dividing the assets that count as capital, with the protections and privileges the designation confers, from the far larger universe of assets that do not, is a line drawn by lawyers, refined across centuries of jurisprudence, and maintained by a transnational infrastructure of private legal practice that answers to very little democratic oversight. "Capital," she writes, "is coded in law." The lawyers who do the coding are not bystanders to the production of wealth. They are, on her account, its chief architects, and the inequalities the economic literature has been content to lay at the door of luck, talent, market discipline, or the inscrutable verdict of price are in large measure their handiwork.

The book proceeds as an anatomy of six legal modules: property, collateral, trust, the corporate form, bankruptcy, and the choice-of-law clause. Each is independently legitimate; each carries a long and defensible jurisprudential history; each was developed for the sober purposes of social order, predictability, and exchange. Together they constitute a code in the technical sense Pistor borrows from computing, a layered set of instructions that, applied to an asset, return four properties the legal system will then enforce. Priority moves the coded asset ahead of competing claims in any distribution. Durability allows it to survive changes of ownership and the passage of generations. Universality secures recognition of its legal character across jurisdictions, not merely in the venue of its creation. Convertibility permits its exchange into legal-tender money when the holder wants liquidity, even where the underlying object would never yield such a conversion on its own. An asset that carries all four properties is capital. An asset missing one or more is, by degrees, something less.

The force of the argument lies in what the coding has been made to do over the past four decades. Subprime mortgages were coded as capital and bundled into securities with priority claims against the resulting cash flows. Pharmaceutical molecules whose underlying research had been substantially funded out of the public purse were coded as private capital and endowed with durability across generations. Sovereign-debt instruments were coded with universality such that the same paper, sold in New York, became enforceable against the issuing nation in the courts of distant jurisdictions. Trusts were coded such that family wealth could be carried offshore and preserved across centuries while the political-economic activity that generated it remained onshore, where it could be taxed. The corporate form was refined until a parent could insulate itself from the obligations of its subsidiaries, and subsidiaries could be designed for disposal once their liabilities matured. Bankruptcy was structured so that financial claims survived the discharge of operational ones, and the residual cost of decades of activity could be left to local public-health systems, to federal cleanup funds, and to the communities that happened to live downwind.

Pistor's strongest pages are the ones that show how each module, taken alone, reads as a refinement of legal craft, while the modules in combination produce outcomes the craft itself cannot address. Her treatment of the choice-of-law clause is exemplary. A drafter at a major commercial firm selects, almost as a matter of routine, the jurisdiction whose courts will hear disputes arising under a contract. The selection is presented as a technicality. It is in practice a sovereign decision. The parties have chosen the legal architecture that will govern their relationship and, by implication, the architecture that will allocate the costs of the relationship's failure. The northern shore and the southern shore with which we began are a choice-of-law case in precisely Pistor's sense. The contamination did not respect the border. The architecture did.

Her discussion of the *NML Capital v. Argentina* litigation is the case in which the choice-of-law module becomes most legible as the manufacture of capital out of paper. Argentine sovereign bonds issued under New York law carried a clause providing that the bonds would be treated *pari passu*, on equal footing, with one another. When Argentina restructured most of its debt at deep discounts after its 2001 default, a small group of holdout creditors led by NML Capital declined the restructuring and sued in the Southern District of New York to enforce the clause. The litigation eventually yielded an order that effectively barred Argentina from paying its restructured bondholders until it had also paid the holdouts in full, on terms recovering close to face value for paper that had been bought at a steep discount. This is, in Pistor's telling, neither a tale of judicial overreach nor a tale of greedy creditors. It is a tale of how a legal module, selected by the original parties as a matter of contract drafting, met a court bound by the same legal architecture and produced a result none of those parties had imagined. The architecture did what it was built to do. The costs came to rest where they came to rest.

Her treatment of the trust is, if anything, the more sobering. Pistor follows the form from its medieval origins through its early-modern English refinement and its nineteenth-century commercial expansion to its twentieth-century redeployment as the instrument of choice for cross-jurisdictional wealth preservation. The trust separates an asset's legal ownership from its beneficial enjoyment; it permits the holding of assets across generations on terms fixed by the original settlor; and, with a choice-of-law clause pointing toward a hospitable jurisdiction, it permits those assets to be carried beyond the practical reach of the political-economic systems that produced them. The cumulative effect, which she documents with patient attention to the case material, is a regime in which the descendants of the original wealth-makers continue to enjoy the returns while the systems within which the original making took place have lost their capacity to draw on those returns through tax, regulation, or redistribution. The trust, like the choice-of-law clause, is defensible in isolation. The combination, scaled across generations and jurisdictions, is the architecture by which intergenerational concentration has insulated itself from the political processes that might, in another configuration, have reached it.

What Pistor's diagnosis names, in language a non-lawyer can follow, is the institutional location at which any architecture of accountability would have to attach. The modules are where the work would be done. Property law would have to be reformed if the unprotected interests of those without title were ever to find a hearing. Collateral law would have to be reformed if the priority of secured creditors over unsecured cost-bearers were to be reconsidered. The trust would have to be reformed if intergenerational wealth were to be subject to intergenerational accounting. The corporate form would have to be reformed if liability were to follow the corporate person across its reorganizations. Bankruptcy would have to be reformed if operational obligations were not to be dissolved by financial maneuver. Choice-of-law would have to be reformed if jurisdictions could no longer be shopped for their indulgences. Pistor herself proposes no such list. She writes as a diagnostician, with the reserve of a legal scholar who knows how much hangs on the granularity of any prescription. But the location is named. The institutional surface on which a redesign would have to operate is now in view.

There is a scope the book leaves to others, and Pistor would be the first to say so. The legal-modules account assumes a relative homogeneity of interest among those for whom an alternative architecture might be devised. The longer literature on the commons, from Elinor Ostrom onward, has taken care to specify the conditions under which self-governance succeeds and those under which it fails, and homogeneity of interest among stakeholders has emerged as one of the load-bearing conditions. Where the cost-bearers of a given extraction are heterogeneous, with different exposures, different access to legal apparatus, and different political weight, the reform of the legal architecture is itself a contested space. Pistor's modules supply the institutional location. They do not supply the political composition of the constituency that would have to carry the reform. The book is honest about its own borders. The reader who takes the diagnosis seriously must take seriously, too, the further work of constituency-building it makes legible.

What *The Code of Capital* leaves in the hand, then, is the architecture as engineered object. Once the building has been described in these terms, the next question follows almost mechanically. Who has been describing the activity the building was designed to make extractable, and from what vantage have they been doing it?

Mazzucato has. *The Value of Everything* opens with the post-2008 spectacle of a chief executive of Goldman Sachs announcing, apparently without irony, that his firm was doing God's work. The remark supplies the book's animating question. If the financial sector's contribution to the production of value is, as its leadership claimed, theological in its scale, why is it so hard to say what the value actually consists in? And if that difficulty is not accidental but built into the architecture of the national accounts, what work is the accounting performing on behalf of an activity it cannot quite describe?

Mazzucato's answer threads through two centuries of economic thought. She begins with the physiocrats, who located value in the land and only the land. She works through the classical economists, Smith and Ricardo and Marx, who in their several ways drew lines between productive and unproductive labor. She traces the marginalist revolution of the late nineteenth century, in which the line between productive and unproductive activity dissolved into the more abstract proposition that value is simply whatever someone is willing to pay. The contemporary national-income accounts inherit the marginalist settlement. They register as a contribution to gross domestic product whatever activity passes through a market at a price, and they leave the question of whether the activity produced anything to the market itself, on the circular logic that if it had produced nothing no one would have paid for it.

The book's most consequential move is its rehabilitation of the distinction between value creation and value extraction. Value creation, in Mazzucato's lexicon, refers to activity that adds to the productive capacity of the economy: research, manufacture, infrastructure, education, care. Value extraction, by contrast, refers to "activities focused on moving around existing resources and outputs, and gaining disproportionately from the ensuing trade." The phrase is exact, and by her own demonstration it is no longer fringe. The financial sector's swollen share of corporate profits across four decades, the pharmaceutical industry's pricing of molecules developed through publicly funded basic research, the fee structures of asset managers that compound regardless of fund performance: these are her cases, and they are not curiosities. They are, on her reading, the architecture of contemporary capitalism in its most consequential form.

The chapter on the pharmaceutical industry is among her sharpest. The molecule that became Sovaldi, Gilead's hepatitis-C treatment, emerged from a chain of basic research conducted under National Institutes of Health funding and small-biotech work supported substantially by federal grants. Gilead acquired the resulting compound through a corporate transaction and priced the drug at eighty-four thousand dollars for a twelve-week course in the United States. The price was justified in industry communications as a reflection of research cost and development risk. Mazzucato's accounting shows that the public's contribution to the underlying research considerably exceeded Gilead's, and that the price reflected not the cost of creating value but the firm's capacity to extract a return on a coded asset. Her point is not that the pharmaceutical industry is uniquely venal. Her point is that the conventional accounting of value, which counts what passes through markets and not what underlies the priced thing, cannot tell a value creator from a value extractor. Both register as contributors to GDP. The distinction is invisible to the very system charged with measuring economic activity.

The same blindness, she argues, attends the financial sector. The national accounts count financial intermediation as productive in proportion to the spread between borrowing and lending costs, the volume of transactions executed, and the fees collected. The presence of the activity in the market is taken as proof of its value. The assumption survived the crisis of 2008 with a striking inelasticity. The same firms whose value-extracting activity had produced the crisis, by the conventional accounting, recovered the largest share of value as the crisis was resolved, by the same accounting. The accounting could not distinguish the rescue from the activity that had made the rescue necessary.

The chapter on asset management is, on its own terms, one of the book's quieter performances and one of its most devastating. Mazzucato walks through the fee structures of the major institutional managers with the patience of someone demonstrating how a card trick is done. The standard two-and-twenty of the private-equity and hedge-fund industries, two percent of assets under management and twenty percent of returns above a threshold, compounds across decades into a structural transfer from the institutional investors whose savings are being managed to the firms whose fees are being collected. Across the larger institutional pools the compounded effect runs into the hundreds of billions of dollars over recent decades. The fees are justified as the price of expert management. Yet the long-run performance of actively managed funds, net of fees, is on average no better than that of low-fee index funds, and so the value-creation claim is, on the available evidence, hard to substantiate. What the fee structure does substantiate is the managers' capacity to convert a position in the financial system into a durable claim on the returns generated by the productive economy underneath it. That conversion is what Mazzucato means by extraction.

The broader argument about productive and unproductive activity is the kind of intellectual contribution that grows invisible once it has been absorbed into common speech. Before *The Value of Everything*, the public conversation about whether high-frequency trading, leveraged buyouts, or pharmaceutical price-arbitrage actually produced anything was conducted, when it was conducted at all, in a moral rather than an analytical register. After the book, the same conversation has access to a sharper vocabulary, one in which the question of whether an activity creates new productive capacity or merely repositions claims against capacity that already exists is operationalizable in terms a policy audience can follow. The literature on rentier capitalism, the policy debate over platform monopolies, the regulatory argument about share buybacks and corporate financialization, all draw on a vocabulary this book substantially established. The achievement is the recovery of a distinction the marginalist tradition had quietly retired from serious analytical use.

Mazzucato shows, persuasively, that the architecture of value extraction is the operative reality of the contemporary economy and that the architecture of value creation is largely its rhetorical complement. The question her diagnosis raises is how to operationalize the corrective it demands. The book gestures at an answer in its closing chapters, which call for what she has elsewhere named a "mission economy": public investment organized around large-scale objectives, with the state restored to the role of risk-taker and value-creator that the conventional accounting has erased. The mission frame is generative. It has produced policy programs in several jurisdictions and informed the design of the European Union's Recovery and Resilience Facility, among other instruments. The operationalization of the corrective at the level of the individual transaction, the question of how value extraction would be priced and counted, of how a particular fee arrangement or pricing decision would be flagged as extractive rather than creative in real time, of how the legal modules Pistor names would be adjusted to make the distinction enforceable, remains a further task that the frame opens rather than closes. The diagnostic vocabulary has been built. The instrumentation that would put the diagnosis to work at the level of the per-transaction mechanism is the labor the frame makes legible.

A further acknowledgment belongs here. The vocabulary Mazzucato has rehabilitated, and "value extraction" above all, has by now become close to common currency in serious political-economic writing. The phrase turns up in the academic literature on rentier capitalism, in policy reports on platform monopolies, in commentary on private equity. Its passage from specialist coinage to common reference is itself an event in intellectual history, and the diagnostic frame she put into circulation is now load-bearing in a way few books of the past decade can claim. The work that follows, including the books reviewed here, builds on a vocabulary that *The Value of Everything* substantially established. The acknowledgment matters because the cumulative architecture an honest accounting would require depends on a vocabulary it did not have to invent.

If Pistor names the institutional location at which an accountability architecture would attach, and Mazzucato names the activity such an architecture would have to distinguish from genuine creation, Christophers names the mechanism through which the architecture would have to operate, and the present failure of that mechanism.

*The Price Is Wrong* declares its argument in its title and takes the renewable-energy transition as the case in which the failure is most visibly costly. By every measure that matters to climate policy, the world is failing to transition at the pace climate physics requires. Technology is no longer the constraint. The levelized cost of energy from solar photovoltaics and onshore wind has fallen to or below the cost of fossil-fuel generation in most major markets, and it continues to fall. Nor will the familiar narratives of denial and absent political will any longer do the explanatory work. Most governments have adopted some version of a net-zero target, most large financial institutions have made some version of a climate commitment, and publics across the developed world and much of the developing world broadly favor a faster transition. And still the investment in renewable generation lags what would be required, by the most accommodating accounting, by something like a factor of three or four against the pace that would bring the trajectory back into line with the targets set in Paris.

Christophers locates the explanation in pricing. Not pricing in the sense of the consumer cost of energy, where the renewable case is in fact strong, but pricing in the sense of the rate-of-return calculations that determine whether private capital will actually allocate itself to renewable projects at the scale required. The asset-management firms whose decisions are the proximate cause of how capital moves, the Brookfields and BlackRocks and Macquaries and KKRs and the great institutional pools behind them, evaluate renewable projects against a hurdle rate set by their fiduciary obligations to investors and against the alternative returns available in fossil fuels, in private equity, in real estate, in technology. Many renewable projects do not clear the hurdle. They fail to clear it not because the underlying economics is poor but because the structure of returns available in renewables differs from the structure available in fossil-fuel investment in ways the rate-of-return framework was never designed to accommodate. Renewable projects carry higher capital costs paid up front, lower operating costs that compound over time, and a return profile that depends on the regulatory and grid-infrastructure environment in which the project sits. Fossil-fuel projects present return profiles to which institutional capital has long been accustomed, and the familiarity is itself part of the pricing.

The book's central formulation, which has acquired the currency a precise phrase earns when it captures a structural truth, is that "profit, not price, is what reshapes the world." The line is not a slogan, though it has been pressed into service as one. It is the conclusion of an extended analysis. Price, in Christophers's account, is the signal a market uses to communicate scarcity and demand. Profit is the signal capital uses to decide where to go. The two are not the same. A market can price something correctly, in the sense of faithfully reflecting its scarcity and demand, and capital can still decline to deliver what the price calls for, because the structure of profit on offer does not justify the deployment. This is the mechanism of the renewable shortfall. The price is right. The profit is wrong.

His case material is detailed and disciplined. He walks the reader through the rate-of-return mathematics asset managers actually use, with the specificity of someone who has read the prospectuses and modeled the structures. He shows how the financial-derivatives markets price renewable revenue streams in ways that load in regulatory risk premia the fossil-fuel incumbents do not face. He documents the role of public-purpose capital in the jurisdictions that have made faster progress: the Danish offshore-wind buildout, the public investment vehicles that anchored the early Spanish solar expansion, the development-finance institutions that financed renewables in markets where private capital found the risk profile uncongenial. His conclusion is unsparing. Capitalism, as presently constituted, will not finance the climate transition at the pace required, because the price signal does not translate into the profit signal at the magnitude and on the timeline the physical problem demands. The remedy he names is some combination of public ownership, public investment, public-purpose capital allocation, and regulatory restructuring that aligns the private profit calculation with the social-cost calculation in a way the private profit calculation will not, on its own, perform.

The chapter that develops the asset-manager argument most fully is among the most carefully constructed in the recent political-economy literature. Christophers shows that the firms now commanding the largest pools of capital available for global energy investment are not, in any straightforward sense, investors in the underlying assets at all. They are managers of pooled capital deployed against a structure of fiduciary obligations to the underlying investors, who are themselves substantially institutional: pension funds, insurers, sovereign-wealth funds, family offices, university endowments. The managers are intermediaries. Their fiduciary duty is to deliver risk-adjusted returns at the level the underlying investors expect, and the levels the underlying investors expect have been calibrated against the historical returns available across the post-1980 period in fossil-fuel investment, in real estate, and in the broader equity markets. Renewable returns, in their current configuration, sit below those calibrations, and the managers respond accordingly. The case material here is not the case material of bad faith. It is the case material of an incentive structure operating exactly as designed.

The book's treatment of the development-finance institutions supplies the necessary counterpoint. The Danish state's involvement in the early offshore-wind buildout, through public ownership of Ørsted, formerly DONG Energy, in the years when the technology's commercial viability was still in doubt, is one of the cases in which a non-market actor absorbed the early-stage risk that the private-capital pricing model would not absorb. The public-finance vehicles of the German Energiewende played a comparable role, and the Spanish solar buildout of the late 2000s drew, until its politically motivated withdrawal, on public-investment instruments that anchored its early commercial deployment. The pattern Christophers traces is consistent. The cases of faster transition are cases in which public-purpose capital was present at the scale required, and the cases of slower transition are cases in which the private-capital pricing model was left to govern the allocation. The implication is structural rather than ideological. Where the architecture of capital allocation has included a public-purpose component sized to the magnitude of the externalities the private pricing fails to register, the transition has proceeded at a different pace than where it has not.

What Christophers's diagnosis names, then, is the mechanism-level evidence a constructive move would have to correct. An accountability architecture would have to operate at the level of the pricing mechanism, not only at the level of the legal architecture that produces priced assets. Pricing the cost severance that Pistor's modules make possible is one of the things such an architecture would do. Christophers's argument is the demonstration that the failure of pricing to perform this work is not an oversight but a structural feature of how capital, under contemporary fiduciary and regulatory conditions, evaluates returns. It is, in the most exact sense, the mechanism that Mazzucato's diagnostic frame predicts, the place where the pricing of value extraction either is or is not made structurally visible. In Christophers's accounting it currently is not.

The book is less interested in the politics of resistance than in the political economy of the structural condition itself. There is little manifesto in *The Price Is Wrong*; the posture is closer to engineering than to advocacy. The pages on the asset managers are particularly cold-eyed, portraying them not as villains but as agents responding rationally to the incentives they face. The question the book leaves open, and that the cumulative reading of these four books makes legible, is what an alternative structure would consist in. The Norwegian sovereign-wealth fund's allocation patterns, which Christophers references, are one example of an institutional alternative; the development-finance institutions are another; the public-ownership models among the European utilities are a third. None of these, taken alone, is the corrective the diagnosis demands. Taken together, they amount to a body of practice from which a pricing architecture different from the present one might be assembled.

The last of the four diagnostic vocabularies belongs to Susskind, whose book works at a different scale.

*Growth: A Reckoning* takes as its subject what the other three describe without quite naming directly: the metric against which the architecture has been judged for two centuries. Susskind opens with a historical observation that proves load-bearing. Economic growth, as the concept now operates in political and policy discourse, is younger than we usually suppose. The systematic measurement of national income dates to the work of Simon Kuznets and the construction of the national accounts in the 1930s and 1940s. The deliberate orientation of policy toward growth as such, the commitment that any year of growth is a successful one and any year of decline a failure, emerges only in the 1950s and 1960s, with Robert Solow's work on the residual and the institutional consolidation of Cold War development economics. Susskind's first thesis is that an idea this recent, in operation as governing policy for not yet a century, ought not to be received as a fact of nature.

His second thesis is that growth has done substantially what its defenders claim. The acceleration of living standards across the developed world between 1945 and the early 1970s, the lifting of more than a billion people out of extreme poverty in East Asia between 1980 and 2020, the accompanying declines in child mortality and the gains in life expectancy, are real, and they are causally connected, in the literature Susskind reads with care, to growth as conventionally measured. The book does not dismiss growth. Its tone throughout is the opposite of dismissive. It is the tone of a careful accounting, in which the magnitudes on both sides of the ledger are taken seriously.

What growth has also done, as Susskind documents at length, is generate a class of costs the conventional accounting has been unable or unwilling to register. Climate is the most legible of them. The cumulative emissions trajectory associated with twentieth-century growth has loaded the atmosphere with carbon faster than the natural absorption rate, and the resulting warming is already producing costs that will compound across the coming century at a magnitude the growth-as-success accounting never included. Inequality is the second. The distribution of growth's benefits, particularly in the developed economies since 1980, has concentrated in the upper tail of the income distribution, with stagnant or declining real wages for the middle and the bottom and a corresponding accumulation of political-economic disaffection. Wellbeing is the third. The empirical literature on subjective wellbeing, which Susskind reviews with appropriate caution, suggests that the relationship between aggregate growth and reported life satisfaction weakens or flattens at the income levels the developed economies have reached, and that the strongest correlates of life satisfaction in these economies are not income but social connection, meaning, and security. Meaning is the fourth, and the hardest to operationalize. The displacement of the forms of work and community that organized lives across earlier generations has produced what Susskind, drawing on a wide literature, calls a crisis of significance, in which the affluence growth has delivered fails to deliver the kinds of structured engagement with the world that the previous economic order, for all its material limitations, threw off as a byproduct.

The book's central move is the redirection rather than the abandonment of growth, and Susskind is explicit about the choice. The post-growth and degrowth literatures, which take the disaffection with growth-as-such as license to argue for an economy organized around a different aggregate, are in his account insufficiently attentive to the material conditions of the developing economies that have not yet completed the growth-driven escape from subsistence-level poverty. The argument that growth must be abandoned in the developed economies sits awkwardly beside the argument that it must continue in the developing ones, and the existing literature has not resolved the awkwardness. Susskind's alternative is to redirect: to specify, with the apparatus the national accounts already possess, which kinds of growth compound human flourishing and which compound the costs the conventional accounting fails to register, and to organize policy around the former.

The chapters on the costs of growth are carefully sequenced. Susskind takes climate first, with the technical literature on the social cost of carbon and the cumulative-emissions accounting the post-Paris discussion has substantially established. He takes inequality second, drawing on the work of Piketty and Saez and Zucman on the distribution of income and wealth in the developed economies, on Branko Milanović's analysis of the global distribution and the so-called elephant curve, and on Anne Case and Angus Deaton's work on the mortality consequences of stagnant or declining real wages in the United States. He takes wellbeing third, drawing on Richard Layard's foundational work in the economics of happiness, Daniel Kahneman's distinction between experienced and remembered wellbeing, and the more recent treatments of the Easterlin paradox across nations and over time. He takes meaning fourth, the hardest to operationalize and, in his careful handling, perhaps the most analytically generative. The displacement of the forms of work that once organized lives, the corresponding decline of the institutions, religious and civic and occupational, that supplied the structuring narratives within which a life made sense, and the rise of what he calls the significance gap, are documented with empirical care and connected, through the political-economy literature, to the same growth-as-measured that produced the material gains.

The redirection is not, in Susskind's hands, a vague aspiration. He proposes specific reforms to the measurement apparatus: revised national accounts that distinguish productive from extractive activity, subjective-wellbeing indices to complement the income measures, satellite accounts for environmental and social externalities, intergenerational accounting that registers the present's claims on the future as the obligations they are. He proposes specific reforms to the policy apparatus: tax structures that price the extractive forms of growth more accurately, public investment in the forms that compound human flourishing, regulatory restructurings that align corporate incentives with the redirection. The policy chapters are, in their way, the most ambitious of any of the four books. They constitute a serious attempt to specify what the redirection would look like at the level of governing instruments.

Susskind's growth-redirection is the macro analog of the per-extraction redirection the cumulative reading makes legible. Where Pistor names the legal location, Mazzucato the diagnostic frame, and Christophers the pricing mechanism, Susskind names the aggregate metric against which the activity has been judged and proposes the reorientation that would do, at the macro level, what an accountability architecture at the per-transaction level would do at the granular scale. The two scales are not in tension. They are complementary. A redirected national accounting at the growth level, joined to a redesigned pricing mechanism at the transaction level, a corrected diagnostic frame for value, and an adjusted institutional location at the legal-modules level, is what the cumulative vocabulary the four books supply would, taken together, enable.

What the four books help one to see, then, is the shape of an architecture: how it operates, where it attaches institutionally, what activity it produces, how that activity is priced or fails to be priced, and what aggregate measurement it produces or fails to produce. Taken together they constitute a more substantial diagnosis of contemporary political economy than any one of them provides alone. Pistor's legal-modules account would be less consequential without Mazzucato's vocabulary for the activity the modules enable. Mazzucato's vocabulary would be less grounded without Pistor's specification of the legal architecture that produces the priced assets. Christophers's pricing analysis would be less compelling without Mazzucato's prior work on the distinction between creation and extraction. Susskind's growth-redirection would be less specifiable without Christophers's mechanism-level diagnosis of how prices currently fail to align private and public returns. Read together, the four do for contemporary political economy something close to what the canonical works of the 1936 to 1948 mid-century pivot, *The General Theory*, *The Great Transformation*, *The Road to Serfdom*, did for the previous mid-century moment. They make a vocabulary available without which the next analytical step cannot be taken.

The lineage in which the four sit reaches back through several earlier traditions that deserve acknowledgment. Karl Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* supplied the original vocabulary of disembedded markets and the social costs of their disembedding, and the four books reviewed here are, in their different registers, descendants of the Polanyian diagnosis. David Harvey's work on accumulation by dispossession, developed across his books from the late 1990s into the 2010s, supplied the political-geographic analysis of how value extraction operates across spatial scales, and Mazzucato's vocabulary is in productive dialogue with his, though it works in a different register and addresses a different readership. Elinor Ostrom's lifetime of work on the commons, culminating in *Governing the Commons* and the collaborative literature that followed, supplied the institutional analysis of how collective resources are managed under conditions of heterogeneity, scale, and external pressure, and the question of where Ostromian self-governance succeeds and where its conditions fail to hold runs implicitly through the cases all four books describe. Kate Raworth's *Doughnut Economics* and Herman Daly's earlier work on steady-state economics supplied the ecological-economic frame within which Susskind's redirection argument is most coherent. The four books do not displace these traditions. They extend them into the contemporary case material, with the additional specificity that two decades of legal scholarship, financial-economic case studies, and political-economic data have made available.

What the four books do not do, taken together, is operationalize the corrective the cumulative diagnosis demands at the level of the per-extraction-event mechanism. The architecture they describe between them is the architecture by which value extraction is enabled, value creation is undercounted, prices fail to align private and social returns, and growth is measured against a metric that hides the costs of the activity it registers. The architecture is engineered. It can be engineered differently. The four books name the location, the activity, the mechanism, and the metric. The instrumentation that would, at the level of the individual transaction, price the cost severance that the location enables and the activity produces and the mechanism fails to register and the metric obscures, is the work the four diagnoses cumulatively make legible and that the four books, by the structure of their respective inquiries, do not undertake.

Two observations from the negative space sharpen the point. First, none of the four books offers a per-transaction pricing instrument designed to attach at the legal-modules level Pistor names. Each gestures, in its own register, at the institutional question of what would be required, but the gesture stays at the level of policy aspiration rather than instrument specification. Second, none offers a treatment of the intergenerational pricing question, the question that connects the present extraction to the future cost-bearer, that is operationalizable at the level of the individual transaction. Susskind's intergenerational accounting is macro. Christophers's pricing analysis is the rate-of-return analysis of the present-time investor. Mazzucato's distinction between making and taking is a frame rather than a metric. Pistor's legal-modules account is a diagnosis of architecture rather than a prescription for redesign. The intergenerational pricing gap, the gap between what the present transaction pays and what the future cost-bearer will be made to absorb, is named by implication across all four. It is given a specific instrument in none.

The reader who has followed the cumulative diagnosis through the four books is now in a position to ask the obvious next question. If the architecture is engineered, and if the four books cumulatively name its location, its activity, its mechanism, and its metric, what would the redesign consist in? What would the instruments that priced the cost severance at the per-transaction level have to do, what would they attach to, and how would they have to be sized?

They would have to attach at the legal-modules location Pistor names. The pricing they performed would have to distinguish, in real time, between value creation and value extraction as Mazzucato describes them. The mechanism by which they operated would have to correct the failure Christophers diagnoses, in which private rate-of-return calculations decline to deliver the public goods the price signal nominally calls for. The aggregate measurement to which they contributed would have to register, in the national accounts Susskind proposes to redirect, the kinds of activity that compound human flourishing and the kinds that compound cost severance. The instruments would be, in other words, the mechanism by which the four diagnoses are operationalized as a single architecture of accountability rather than left as four independent observations.

What such an architecture would not consist in is equally specifiable. It would not consist in a single regulatory program imposed by central decree, because the diagnostic literature is clear that centrally imposed corrections to mechanism-level failures rarely survive contact with the granularity of the cases they are required to address. It would not consist in a market-based solution unanchored to public-interest instruments, because the same literature is clear that markets, left to the rate-of-return calculations Christophers documents, do not deliver the corrections the diagnosis demands. It would not consist in the abandonment of growth as such, because Susskind's work is clear that redirection rather than abandonment is the available path. And it would not consist in a reparations program confined to the historical wrongs already in the political conversation, though the methodological apparatus reparations economics has developed is one of the available templates for the backward-looking instrument such an architecture would also require.

The corrective the four diagnoses point toward is structural, not partisan. It does not depend on any particular electoral coalition. It does not depend on any ideological commitment beyond a willingness to take the diagnostic vocabulary the four books supply seriously as a description of how contemporary capitalism actually works. The architecture of legal modules Pistor describes operates regardless of which party holds office. The value-extraction activity Mazzucato documents continues across electoral cycles. The price-failure mechanism Christophers diagnoses is structural to the present configuration of capital markets. The growth metric Susskind proposes to redirect is the operative measure of policy success across the developed-economy political spectrum. A corrective architecture would be structural in the same way. It would hold whether it were reached out of regulatory caution about the costs of unaddressed extraction, out of market discipline that priced what the present pricing mechanism fails to price, or out of the worker and citizen solidarity that demanded the cost-bearing side of the ledger be honored. The construction the diagnoses cumulatively make available does not depend on the route by which it is reached.

Nor would such an architecture require new philosophical foundations. The foundations have been built. The reparations-economics tradition has developed the methodological apparatus for pricing backward-looking obligations across cohorts. The civic-republican tradition, from Pettit and Skinner back through the early-modern English writers to the Roman law before them, has developed the normative apparatus for thinking about freedom as non-domination, and the intergenerational application of that tradition is by now well established. The Public Choice tradition, from Buchanan and Tullock forward, has developed the complementary analysis of how political and regulatory institutions are themselves arenas in which concentrated interests can capture returns at the expense of dispersed ones; that tradition's account of rent-seeking as architecture-shaping sits on the same side of the structural-mechanism question as the four books reviewed here, not against them. The Hartwick rule, formulated in 1977 in a paper on non-renewable resources and intergenerational equity, has been operationalized in the Norwegian sovereign-wealth architecture and in the bonded-coverage mechanisms used in the American coal and offshore-energy industries. The institutional templates exist. The legal modules Pistor describes are precisely the modules through which any new instrument would be coded. The pricing mechanism Christophers analyzes is the mechanism into which any corrective price signal would have to be introduced. The diagnostic frame Mazzucato provides is the frame within which any corrective accounting would be legible. The national-account redirection Susskind proposes is the macro complement to any per-transaction redirection at the mechanism level. The intellectual work has been done, in pieces, across the books these four authors have written and the traditions on which they draw.

What remains is the assembly. The instruments would be conventional in their components. What is new is the willingness to assemble those components into an architecture, and the conditions for that assembly, political-economic and institutional and intellectual alike, are now substantially in place. The diagnostic vocabulary is available, after these four books, in a way it was not available a decade ago. The institutional templates have been operated at industrial scale across three decades. The legal modules have been refined for centuries. The reparations-economics methodology has been developed to the level of detail the backward-looking instrument requires. The civic-republican tradition has been developed to the level of philosophical detail the forward-looking instrument requires. What remains is the work of construction itself, the granular, jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction, case-by-case work of building the instruments and seating them in the architecture the four books describe.

The work is not, by its nature, the kind that a single book or a single program completes. It is the work of several decades. The four books reviewed here are, properly understood, the predicate for that work rather than the work itself. Their authors have made the diagnostic vocabulary available. The further task they cumulatively make legible is the construction of the architecture the diagnoses describe.

Return now to the coastline. The same water still runs from one shore to the other; the same storms still cross the line; the same fish still feed in the shallows. The barrel of oil on the northern shore still encounters a different legal architecture from the barrel on the southern shore, and the architecture, not the contamination, still governs how the cost is borne. The contingency of that architecture is what the four books cumulatively make impossible to overlook. The fact of its engineering is what makes its redesign conceivable. The next contamination, on the next coastline, in whatever extractive sector or financialized arrangement or intergenerational obligation it happens to inhabit, will pass through the same modules, register the same value-extraction signature, escape the same pricing mechanism, and vanish into the same aggregate metric that hides its cost. The vocabulary for naming what is being lost is now available. The architecture for ensuring that what is named is also paid for is the work the coming decade's political economy will be required to do.

The four books under review do not name themselves a quartet. They were written separately and arrived at the same conclusion from different directions, and in none of them is that conclusion stated as the conclusion of an architecture-of-accountability program. It is stated, more modestly, as the conclusion of an inquiry into a particular facet of contemporary political economy. Read together, what the four conclusions add up to is something larger than any of them: a cumulative diagnosis of the architecture by which contemporary capitalism produces the costs it does not pay for, and an account of the location at which the work of redesign would have to begin. The shape of what is missing, after these four books, is no longer a matter of speculation. It is a matter of construction.
