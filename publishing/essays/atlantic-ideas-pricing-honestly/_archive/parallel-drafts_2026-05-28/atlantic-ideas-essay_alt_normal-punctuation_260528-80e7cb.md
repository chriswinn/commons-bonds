# Pricing Honestly

The ton of coal that left McDowell County, West Virginia, in 1960 sold at the mine mouth for four dollars and fifty cents. Counting every cost that ton actually produced — the coal and silica dust that scarred the miner's lungs, the carbon released into the atmosphere over the next century, the streams that ran orange and acid for fifty miles downhill, the schools and clinics that emptied when the operators decamped, the longevity gap that opened between McDowell residents and the American median — the honest price comes in somewhere between five hundred and twenty and six hundred dollars a ton. The market price was wrong by two orders of magnitude. It is wrong now, for coal and for almost everything else.

The McDowell ton is not an outlier. It is a worked example of a problem the price signal has been getting wrong, systematically and across categories, for as long as there has been a price signal. The carbon released by burning fossil fuels. The freshwater pumped from aquifers that took millennia to fill. The topsoil mined out of farmland over a generation. The seabed plain about to be opened to commercial extraction. The unit price the market posts for each of these does not include the costs the use produces. The price gets the trade done. The costs land somewhere else. The someone-else is sometimes a downstream community, sometimes an ecosystem with no standing to send the bill, and very often a generation that has not yet been born.

Four traditions, in the historical order they took up adjacent territory: Marx, Ostrom, Daly, Hartwick. Each addressed part of what economics is for: who labors, who governs the commons, who lives inside the biosphere, who inherits what is left. None of them operationalized the case of permanent foreclosure. Neither does the everyday model the rest of us reason inside, which is supply and demand at the moment of sale. Supply and demand clears the transaction between the parties present. Permanent foreclosure is not present at the moment of sale. The generation that pays for it is not seated at the table. The price clears without them, on whatever terms the parties at the table find tolerable, and the bill comes due in their lifetimes rather than ours.

Treat the gap between the market price and the honest price as an arithmetic problem and the response follows. If a society decided to price its extractions honestly, counting every cost the current price misses, it would have four moves to make, in order: classify what cannot be replaced, reserve a fraction of it, invest in substitutes and alternative sources, and reassess as it learns. This is not a manifesto. It is a sequence of accounting decisions of the kind governments and firms make every day, applied to materials and externalities they currently do not.

## Classify

Not every resource sits in the same category. Iron and coal are abundant; the recoverable reserve runs to hundreds of years, and the substitutes for what they do (structural steel, primary energy) are technically available at known prices. Other materials are more constrained, and a few are not really substitutable at all on any horizon worth planning around.

Call the spread between what can be replaced and what cannot the substitution gap, and call the materials at the wide end of the gap the irreplaceables. The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act, passed in 2023, lists thirty-four such materials. The list is a useful first cut. It catches the rare-earth elements used in the magnets that turn wind turbines and electric motors, the platinum-group metals used in fuel cells and catalytic converters, the lithium and cobalt that go into batteries, and the gallium and germanium that go into semiconductors. These are materials whose absence would not be an inconvenience but a redesign.

Some of the entries on lists like the EU's are matters of supply concentration. Cobalt is abundant in the earth's crust, but the Democratic Republic of Congo holds most of the recoverable concentration. Others are matters of geological scarcity. Phosphorus is the textbook case. There is no biological substitute for it in agriculture, and the workable concentrations sit at a few sites globally. Helium in industrial concentrations is a fugitive resource: produced by deep-time radioactive decay, lost to space the moment it surfaces, and required for cryogenic applications where no replacement is on the horizon.

Fossil aquifers belong in the irreplaceable category for the same reason. The Ogallala under the central United States, the North China Plain aquifer under one of the world's most populous breadbaskets, and the Guarani under South America were laid down on millennial timescales by glaciers and slow seepage. They are being drawn down at decadal speed. When they are gone, they are gone in the only sense that matters for the people downstream of them. Not gone forever, but gone for any horizon a farm or a city plans on.

There are edge cases worth naming. Sunlight is renewable; the silicon, indium, and tellurium that turn it into electricity are not, and the rare earths that anchor wind-turbine magnets are not. Atmospheric oxygen is, for practical purposes, abundant. The conditions under which atmospheric carbon dioxide remains compatible with the climate inside which civilization was built are not. The categorization is fuzzy at the margins, which is exactly why the classification has to be done seriously and revisited as we learn more. Discomfort is not a reason to skip the exercise.

The classification is not just a list. It is a set of judgments about substitution horizons that have to be made under uncertainty, by people qualified to make them, and revisited when the underlying science moves. The CRMA process names that work as a continuing administrative function rather than as a one-time legislative product; the 2023 list is the second iteration, and the next refresh is already on a schedule. Doing this seriously means treating the classification as a living instrument, the way the Federal Reserve treats its dual mandate or the way the National Institutes of Health treat their grant priorities. The point is not to settle the question once but to keep it in view.

Naming the categories does no work on its own. The work is what comes next.

## Reserve

For materials at the wide end of the substitution gap, the right move is the one the United States already makes for oil. Put a fraction aside. The Strategic Petroleum Reserve is a clumsy instrument, but the logic is sound and the precedent is established. The argument extends.

Consider rare-earth elements. The United States Geological Survey estimates global economically recoverable reserves at roughly thirty million metric tons, against annual production of around three hundred thousand metric tons. A reserve of twenty percent of recoverable supply, six million metric tons, equals about twenty years of current production at near-zero near-term cost. The materials remain in the ground, where they keep effectively forever, and the cost of the policy is the foregone royalty income on the deferred fraction. This is the cheap-insurance logic at its starkest. If the assumption that rare earths are irreplaceable turns out to be too strong, we have given up a small slice of present consumption. If it turns out to be too weak, and the substitutes do not materialize, and the country that holds the supply has the leverage that follows, we have foreclosed the option of doing things that depend on those materials at all.

The policy is not hypothetical. Japan built rare-earth reserves after a 2010 export-restriction crisis with China over the Senkaku Islands made the supply chain's brittleness legible to its industrial base, and funded the Lynas Corporation's Australian-Malaysian processing infrastructure as the alternative. South Korea built reserves after the 2019 trade dispute with Japan made the same lesson legible to its semiconductor industry. The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act sets stockpile-and-alternative-sourcing targets for the thirty-four materials it covers. The United States restarted production at MP Materials' Mountain Pass mine in California in 2018, after the consolidation of the 2000s left it with almost no domestic processing capacity. The reserve logic is being implemented around the irreplaceable materials of the current decade, but not yet for the irreplaceable materials of the decades after that.

The asymmetry between the cost of reserving and the cost of foreclosing is what makes the math work. Reserve six million metric tons of rare earths that turn out to be readily substitutable, and you have left some royalty income on the table. Fail to reserve, and the substitutes turn out not to materialize, and you have handed the country with the supply a leverage no policy in your toolkit can buy back — the downside of one error is bounded; the downside of the other is not. When the costs are that lopsided, the policy chooses itself.

The same logic extends to materials we have barely begun to extract. The Clarion-Clipperton Zone, the seabed plain between Hawaii and Mexico where polymetallic nodules sit rich in nickel, cobalt, copper, and manganese, is currently the subject of negotiations at the International Seabed Authority over whether and how to license commercial mining. The honest version of the reserve principle says this. Before opening the zone to extraction at a scale that may be irreversible, set aside the fraction that will not be touched. The current debate is not framed that way. It should be.

Reserves are not a substitute for thinking about substitutes. They are the cheap insurance that buys time while the next move runs.

## Invest

The next move is investment. Two priorities sit on the same horizon, and a serious classification of the substitution gap routes investment to both.

The first is research into what would close the gap. Photovoltaic cells already work at falling silicon and indium intensity. Wind-turbine magnets can be engineered to achieve their flux density without the rare earths currently required, and battery chemistries can displace cobalt and lithium with materials higher up the abundance curve. Recycled material flows recover what is already in service rather than mining what is not. Regenerative agriculture returns phosphorus to soils rather than running it off into eutrophied estuaries; plant-based and cellular protein decouples food security from concentrated-animal-feeding-operation throughput. None of these are speculative; all of them are funded; the question is how much of the work the price signals carry on their own and how much requires what Mariana Mazzucato, in *The Entrepreneurial State* and *Mission Economy*, has called the public-purpose entrepreneurial state, taking on the risk and choosing the direction that private capital will not.

The second priority is investment in alternative sources of supply. This is where the conversation gets uncomfortable for some readers and improbable for others, and where the irreplaceability classification does its sharpest work. If a material is genuinely on the wide end of the substitution gap, and no substitution chemistry materializes inside a planning horizon that matters, the question becomes whether there are other places to get it. Asteroid extraction is the obvious case. The metallic body 16 Psyche, currently the subject of an active NASA mission, is estimated to contain iron and platinum-group concentrations that dwarf any terrestrial reserve. The mission's purpose is scientific; the implication is economic. An honest accounting of the irreplaceable-materials inventory would route a fraction of the price wedge into the technology that would access it. Not because we are confident we will need to, but because being wrong in the cautious direction is cheaper than being wrong in the complacent one. Same asymmetry as the reserve. Same conclusion.

Renewable substitution and alternative sourcing are two legs of the same policy, not competing options. The classification that names the gap is what decides how the investment fraction is split.

The fourth move follows from the first three and is the easiest one to forget. Reassessment. None of these decisions are set once and never revisited. Substitution chemistries land or fail to land. Recovery flows scale or do not. Aquifers refill against expectation, or draw down faster than projected. The reserve fraction that looked generous in 2026 may look thin in 2046, or it may look paranoid. The classification has to be a standing piece of administrative work, not a legislative artifact that goes stale the moment it is enacted. The Norwegian fiscal rule has been re-evaluated several times since 2001. The European Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism is on a multi-year staged trajectory by design. Honest pricing assumes the price will keep being wrong, in known ways, and the instruments will keep needing to be tuned. That assumption is what separates the kind of policy this argument describes from the kind that gets packaged for a single political cycle and then left to weather.

## Border

Honest pricing runs into the international system the way it actually works.

If a country prices its extractions honestly, and adds the costs of carbon, of community displacement, and of long-tail remediation to the producer's accounts, and its trading partners do not, the country that priced honestly has handed its industries a competitive disadvantage. The cost has not gone away. It has been moved across a border, to a tailings pond in Inner Mongolia or a mangrove swamp in the Niger Delta or a hand-dug pit in Lualaba. The Baotou rare-earth processing facility's tailings pond covers approximately five square miles of radioactive slurry. The Niger Delta has been receiving spilled oil and gas-flare emissions for fifty years across thousands of square kilometers of mangrove and farmland. The Democratic Republic of Congo's artisanal cobalt mines were, at peak, employing roughly forty thousand children before reform efforts began to push the numbers down. The price tag on these places is the difference between honest pricing somewhere and the absence of honest pricing here. If the move is going to work, the trading regime has to price what crosses borders the same way it prices what is produced inside them.

The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism is the working version of this principle. CBAM was introduced in staged form starting in 2023 and entered its full compliance phase on 1 January 2026. It applies a levy at the border on imports of carbon-intensive goods equal to the carbon price the importing producer would have paid had the goods been made inside the EU. The legal machinery exists. The administrative regime is funded. The political settlement has survived three years of objection from outside the EU and three years of lobbying from inside it. The mechanism is partial; it covers a handful of carbon-intensive categories rather than the full inventory the substitution-gap classification identifies. The principle, however, has been proven. The next move is to generalize. Extend the same border-adjustment logic to the broader set of severed costs, and apply it to goods produced in jurisdictions where the producer does not pay the residual price.

This is not protectionism in the conventional sense. It does not protect domestic producers from competitors who can make the same goods more cheaply by virtue of superior process or location. It declines to give producers a competitive advantage that consists, in its entirety, of pricing the externalities at zero. The distinction matters. Adam Smith would have recognized it. He spent much of *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) on the difference between a price that reflects the cost of production and a price that has been rigged by an arrangement that captures rents the production did not earn. The honest-pricing argument is closer to Smith than to anything that gets called protectionism by the discipline he founded.

## The politics of resistance

The classification, the reserve, the investment, and the border describe accounting decisions of a kind countries make all the time. None of them are technically difficult. The question is why honest pricing has not happened, and the question has a serious answer.

Mancur Olson, in *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965), named the dynamic that makes it hard. When the beneficiaries of a policy are diffuse and the costs are concentrated, the concentrated bearers of the cost organize and the diffuse beneficiaries do not. The producer who extracts rare earths under a regime that does not price the residual costs has every incentive to lobby, organize, and finance the protection of the favorable regime. The future generations who will inherit the tailings, the carbon, and the foreclosed options have no way to lobby at all. The claim is structural, not partisan, and James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock built much of their analytical tradition on the public-choice extensions of it. Honest pricing has to be designed against this dynamic, not in spite of it. It complements the rent-seeking analysis that tradition produced rather than competing with it.

The shape of that design is visible in the difference between two recent responses to the same kind of shock. The United States in 2008, and Sweden in 1933 and again in 1992. The 2008 American financial crisis destroyed roughly eleven trillion dollars in household wealth and produced about ten million foreclosure filings over the following five years. The federal response was the Troubled Asset Relief Program at seven hundred billion dollars, plus trillions of dollars in Federal Reserve emergency liquidity and guarantees. Almost all of the deployed capital went to recapitalizing bank balance sheets. Atif Mian and Amir Sufi, in *House of Debt* (2014), showed that an alternative response, directing the same money at the household balance sheets that had taken the underwater losses, would have produced substantially more consumption recovery over 2009 through 2011, with redistributive effects benefiting the bottom forty percent of the income distribution by a factor on the order of three- to four-fold relative to top-quintile households. The architecture that was chosen was a choice — not a necessity, not the only response available, not what the arithmetic alone produced. The political-economy dynamic Olson named is how the choice got made.

Sweden took the other path twice in a century. The 1933 Swedish response to the Depression devalued the krona to reduce the real burden of debt, wrote down nominal mortgage debt for distressed households, and ran public-works employment programs that absorbed unemployed labor. The 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement institutionalized labor-employer wage bargaining at a level designed to maintain consumption. The framework survived the war, the 1973 oil shock, and the long descent into the present. In 1992, when several major Swedish banks collapsed in a domestic banking crisis, the government recapitalized them, but it captured equity in the process and recovered most of the deployed capital within five years, combined with countercyclical fiscal policy that kept household consumption from collapsing through the contraction. Sweden priced the household balance sheets the American response did not. The outcomes diverge by the exact amount the responses diverge.

William A. Darity, Jr., and A. Kirsten Mullen walked the same arithmetic across a longer horizon. *From Here to Equality* (2020) estimated the cumulative wealth deficit between Black and white American households, accounting for slavery, sharecropping, redlining, convict leasing, discriminatory homeownership and educational policy, and a century and a half of compounding exclusion, at roughly fourteen trillion dollars. That figure is the historical counterpart of the accountability accounting honest pricing would do going forward. Same logic. Different ledger.

The point of pairing these examples is not to argue that the United States can or should become Sweden. It is to show that the same shock can be metabolized by different responses, with sharply different consequences for the people who carry the shock's weight, and that the response is a choice. A society that prices honestly is one that has decided to make those choices visibly rather than invisibly.

## What works already

Honest pricing is sometimes treated as utopian; the treatment is empirically wrong. Substantial portions of the architecture are already operating, in various jurisdictions, and have been for decades.

Norway's Government Pension Fund Global was established in 1990 (renamed in 2006) to manage the rents from the country's North Sea hydrocarbon discoveries after the 1969 Ekofisk find. Total assets sit at roughly two trillion dollars, or about three hundred ninety thousand dollars per Norwegian citizen. A fiscal rule adopted in 2001 caps annual spending at about three percent of returns, with the capital itself inviolate. The rents are captured at extraction, through state licensing, taxation, and direct equity ownership of the state operating company. They are invested outside Norway to avoid distorting the domestic economy, and the returns flow back as the consumption-maintaining income of a country that has decided its sub-soil hydrocarbons are not the private property of whichever firm pulled them up.

Alaska's Permanent Fund, established in 1976, captures twenty-five percent of state mineral royalty income as an irrevocable constitutional trust. The fund currently holds about eighty-five billion dollars and has paid an annual dividend to every state resident since 1982, most recently one thousand dollars, with the dividend across the past several years ranging from one thousand to one thousand seven hundred. The rent-capture and the disbursement are both partial; twenty-five percent rather than a higher figure, dividend rather than full insurance against extraction's long tail. The structural elements are there.

Smaller-scale demonstrations cluster around the same logic. Chattanooga's municipally-owned electric utility, EPB, launched a fiber network in 2010 that delivers gigabit service citywide and up to twenty-five gigabits per second for those who want it, at prices well below the regional Comcast benchmark. Comcast sued; the Tennessee state legislature passed laws to limit EPB's expansion beyond its electric service territory; the network still exists and the city still has the asset.

The cooperative tradition has been doing similar work at larger scale for nearly seventy years. Mondragon Corporation, founded in 1956 by Father José María Arizmendiarrieta in the Basque region of Spain, operates about eighty cooperatives in the Basque region and across Spain, plus a growing international footprint, employing roughly seventy thousand workers in total, with pay ratios institutionally capped: historically near three to one, widening to six or nine to one in the largest industrial cooperatives, against a Fortune 500 ratio that approaches three hundred to one. Caja Laboral, the federation bank that anchors the cooperative network, was founded in 1959. When the flagship appliance manufacturer Fagor went bankrupt in 2013, the federation absorbed the workers into other cooperatives, and the federation itself survived.

Housing built and maintained as civic infrastructure runs the same architecture at the scale of a national capital. Vienna's Gemeindebau and cooperative housing system, which dates to Red Vienna in 1919 and survived an interwar fascist coup, Nazi occupation, and every administration since, houses roughly sixty percent of the city's population in more than two hundred thousand apartments built and maintained by the municipality, funded through a one percent payroll Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag along with tenant rents and subsidized federal loans. These are not utopian arrangements. They are working civic infrastructure of communities that decided to price the building blocks of human life differently from how the surrounding market default does.

Carbon pricing operates in over eighty jurisdictions and covers nearly thirty percent of global greenhouse emissions. American mine reclamation has been governed since 1977 by the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act, which requires reclamation bonds posted at the time of permit. The National Environmental Policy Act's environmental-impact-assessment framework has provided the legal machinery for surfacing severed costs since 1970. The system is partial, contested in places, and sometimes captured by the interests it was designed to constrain. It is also there.

A worker in Sweden and a worker in the United States face roughly comparable total tax wedges. The Tax Foundation's 2024 figures put the gap at about eleven percentage points. The American worker pays nearly as much, in total, and gets almost none of it back as guaranteed services. The Swedish worker pays a bit more and gets universal healthcare, education through university, parental leave, and longer life expectancy with less class variance. About twenty-eight million Americans are uninsured at any given time, and medical crowdfunding platforms run more than two hundred fifty thousand campaigns a year, covering on average between a quarter and forty percent of stated need. The eleven-cent difference at the margin of the tax wedge is buying a categorically different relationship between the citizen and the costs of being alive. This is what an entering wedge into honest pricing looks like at the scale of a country.

## What a framework does

A framework is not a plan. The arrangement just described would not happen because someone wrote it down. David Harvey gave the long pattern this argument addresses the name — *accumulation by dispossession*. Elinor Ostrom, whose 2009 Nobel rewarded a career documenting commons-governance design principles, spent that career on the cases where communities built institutions that priced shared resources honestly. Kate Raworth's doughnut economics has been adopted as a planning lens by Amsterdam and a growing number of other cities. Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* (2019) traced how the legal modules of property and contract systematically encode the wealth-creating side of the arrangement while leaving the cost-bearing side legally invisible. Brett Christophers' *The Price is Wrong* (2024) charted how far the gap between the price signal and the underlying allocation problem currently runs. Daniel Susskind's *Growth: A Reckoning* (2024) asked what growth, honestly understood, would have to look like to remain compatible with the conditions inside which it became possible.

The framework just described does not replace any of these. It supplies what they collectively converge on the need for. A framework will not lay reclamation bonds, write border-adjustment rules, build substitution chemistries, or vote in city councils. People do that work. What a framework gives the people doing the work is a shared vocabulary and a shared yardstick, and a way of recognizing that what they are working on is the same problem. Whether enough of the work gets done is a question each generation hands to the next.
