# Pricing Honestly

One ton of McDowell County coal, sold at the mine mouth in 1960 for four dollars and fifty cents, cost — when every severed cost was priced honestly — somewhere between five hundred and six hundred dollars. The difference is not a rounding error and it is not an opinion. It is what an accountant would find if she walked the seam back from the railcar to the headwaters of the Tug Fork and totaled what the price did not include: the black lung settlements, the orphaned highwalls, the streams whose pH has not yet been chemistry, the school taxes a county lost when the seam closed, the climate damage spread across two centuries, the medical bills paid by counties two states away when the soot landed on their lungs. The mine mouth got four-fifty. Someone else, somewhere else, often later, paid the rest.

This is not a McDowell County problem. It is a pricing problem. The price of nearly everything Americans extract, burn, harvest, or pump arrives at the cash register with a portion of its real cost removed and handed to someone else. Some of that handoff is acceptable — every transaction generates externalities the parties cannot reasonably internalize, and a society can absorb modest accounting fictions. But the structural pattern of large, persistent, decades-long handoffs from extracted communities to the rest of the country, and from the present to the future, is what makes pricing the most consequential and least examined policy lever in American political economy. If a society decided to price its extractions honestly, it would have four moves to make in order: classify what the resource is, reserve what cannot be replaced, invest in what can replace it, and adjust at the border so the honest price is not undercut by the dishonest one across the line. Each move is technically tractable. None of them is politically easy. All of them are already happening, in fragments, in places people do not generally look for an economic future.

## What the resource is

The first move is the one that gets skipped, because it sounds like academic taxonomy and because the political conversation in the United States about resources treats them as either commodities to be produced more cheaply or commodities to be regulated more strictly. The technical question, which determines what every subsequent policy can do, is whether the resource is replaceable. Three categories cover most of what matters.

The first category is resources we have a lot of, or can substitute for relatively cheaply, on the timescales that matter to a human being or a county budget. Iron is in this category. So is coal, as a fuel, now that the price of solar has fallen by an order of magnitude in fifteen years and storage has crossed the threshold of grid utility. The argument for pricing the externalities of these resources honestly is the standard environmental argument: the externalities are large and the substitutes are real, so the honest price moves consumption toward the substitute without immiserating the people who depend on the cheaper version. Carbon pricing is the canonical instrument for this category, and the global record now stretches across eighty discrete instruments — forty-three carbon taxes and thirty-seven emissions-trading systems — covering roughly twenty-eight percent of greenhouse emissions. The instruments differ in shape; the underlying logic is the same.

The second category is resources where substitution is hard, expensive, slow, or all three, but possible in principle. Rare earths sit here, by most current accounts. So do platinum group metals, cobalt for battery chemistries, and the long tail of mineral inputs the European Union now formally tracks. In 2023 the European Union enacted the Critical Raw Materials Act, which classifies thirty-four materials as strategically critical and requires member states to establish stockpiles, diversify supply, and fund substitution research. This is not a green agenda; it is a national-security agenda the European Union felt obligated to write down after watching China demonstrate, in the 2010 rare-earth export restrictions following the Senkaku Islands incident, that single-source dependence is a policy lever the source country will use. Japan funded Lynas Corporation's Australian-Malaysian processing infrastructure in direct response. South Korea built rare-earth reserves following its 2019 trade dispute with Japan. MP Materials reopened the Mountain Pass mine in California in 2018, restoring a piece of domestic rare-earth supply that the United States had lost to consolidation in the previous decade. None of these moves are speculative. They are insurance the buyers of these materials decided was cheaper than the alternative.

The third category is resources for which substitution is, as best we can tell, either impossible or so distant on a planning horizon that treating it as possible is irresponsible. Phosphorus is here. Helium, when used in cryogenics where its physical properties cannot be reproduced, is here. So are the great fossil aquifers — the Ogallala beneath the American High Plains, the North China Plain aquifer, the Guarani in South America — which recharge on millennial timescales and are being drawn down on decadal ones. Helium-3 is effectively absent from Earth's atmosphere and present in lunar regolith in concentrations that have triggered serious proposals for extraction; whether fusion reactors will ever actually need it is uncertain enough that a reservation policy looks more like cheap insurance than a research bet.

It is helpful, before going further, to name what is happening when an extractive economy treats categories one and two and three identically. It is treating the cost of being wrong as zero. For category one, where substitutes are cheap and abundant, the cost of pricing the resource as if it were finite and irreplaceable is modest — some cleaner electricity, some early retirement of capital stock that would have retired anyway. For category three, the cost of pricing the resource as if it were category one is potentially civilizational. Treating fossil aquifers as fungible with surface water has consequences that no carbon price can undo. Treating phosphorus reserves as commercial commodities subject to whatever export rules a given decade's free-trade ideology permits has consequences that no substitution research will fix in time. The cost of mis-classifying upward — treating category one as if it were category three — is mainly inefficient. The cost of mis-classifying downward is unbounded. A society that is not certain which category a given resource falls into has a strong default toward caution, for the same reason that a household does not run its only well to the gravel layer in order to settle a question about how deep the water actually goes.

This is what an honest pricing system has to do first: classify. Not every commodity needs every protection. But every commodity needs the question asked.

## Reserve what cannot be replaced

The second move is the one Americans are most quietly competent at, when they bother. The Strategic Petroleum Reserve, established in 1975 after the first oil shock, is the canonical American example. It is not a free-market mechanism. It is a public stock of physical material, held in salt caverns, capable of being drawn down in a supply emergency and topped up when conditions return to normal. The reserve has been drawn on infrequently and partially over half a century, and it has cost the Treasury essentially nothing in proportion to the catastrophe it insures against. This is what cheap insurance against bigger-than-we-think downside actually looks like in the operational sense. Not a sophisticated derivatives portfolio. Not a complicated regulatory framework. A pile of physical material in a hole in the ground, with a procedure for releasing it.

The logic generalizes. Consider the rare earths the United States Geological Survey and its counterparts now tracks. Global economically recoverable reserves are on the order of thirty million metric tons. Annual global production runs around three hundred thousand metric tons. A strategic reserve covering twenty percent of those proven reserves would be six million metric tons — about twenty years of current global production — purchasable, in current conditions, at a fraction of the cost of replacing the supply chain after a disruption. There is no operational mystery about how to do this. Japan, after 2010, did a smaller version of it. South Korea, after 2019, did a smaller version of it. The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act mandates a coordinated version of it across thirty-four materials and twenty-seven member states.

The objection to strategic reserves, in the United States and elsewhere, is rarely operational. It is ideological. The reserve costs money up front. The crisis it insures against is hypothetical, until it isn't. The political economy of reserve creation rewards patience in a way that legislative cycles do not. And the resources in question — phosphorus, helium, rare earths, cobalt — are mostly invisible to the average voter, who interacts with them only indirectly through smartphones and fertilizer and medical imaging. A politician who proposes building a strategic helium reserve is not running on a winning issue, even when the reserve would cost a fraction of a percent of what its absence would eventually cost.

This is part of why the deep-sea mineral debate in the Clarion-Clipperton Zone, in the central Pacific, is being conducted at the speed it is. The International Seabed Authority has been writing commercial mining codes for several years. Whether deep-sea polymetallic nodules become a meaningful source of cobalt and nickel is contingent on technologies and prices that are not yet stable. But the reserve logic applies: even if commercial extraction is eventually approved, the resource is in a regime where reservation against unknown future need is cheap relative to the cost of finding out, halfway into mining, that the seabed is the wrong place to look for the question's answer.

Reservation is not the same as prohibition. A strategic petroleum reserve does not stop the United States from consuming petroleum. A rare-earth reserve does not stop the United States from importing rare earths. What a reserve does is give a society a temporary buffer against discovering its own irreplaceabilities the hard way. The question is not whether reserves are useful. The question is which resources warrant them, and at what fraction of current production they should be sized, and how their fill schedules should be financed. These are technical questions with technical answers.

## Invest in what can replace it

The third move is the one most associated with the climate conversation, which tends to obscure how much broader the principle is. If a resource is in the second category — substitution is hard but possible — the honest price creates a market signal toward the substitute, and the public investment system has to ensure that the substitute actually exists by the time the substitution is needed. The market does this on its own only for the cases where the substitution timeline matches the patient-capital horizon of private firms, which is to say, in some categories and not in others.

The categories where public investment matters most are the ones where the substitute is real but the development cycle exceeds what private capital will fund on its own. Photovoltaics are now the standard example, but the lineage runs longer: the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency's predecessor agencies funded the basic research that became the internet, the Global Positioning System, and a substantial fraction of modern pharmaceuticals. The National Institutes of Health funds clinical-stage research that pharmaceutical companies would not fund on their own. The work the political economist Mariana Mazzucato has done on the entrepreneurial state — tracing the public investments that produced the underlying technologies in nearly every commercial device a typical American carries in a pocket — is by now established enough that it should not need restating, except that it keeps needing restating, because the conventional account of where innovation comes from is so durable.

The category of investment relevant to honest pricing includes the obvious substitutes — renewable electricity generation, grid-scale storage, alternative battery chemistries, recycled-material recovery — but also the less obvious ones. Regenerative agriculture is an investment in substitution for synthetic fertilizers whose nitrogen input depends on natural-gas feedstocks. Plant-based and cellular protein research is an investment in substitution for animal agriculture, whose externalities (methane, deforestation, water consumption, antibiotic resistance) are large and whose substitutes are not yet at price parity with what they would replace. Even alternative supply sources for materials we currently mine on land — the long-discussed possibility of extracting platinum-group metals from metal-rich asteroids, of which the asteroid 16 Psyche is the best-studied example — sit on the substitution side of the ledger, however speculative they look from a 2026 vantage. Whether asteroid extraction will ever be economically competitive is contingent on a long chain of technological and political conditions. The point is not that it certainly will. The point is that the substitution category includes options that look exotic at the moment of discussion and prosaic by the time the substitution actually matters.

What public investment does, in this part of the system, is buy down the gap between when a substitute is technically possible and when it is commercially competitive at scale. The cost of that buy-down is real. So is the cost of not making it: a society that decides not to invest in substitution for a category-two resource is making a bet that the resource will remain accessible for as long as the substitute would have taken to develop. That bet often turns out to be wrong, and the cost of being wrong falls disproportionately on the cohort that would have benefited from the substitute and now has to pay for the lack of one.

## Adjust at the border

The fourth move is the one that has, in the last three years, gone from a theoretical possibility to operational policy. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism began its staged implementation in 2023 and entered its full compliance phase on the first of January, 2026. The mechanism works by requiring importers of carbon-intensive goods — initially cement, iron and steel, aluminum, fertilizer, electricity, and hydrogen — to surrender certificates whose price tracks the European Union's internal carbon price. The intent is straightforward: a steelmaker in Poland pays the European carbon price; a steelmaker in a jurisdiction without a carbon price who exports to the European Union pays an equivalent fee at the border; the result is that the honest price of carbon-intensive production cannot be evaded by relocating the production to a jurisdiction with looser rules.

The broader principle is what matters. Honest pricing within a single country, in a globalized economy, is vulnerable to undercutting from jurisdictions that decline to price honestly. This is not a hypothetical vulnerability. The history of rare-earth processing is its own demonstration. The Baotou region of Inner Mongolia became the world's dominant rare-earth processing center in part because the Chinese state was willing to absorb environmental costs that other jurisdictions would not. The radioactive tailings impoundment at Baotou — approximately five square miles of slurry — is the visible artifact of a price the rest of the world's rare-earth consumers were not paying. The Niger Delta is the visible artifact of a similar dynamic in petroleum: fifty years of spilled oil have made thousands of square kilometers of mangrove and farmland uninhabitable. Artisanal cobalt mining in the Democratic Republic of Congo, which has at times involved tens of thousands of children in workforces operating outside formal labor regulation, is the visible artifact of the same dynamic in the lithium-ion supply chain.

A border adjustment is the policy instrument that prevents honest pricing from being a competitive disadvantage. It says: if you want to sell into this market, the price you pay at the border has to reflect the costs that pricing in your own jurisdiction does not reflect. The European Union has now demonstrated that such a mechanism is operationally feasible at the scale of a continental economy. The political and legal architecture for extending the principle to other categories — to a generalized substitutability-gap border adjustment, scaled to whatever the importing jurisdiction decides is being underpriced — exists in outline, in the European Union's own legal regime, and is being studied at the World Trade Organization with varying degrees of enthusiasm.

The objection to border adjustments is that they look like protectionism. So they do, and sometimes they are. The distinction that matters is whether the adjustment levels the playing field around an honestly priced cost or builds a moat around an inefficient domestic industry. The honest version of the policy is the former. The dishonest version is the latter. Distinguishing between them, in any given case, requires the same kind of accounting that the first three moves require — a clear understanding of what the actual cost of the production is, and what fraction of that cost the current price covers. Where the gap is real, the border adjustment is honest. Where the gap is invented, the border adjustment is protectionism. The accounting is the discipline that holds the policy honest.

## The politics of resistance

None of the four moves is intellectually controversial in the way that, say, the existence of human-caused climate change is intellectually controversial in American politics. The moves are controversial because they redistribute costs. A society that begins pricing its extractions honestly is, by definition, beginning to allocate previously externalized costs to the parties that currently receive the benefits of underpricing — to the firms that profit from cheap inputs, to the consumers who pay grocery and gasoline prices that omit a portion of what the inputs actually cost, to the financial-system intermediaries whose models depend on the cost-omission persisting. Each of those constituencies has a political reason to oppose the move. Each of them is, in the political-economy idiom that Mancur Olson named in 1965 in *The Logic of Collective Action*, a concentrated interest facing a diffuse one.

The general dynamic is well understood. Concentrated interests have low coordination costs and high per-member stakes. Diffuse interests have high coordination costs and low per-member stakes. The result is that, in a system where political access is broadly proportional to coordination capacity, concentrated interests usually win the policy fights they care most about. The American policy record over the last four decades is, to a substantial extent, a record of this dynamic playing out across sector after sector, with the externality-bearing public on the losing side of fights it did not know it was in.

It is worth being precise about what this is and is not. James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock, in *The Calculus of Consent* (1962) and the rent-seeking literature that grew out of Tullock's 1967 work on tariffs, monopolies, and theft, laid the analytical foundation for understanding why the political process generates the patterns of concentrated benefit and diffuse cost that the pricing-honest framework is attempting to reverse. The Public Choice tradition and an honest-pricing framework are not natural antagonists. They are complementary tools. The Public Choice tradition gives a precise account of why the work is hard. The pricing-honest framework gives a precise account of what the work is.

The strongest American case for the political-economy point is the response to the 2008 financial crisis, because the architecture chosen was a policy choice rather than a technical necessity, and the alternatives have been worked through in detail. Approximately eleven trillion dollars in American household wealth was destroyed in the crisis. Approximately ten million foreclosures followed over the next five years. The federal response — the seven hundred billion dollars of direct authorization under the Troubled Asset Relief Program, the trillions in Federal Reserve emergency liquidity, the targeted bailouts of specific firms — was overwhelmingly directed at recapitalizing bank balance sheets rather than at restructuring household debt. Atif Mian and Amir Sufi, in *House of Debt* (2014), worked through the welfare-economic counterfactual at length. Their conclusion was that a household-debt-restructuring response at the scale the crisis actually required would have produced substantially more consumption recovery in 2009 through 2011, with redistributive effects benefiting the bottom forty percent of the income distribution by something on the order of three to four times the bank-rescue architecture's effects on the same population.

That counterfactual was not chosen. The choice that was made reflected a political economy in which the parties whose balance sheets were rescued had standing in the rescue conversation and the parties whose homes were foreclosed did not. There is no economic doctrine, including the most orthodox Chicago-school doctrine, that requires the rescue architecture to have been shaped that way. It was shaped that way because the concentrated interests at the bargaining table were able to coordinate. The diffuse interest — homeowners with underwater mortgages spread across forty thousand jurisdictions — was not.

The Swedish response to its own financial crises is the negative space against which the American choice can be read. In 1933, in response to the Great Depression, Sweden devalued its currency, which reduced real debt burdens for the household sector. Through the rest of the decade, mortgage-modification programs wrote down nominal mortgage debt for distressed households, and public-works employment programs absorbed the unemployed. In 1938, at Saltsjöbaden, the labor federation and the employers' federation institutionalized wage bargaining at a level designed to maintain household consumption. The 1938 agreement survived the Second World War, the 1973 oil shock, the 1992 Swedish banking crisis, and the intervening shifts in Swedish political composition. In 1992, when several major Swedish banks failed, the Swedish government recapitalized them on terms that captured their equity, allowed for an orderly winding-down of the institutions that could not be saved, and combined the response with countercyclical fiscal policy that maintained household consumption through the recession. The Swedish government recovered most of its deployed capital within five years.

The point is not that the American political system should imitate the Swedish one. The histories diverge enough that direct imitation is a category error. The point is that the choices were choices. The political economy of the 2008 American response, and the policy economy of the 1933 and 1992 Swedish responses, were each rationally explicable in their own contexts. What an honest pricing system gives is a vocabulary for naming the costs that get borne and by whom, so that the next round of choices can be made with the costs visible rather than externalized.

The longer history of who bears costs has its own American body of work. William Darity and A. Kirsten Mullen, in *From Here to Equality* (2020), worked out the cumulative wealth deficit between Black and white American households across the policy regimes of slavery, sharecropping, convict leasing, redlining, the discriminatory administration of federal homeownership and education programs, and the labor-market exclusions that compounded across generations. Their figure for the cumulative gap is approximately fourteen trillion dollars. The argument is not a separate argument from the pricing-honest one. It is a continuous extension of it. The same accounting that asks whether a ton of coal costs four-fifty or five hundred and fifty asks whether a federal mortgage program that excluded Black applicants for thirty years had a cost, who bore it, and whether any portion of the resulting wealth differential is still bearable as a market outcome rather than as a policy debt.

These are not separate questions. They are the same question — what costs are being severed from what prices, and who is paying the difference — applied to different categories of extraction. A coal seam, a federal lending program, a continental atmosphere, a fossil aquifer. The accounting discipline is the same in each case. So is the political-economy obstacle.

## What already works

It is easy, given the political-economy diagnosis, to write the rest of the essay as a despair note. The diagnosis suggests that nothing in the architecture is movable. Which is one reading; it is also wrong on the evidence. The architecture is movable, in fragments, in places.

The clearest American demonstration of the principle is the Alaska Permanent Fund. Established in 1976, the fund captures twenty-five percent of state mineral royalty income as an irrevocable constitutional trust. Its assets in 2025 are in the range of eighty-three to eighty-six billion dollars. Since 1982 it has paid an annual dividend to every Alaskan resident. The dividend's recent range has run from approximately a thousand dollars in 2025 to seventeen hundred in 2024 (including an Energy Relief Payment) and thirteen hundred in 2023. The Alaska Permanent Fund is, in the operational sense, a rent-capture mechanism that returns a portion of extracted resource value to the residents in whose name the resource was extracted. It is constitutionally insulated against political pressure. It is administered with low overhead. It is, in the local political culture, a piece of state pride that crosses partisan lines.

The international comparison is the Norway Government Pension Fund Global, which has, in the quarter-century since its 2001 fiscal rule was established, accumulated approximately two trillion dollars in assets — about three hundred ninety thousand dollars per Norwegian citizen. The Norwegian rule caps annual spending from the fund at roughly three percent of returns. The capital itself is treated as inviolate. Rents are captured through state licenses, taxation, and direct ownership of the state operating company; assets are invested outside Norway to avoid distorting the domestic economy. The fund is, again, a working demonstration that an extracted resource can generate a public asset on the order of magnitude of the country's annual gross domestic product, held in trust against the moment the resource itself runs out.

The municipal-utility example is Chattanooga, Tennessee, where the public electric utility known as EPB launched a fiber-optic network in 2010 and now offers gigabit service across the city, with twenty-five-gigabit service available where it is wanted. The project was sued by Comcast and the Tennessee state legislature passed laws restricting EPB's ability to extend the service beyond its electric-utility territory, both of which are themselves data points about how concentrated incumbents respond when a public alternative starts to work. The service exists. The city's broadband economy has measurably benefited from it. The political resistance is part of the demonstration, not a refutation of it.

The cooperative-ownership example is Mondragon, in the Basque region of northern Spain, founded in 1956 by the priest José María Arizmendiarrieta. Mondragon today operates approximately eighty-one self-governing worker-owned cooperatives in Spain, with about seventy thousand workers and a substantial international footprint. The cooperative federation has its own bank, Caja Laboral, founded in 1959. Mondragon's flagship appliance manufacturer, Fagor, went bankrupt in 2013; the workers were absorbed into other cooperatives in the federation and the federation itself survived. The pay ratio inside the cooperatives is institutionally capped — historically at three to one, currently in the range of six to one and at most nine to one in the largest industrial cooperatives. The Fortune 500 ratio is on the order of three hundred to one. Mondragon is not a utopia and would not claim to be. It is a working demonstration that a worker-owned firm structure can scale into the manufacturing economy without collapsing into either inefficiency or into a quasi-traditional ownership pattern.

The housing example is Vienna, where roughly sixty percent of the population lives in municipal Gemeindebau or non-profit cooperative housing. The total stock is somewhere on the order of four hundred thousand units, of which about two hundred twenty thousand are city-owned Gemeindebau apartments and the balance are subsidized non-profit cooperative dwellings. The system was founded in 1919, during the Red Vienna period, and has survived the interwar fascist coup, Nazi occupation, the partition of Austria, and every postwar administration. It is funded through a one percent dedicated payroll contribution called the Wohnbauförderungsbeitrag (rising to one and a half percent effective 2026), supplemented by tenant rents and subsidized federal loans. The Vienna model is not transposable in one piece to American conditions. The structural principle — dedicated long-cycle financing for a class of capital stock that the private market reliably underproduces — is.

The carbon-pricing record discussed earlier sits in the same category as these cases. So does the United States' own Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977, which requires coal operators to post reclamation bonds and to restore surface-mined land to a defined standard after extraction. The National Environmental Policy Act, the federal Environmental Impact Statement regime, is the procedural backbone that brings public visibility to the cost-bearing question for federal actions. Neither SMCRA nor NEPA does what an honest pricing system would do at the limit, but both are partial implementations of the principle, and both have decades of operational record to learn from.

It is worth pausing on what the eleven-cent difference looks like. The single average-wage worker in the United States pays a total tax wedge of roughly thirty percent. The same worker in Sweden pays roughly forty-two percent. The eleven-cent gap, at the level of national tax-to-GDP comparison, runs to about fifteen percentage points. What the eleven cents purchases on the Swedish side — universal healthcare, free university education with monthly student stipends, four hundred eighty days of parental leave per family, subsidized childcare, housing support, a measurably longer life expectancy with measurably less class variance, a stronger social-trust environment — is a real list. The American side of the comparison has, alongside its lower tax wedge, approximately twenty-eight million people without health insurance at any given moment, and a medical-crowdfunding industry that runs more than a quarter of a million campaigns annually, with average campaigns covering only twenty-five to forty percent of the stated need. The point is not that Americans should adopt the Swedish tax wedge. The point is that the eleven cents has a destination. In one system it pays for a particular set of services that have a particular set of consequences; in the other system it pays for itself, in the sense that the cost-bearing the other system socializes is, in the American case, dispersed onto households at the level of the medical-crowdfunding industry, the foreclosure-rate variance, the longevity-gap statistics. A pricing system is also a system for naming what the cost-bearing actually looks like in either configuration.

## What property rights can and cannot do

A pricing argument cannot avoid the property-rights tradition, and would not want to. The strongest claim in the tradition is Ronald Coase's, in "The Problem of Social Cost" (1960): in the absence of transaction costs, the parties to an externality can bargain to an efficient outcome regardless of how the property rights are initially assigned. The Coase theorem is the cleanest available statement of the proposition that, if the institutional plumbing can be made cheap enough, the market can handle the externality problem on its own.

Elinor Ostrom, in *Governing the Commons* (1990), and in the long body of empirical work that earned her the 2009 Nobel in Economic Sciences, demonstrated that commons can in fact be governed sustainably under a defined set of design principles. The Ostrom case studies — the alpine meadows of Switzerland, the irrigation systems of the Philippines, the inshore fisheries of Maine — are not folk anecdotes. They are detailed institutional studies of arrangements that have operated over generations because the people who designed them solved the practical problems of monitoring, enforcement, boundary definition, and dispute resolution that the textbook treatment of common-pool resources tends to skip.

What Coase and Ostrom together establish is that, where the conditions for property-rights solutions can be met, those solutions work and should be preferred. What an honest pricing framework adds — and does not subtract — is a treatment of the cases where the conditions cannot be met. There are at least three categories where they cannot. The first is intergenerational foreclosure: the people who would bear the cost of an extraction decision being wrong have not yet been born, and cannot be parties to the bargain. The second is transboundary externality at civilizational scale: the consequences of a fossil-aquifer drawdown or a continental-atmosphere alteration are not negotiated between identifiable parties; they are dispersed across hundreds of jurisdictions and billions of people, none of whom is in the room when the relevant decisions are made. The third is information asymmetry at civilizational scale: the parties whose well-being is at stake cannot, in many cases, have the technical information about the resource flows that the parties making the decisions have, and the cost of correcting the asymmetry exceeds the value any individual party can capture from correcting it. These are not Coase-tractable conditions. They are not Ostrom-tractable conditions either, in the standard sense, although Ostrom's later work on polycentric governance at multiple scales is one of the more productive engagements with what to do when the standard conditions do not hold. They are conditions where a pricing instrument with a public-purpose component is the right tool, not because the market is wrong in principle but because the market is structurally incomplete in this category of case.

The Public Choice tradition's contribution, at this junction, is a warning. Any pricing instrument with a public-purpose component creates a rent that the political process will compete for. The competition is the rent-seeking dynamic Tullock named. The instrument design has to anticipate it. The Alaska Permanent Fund's constitutional insulation, the Norwegian fiscal rule's spending cap, the Vienna housing system's dedicated payroll source, the European Union's CBAM's tracked compliance regime — each of these is, in part, an instrument-design response to the political-economy problem of how to keep an honest pricing instrument from being captured by the same dynamics that produced the dishonest pricing it was built to correct.

## What a framework does

This is a sketch, not a program. The four moves — classify, reserve, invest, adjust at the border — are not a legislative agenda. They are the analytical decomposition of a problem that, in the policy conversation, tends to get treated either piecemeal (a carbon tax here, a critical-minerals stockpile there, a sovereign wealth fund proposal in a state legislative session that closes without action) or holistically in a register that elides the technical questions ("price the externalities") in favor of the political ones ("change the system"). The work of an honest pricing framework, if it has any work to do, is to insist that the technical questions and the political questions are the same question seen from two sides.

David Harvey's vocabulary of accumulation by dispossession names a portion of what an honest pricing system would measure, and Harvey's diagnostic register is one of the more durable contributions to the larger conversation. Ostrom's work on commons governance is the empirical anchor for the proposition that a society can do better than the conventional binaries of state versus market when designing the institutions that allocate access to shared resources. Kate Raworth's *Doughnut Economics* (2017) names a planning frame — staying within ecological ceilings while ensuring social floors — that the city of Amsterdam has formally adopted as its development guideline and that other cities are studying. Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* (2019) traces, in granular detail, the legal modules by which capital has historically been encoded across jurisdictions in ways that systematically privilege some claims over others. Brett Christophers's *The Price is Wrong* (2024) documents, with care, the disconnection between renewable-energy prices and renewable-energy investment that makes the price-only theory of the energy transition inadequate. Daniel Susskind's *Growth: A Reckoning* (2024) reframes the growth question as one of redirection rather than rejection.

The framework I have been describing is one more entry in this lineage, not a departure from it. Its closest thematic neighbors are Mariana Mazzucato's work on value, which reframes the question of where economic value comes from in a way that makes the cost-severance question askable in the first place, and Christophers's pricing diagnosis, which makes the gap between price and consequence visible in a sector that should have been the easiest case for the conventional price-mechanism theory. The framework's distinctive emphasis — that the cost-severance pattern is generalizable across resource categories, that the classification step is the prerequisite for getting the response right, and that the institutional pieces of an honest pricing response already exist in functioning fragments across multiple jurisdictions — is a contribution to a conversation that has many participants and no single canonical text. The framework neither completes the conversation nor replaces it.

What Adam Smith would have recognized, reading any of this, is that none of it is a departure from the market mechanism. It is the completion of the mechanism. The market price, in *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), is the most efficient instrument the social technology has yet produced for coordinating the production and distribution of goods, on the condition that the price reflects the actual cost. Smith was clear that, where the price does not reflect the cost, the mechanism fails — and he was clear that public action could be necessary in those cases. The work of making the price reflect the cost is the work of completing the mechanism, not of replacing it. A society that has decided to price its extractions honestly has not abandoned the market. It has begun to take the market seriously.

The framework will not do this on its own. No framework ever does. What it does is give the people who do the work a common language and a common instrument to do the work with. Whether the work gets done is the question every generation answers for the one that follows. McDowell County, in 1960, was a place where the answer was given on terms its residents did not control and whose costs are still being paid sixty-six years later. The next round of decisions about coal, about rare earths, about phosphorus, about the Ogallala, about the cobalt in the smartphones that are reading this, will be made on terms that include whatever vocabulary the conversation has by then assembled to describe what is being decided. The vocabulary is the work. The instruments are the work. Whether what gets priced honestly are the things that matter most, and whether the cost-bearing falls where the cost-benefit actually accrues, is the work too. None of it is finished. All of it is moveable. Some of it is already underway in places people do not generally look for an economic future, and the test of the framework is whether it can name what is already working so that the next attempt has somewhere to begin.
