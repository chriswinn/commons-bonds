# Pricing Honestly

A ton of coal came out of McDowell County, West Virginia, in 1960 and sold at the mine mouth for $4.71, just under five dollars. That was the price. It was not the cost. Add up everything that ton took out of the world and never paid back, and the honest number is more than five hundred dollars, a conservative floor, nearly all of it carbon. The black lung that shortened a miner's breathing. The streams that ran orange. The mountains taken down to stumps. The carbon dioxide that will sit in the atmosphere for centuries, warming a planet none of the people in that 1960 transaction will live to see. The price was just under five dollars. The cost was more than a hundred times that. The difference did not vanish. The value went to the people who captured it. The cost went to the miner, the community, and the river.

Nothing about this required a villain, and that is the part worth sitting with. The market did what markets are built to do: it found the price two willing parties would accept that day. About the costs that land on people absent from the table, it is silent by design. Ronald Coase, in "The Problem of Social Cost" (1960), showed how much of the problem bargaining can repair. Where the affected parties can meet and deal, they will price the harm between themselves, and the property-rights tradition that grew from his insight is right as far as it reaches. It reaches only the parties who can get to the table. The community downstream was never offered a contract. The river cannot hold one. And the future, which carried the largest stake of anyone in that ton of coal, had not been born. An honest price would have to account for what the absent would have bid for the lungs, the streams, and the centuries. Coase mapped the bargain. The unfinished work is the accounting for the bidders who could not show up.

I spent twenty years inside institutions whose ledgers balanced this way, federal agencies and the medical-research and contracting worlds that surround them, budgets whose costs were managed by being moved onto people outside the building. The work, considered narrowly, was honest. What I learned is that a ledger like that never announces itself. The books close. The numbers reconcile. The orange stream is in another state, the shortened breath is in another decade, and nothing on the page asks after either one.

The diagnosis, by itself, is old. Karl Polanyi, in *The Great Transformation* (1944), watched land and labor being treated as ordinary commodities and named the damage that follows. Elinor Ostrom showed that communities can govern shared resources without ruining them. Herman Daly insisted that the economy live inside the biosphere's budget. John Hartwick worked out what a generation that consumes its resource wealth owes the ones that come after. Each holds a piece of what economics is for, and the diagnostic work is largely done. What the tradition never built is the instrument: every handed-off cost on one ledger, carried through to the resources that cannot be replaced, in a form a government could levy, hold, and spend. The principle is simple to state. The price of a thing should carry every cost its taking produces, including the costs now handed off. Acting on it asks four moves of a society, roughly in order. Classify what you are taking. Hold some of it back. Invest in needing less of it. Adjust at the border so the cost travels with the good. Then look at what happened and start again.

The moves are less radical than they sound; most of them are already running somewhere. Nothing in physics fixes the gap between five dollars and five hundred. Societies set it, and a society could set it honestly.

## Classify

In the Texas Panhandle, farmers can stand on their own land and watch the irrigation pivots in the next county come off the fields one at a time, because the wells have stopped drawing what they used to draw. Nothing above ground has changed. The change is underneath, in the Ogallala aquifer, and it is the kind of change the first move exists to catch: not everything we take is the same kind of taking.

Some materials are common, substitutable, and effectively unlimited at any scale that matters. Iron is the fourth most abundant element in the crust. Ordinary sand and gravel are nearly everywhere. Burn through the convenient deposits and the price rises, and the rising price does its ordinary work, summoning new supply and substitutes. These materials need honest pricing too, because extracting them still does damage the price ignores. They do not need to be hoarded. Nobody runs out of iron.

Other materials sit at the far end. They do a job nothing else can do, they exist in workable concentrations in only a few places, and once gone or contaminated they do not come back on any timescale a civilization can plan around. Phosphorus is the clearest case: essential to agriculture because it is essential to life, no replacement for its biological role, the workable deposits concentrated in a handful of places. Helium is another; the helium-3 that certain fusion designs would use is essentially absent from Earth's atmosphere yet relatively abundant in lunar regolith, so holding it back turns on whether the fusion application ever arrives. And there are the fossil aquifers, groundwater laid down over thousands of years that recharges so slowly it is closer to a mineral than to rain. The Ogallala under the High Plains. The aquifer under the North China Plain. The Guarani in South America. Pump them faster than their millennial recharge and the accurate word for what you are doing is mining. This is the boundary where Ostrom's great subject hands off to a different one. The commons she documented, fisheries, forests, irrigation systems, regenerate when governed well; her design principles tell a community how to share a flow. A fossil aquifer drawn past its recharge is a stock being foreclosed, and the foreclosure is what the price has to carry.

In between sit the materials that are replaceable in principle and strategically critical in practice: supply concentrated, substitution slow or expensive, a sudden cutoff a national emergency. The European Union has done the useful work of writing the middle category down. Its Critical Raw Materials Act, in force since May 2024, lists 34 materials as critical and designates 17 of them strategic, with programs to stockpile them and to develop alternative sources. Rare-earth elements are the marquee case, with the platinum-group metals close behind. Despite the name, they are not especially rare in the crust; the problem is the refining, difficult and dirty, and the world let a single country corner most of the capacity, which converts a chemistry problem into a geopolitics problem.

Call the thing that separates these categories the substitution gap: the distance between a material and its nearest replacement, measured in time, money, and feasibility. For iron the gap is trivial and the market handles it. For phosphorus the gap is, at present, infinite. Most materials a modern economy worries about sit somewhere between, and classification means knowing where. You cannot decide how much of something to hold back until you know how badly you would miss it.

The genuinely renewable resources mark the outer boundary, worth drawing exactly. Extraction hands a cost forward when what is consumed forecloses future access faster than restoration can replace it. For the non-renewables, that is every ton. For the renewables it happens only when the harvest outruns the regeneration; within the regeneration rate, the handed-forward cost is zero. Sunlight arrives whether we use it or not. Wind blows whether we harvest it or not. The trouble appears at the edges, where someone captures the flow and converts an open commons into a private toll. The sun is free; the land you must stand on to collect it is not. The wind is free; the grid you must connect to is not. Even the inexhaustible resources have chokepoints, and the chokepoints are where the handing-off happens.

## Reserve

Once you know what cannot be replaced, the second move nearly states itself: hold some of it back. The logic is a fire extinguisher's. Either you never need it and have paid a small storage cost for nothing, or the day comes when it is worth many times what it cost to hold. The downside of reserving too much is small and bounded. The downside of reserving too little is large and, in the bad cases, unbounded. When mistakes are that lopsided, you do not need exact odds to know which way to lean.

For rare earths, the arithmetic comes close to free. Take round figures of the right shape, say 30 million metric tons of recoverable reserves worldwide against 300,000 tons of annual production; the survey numbers move year to year, the proportions much less. A reserve of 20 percent of what is known would be 6 million tons, roughly 20 years of current world production. Because the material is already in the ground, holding it costs almost nothing. No warehouse gets built. A country simply declines to dig, which is the cheapest storage there is.

We already run this institution for oil: strategic petroleum reserves are a buffer against the day the ordinary supply is interrupted, held on exactly this insurance logic. The harder lesson of the last fifteen years is that the logic runs far past oil, and the countries that learned it learned it the hard way. In 2010, after a confrontation in the waters near the Senkaku Islands, rare-earth exports from the dominant supplier to Japan were curtailed. The shock pushed Japan to help fund the Lynas Corporation's mining in Australia and processing in Malaysia, an alternative supply line built where there had been near-total dependence. In the United States, the Mountain Pass mine in California, the country's main domestic rare-earth source, had gone quiet while the refining business migrated overseas; it was acquired by MP Materials in 2017 and restarted in 2018. Europe wrote the whole lesson into law with the Critical Raw Materials Act. Each case is a country discovering, after a scare, that it had priced a critical material as though it were abundant and substitutable when it was neither. Reserving is the honest price arriving before the scare instead of after it.

Live cases are still being argued, the deep seabed of the Clarion-Clipperton Zone among them, where the question is whether to extract a contested commons at all. Those are the cases where holding back until we understand what would be destroyed is the cautious choice and the cheap one.

## Invest

Reserving buys time; the third move spends it. A battery that does not need cobalt is a mine that never has to open. That is the first investment, substitution, the work of needing less of the irreplaceable thing. Better photovoltaics, wind, and storage cut how much of any fuel a society burns. Alternative battery chemistries cut dependence on the metals today's batteries demand. Recovering and recycling the material flows that now end in landfills turns waste back into supply. Regenerative agriculture and the emerging plant and cellular proteins cut the land and water and nutrient load of feeding people. None of it is guaranteed to work, which is the argument for starting early. A breakthrough cannot be summoned in the year it is needed; it is funded across the years it is not.

One objection deserves to be met squarely, because the people who study energy markets press it with reason: price signals alone will not carry a transition. Building the replacement economy is an investment problem, and a penalty on the old thing does not automatically finance the new one. The observation is correct, and it cuts against treating a carbon price as the whole answer, which the four moves never do. Investment has to be aimed, and an honest ledger is the aiming instrument. It makes the standing subsidies to extraction stop looking like neutrality, and it stops a market from quietly discounting every substitute against an incumbent whose costs are priced at zero.

The far edge of the supply map is off the planet, and it belongs in an openly speculative register; nothing out there is a policy for this year. But the logic runs all the way out. An object like 16 Psyche, a metal-rich asteroid, sits where extraction would harm no community and wreck no ecosystem, which means the honest price of metal pulled from a lifeless rock runs far below the honest price of the same metal torn out of an inhabited valley, before the launch cost is even counted. Eventually that gap is a reason to look up, though no excuse for slackening the nearer work; substitution and recycling will carry any timescale we can presently plan.

The investment move is where the program stops sounding like restraint, because a society that needs less of what cannot be replaced has enlarged what it can safely do.

## Border

In Baotou, in Inner Mongolia, there are kitchen windows that look out across a narrow band of houses at the tailings impoundment left by rare-earth processing, a lake of radioactive and toxic slurry covering some 4 square miles, about 10 square kilometers. That lake is the unpriced cost of the cheap magnets in devices sold everywhere. A society can make every move above inside its own borders and still buy that lake back, one container of magnets at a time. So the fourth move reaches across the line.

The mechanism has a working prototype. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, introduced in staged form beginning in 2023 and entering its operational compliance phase at the start of 2026, puts a price at the border on the carbon embedded in certain imports. Its purpose is to stop a predictable evasion: make pollution expensive at home and production migrates to wherever it is still free, leaving the planet no better off and your own industries worse off. A border adjustment closes the loophole by pricing the cost wherever it was incurred. Carbon pricing is no longer fringe. By the World Bank's *State and Trends of Carbon Pricing 2025* accounting, around 80 carbon-pricing instruments operate worldwide, taxes and trading systems together covering on the order of 28 percent of global greenhouse emissions. The border adjustment is what makes a domestic carbon price durable in an open economy.

The principle generalizes past carbon, though not for free. The carbon mechanism works because embedded carbon can be measured; each further cost has to earn the same measurable basis, which is real administrative work, a real constraint, and no license to price imports at whatever number a regulator prefers. The case for doing the work is what the work would price. In the Niger Delta, half a century of oil spillage has left thousands of square kilometers of mangrove and farmland ruined; there are fishermen there whose grandfathers fished creeks their sons never will. In the Democratic Republic of Congo, the cobalt that powers the global battery economy has at its worst drawn tens of thousands of children into artisanal mining. These costs are real and enormous, and they are subtracted from the lives of the people nearest the extraction while appearing on no buyer's invoice. A border that priced them would do for trade what the honest ton does for a single transaction: the cost would travel with the good.

## The politics of resistance

If the four moves are this reasonable, why have they not happened? Not for want of being thought of. Mancur Olson supplied the durable answer in *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965): a small group with a large concentrated stake will out-organize a large group with a small diffuse stake almost every time, even when the diffuse group is far larger. The few who profit from an arrangement know exactly what they would lose if it changed, and they will spend to defend it. The many who pay the diffuse cost each pay a little, often without knowing, and cannot easily coordinate to object. Honest pricing is the textbook case. The benefit of cheap extraction concentrates in the firms and shareholders who capture it now; the cost spreads across communities, ecosystems, and generations who are not organized and, in the case of the future, cannot be. No conspiracy is required. The arithmetic of organizing favors the five-dollar price and works against the five-hundred-dollar truth.

The same political economy has been beaten before, and the cleanest way to see it is to set two crises side by side. When the United States met the collapse of 2008, the Federal Reserve's flow-of-funds accounts recorded the destruction of roughly $11 trillion in American household wealth. Roughly 4.1 million households went through completed foreclosures in the years that followed, by the tallies of CoreLogic and the other mortgage-data firms, with foreclosure filings reaching millions more. The direct federal response, the Troubled Asset Relief Program, authorized about $700 billion, and the Federal Reserve added trillions more in emergency liquidity and guarantees; nearly all of that capital recapitalized bank balance sheets rather than restructuring the household debt at the center of the crisis. Atif Mian and Amir Sufi, in *House of Debt* (2014), measured what that choice cost: their analysis of household leverage and consumption showed that restructuring the debt at the scale the crisis demanded would have produced a stronger recovery, aimed at the households that needed it rather than the top of the distribution. No villainy is needed to explain any of this. An architecture was chosen, and another architecture was available.

Sweden had shown the other one, twice. In 1933, in the depths of the Depression, it devalued its currency, lightening real debt burdens across the economy; through the middle of the decade it wrote down nominal mortgage debt for distressed households and put the unemployed to work on public projects; and in 1938 the Saltsjöbaden Agreement institutionalized wage bargaining between labor and employers at a level meant to maintain consumption, a settlement durable enough to survive a world war and the oil shocks. When Sweden's own banking crisis arrived in 1992, the state recapitalized its failing banks but took equity in exchange, recovered most of the deployed capital within a few years, and aimed fiscal policy at holding up household consumption. Two countries, two crises, two architectures. One protected balance sheets. One protected households. Neither outcome was inevitable, and an honest pricing system is, in part, the habit of seeing a bill before it comes due rather than fighting over who pays it afterward.

A ledger that can see a bill coming can also read one long overdue. Forward, it prices what has not yet been taken: the ore still in the ground, the carbon not yet burned, the aquifer not yet drawn down. Backward, it reads costs already paid by the wrong people. The most worked-out accounting in that direction belongs to William Darity, Jr., and A. Kirsten Mullen, who in the second edition of *From Here to Equality* and a 2022 *Journal of Economic Perspectives* accounting tally the cumulative wealth deficit between Black and white American households, built by slavery, sharecropping, redlining, convict leasing, and a long sequence of discriminatory policy, at a figure on the order of $14 trillion. That number is the same kind of object as the gap between five dollars and five hundred: a cost severed from the people who captured the benefit and carried by the people who bore the harm, compounding across generations because no honest ledger ever recorded it as owed. Their work demonstrates that the backward page belongs on the same ledger as the forward one, written with the same discipline. And the deficit does not stop at money. A 2022 study in *JAMA Network Open*, by a research team that included Darity, put Black Americans' median life expectancy at 77.5 years against 81.5 for white Americans, and found that adjusting for differences in wealth made the racial difference in survival disappear. The severed cost is paid, in the end, in years.

The people best placed to write that page are already writing it. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not mine to do, and I leave that work to those who are continuing it. What the framework offers is the ledger.

A tradition that arrives from the opposite political direction lands in a usefully compatible place. The Public Choice economists, James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock foremost among them, built a body of work on the very dynamic Olson named, concentrated interests capturing rules and bending public arrangements toward private advantage. Read honestly, that analysis is an account of why pricing the commons is hard, and it belongs in the toolkit of anyone serious about the problem rather than in an opposing camp.

## What already works

The standing objection is that all of this is utopian. It would be stronger if the pieces were not already running, separately, in ordinary places, built by ordinary political coalitions and surviving ordinary politics for decades. Most of what the four moves require is recognition rather than invention: noticing that these scattered working parts are versions of the same idea, and connecting them.

Norway treats its oil as a finite inheritance rather than as income. Petroleum revenue flows into the Government Pension Fund Global, which has grown to roughly $2 trillion, near $390,000 for every Norwegian citizen, invested abroad so the money does not distort the home economy. A fiscal rule caps what the government may draw in a normal year at the fund's expected real return, tightened in 2017 from the original 4 percent to 3 percent, and the capital itself stays untouchable. Alaska built a smaller version of the same instinct. Since 1976, at least a quarter of the state's mineral royalty income has gone into a constitutionally protected permanent fund, holding about $89 billion as of 2026, and since 1982 every resident has drawn an annual dividend, in recent years between roughly $1,000 and $1,700. Both are line items in real budgets, defended by voters who would not give them up.

Ownership is a second wedge. In the Basque country of northern Spain, a Catholic priest named José María Arizmendiarrieta founded what became the Mondragon Corporation in 1956, a federation of worker-owned cooperatives now numbering dozens of enterprises and tens of thousands of workers. Pay is capped by rule, the ratio between highest and lowest earners held to single digits where the comparable figure in a large American corporation runs into the hundreds. The federation built its own cooperative bank, the Caja Laboral, in 1959, which is part of why it endured. When its flagship appliance maker, Fagor, failed in 2013, the federation absorbed many of the workers into other cooperatives rather than shedding them. Seventy years on, it handles failure differently because it is owned differently.

Housing is a third. Vienna has been building and maintaining municipal and cooperative housing since the era of Red Vienna in 1919, and today about 60 percent of the city's residents live in municipal or non-profit cooperative homes held off the speculative market, funded in part by a dedicated payroll levy, historically about 1 percent, a small steady contribution whose existence is the structural point even as the rate moves. The system survived an interwar fascist coup, Nazi occupation, and every administration since. And an American city shows the same municipal instinct at network scale. Chattanooga's public electric utility launched a citywide fiber network in 2010 and offered gigabit service to everyone, multi-gigabit now where it is wanted, treating connectivity as the kind of good a public utility provides. The incumbent cable company sued, and the Tennessee legislature restricted how far the network could expand. The resistance is Olson's dynamic made visible, a concentrated incumbent against a diffuse public benefit. The utility built the thing anyway, and people use it.

These wedges are the arrangements Elinor Ostrom spent a career documenting: a community governing a shared resource by rules it owns, monitored by the people who live with the results. The funds and the fiber are her design principles in modern dress. What the four moves add is the half her cases were never asked to cover, the stocks that do not regenerate, where the task is pricing a foreclosure rather than sharing a flow.

One more comparison punctures a comfortable assumption about what honest accounting would cost an ordinary household. Americans think of themselves as lightly taxed next to the European welfare states, and at headline rates that is true, but the OECD's *Taxing Wages* accounting puts the total tax wedge on an average single worker around 30 percent in the United States against roughly 42 percent in Sweden. For that difference the Swede receives universal health care, tuition-free university with a living stipend, generous parental leave, subsidized childcare, and a longer life expectancy with less variation by class. The American, paying not so much less, takes on the obligation to arrange and finance most of that privately, with roughly 28 million people uninsured at any given moment by the Census Bureau's count, and a charitable economy grown up to fill the gap. Berliner and Kenworthy's 2017 study of medical crowdfunding found more than 250,000 campaigns a year, most of them raising only a quarter to a third of what the campaigner said they needed. The difference between the two systems is less the size of the bill than what the bill buys, and who is left to absorb the cost when it buys little.

There is more in this register. Mining-reclamation bonds make an operator post money against restoring the land. Environmental-impact statements make a project say its costs out loud before it proceeds. Carbon pricing, as noted, covers a meaningful share of the world's emissions. None of these is the whole of honest pricing, but each is a fragment of it, already legislated, already operating, defended in its own jurisdiction by people who never needed a unifying name for what they had built.

## What a framework does

It would be a mistake to end by claiming that naming four moves accomplishes them. The wealth that flows from the five-dollar price will defend the five-dollar price, and the people who carry the five-hundred-dollar cost are the ones least equipped to organize against it. That difficulty is durable, and no framework dissolves it.

What an honest ledger can do is what the best work near this ground has always done: change what the rest of us can see, and say, and demand. David Harvey gave the pattern its name, accumulation by dispossession; the name tells you what is being taken, and the four moves are a way of counting it. Katharina Pistor, in *The Code of Capital* (2019), showed how the legal scaffolding of property and contract encodes the wealth-creating side of the arrangement while leaving the cost-bearing side invisible to the law. Her readers will want one thing said plainly: pricing the commons does not mean coding it into a new asset class. The point of the number is to make the handed-off cost legible to law and budget, rather than to mint a new instrument for finance to trade.

Others built the adjacent rooms. Mariana Mazzucato, in *The Value of Everything* (2018), pressed the question of which activities create value and which merely extract it; a ledger of handed-off costs is that question's accounting complement. Kate Raworth drew the doughnut, the safe band between human need and planetary limit, and watched Amsterdam adopt it as a planning tool. Matthew Desmond, in *Evicted*, showed that a careful accounting of who bears a cost can change a national conversation about it. Daniel Susskind, in *Growth: A Reckoning* (2024), argued that growth can be redirected rather than worshipped or abandoned. None of them ended the problem they described. Each changed what could be seen and argued about, which is the precondition for doing anything at all.

Adam Smith would have recognized the project. Honest pricing is not a departure from the market he described. It is the completion of it. A price that reflects every cost is the price the market was always supposed to produce, and the work is to make the market tell the truth it was built to tell. Classify what cannot be replaced. Reserve enough of it. Invest in needing less. Adjust at the border so the cost travels with the good. Then look at the result and start again.

Whether the work gets done is the question each generation answers for the one that comes after it. The ton of coal that left McDowell County for just under five dollars in 1960 is long since burned; the cost of it is still being paid, by people who never saw the five dollars. I spent twenty years watching how quietly a bill like that moves down the table. Pricing honestly is the decision to stop passing it, and to write down what things actually cost while there is still time to pay some of it ourselves.
