# Pricing Honestly

A ton of coal came out of McDowell County, West Virginia, and sold at the mine mouth in 1960 for $4.71. Just under five dollars. That was the price. It was not the cost.

Add up everything that ton took out of the world and never paid back, and the honest number is more than five hundred dollars, a conservative floor, nearly all of it carbon. The black lung that shortened a miner's breathing. The streams that ran orange. The mountains taken down to stumps. The carbon dioxide that will sit in the atmosphere for centuries, warming a planet no one in that 1960 transaction will live to see. The price was just under five dollars. The cost was more than a hundred times that. The difference did not vanish. The value went to the people who captured it. The cost stayed with the miner, the community, and the river.

None of this was an aberration. A market is an instrument for finding the price two parties will accept today, and it is silent about everyone who is not in the room. Ronald Coase showed in 1960, in "The Problem of Social Cost," that where the parties to a harm can bargain, they will often price it between themselves, no regulator required, and within its conditions the insight holds. Extraction breaks the conditions. The communities downstream were not at the table. The river was not at the table. The future was not at the table; it has no agent, and it holds no money. An honest price has to account for what the absent parties would have bid, and that accounting is the whole distance between five dollars and five hundred.

I spent twenty years inside institutions whose ledgers managed costs this way, by passing them to someone outside the building. Federal agencies, and the contractor economies that surround them. The work I was hired to do was, considered narrowly, honest. The ledgers, considered honestly, were not. After enough years inside a ledger that does not tell the truth, you begin to feel the absence as something physical, a slight wrong pressure on the inside of the chest that you cannot place until you stop and listen for it. I stopped, eventually, and went looking for the design.

Others had looked before me, from adjacent ground. Karl Polanyi, in *The Great Transformation* in 1944, watched land and labor traded as ordinary commodities and named the fiction at the heart of the arrangement. Elinor Ostrom asked who governs a commons, and answered with evidence rather than parables. Herman Daly asked what size an economy can be inside a finite biosphere. John Hartwick asked what the people who inherit what is left are owed. Each found part of what economics is for. The diagnosis has been complete for decades. The accounting has never been built: a single ledger that carries the diagnosis through to the resources that cannot be replaced and turns the total into something a government could adopt.

Building it would not take a revolution. It would take four moves, roughly in order. Classify what is being taken. Hold some of it back. Invest in needing less of it. Adjust at the border. Then look at what the four moves have produced, and start again. The cases are real, and most of them are already running somewhere, built by ordinary coalitions, surviving ordinary politics. The gap between five dollars and five hundred was a choice, and choices can be remade.

## Classify

Begin with the fact that not everything we take is the same kind of thing.

Some materials are common, substitutable, and effectively unlimited at any scale that matters. Iron is the fourth most abundant element in the crust. Construction sand and gravel are nearly everywhere. Exhaust the convenient deposits and the price rises, and the rising price does its ordinary work, summoning new supply and new substitutes. These materials need honest pricing too, because taking them still does damage the price ignores. They do not need to be hoarded. Humanity will not run out of iron.

Other materials sit at the far end. No substitute for the job they do. Workable concentrations in a few places only. Once gone or contaminated, gone on any timescale a civilization can plan around. Phosphorus is the clearest case: essential to agriculture because it is essential to life, with no replacement for its biological role and the workable deposits concentrated in a handful of places. Helium is another; helium-3, useful in certain fusion designs, is essentially absent from Earth's atmosphere and relatively abundant in lunar regolith, so the question of holding it back waits on whether the fusion application ever arrives. And there are the fossil aquifers, groundwater laid down over thousands of years, recharging so slowly it is closer to a mineral than to rain. The Ogallala beneath the High Plains. The aquifer under the North China Plain. The Guarani in South America. Pumped faster than their millennial recharge, they are being mined.

Elinor Ostrom showed, against a long pessimism, that communities can govern a regenerating commons for generations without ruining it; her commoners manage flows. The fossil aquifers mark the boundary of that finding. A flow that will not return for a thousand years is a stock, and a stock has to be classified before it can be governed. The line that matters runs between taking and foreclosing. Extraction severs a cost whenever consumption closes off future access faster than the resource restores itself: always, for the materials that never restore; for the renewable ones, only when the harvest outruns the regeneration; not at all while it stays within it.

In between sit the materials that are replaceable in principle and critical in practice, where supply is concentrated, substitution is slow or expensive, and a sudden cutoff would be a national emergency. The European Union has written this category down. Its Critical Raw Materials Act, in force since May 2024, lists 34 materials as critical, 17 of them as strategic, and requires programs to stockpile and to develop alternative supplies. The rare-earth elements are the marquee case, along with the platinum-group metals. They are abundant enough in the crust; what they are is difficult and dirty to refine, and the world allowed a single country to corner most of the refining capacity, which converted a chemistry problem into a geopolitics problem.

Call the distance between a material and its nearest replacement the substitution gap: time, money, feasibility. For iron the gap is small and the market closes it on its own. For phosphorus the gap is, at present, infinite. The first move of honest pricing is knowing where on that spectrum each material sits, because you cannot decide how much of something to hold back until you know how badly you would miss it.

The genuinely renewable resources sit off the spectrum entirely, and the trouble with them lives at the edges. Sunlight arrives whether we use it or not. The sun is free; the land you must stand on to collect it is not. The wind is free; the grid you must connect to is not. Even the inexhaustible resources have chokepoints, and the chokepoints are where the handoff happens.

## Reserve

Once you know what cannot be replaced, the second move nearly states itself. Hold some of it back.

The logic is insurance logic, and it usually arrives dressed as something grander than it is. Set aside a reserve of a critical material and one of two things happens. Either you never need it, and you have paid a modest carrying cost for nothing, the way you pay for a fire extinguisher you never discharge. Or you need it, in a cutoff or a price shock or a war, and the reserve is worth many times what holding it cost. The mistake on one side is small and bounded. The mistake on the other side is large and, in the bad cases, unbounded. When the errors are that lopsided, you do not need exact odds to know which way to lean.

For material still in the ground, the carrying cost falls toward zero. Known reserves of the rare earths exceed annual world production many times over; hold back, say, a fifth of them and a country has banked decades of supply at the cost of leaving it where it already sits. <!-- structure-note: reserve arithmetic kept illustrative (Path A) per brief §8 call #6 fallback; USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries edition not verified at draft time, so the tonnages are omitted rather than typeset to look sourced. --> No warehouses. A reserve of this kind is a decision to decline to dig, or to decline to sell, which is the cheapest storage there is.

The institution is not hypothetical; strategic petroleum reserves already run on exactly this logic, a buffer against the day the ordinary supply is interrupted. The lesson of the last fifteen years is that the logic reaches well past oil, and the countries that learned it learned it the hard way. In 2010, after a confrontation in the waters near the Senkaku Islands, rare-earth exports from the dominant supplier to Japan were curtailed. Japan answered by helping fund the Lynas Corporation's mining in Australia and processing in Malaysia, an alternative supply line built where there had been near-total dependence. In the United States, Mountain Pass, the country's main domestic rare-earth mine, had been allowed to lapse while the refining business migrated overseas; MP Materials acquired it in 2017 and restarted it the next year. Europe wrote the lesson into the law already described.

Each is a country discovering, after a scare, that it had been pricing a critical material as abundant and substitutable when it was neither. The reserve move is the same discovery, made before the scare. The live cases are the ones still being argued: in the Clarion-Clipperton Zone, on the deep Pacific seabed, the question of whether to extract a contested commons at all is not yet settled, and holding back until we understand what we would be destroying is the cautious choice and the cheap one.

## Invest

Reserving buys time. The third move spends it, on two things at once.

The first is substitution, the work of needing less of the irreplaceable thing, which widens the gap from the far side. Improvements in solar, wind, and storage shrink how much of any particular fuel a society must burn. Alternative battery chemistries loosen the hold of the metals today's batteries demand. Recovering material that now ends in landfills turns waste back into supply. Regenerative agriculture and the emerging plant-based and cultured proteins lower the land and water and nutrient cost of feeding people. None of it is guaranteed, which is the argument for starting early. Substitution research is slow, and a breakthrough cannot be summoned in the year it is needed. You fund it through the years you do not need it, so that the option exists in the year you do.

There is a serious argument that price signals alone will never build the replacement system, that the transition is an investment problem before it is a pricing problem. The objection is right about the building and understates the lever. A ledger that tells the truth about extraction puts the existing subsidies and rents on the page, and a public that can see what it is already paying can choose what to pay for instead. The investment case is downstream of the visibility.

The second investment is alternative supply, and at the far edge of the map it leaves the planet. This belongs in the speculative register and should be read there; none of it is policy for this year. But the logic runs all the way out. An asteroid like 16 Psyche, thought to be rich in metal, sits where the residual costs of extraction fall toward zero, because there are no communities to harm and no ecosystems to wreck. <!-- structure-note: NASA Psyche mission composition characterization not re-verified at draft time; claim held in the declared speculative register with no authority gesture per addendum §D item 3 L1; source-manifest item-46 status update pending at promotion. --> Metal pulled from a lifeless rock carries a lower honest price than the same metal torn out of an inhabited valley, before the launch cost is even counted. That gap is, eventually, a reason to look up, and meanwhile the nearer work of substitution and recycling will carry any horizon we can presently plan.

Needing less of what cannot be replaced enlarges what a society can safely do.

## Border

A society can do all of this at home and still import the costs it refused to bear. The fourth move reaches across the line.

The prototype is already running. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, phased in from 2023 and entering its operational compliance phase at the start of 2026, prices the carbon embedded in certain imports when they cross the border. It exists to stop a simple evasion: make pollution expensive at home and production migrates to wherever pollution is still free, leaving the planet no better off and the home industry worse. The adjustment closes that loophole by pricing the cost wherever it was incurred. Carbon pricing itself is no longer fringe; by the World Bank's 2025 accounting, around 80 carbon-pricing instruments operate worldwide, taxes and trading systems together covering on the order of 28 percent of global emissions. The border adjustment is what lets a domestic carbon price survive an open economy.

The principle generalizes past carbon, and not for free. The carbon mechanism works because embedded carbon can be measured; extending it means building the same measurable basis cost by cost, slow administrative work and a real constraint, not a license to price imports at whatever number a regulator prefers. What the work would buy is visible in the places where the handed-off costs land now. At Baotou, in Inner Mongolia, rare-earth processing has filled a tailings impoundment with radioactive and toxic slurry covering roughly four square miles, a lake of waste behind the cheap magnets in devices sold everywhere. In the Niger Delta, half a century of oil spillage has left thousands of square kilometers of mangrove and farmland unlivable. In the Democratic Republic of Congo, the cobalt that powers the battery economy has at its worst drawn tens of thousands of children into the pits of artisanal mines. These costs are subtracted from the lives of the people nearest the extraction and added to no one's ledger. A border that priced them would do for trade what honest pricing does for the single ton of coal. The cost would travel with the good.

## The politics of resistance

If the four moves are this reasonable, why have they not happened? Not for want of being thought of. The reason is a structural feature of politics that Mancur Olson described sixty years ago, and that no one has much improved on since.

In *The Logic of Collective Action*, published in 1965, Olson showed that a small group with a large concentrated stake will out-organize a large group with a small diffuse stake almost every time. The few who profit from an arrangement know exactly what they would lose if it changed, and they spend to defend it. The many who pay the diffuse cost each pay a little, often without knowing it, and cannot easily coordinate to object. Honest pricing is the textbook case. The benefit of cheap extraction concentrates in the firms and shareholders who capture it now. The cost spreads across communities, ecosystems, and generations who are not organized and, in the future's case, cannot be. The arithmetic of organizing favors the five-dollar price and works against the five-hundred-dollar truth. No villain is required. The structure does the work by itself.

The same political economy has been beaten before, and the cleanest way to see it is to set two crises side by side. When the crisis of 2008 came, on the order of $11 trillion in American household wealth was destroyed, by the Federal Reserve's flow-of-funds accounting. Millions of households received foreclosure filings in the years that followed; roughly 4.1 million foreclosures were completed. <!-- structure-note: completed-foreclosure count per canonical ledger (CoreLogic-class series); confirm the preferred named-series attribution at promotion. --> The direct federal response, the Troubled Asset Relief Program, authorized about $700 billion, and the Federal Reserve added trillions more in emergency liquidity and guarantees, and nearly all of that capital went to recapitalizing bank balance sheets rather than to restructuring the household debt at the center of the collapse. Atif Mian and Amir Sufi, in *House of Debt* in 2014, measured the choice: restructuring household debt at the scale the crisis demanded would have put the recovery into consumption, and into the households that needed it most, rather than into the top of the distribution. Their argument was not that the rescue was villainy. The architecture was chosen, and another architecture was available.

Sweden had run the other architecture, twice. In 1933 it devalued, which lightened real debt across the economy; through the middle of the decade it wrote down nominal mortgage debt for distressed households and put the unemployed onto public works; in 1938 the Saltsjöbaden Agreement set wage bargaining at a level meant to keep households consuming, and the settlement held through a world war, the oil shock of the 1970s, and the country's own banking crisis in 1992. When that 1992 crisis came, Sweden recapitalized its failing banks but took equity in exchange, recovered most of the deployed capital within a few years, and aimed fiscal policy at household consumption. Two countries, two crises. One protected balance sheets. One protected households. Neither outcome was forced, and an honest pricing system is, among other things, the habit of seeing a bill before it comes due rather than fighting afterward over who pays it.

The accounting that sees a bill coming can also read one long overdue. Forward, it prices what has not yet been taken: the ore left in the ground, the carbon not yet burned, the aquifer not yet drawn down. Backward, it reads a cost already paid, by the wrong people. William Darity, Jr., and A. Kirsten Mullen have done that reading with more rigor than anyone, in the second edition of *From Here to Equality* and in a 2022 *Journal of Economic Perspectives* article: a tally of the cumulative wealth deficit between Black and white American households, compounded out of slavery, sharecropping, redlining, convict leasing, and a long sequence of discriminatory policy, arriving at a figure on the order of $14 trillion. That figure is the same kind of object as the gap between five dollars and five hundred. A cost severed from the people who captured the benefit, carried by the people who bore the harm, compounding because no ledger ever recorded it as owed, and because the people who carry it and the people who carry the miner's ledger have rarely been shown the two columns side by side.

What a deficit like that costs is not only money. In 2022, in *JAMA Network Open*, a research team that included Darity reported Black Americans' median life expectancy at 77.5 years against 81.5 for white Americans, and found that after adjusting for differences in wealth, the racial difference in survival disappeared. Four years of life, sitting in the wealth column. The ledger has room for this accounting, and the people best placed to make it are already making it. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not mine to do, and I leave that work to those who are continuing it. What the framework offers is the ledger.

A tradition that arrives from the opposite political direction lands in a usefully compatible place. The Public Choice economists, James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock foremost among them, built a body of work on the very dynamic Olson named, concentrated interests capturing rules and bending public arrangements toward private rent. Read honestly, their analysis explains why pricing the commons is hard rather than why it is wrong, and it belongs in the toolkit of anyone serious about the problem.

## What already works

The common objection to all of this is that it is utopian, the dream of a society that does not exist. The objection would be stronger if the pieces were not already running, separately, in ordinary places, built by ordinary political coalitions and surviving ordinary politics for decades. What remains is mostly noticing that the scattered working parts are versions of the same idea, and connecting them.

Start with the resource funds, the cleanest demonstration that the value of an extraction can be held for the people who own the underlying commons. Norway treats its oil as a finite inheritance rather than as income. The state's petroleum revenues, from taxation, licensing, and direct ownership stakes, flow into the Government Pension Fund Global, now roughly $2 trillion, near $390,000 for every Norwegian citizen, invested abroad so the money does not distort the home economy. A fiscal rule caps what the government may draw from the fund each year, set at 4 percent when the rule was adopted and tightened to 3 percent in 2017, and the capital stays whole. Alaska built a smaller version of the same instinct: since 1976 at least a quarter of the state's mineral royalty income has gone into a constitutionally protected permanent fund, standing in 2026 at about $89 billion, and since 1982 every resident has received an annual dividend, in recent years between roughly $1,000 and $1,700. These are line items in real budgets, defended by voters who would not give them up.

Then there is the question of who owns the enterprise and how the gains divide. In the Basque country, a Catholic priest named José María Arizmendiarrieta founded what became the Mondragon Corporation in 1956, a federation of worker-owned cooperatives that today runs to dozens of cooperatives employing tens of thousands of people. Pay inside them is capped by rule, the ratio between the highest and lowest earners held to single digits, where the comparable ratio in a large American corporation runs into the hundreds. The federation built its own bank, the Caja Laboral, in 1959, which is part of why it has endured; when its flagship appliance maker, Fagor, went under in 2013, the federation absorbed many of the workers into other cooperatives and carried on. Mondragon is a sixty-year-old business that handles failure differently because it is owned differently.

Housing gives a third demonstration. Vienna has been building and holding municipal and cooperative housing since Red Vienna in 1919, and today close to 60 percent of the city's residents live in municipal or non-profit cooperative homes, held off the speculative market. The system was funded in part by a dedicated payroll levy, historically about 1 percent, small and steady; the levy's existence is the structural point, even as the rate moves. It survived an interwar fascist coup, Nazi occupation, and every administration since. Chattanooga shows the same municipal instinct closer to home. The city's public electric utility launched a municipal fiber network in 2010 and offered gigabit service across the whole city, multi-gigabit where it is wanted, treating connectivity as the kind of good a utility provides. The incumbent cable company sued, and the Tennessee legislature passed laws limiting how far the network could expand. The resistance is Olson's dynamic out in the open, the concentrated incumbent against the diffuse public benefit. The utility built the thing anyway, and people use it.

Alongside these sits a structural fact about taxation that punctures a comfortable assumption. Americans are accustomed to thinking of themselves as lightly taxed next to the European welfare states, and at the level of headline rates they are, but the distance is smaller than the rhetoric suggests. In the OECD's Taxing Wages accounting, the total tax wedge on an average single worker runs around 30 percent in the United States against roughly 42 percent in Sweden. <!-- structure-note: OECD Taxing Wages figures per canonical ledger; year-anchor the edition at promotion per brief §2. --> For that difference the Swedish worker receives universal health care, tuition-free university with a living stipend, parental leave, subsidized childcare, and a longer life expectancy with less variation by class. The American worker, paying not so much less, receives the obligation to arrange and finance most of that privately, in a country where more than 25 million people are uninsured by the Census Bureau's count <!-- structure-note: stated conservatively below the ledger's ~28M form; verify Census CPS/ACS (or KFF) vintage and exact figure at promotion per brief §2. --> and where a charitable economy has grown up to fill the gap. Berliner and Kenworthy, studying medical crowdfunding in 2017, found more than 250,000 campaigns a year, most of them raising a quarter to a third of what the campaigner said they needed. The difference between the two systems is what the bill buys, and who is left to absorb the cost when it buys little.

There is more in this register. Mining-reclamation bonds make an operator post money against restoring the land. Environmental-impact assessment makes a project state its costs before it proceeds. Carbon pricing, as noted, now covers a meaningful share of the world's emissions. Each is a fragment of honest pricing, already legislated, already operating, defended in its own jurisdiction by people who built it without any shared vocabulary for what they had in common.

## What a framework does

Naming four moves does not accomplish them. The wealth that flows from the five-dollar price will defend the five-dollar price, and the people who carry the five-hundred-dollar cost are the ones least equipped to organize against it. The difficulty is durable, and no instrument dissolves it.

What an instrument can do is more modest, and the people who have worked nearest this ground show what the modest thing is worth. David Harvey gave the pattern a name, accumulation by dispossession, value taken from the many and concentrated in the few; the name was the diagnosis, and a ledger is the count that follows it. Katharina Pistor, in *The Code of Capital*, showed how the legal scaffolding of property and contract encodes the wealth-creating side of the arrangement while leaving the cost-bearing side invisible to the law. Pricing the commons does not require coding it into one more asset class of the kind she anatomizes; an obligation entered on a ledger is not a security, and the accounting can stay an accounting. Mariana Mazzucato traced how value extraction came to be booked as value creation, and an honest cost account is the complement her diagnosis asks for. Elinor Ostrom, against the prevailing pessimism, catalogued the design principles by which communities govern what they share without ruining it. Kate Raworth drew the doughnut, the safe band between human need and planetary limit, and watched Amsterdam adopt it as a planning tool. Matthew Desmond showed in *Evicted* that one careful accounting of who bears a cost can change a national conversation. Daniel Susskind, in *Growth: A Reckoning*, argued that growth can be redirected rather than worshipped or abandoned. None of them ended the problem they described. Each changed what the rest of us could see and name, and seeing is where doing begins.

Adam Smith would have recognized the work. Honest pricing is not a departure from the market he described. It is the completion of it. A price that reflects every cost is the price the market was always supposed to find, and the task is to make the market tell the truth it was built to tell. The moves are four. Classify what cannot be replaced. Reserve enough of it. Invest in needing less. Adjust at the border so the cost travels with the good. Then look at the result and start again.

What a common ledger gives the people who do the work is a common language, so that the city councilor funding the fiber network, the legislator writing the reclamation bond, the negotiator at the carbon border, and the voter defending the dividend can recognize one another as doing versions of the same thing. Whether the work gets done is the question each generation answers for the one after it. The ton of coal that sold for $4.71 in 1960 is long since burned. The cost is still being paid, by people who never saw the five dollars. Pricing honestly is, in the end, the decision to stop handing that bill forward, and to write down what things actually cost while there is still time to pay it ourselves.
