# Pricing Honestly

A ton of coal came out of McDowell County, West Virginia, and sold at the mine mouth in 1960 for four dollars and fifty cents. That was the price. It was not the cost. When you add up everything that ton of coal actually took out of the world and never paid back, the honest number is at least five hundred and twenty dollars, and that is a conservative floor. The black lung that shortened a miner's breathing. The streams that ran orange. The mountains taken down to stumps. The carbon dioxide that will sit in the atmosphere for centuries, warming a planet none of the people in that 1960 transaction will live to see. The price was four and a half dollars. The cost was more than a hundred times that. The difference did not vanish. The value went to those who captured it. The cost went to the miner, the community, and the river.

This is the ordinary condition of how we price the things we take out of the ground, and it is not an accident or a scandal. It is a design. The market is very good at finding the price two parties will agree to today. It is structurally indifferent to the costs that fall on people who are not at the table. Some of those costs the affected parties can bargain away on their own, and Ronald Coase named the conditions under which they do. The costs this argument is about are the ones that fall on parties who cannot get to the table at all: communities downstream, ecosystems that cannot bargain, and a future that has not been born yet.

Four traditions took up adjacent territory. Polanyi asked what happens when labor and land are treated as ordinary commodities. Ostrom asked who governs the commons. Daly asked who lives inside the biosphere. Hartwick asked who inherits what is left. Each got part of what economics is for. None put the parts on a single ledger, carried it through to the resources that cannot be replaced, and made the result into something a government could act on. That is the work this essay takes up.

Honest pricing means something simple to say and hard to do. It means the price should reflect every cost the extraction produces, including the costs that currently get handed off. If a society decided to do that, it would not need a revolution. It would need to make four moves, roughly in order. Classify what it is taking. Hold some of it back. Invest in needing less of it. Then check its work and start again.

What follows is a description of those four moves and an argument that they are more reachable than they sound. The cases are real and most of them are already running somewhere. The point is not that any of this is easy. The point is that the gap between four dollars and five hundred is not a law of nature. It is a choice, and choices can be made differently.

## Classify

Begin by noticing that not everything we extract is the same.

Some materials are common, substitutable, and effectively unlimited at any scale that matters. Iron is the fourth most abundant element in the crust. Ordinary construction sand and gravel are nearly everywhere. If we burned through the most convenient deposits, we would move to less convenient ones, and the price would rise, and the rising price would do its ordinary work of summoning new supply and new substitutes. These materials need honest pricing too, because their extraction still does damage that the price ignores. But they do not need to be hoarded. There is no scenario in which humanity runs out of iron.

Other materials sit at the opposite end. They have no substitute for the job they do, they exist in workable concentrations at only a few places on Earth, and once they are gone or contaminated they do not come back on any timescale a civilization can plan around. Phosphorus is the clearest case. It is essential to agriculture because it is essential to life, with no replacement for its biological role, and the workable deposits are concentrated in a handful of locations. Helium is another. Helium-3, useful in certain fusion designs, is essentially absent from Earth's atmosphere while being relatively abundant in lunar regolith, which means the reservation question turns on whether the fusion application ever materializes. And there are the fossil aquifers, groundwater laid down over thousands of years that recharges so slowly it is closer to a mineral than to a renewable resource. The Ogallala beneath the American High Plains. The aquifer under the North China Plain. The Guarani in South America. Pump them faster than their millennial recharge and you are not using a resource. You are mining it.

In between sit the materials that are not irreplaceable in principle but are strategically critical in practice, where supply is concentrated, substitution is slow or expensive, and a sudden cutoff would be a national emergency. The European Union has done the useful work of writing this category down. Its Critical Raw Materials Act, enacted in 2023, classifies thirty-four materials as strategically critical and requires programs to stockpile them and to develop alternative sources. Rare-earth elements are the marquee case, along with the platinum-group metals. These are not exotic in the sense of being rare in the crust. They are difficult and dirty to refine, and the world allowed a single country to corner most of the refining capacity, which converts a chemistry problem into a geopolitics problem.

Call the thing that separates these categories the substitution gap. It is the distance between a material and its nearest replacement, measured in time, money, and feasibility. For iron the gap is small and the market handles it. For phosphorus the gap is, at present, infinite. Most of the materials a modern economy worries about sit somewhere on that spectrum, and the first move of honest pricing is simply to know where each one sits. You cannot decide how much of something to hold back until you know how badly you would miss it.

A note on the resources that are genuinely renewable, because the boundary is where the interesting cases live. Sunlight arrives whether we use it or not. Wind blows whether we harvest it or not. The atmosphere's oxygen replenishes on a biological cadence. These are the resources that seem to need no management at all, and at the level of the resource itself that is true. The trouble shows up at the edges, where someone captures the flow and converts an open commons into a private toll. The sun is free. The land you must stand on to collect it is not. The wind is free. The grid you must connect to is not. Even the inexhaustible resources turn out to have chokepoints, and the chokepoints are where the cost-handoff happens.

## Reserve

Once you know what cannot be replaced, the second move follows almost automatically. Hold some of it back.

The logic here is the logic of insurance, and it is worth saying plainly because it is often dressed up in something more complicated. Set aside a strategic reserve of a critical material and one of two things happens. Either you never need it, in which case you have paid a modest storage cost for nothing, the way you pay for a fire extinguisher you never discharge. Or you do need it, in a cutoff or a price shock or a war, in which case the reserve is worth many times what it cost to hold. The downside of reserving too much is small and bounded. The downside of reserving too little is large and, in the bad cases, unbounded. When the costs of a mistake are that lopsided, you do not need to know the exact odds to know which way to lean. You buy cheap insurance against a downside bigger than you think.

The arithmetic for rare earths shows how close to free a reserve can be. Global economically recoverable reserves run on the order of thirty million metric tons. Annual world production is on the order of three hundred thousand metric tons. A strategic reserve of twenty percent of known reserves would be six million metric tons, which is roughly twenty years of current global production, and because the material is already in the ground, reserving it costs almost nothing in the near term. You are not building warehouses. You are declining to dig, or declining to sell, which is the cheapest form of storage there is.

We already do this for oil, so the institution is not hypothetical. Strategic petroleum reserves exist precisely on the insurance logic, a buffer held against the day the ordinary supply is interrupted. The harder lesson of the last fifteen years is that the same logic applies far beyond oil, and that the countries learning it learned it the hard way. In 2010, after a confrontation in the waters near the Senkaku Islands, rare-earth exports from the dominant supplier to Japan were curtailed. The shock pushed Japan to help fund the Lynas Corporation's Australian mining and Malaysian processing capacity, building an alternative supply line where there had been near-total dependence. South Korea drew the same conclusion and built rare-earth reserves following its 2019 trade dispute with Japan. In the United States, the Mountain Pass mine in California, the country's main domestic rare-earth source, had been allowed to lapse during the years when the refining business migrated overseas. It was acquired by MP Materials in 2018 and brought back toward restoring a domestic supply capacity the country had let slip away. Europe wrote the whole lesson into law with the 2023 Critical Raw Materials Act.

Each of these is a country discovering, after a scare, that it had priced a critical material as though it were abundant and substitutable when it was neither. The reserve move is what honest pricing looks like before the scare instead of after it. There are live cases still being argued, the deep seabed of the Clarion-Clipperton Zone among them, where the question of whether to extract a contested commons at all is not yet settled. Those cases are exactly the ones where reserving, holding back until we understand what we would be destroying, is the cautious and the cheap choice.

## Invest

Reserving buys time. The third move is to use the time, by spending on two things at once.

The first is substitution, the work of needing less of the irreplaceable thing. This is the research that widens the substitution gap from the other side, finding ways to do the job with materials that are not scarce. Improvements in solar photovoltaics, in wind, and in storage reduce how much of any particular fuel a society needs to burn. Alternative battery chemistries reduce dependence on the metals that today's batteries demand. Recovering and recycling material flows that now end in landfills turns waste back into supply. Regenerative agriculture and the emerging plant-based and cellular proteins reduce the land and water and nutrient load of feeding people. None of this is guaranteed to work, and that is the point of starting early. Substitution research is slow, and you cannot summon a breakthrough at the moment you need it. You fund it across the years when you do not yet need it, so that the option exists when you do.

The second investment is in alternative sources of supply, which at the far edge of the map means going off the planet for the materials that are concentrated elsewhere. This belongs in the speculative register and should be flagged as such, because it is not a policy you can adopt this year. But the logic is the same logic that runs through the whole framework, and it is worth following to its end. Consider the asteroids. An object like 16 Psyche is, by the estimates, extraordinarily rich in metals, and it sits in a place where the residual costs of extraction are close to zero, because there are no communities to harm and no ecosystems to wreck. The honest price of metal pulled from a lifeless rock in space is, in the terms this essay has been using, far lower than the honest price of the same metal torn out of an inhabited valley, even before you count the launch cost. That gap is, eventually, a reason to look up. It is not a reason to stop the harder work on the ground, which substitution and recycling will carry for any timescale we can presently plan.

The investment move is where the framework stops sounding like restraint and starts sounding like ambition, because needing less of the irreplaceable thing is not a sacrifice. It is an expansion of what a society can safely do.

## Border

A society can do all of this inside its own borders and still find itself importing the costs it refused to bear at home. So the fourth structural move reaches across the line.

The mechanism already has a working prototype. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, introduced in staged form beginning in 2023 and entering its operational compliance phase at the start of 2026, puts a price at the border on the carbon embedded in certain imports. Its purpose is to stop a simple and predictable evasion. If you make it expensive to pollute at home, production migrates to wherever pollution is still free, and the planet is no better off while your own industries are worse off. A border adjustment closes that loophole by pricing the cost wherever it was actually incurred. Carbon pricing itself is no longer a fringe instrument. By the World Bank's 2025 accounting there are around eighty carbon-pricing instruments in operation worldwide, a mix of taxes and trading systems, covering on the order of twenty-eight percent of global greenhouse emissions. The border adjustment is what makes a domestic carbon price durable in an open economy.

The principle generalizes past carbon, though not for free. A border adjustment scaled to the substitution gap would price imported goods according to the full cost of producing them, including the costs that were handed off in the country of origin. The carbon mechanism works because embedded carbon can be measured; extending it means building that same measurable basis cost by cost, which is real administrative work and a real constraint. It is not a license to price imports at whatever number a regulator prefers. Walk through what is currently handed off and the case makes itself. At Baotou, in Inner Mongolia, the processing of rare earths has produced a tailings impoundment of radioactive and toxic slurry covering roughly five square miles, a lake of waste that is the unpriced cost of the cheap magnets in devices sold around the world. In the Niger Delta, half a century of oil spillage has left thousands of square kilometers of mangrove and farmland uninhabitable. In the Democratic Republic of Congo, the cobalt that powers the global battery economy has at its worst drawn tens of thousands of children into artisanal mining. These costs are real and enormous, and right now they are subtracted from the lives of the people nearest the extraction and added to no one's ledger. A border that priced them would do at the level of trade what honest pricing does at the level of a single transaction. It would make the cost travel with the good.

## The politics of resistance

If the four moves are this reasonable, the obvious question is why they have not happened. The answer is not that nobody thought of them. The answer is a structural feature of politics that the economist Mancur Olson described with great clarity sixty years ago.

In *The Logic of Collective Action*, published in 1965, Olson observed that a small group with a large concentrated stake will out-organize a large group with a small diffuse stake almost every time, even when the diffuse group is far larger in total. The few who profit from an arrangement know exactly what they would lose if it changed, and they will spend to defend it. The many who pay the diffuse cost each pay only a little, do not always know they are paying, and cannot easily coordinate to object. Honest pricing is the textbook case. The benefit of cheap extraction is concentrated in the firms and workers and shareholders who capture it now. The cost is spread across communities, ecosystems, and generations who are not organized and, in the case of the future, cannot be. The arithmetic of organizing favors the four-dollar price and works against the five-hundred-dollar truth.

This is not a counsel of despair, because the same political economy has been overcome before, and the clearest way to see that is to set two responses to two financial crises side by side. Consider how the United States met the crisis of 2008. By the standard reckonings, somewhere around eleven trillion dollars of American household wealth was destroyed, and roughly ten million households received foreclosure filings over the next five years, about five million of them losing their homes. The direct federal response, the Troubled Asset Relief Program, authorized about seven hundred billion dollars, and the Federal Reserve added trillions more in emergency liquidity, guarantees, and rescues of individual firms. Almost all of that capital went to recapitalizing bank balance sheets rather than to restructuring the household debt at the center of the collapse. The economists Atif Mian and Amir Sufi, in *House of Debt* in 2014, argued that this was a choice with a measurable cost, that restructuring household debt at the scale the crisis demanded would have produced substantially more consumption recovery in the years that followed, with the benefit falling on the households that needed it most rather than on the top of the distribution. The point is not that the bank rescue was simple villainy. The point is that the architecture was chosen, and another architecture was available.

Sweden had shown what the other architecture looked like, decades earlier and again in living memory. In the depths of the Depression, the Swedish response of 1933 devalued the currency, which lightened real debt burdens across the economy. Through the middle of the decade the country wrote down nominal mortgage debt for distressed households and put the unemployed to work on public projects. In 1938 the Saltsjöbaden Agreement institutionalized wage bargaining between labor and employers at a level meant to maintain consumption, and that settlement proved durable enough to survive a world war, the oil shock of the 1970s, and the country's own banking crisis in 1992. When that 1992 crisis came, Sweden recapitalized its failing banks but took equity in exchange, recovered most of the deployed capital within a few years, and paired the rescue with fiscal policy aimed at holding up household consumption. Two countries, two crises, two architectures. One protected balance sheets. One protected households. Neither outcome was inevitable, and an honest pricing system is, in part, the habit of seeing the bill before it comes due rather than fighting over who pays it afterward.

The same accounting that sees a bill before it comes due can also read one already overdue. Looking forward, it prices what has not yet been taken: the resource left in the ground, the carbon not yet burned, the aquifer not yet drawn down. Looking backward, it reads a cost already paid by the wrong people. William Darity, Jr., and A. Kirsten Mullen do precisely that reading in *From Here to Equality* (2020), tallying the cumulative wealth deficit between Black and white American households produced by slavery, sharecropping, redlining, convict leasing, and a long sequence of discriminatory policy, and arriving at a figure on the order of fourteen trillion dollars. That number is the same kind of object as the gap between four dollars and five hundred: a cost severed from the people who captured the benefit and carried by the people who bore the harm, compounding across generations because no honest ledger ever recorded it as owed.

What a deficit like that costs is not only money. In a 2022 study in *JAMA Network Open*, a research team that included Darity found that Black Americans' median life expectancy was 77.5 years against 81.5 for white Americans, a gap of four years, and that after adjusting for differences in wealth the racial difference in survival disappeared. The gap in years was, in effect, a gap in dollars; the severed cost is paid, in the end, not only in money but in lives. The framework has room for accounting of this kind, and the people best placed to do it are already doing it. Putting a price on the costs of coercion borne by a group I do not belong to is not mine to do, and I leave that work to those who are continuing it. What the framework offers is the ledger. What belongs on it, looking backward, is theirs to enter.

A tradition that comes at all of this from a different political direction arrives at a usefully compatible place. The Public Choice economists, James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock foremost among them, built a body of work on exactly the dynamic Olson named, the way concentrated interests capture rules and bend public arrangements toward private rent. Read honestly, that analysis is not an argument against pricing the commons. It is an account of why doing so is hard, and it belongs in the toolkit of anyone serious about the problem rather than in the opposing camp.

## What already works

The most common objection to all of this is that it is utopian, the dream of a society that does not exist and a human nature we do not have. The objection would be stronger if the pieces were not already running, separately, in ordinary places, built by ordinary political coalitions and surviving ordinary politics for decades. The framework is mostly a matter of noticing that these scattered working parts are versions of the same idea, and connecting them.

Start with the resource funds, because they are the cleanest demonstration of capturing the value of an extraction and holding it for the people who own the underlying commons. Norway treats its oil as a finite inheritance rather than as income. The state captures the rents through licenses, taxation, and direct ownership, and routes them into the Government Pension Fund Global, which has grown to roughly two trillion dollars, near three hundred ninety thousand dollars for every Norwegian citizen. A fiscal rule adopted in 2001 caps annual spending at around three percent of the fund's returns and leaves the capital itself untouchable, and the money is invested abroad so as not to distort the home economy. Alaska built a smaller version of the same instinct. Since 1976 the state has captured a quarter of its mineral royalty income into a constitutionally protected permanent fund, now holding on the order of eighty-five billion dollars, and since 1982 it has paid every resident an annual dividend, recently around a thousand dollars and ranging in the years just past from roughly a thousand to seventeen hundred. These are not thought experiments. They are line items in real budgets, defended by voters who would not give them up.

Then there is the question of who owns the productive enterprise and how the gains are split. In the Basque region of northern Spain, a Catholic priest named José María Arizmendiarrieta founded what became the Mondragon Corporation in 1956, a federation of worker-owned cooperatives that today numbers around eighty-one cooperatives in Spain and employs roughly seventy thousand people. Pay inside the cooperatives is capped by rule, the ratio between the highest and lowest earners held to single digits where the comparable ratio in a large American corporation runs into the hundreds. Mondragon built its own cooperative bank, the Caja Laboral, in 1959, which is part of why it has endured. When its flagship appliance manufacturer, Fagor, went bankrupt in 2013, the federation did not simply shed the workers. It absorbed many of them into other cooperatives and survived. That is not a utopia. It is a sixty-year-old business that handles failure differently because it is owned differently.

Housing offers a third demonstration. Vienna has been building and maintaining city-owned and cooperative housing since the era of Red Vienna in 1919, and today something close to sixty percent of the city's population lives in municipal or non-profit cooperative homes, hundreds of thousands of apartments held off the speculative market. The system was funded in part by a dedicated payroll contribution, historically about one percent, a small and steady levy whose existence is the structural point even as the rate moves. It survived an interwar fascist coup, Nazi occupation, and every administration since. An American city closer to home shows the same municipal instinct at a smaller scale. Chattanooga's public electric utility launched a municipal fiber network in 2010 and offered gigabit service across the whole city, with speeds up to twenty-five gigabits per second for those who need them, treating connectivity as the kind of public good a utility provides. The incumbent cable company sued, and the Tennessee legislature passed laws limiting how far the municipal network could expand. The resistance is the Olson dynamic made visible, the concentrated incumbent fighting the diffuse public benefit, and it is worth noticing that the public utility built the thing anyway and that people use it.

These working parts sit alongside a structural fact about taxation that is worth pausing on, because it punctures a comfortable assumption. Americans are accustomed to thinking of themselves as lightly taxed compared with the European welfare states, and at the level of headline rates that is true, but the gap is smaller than the rhetoric suggests. The total tax wedge on an average single worker runs around thirty percent in the United States against roughly forty-two percent in Sweden, a difference of about eleven points. For that eleven-point difference the Swede receives universal health care, tuition-free university with a living stipend, generous parental leave, subsidized childcare, and a longer life expectancy with less variation by class. The American, paying not so much less, receives the obligation to arrange and finance most of that privately, with roughly twenty-eight million people uninsured at any given moment and a charitable economy that has come to fill the gap. One study of medical crowdfunding by Berliner and Kenworthy in 2017 found more than two hundred fifty thousand campaigns a year, most of them raising only a quarter to a bit over a third of what the campaigner said they needed. The difference between the two systems is not mainly the size of the bill. It is what the bill buys, and who is left to absorb the cost when it buys little.

There is more in this register that a longer treatment would unfold. Mining-reclamation bonds require operators to post money against the cost of restoring the land. Environmental-impact assessments force a project to state its costs before it proceeds. Carbon pricing, as noted, now covers a meaningful share of the world's emissions. None of these is the whole of honest pricing. Each is a fragment of it, already legislated, already operating, already defended in its own jurisdiction by people who built it without any of the vocabulary this essay has been using.

## What a framework does

It would be a mistake to end by claiming that naming the four moves accomplishes them. It does not. The wealth that flows from the four-dollar price will defend the four-dollar price, and the people who bear the five-hundred-dollar cost are the ones least equipped to organize against it. That is the durable difficulty, and no framework dissolves it.

What a framework can do is more modest and more useful than solving the problem. The thinkers who have worked nearest this ground each supplied a piece of it. David Harvey gave us the phrase accumulation by dispossession for the pattern of value taken from the many and concentrated in the few. Katharina Pistor, in *The Code of Capital*, showed how the legal scaffolding of property and contract quietly encodes the wealth-creating side of the arrangement while leaving the cost-bearing side invisible to the law. Elinor Ostrom showed, against the prevailing pessimism, that communities can in fact govern shared resources without ruining them, and spent her career cataloguing the design principles by which they do. Kate Raworth drew the doughnut, the safe space between human need and planetary limit, and watched Amsterdam adopt it as a planning tool. Matthew Desmond, in *Evicted*, demonstrated that a single well-told accounting of who bears a cost can change the national conversation about it. Daniel Susskind, in *Growth: A Reckoning*, argued that growth itself can be redirected rather than worshipped or abandoned. None of them ended the problem they described. Each of them changed how the rest of us could see and name and argue about it, which is the precondition for doing anything about it at all.

Adam Smith would have recognized the project, because honest pricing is not a departure from the market he described. It is the completion of it. A price that reflects every cost is the price the market was always supposed to produce, and the work is to make the market tell the truth it was built to tell. The instrument for doing that is the one this essay has sketched. Classify what cannot be replaced. Reserve enough of it. Invest in needing less. Adjust at the border so the cost travels with the good. Then look at the result and start again.

The framework will not do any of this on its own. No framework ever does. What it can do is hand the people who do the work a common language and a common instrument to work with, so that the city councilor funding the fiber network, the legislator writing the reclamation bond, the negotiator at the carbon border, and the voter defending the dividend can recognize that they are all doing versions of the same thing. Whether the work then gets done is the question every generation answers for the one that comes after it. The ton of coal that sold for four dollars and fifty cents in 1960 is long since burned. The cost of it is still being paid, by people who never saw the four dollars. Pricing honestly is, in the end, the decision to stop handing that bill forward and to write down what things actually cost while there is still time to pay it ourselves.
