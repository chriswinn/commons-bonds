# Atlantic Ideas pitch — *Pricing Honestly*

**Suggested subject line:** Submission: *Pricing Honestly* (~4,000 words) — Chris Winn

---

Dear Editors,

A ton of McDowell County coal sold at the mine mouth in 1960 for four dollars and fifty cents. Counting every cost that ton produced — the miner's scarred lungs, the carbon released over the next century, the streams that ran orange for fifty miles, the longevity gap that opened between McDowell residents and the American median — the honest price was somewhere between five hundred and twenty and six hundred dollars. The price was wrong by two orders of magnitude. It is wrong now, for coal and for almost everything else. A society that decided to count what the current price misses would have four moves to make, in order.

The moves are classification, reservation, investment, and reassessment. Classify what cannot be replaced — the European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act, passed in 2023, lists thirty-four such irreplaceables; phosphorus and fossil aquifers belong on the same list. Reserve a fraction of them, on the cheap-insurance logic the Strategic Petroleum Reserve already applies to oil. Invest in substitutes, and in alternative sources the price signal alone will not fund. Reassess as the underlying science moves. Border-adjustment — which the EU's CBAM became fully operational in 2026 to prove — runs as a cross-cutting concern of the four, not as a fifth step.

The not-utopian claim is anchored in working cases: Norway's two-trillion-dollar sovereign-wealth fund; Alaska's Permanent Fund and its annual dividend; Chattanooga's municipal fiber network; Mondragon's seventy-year cooperative tradition; Vienna's Gemeindebau, founded in 1919 and housing sixty percent of the city today. Mancur Olson's *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965) and Mian and Sufi's *House of Debt* (2014) anchor the political-economy reading of why honest pricing has not been done.

The essay reads in the lineage of Mazzucato, Christophers, Pistor, and Susskind. I am a nursing student at Virginia Peninsula Community College and a former healthcare-IT executive whose career spanned the National Institutes of Health during the Human Genome Project, federal research and consulting, and the CEO seat of a healthcare-IT firm. My forthcoming book *Commons Bonds* develops the framework at length. Full essay available on request.

*Disclosure:* the book this essay derives from was developed in collaboration with Claude (Anthropic) as a research and rigor-testing partner; the originating intuitions, the case selection, and the argumentative posture are mine.

Best,
Chris Winn
