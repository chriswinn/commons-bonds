## The bill that arrives with every breath

Imagine you are standing in an airlock, and the inner door will not open until you answer one question. How do you intend to pay for your air?

The question is not cruel, and here it is not even strange. Inside the wall, the air is made. A machine splits it out of water, drawing on power that something had to generate, and pulls the carbon dioxide back out of the room before it rises high enough to kill you. Every litre is counted, because every litre had to be produced. So before they let you into the only breathable space for a long way in any direction, they need to know how your breathing will be paid for.

You have no answer ready. You have never needed one. You have spent your whole life drawing breath with no meter and no bill, and somewhere along the way you came to believe that air is simply free. It is not. It never was. On Earth the cost was real the entire time; it was only ever paid by something else, by the forests and the oceans and the slow chemistry of a living planet, quietly, on your behalf, with no invoice ever sent to you. Behind the wall there is no planet to carry the cost, so it comes to you, by the hour, on a meter you can read.

None of this is a prediction. The closed habitat is a way of seeing, not a forecast of where anyone will live. It is the cleanest version of a move this book runs throughout: imagine the commons absent, and watch the cost appear. Call it the *Commons Inversion Test*. Remove the planet underneath the priceless things — the air, the water, the climate, the quiet work of taking carbon back — and they reappear instantly as metered line-items, because the only reason they were ever free is that something else was paying. Mars makes that visible by construction. It is the inversion test made physical.

Then comes the harder thought, the one that follows you back through the airlock and home. The cost on Earth is just as real as it was behind the wall. It is only paid by someone else — a forest on another continent, an ocean turning slowly more acidic, a person not yet born who will inherit a thinner margin than the one we were handed. The bill was never cancelled. It was sent somewhere we could not see, to someone who could not send it back. We simply never had to write the number down.

## What it costs once you write it down

The habitat is a thought experiment, and a thought experiment proves nothing on its own. So put a real number against it.

In 1960, a ton of McDowell County, West Virginia coal sold at the mine mouth for just under five dollars, and the price was honest about everything except what it left out. Walk that ton through every documented cost at the low end of every range — the lungs, the water, the emptied county, the carbon it would become — and the floor is roughly five hundred ten dollars. That is over a hundred times the mine-mouth price by the carbon-externality measure, and still eight and a half to twenty-six times by the framework's most conservative lens, which prices only the foreclosure premium and does not depend on the price of coal at all. The line for the difference has been blank for a century, not because the cost was small, but because no one was made to write the number down.

A vivid hypothetical and a hard real figure point at the same thing. The pattern, once you see it, you cannot stop seeing. Value moves one way. Cost moves the other. The arithmetic on the listing — the line that says *rent* or *wage* or *price per barrel* — measures only one of them. The other is real, it is large, and the line for it is blank.

## The pattern has a name

This book gives that pattern a name. *Cost severance* is the mechanism by which value is drawn from a shared resource — a mineral seam, a fishery, an atmosphere, a body of labor, the trust between citizens, the future itself — and the cost of that drawing is detached from the party that benefited and fastened onto a party with less power, less mobility, and less ability to refuse. By the time the cost comes due, the party that benefited has usually moved on. The mine has closed. The company has reorganized. The executives have retired. The politicians who made the promises have left office. The cost remains, and it has been routed to whoever happened to be standing where it landed.

This is not a partisan claim. It is a structural one. It does not require anyone to have acted in bad faith, and the cases in this book frequently show that no one did. What it requires is distance — between the place value is generated and the place cost is borne — and the modern economy, by its design, manufactures distance for a living.

The pattern repeats across a striking range of cases, and a discipline of the book is that naming the shape they share is not the same as making the cases equal. Coerced labor and a long commute do not share a line.

## The book

*Commons Bonds* is a single argument carried through ten chapters and a technical appendix; the manuscript is complete at roughly ninety thousand words. The argument is that cost severance is the load-bearing extraction mechanism of the modern economy; that it can be measured with discipline and from public sources; that what restoration requires can be estimated two independent ways — from the documented damage record, and from the foreclosure premium on the options extraction closes — and read against the bonds actually posted; that the gap between the needed and the paid is large enough, across enough domains, to constitute a material claim about how the economy actually works; and that a corrective accounting apparatus — two coordinated bond instruments, one backward-looking, one forward-looking — would not require any institution that does not already exist.

The framework rests on a small number of named instruments that the chapters build cumulatively:

- ***Cost severance.*** The structural mechanism: value drawn from a shared resource and cost severed from the beneficiary onto a party who cannot refuse.
- ***Residual Commons Value (RCV).*** The pricing apparatus. What every future generation loses by not having a resource, weighted by the probability that no substitute will exist when they need it, plus the externality tail that runs independently of substitutability.
- ***The Restitution Bond.*** The backward-looking instrument, person-affecting in the philosophical sense: discharges harm already realized. The lungs of Libby, the marshes of the Gulf, the foreclosed households of 2008, the McDowell County life-expectancy gap, the unpaid caregiving labor of the dying body's spouse. Its primary informing tradition is the reparations-economics methodology of Darity and Mullen, Hamilton and colleagues, and Conley.
- ***The Foreclosure Bond.*** The forward-looking instrument, impersonal in Parfit's sense: discharges harm not yet realized. The habitable atmosphere being spent, the irreplaceable materials being drawn down, the options being closed off for people who do not yet exist. Its lineage runs through Hartwick's rule, the civic-republican tradition of Pettit and Skinner, and the practical precedent of Norway's sovereign wealth fund, whose assets reserved from the country's oil rents stood at NOK 21,268 billion at the end of 2025 — more than $2 trillion at the exchange rate of that date.
- ***The Commons Inversion Test.*** The disciplinary gate every admitted cost claim must pass — the move the closed habitat opened this overview with. Imagine the commons absent; ask what cost appears. If no cost appears, the claim is not admitted. This keeps the framework honest against its own temptations.

The cases the framework is tested against are deliberately scattered, because a mechanism demonstrated once is an anecdote and a mechanism demonstrated across cases is a pattern. McDowell County, West Virginia (coal). The harbor at Old Point Comfort and the Chesapeake fishery (oysters, blue crab, menhaden, the food web itself). Norway and Nigeria (oil). Libby, Montana (vermiculite). Deepwater Horizon (Gulf oil). Baotou, Inner Mongolia (rare earths). The 2008 financial crisis (the household side of the ledger). Social Security (severed in the decades the actuaries had told us it would be severed in). The healthcare commons (the medical-crowdfunding system with a roughly ninety-percent failure rate, the pacemaker reimbursed at $461 against the long conversation reimbursed at $54). And, as a universality test, the hypothetical Mars closed habitat the overview opened with, constructed precisely so that the operational arrangements that ordinarily hide cost severance — geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry — collapse by construction.

The book argues, and Chapter 6 demonstrates — estimating what restoration requires two independent ways and reading what has actually been paid off the record — that the gap between what extraction has cost and what has been paid runs an order of magnitude or more above the figures normally cited. The McDowell ton is the worked example: roughly five hundred ten dollars of floor against just under five at the mine mouth, over a hundred times by the carbon measure and still eight and a half to twenty-six times by the most conservative lens. Libby returns the same direction: documented public costs an order of magnitude above the mine's lifetime revenue before a single uncompensated death is counted. Direction never flips across the lenses.

The book also names what the framework cannot do, directly and without hedging. It cannot resolve the discount rate. It cannot price a life the model does not know how to recognize. It cannot predict technological breakthroughs. It cannot eliminate the tension between present needs and future preservation. What it can do is make visible what has been invisible, structure an honest accounting of it, and show that the insurance against the asymmetric downside is usually cheap.

## What this book uniquely does

Most of the recent wave of trade nonfiction on the structural economics of value diagnoses the problem with great force. This book does something the comps do not: it hands the reader a tool that can be picked up and run.

**It is a decision procedure, not only a diagnosis — and it scales.** The same accounting that prices a century-old ton of coal after the fact can be run forward, on a choice not yet made, to see what that choice would sever and what restoration it would owe. And it runs at any level the chooser occupies. An individual weighing a longer commute against a cheaper rent. A community deciding whether to draw down a shared resource faster than it refills. A nation deciding which materials to leave in the ground and which to spend. The book demonstrates this directly. Chapter 7 walks the framework through the decision of a habitat administrator on Mars — whether to grant a consortium's request to accelerate extraction from a fifty-year ice supply — and then runs the same instrument across asteroid iron, lunar helium-3, comet volatiles, the subsurface ocean of Europa, and, closer to home, a self-imposed commute lease that severs cost from a past self onto a future self with no extracting corporation anywhere in the picture. The same equation, different parameters, returns a calibrated range of verdicts: extract freely; extract aggressively; extract only with extreme accountability; preserve; or wait, study, and decide later when the cost is genuinely not yet priceable. Chapter 9 carries the forward procedure to national and international scale as an explicit four-step sequence — classify resources by how irreplaceable they are, reserve the most irreplaceable, invest the proceeds in substitutes, and reassess as the science changes — with an *Asymmetric Regret Rule* as the discipline for choosing under irreversibility. **This is the sharpest line between this book and its neighbors. They diagnose how value is extracted; this hands the reader an apparatus for deciding, before the fact, what an extraction will cost and who will be made to carry it.** [UPGRADE on Darity written consent: external-validation sentence — do not fill]

The book sits inside the most generative period the field has seen in a generation, and extends it in three further directions.

The recent wave demonstrates the appetite. Mariana Mazzucato's *The Value of Everything* (2018) renamed the value-extraction category in the public vocabulary. Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* (2019) showed how the legal modules that encode capital are not natural facts but constructed architectures. Brett Christophers's *The Price is Wrong* (2024) demonstrated that market pricing alone does not redirect capital. Daniel Susskind's *Growth: A Reckoning* (2024) treated the costs of growth as the explicit subject. Sandy Darity and Kirsten Mullen's *From Here to Equality* (2020; 2nd ed. 2022) priced a specific historical extraction and designed the apparatus that would discharge it. *Commons Bonds* sits inside that lineage.

It extends the diagnosis from financial-capital rent to physical-resource extraction, and from a single domain to a universality claim across coal, oil, fisheries, rare earths, financial instruments, pharmaceutical distribution, and — as the stress-test — off-Earth extraction. Mazzucato names the extraction; this book extends the diagnosis to the substrates Mazzucato did not price and adds the pricing apparatus.

It moves from diagnosis to instrument. Pistor diagnoses the modules that encode cost severance; the framework here prices what the modules sever and proposes the bond instruments that would internalize the cost. Darity and Mullen's *diagnose-and-price-and-design* discipline is the closest contemporary methodological precedent; the framework generalizes that discipline to extraction contexts beyond reparations.

And it answers the deepest objection that any pricing framework faces. Michael Sandel's *What Money Can't Buy* (2012) stated the serious version of the worry: to put a price on a sacred landscape, on a community, on the unborn is to launder the priceless into a transaction. The framework engages Sandel's worry directly. The choice is not between a sacred thing left unpriced and a sacred thing reduced to a number. The choice is between two numbers, because the thing already has a number, and that number is zero. A cost set at zero is not a cost left sacred and untouched. Zero is how the extraction got financed. The framework refuses that zero, in the lineage of Karl Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* (1944) and its operational descendant in Nancy Fraser's *Cannibal Capitalism* (2022): pricing-to-reserve, not pricing-to-sell. Pricing the commons in order to hold it back is the opposite operation from pricing the commons in order to sell it. The two operations happen to share the word *price*.

The book's location against economic orthodoxy itself is equally precise. Standard welfare economics guarantees mutual gain between the parties to a transaction and is silent about everyone else. Pigou named that silence a century ago. Coase showed how to resolve it wherever rights are clear and the parties can bargain. The framework operates where both run out: where the party carrying the cost is diffuse, voiceless, or not yet born, with no title, no remedy, and no market in which to be made whole. That is the silence the framework prices. Its commons are likewise not a departure from Ostrom's: where Ostrom governed the renewable coordination commons, the framework prices the drawn-down extraction commons, an extension of her tradition rather than a competitor to it, and the book keeps that boundary explicit. And where the accounting meets standard resource economics, it meets it exactly: per-unit residual commons value minus standard Hotelling rent equals per-unit cost severance, the bridge Chapter 6 formalizes as the Hotelling Identity. A reader trained on Pigou, Coase, Ostrom, and Hotelling can locate every move the book makes relative to all four. The contribution is not a new diagnosis. It is the apparatus that measures what no property right or market can price, built to err low at every step, and built to be run forward on choices that have not been made yet.

The framework also names what it cannot reach. It reaches the legacy effects of historical coercion — wealth gaps, longevity gaps, disrupted knowledge transmission, intergenerational trauma — through the same matched-comparison method by which it reaches the lung damage at Libby or the foreclosed households of 2008. It defers the direct pricing of coercion itself as an open question, one the reparations-economics field is already carrying forward and one this book does not undertake. The reach is real; the limits are named.

The book is also deliberately non-partisan. Chapter 5 names rent-seeking analysis from the Public Choice tradition — Buchanan and Tullock and their successors — as the structural complement to the framework's ledger, and extends an explicit invitation to readers at the American Enterprise Institute, Cato, Mercatus, and George Mason University, where the rent-seeking analytical vocabulary is most at home: the ledger this book builds is the natural complement to the analysis of how concentrated interests shape the architecture, and the two halves are stronger joined.

The framework is not an argument for capitalism, and it is not an argument against it. The model measures what extraction costs. A state-owned mine that severs costs is still severing costs. A worker cooperative that severs costs is still severing costs. The mechanism is not a feature of any particular ownership structure; it is a feature of what happens when value extraction and cost bearing are separated. The framework's success criterion is twofold, and both halves are achievable only by a framework that reads as honest across the political range: that *cost severance* becomes a phrase a labor lawyer or an environmental lawyer or a state attorney general can use in a brief without translation, and that the forward decision procedure becomes something an actual decision-maker — a fund, a regulator, a community board, a household — can run on a choice in front of them.

## Why this author

The framework was not built by an academic economist. It was built by someone who absorbed the cost-severance pattern into his own life across two decades inside institutions that managed costs by passing them to others, and who finally had the time, after the cost had landed on him, to look at the shape of what had been happening.

The author's working life began at the National Institutes of Health during the Human Genome Project, where he built the systems that took their research, ran it through peer review, and translated it for the fifteen distinct audiences who needed it — from specialist researchers down to the children living with the conditions the research described. From there to a consulting career that opened through World Bank and IMF engagements and ran for two decades, ending in the CEO seat of a healthcare-IT firm. The skills the work demanded — the willingness to ask the question the role did not include, to work at the seams the documentation never covered, to refocus on what the actual problem was instead of the problem everyone had agreed to keep having — were skills the author inherited rather than acquired. They came down from his grandfather, LaVern E. Winn, who spent a working life at NASA Langley as a model maker in the wind tunnels and as an inventor whose name appears in the patent record. Pooh, as the family called him, built scale aircraft and spacecraft components, so the tunnel would tell the truth about a full-size design that did not yet exist. His patents extended the same discipline: a tool bit for machining the resin-impregnated fiberglass laminates that went into spacecraft components, and a zero-gravity liquid-waste feed system for crewed missions. The territory fed the lunar program. The discipline was specific. His work had to function in spaceflight, where the cost of being wrong is borne by people who cannot recover from the error.

The pattern the framework names was not abstract for the author. It cost him the early years with his son and his marriage. He worked the inherited refusal-to-quit through three weeks back to back of close to a hundred and thirty-eight hours a week, then through the deals that took him on a plane away from a Savannah NICU at exactly the moment his son needed to be held, and finally through the recognition, years later and at altitude, that the system that priced his labor so well, that rewarded the inherited pattern so handsomely, could not, at its most generous to him, cover the thing a life will eventually need covered. In his best years, salary and commission together put him in the top one to two percent of household incomes in the United States. Even that ceiling would not have covered the year and more of surgery and rehabilitation life eventually required. If the ceiling does not cover the bearer of it, the question for the system is what it costs everyone earning beneath. That recognition is what eventually built the framework.

The author is now a nursing student at Virginia Peninsula Community College, in Hampton, Virginia, where his grandfather worked at NASA Langley. He lives aboard a sailboat in Hampton. He is not a professional economist. Elinor Ostrom was not one either when she did the work that won the Nobel; Matthew Desmond is a sociologist; Kate Raworth drew her doughnut because she was frustrated with how economists measured. The most useful frameworks in social science have often come from people who could see the gap precisely because they were not trained to look past it.

The AI ran the rigor that an institutional peer-review process would have run for a book published through academic channels: rigor passes, fact-checks, audience-acceptance audits, adversarial robustness tests, developmental-edit reviews, render-integrity audits. The collaboration left a complete repository of rigor-pass documents, ratification commits, and a Working Principles log — all preserved, all readable. The collaboration is named openly in the manuscript and will be named openly in any agent or editor conversation that follows.

## Why now

Three currents converge on this moment, and the book's forward decision procedure sharpens each: decision-makers at every scale face extractive choices right now.

The first is intellectual. The Mazzucato / Pistor / Christophers / Susskind / Darity-and-Mullen wave demonstrates trade-press audience appetite for serious, mechanism-level work on the structural economics of value. *Growth: A Reckoning* was named one of Obama's Favourite Books of 2024 and shortlisted for the *Financial Times* Business Book of the Year. *The Code of Capital* has shaped the legal-academic and economic-sociology conversation on how capital is constructed. *The Value of Everything* moves through new printings. The audience this work reaches is itself growing, and the next book in the conversation — the one that turns the shared diagnosis into a usable tool — has room to land.

The second is practical. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — in its transitional period from 2023 and entering its definitive phase in 2026 — demonstrates that pricing a severed cost at the border is administratively feasible at scale, and is itself a forward decision procedure run at international scale. The Inflation Reduction Act and the post-pandemic energy-transition debate have brought climate-finance into the mainstream policy conversation. Reparations conversations in California, in cities across the United States, and at federal-commission scale have given the diagnose-and-price-and-design pattern operational momentum. Norway's sovereign wealth fund reserved more than $2 trillion in oil rents by the end of 2025. Sovereign-wealth instruments now exist in Alaska, in several U.S. states funded by resource severance, and in a growing set of jurisdictions. The instruments the framework calls for already exist in partial form across enough domains that the work is no longer to invent them but to connect, strengthen, align, and extend.

The third is the cost-of-living crisis itself. The cumulative weight of medical costs, housing costs, childcare costs, and the unpaid caregiving labor that fills the gaps left by the formal safety net has made the question of *what things actually cost* central to ordinary life in a way it has not been since the inflation of the 1970s. Households reading a fundraising page on the screen of a phone made of materials extracted at a cost they have never seen, while their retirement accounts compound on instruments whose internal complexity is the reason they were sold, are reading the cost-severance pattern in real time. They lack only a name for it, and a way to run the math the next time the choice is theirs. *Commons Bonds* gives them both.
