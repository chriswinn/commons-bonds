## The bill that arrives with every breath

Imagine you're standing in an airlock, and the inner door won't open until you answer one question: how do you intend to pay for your air?

The question isn't cruel, and here it isn't even strange. Inside the wall, the air is made — split out of water by a machine drawing power that something had to generate, with the carbon dioxide pulled back out of the room before it rises high enough to kill you. Every litre is counted, because every litre had to be produced. So before they let you into the only breathable space for a long way in any direction, they need to know how your breathing will be paid for.

You have no answer ready. You've never needed one. You've spent your whole life drawing breath with no meter and no bill, and somewhere along the way you came to believe air is simply free. It isn't, and it never was. On Earth the cost was real the entire time; it was only ever paid by something else — the forests, the oceans, the slow chemistry of a living planet — quietly, on your behalf, with no invoice ever sent. Behind the wall there's no planet to carry it, so the cost comes to you, by the hour, on a meter you can read.

None of this is a prediction. The closed habitat is a way of seeing, not a forecast of where anyone will live. It's the cleanest version of a move this book runs throughout: imagine the commons absent, and watch the cost appear. Call it the *Commons Inversion Test*. Remove the planet beneath the priceless things — air, water, climate, the quiet work of taking carbon back — and they reappear instantly as metered line-items, because the only reason they were ever free is that something else was paying. Mars makes that visible by construction. It's the inversion test made physical.

## What it costs once you write it down

A thought experiment proves nothing on its own, so put a real number against it. In 1960, a ton of McDowell County, West Virginia coal sold at the mine mouth for just under five dollars, and the price was honest about everything except what it left out. Walk that ton through every documented cost at the low end of every range — the lungs, the water, the emptied county, the carbon it would become — and the floor is roughly five hundred ten dollars. That's over a hundred times the mine-mouth price by the carbon-externality measure, and still eight and a half to twenty-six times by the framework's most conservative lens, which prices only the foreclosure premium and doesn't depend on the price of coal at all. The line for the difference has been blank for a century — not because the cost was small, but because no one was made to write the number down.

And you don't need a habitat or a coal seam to have done this math. You did it when the cheaper apartment a little farther from work turned out to be the most expensive place you'd ever lived, because thirteen hundred hours of your year now belonged to the road and the road didn't give them back. A family does it when a parent's last months pull a spouse into eighty hours a week of unpaid care that appears on no ledger anywhere. The pattern, once you see it, you can't stop seeing. Value moves one way. Cost moves the other. The arithmetic on the listing — the line that says *rent* or *wage* or *price per barrel* — measures only one of them. The other is real, it's large, and the line for it is blank.

## The pattern has a name

This book gives that pattern a name. *Cost severance* is the mechanism by which value is drawn from a shared resource — a mineral seam, a fishery, an atmosphere, a body of labor, the trust between citizens, the future itself — and the cost of that drawing is detached from the party that benefited and fastened onto a party with less power, less mobility, and less ability to refuse. By the time the cost comes due, the beneficiary has usually moved on: the mine has closed, the company reorganized, the executives retired, the politicians left office. The cost remains, routed to whoever was standing where it landed.

This isn't a partisan claim; it's a structural one. It doesn't require anyone to have acted in bad faith — the cases in this book frequently show that no one did. What it requires is distance between where value is generated and where cost is borne, and the modern economy, by design, manufactures distance for a living. Naming the shape these cases share is not the same as making them equal: coerced labor and a long commute do not share a line.

## The book

*Commons Bonds* is a single argument carried through ten chapters and a technical appendix; the manuscript is complete at roughly ninety thousand words. The argument: cost severance is the load-bearing extraction mechanism of the modern economy; it can be measured with discipline from public sources; what restoration requires can be estimated two independent ways — from the documented damage record and from the foreclosure premium on the options extraction closes — and read against the bonds actually posted; the gap between needed and paid is large enough, across enough domains, to be a material claim about how the economy works; and the corrective apparatus — two coordinated bond instruments, one backward-looking, one forward-looking — would require no institution that does not already exist.

The framework rests on a few named instruments the chapters build cumulatively:

- ***Cost severance*** — the structural mechanism: value drawn from a shared resource, cost severed from the beneficiary onto a party who cannot refuse.
- ***Residual Commons Value (RCV)*** — the pricing apparatus: what every future generation loses by not having a resource, weighted by the probability that no substitute will exist when they need it, plus the externality tail that runs independently of substitutability.
- ***The Restitution Bond*** — backward-looking, person-affecting: discharges harm already realized (the lungs of Libby, the marshes of the Gulf, the foreclosed households of 2008, the McDowell life-expectancy gap, the unpaid caregiving of a dying body's spouse). Its informing tradition is the reparations-economics methodology of Darity and Mullen, Hamilton and colleagues, and Conley.
- ***The Foreclosure Bond*** — forward-looking, impersonal in Parfit's sense: discharges harm not yet realized (the atmosphere being spent, irreplaceable materials drawn down, options closed off for people who don't yet exist). Its lineage runs through Hartwick's rule, the civic-republican tradition of Pettit and Skinner, and the precedent of Norway's sovereign wealth fund, whose oil-rent reserves stood at NOK 21,268 billion at the end of 2025 — more than $2 trillion at the exchange rate of that date.
- ***The Commons Inversion Test*** — the disciplinary gate every admitted cost claim must pass, the move the airlock opened with: imagine the commons absent; ask what cost appears; if none appears, the claim isn't admitted. It keeps the framework honest against its own temptations.

The cases are deliberately scattered, because a mechanism shown once is an anecdote and shown across cases is a pattern: McDowell County coal; the harbor at Old Point Comfort and the Chesapeake fishery; Norway and Nigeria (oil); Libby, Montana (vermiculite); Deepwater Horizon; Baotou (rare earths); the 2008 crisis (the household ledger); Social Security; the healthcare commons (medical crowdfunding with a roughly ninety-percent failure rate; a pacemaker reimbursed at $461 against the long conversation at $54); and, as a universality test, the Mars habitat the overview opened with, built so that the arrangements that ordinarily hide cost severance — geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry — collapse by construction.

Chapter 6 does the central demonstration — estimating restoration two ways and reading it against what's actually been paid — and finds the gap runs an order of magnitude or more above the figures normally cited. The McDowell ton is the worked example; Libby returns the same direction, documented public costs an order of magnitude above the mine's lifetime revenue before a single uncompensated death is counted. Direction never flips across the lenses.

The book also names what the framework cannot do, without hedging. It can't resolve the discount rate, can't price a life the model can't recognize, can't predict technological breakthroughs, can't dissolve the tension between present needs and future preservation. What it can do is make the invisible visible, structure an honest accounting of it, and show that the insurance against the asymmetric downside is usually cheap.

## What this book uniquely does

Most of the recent wave of trade nonfiction on the structural economics of value diagnoses the problem with great force. This book does something the comps don't: it hands the reader a tool that can be picked up and run.

**It's a decision procedure, not only a diagnosis — and it scales.** The same accounting that prices a century-old ton of coal after the fact runs forward, on a choice not yet made, to show what that choice would sever and what restoration it would owe — at any level the chooser occupies. An individual weighing a longer commute against cheaper rent. A community deciding whether to draw down a shared resource faster than it refills. A nation deciding which materials to leave in the ground. The book demonstrates this directly: Chapter 7 runs the framework through a Mars habitat administrator's decision on accelerating extraction from a fifty-year ice supply, then across asteroid iron, lunar helium-3, comet volatiles, and Europa's subsurface ocean — and, closer to home, a self-imposed commute lease with no extracting corporation anywhere in the picture. Same equation, different parameters, a calibrated range of verdicts: extract freely; extract with extreme accountability; preserve; or wait and decide later, when the cost is genuinely not yet priceable. Chapter 9 carries the procedure to national scale as a four-step sequence — classify resources by irreplaceability, reserve the most irreplaceable, invest the proceeds in substitutes, reassess as the science changes — disciplined by an *Asymmetric Regret Rule* for choosing under irreversibility. They diagnose how value is extracted; this hands the reader an apparatus for deciding, before the fact, what an extraction will cost and who will carry it. Beneath that headline, the book extends the field's most generative period in three further directions. It extends the diagnosis from financial-capital rent to physical-resource extraction, and from a single domain to a universality claim — coal, oil, fisheries, rare earths, financial instruments, pharmaceutical distribution, and, as the stress-test, off-Earth extraction. It moves from diagnosis to instrument: where Pistor diagnoses the legal modules that encode cost severance, the framework prices what they sever and proposes the bonds that would internalize it; Darity and Mullen's diagnose-price-design discipline is the closest contemporary precedent, generalized here beyond reparations. And it answers the deepest objection any pricing framework faces — Sandel's worry that to price the sacred is to launder the priceless into a transaction. The choice isn't between a sacred thing left unpriced and a sacred thing reduced to a number; it's between two numbers, because the thing already has a number, and that number is zero. Zero is how the extraction got financed. The framework refuses that zero, in the lineage of Polanyi and Fraser: pricing-to-reserve, not pricing-to-sell.

Its location against orthodoxy is precise. Standard welfare economics guarantees mutual gain between the parties to a transaction and is silent about everyone else; Pigou named that silence, Coase showed how to resolve it where rights are clear and parties can bargain, and the framework operates exactly where both run out — where the cost-bearer is diffuse, voiceless, or not yet born. Its commons extend Ostrom's rather than competing with hers: where she governed the renewable coordination commons, this prices the drawn-down extraction commons. And where it meets standard resource economics it meets it exactly — per-unit residual commons value minus Hotelling rent equals per-unit cost severance, the bridge Chapter 6 formalizes as the Hotelling Identity. A reader trained on Pigou, Coase, Ostrom, and Hotelling can locate every move.

The book is deliberately non-partisan. It is not an argument for capitalism or against it: a state-owned mine that severs costs is still severing costs, and so is a worker cooperative. Chapter 5 names rent-seeking analysis from the Public Choice tradition as the framework's structural complement and extends an explicit invitation to readers at AEI, Cato, Mercatus, and George Mason, where that vocabulary is most at home. The success criterion is adoption — *cost severance* as a phrase a labor lawyer or a state attorney general can use in a brief without translation, and the apparatus as a procedure a decision-maker can actually run. The framework reaches the legacy effects of historical coercion through the same matched-comparison method it uses for Libby's lungs; it defers the direct pricing of coercion itself as an open question the reparations field is already carrying, and one this book does not undertake.

## Why this author

The framework wasn't built by an academic economist. It was built by someone who absorbed the cost-severance pattern across two decades inside institutions that managed costs by passing them to others, and who finally had the time, after the cost landed on him, to look at the shape of it.

His working life began at the National Institutes of Health during the Human Genome Project, building the systems that translated research for the fifteen distinct audiences who needed it, from specialist researchers to the children living with the conditions the research described. From there, a consulting career that opened through World Bank and IMF engagements and ran for two decades, ending in the CEO seat of a healthcare-IT firm. The disposition the work demanded — to ask the question the role didn't include, to work the seams the documentation never covered — he inherited rather than acquired. It came down from his grandfather, LaVern E. Winn, a model maker and inventor in the NASA Langley wind tunnels, who built scale aircraft and spacecraft components so the tunnel would tell the truth about full-size designs that didn't yet exist, and whose patents — a tool bit for machining resin-impregnated fiberglass laminate, a zero-gravity liquid-waste feed system — had to function where the cost of being wrong is borne by people who can't recover from the error.

The pattern wasn't abstract for him. It cost him early years with his son and his marriage, worked through three back-to-back weeks near a hundred and thirty-eight hours each, and surfaced finally in the recognition — years later, at altitude — that a system that priced his labor into the top one to two percent of US household incomes still could not, at its most generous, cover the year of surgery and rehabilitation a life eventually required. If the ceiling doesn't cover the person at the ceiling, the question for the system is what it costs everyone earning beneath it.

He is now a nursing student in Hampton, Virginia, where his grandfather worked, living aboard a sailboat. He is not a professional economist — but neither was Ostrom when she did the work that won the Nobel; Desmond is a sociologist; Raworth drew her doughnut out of frustration with how economists measured. The most useful frameworks in social science often come from people who could see the gap precisely because they weren't trained to look past it.

A note on collaboration belongs here, because the book makes it directly and a proposal shouldn't hide what the book discloses. The framework was built in extensive collaboration with Claude, an AI system from Anthropic, which ran the rigor an institutional peer-review process would have run — fact-checks, audience-acceptance audits, adversarial robustness tests, developmental edits — and left a complete, readable repository of rigor documents and ratification commits. The originating intuitions, the case selection, the vocabulary, the title, and the moral weight are the author's; the discipline that ran the framework against its own best objections at scale is what the collaboration added. The book's Author's Note describes it in full.

## Why now

Three currents converge. The first is intellectual: the Mazzucato / Pistor / Christophers / Susskind / Darity-and-Mullen wave has shown trade-press appetite for serious, mechanism-level work on the economics of value — *Growth: A Reckoning* was named an Obama Favourite Book of 2024 and shortlisted for the *Financial Times* Business Book of the Year; *The Value of Everything* keeps reprinting. There is room for the next book in the conversation, the one that turns the shared diagnosis into a usable tool. The second is practical: the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, entering its definitive phase in 2026, shows that pricing a severed cost at the border is administratively feasible at scale; the Inflation Reduction Act, reparations commissions from California to the federal level, and sovereign-wealth instruments from Norway to Alaska to several US states show that the framework's instruments already exist in partial form — the work is to connect and extend them, not invent them. The third is the cost-of-living crisis itself, which has made *what things actually cost* a kitchen-table question for the first time since the 1970s. Households reading a medical-fundraising page on a phone made of materials extracted at a cost they have never seen are reading the cost-severance pattern in real time. They lack only a name for it, and a way to run the math. This book gives them both.
