## Chapter 1 — The Quiet Math

**The move.** The book opens by establishing, through one working life, that the mechanism it will spend nine chapters formalizing is something the reader has already encountered and never had a name for. Cost severance is not introduced as theory. It is introduced as a recognition: the author, a junior developer with no degree and no certifications, watching the most credentialed man in the building take home about half of what he made — because the man's right to remain in the country was tied to his job, and everyone in the building understood without saying it that he could not leave. The language available — a free employee, freely working, free to quit — had no room in it for a man who could not go, and the chapter is about what it took to find the words.

**The scene.** The colleague opens the chapter: a man who climbed to the highest point of every city he entered because you do not really see a place from inside it, and who greeted the author each morning in a rotating one of the fifteen languages of his blood. Around him the chapter assembles the author's own ledger. The cheaper apartment outside Washington whose unlisted cost was thirteen hundred commuting hours a year — time enough to learn a language, to learn an instrument, to fall in love, and exactly enough to fall out of it. The grandfather at NACA-then-NASA Langley who built wind-tunnel models faithful enough that the air could not lie, machined a dead compressor back to life, sharpened the drill bits nobody asked him to sharpen, and mentioned both the way you mention the weather. The father, a naval architect who traveled on a diplomatic passport and spoke a hair below the room's volume, who asked before rising from his chair whether the children would be proud of what he was about to see. The NIH years during the Human Genome Project, translating genomic findings for around fifteen audiences running from the researchers to the sick children themselves. The hundred-thirty-eight-hour weeks. The NICU in Savannah, and the plane that lifted away from it when the leave ran out.

**The framework move.** The chapter plants the method as a felt experience before any formalization: the math of what a thing actually costs, including the part nobody writes down, including the part that lands on someone who never agreed to carry it. It establishes the no-villain discipline early — the company that ended the leave followed its protocols; the system did exactly what it was designed to do, which is the entire problem. It names the deepest version directly — labor taken entirely, by force, with Harriet Tubman carried as the figure of witness on the Underground Railroad — and draws the boundary the book holds from here forward: the shape repeats, but the weights are not the same; a commute and a stolen life do not belong on the same line, and naming the shape of them is not the same as making them equal. And it closes the author's own ledger against the system that priced him: salary and commission touching four hundred thousand dollars in the best years, a ceiling that still would not have covered the year-plus of surgery and rehabilitation a life eventually required — and if it could not cover the bearer at the ceiling, the question is what it costs everyone earning beneath.

**Connection to the arc.** The chapter ends as a ledger held open: an honest accounting that does not undo the damage, written for the costs still ahead of us. "I can only point out the empty columns. The numbers are yours to enter." The chapters that follow supply the method for entering them; the vocabulary arrives in Chapter 2.

## Chapter 2 — The Miner

**The move.** This is the chapter that names the mechanism. Kennedy drove through McDowell County in September of 1960, saw what he saw, and named it — as he said in Canton, Ohio, the month after — an indictment of neglect. The chapter argues he had the right indictment and the wrong defendant. The defendant is not the people of McDowell County. The defendant is the accounting system in which that extraction took place.

**The case.** McDowell County coal: a county of nearly 100,000 in 1950, under 20,000 by 2020; the richest metallurgical coal seams in the world, peak production through the war years, mechanization in waves, the U.S. Steel Gary closure dropping personal income by two-thirds in a single year. A 13-year male life-expectancy gap. A drug-death rate ten times the national average. The first food stamps in American history were issued there; the latest data suggests the county may need them again. Then the second cycle: Purdue Pharma's documented county-by-county sales targeting laid almost exactly on the map of communities the coal industry had already hollowed.

**The framework move.** The chapter introduces the working vocabulary the rest of the book will use without further apology. *Cost severance* is the mechanism by which costs are cut loose from the transactions that create them and transferred to parties who did not participate in the value extraction. *Severed cost* is the noun. *Value extraction* is the paired mechanism — separating worth from its source while declining to absorb the full cost of the separation. The chapter places this vocabulary explicitly in the lineage Mariana Mazzucato developed in *The Value of Everything* (2018) and David Harvey developed as *accumulation by dispossession* in *The New Imperialism* (2003), and is careful to say what it adds: extension of the diagnosis from financial-capital rent into physical-resource extraction, with a pricing instrument to internalize the resulting cost. The chapter walks one ton of coal through its true cost-to-revenue ratio — by the carbon-externality measure, well over a hundred times the price the coal actually sold for — and names this gap as the floor, not the ceiling.

**Connection to the arc.** The chapter closes by planting Chesapeake. Two hundred and fifty miles from the McDowell County courthouse, watermen go out before dawn for a different resource under a different mechanism with the same structure. "The miner and the waterman do not know each other. The mechanism connecting them has no name in ordinary speech. One of the purposes of this book is to give it one." Chapter 3 takes that hand-off.

## Chapter 3 — The Waterman

**The move.** If McDowell County demonstrated cost severance on a finite resource, the Chesapeake demonstrates the same mechanism operating on a renewable one — a system that, in principle, could have produced its catch indefinitely, and that has been drawn down past the rate of its own renewal across three generations of careful witness from the deck.

**The case.** The harbor at Old Point Comfort before light. Fox Hill, the island fishing community the author's father grew up in, where almost every family had at least one person on the water and the bargain that nets get rebuilt at three in the morning by neighbors who will not be asked. The 99% collapse of the oyster fishery between the late 1880s and the 1990s. The 1985 rockfish moratorium that worked, and the slow-motion repeat that the management architecture is not currently moving fast enough to interrupt. Virginia's 1994 shad closure that did not. The blue crab at the second-lowest level since the survey began. The menhaden — the food web itself — extracted at industrial scale out of Reedville for fish meal and omega-3 supplements that the consumer never connects to the Bay. Biggie eating oysters on his boat through a working day; a man called Phat who keeps the boat going only by running a mechanic shop on the Peninsula after the morning's haul.

**The framework move.** The chapter shows that cost severance is not coal-specific. The geography is water rather than tunnels; the resource regenerates rather than depletes; the externality tail is ecological rather than occupational. The structure is identical: value disperses across a continent of consumers; costs concentrate on the people, the boats, and the Bay that produces the catch. The chapter introduces the *paradox of recovery without honest accounting* through the Maryland oyster case — the population tripling under sanctuary restoration in the same year the state filed a federal disaster declaration for the wild-harvest watermen — and uses it to show that ecosystem recovery and the working waterfront are not the same recovery, and that pricing one without pricing the other leaves the people who paid still paying.

**Connection to the arc.** The chapter closes by naming Norway. The watermen do not know about the fund. If you described it to them, they would listen quietly and say it is a long way away. The question Chapter 4 takes up is whether the instrument that was built in Oslo could, in some adapted form, have been built for the Bay — whether the rents that have been quietly leaving this watershed for two centuries might begin to stay.

## Chapter 4 — The Existence Proof

**The move.** Cost severance is a policy choice. The chapter establishes this with the cleanest available demonstration: the same barrel of oil, extracted from roughly the same ocean, refined into the same products, sold into the same global market, produces radically different outcomes for the community whose commons was drawn from. Appalachia is one outcome. Norway is another. The variable is not geology. The variable is the institutional architecture that decides who captures the value and who bears the cost — and that architecture is engineered.

**The case.** Three days before Christmas 1969, a drill bit on Block 2/4 struck what would become Ekofisk. Norway's leaders, looking at Venezuela and Nigeria and early-phase Saudi Arabia, made a different decision than nearly every other resource-rich jurisdiction had made. The chapter walks the architecture: petroleum revenue flowing through state production licenses, taxation, and Equinor ownership into the Government Pension Fund Global; the 2001 fiscal rule limiting annual withdrawals to the expected real return; the consensus across the center-left and the center-right that has held the rule across Stoltenberg, Bondevik, Solberg, and Støre; assets now exceeding $1.9 trillion. The mirror image is Nigeria — the Niger Delta, the UNEP Ogoniland report, Ken Saro-Wiwa, decades of spillage measured in hundreds of square kilometers, the same oil, the same market, and an accountability bond near zero.

**The framework move.** The chapter applies the apparatus to a case that looks, at first glance, like it should have no cost severance at all. The answer is that the rents have been internalized — the financial cost severance is dramatically reduced — but the framework's residual commons value is not zero. The barrel is still gone. The fund does not put oil back in the North Sea, does not extract atmospheric carbon, does not address Bangladesh and the Sahel and the low-lying Pacific island states whose climate costs are paid largely in vulnerability rather than in krone. Norway is the existence proof for the *partial* solution, not for the complete one. The chapter also engages the U.S. Social Security pay-as-you-go comparison as a parallel demonstration that retirement-security architectures are themselves contingent choices, made in particular historical circumstances, with templates that could have been chosen differently.

**Connection to the arc.** The chapter establishes that cost severance is interruptible. The remaining chapters ask what would be required to engineer the honest outcome in jurisdictions that did not inherit Norway's institutional advantages — and what closes the gap Norway has not yet closed.

## Chapter 5 — The Accountability Gap

**The move.** This is the chapter where the pattern stops being a set of separate misfortunes and becomes one mechanism. An entity — a company, a generation, a political system — draws value from something it depends on but did not produce; the drawing generates a cost; and the cost is detached from the party that benefited and fastened onto a party with less power, less mobility, and less ability to refuse. By the time the bill arrives, the beneficiary is gone. The register climbs deliberately: the chapter starts with a town small enough to hold in one hand and ends at a single dying body, passing through an ecosystem, a global supply chain, a national economy, and time itself.

**The cases.** Libby, Montana, where W.R. Grace's vermiculite mine generated revenue on the order of $100 million and left documented public costs near a billion dollars before counting the uncompensated dead — and where the 2001 bankruptcy and the 2009 acquittal of the indicted executives are read correctly: not as evidence the harm did not happen, but as the legal architecture performing exactly as designed, the chapter's deep engagement with Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* (2019). Deepwater Horizon, where BP's roughly $60–65 billion in penalties and settlements sits against documented regional costs that leave a wide band unrecovered — and where the same oil produced settlements orders of magnitude apart on the two sides of the U.S.–Mexico line, priced not by the chemistry but by which legal modules each set of claimants could invoke. Exxon Valdez, named briefly and handed to Chapter 6 for its full decomposition. Baotou, Inner Mongolia, where the rare earths inside the decarbonization transition leave tailings ponds, illness, and farmland out of use, invisible to the engineer and the household at the bright end of the supply chain — with the evidence constraints of reporting from China stated plainly rather than papered over, and the case's center held by the no-villain beat: the consumer is separated from the cost by the entire length of the supply chain, and the mechanism needs only distance, which it manufactures for a living. The 2008 crisis as a double transfer — the thirty-year migration of retirement risk from institutions onto households, then the manufactured complexity of mortgage securities landing inside those same accounts — with Mian and Sufi's leverage arithmetic showing why the losses concentrated on the families with the thinnest cushions, and with the four strongest objections (borrower agency; global causes; TARP repayment; Dodd-Frank reform) each answered at full strength, closed by the Swedish 1930s proof that a society can choose to rescue both the banks and the households. Social Security, where the chapter polices its own side of the ledger: the circulating $108-trillion "foregone returns" figure is named as indefensible and retired, the honest counterfactual is rebuilt from the investable surplus into the trillions, and the severance is relocated from the portfolio choice (which was sound) to the obligation-generation — benefit expansions enacted during the demographic surplus by legislators who would be gone before the actuaries' long-projected deficit window arrived. And the healthcare commons, where medical crowdfunding fails roughly nine times in ten and reliably routes the least help to the thinnest networks, and where Katy Butler's documented account of her father's pacemaker — $54 for the long conversation, $461 for the implantation, a $12,000 hospital fee — shows the reimbursement architecture operating on a single body, the unpaid eighty-hour weeks of an elderly spouse appearing on no ledger anywhere.

**The framework move.** Two moves, and they are the chapter's heaviest lifting. First, the structural-commons identity: define a commons by structure rather than substance — anything depended upon and shared but not produced as a commodity — and the reef, the atmosphere, the fishing community, the body of labor, and the unborn generation collapse into one object a single apparatus can price. The chapter answers Sandel's commodification worry directly (the alternative to pricing is not the sacred left untouched; it is the number zero, which is how the extraction got financed), claims the lineage of Polanyi's fictitious commodities and Fraser's exploitation/expropriation distinction, and uses that lineage to disarm the financialization reading: accounting a cost is not coding an asset. Second, the two-instrument response, named here for the first time: the *Restitution Bond* discharging harm already realized — its economics running through Coates, Darity and Mullen, Hamilton and colleagues, and Conley, with the umbrella term chosen deliberately to join rather than rebrand the reparations tradition — and the *Foreclosure Bond* discharging harm not yet realized, on Parfit's impersonal standing, Pettit and Skinner's non-domination extended across time, Hartwick's rule, and Norway's fund as the bond-like instrument at national scale. The chapter explains why *bond* is the deliberate umbrella term — taxes vanish into general revenue, fees license the activity, funds pool without discharging; only a bond is posted before the cost is borne, carries across the liability's long tail, and discharges observably. It restores the complementary question — who shaped the architecture? — through the Public Choice rent-seeking toolkit, run honestly: cleanly where the lobbying record is documented (Libby, the Gulf's captured regulator, the 2008 deregulatory wave, pharmaceutical lobbying), admitted blurry where it is not (Baotou, Social Security), with an explicit invitation to readers at AEI, Cato, Mercatus, and George Mason, for whom that toolkit is home. And it draws the boundary the apparatus keeps: the framework reaches coerced cases through their measurable legacy effects and defers the direct pricing of coercion as an open question the reparations-economics field is already carrying forward — one this book does not undertake.

**Connection to the arc.** Diagnosis hands off to methodology: Chapter 6 formalizes the apparatus, Chapter 9 takes up the politics of closing the gap. The chapter's last word is the book's pivot — the question is no longer whether cost severance is real; the cases settle that. The question is what we are going to do about it.

## Chapter 6 — Three Ways of Counting

**The move.** A mechanism demonstrated once is an anecdote. A mechanism demonstrated across cases is a pattern. A mechanism whose price can be computed by three independent methods built on different foundations — and whose direction never flips across them — is a structural claim about how the economy works. The chapter does the computing.

**The cases.** McDowell coal, Deepwater Horizon, Libby, Exxon Valdez — the chapter applies all three methods to the same historical extractions and lays the results in a convergence table. Bottom-up damage accounting; the real-options model that values an unextracted resource as an option whose information value rises with substitutability; and the Residual Commons Value model the book has been building toward, which prices what every future generation loses by not having the resource, weighted by the probability that no substitute will exist by the time they need it, plus the externality tail that runs independently of substitutability. McDowell, run lens-explicit: over a hundred times the 1960 mine-mouth price by the carbon-externality measure; 67–134× by the RCV integral; 8.5–26× (center ~15×) by the most conservative lens, the price-independent foreclosure premium. Libby and the marine cases return the same direction at every lens. Direction never flips. The methods converge not because they were designed to but because the thing they measure is real.

**The framework move.** This is the methodological chapter. It introduces the RCV integrand — substitutability-weighted foreclosure plus externality tail, discounted under Weitzman's declining-rate framework — and the framework's central equation, *CS = RCV − B*. It introduces the *Hotelling Identity* (per-unit RCV minus standard Hotelling rent equals per-unit cost severance) as the bridge between the framework and standard resource economics. It runs the *Norway backtest*: applied to the best-managed extraction case in the world, the model returns CS small but positive. A calibrated instrument is supposed to differentiate; this one does. The chapter then introduces the four disciplinary gates every admitted cost claim must pass — the *Commons Inversion Test* (in two sub-forms: Commons-Absence Inversion and Commons-Consumption Inversion); units consistency; boundedness; independence. It distinguishes the *coordination commons* Ostrom addressed from the *extraction commons* this framework prices, and is explicit that the two methodologies extend rather than compete. It engages Parfit's non-identity problem and locates the framework's two-instrument architecture (Foreclosure Bond / Restitution Bond) within Parfit's distinction between forward-looking impersonal-outcomes evaluation and backward-looking person-affecting evaluation. It names the Asymmetric Regret Rule as the decision discipline under irreversibility. It surfaces, illustratively, ten commons categories that recurred across the eighteen cases tested — Habitability, Spatial, Temporal, Institutional, Kindred, Ecosystem, Political, Cohesion, Epistemic, Autonomy — and is careful to say these are examples drawn from cases, not a metaphysical claim about what exists, and that the framework accommodates several political-philosophical traditions on what counts as commons.

**Connection to the arc.** The mathematics is done. Chapter 7 tests whether it is universal.

## Chapter 7 — On Other Worlds

**The move.** If the framework is a political critique of a particular earthly arrangement, it is something less than it claims. If it is a general theory of pricing extraction from any commons, it should hold under maximally different conditions — and the chapter constructs those conditions to find out.

**The case.** A habitat of ten thousand people on Mars, several decades old, with three known subsurface water ice deposits representing roughly a fifty-year supply. A trading consortium asks the administrator's permission to accelerate extraction at a return that completes in five years and a horizon that drops to twenty. The chapter walks the framework through her decision and then runs the same instrument across a series of increasingly different scenarios — asteroid iron, lunar helium-3, lunar regolith, comet volatiles during flyby, the subsurface ocean of Europa, rare earths near an inhabited Mars habitat, and (closer to home) a self-imposed Maryland commute lease that severs cost from past-self to future-self with no extracting corporation in the picture.

**The framework move.** The same equation, applied with different parameters, produces a calibrated range of answers — extract freely; extract aggressively; extract only with extreme accountability; preserve; wait, study, decide later when the externality is genuinely not priceable. The chapter then makes the structural finding explicit: cost severance requires *operational arrangements that hide what is being paid*, and the chapter names six common patterns — illustrative not exhaustive, in the Ostrom-path register the book uses for case-derived enumerations — through which the arrangement is produced: geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry, power asymmetry, normative routinization, and abundance masking. On Mars, in a closed habitat, all six collapse by construction; severance is not impossible, but it is impossible to *hide*. The chapter introduces the *existential substitutability gap* as the property of a resource that determines whether the framework adds little (coal, sand, common stone — substitutable for any imaginable use) or whether it reveals systematic underpricing (rare earths, platinum group metals, helium-3 conditional on fusion). The chapter also stress-tests against the objection that cost severance is a feature of capitalism specifically — the pyramids at Giza, Qin Shi Huang's Wall and mausoleum, feudal agriculture — and against the *immune-response* objection that any accountability framework will be captured by simulated-reattachment performances (Kimberley Process, fair-trade certifications that do not reach producers, ESG screens that do not move capital).

**Connection to the arc.** The instrument holds where the parameters change and names its limits where it cannot price the externality. Chapter 8 makes the gap concrete for one ton of one resource the reader has met before.

## Chapter 8 — What Things Actually Cost

**The move.** The chapter performs the one worked example the book promises from its first pages. It takes a single ton of Appalachian coal — from the county Chapter 2 walked through, in the year Kennedy drove through it — and walks it, cost-component by cost-component, through the framework's Four Gates. It uses the low end of every range and omits every contested externality. The goal is a floor, not a ceiling.

**The case.** One ton of McDowell County coal. 1960. Mine-mouth price: $4.71 — just under five dollars.

**The framework move.** The chapter prices eight components the Commons Inversion Test admits for this extraction. Direct Health (Black Lung Trust Fund allocations, Medicare/Medicaid shifts, cardiovascular and respiratory mortality near processing facilities): $2 per ton. Environmental Degradation (acid mine drainage, the $4–6 billion reclamation bonding gap, mountaintop-removal cancer cases, downstream mercury): $1. Community Disruption (population collapse, regional multiplier losses, public-program costs absorbed by the federal ledger): $5. Foreclosure (2.61 tons CO₂ — the Pocahontas-seam factor, derived from the EPA's emission coefficients and the seam's measured heat content — × the EPA's 2023 $190 social cost of carbon): $496. Lineage Labor: $2. Knowledge and Cultural: $1. Political Capture: $1. Temporal Displacement: $2. Total: $510 per ton against $4.71, against every price coal has ever cleared at. The pattern is then traced briefly across Deepwater, Libby, Baotou, Exxon Valdez, the Canadian tar sands with Athabasca Chipewyan, Mikisew Cree, and Fort McKay First Nations treaty engagement, and the rent-burdened household where landlord-surplus capture operates on top of supply-elasticity differences — with serious engagement of the YIMBY supply-restriction objection.

**Three misreadings, named directly.** Honest pricing is not a claim that the extraction should never have happened; it is a claim about who paid and whether they consented. Honest pricing is not a claim that the market is fake; it is a claim that the market is incomplete. Honest pricing is not a tool by which already-industrialized nations pull up the ladder; differentiated transition timelines are a structural feature of honest accounting, and the revenue captured at the source funds the clean industrialization of the economies that are being asked to leapfrog.

**The brief extrapolation.** Run the same logic across the global fossil-fuel industry's annual revenue and add the non-fossil extraction sectors, and the global figure lands somewhere between ten and fifteen trillion dollars in annual severed cost. The chapter is explicit that this is a gestured claim, not a policy program, and that the sectoral simulation work it would require is outside what a framework book can honestly compress.

**Connection to the arc.** The framework has been validated, tested, and applied. Chapter 9 asks what an economy committed to honest accounting would actually do.

## Chapter 9 — Pricing Honestly

**The move.** The honest response to the previous chapter's arithmetic is not outrage. It is to ask what to do about it. The chapter is that answer — not a legislative blueprint, not a manifesto, but the set of practical moves a society would make if it decided to close the gap between what things cost and what we pay, sequenced into four steps operable with institutions that already exist.

**The four-step sequence.** *Classify.* Sort resources by existential substitutability gap — low (coal, common iron, sand: price the externality tail, bond the reclamation, let the market work within a corrected signal); medium (lithium, cobalt, uranium: progressive extraction rates and sovereign-wealth mechanisms); high (rare earths, platinum group metals, phosphorus, helium-3 conditional, fossil aquifer water: reservation). The USGS, the EU Joint Research Centre, and the IEA already produce most of the underlying assessments; what is missing is a single legible ranking that integrates all four RCV components. *Reserve.* For high-existential-substitutability-gap resources, leave a fraction of known economic deposits in the ground. The ground is free, permanent storage; the cost of in-situ preservation is the foregone revenue on the reserved fraction, which the Asymmetric Regret Rule shows is a cheap insurance premium against a potentially unbounded downside. *Invest.* Two simultaneous priorities: renewable substitutes (which raise the substitutability function and reduce future need at the same time) and alternative supply sources, including the gradually-arriving economics of extraterrestrial extraction from bodies whose RCV components are by construction near zero. *Reassess.* The classification is a living document. Cobalt looked abundant twenty years ago; helium was once given away.

**The international dimension.** Honest pricing at the national level relocates rather than reduces severance unless it applies to imports. The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, staged from 2023 and fully operational in 2026, demonstrates that border adjustment is feasible. A generalized version scaled to existential substitutability gap and widened across resource categories is the international-dimension analog of national pricing.

**Property rights, and their limits.** The chapter takes the property-rights tradition seriously — Coase, Ostrom, the free-market environmental literature, the empirical successes of well-defined ownership for rivalrous and excludable resources. It then identifies the three problems property rights structurally cannot reach: intergenerational foreclosure (future generations hold no deeds and cannot bid), transboundary externality (acid drainage and atmospheric carbon do not respect fences), and information asymmetry at civilizational scale (markets cannot price applications not yet invented). The framework is a supplement, not a replacement. Brett Christophers's *The Price is Wrong* (2024) is engaged at depth on the climate-finance side: profit, not price, drives investment, and the framework's contribution is to make the subsidy-and-rent architecture visible so the political work of redirecting capital becomes possible. Adam Smith would have recognized the move; it is the completion of the market mechanism, not a departure from it.

**The Nordic objection.** The serious form — scale, demographic homogeneity, resource base, historical contingency — is engaged on its strongest terms. The U.S. has not had to invent every piece from scratch; the chapter walks through the U.S.-domestic state-and-municipal-scale partial implementations (Alaska Permanent Fund, Medicare/Medicaid, Mitchell-Lama). The structural claim is not that Americans should adopt Swedish tax rates; the structural claim is that Americans already pay comparable total tax burden and merely fail to receive comparable provision, with the difference routed through a value-capture architecture (insurance, pharma, private student-loan servicing) that the framework's accounting makes visible.

**The political economy of resistance.** Mancur Olson named the dynamic in 1965. The chapter compares the U.S. 2008 architecture (bank rescue without household-debt restructuring, with Mian and Sufi's welfare-cost analysis quantifying the alternative) against the Swedish 1932–1937 architecture (bank rescue *plus* household relief, devaluation, mortgage modification, public-works employment, the 1938 Saltsjöbaden Agreement that survived through 1992) — both as policy choices, not market outcomes. Daniel Susskind's *Growth: A Reckoning* (2024) is engaged here as the growth-redirection complement to the framework's pricing-redirection. *Entering wedges* close the chapter: carbon pricing in seventy-plus jurisdictions; sovereign wealth funds in Norway and Alaska; Chattanooga's municipal EPB fiber network; Mondragon's nearly seventy years of worker-cooperative operation; Vienna's century-plus Gemeindebau; the Clarion-Clipperton Zone live decision; reclamation bonds; NEPA; the Community Reinvestment Act. None implements the full framework. Every component exists. The work is to connect, strengthen, align, and extend.

**Connection to the arc.** The framework has been laid out and the entering wedges named. The closing chapter asks what follows from recognizing that the miner, the waterman, the villager in Baotou, the off-world administrator, and the rent-burdened household are not separate problems but the same problem in different parameters.

## Chapter 10 — Common Bonds

**The move.** The book closes not with policy but with recognition. The harbor at Old Point Comfort before light. Watermen heading out. The miner in southern West Virginia pulling on boots at the same hour. The villager in Baotou. The fisherman in the Niger Delta. They live in different states or different continents, watch different news, vote for different people, have been given different enemies. They are bonded. They are the people from whom value is taken and to whom costs are left. That recognition is what the book has been building toward.

**The framework as compass.** The chapter offers the metaphor the book has been working toward without naming until here. The author's grandfather, who built wind-tunnel models at NASA Langley, did not invent the airplane and did not design the wing. What he did was build a thing accurate enough that reality would not lie about it. The book is a model in that sense — built faithfully, with the boundaries between what it can say and what it cannot precise enough that the reader can trust its outputs where it speaks and respect its silence where it does not. The framework does not lift the boulder. It does not flatten the hill. It does not grant Sisyphus rest. What it does is tell him which way is north — which direction adds to the total weight of severed cost in the world and which diminishes it.

**What the framework cannot do, named directly.** It cannot resolve the discount rate (Weitzman's declining-rate approach reduces sensitivity but does not eliminate the underlying moral question). It cannot price a life the model does not know how to recognize (Europa was the early surfacing of this limit; the Asymmetric Regret Rule is what the model does in those cases). It cannot predict technological breakthroughs. It cannot eliminate the tension between present needs and future preservation. What it can do: make visible what has been invisible; structure an honest accounting; show that the insurance is usually cheap.

**The Lakota / Black Hills case, named as the limit not yet carried.** The chapter places the *United States v. Sioux Nation* compensation — sitting in federal escrow at more than two billion dollars, refused for forty-five years and counting — as the case whose weight most tests whether the framework is capable of honoring what it claims to measure. The author acknowledges he has not given this case its full weight in this book and names the work as ahead of him, in engagement with Robin Wall Kimmerer, Leanne Betasamosake Simpson, Glen Coulthard, Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz, and Vine Deloria Jr. The framework's claim is structural, not moral-equivalence: what was done to indigenous peoples in North America was the American system operating as designed, visible at its most severe.

**The serious-left objection, taken seriously.** The chapter answers, plainly: this framework is not an argument for capitalism, and it is not an argument against it. The model measures what extraction costs. A state-owned mine that severs costs is still severing costs. A worker cooperative that severs costs is still severing costs. The mechanism is not a feature of any particular ownership structure; it is a feature of what happens when value extraction and cost bearing are separated. Whatever else we do, we have to stop pretending the price is the cost. The rest follows from that, or it does not.

**The two meanings of the title.** *Commons Bonds* names the obligations owed to the shared inheritance of air, water, land, minerals, knowledge, institutions, time. The mechanism of extraction, priced honestly, is meant to honor those bonds. *Common bonds* — with the emphasis shifted — names what the miner and the waterman and the off-world administrator share with one another. They are bonded by the same position. The title was always doing both jobs. You cannot honor the first without making visible the second; you cannot make the second visible without beginning to honor the first.

**The closing.** The watermen are almost at the mouth of the harbor now, and the first real light is touching the top of the Fort. The day is not long. The work is not finished. *Let us begin.*
