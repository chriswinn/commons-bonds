## A note worth a name

There is a kind of math most people have done, on a plane or in a car or in a hospital room, that economics has no good vocabulary for.

It runs something like this. You took a cheaper apartment farther from work, because the listing said three hundred dollars a month less and you can read a listing. A year in, you understand that the cheaper apartment was the most expensive place you have ever lived, because thirteen hundred hours of your year now belonged to the road, and the road did not give them back. Or: your father's last months pulled your mother into eighty hours a week of unpaid work that appeared on no ledger anywhere. Or: a coal company drew metallurgical seam out of your county for the better part of a century, the company left, and the county is now a fifth of what it was, where the first food stamps of the modern program were issued and where the latest data suggests they may be needed again. Or: a rig in the Gulf released 4.9 million barrels of oil into water other people fished, and the settlement covered some of what was lost but not all of it, and the rest stayed.

The pattern, once you see it, you cannot stop seeing. Value moves one way. Cost moves the other. The arithmetic on the listing — the line that says "rent" or "wage" or "price per barrel" — measures only one of them. The other is real, it is large, and the line for it has been blank, because no one was made to write the number down.

This book gives that pattern a name. *Cost severance* is the mechanism by which value is drawn from a shared resource — a mineral seam, a fishery, an atmosphere, a body of labor, the trust between citizens, the future itself — and the cost of that drawing is detached from the party that benefited and fastened onto a party with less power, less mobility, and less ability to refuse. By the time the cost comes due, the party that benefited has usually moved on. The mine has closed. The company has reorganized. The executives have retired. The politicians who made the promises have left office. The cost remains, and it has been routed to whoever happened to be standing where it landed.

This is not a partisan claim. It is a structural one. It does not require anyone to have acted in bad faith, and the cases in this book frequently show that no one did. What it requires is distance — between the place value is generated and the place cost is borne — and the modern economy, by its design, manufactures distance for a living.

The pattern repeats across a striking range of cases, and a discipline of the book is that naming the shape they share is not the same as making the cases equal. Coerced labor and a long commute do not share a line.

What follows is the framework that prices what the distance has hidden.

## The book

*Commons Bonds* is a single argument carried through ten chapters and a technical appendix. The argument is that cost severance is the load-bearing extraction mechanism of the modern economy; that it can be measured with discipline and from public sources; that three ways of counting it — what the harm and its repair cost, what bonds have actually been posted against it, and what the foreclosed options were worth — point the same direction in every case tested; that the gap between what extraction costs and what is paid is large enough, across enough domains, to constitute a material claim about how the economy actually works; and that a corrective accounting apparatus — two coordinated bond instruments, one backward-looking, one forward-looking — would not require any institution that does not already exist.

The framework rests on a small number of named instruments that the chapters build cumulatively:

- ***Cost severance.*** The structural mechanism: value drawn from a shared resource and cost severed from the beneficiary onto a party who cannot refuse.
- ***Residual Commons Value (RCV).*** The pricing apparatus. What every future generation loses by not having a resource, weighted by the probability that no substitute will exist when they need it, plus the externality tail that runs independently of substitutability.
- ***The Restitution Bond.*** The backward-looking instrument, person-affecting in the philosophical sense: discharges harm already realized. The lungs of Libby, the marshes of the Gulf, the foreclosed households of 2008, the McDowell County life-expectancy gap, the unpaid caregiving labor of the dying body's spouse. Its primary informing tradition is the reparations-economics methodology of Darity and Mullen, Hamilton and colleagues, and Conley.
- ***The Foreclosure Bond.*** The forward-looking instrument, impersonal in Parfit's sense: discharges harm not yet realized. The habitable atmosphere being spent, the irreplaceable materials being drawn down, the options being closed off for people who do not yet exist. Its lineage runs through Hartwick's rule, the civic-republican tradition of Pettit and Skinner, and the practical precedent of Norway's sovereign wealth fund, whose assets reserved from the country's oil rents stood at NOK 21,268 billion at the end of 2025 — more than $2 trillion at the exchange rate of that date.
- ***The Commons Inversion Test.*** The disciplinary gate every admitted cost claim must pass. Imagine the commons absent; ask what cost appears. If no cost appears, the claim is not admitted. This keeps the framework honest against its own temptations.

The cases the framework is tested against are deliberately scattered, because a mechanism demonstrated once is an anecdote and a mechanism demonstrated across cases is a pattern. McDowell County, West Virginia (coal). The harbor at Old Point Comfort and the Chesapeake fishery (oysters, blue crab, menhaden, the food web itself). Norway and Nigeria (oil). Libby, Montana (vermiculite). Deepwater Horizon (Gulf oil). Baotou, Inner Mongolia (rare earths). The 2008 financial crisis (the household side of the ledger). Social Security (severed in the decades the actuaries had told us it would be severed in). The healthcare commons (the medical-crowdfunding system with a roughly ninety-percent failure rate, the pacemaker reimbursed at $461 against the long-conversation reimbursed at $54). And, as a universality test, a hypothetical Mars closed habitat constructed precisely so that the operational arrangements that ordinarily hide cost severance — geographic distance, temporal asymmetry, informational asymmetry — collapse by construction.

The book argues, and Chapter 6 demonstrates by counting the same extractions three different ways, that the gap between what extraction has cost and what has been paid runs an order of magnitude or more above the figures normally cited. One ton of 1960 McDowell County coal sold at the mine mouth for just under five dollars. Walked through eight cost-severance components priced at the low end of every range and stripped of every contested externality, the floor was roughly five hundred ten dollars per ton — over a hundred times the mine-mouth price by the carbon-externality measure, and eight and a half to twenty-six times even by the framework's most conservative lens, which prices only the foreclosure premium and does not depend on the price of coal at all. Libby returns the same direction: documented public costs an order of magnitude above the mine's lifetime revenue before a single uncompensated death is counted. Direction never flips across the lenses. The counts measure different quantities, and one of them is a floor rather than an estimate; they still point the same way, because the thing they are counting is real.

The book also names what the framework cannot do, directly and without hedging. It cannot resolve the discount rate. It cannot price a life the model does not know how to recognize. It cannot predict technological breakthroughs. It cannot eliminate the tension between present needs and future preservation. What it can do is make visible what has been invisible, structure an honest accounting of it, and show that the insurance against the asymmetric downside is usually cheap.

## What this book uniquely does

The recent wave of trade nonfiction on the structural economics of value has been the most generative period in the field in a generation. Mariana Mazzucato's *The Value of Everything* (2018) renamed the value-extraction category in the public vocabulary. Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* (2019) showed how the legal modules that encode capital are not natural facts but constructed architectures. Brett Christophers's *The Price is Wrong* (2024) demonstrated that market pricing alone does not redirect capital. Daniel Susskind's *Growth: A Reckoning* (2024) treated the costs of growth as the explicit subject. Sandy Darity and Kirsten Mullen's *From Here to Equality* (2020; 2nd ed. 2022) priced a specific historical extraction and designed the apparatus that would discharge it.

*Commons Bonds* sits inside that lineage and extends it in three specific directions.

It extends the diagnosis from financial-capital rent to physical-resource extraction, and from a single domain to a universality claim across coal, oil, fisheries, rare earths, financial instruments, pharmaceutical distribution, and (as the stress-test) off-Earth extraction. Mazzucato names the extraction; this book extends the diagnosis to the substrates Mazzucato did not price and adds the pricing apparatus.

It moves from diagnosis to instrument. Pistor diagnoses the modules that encode cost severance; the framework here prices what the modules sever and proposes the bond instruments that would internalize the cost. Darity and Mullen's *diagnose-and-price-and-design* discipline is the closest contemporary methodological precedent; the framework generalizes that discipline to extraction contexts beyond reparations.

And it answers the deepest objection that any pricing framework faces. Michael Sandel's *What Money Can't Buy* (2012) stated the serious version of the worry: to put a price on a sacred landscape, on a community, on the unborn is to launder the priceless into a transaction. The framework engages Sandel's worry directly. The choice is not between a sacred thing left unpriced and a sacred thing reduced to a number. The choice is between two numbers, because the thing already has a number, and that number is zero. A cost set at zero is not a cost left sacred and untouched. Zero is how the extraction got financed. The framework refuses that zero, in the lineage of Karl Polanyi's *The Great Transformation* (1944) and its operational descendant in Nancy Fraser's *Cannibal Capitalism* (2022): pricing-to-reserve, not pricing-to-sell. Pricing the commons in order to hold it back is the opposite operation from pricing the commons in order to sell it. The two operations happen to share the word *price*.

The framework also names what it cannot reach. It reaches the legacy effects of historical coercion — wealth gaps, longevity gaps, disrupted knowledge transmission, intergenerational trauma — through the same matched-comparison method by which it reaches the lung damage at Libby or the foreclosed households of 2008. It defers the direct pricing of coercion itself as an open question, one the reparations-economics field is already carrying forward and one this book does not undertake. The reach is real; the limits are named.

The book is also deliberately non-partisan. Chapter 5 names rent-seeking analysis from the Public Choice tradition — Buchanan and Tullock and their successors — as the structural complement to the framework's ledger, and extends an explicit invitation to readers at the American Enterprise Institute, Cato, Mercatus, and George Mason University, where the rent-seeking analytical vocabulary is most at home: the ledger this book builds is the natural complement to the analysis of how concentrated interests shape the architecture, and the two halves are stronger joined.

The framework is not an argument for capitalism, and it is not an argument against it. The model measures what extraction costs. A state-owned mine that severs costs is still severing costs. A worker cooperative that severs costs is still severing costs. The mechanism is not a feature of any particular ownership structure; it is a feature of what happens when value extraction and cost bearing are separated. The framework's success criterion is vocabulary adoption — *cost severance* as a phrase a labor lawyer or an environmental lawyer or a state attorney general can use in a brief without translation — and that criterion is achievable only by a framework that reads as honest across the political range.

## Why this author

The framework was not built by an academic economist. It was built by someone who absorbed the cost-severance pattern into his own life across two decades inside institutions that managed costs by passing them to others, and who finally had the time, after the cost had landed on him, to look at the shape of what had been happening.

The author's working life began at the National Institutes of Health during the Human Genome Project, where he built the systems that took their research, ran it through peer review, and translated it for the fifteen distinct audiences who needed it — from specialist researchers down to the children living with the conditions the research described. From there to a consulting career that opened through World Bank and IMF engagements and ran for two decades, ending in the CEO seat of a healthcare-IT firm. The skills the work demanded — the willingness to ask the question the role did not include, to work at the seams the documentation never covered, to refocus on what the actual problem was instead of the problem everyone had agreed to keep having — were skills the author inherited rather than acquired. They came down from his grandfather, LaVern E. Winn, who spent a working life at NASA Langley as a model maker in the wind tunnels and as an inventor whose name appears in the patent record. Pooh, as the family called him, built scale aircraft and spacecraft components, so the tunnel would tell the truth about a full-size design that did not yet exist. His patents extended the same discipline: a tool bit for machining the resin-impregnated fiberglass laminates that went into spacecraft components, and a zero-gravity liquid-waste feed system for crewed missions. The territory fed the lunar program. The discipline was specific. His work had to function in spaceflight, where the cost of being wrong is borne by people who cannot recover from the error.

The pattern the framework names was not abstract for the author. It cost him the early years with his son and his marriage. He worked the inherited refusal-to-quit through three weeks back to back of close to a hundred and thirty-eight hours a week, then through the deals that took him on a plane away from a Savannah NICU at exactly the moment his son needed to be held, and finally through the recognition, years later and at altitude, that the system that priced his labor so well, that rewarded the inherited pattern so handsomely, could not, at its most generous to him, cover the thing a life will eventually need covered. In his best years, salary and commission together touched around four hundred thousand dollars at the ceiling. Even that ceiling would not have covered the year and more of surgery and rehabilitation life eventually required. If the ceiling does not cover the bearer of it, the question for the system is what it costs everyone earning beneath. That recognition is what eventually built the framework.

The author is now a nursing student at Virginia Peninsula Community College, in Hampton, Virginia, where his grandfather worked at NASA Langley. He lives aboard a sailboat in Hampton. He is not a professional economist. Elinor Ostrom was not one either when she did the work that won the Nobel; Matthew Desmond is a sociologist; Kate Raworth drew her doughnut because she was frustrated with how economists measured. The most useful frameworks in social science have often come from people who could see the gap precisely because they were not trained to look past it.

A note on collaboration belongs here, because the book makes it directly and a proposal should not hide what the book discloses. The framework was built by the author in extensive collaboration with Claude, an AI system made by Anthropic, beginning in March 2026 and continuing through publication. The AI ran the rigor that an institutional peer-review process would have run for a book published through academic channels: rigor passes, fact-checks, audience-acceptance audits, adversarial robustness tests, developmental-edit reviews, render-integrity audits. The collaboration left a complete repository of rigor-pass documents, ratification commits, and a Working Principles log — all preserved, all readable. The originating intuitions, the case selection, the vocabulary, the title, the moral weight: those are the author's. The discipline that ran the framework against its own best objections at scale: that is what the AI added. The book's Author's Note describes the collaboration in full; the framework's own apparatus is applicable, recursively, to the question of how AI-training datasets were compensated, and the author acknowledges directly that he is not the person to settle that question but is someone who used the tool while the question remained open. The collaboration is named openly in the manuscript and will be named openly in any agent or editor conversation that follows.

## Why now

Three currents converge on this moment.

The first is intellectual. The Mazzucato / Pistor / Christophers / Susskind / Darity-and-Mullen wave demonstrates trade-press audience appetite for serious, mechanism-level work on the structural economics of value. *Growth: A Reckoning* was named one of Obama's Favourite Books of 2024 and shortlisted for the *Financial Times* Business Book of the Year. *The Code of Capital* has shaped the legal-academic and economic-sociology conversation on how capital is constructed. *The Value of Everything* moves through new printings. The audience that this work reaches is itself growing, and the next book in the conversation has room to land.

The second is practical. The European Union's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — in its transitional period from 2023 and entering its definitive phase in 2026 — demonstrates that pricing a severed cost at the border is administratively feasible at scale. The Inflation Reduction Act and the post-pandemic energy-transition debate have brought climate-finance into the mainstream policy conversation. Reparations conversations in California, in cities across the United States, and at federal-commission scale have given the diagnose-and-price-and-design pattern operational momentum. Norway's sovereign wealth fund has crossed $2 trillion. Sovereign-wealth instruments now exist in Alaska, in several U.S. states funded by resource severance, and in a growing set of jurisdictions. The instruments the framework calls for already exist in partial form across enough domains that the work is no longer to invent them but to connect, strengthen, align, and extend.

The third is the cost-of-living crisis itself. The cumulative weight of medical costs, housing costs, childcare costs, and the unpaid caregiving labor that fills the gaps left by the formal safety net has made the question of *what things actually cost* central to ordinary life in a way it has not been since the inflation of the 1970s. Households reading a fundraising page on the screen of a phone made of materials extracted at a cost they have never seen, while their retirement accounts compound on instruments whose internal complexity is the reason they were sold, are reading the cost-severance pattern in real time. They lack only a name for it. *Commons Bonds* gives them one.
