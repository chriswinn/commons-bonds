Greenland is auctioning mineral tracts. Guyana has gone in a decade from one of South America's poorest countries to its fastest-growing, on offshore oil it had no institutions to govern when the first barrels came up. Mining companies and foreign ministries are mapping the lithium triangle of Chile, Argentina, and Bolivia, and the International Seabed Authority is still negotiating the terms on which the deep Pacific ocean will be opened. In each of these places a society is about to make the decision Norway made in the early 1970s, and most are about to make it badly.

The reason is not geology. It is institutional design, and the design is engineerable.

In 1969, three days before Christmas, a drill bit in the North Sea struck oil on a Norwegian concession that became the Ekofisk field. Norway then had fewer than 4 million people and a cold fishing economy on the margin of Europe. Today the Government Pension Fund Global, built from that oil, holds at its 2025 peak roughly $2.2 trillion, about $390,000 for every Norwegian alive. That was not luck. A small country looked at the producers ahead of it, saw how the windfall is usually lost, and wrote rules to prevent each failure before the first commercial barrel was sold.

The mechanism is plain. The state captures the petroleum revenue through taxation, production licenses, and majority ownership of Equinor, the former Statoil, and sends it into a fund that invests deliberately outside the domestic economy, so the windfall cannot inflate Norway's own wages and prices into the Dutch disease that has hollowed out other resource economies. A fiscal rule, *handlingsregelen*, caps annual spending at the fund's expected real return; it was adopted in 2001 at 4 percent and tightened to 3 percent in 2017. The capital itself is not consumed. The fund is structured to outlive the oil.

What is hardest to copy is the discipline. The rule has held across a remarkable run of governments: Stoltenberg's Labour ministry that wrote it, Bondevik's center-right coalition, Stoltenberg again, Solberg's Conservative-led government that tightened it rather than loosening it, and Støre's Labour-Centre coalition since. The doctrine predates them all. The Storting laid down its "ten oil commandments" in 1971, before commercial production, when the revenue was still hypothetical and there was nothing yet to fight over. Norway's investment returns now exceed its annual extraction revenue, so the instrument has outgrown the resource that created it.

Here is where the standard telling goes wrong. The usual praise runs: capture the rent, avoid the Dutch disease, hold the fiscal line. All true, and all measuring the wrong thing. The fund did not pay for the oil. The barrel is still gone. The carbon is still in the sky, falling on populations from Bangladesh to the low-lying Pacific that will never see a krone of the fund. And what the state banked, even at 70 to 80 percent of the net petroleum wealth, is a fraction of what the resource was worth once the foreclosed futures and the unpriced atmosphere are counted in. Measure Norway by rent captured and you record a triumph while missing the cost.

What the fund actually bought is reversibility: a permanent, compounding capacity to repair, substitute, and restore, held on behalf of the generations who will no longer have the oil. That is a smaller and truer claim than "Norway solved it," and the one that survives scrutiny. It is why Norway is the existence proof and not the finish line.

The contrast that proves the point is Nigeria, where the same oil, the same global price, and the same demand curve produced the mirror outcome. Revenue flowed through international operators and tax structures into accounts outside the country, and the Niger Delta was left with the externality tail. The 2011 UNEP assessment put initial cleanup of Ogoniland at the order of $1 billion just to begin, full restoration at 25 to 30 years, and Ogoniland is only a fraction of the affected region. That is not a Nigerian character flaw. It is an international revenue-flow architecture in which institutional weakness is one component among others. Venezuela drew its revenue down for current consumption; Alberta's Heritage Fund, founded the same year as Alaska's, was repeatedly tapped for the general budget; Alaska took a defensible different path, paying citizens an annual dividend rather than compounding sovereign capital.

The honest objection is that Norway was exceptional: strong institutions, high trust, clean administration. It is correct, and it does not rescue fatalism. Sachs and Warner showed in 1995 that resource wealth tends to track with weaker growth; Mehlum, Moene, and Torvik showed in 2006 that the curse is conditional on institutions, not a law of geology. The pattern targets vulnerability the way a market targets demand. Against the reading that institutions are destiny, fixed by history and not replicable, Norway is the counterexample: the doctrine was written before the money arrived, and the discipline held across every coalition since.

So the lesson is not "be Norwegian." It is that the defenses are an instrument a society can build, and the time to build them is before extraction begins, not after the wells run dry. Greenland, Guyana, the lithium triangle, and the seabed are open negotiations right now. Whether the architecture that governs them protects the communities whose commons is being drawn down, or routes the value around them, is not fixed by geology or national character. It is a choice, and it is being made this year.
