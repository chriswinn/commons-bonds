# The Norway Choice

When a country finds petroleum under its waters or critical minerals under its mountains, an old question returns. Who captures the wealth, and for how long? The question is being asked right now from Greenland to Guyana, across the lithium triangle of South America, and along the new frontier of deep-sea mining. Most of the answers being given are the answers that have always been given. Spend it, and leave the future to fend for itself.

There is one major exception worth examining at exactly this moment, because so many countries are about to make the decision Norway made fifty years ago.

Oil was confirmed under the North Sea just before Christmas in 1969. Norway could have done what almost every other windfall economy has done before and since. It could have let the revenue wash through the national budget, financing tax cuts and subsidies and public payrolls until the wells ran dry and the obligations remained. Instead it chose, slowly and with unusual discipline, not to spend the windfall but to convert it.

The mechanism is simple to describe and was hard to sustain. A sovereign fund was established in 1990, the Government Petroleum Fund, renamed the Government Pension Fund Global in 2006. Petroleum revenue flows into it rather than into current consumption. A fiscal rule adopted in 2001 then governs what the state may take out. The government may spend only the fund's expected real return, roughly three percent a year, while the capital itself remains untouched. The oil money is not income to be consumed. It is principal to be preserved, with only the harvest available for use.

The result is the largest fund of its kind in the world. It now holds well over two trillion dollars, more than 390,000 dollars for every Norwegian citizen. To keep the inflows from overheating its own small economy and inflating its own currency, the fund invests almost entirely abroad, holding stakes in thousands of companies across dozens of markets. A nation of barely five million people has become one of the world's largest owners of global equity, and it got there by treating a depleting asset as a permanent one.

What makes the Norwegian case instructive is not the size of the fund. It is the durability of the rule. The fiscal discipline adopted in 2001 has held across every government since, of the left and of the right. Coalitions have changed, prime ministers have come and gone, budgets have been fought over line by line, and through all of it no government has raided the principal. That is the genuinely rare achievement. Plenty of countries have set up commodity funds. The difficulty has never been the founding. It has been the second decade, when the original architects are gone and a politician facing an election can point to billions sitting idle and ask why they should not be spent now. Norway built a rule that answered that question in advance, and then honored it.

Underneath the machinery sits a clear principle, one long associated with the economist John Hartwick. The rents from a finite resource, if they are reinvested rather than consumed, can sustain a society's prosperity after the resource itself is gone. A barrel of oil is sold once. The capital it becomes can keep working indefinitely. Norway turned a wasting asset under the seabed into a renewable claim on the world economy, and in doing so converted a one-time geological accident into a standing inheritance.

This is the lesson for the new resource frontiers, and it is not the lesson usually drawn. The point is not that Greenland or Guyana should copy Norway in every institutional detail. Their economies are different, their politics are different, their resources are different, and slavish imitation would fail. The point is more fundamental and more useful. Norway's outcome was engineered, not inherited. There is nothing about North Sea oil that made prudence automatic. The same geology, handed to a different political settlement, would have produced a boom and a bust and very little to show for either. The discipline was a choice, and it is a choice that the new windfall economies can make as well.

It is also a choice that is far easier to make before the money starts to flow than after. Once revenue is arriving and constituencies have formed around spending it, the politics of restraint become punishing. Every dollar moved into a locked fund is a dollar visibly withheld from a hospital or a road or a tax cut. The window in which a society can credibly commit its future self to discipline opens before the first cargo ships, and it narrows quickly. Greenland is debating its mineral future now. Guyana is already an oil producer of consequence. The lithium economies of South America and the prospectors eyeing the deep seabed are making foundational decisions in this decade.

Each of them faces the same question Norway faced just before Christmas in 1969. The answer is not preordained by the size of the deposit or the price of the commodity. It is decided by the rules a country writes for itself, and by whether it can keep them. Norway's record suggests that the durable version of that discipline is built into law and structure early, before the windfall arrives to test it, rather than left to the good intentions of whoever happens to hold power when the money is at its most tempting.

The resource will be exhausted. Whether the wealth outlives it is a matter of design.
