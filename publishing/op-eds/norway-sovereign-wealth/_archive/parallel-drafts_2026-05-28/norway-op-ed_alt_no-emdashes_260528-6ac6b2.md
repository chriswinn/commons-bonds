## Op-ed body (993 words)

<!-- LEDE: news-peggable; current placeholder is generic. Swap for a specific news event (~80-100w) when triggers arrive. -->

When a country finds petroleum under its waters or rare earths under its mountains, the question that follows is the one resource economists have spent half a century asking. Who captures the value, and for how long? The question is being put again now, in jurisdictions from Greenland to Guyana to the deep-sea mining frontier in the Pacific, and the answers being given are mostly the answers given before. There is one major exception. It is worth examining now, when many countries are about to make the same decision Norway made fifty years ago.

<!-- NUT GRAF: states the argument. Should survive any news-peg swap. -->

What happens to a country's resource wealth is not destiny. It is institutional design. The sovereign vehicle today known as Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, founded in 1990 as the Government Petroleum Fund and renamed in 2006, was built from the petroleum revenues of a country with fewer than four million citizens. It now holds more than $1.9 trillion in assets, roughly $340,000 for every Norwegian alive today. The fund is not a Scandinavian cultural artifact, and Norway is not exceptionally virtuous. The fund exists because the rules were designed to prevent a specific failure mode, and because no government since has decided that consuming the inherited capital was politically worth doing.

The architecture is not complicated. Through a combination of corporate taxation, royalties on production licenses, and direct state ownership of Equinor (formerly Statoil), the Norwegian state captures something on the order of seventy to eighty percent of the net economic value generated by the country's petroleum sector. That revenue feeds the fund. The fund's holdings are deployed, by deliberate design, across diversified global equities, bonds, and real estate, all of it placed outside the domestic economy so the petroleum windfall does not distort Norwegian wages and prices. Under the fiscal rule adopted in 2001, known in Norwegian as *handlingsregelen*, the government may draw on roughly the fund's expected real return each year, currently set near three percent. The capital itself is not consumed. The fund is structured to exist in perpetuity.

The discipline required to maintain that structure is the part the rest of the world has consistently failed to copy. The 2001 rule was adopted under Jens Stoltenberg's first Labour-led ministry, and it has held through every administration since: through Kjell Magne Bondevik's center-right coalition, Stoltenberg's second Labour government, Erna Solberg's Conservative-led coalition, and Jonas Gahr Støre's current Labour-Centre coalition. Successive governments of left and right have each, in turn, refused to consume the capital they inherited. Investment returns on the fund now exceed the revenue Norway earns from new petroleum extraction. The instrument built to capture the rents of a finite resource has outscaled the resource itself.

Compare the petrostates that did not build the architecture. Nigeria has been a major producer since the 1950s. Over six decades, hundreds of billions of dollars in petroleum revenue have flowed through international oil companies and the country's political accounts, while the communities of the Niger Delta have borne environmental and health costs that the United Nations Environment Programme estimated in 2011 would require approximately one billion dollars and thirty years of remediation work to repair in Ogoniland alone, a fraction of the affected region. Venezuela's revenue was drawn down by successive governments to fund current consumption rather than capitalized for long-horizon use. Each jurisdiction answered the same question Norway answered, with the same commodity, in roughly the same global market, and produced almost the opposite outcome.

The closest North American imitations have fallen short. Alberta opened its Heritage Trust Fund in 1976, intending something Norway-like; subsequent governments tapped it to plug general budget gaps during oil-price downturns. Alaska's Permanent Fund, founded the same year, distributes petroleum revenues as an annual dividend to every resident. That is a defensible answer to a different question, but it is not the multigenerational sovereign instrument Norway built.

The obvious objection to drawing a lesson from any of this is that Norway began with strong public institutions, low corruption, and administrative competence most extraction regions do not have. The objection is correct, and it strengthens rather than weakens the argument. The pattern by which resource wealth is captured by elites or foreign operators while local populations absorb the damage is not random. The economic literature on the "resource curse," from Sachs and Warner's 1995 formulation to Mehlum, Moene, and Torvik's 2006 refinement, shows the outcome is conditional on institutional quality. The pattern occurs reliably wherever institutions are weak, political representation is fragmented, and the people who would resist are also the people whose paychecks come from the extraction itself. It targets vulnerability the way a market targets demand.

Norway's relevance for the rest of us is precisely this. Vulnerable jurisdictions are the rule, not the exception. The lesson is not "be Norwegian." It is to write the institutional defenses explicitly into law, structure them so the next government has to actively reverse them rather than passively erode them, and accept that defending the structure will be a continuous political project. The Storting's Industry Committee published its "ten oil commandments" in 1971, setting the doctrine before the first barrel was lifted. The 2001 fiscal rule put the discipline into law. The fund is what those choices have compounded into.

<!-- CALL: what should change -->

This is not a small lesson at the present moment. Critical-minerals reserves are being licensed across Africa, South America, and the Arctic. Deep-sea-mining permits are being negotiated in the Pacific. Lithium, cobalt, nickel, and rare-earth extractions are about to redraw the resource map of the energy transition. Each of these decisions is the same decision Norway faced in the 1970s, in different commodities, and most are about to be made the way Venezuela and Nigeria made them: by default, under industry pressure, without the architecture that would route the revenue to the populations whose commons the resource was drawn from. The architecture is engineerable. Whether it gets engineered is a choice. Norway proved that fifty years ago. The proof has not been refuted.
