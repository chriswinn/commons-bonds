When a country finds petroleum under its waters or rare earths under its mountains, the question that follows is the one resource economists have been asking for half a century: who captures the wealth, and for how long? The question is being asked again now, from Greenland to Guyana to the lithium triangle of South America to the deep-sea-mining frontier in the Pacific, and the answers being given are mostly the answers that have been given before. There is one major exception, and it is worth examining at a moment when so many countries are about to make the same decision Norway made fifty years ago.

What happens to a nation's resource wealth is not geological destiny. It is institutional design. Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, founded in 1990 as the Government Petroleum Fund and renamed in 2006, was built from the petroleum revenues of a country with fewer than four million citizens and now holds more than $1.9 trillion in assets — roughly $340,000 for every Norwegian alive today. The fund did not appear because Norway is small or virtuous or culturally exceptional. It exists because the people who designed it understood what they were preventing, and because every government since has refused to undo their work.

The architecture is not complicated. The Norwegian state captures something on the order of seventy to eighty percent of the net economic value generated by the country's petroleum sector, through corporate taxation, production-license royalties, and the state's majority ownership in Equinor (the listed national oil company formerly known as Statoil). That revenue feeds the fund. The fund's holdings are deployed across diversified global equities, bonds, and real estate, deliberately placed outside the domestic economy. Under a fiscal rule adopted in 2001, the *handlingsregelen*, the government may draw on roughly the fund's expected real return each year, currently around three percent. The capital itself is not consumed. The fund is structured to outlive the oil.

The discipline required to maintain that structure is the part the rest of the world has consistently failed to copy. The 2001 rule was adopted under Jens Stoltenberg's first Labour-led ministry and has held through every administration since: Kjell Magne Bondevik's center-right coalition, Stoltenberg's second Labour government, Erna Solberg's Conservative-led coalition, and Jonas Gahr Støre's current Labour-Centre coalition. Investment returns on the fund now exceed the revenue Norway earns from new petroleum extraction. The instrument built to capture the rents of a finite resource has outscaled the resource itself. Outside-the-economy investment is the discipline that prevents Dutch disease — the chronic dependency that has flattened other petroleum economies.

Compare this with the petrostates that did not build the architecture. Nigeria has been a major oil producer since the 1950s; over six decades, hundreds of billions of dollars in petroleum revenue have flowed through international oil companies and the country's political accounts, while the communities of the Niger Delta have borne environmental and health costs that the United Nations Environment Programme estimated, in 2011, would require approximately one billion dollars and thirty years of remediation work to repair in Ogoniland alone, a fraction of the affected region. Venezuela's petroleum revenue was drawn down by successive governments to fund current consumption rather than capitalized for long-horizon use. Each of these jurisdictions answered the same question Norway answered, with the same commodity, in roughly the same global market, and produced almost the opposite outcome for the people whose territories the resource was drawn from.

Even the closest North American imitations have fallen short. Alberta opened its Heritage Trust Fund in 1976, intending something Norway-like; subsequent governments repeatedly tapped the fund to plug general-budget gaps during oil-price downturns, and its assets today sit at a small fraction of what Norwegian discipline would have produced. Alaska's Permanent Fund, founded the same year, took a different and defensible path, distributing petroleum revenues as an annual citizen dividend rather than capitalizing them for long-horizon sovereign use.

The obvious objection writes itself. Norway began with strong public institutions, low corruption, a tradition of administrative competence, and a level of social trust most extraction regions do not have. The objection is correct, and it strengthens rather than weakens the argument. The pattern by which resource wealth is captured by elites or foreign operators while local populations absorb the environmental damage is not random. The economic literature on the resource curse, from Sachs and Warner's 1995 formulation to Mehlum, Moene, and Torvik's 2006 institutional-quality refinement, shows the outcome is conditional on institutions. It locates jurisdictions where political representation is fragmented, administrative capacity is thin, and the people who would resist are also the people whose paychecks come from the extraction itself. Then it concentrates the costs there. The pattern targets vulnerability the way a market targets demand.

That is what Norway's existence proof is good for. Vulnerable jurisdictions are the rule, not the exception. The Storting's Industry Committee set the doctrine before commercial production began, publishing its "ten oil commandments" (*de ti oljebud*) in 1971. The 2001 fiscal rule put the discipline into statute. The fund is what those choices have compounded into. The lesson is not "be Norwegian." It is to write the institutional defenses explicitly into law, embed them in instruments the next government cannot easily undo, and accept that the political fight to defend them will be ongoing.

<!-- CALL: what should change -->

This is not a small lesson at the present moment. Critical-minerals concessions are being awarded across Africa, Latin America, and the Arctic. Deep-sea mining permits are under negotiation at the International Seabed Authority. Lithium, cobalt, nickel, and rare-earth extractions are about to redraw the resource map of the energy transition. Each of these decisions is the same decision Norway faced in the 1970s, in different commodities, and most of them are about to be made the way Venezuela and Nigeria made them — by default, under industry pressure, without the institutional architecture that would route the revenue to the populations whose commons the resource was drawn from. The architecture is engineerable. Whether it gets engineered is a choice. Norway proved that fifty years ago, and the proof has not been refuted.
