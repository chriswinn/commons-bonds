When a country finds petroleum under its waters or rare earths under its mountains, the question that follows is the one resource economists have been asking for half a century: who gets the money, and for how long? The question is being asked again now, in jurisdictions from Greenland to Guyana to the deep-sea mining frontier in the Pacific, and the answers being given are mostly the answers that have been given before. There is one major exception, and it is worth examining at a moment when so many countries are about to make the same decision Norway made fifty years ago.

What happens to a nation's resource wealth is not destiny. It is institutional design. The sovereign vehicle today known as Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, founded in 1990 as the Government Petroleum Fund and renamed in 2006, was built from the petroleum revenues of a country with fewer than four million citizens and now holds more than $1.9 trillion in assets — roughly $340,000 for every Norwegian alive today. The fund did not appear because Norway is small or virtuous or culturally exceptional. It appeared because the people who designed it understood what they were preventing, and because every government since has refused to undo the rules that prevent it.

The architecture is not complicated. Through a combination of corporate taxation, royalties on production licenses, and direct state ownership of Equinor (the national oil company formerly known as Statoil), the Norwegian state captures something on the order of seventy to eighty percent of the net economic value generated by the country's petroleum sector. That revenue feeds the fund. The fund's holdings are deployed across diversified global equities, bonds, and real estate, deliberately placed outside the domestic economy to keep the petroleum windfall from distorting Norwegian wages and prices. Under a fiscal rule adopted in 2001, the government is permitted to draw on roughly the fund's expected real return each year, currently around three percent. The capital itself is not consumed. The fund is structured to exist in perpetuity.

The discipline required to maintain that structure is the part the rest of the world has consistently failed to copy. The 2001 rule was adopted by a Labour-led government and has held through every administration in the quarter-century since — across coalitions led by Centre-right Christian Democrats, by Conservatives, and by Labour again. Successive Norwegian governments of left and right have each, in turn, refused to spend the capital they inherited. Investment returns on the fund now exceed the revenue Norway earns from new petroleum extraction. The instrument built to capture the rents of a finite resource has, in effect, outscaled the resource itself.

Compare this with the petrostates that did not build the architecture. Nigeria has been a major oil producer since the 1950s; over six decades, hundreds of billions of dollars in petroleum revenue have flowed through international oil companies and the country's political accounts, while the communities of the Niger Delta have borne environmental and health costs that the United Nations Environment Programme estimated, in 2011, would require approximately one billion dollars and thirty years of remediation work to repair in Ogoniland alone — a fraction of the affected region. Venezuela's petroleum revenue was drawn down by successive governments to fund current consumption rather than capitalized for long-horizon use. Each of these jurisdictions answered the same question Norway answered, with the same commodity, in roughly the same global market, and produced almost the opposite outcome for the people whose territories the resource was drawn from.

Even the closest North American imitations have fallen short. Alberta opened its Heritage Trust Fund in 1976, intending something Norway-like; subsequent governments tapped the fund repeatedly to plug general budget gaps during oil-price downturns, and its assets today sit at a small fraction of where comparable Norwegian discipline would have placed them. Alaska's Permanent Fund, founded the same year, took a different path entirely — distributing petroleum revenues as an annual dividend to every resident, which is a defensible policy answer to a different question (immediate distribution rather than long-horizon capitalization) but is not the multigenerational sovereign instrument Norway built. Both Alberta's and Alaska's funds have now been operating for roughly fifty years; neither has approached the per-citizen scale that Norway's compounding produced over the same span.

The objection to drawing a lesson from this is the obvious one. Norway began with strong public institutions, low corruption, a tradition of administrative competence, and a level of social trust most extraction regions do not have and have never had. The objection is correct, and it strengthens rather than weakens the argument. The pattern by which resource wealth is captured by elites or foreign operators while local populations absorb the environmental damage is not random. It is a pattern that occurs reliably wherever institutions are weak, political representation is fragmented, and the people who would resist are also the people whose paychecks come from the extraction itself. The pattern targets vulnerability the way a market targets demand.

That is what Norway's existence proof is good for. Vulnerable jurisdictions are the rule, not the exception. The lesson Norway offers them is not "be Norwegian." It is "design the protections explicitly, build them into instruments that the next government cannot easily undo, and assume the political fight to defend them will be ongoing."

<!-- CALL: what should change -->

This is not a small lesson at the present moment. Critical-minerals reserves are being licensed across Africa, South America, and the Arctic. Deep-sea mining permits are being negotiated in the Pacific. Lithium, cobalt, nickel, and rare-earth extractions are about to redraw the resource map of the energy transition. Each of these decisions is the same decision Norway faced in the 1970s, in different commodities, and most of them are about to be made the way Venezuela and Nigeria made them — by default, under industry pressure, without the institutional architecture that would route the revenue to the populations whose commons the resource was drawn from. The architecture is engineerable. Whether it gets engineered is a choice. Norway proved that fifty years ago, and the proof has not been refuted.
