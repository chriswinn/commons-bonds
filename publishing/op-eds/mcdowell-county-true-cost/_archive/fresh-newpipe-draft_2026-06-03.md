# What a Ton of Coal Actually Costs

In 1960, McDowell County, West Virginia led the United States in coal production. It also queued for federal surplus food. That spring a young senator named John Kennedy campaigned through the hollows and saw both facts at once, the richest coal seams in the country and families lined up for government rations. The contradiction did not register as a warning. It registered as scenery, the kind of hard-luck backdrop a candidate notes and moves past. Sixty years later the contradiction is the whole story, and the country still has not learned to read it.

At the mine mouth in 1960, coal sold for about four or five dollars a ton. That was the price on the ticket, the number that cleared the market and built fortunes far from the hollows that produced it. It was also a fiction. The four dollars covered the cost of getting the coal out of the ground and onto a rail car. It covered almost nothing else.

Consider what the four dollars did not cover. Burning a ton of coal releases roughly two and a half tons of carbon dioxide into an atmosphere that does not bill in advance. At a defensible social cost of carbon near $190 a ton, a figure within the current federal range, that carbon term alone runs to about $510. Add the black lung that scarred the men who dug it, the streams poisoned downstream of the works, and the slow hollowing of a county that gave up its future one seam at a time. The honest price of that ton, once the shed costs are added back, lands somewhere above $500, on the order of $524.

The gap between four dollars and five hundred was not a bargain. A bargain is a price honestly low. This was a price falsely low, and the difference did not vanish. It was a loan, drawn silently against the future and against the county itself, and the bill has been arriving in installments ever since. It arrived as widows and oxygen tanks. It arrived as water you would not give a dog. It arrived as a population that fell by the tens of thousands as the work left and the damage stayed. McDowell County did not fail to share in coal's prosperity. McDowell County financed it, at terms no one ever read aloud.

The instinct here is to reach for blame, and blame is available. But blame is not the point, and McDowell is not really the point either. The point is the column. For most of a century, American energy accounting has written a zero where the real costs belong. Not a small number, not a contested number. A zero. The carbon went somewhere. The illness went to someone. The ruined water flowed to a real creek in a real place. None of it was free, and all of it was booked as if it were. A country that keeps a ledger like that is not thrifty. It is flying blind, mistaking the costs it has hidden for costs it has avoided.

This is where the usual political reflexes fail. Pricing the true cost of coal is heard on one side as an attack on a way of life and dismissed on the other as a problem already solved by cheaper alternatives. Both reactions miss what the McDowell ledger actually demonstrates, which is older and simpler than any current fight. Whoever creates a cost should bear it. That principle does not belong to the left or the right. It is the premise under every functioning market and every theory of personal responsibility ever taught. A firm that dumps its waste into a river and books the cleanup as someone else's problem is not running a tight operation. It is running a hidden subsidy, and the subsidy is paid by people who never signed the contract.

The objection writes itself. Nobody in 1960 knew the social cost of carbon, and a county clawing its way out of the Depression was not about to turn down work over costs no one could yet name. True enough. But the excuse expires. We can name the costs now. We have the carbon figure, the health figure, the long demographic arc of a county that bet everything on a fuel that paid in installments of loss. The question is no longer whether the costs exist. It is whether we will keep writing zero in the column that should hold them.

None of this requires settling, today, the precise tariff a ton of coal should carry, or the exact pace of any transition, or who should write the check. Those are real arguments and they deserve real argument. The prior step is humbler and harder. It is to stop pretending the costs are zero. A society cannot make a sane decision about what to burn while one whole side of the ledger is blacked out. The first honest act is not a policy. It is an accounting, the willingness to put a real number where a false one has sat for sixty years.

McDowell County paid that number already, in advance, without consent. Its streams and its lungs and its emptied towns are the receipt. The least the rest of the country can do, before it argues about anything else, is read the receipt and admit what the coal actually cost. The price was never four dollars. It only looked that way to the people who did not have to pay the rest.
