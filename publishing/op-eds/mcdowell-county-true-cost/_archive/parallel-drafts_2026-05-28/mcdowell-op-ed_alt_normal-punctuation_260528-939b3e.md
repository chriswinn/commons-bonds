# What a Ton of Coal Actually Cost

<!-- LEDE: news-peggable; current placeholder is generic. Swap for a specific news event (~80-100w) when triggers arrive. -->

Every few years, an argument resurfaces in U.S. policy about what energy actually costs. It tends to arrive when an agency revises a number, a coal company files for bankruptcy and pushes its liabilities onto the public balance sheet, or an Appalachian community lands in the headlines. The argument is older than any of those events. The United States has been postponing it for sixty years, since the year a single Appalachian county led the nation in coal production while its residents queued for federal surplus food. The accounting that should have happened then did not. It is still owed.

<!-- NUT GRAF: states the argument. Should survive any news-peg swap. -->

When energy looks cheap, the right question is who is paying the rest of the bill. A ton of Appalachian coal sold at the mine mouth in 1960 for roughly $4.50. The full cost of producing that ton — once health damage, environmental destruction, community collapse, and atmospheric carbon are priced honestly — was on the order of $558. The difference is not an accounting curiosity. It was paid, in lungs and watersheds and county populations and an atmosphere that does not send invoices, by people and ecosystems and futures that were not consulted about the transaction.

McDowell County, West Virginia is where this is most legible. McDowell peaked at roughly 100,000 residents in 1950; by 2020 fewer than 20,000 remained. When U.S. Steel closed its McDowell mine in 1990, personal income in the county fell by two-thirds in a single year. By 2022, the county ranked sixteenth-poorest in the United States, with a median household income near $28,000 against a national figure above $67,000 and a poverty rate of roughly thirty-eight percent. In 2015, McDowell recorded the highest drug-induced death rate of any county in America, at 141 per 100,000, close to ten times the national rate. Each of these numbers is the downstream product of an accounting system that priced one side of the ledger and left the other side blank.

Begin with what was left blank. The Black Lung Disability Trust Fund, the federal program that pays disabled miners and their widows, has distributed more than $44 billion since Congress established it in 1969. It has never collected enough revenue to meet its obligations. The Fund's debt stood at $4.5 billion in 2021 and is projected to exceed $15 billion by 2050. When a coal-producing company files for bankruptcy, its black-lung obligations transfer onto the public balance sheet. That cost was real all along. It just was not on the invoice that cleared at the mine mouth. Black lung was identifiable as an occupational disease by the early twentieth century; federal compensation came only in 1969, after a miners' strike and the West Virginia Black Lung Association forced the legislative issue.

Step outside the mine and the arithmetic continues. Across Appalachia, roughly 633,000 acres are awaiting reclamation under the 1977 Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act. The bonds coal companies were required to post total about $3.8 billion against documented cleanup needs of $7.5 to $9.8 billion. The shortfall, between $3.5 and $6 billion, sits unfunded in the federal ledger. Acid drainage from abandoned mines does not stop when extraction stops. Sulfide minerals exposed during mining keep reacting with rainwater and air for centuries, releasing heavy metals into watersheds long after the producing company has dissolved. The cleanup, where it occurs, is a perpetual public obligation.

Then add the carbon. Burning one ton of coal puts roughly 2.86 tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. The EPA's most recent estimate of what each ton of CO₂ costs the world, anchored to the Rennert et al. 2022 *Nature* integrated assessment and adopted in the agency's 2023 update, is $190. The implied climate damage from a single ton of coal, at that figure, is approximately $544. That number alone exceeds the inflation-adjusted price coal has commanded for most of the industry's history.

Three responses need to be addressed before anyone draws the wrong conclusion. The first is that this argument runs against the extraction having happened. It does not. Inexpensive energy lifted billions of people out of poverty in the twentieth century, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. The argument is narrower: the people who bore the costs should have been compensated at rates reflecting what they actually sacrificed. The second is that this treats markets as fake. They are not. The transactions cleared, the coal moved, the money changed accounts. What the market measured was the part of the trade it could see; a second trade was running in parallel, unmeasured, binding on parties who never sat at the table. The third is that honest pricing is a way for wealthy nations to pull the cheap-energy ladder up behind them. The argument runs the other way. Industrialized economies built much of their twentieth-century wealth by routing extraction costs onto a global atmosphere and biosphere that could not invoice them; those costs come due now primarily as obligations of the jurisdictions that benefited.

<!-- CALL: what should change -->

The policy question facing the United States now is whether to keep treating these numbers as theoretical. The social cost of carbon has been revised by four administrations on grounds that have less to do with carbon physics than with which side of the political fight had more resources at the moment. The Obama-era figure of $42 per ton was cut to between $1 and $7 under the Trump administration by restricting the externality calculation to U.S. borders — a methodological change Wagner, Anthoff, Cropper and colleagues flagged in 2021 as ungrounded in standard cost-benefit practice. The Biden administration restored the global-scope methodology, and the EPA's 2023 update raised the figure to $190. The Black Lung Trust Fund's structural deficit will not close itself. The reclamation shortfall will not fill in. The communities that bore the cost the first time are still bearing it. McDowell County is one. There are hundreds of others. The bill is not new. The decision is whether to keep deferring it or to begin paying it down, in a way that funds the transition to energy whose costs the price actually carries.
