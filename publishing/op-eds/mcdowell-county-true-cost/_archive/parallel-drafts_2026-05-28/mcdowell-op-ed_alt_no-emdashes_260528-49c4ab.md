# What a Ton of Coal Actually Cost

<!-- LEDE: news-peggable; current placeholder is generic. Swap for a specific news event (~80–100w) when triggers arrive. -->

The debate over what energy "really costs" returns to public attention whenever a federal agency revises a number, a coal company files for bankruptcy, or a coal-producing community shows up in headlines that nobody in the community wanted. The pattern is older than the news. It is the argument the United States has been postponing for sixty years, since the era when a single Appalachian county simultaneously produced more coal than any other county in the nation and ranked among the poorest places its residents could live. The accounting that should have happened at that point did not. It is still owed.

<!-- NUT GRAF: states the argument. Should survive any news-peg swap. -->

When energy looks cheap, the question to ask is who is paying the rest of the bill. At the mine mouth in 1960, a ton of Appalachian coal sold for roughly $4.50. The full cost of producing that ton, once health damage, environmental destruction, community collapse, and atmospheric carbon are priced honestly, was approximately $558. The gap between those two figures is not an accounting curiosity. It was paid in lungs, in watersheds, in county populations, and in an atmosphere that does not send invoices, by people and ecosystems and futures that were never consulted about the transaction.

McDowell County, West Virginia, is the place where the gap is most visible. McDowell peaked at roughly 100,000 residents in 1950. When John F. Kennedy campaigned through the West Virginia primary in 1960, McDowell was the country's largest coal producer and its residents queued for federal surplus food. Mechanization cut the mining workforce by more than half within a decade. The 1990 U.S. Steel mine closure cut personal income across the county by two-thirds in a single year. By 2020, fewer than 20,000 residents remained. In 2022, McDowell ranked as the sixteenth-poorest county in the United States: median household income near $28,000 against a national figure above $67,000, with a poverty rate near thirty-eight percent. In 2015, the county recorded the highest drug-induced death rate of any U.S. county.

Take the part of the bill that landed on the federal ledger first. The Black Lung Disability Trust Fund, the federal program that compensates disabled miners and their widows, has distributed more than $44 billion since its creation. It has never collected enough revenue from active producers to meet its obligations. The fund's debt stood at $4.5 billion in 2021 and is projected to exceed $15 billion by 2050. When a coal-producing company enters bankruptcy, its unpaid black-lung obligations transfer to the public balance sheet, which is another way of saying they transfer to taxpayers who never received any of the coal revenue. Black lung was identifiable as an occupational disease early in the twentieth century. Federal compensation recognition came only in 1969, after the West Virginia Black Lung Association forced the issue through a wildcat strike. The United Mine Workers under John L. Lewis had declined to press it at earlier moments.

The same arithmetic continues at the surface. The Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act of 1977 required coal operators to post bonds against future cleanup obligations. Across Appalachia, roughly 633,000 acres now await reclamation, with posted bonds totaling approximately $3.8 billion against documented cleanup needs of $7.5 to $9.8 billion. The shortfall, between $3.5 and $6 billion, sits unfunded in the federal ledger. Acid mine drainage compounds the problem. Sulfide minerals exposed by mining keep reacting with rainwater and air for centuries after extraction stops, releasing heavy metals into watersheds downstream. Where cleanup happens at all, it is a perpetual treatment obligation rather than a one-time remediation, paid from public funds long after the producing company has dissolved.

Then add the carbon. Burning a single ton of coal puts roughly 2.86 tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. The Environmental Protection Agency's most recent estimate of what each ton of CO₂ costs the world, the figure economists call the social cost of carbon, is $190, anchored to the Rennert et al. 2022 *Nature* meta-analysis and adopted in the EPA's 2023 update. The implied climate damage from a single ton of coal at that figure is approximately $544. That figure alone exceeds the inflation-adjusted price coal has commanded for most of the industry's history. The social cost of carbon has been revised four times by four administrations: $42 under Obama on global-scope methodology; $1 to $7 under the first Trump administration on a U.S.-only restriction that Wagner, Anthoff, Cropper, and co-authors flagged in 2021 as ungrounded in standard cost-benefit norms; $51 under Biden on restored global scope; $190 under the 2023 EPA update. The revisions had less to do with carbon physics than with which side of the political fight commanded more resources at the moment.

Three responses are worth addressing before anyone draws the wrong conclusion. First, this is not an argument against the extraction having happened. Inexpensive energy allowed billions of people to escape poverty in the twentieth century. The argument here is narrower: the people who bore the costs of producing that energy should have been compensated at rates reflecting what they actually sacrificed. Second, this is not a claim that markets are "fake." The transactions cleared; the coal moved; the money changed accounts. What the market measured was the part of the trade it could see. A second, unmeasured trade ran in parallel, binding on parties who never sat at the table. Third, this is not a case for wealthy nations to pull the ladder up behind developing economies. Industrialized economies built much of their twentieth-century wealth by routing extraction costs onto a global atmosphere and biosphere that could not invoice them, and those costs come due primarily as obligations of the jurisdictions that benefited from the routing.

<!-- CALL: what should change -->

The policy question now is whether to continue treating these numbers as theoretical. The social cost of carbon will continue to be revised. The Black Lung Trust Fund's structural deficit will not close itself. The reclamation shortfall will not fill in. The communities that bore the cost the first time are still bearing it. McDowell County is one of these communities. There are hundreds of others. The bill is not new. The decision is whether to keep deferring it or to begin paying it down deliberately, in a way that funds the transition to energy whose costs the price actually carries.

---

*Chris Winn is the author of* Commons Bonds, *a forthcoming book on the institutional architecture of cost and accountability. He spent his research career at the National Institutes of Health.*
