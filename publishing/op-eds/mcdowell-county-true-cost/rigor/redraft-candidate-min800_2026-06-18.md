When a coal company files for bankruptcy, or a regulator revises the price it puts on carbon, or a plant burns its last ton, the same argument starts up again. One side says coal built the country and the rules are strangling what is left of it. The other side says coal poisoned the air and the sooner it ends the better. Both sides talk as though the question were whether the coal was worth burning. That was never the hard question. The hard question is what the coal actually cost, and who paid the part of the bill that never appeared on the invoice.

Here is the gap. At the mine mouth in 1960, a ton of bituminous coal sold for just under five dollars. The Energy Information Administration puts the figure at $4.71. That was the price the buyer paid and the seller booked, and by the standards of the time it was an honest transaction. It was also a radically incomplete one. Add up the costs that the price left out, the ones that landed on other people and on people not yet born, and a careful accounting comes to roughly $510 a ton. The five dollars was real. So was the rest. The difference did not vanish because no one wrote it down. It was simply charged to an account that no one was asked to read aloud.

To see who held that account, go to McDowell County, West Virginia, in the years when the coal was moving. In May 1960, John F. Kennedy campaigned through the southern coalfields, and what he saw there he repeated that September in Canton, Ohio. McDowell County, he said, mines more coal than it ever has in its history, and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than in any county in the United States. Both halves of that sentence were true at once. Record production and record hunger, in the same place, in the same year. The county was not failing to share in the prosperity it was making. It was financing that prosperity, at terms no one ever read it.

What followed is the part usually told as a story of character. The mines mechanized, the company that ran much of the county wound down its operations across the 1980s, and the people left. A county that held 98,887 residents in 1950 holds 19,111 today, by the Census count. The standard reading of that collapse, the one J.D. Vance made into a national bestseller, locates the cause in the people who stayed: a culture of dependency, of fatalism, of choices badly made. The historians who have actually worked the record tell a different story. Ronald Eller's study of the region after 1945 describes development that produced wealth without producing prosperity, and McDowell is its sharpest case. The county did not decline because its people lost their habits. It declined because the wealth it generated was designed to leave, and the costs of generating it were designed to stay. That is not a moral failing. It is an accounting one, and it can be added up.

Start with the lungs the coal was dug with. The federal black lung program has paid out more than $44 billion in benefits through 2009, the last year for which a reliable cumulative figure exists, according to government accounting. That is money paid because the men who mined the coal could not breathe afterward, and it is a floor, not a ceiling. The fund that pays it has been borrowing from the general treasury almost every year since 1979; its debt stood at $5.1 billion as of September 2024. Black lung was supposed to be a disease of the past. It is not. It is resurgent, and it is moving fastest among younger miners. The cost did not stay in the 1960s any more than the coal did.

Then there is the ground itself. More than 633,000 acres of mined Appalachian land are still awaiting reclamation, according to a 2021 accounting by Appalachian Voices, with a documented cleanup need of $7.5 to $9.8 billion against roughly $3.8 billion in posted bonds. The shortfall, somewhere between four and six billion dollars, is a bill that comes due whether or not anyone has set aside the money. And where the rock has been broken open, the chemistry does not stop. Exposed sulfide minerals oxidize on contact with air and water and acidify the streams, a reaction that does not run down and stop. It runs for as long as anyone is willing to keep treating the water, which is to say indefinitely. That, too, is a cost. It was simply never priced.

And then the part that travels furthest. Burning a ton of coal from the Pocahontas seam that runs under McDowell releases about 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide, a figure that follows from the EPA's standard emission factor applied to that seam's measured heat content. Put against that the Environmental Protection Agency's 2023 central estimate of the damage each ton of carbon does, $190, and the climate cost of a single ton of McDowell coal comes to about $496. That one component, by itself, is a hundred times the 1960 price the coal sold for. And it is a conservative number by construction. The $190 is the government's own mainstream estimate, not the highest credible one in the literature; it is roughly the figure that Rennert and colleagues arrived at in Nature in 2022, which the EPA drew on. Lowball the carbon deliberately, and the cost is still close to five hundred dollars a ton.

Set the four real numbers next to each other. Black lung, abandoned land, perpetual acid drainage, carbon. Add the smaller heads that round out the ledger, and the honest price of that ton of coal is the $510 already named. The county got the five dollars. It is still paying the rest.

A few clarifications, because the argument is easy to mistake for three other arguments it is not. It is not a claim that the coal should have stayed in the ground. Cheap energy lifted billions of people out of poverty, and that is a real good, not a rhetorical concession. The narrow claim is that the people who bore the costs should have been compensated out of the value they helped create, and were not. It is not a claim that markets are a fiction. The priced transaction cleared exactly as advertised; the trouble is that a second, unpriced transaction ran alongside it, binding parties who were never at the table. The largest of those parties is the generation now paying the carbon bill, which could not have negotiated a better price because it did not yet exist. No bargain, however efficient, can include a party who is not there to bargain. And it is not a claim that today's developing economies should be denied the cheap energy the rich world used. The costs in this ledger come due first and heaviest on the places that already took the benefit. The point is not to pull the ladder up. It is to stop pretending the ladder was free.

One more thing the ledger makes visible. Coal is not a fishery. A fishery taken within its power to regenerate comes back; the harvest and the stock can be balanced. Coal cannot be regrown. A ton burned is a ton gone, and the loan it represents against the future can be repaid only in money, never in kind. That is why the bill keeps arriving long after the mine has closed, and why no amount of subsequent prosperity in McDowell, had there been any, could have settled it.

None of this is hidden knowledge. The number that does the most work in the ledger, the social cost of carbon, has been a public figure for fifteen years, and its recent history is its own small lesson in how a cost gets managed. The Obama administration set it near $42 a ton, counting the damage to the whole world. The Trump administration cut it to somewhere between one and seven dollars, not by finding new evidence but by deciding to count only the damage that falls inside U.S. borders, a move that William Nordhaus's fellow economists, among them Wagner, Anthoff, and Cropper, criticized in 2021 as a departure from ordinary cost-benefit practice. The Biden administration restored the global scope, and the EPA's 2023 revision raised the figure to $190. The cost did not change across those four revisions. Only the willingness to write it down did.

Which is the whole decision, finally, stripped of the news peg that brought it up. A cost that is real but unpriced does not disappear. It defers, and it compounds, and it lands somewhere, usually on whoever is least able to refuse it. McDowell County has been refusing it for sixty years and losing. The principle underneath all of this is older than any framework and belongs to no political party: whoever creates a cost should be the one to bear it. We can keep deferring the bill, or we can begin to pay it down on purpose, and price the next energy source so that the ton carries its own true weight before it is burned, not a lifetime after. The five-dollar coal was never a bargain. A bargain is a price that is honestly low. This was a price that was falsely low, and the people who made up the difference are still making it up.

*Chris Winn is the author of* Commons Bonds, *a forthcoming book on the institutional architecture of cost and accountability. He spent his research career at the National Institutes of Health.*
