# What a Ton of Coal Actually Cost

Every so often the country is reminded that the coalfields are still on its books. A bankruptcy filing moves a pension or a black-lung obligation onto the public ledger; a cleanup figure lands; an agency revises what a ton of carbon costs. The reminder passes, and the underlying question goes unasked: what did the coal actually cost, and who paid the part of the bill that never appeared on the invoice.

Start with the price. In 1960, a ton of bituminous coal sold at the West Virginia mine mouth for $4.71, just under five dollars, according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration. That was the number the market saw. It was not the number the country paid. Add the damage the same ton does to the atmosphere, the lungs it eventually scars, the water it leaves acidic for centuries, the land that has to be put back, and a fuller accounting runs to roughly $510 a ton. The gap between five dollars and five hundred did not vanish because no one wrote it down. Somebody paid it. The question this piece is about is who.

The answer, for the better part of a century, was McDowell County, West Virginia. In 1960, when John F. Kennedy came through during the primary campaign, McDowell held nearly a hundred thousand people. That September, in Canton, Ohio, he put the paradox plainly: McDowell County, he said, was mining more coal than it ever had in its history, and more people there were receiving surplus food packages than in any county in the United States. Both halves were true at once. The coal was leaving at record volume. The prosperity it represented was leaving with it. Today the county holds under twenty thousand people, according to the Census. A place of nearly a hundred thousand now holds fewer than one in five of them. In the worst single year after U.S. Steel began winding down its operations across the 1980s, county personal income fell by about thirteen percent, by the Bureau of Economic Analysis figures. The drug-overdose death rate reached 141 per hundred thousand in 2015, the highest of any county in the nation against a national rate near sixteen, by the Centers for Disease Control. This is not a county that failed to share in coal's prosperity. It is a county that financed it, at terms no one ever read aloud.

It is worth being precise about what that financing consisted of, because the precision is the argument. Take three of the unpriced columns.

The first is what the coal did to the men who dug it. Black-lung benefits paid out through the federal program came to more than $44 billion through 2009, by the accounting of the Government Accountability Office and the Congressional Research Service, a conservative and honestly dated floor, since no authoritative cumulative total has been compiled since. The fund that pays those benefits is itself $5.1 billion in debt to the general treasury as of September 2024, by the Department of Labor's figures, having borrowed from the public fund nearly every year since 1979. Those are not abstractions. They are a dollar count placed on a specific and continuing injury to a specific population of men, a disease that is resurgent today and advancing fastest among the youngest miners. Compensation for it did not arrive until 1969, and then only after the West Virginia Black Lung Association struck for it; the union under John L. Lewis had earlier declined to press the claim.

The second column is the land and the water. More than 633,000 acres across Appalachia still await reclamation, with a documented cleanup need of $7.5 to $9.8 billion against roughly $3.8 billion in posted bonds, a gap of four to six billion dollars, by the 2021 accounting of Appalachian Voices. The chemistry does not negotiate. Sulfide rock, once exposed and oxidizing, drains acid into streams on a timescale of centuries, which is to say that some of the bill comes due not annually but perpetually, long after the last operator has filed its last return.

The third column is the carbon. A ton of the Pocahontas-seam coal that built McDowell releases about 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide when burned, a figure that follows from the Environmental Protection Agency's emission factor and the seam's measured heat content as recorded by the U.S. Geological Survey and the EIA. Priced at the EPA's 2023 central social cost of carbon, $190 a ton, that single externality runs to about $496 a ton of coal. It is worth dwelling on what $190 is and is not. It is the government's own mainstream number, the central estimate, not the highest credible one; the EPA drew it from work by Rennert and colleagues published in *Nature* in 2022, whose own central figure was slightly lower. We are using the conservative figure, and one column alone already roughly equals the entire honest total. That is the lowball. The truth is heavier.

Add the eight components an honest accounting recognizes and the total reaches about $510 a ton, against the $4.71 the market charged in 1960. There is no need to convert that into a single dramatic multiple; the two numbers do the work side by side. A bargain is a price honestly low. This was a price falsely low, and the difference did not disappear. It was borne, in lungs and streams and a depopulated county, by people who were never party to the sale.

That last point is where the accounting turns into something more than bookkeeping, and it is worth being exact about three things this argument is not.

It is not a claim that the coal should have stayed in the ground. Cheap energy lifted billions of people out of poverty, and that achievement is real. The narrow claim is only that the people and places that bore the unpriced costs should have been compensated out of the value they helped create, and were not.

It is not a claim that markets are a fiction. The priced transaction cleared honestly; coal changed hands at $4.71 and the buyer got his ton. The trouble is that a second, unpriced transaction ran alongside the first, binding parties who were never at the table. Some of those parties did not yet exist. Coal, unlike a fishery harvested within its power to regenerate, cannot be regrown; the loan drawn against the future can be repaid only in cash, never in kind, and the generation now paying it back never sat at the table where the price was set, which is precisely why no bargain struck there could have caught the cost.

And it is not a case against the developing world's right to cheap power, the familiar ladder-pulling charge. The costs catalogued here come due first and heaviest on the very jurisdictions that took the benefit. McDowell is not an argument made at someone else's expense; it is the receipt the country wrote to itself and then declined to read.

All of which bears directly on a story the country has told itself about places like McDowell, and here it is worth naming the foil. The popular reading, given its most-read recent form in J.D. Vance's *Hillbilly Elegy*, locates the region's decline in habit and character, a culture that failed to keep up. The ledger says otherwise, and so do the historians who have worked the ground. Ronald Eller's *Uneven Ground* traced an Appalachia that saw a century of development without prosperity; the McDowell arc, from ninety-nine thousand people to nineteen thousand, is that pattern at its sharpest. What the record here adds to Eller, and to Elizabeth Catte's corrective against the stereotype, and to Steven Stoll's history of how the region's wealth was carried off, is a number and a mechanism: an honest per-ton price, and the place where the unpaid balance landed. The decline was not a verdict on the people. It was an accounting that priced one side of the ledger and left the other blank.

The blank was a choice, and choices can be revisited. The social cost of carbon itself is the proof. The Obama administration set it near $42, measured against the damage to the whole world. A later administration cut it to somewhere between one and seven dollars, largely by deciding to count only the harm that falls inside U.S. borders, a move that Gernot Wagner, David Anthoff, Maureen Cropper and colleagues argued in 2021 had no grounding in standard cost-benefit practice. The figure was restored to a global basis, then raised by the EPA to $190. The same ton of coal did the same damage throughout. Only the willingness to write the number down changed.

That is finally the decision in front of the country, and it does not belong to one political coalition. Whoever creates a cost should bear it. That principle is not the property of the left or the right; it is the oldest rule of a fair sale, that you pay for what you take. The choice is whether to keep deferring the unpaid balance, letting it fall where it has always fallen, or to begin paying it down on purpose, and to build the next energy system on prices that carry their own costs rather than posting them, as McDowell's were posted, to an account in someone else's name that no one expected to ever come and read.
