When a coal company files for bankruptcy and asks a court to move its black-lung obligations onto the public, the headlines treat it as a story about one failing firm. It is really a story about an old bill. The firm is not absorbing a new cost; it is declining to pay one incurred decades ago and parked on everyone downwind, downstream, and not yet born. The receipt has been in a drawer in southern West Virginia the whole time.

In 1960 a ton of coal sold at the mine mouth in West Virginia for just under five dollars. That was the cleared market price, and it was not the cost. Add up what that same ton actually imposed, in carbon, in poisoned water, in ruined lungs and land that will need treatment for centuries, and the honest figure comes to roughly five hundred and ten dollars a ton. Somebody paid the difference, and it was neither the buyer nor the seller. Who did is the question this country has never put on a ledger.

McDowell County, West Virginia, is one place that paid. Campaigning there in 1960, John F. Kennedy came away with a line he repeated that September in Canton, Ohio: McDowell County was mining more coal than it ever had in its history, and more people there were receiving surplus food packages than in any county in the United States. Both halves were true at once. The wealth was real, and it was leaving. A county of nearly a hundred thousand people then holds under twenty thousand now, by the Census count. McDowell did not fail to share in coal's prosperity. It financed that prosperity, at terms no one read aloud.

In *Hillbilly Elegy*, J.D. Vance read the region's decline as a failure of character. The record does not support him. Elizabeth Catte and the historian Ronald Eller have shown that the decline was structural: a region developed without being allowed to keep what its development produced. What that scholarship has lacked is the arithmetic. McDowell was running an account whose other column was never posted.

Look at the unposted column. The first entry is the lungs. Federal black-lung compensation has paid more than forty-four billion dollars through 2009, by the government's own accounting, and the floor has only risen since. The fund that pays it has borrowed from the treasury in nearly every year since 1979, and owed about five point one billion dollars as of September 2024. Those are different numbers: what damaged lungs have already cost, and what the country still owes because it never collected enough at the mine mouth to cover them.

The second entry is the land and the water. More than six hundred thousand acres across the coalfields are still waiting to be reclaimed, with a documented cleanup need near eight to ten billion dollars against posted bonds of less than four billion, a shortfall of several billion, as of 2021, by the count of Appalachian Voices. Some of it is acid mine drainage, the slow chemistry of exposed sulfides turning streams orange, a reaction that has to be treated more or less forever. A cost with no end date was never a one-time sale.

The third entry is the air, and it is the largest. Coal from McDowell's Pocahontas seam releases about 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide per short ton burned, by the EPA's emission factor and the seam's measured heat content. The agency's 2023 central estimate of the social cost of carbon is one hundred ninety dollars per ton of carbon dioxide. Multiply, and one ton of McDowell coal carried roughly four hundred ninety-six dollars in climate damage alone. And one hundred ninety dollars is the government's own mainstream figure, lower than several credible estimates. We are lowballing, and the carbon alone still runs close to five hundred dollars a ton.

A few things this argument is not. It does not say the coal should have stayed in the ground; cheap energy lifted billions out of poverty, and the point is only that the cost-bearers should have been paid out of the price. It does not say markets are a fiction; the priced sale cleared honestly, while a second, unpriced transaction ran alongside it, binding a future generation that did not yet exist and so could never have bargained at the table. And it is not a brief against poorer economies burning coal today, since the costs fall first on the places that took the benefit. Unlike a fishery worked within its limits, coal cannot be grown back; the loan against the future is repayable in cash, never in kind.

We know how to price this, because the country keeps relitigating it. Obama set the social cost of carbon near forty-two dollars; Trump cut it to between one and seven by counting only damages inside U.S. borders, which economists including Gernot Wagner, David Anthoff, and Maureen Cropper criticized in 2021 as a departure from standard practice; Biden restored the global scope, and the EPA raised it to one hundred ninety. The exact number is contested at the edges. That it belongs on the price is not.

The choice now is whether to keep deferring the bill or begin paying it down deliberately, by moving toward energy whose price carries its costs. McDowell County has held the receipt for two generations. Reading it aloud is the least we owe.

*Chris Winn is the author of* Commons Bonds, *a forthcoming book on the institutional architecture of cost and accountability. He spent his research career at the National Institutes of Health.*
