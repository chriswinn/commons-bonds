# What a Ton of Coal Actually Cost

<!-- structure-note: lede slot, peg-agnostic placeholder, ~85w, swap-ready at fire time per brief §1 trigger list (EPA SCC revision; coal-company bankruptcy with black-lung-liability transfer; cleanup announcement; mine closure or plant retirement; SCOTUS environmental decision; transition-implementation event). Every few years the country reopens an old argument about coal. The terms shift with the headline, but the question underneath does not change: what did the coal actually cost, and who paid it. The honest answer is not the one printed on the old invoices. It is much larger, and it has been coming due for sixty years in a place most of the argument never mentions.

Start with a number that looks small. In 1960, a ton of bituminous coal sold at the West Virginia mine mouth for $4.71, just under five dollars, according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration. That was the price the market cleared. It was also a price with most of the bill left off. Count the costs that the sale did not, and a single ton from a McDowell County seam runs to roughly $510, by an eight-component accounting that adds the climate damage, the ruined lungs, the poisoned water, and the rest. The gap between five dollars and five hundred did not vanish because no one wrote it down. Someone paid it. The question is who.

McDowell County is where you can read the receipt. In 1960, John F. Kennedy campaigned there, and that September, in Canton, Ohio, he named what he had seen: "McDowell County mines more coal than it ever has in its history, and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than any county in the United States." Record production and record hunger, in the same sentence, in the same county. The coal left. The prosperity it financed left with it. In 1950 the county held 98,887 people, nearly a hundred thousand. By 2020 it held 19,111, under twenty thousand. A place does not lose four out of five of its people because its people lost their character. It loses them because the thing they were paid to produce was priced as if it cost almost nothing, and the difference was charged to them.

This is where the easy story gets told, and it is the wrong one. J.D. Vance's *Hillbilly Elegy* read Appalachian decline as a failure of habit and culture, a region that fell because of how its people lived. The county's own ledger refutes that without raising its voice. McDowell did not fail to share in coal's prosperity. McDowell financed it, at terms no one ever read aloud. The historian Ronald Eller called the pattern development without prosperity, and McDowell is its sharpest instance: a county that produced more and kept less the harder it worked. What the culture reading mistakes for a flaw in the people is a flaw in the price.

Take the unpaid columns one at a time.

The lungs come first. Federal black lung benefits have paid more than $44 billion through 2009, by the Government Accountability Office's accounting, a conservative and honestly dated floor, because no authoritative cumulative total has been compiled since. The fund that pays those benefits has borrowed from the U.S. Treasury almost every year since 1979 and carried a debt of $5.1 billion as of September 2024, according to the Department of Labor. That is a cost the original price of coal did not carry. It was real the whole time. It simply landed on a different page, decades later, denominated in the breathing of men who are still alive.

The water comes next. More than 633,000 acres across the coalfields await reclamation, with a documented cleanup need of $7.5 to $9.8 billion against roughly $3.8 billion in posted bonds, leaving a gap of four to six billion dollars, as of 2021, by the accounting of the nonprofit Appalachian Voices. Some of that damage does not end. Acid mine drainage is a chemical reaction in exposed rock that runs for centuries and has to be treated more or less forever. The price of the coal did not include the treatment. It still does not.

The carbon comes last and largest. A short ton of Pocahontas-seam coal releases about 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide when burned, using the Environmental Protection Agency's emission factor and the published heat content of that seam. At $190 per ton, the EPA's 2023 central estimate of the social cost of carbon, that single column comes to $496 a ton. Add it to the others and you reach the $510 honest total. And $190 is the government's own mainstream figure, not the highest a credible economist would defend. We are lowballing, and the answer is still roughly five hundred dollars.

There is a deeper reason the price could never have caught all of this on its own. Coal is not a fishery harvested inside its own regeneration. It does not grow back. A ton burned is a ton gone, and the loan it represents against the future can be repaid only in cash, never in kind. The party who will pay the most, the generation downstream, was not at the table when the deal was struck, because it did not yet exist. No bargain between buyer and seller, however free, can price a cost that falls on someone who was never in the room. That is not a market failure in the usual sense. It is a market doing exactly what it was built to do, on a transaction whose largest party it had no way to seat.

Three objections answer themselves, and worth naming before they are raised.

This is not an argument that the coal should have stayed in the ground. Cheap energy lifted billions of people out of poverty, and that achievement is real. The narrow claim is only that the people and places that bore the costs should have been paid for bearing them, and were not.

It is not an argument that markets are a fiction. The priced transaction cleared honestly; the buyer and the seller got what they bargained for. A second transaction ran alongside it, unpriced, binding parties who never agreed to be bound, which is precisely why the market could not catch it.

And it is not an argument for pulling the ladder up behind the rich world. The costs of fossil energy come due primarily on the jurisdictions that burned the fuel and banked the proceeds. Telling a poorer country to skip what enriched you is a separate question. The bill discussed here is mostly addressed to the places that already collected.

The figure used above is itself a record of how political the accounting has been. The social cost of carbon was about $42 a ton under the Obama administration, on a global accounting. The Trump administration cut it to between $1 and $7 by counting only damages inside U.S. borders, a move that Gernot Wagner, David Anthoff, Maureen Cropper and colleagues criticized in 2021 as a departure from standard cost-benefit practice. The Biden administration restored the global scope at an interim $51, and the EPA raised it to $190 in 2023. The number moved by a factor of more than a hundred not because the physics changed but because the willingness to write the cost down did.

Which leaves a choice, and it is not a partisan one. Whoever creates a cost should bear it. That principle does not belong to the left or the right; it is the oldest rule of a fair dealing, the one a child uses about a broken window. The decision in front of the country is whether to keep deferring the bill, as it has for sixty years, mistaking the costs it has hidden for costs it has avoided, or to begin paying it down on purpose and to build the next energy system on a price that carries its own freight. A bargain is a price honestly low. What McDowell County got was a price falsely low, and the difference did not disappear. It is still being paid, in a place that produced more coal than it ever had in its history and got hunger in return.

*Chris Winn is the author of* Commons Bonds, *a forthcoming book on the institutional architecture of cost and accountability. He spent his research career at the National Institutes of Health.*
