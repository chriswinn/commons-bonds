# What a Ton of Coal Actually Cost

When a coal company files for bankruptcy and asks a court to release it from the black-lung benefits it once promised, the headline calls it a restructuring. It is closer to a receipt arriving generations late. Cheap energy was bought decades ago, and the gap between its price and its cost is still coming due. The court is being asked, again, whether that gap falls on the people who incurred it or on a public that never signed for it.

In 1960 a ton of coal sold at the mouth of a West Virginia mine for $4.71, just under five dollars, according to the federal Energy Information Administration. That was the price the market cleared. It was not the price the coal cost.

Tally what the ton actually required and you reach about $510. The largest single piece is carbon. Coal from McDowell County's Pocahontas seam releases roughly 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide per ton burned, a figure built from the EPA's emission factors and the seam's measured heat content. Multiplied by $190, the EPA's own central estimate of the social cost of a ton of carbon as of 2023, that is about $496 in climate damage alone. The rest sits in things the market never quoted: the lungs, the land, the water.

This is not a claim that the coal should have stayed in the ground; cheap energy lifted billions out of poverty, and McDowell's coal was part of that lift. The point is narrower. A price near five dollars and a cost near five hundred are not the same transaction, and the difference did not evaporate. It was deferred, and a deferred bill is always handed to someone else.

McDowell County was handed it. Campaigning there in 1960, John F. Kennedy told a crowd that September in Canton, Ohio, that McDowell mined more coal than it ever had in its history and that more people were receiving surplus food packages there than in any county in the United States. The county held 98,887 people then. It holds 19,111 now, and it did not shrink that far because its people lost their character.

That is where this enters a fight that has run for a decade. The popular reading, the one J.D. Vance made famous, treats Appalachian decline as a matter of habit and culture. The ledger says otherwise, and the regional historians have said so for years: Elizabeth Catte answered the stereotype directly, and Ronald Eller called the long pattern development without prosperity. McDowell did not fail to share in coal's boom. It financed the boom, at terms no one read aloud.

The 1960 price left three columns blank. The first is human. Black lung was finally compensated in 1969, after West Virginia miners struck for it. Federal payments to disabled miners and their survivors passed $44 billion through 2009, the last year for which a sober cumulative figure exists, according to federal auditors. The fund that pays them has borrowed from the Treasury almost every year since 1979 and stood $5.1 billion in debt as of September 2024. The disease is not history; it is resurgent, fastest among the youngest miners.

The second column is the land. More than 633,000 acres of Appalachia await reclamation, a documented cleanup need of $7.5 to $9.8 billion against roughly $3.8 billion in posted bonds, a gap of four to six billion dollars, as of 2021, by the accounting of Appalachian Voices. Abandoned mines drain acid into the streams through a reaction that runs for centuries on its own. The third column is the air, the $496 already counted.

Two objections deserve answers. This is no claim that markets are fake: the 1960 sale cleared honestly between willing buyer and willing seller. But a second transaction ran alongside the first, binding parties who were not at the table. The generation now paying for the carbon never sat in that room, because it did not yet exist, which is exactly why no bargain struck in 1960 could have priced it. Nor does this pull the ladder up behind developing economies; the costs come due first on the places that did the burning. And coal, unlike a fishery harvested inside its capacity to regrow, cannot be repaid in kind. A seam is drawn down once, so the loan against the future can be settled only in cash.

The country already knows how to set this price. The social cost of carbon has moved with the politics. One administration set it near $42; the next cut it to between one and seven dollars by counting only damage inside U.S. borders, which the economists Gernot Wagner, David Anthoff, and Maureen Cropper called a departure from standard practice; the next restored the global figure, and the EPA raised it to $190. That $190 is the cautious choice, not the highest credible one. We are lowballing, and the honest total is still about five hundred dollars.

The bill always existed, and we have been paying it sideways, in breath and water and weather, while telling ourselves the coal was cheap. The choice now is between deferring it again and paying it down deliberately, funding energy whose price carries its cost. Whoever creates a cost should bear it. That principle belongs to neither the left nor the right. It belongs to anyone who has read a bill and noticed who was asked to pay it.
