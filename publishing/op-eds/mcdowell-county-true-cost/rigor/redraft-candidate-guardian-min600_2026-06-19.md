This week the news returned, as it does every few years, to the price of coal. A bankruptcy, a regulatory revision, a plant scheduled to go dark: the form changes, the argument does not. One side calls the fuel cheap and says so was the prosperity it bought. The other calls it dirty and says the bill is finally due. Both are describing the same ton of coal. Neither is reading the whole receipt. The number that decides the argument was never printed on the invoice, and it has been accruing, quietly, for sixty years.

Here is the gap, stated plainly. A ton of bituminous coal sold at the West Virginia mine mouth in 1960 for $4.71, just under five dollars, according to the federal Energy Information Administration. The honest cost of that same ton, the priced part and the unpriced part together, was about $510. The transaction recorded one of those numbers. The question this piece asks is who paid the rest of the bill, the part that never appeared on the invoice, and whether anyone intends to keep deferring it.

Begin where the contradiction is sharpest. In May 1960, campaigning through the southern coalfields, John F. Kennedy saw the place his statistics had described. That September, in Canton, Ohio, he put it in a sentence that has not lost its edge: "McDowell County mines more coal than it ever has in its history, and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than any county in the United States." Record production and record hunger, the same county, the same year. A place can produce a fortune and not keep it. There is nothing paradoxical in it once you see the accounting that priced one side of the ledger and left the other side blank.

The blank side has names and a census. In 1950 McDowell County held 98,887 people, nearly a hundred thousand. By 2020 it held 19,111, under twenty thousand, according to the U.S. Census. J.D. Vance, in *Hillbilly Elegy*, read a decline like this as a story about character, a culture that had learned to depend rather than to work. The ledger refuses that reading. McDowell did not lose four out of every five people because its people stopped trying. The coal seams thinned, the work mechanized, the companies wound down across the 1980s, and the value that had been cut out of the ground had long since gone somewhere else. The historian Ronald Eller called this development without prosperity, the title of his 2008 study *Uneven Ground*. McDowell is its sharpest instance: a county that generated wealth on a national scale and was left, when the seams gave out, poorer than almost anywhere in the country.

Look closely at what stayed behind, because the unpriced costs are not abstractions. They are columns in a ledger anyone can read.

The first column is written in lungs. The federal black lung program has paid out more than $44 billion in benefits through 2009, by the accounting of the Government Accountability Office and the Congressional Research Service, and that figure is a floor, not a ceiling. The trust fund that pays those benefits has borrowed from the general treasury almost every year since 1979; its debt stood at $5.1 billion as of September 2024, a separate number from the benefits already paid, money the public has fronted because the price of the coal never covered the cost of the men who dug it. The disease was supposed to be receding. It is not. It is resurgent, and it is moving fastest through younger miners, men who came to the work after everyone agreed the danger had been solved.

The second column is written in land and water. More than 633,000 acres of mined Appalachian land were still awaiting reclamation as of 2021, by the count of the advocacy group Appalachian Voices in its report *Repairing the Damage*. The documented cost of cleaning it runs between $7.5 and $9.8 billion. The bonds the operators posted against that work total roughly $3.8 billion, leaving a shortfall of four to six billion dollars that someone will have to find or the ground will simply stay broken. And some of the damage does not end when the cleanup money runs out. Where mining exposes sulfide minerals, they oxidize and acidify the streams that drain the site, and they keep acidifying them. Acid mine drainage is treated for exactly as long as someone keeps paying to treat it, which is to say indefinitely, which is to say forever.

The third column is written in the atmosphere, and it is the one the original price most clearly ignored. Burning a ton of Pocahontas-seam coal, the high-carbon coal McDowell shipped, releases about 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide. That figure is not a guess; it is the Environmental Protection Agency's standard AP-42 emission factor applied to the measured heat content of that particular seam. Set it against the damage each ton of carbon dioxide does, which the EPA in 2023 put at a central estimate of $190, and one ton of McDowell coal carries a climate cost of about $496. That single component, the carbon alone, is about a hundred times the price the coal sold for in 1960. And $190 is a conservative figure. It is the government's own mainstream estimate, the figure the EPA settled on in 2023 after drawing on the work of Rennert and colleagues in *Nature* in 2022, and it sits well below the high end of the published range. Lowball the number deliberately, take the government's own conservative figure, and the carbon cost of a single ton is still close to five hundred dollars. Add the lungs, the broken land, the perpetual drainage, and the carbon, and you reach the honest price already named: about $510 a ton. The county got the five dollars. It is still paying the rest.

Three things this argument does not claim, because the costs come due first on whoever is least equipped to refuse them, and that makes precision a duty.

It is not a claim that the coal should have stayed in the ground. Cheap energy lifted billions of people out of poverty, and that is a real good, not a rhetorical concession. The narrow claim is that the people who absorbed the costs should have been paid out of the value they helped create, and were not.

It is not a claim that markets are a fiction. The priced transaction cleared exactly as advertised. The trouble is that a second transaction ran alongside the first, unpriced, binding parties who were never at the table. The largest of those parties is the generation now paying the carbon bill, which could not have bargained for a better price because it did not yet exist. No bargain, however efficient, can include a party who is not there to bargain.

And it is not a claim that today's developing economies should be denied cheap energy. The point is the reverse. The costs land first and heaviest on the places that already took the benefit, which is why pulling the ladder up now would be its own injustice. The point is not to deny anyone the climb. It is to stop pretending the climb was free.

There is one more thing coal is, that a fishery is not. A fishery harvested within its power to regenerate comes back; coal does not. The ton burned is gone, and the loan it represents against the future can be repaid only in money, never in kind.

A cost that is real but unpriced does not vanish. It defers, it compounds, and it lands in the end on whoever is least able to say no. That principle belongs to no party. Whoever creates a cost should be the one to bear it, and a conservative can hold that as easily as a liberal, because it is simply what a price is supposed to do. There are two choices left. Keep deferring the bill, as it has been deferred since before Kennedy stood in Canton, or begin paying it down on purpose, and price the next source of energy to carry its true weight before it is burned rather than after. McDowell County did not fail to share in coal's prosperity. McDowell County financed it, at terms no one ever read aloud.

*Chris Winn is the author of* Commons Bonds, *a forthcoming book on the institutional architecture of cost and accountability. He spent his research career at the National Institutes of Health.*
