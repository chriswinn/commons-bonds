This week's news out of coal country, whatever its particular shape, will be read mostly as a story about jobs and decline. It is also a story about a bill. For most of the twentieth century, the coalfields of southern West Virginia produced the cheap energy that ran American industry, and they did it at a price that left out most of what the coal actually cost. The accounting that governed the trade priced one side of the ledger and ignored the other. The other side did not disappear. It came due later, on people who never saw the invoice.

A ton of bituminous coal at the mine mouth in 1960 sold for $4.71, according to the Energy Information Administration, just under five dollars. The honest cost of that ton, once you add in everything the price left out, was somewhere near $510. McDowell County, West Virginia, where a great deal of that coal came out of the ground, got the five dollars. It has been paying the rest of the bill ever since, and so have the rest of us.

In May 1960, John F. Kennedy campaigned through the southern coalfields and saw what the trade looked like up close. That September, in Canton, Ohio, he put it into one sentence: "McDowell County mines more coal than it ever has in its history, and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than any county in the United States." Record production and record hunger, the same place, the same year. The coal was leaving at full volume. The value was leaving with it.

J.D. Vance, in *Hillbilly Elegy*, read Appalachian decline as a story of habit and character, a culture that had learned helplessness. The ledger tells a different story. McDowell County held 98,887 people in 1950, nearly a hundred thousand, by the federal census. In 2020 it held 19,111, under twenty thousand. A place does not shrink by four-fifths because its people grew soft. The historian Ronald Eller, in *Uneven Ground*, described an Appalachia where a century of development produced enormous wealth and almost no lasting prosperity, the wealth flowing out as fast as it was made. McDowell is the sharpest case he could have chosen. The county did not fail to share in coal's prosperity. It financed that prosperity, at terms no one ever read aloud.

Read the terms now, the parts the five dollars never covered.

There is the cost carried in the men's lungs. Federal black lung benefits have paid out more than $44 billion through 2009, by the accounting of the Government Accountability Office and the Congressional Research Service, and that is a conservative floor, not a ceiling, with no authoritative total since. The trust fund that pays those benefits has borrowed from the general treasury almost every year since 1979; the Department of Labor put its debt at $5.1 billion as of September 2024. And the disease is not behind us. It is resurgent, moving fastest through the youngest miners.

There is the cost left in the ground and the water. More than 633,000 acres of mined Appalachian land were awaiting reclamation as of 2021, by the count of the advocacy group Appalachian Voices in its report *Repairing the Damage*. The documented cleanup need runs $7.5 to $9.8 billion against roughly $3.8 billion in posted bonds, a shortfall of about four to six billion dollars that someone will have to cover. And the streams keep a longer ledger still. Where mining exposes sulfide minerals, they oxidize and acidify the water, and the acid runs for as long as anyone keeps treating it, which is to say indefinitely.

Then there is the carbon. Burning a ton of the Pocahontas-seam coal that McDowell produced releases about 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide, a figure that comes from applying the Environmental Protection Agency's AP-42 emission factor to the seam's measured heat content. Set that against the EPA's 2023 central estimate of the damage one ton of carbon dioxide does, $190, and the climate cost of a single ton of McDowell coal is about $496. That one component, carbon alone, is about a hundred times what the coal sold for in 1960. And $190 is not the high end of the literature; it is the government's own mainstream figure, roughly what Rennert and colleagues arrived at in *Nature* in 2022, which the EPA drew on. Lowball the number deliberately and the climate cost is still close to five hundred dollars a ton. Add the lungs, the abandoned land, the perpetual drainage, and the carbon, and you reach the honest price already named, about $510. The county got the five dollars and is still paying the rest.

Three things this is not.

It is not an argument that the coal should have stayed in the ground. Cheap energy lifted billions of people out of poverty, which is a real and enormous good. The narrow claim is only that the people who bore the costs should have been paid out of the value they helped create, and they were not.

It is not a claim that markets are a fiction. The priced transaction cleared exactly as advertised. The trouble is that a second transaction ran alongside the first one, unpriced, binding parties who were never at the table. The largest of those parties is the generation now paying the carbon bill, which could not have bargained for a better price because it did not yet exist. No deal, however efficient, can include a party who is not there to make it.

And it is not a claim that today's poorer economies should be denied the cheap energy that lifted the rich ones. The costs come due first and heaviest on the places that already took the benefit. The point is not to pull the ladder up. It is to stop pretending the climb was free.

One more distinction is worth marking. A fishery, harvested within its power to renew, regrows. Coal does not. What it represents is a loan against the future that can be repaid only in money, never in kind.

None of this belongs to one party. A cost that is real but unpriced does not go away. It defers, it compounds, and it lands on whoever is least able to refuse it. Whoever creates a cost should be the one to bear it, and that principle is not the property of the left or the right. The choice left to us is simple to state and hard to make: keep deferring the bill, or begin paying it down on purpose, and price the next source of energy to carry its true weight before it is burned rather than after. McDowell already paid for the lesson. The least the rest of us can do is read the receipt.

*Chris Winn is the author of* Commons Bonds, *a forthcoming book on the institutional architecture of cost and accountability. He spent his research career at the National Institutes of Health.*
