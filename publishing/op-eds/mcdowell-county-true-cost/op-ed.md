Every few years, an argument resurfaces in U.S. policy about what energy actually costs. It tends to arrive when an agency revises a number, a coal company files for bankruptcy, or an Appalachian community lands in headlines for some reason it would rather not. The argument is older than the news. The United States has been postponing it for sixty years, since the year a single Appalachian county led the nation in coal production while its residents queued for federal surplus food. The accounting that should have happened then did not. It is still owed.

When energy looks cheap, the question to ask is who is paying the rest of the bill. A ton of Appalachian coal sold at the mine mouth in 1960 for roughly $4.50. The full cost of producing that ton — once health damage, environmental destruction, community collapse, and atmospheric carbon are priced honestly — was roughly $558. The gap between those two figures is not an accounting curiosity. It was paid, in lungs and watersheds and county populations and an atmosphere that does not send invoices, by people and ecosystems and futures that were not consulted about the transaction.

McDowell County, West Virginia, is where the gap is most visible. McDowell peaked at roughly 100,000 residents in 1950. When President Kennedy campaigned through the West Virginia primary in 1960, McDowell led the nation in coal production and its residents queued for federal surplus food. Mechanization cut the mining workforce by more than half within a decade. When U.S. Steel closed its McDowell mine in 1990, personal income across the county fell by two-thirds in a single year. By 2020, fewer than 20,000 residents remained. In 2022, McDowell ranked sixteenth-poorest in the United States, with median household income near $28,000 against a national figure above $67,000 and a poverty rate near thirty-eight percent. In 2015, the county recorded the highest drug-induced death rate of any U.S. county: 141 per 100,000, nearly ten times the national rate.

Begin with what was left blank. The Black Lung Disability Trust Fund, the federal program that pays disabled miners and their widows, has distributed more than $44 billion since Congress established it in 1969. It has never collected enough revenue from active producers to meet its obligations. The fund's debt stood at $4.5 billion in 2021 and is projected to exceed $15 billion by 2050. When a coal-producing company files for bankruptcy, its unpaid black-lung obligations transfer onto the public balance sheet, onto taxpayers who never received any of the coal revenue. Black lung was identifiable as an occupational disease early in the twentieth century; federal compensation came only in 1969, after a wildcat strike led by the West Virginia Black Lung Association forced the issue. The United Mine Workers under John L. Lewis had declined to press it earlier.

The arithmetic continues at the surface. The 1977 Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act required coal operators to post cleanup bonds. Across Appalachia, roughly 633,000 acres now await reclamation, with posted bonds totaling about $3.8 billion against documented cleanup needs of $7.5 to $9.8 billion. The shortfall, between $3.5 and $6 billion, sits unfunded in the federal ledger. Acid mine drainage compounds the problem: sulfide minerals exposed by mining keep reacting with rainwater and air for centuries, releasing heavy metals into watersheds downstream. Where cleanup happens at all, it is a perpetual public obligation, paid long after the producing company has dissolved.

Then add the carbon. Burning one ton of coal puts roughly 2.86 tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. The Environmental Protection Agency's most recent estimate of what each ton of CO₂ costs the world, the social cost of carbon, is $190, anchored to the Rennert et al. 2022 *Nature* meta-analysis and adopted in the EPA's 2023 update. The implied climate damage from a single ton of coal at that figure is about $544. That number alone exceeds the inflation-adjusted price coal has commanded for most of the industry's history.

Three responses need addressing before anyone draws the wrong conclusion. The first is that this is an argument against the extraction having happened. It isn't: inexpensive energy is what allowed billions of people to escape poverty in the twentieth century, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. The argument here is narrower: the people who bore the costs of producing that energy should have been compensated at rates reflecting what they actually sacrificed. The second is that this is an argument that markets are "fake." They aren't. The transactions cleared, the coal moved, the money changed accounts. What the market measured was the part of the trade it could see; a second, unmeasured trade ran in parallel, binding on parties who never sat at the table. The third is that honest pricing is a way for wealthy nations to pull the cheap-energy ladder up behind developing economies. The argument runs the other way. Industrialized economies built much of their twentieth-century wealth by routing extraction costs onto a global atmosphere and biosphere that could not invoice them; those costs come due primarily on the jurisdictions that benefited.

<!-- CALL: what should change -->

The policy question now is whether to keep treating these numbers as theoretical. The social cost of carbon has been revised four times by four administrations on grounds that have less to do with carbon physics than with which side of the political fight had more resources at the moment. The Obama-era figure of $42 per ton was cut to $1–7 under the Trump administration by restricting the externality calculation to U.S. borders — a methodological change Wagner, Anthoff, Cropper, and colleagues flagged in 2021 as ungrounded in standard cost-benefit practice. The Biden administration restored the global-scope methodology; the EPA's 2023 update raised the figure to $190. The Black Lung Trust Fund's structural deficit will not close itself. The reclamation shortfall will not fill in. The communities that bore the cost the first time are still bearing it. McDowell County is one. There are hundreds of others. The bill is not new. The decision is whether to keep deferring it or to begin paying it down deliberately, in a way that funds the transition to energy whose costs the price actually carries.
