The argument over what energy "really costs" tends to surface in policy debates whenever an agency revises a number, a company files for bankruptcy, or a coal-region community appears in the headlines for some reason it would prefer not to. The argument is older than the news. It is the argument the United States has been postponing for sixty years, since the era when a single Appalachian county was simultaneously the country's largest coal producer and one of the poorest places in the nation to live. The accounting that should have happened at that point did not. It is still owed.

When energy looks cheap, the question to ask is who is paying the rest of the bill. The price of a ton of Appalachian coal at the mine mouth in 1960 was about $4.50. The full cost of producing that ton — once health damage, environmental destruction, community collapse, and atmospheric carbon are priced honestly — was roughly $558. The gap between those two figures is not an accounting curiosity. It was paid, in lungs and watersheds and county populations and an atmosphere that does not send invoices, by people and ecosystems and futures that were not consulted about the transaction.

The county where this is most visible is McDowell County, West Virginia. McDowell peaked at roughly 100,000 residents in 1950. By 2020, fewer than 20,000 remained. The U.S. Steel mine closure in 1990 cut personal income in the county by two-thirds in a single year. The last Walmart left in 2016. By 2022, the county ranked sixteenth-poorest in the United States, with a median household income near $28,000 against a national figure above $67,000, and a poverty rate of roughly thirty-eight percent. In 2015, McDowell recorded the highest drug-induced death rate of any county in America — 141 per 100,000, close to ten times the national rate. None of these numbers were inevitable. Each was the downstream product of an accounting system that priced one side of the ledger and left the other side blank.

Walk through what was left blank. The Black Lung Disability Trust Fund — the federal program that pays disabled miners and their widows — has distributed more than $44 billion since its creation. It has never collected enough revenue to meet its obligations. The fund's debt stood at $4.5 billion in 2021 and is projected to exceed $15 billion by 2050. Bankruptcy by a coal-producing company moves the unpaid black-lung obligations onto the public balance sheet — which is to say, onto everyone who isn't currently extracting coal. That cost was real all along. It just wasn't on the invoice that cleared at the mine mouth.

Walk outside the mine and the same arithmetic continues. Across Appalachia, roughly 633,000 acres are awaiting reclamation under the 1977 Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act. The reclamation bonds the law required coal companies to post total roughly $3.8 billion against documented cleanup needs of $7.5 to $9.8 billion — a shortfall of three and a half to six billion dollars sitting unfunded in the federal ledger. The acid drainage from abandoned mines is a chemistry problem that does not stop when extraction stops: sulfide minerals exposed by mining keep reacting with rainwater and air for centuries, generating acid that releases heavy metals into the watershed downstream. The cleanup, where it occurs, is not a one-time remediation but a perpetual treatment obligation, paid out of public funds long after the producing company has dissolved.

Then add the carbon. Burning a single ton of coal puts roughly 2.86 tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. The Environmental Protection Agency's most recent estimate of what each ton of CO₂ costs the world — what economists call the social cost of carbon, anchored to a 2022 *Nature* meta-analysis and adopted in the 2023 EPA update — is $190. The implied climate damage from a single ton of coal, at that figure, is approximately $544. That number alone exceeds the inflation-adjusted price coal has commanded for most of the industry's history.

Three responses have to be addressed before anyone draws the wrong conclusion. The first is that this is an argument against the extraction having happened. It isn't. Inexpensive energy is what allowed billions of people to escape poverty in the twentieth century, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. The argument here is narrower: the people who bore the costs of producing that energy should have been compensated at rates reflecting what they actually sacrificed. The second is that this is an argument that markets are "fake." They aren't. The transactions cleared. The coal moved. The money changed accounts. What the market measured was the part of the trade it could see; a second, unmeasured trade was running in parallel, binding on parties who never sat at the table. The third is that honest pricing is a way for wealthy nations to deny developing economies the cheap-energy ladder that wealthy nations once climbed. It would be, if applied that way. The honest-pricing argument runs in the opposite direction: industrialized economies built much of their twentieth-century wealth by routing extraction costs onto a global atmosphere and biosphere that could not invoice them, and the present moment is when those costs come due — primarily as obligations of the jurisdictions that benefited from the routing, not as new burdens on jurisdictions still industrializing.

<!-- CALL: what should change -->

The policy question facing the United States now is whether to continue treating these numbers as theoretical. The social cost of carbon has been revised four times by four administrations on grounds that have less to do with carbon physics than with which side of the political fight had more resources at the moment of revision. The Black Lung Trust Fund's structural deficit will not close itself. The reclamation shortfall will not fill in. The communities that bore the cost the first time are still bearing it. McDowell County is one of these communities. There are hundreds of others. The bill is not new. The decision is whether to keep deferring it, or to begin paying it down deliberately, in a way that funds the transition to energy whose costs the price actually carries.
