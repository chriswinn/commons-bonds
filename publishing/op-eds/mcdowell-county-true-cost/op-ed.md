This week the cost of burning coal is back in the news, the way it returns every few years when a court, a regulator, or a bankruptcy court reopens the question of who owes what to whom. The argument always runs the same way. One side says the price of energy is too high; the other says it is too low. Both sides are debating the wrong number. The price of coal was never too high or too low. It was simply incomplete, and it has been incomplete for as long as anyone has been keeping the books.

Here is the gap. A ton of bituminous coal sold for about $4.71 at the West Virginia mine mouth in 1960, just under five dollars, according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration. The honest cost of that same ton, once you add up everything the burning of it set in motion, was closer to $510. The transaction recorded one of those numbers and not the other. The question worth asking is not why coal was cheap. It is who paid the rest of the bill, the part that never appeared on the invoice.

You can read the answer in one county. In September 1960, campaigning in Canton, Ohio, John F. Kennedy described what he had seen that spring in the southern West Virginia coalfields. "McDowell County mines more coal than it ever has in its history," he said, "and there are more people getting surplus food packages in McDowell County than any county in the United States." Record production and record hunger, in the same place, in the same year. The coal was leaving. Whatever it was worth was leaving with it.

What stayed was the bill. McDowell County held 98,887 people in 1950, nearly a hundred thousand, by the U.S. Census. By 2020 it held 19,111, under twenty thousand. A place does not shrink by four-fifths because its people grew soft. That is not the arithmetic of a place that failed to share in the country's prosperity. It is the arithmetic of a place that financed it. The reading that has sold best lately, J.D. Vance's *Hillbilly Elegy*, attributes Appalachian decline to a culture of dependency, to character and habit. Ronald Eller, in *Uneven Ground: Appalachia Since 1945*, called this development that produced wealth without prosperity, and named McDowell its sharpest case. You do not refute Vance by calling him names. You refute him by adding up the receipts.

So add them up. Start with the men. Federal black-lung benefits paid out more than $44 billion through 2009, by the accounting of the Government Accountability Office and the Congressional Research Service, and that is a conservative floor, not a ceiling, because no authoritative total since exists. The trust fund that pays those benefits has borrowed from the general treasury almost every year since 1979; its debt stood at $5.1 billion as of September 2024, by the Department of Labor's own figures, a separate number from the benefits paid and worth keeping separate. This money is denominated in lungs, in men who went down and came up coughing, and the disease is resurgent now, fastest among the youngest miners, too young to have been there for the worst of the dust.

Next, the land. More than 633,000 acres of mined Appalachian land were awaiting reclamation as of 2021, by the count of Appalachian Voices in its report *Repairing the Damage*. The documented cost of cleaning it runs $7.5 to $9.8 billion. The bonds the operators posted against that cost total about $3.8 billion. The shortfall, somewhere between four and six billion dollars, is a bill addressed to no one in particular, which in practice means it is addressed to the public. And the streams keep the longest account of all. Where mining exposed sulfide minerals, those minerals oxidize and acidify the water, and they will go on doing it for as long as anyone keeps treating it, which is to say forever.

Then the air. Burning a ton of Pocahontas-seam coal releases about 2.61 tons of carbon dioxide, using the Environmental Protection Agency's AP-42 emission factor applied to that seam's measured heat content. Against the EPA's 2023 central estimate of the damage carbon does, $190 a ton, the climate cost of one ton of McDowell coal comes to about $496. Read on the carbon lens alone, against the price the coal actually sold for in 1960, that single cost is about a hundred times what the county got for the coal. And $190 is a conservative figure, not the high end of the literature but the government's own central estimate, roughly what Rennert and colleagues reached in *Nature* in 2022, which the EPA drew on, and the kind of number Wagner, Anthoff and Cropper have defended as ordinary cost-benefit practice. Lowball it deliberately and it still lands near five hundred dollars a ton. Set the four heads side by side, the ruined lungs, the abandoned land, the perpetual drainage, the carbon, and the honest price is the $510 already named. The county got the five dollars. It is still paying the rest.

Three things this is not.

It is not a claim that the coal should have stayed in the ground. Cheap energy lifted billions of people out of poverty, and that is a real good, not a rhetorical concession. The narrow claim is only this: the people who bore the costs should have been compensated out of the value they helped create, and they were not.

It is not a claim that markets are a fiction. The priced transaction cleared exactly as advertised. The trouble is that a second transaction ran alongside it, unpriced, binding parties who never sat at the table. The largest of those parties is the generation now paying the carbon bill, which could not have bargained for a better price for the simple reason that it did not yet exist. No deal, however efficient, can include a party who is not there to make it.

And it is not a claim that today's developing economies should be denied cheap energy. The costs of fossil fuel come due first and heaviest on the places that already took the benefit. The point is not to pull the ladder up behind us. The point is to stop pretending the ladder was free.

There is one more thing the ton of coal does not share with, say, a fishery worked within its power to regenerate. The fishery comes back. The coal does not, and the loan it represents against the future can be repaid only in money, never in kind.

A cost that is real but unpriced does not disappear when you decline to write it down. It defers, and it compounds, and it lands in the end on whoever is least able to refuse it. McDowell County was least able to refuse it. The principle that should have governed the price was never a politics: whoever creates a cost should be the one to bear it. A conservative can hold that as easily as a liberal, because it is simply honest accounting and balanced books. A country can do one of two things with a bill like this. It can keep deferring it, the path of least resistance and the one we are on. Or it can begin paying it down on purpose, and price the next source of energy to carry its true weight before it is burned rather than after. McDowell County did not fail to share in coal's prosperity. McDowell County financed it, at terms no one ever read aloud.
