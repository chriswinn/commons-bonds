**Subject:** Query: Commons Bonds — the $5 ton of coal that cost $510

*(Alternate subject: "Query: Commons Bonds — pricing what extraction severs")*

[¶1 — PERSONALIZATION SLOT, 2–3 sentences, per `personalization-snippets.md`. Genuine tie only; otherwise the plain public reason.]

In 1960, a ton of McDowell County coal sold at the mine mouth for just under five dollars, and the price was honest about everything except what it left out. Walked through every documented cost at the low end of every range — the lungs, the water, the emptied county, the carbon — the floor is roughly $510 a ton. The line for the difference has been blank for a century, because no one was made to write the number down.

*Commons Bonds* (complete at roughly 90,000 words, plus a technical appendix) names the mechanism that keeps that line blank — *cost severance*: value drawn from a shared resource while the cost is detached from whoever benefited and fastened onto whoever could not refuse — and builds the apparatus that prices it, *Residual Commons Value*, with two bond instruments that would close the gap using no institution that does not already exist. The cases run from Appalachian coal and the Chesapeake oyster fishery to Norway's $2 trillion fund, the Gulf after Deepwater Horizon, the 2008 foreclosures, and a Mars habitat built to test whether the mechanism is universal. It is written from inside the pattern, not above it.

Readers of Mariana Mazzucato's *The Value of Everything* and Katharina Pistor's *The Code of Capital* will recognize the territory: Mazzucato named the extraction, Pistor showed how law encodes it, and this book prices it. It arrives as the EU's carbon border mechanism goes fully operational, reparations commissions move from argument to design, and the cost-of-living crisis has made "what things actually cost" a kitchen-table question.

I am a former healthcare-IT executive — the National Institutes of Health during the Human Genome Project, then a two-decade consulting career that opened through World Bank and IMF engagements and ended in a CEO seat — now a nursing student at Virginia Peninsula Community College, living aboard a sailboat in Hampton, Virginia. The framework was built after the costs the book describes landed on me. My essay "The $100 Barrel" is under consideration at *Phenomenal World*, with a finished portfolio of derivative essays queued across venues from Aeon and Noema to *Foreign Affairs*.

The full proposal and two sample chapters are ready to send. May I send the proposal?

Chris Winn · [contact] · [single relevant link]
