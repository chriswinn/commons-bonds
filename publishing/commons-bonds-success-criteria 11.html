Commons Bonds — Success Criteria and Project Goals

Planning document · Session: April 19, 2026

Purpose

This document captures the explicit success criteria for the Commons Bonds project as clarified during the April 19 session. It exists to provide a reference point for future decisions about scope, ambition, and trade-offs. When the question arises "is this worth doing / is this worth adding / am I trying to do too much," this document answers it.

What the Project Can Reasonably Accomplish

Most Likely Outcomes (Realistic)

Vocabulary entering discourse. A small but meaningful number of readers adopt the framework's terms. A labor lawyer uses "severed cost" in a brief. A community organizer uses "residual commons value" in negotiation. A journalist uses "intergenerational pricing gap" as a headline concept. A graduate student builds on the framework in a dissertation. This is the most likely outcome and the primary measure of success.

Individual decision-tool applicability. A coal miner, a nurse, a gig worker, or an IT consultant reads the book and has a moment of recognition that changes how they understand their own situation. They do not quit or revolt. They renegotiate a contract with a clearer question in their head: am I being asked to donate unpriced tiers? That internal reframing is what books actually do — they change how people understand what is happening to them.

Community negotiating framework. A community negotiating an extraction agreement uses the framework to ask for specific tier-priced protections that would not have been on the table otherwise. Not all will win. Some will. Partial wins that would not have happened without the framework are real outcomes.

Next-generation scholarship. Someone reading Ostrom in 2030 also reads Commons Bonds and builds something that integrates both — sustainable commons governance plus honest pricing of severance costs. This is the academic continuation of the framework.

Possible But Less Likely Outcomes

Specific policy adoption. The Community Transition Reserve or ring-fenced intergenerational funds with legibility requirements get adopted somewhere. A state, a tribal nation, a small country. Not as comprehensive solution but as demonstration case.

Space law incorporation. Space law frameworks being drafted for asteroid mining and lunar resources incorporate tier-priced accountability because someone in the drafting process read the asteroid miner chapter. The long-horizon possibility.

Unrealistic Outcomes (Not Goals)

  • Personal financial independence
  • Systemic replacement of capitalism
  • Academic prizes as measure of success
  • Immediate policy change

Stated Goals and Alignment

Goal 1: Future generations might be compensated more fairly

The book's realistic contribution: vocabulary and visibility. Compensation requires institutional infrastructure the book cannot build alone. But institutional infrastructure does not get built until the thing it protects is nameable. This contribution is real and upstream of the outcome. Whether future generations are actually compensated more fairly depends on what happens downstream in the hands of readers.

Alignment: The book can contribute to this goal but cannot accomplish it alone. Your contribution is upstream. That is enough.

Goal 2: People might accept a job or project with open eyes and honest accounting

The book can accomplish this more directly. It happens reader by reader, individually, at the scale of personal lives rather than through institutions. A reader applies the eight-tier framework to their own situation and makes a different decision — or the same decision with clearer understanding of what they are trading.

Alignment: This is the goal the book is best equipped to serve directly. The IT stories and worked individual examples are the key vehicles.

The Measure of Success, Stated as a Single Sentence

If, ten years from now, a labor lawyer uses "severed cost" in a brief and the judge does not need it explained — and the author whose name is on the book never knows this happened — the book succeeded.

That is the appropriate benchmark. Not royalties. Not prizes. Not revolution. Vocabulary used by people solving real problems, without attribution, because the vocabulary is useful.

What the Book Is, Stated Cleanly

A framework for making visible the costs that every extraction transaction in human history has excluded from its price, told through human stories and validated by formal mathematics, written so that the words enter the discourse and the reader cannot unsee what the book shows them.

That is enough. It is more than most books do. And it aligns with the stated goals without trying to do more than one book can do.

Decision Heuristic for Future Scope Questions

When considering whether to add something to the book, ask:

  1. Does this sharpen the framework or expand its scope? Sharpening is usually good; expanding risks dilution.
  2. Does this help a reader recognize the mechanism in their own situation? If yes, prioritize. If no, question.
  3. Does this add vocabulary the discourse needs, or duplicate vocabulary already established? Only novel terms that do real work should be added.
  4. Does this require Book One to make claims that would be stronger if held for Book Two? Hold them.
  5. Does this increase the book's legal or political exposure without proportionate increase in its utility? Reconsider.

When in doubt, trim. Book One's job is to make the invisible visible and hand the vocabulary to the discourse. Anything beyond that job risks weakening the book's ability to do its actual job.
