Commons Bonds — Vocabulary Additions

Proposed additions to the existing Vocabulary Glossary · Session: April 19, 2026

Purpose

The existing Vocabulary Glossary contains 15 terms: Commons Bonds, Commons Resource, Value Capture, Cost Severance, Severed Cost, Spatial Cost Severance, Temporal Cost Severance, Accountability Bond (B), Residual Commons Value (RCV), Foreclosure Cost, Externality Tail, Substitutability Function, Intergenerational Pricing Gap (IPG), Civilizational Substitutability Gap (CSG), Asymmetric Regret Principle, and Universality Test.

The additions below supplement (do not replace) the existing vocabulary. Each term does work that the existing vocabulary does not, and each was stress-tested during the April 19 session for precision, distinctiveness, and staying power.

Proposed New Terms

Full Generational Cost (FGC)
The complete price of an extraction transaction when all tiers that have been systematically excluded are accounted for. FGC is the decomposed form of RCV — the same total, broken into eight named components (Lifetime Survival, Individual Risk, Mission Failure Reserve, Dynastic Labor, Foreclosure, Community Transition Reserve, Ecological Carrying, Political Capture). Used when operational legibility matters more than mathematical compactness. Every extraction in human history has been conducted at a price below its FGC.
Horizon Cost
Accessible-register companion term for Full Generational Cost. Used in prose where FGC would be too technical. "The horizon cost of extraction" means the same as "the full generational cost of extraction." Two terms, one concept, different reading registers.
Dynastic Labor Cost
The cost imposed on a worker's lineage when their productive capacity is extracted below Full Generational Cost. Parallels the way capital owners understand their transactions as affecting their lineages. Refuses the asymmetry in which capital compounds across generations but labor does not. The Social Security $108 trillion figure is Dynastic Labor Cost made quantitative across 88 years of American workers.
Community Transition Reserve
A pre-committed, independently held fund sized to finance the complete economic transition of any community whose existence became structurally dependent on an extraction operation. Calculated at inception of extraction, held outside the control of the extracting entity, required to fund full transition when extraction concludes. Distinct from decommissioning funds (which pay for physical cleanup only) and severance packages (which pay individual workers only). The most policy-actionable concept in the framework — can be added to extraction contracts directly without requiring the rest of the framework to be accepted first.
Political Capture Cost
The cost imposed on democratic and institutional systems by extraction industries that use a fraction of their captured value to suppress honest pricing of their own costs. Includes lobbying expenditures, regulatory capture, suppression of safety research, narrative capture through funded media, and broader institutional corrosion. Both a consequence of cost severance and a cause of its persistence. Explains why the other tiers have remained invisible: the parties benefiting from their invisibility have invested in keeping them invisible.
Abundance Inversion Test
Methodological principle used to identify hidden tiers. To find a cost that has been invisible on Earth, strip a dimension of abundance (atmospheric, geographic, temporal, institutional, demographic, ecological, or political) and ask what becomes visible. Costs that become visible when a specific abundance is stripped and disappear when it is restored are tiers. Costs visible regardless of abundance dimension are already priced. Costs that do not become visible under any stripping are not tiers. Prevents tier proliferation by requiring each proposed tier to survive a falsifiable test.
Abundance Dimension
A dimension along which the real cost of extraction is hidden from market pricing on Earth. Seven have been identified: atmospheric (free oxygen), geographic (distance between extractor and affected community), temporal (slow extraction rates relative to regeneration), institutional (enforcement infrastructure), demographic (knowledge-bearing community size), ecological (free ecosystem services), and political (democratic infrastructure that would permit honest accounting). The asteroid miner scenario strips all seven simultaneously, which is why it is clarifying.

Terms That May Graduate to Formal Status

The following terms emerged during the April 19 session but were judged provisional. They should be tracked for possible addition if future work validates them:

Ecological Carrying Cost
The cost of extraction processes on surrounding ecology, priced across the full recovery timeline. Distinct from Foreclosure Cost (the absent resource) and from standard externality pricing (which typically underprices recovery timelines). Currently treated as prose rather than formal glossary term pending further development.
Knowledge and Culture Cost
The cost of accumulated community knowledge, skill, and cultural continuity lost when an extraction-dependent community disperses below the threshold needed to maintain collective expertise. Currently treated as prose rather than formal glossary term.
Temporal Displacement Cost
The premium that should be charged for the rate of extraction rather than only the fact of it. A coal seam extracted over 500 years has a different temporal displacement cost than the same seam extracted over 50 years. Currently treated as prose pending mathematical formalization.
Mission Failure Reserve
The cost of the operation not returning expected value, borne by workers and communities who planned around it. Sub-tier within Individual Risk Cost. May warrant formal status in future editions.

Integration Notes

The additions above bring the total vocabulary to approximately 21 terms, which is still a manageable set. The critical constraint on glossary growth is whether each new term does work no existing term does. Terms that duplicate existing meaning (even with different wording) should be rejected. Terms that describe genuinely distinct concepts should be added. The Abundance Inversion Test serves as a methodology; the tiers it generates can be added to the glossary as they are validated by repeated application across scenarios.
