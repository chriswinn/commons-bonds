Commons Bonds

Canonical Vocabulary Glossary — v10 Eight-Tier Decomposition

By Chris Winn

Updated April 20, 2026 · Tier 2a renamed: "Individual Risk Cost" → "Actuarial Risk Cost" · Canonical eight-tier list per Decision A (April 19, 2026)

Core Framework Terms

Commons Bonds
The obligations owed to shared resources and the communities that steward them. Also refers to the financial instruments that could enforce these obligations. The title's double meaning: obligations (commons bonds) becoming shared identity (common bonds).
Cost Severance
The act of cutting the cost of extraction loose from the profit and transferring it to people who had no say. The central mechanism operating across all economic systems. Measured as CS = RCV − B. Cost severance requires distance between value-capturer and cost-bearer — geographic, temporal, or social. When that distance collapses to zero, cost severance approaches zero automatically.
Value Capture
The taking of value from a commons resource. Paired with cost severance in every extraction event. The profit side of the transaction. "Value Capture" — not "Value Extraction" — because physical extraction is a distinct operation; this term names the economic appropriation.
Severed Cost
The cost communities carry when extractors keep the profit and leave the bill. The result of successful cost severance.
Spatial Cost Severance
Costs concentrate where extraction happens; value disperses to distant consumers. Geographic dimension of cost severance. The 13-year life expectancy gap between McDowell County and the national average is the human face of spatial cost severance: coal's value dispersed nationally while health costs and community collapse concentrated locally.
Temporal Cost Severance
Present extractors take the value; future generations inherit the cost. Intergenerational dimension of cost severance.

Methodology Terms

Abundance Inversion Test
The framework's primary discovery methodology. To identify hidden cost tiers in any extraction transaction, systematically strip away abundance dimensions and observe which costs become visible at each dimension. Costs that become visible when a specific abundance dimension is stripped — and invisible when it is restored — are tiers. Costs visible regardless of abundance dimension are already priced by existing frameworks. Costs that do not become visible under any stripping are not tiers. The test prevents tier proliferation by requiring each proposed tier to survive a falsifiable test. Formal shorthand: "Abundance Test" is acceptable in prose; "Abundance Inversion Test" is the canonical name.
Abundance Masking
The mechanism by which abundance makes costs invisible until scarcity forces them into view. Oxygen feels free because Earth's atmosphere is abundant. Ecological services feel free because ecosystems haven't collapsed yet. Abundance Masking is the mechanism; Abundance Dimension is the dimension along which it operates. These are distinct entries: masking is a process, dimension is a category.
Abundance Dimension
A dimension along which the real cost of extraction is hidden from market pricing on Earth. Seven have been identified: atmospheric (free oxygen, climate regulation, radiation shielding), geographic (distance between extractor and affected community), temporal (slow extraction rates relative to regeneration), institutional (enforcement and accountability infrastructure), demographic (knowledge-bearing community size above critical thresholds), ecological (free ecosystem services: pollination, hydrology, soil formation), political (democratic infrastructure that would permit honest accounting). The asteroid miner scenario strips all seven simultaneously, which is why it is clarifying rather than exotic.
Universality Test
Run the model against extraction anywhere — Mars, asteroids, Earth — and it must produce the answer a thoughtful administrator would recognize as correct. Validates framework across all scenarios including boundary conditions (Europa) where the model correctly reports its own limits. Complements the Abundance Inversion Test: the Abundance Test is the discovery methodology; the Universality Test is the validation methodology.
Full Generational Cost (FGC)
The complete price of an extraction transaction when all tiers that have been systematically excluded are accounted for. FGC is the decomposed form of RCV — the same total, broken into eight named components (see Eight-Tier Decomposition below). Every extraction event in human history has been conducted at a price below its FGC. Used when operational legibility matters more than mathematical compactness: a coal miner, nurse, or rare-earth worker can identify their own situation in each tier.

Mathematical and Measurement Terms

Residual Commons Value (RCV)
The true intergenerational cost of extraction: foreclosure cost plus externality tail, weighted by substitutability and discounted across time. The price that includes what markets structurally exclude. Formally: RCV(R, t₀) = ∫[t₀ to ∞] { [1 − S(t|t₀)] · U(R, t, Q(t)) + E(R, t) } · D(t, t₀) dt.
Accountability Bond (B)
Every mechanism that forces extractors to bear costs: reclamation bonds, sovereign wealth funds, carbon taxes, insurance requirements. The formal quantity that should equal RCV for honest accounting. Under current extraction regimes, B reflects only identified, legally-enforceable costs; RCV includes intergenerational foreclosure costs that no existing B mechanism prices.
Foreclosure Cost
What future generations lose from not having the resource, weighted by substitutability probability. Zero if perfect substitutes exist; maximum when no alternatives are possible. The core RCV term: [1 − S(t)] · U(R, t, Q(t)), integrated to infinity. Markets structurally cannot price foreclosure costs because future generations cannot bid in present markets.
Externality Tail
Damage from extraction that persists after the resource is gone. A substitute for coal doesn't clean up the mine. A substitute for oil doesn't remove carbon from the atmosphere. The externality tail runs on its own clock, independent of substitution status.
Substitutability Function
The probability that a functionally adequate substitute exists at any given future time. Determines whether foreclosure cost approaches zero (high substitutability) or remains high (low substitutability). S(t) may be influenced by extraction timing — earlier extraction can slow innovation by reducing economic pressure to develop alternatives.
Intergenerational Pricing Gap (IPG)
The ratio RCV/market price; the factor by which markets underprice extraction. Measures how much cost severance has occurred in numerical terms. IPG = 33 means the market price covers approximately 3% of the true intergenerational cost. All empirical cases tested yield IPG ≫ 1.
Civilizational Substitutability Gap (CSG)
The gap between how replaceable a resource is for current industry versus long-term civilizational survival. Rare earths may be substitutable for consumer electronics but irreplaceable for space travel. CSG(R) = S_max(industrial) − S_max(existential). High-CSG resources warrant strategic in-situ preservation.
Asymmetric Regret Principle
When costs cannot be precisely quantified, default to what is reversible. Preserving something you didn't need is cheap; extracting something irreplaceable is not. "We can always extract later. We can never un-extract." Applies across all uncertainty ranges for high-CSG resources.

Eight-Tier FGC Decomposition — Canonical v10

Per Decision A ratified April 19, 2026. Each tier maps to the abundance dimension whose stripping makes it visible — this mapping is what makes the tiers falsifiable. All tier names are canonical; abbreviations (e.g., "Actuarial Risk" for Tier 2a) are acceptable in prose after first use.

Tier 1 · Abundance dimension: Atmospheric + Ecological
Lifetime Survival Cost
Partially priced (acute threats only)
The cost of sustaining life itself — oxygen, water, food — where extraction directly threatens these systems across a worker's or community's lifetime. Distinct from acute emergency injury: Lifetime Survival Cost captures the long-duration attrition of basic life-support conditions (contaminated water supply, degraded air quality over decades, loss of food-producing ecosystems). Most starkly visible at the Mars limit, where all atmospheric and ecological abundance is absent. Present in Appalachia via groundwater contamination; in Baotou via multi-decade air quality emergencies. Maps to E(R, t) in the RCV formula.
Tier 2a · Abundance dimension: Institutional
Actuarial Risk Cost
Partially priced (acute injury only)
Renamed from "Individual Risk Cost" — Decision A, April 19, 2026. The actuarial framing is more precise: this tier prices a population-level statistical distribution of health outcomes, not individual harms. This aligns with how insurance, public health, and regulatory systems actually measure and absorb these costs.
The statistically expected cost of occupational and environmental health risk borne by workers and affected populations across the full extraction lifecycle — chronic disease burden, reduced lifespan, long-latency illness. Black lung in McDowell County; mesothelioma in Libby; elevated cancer rates in Baotou. The "actuarial" framing names the key feature: these costs manifest as population-level probability distributions, not individual certainties, but constitute quantifiable costs that insurance and public health systems must absorb without reimbursement from extractors. Revealed by stripping institutional abundance: without enforcement infrastructure, the extractor walks away and the public balance sheet absorbs the distribution. Markets price acute injury partially; long-latency actuarial risk is structurally excluded from extraction contracts.
Tier 2b · Sub-tier of Actuarial Risk Cost · Abundance dimension: Geographic + Temporal
Mission Failure Reserve
Rarely priced
The cost of an extraction operation failing to deliver its projected economic value to workers and communities who planned around it — premature mine closures, production shortfalls below promised capacity, bankruptcies that discharge pension obligations and cleanup costs while leaving communities stranded. Distinct from Actuarial Risk Cost (which prices health and physical harm) and from Community Transition Reserve (which prices the transition itself): the Mission Failure Reserve prices the broken promise and its cascading economic consequences. Structurally absent from most extraction contracts. "Mission Failure Reserve is structurally absent from most extraction contracts" is a strong line precisely because the failure is foreseeable and the reserve is never required.
Tier 3 · Abundance dimension: Temporal
Dynastic Labor Cost
Never priced
The cost imposed on a worker's lineage when their productive capacity is extracted below Full Generational Cost. Capital compoundsacross generations; labor is asked not to. "Dynastic" refuses this asymmetry: 120-hour work weeks extract not just the worker's time but their children's future, their family's stability, the compounding value that sustained and bounded work might have created. The Social Security $108 trillion figure is Dynastic Labor Cost made quantitative across 88 years of American workers. Revealed by stripping temporal abundance — intergenerational effects become visible only when extraction rates are seen from a multi-decade or multi-generational timescale. Never priced in extraction contracts.
Tier 4 · Abundance dimension: Temporal
Foreclosure Cost
Priced in RCV only — never in markets
The permanent loss of resource access to all future generations, weighted by substitutability probability. This is the core RCV term: [1 − S(t)] · U(R, t, Q(t)), integrated to infinity. Markets price Tier 1 and partially Tier 2a; they structurally cannot price Tier 4 because future generations cannot bid in present markets. Zero if perfect substitutes are achievable; maximum when the resource is irreplaceable. Appalachian coal extracted in 80 years rather than the 300+ years it would have lasted at measured rates foreclosed all 22nd- and 23rd-century optionality permanently.
Tier 5 · Abundance dimension: Geographic + Demographic
Community Transition Reserve
Never priced (decommissioning ≠ transition)
A pre-committed, independently held fund sized to finance the complete economic transition of any community whose existence became structurally dependent on an extraction operation. Calculated at extraction inception, held outside the control of the extracting entity, required to fund full transition when extraction concludes. Revealed by stripping geographic and demographic abundance: the community's immobility and concentrated dependence become visible when abundance is removed. Distinct from decommissioning funds (physical cleanup of the site only) and severance packages (individual workers only). The Community Transition Reserve funds the town — its institutions, its economic diversification, its population's ability to remain and rebuild. The most policy-actionable tier: can be added to extraction contracts without requiring the rest of the framework to be accepted first.
Tier 6 · Abundance dimension: Ecological
Ecological Carrying Cost
Partially priced (short timelines only)
The ongoing cost of ecosystem service degradation — lost pollination, disrupted hydrology, soil loss, aquatic food chain collapse — priced across the full ecological recovery timeline. Distinct from Foreclosure Cost (the absent resource itself) and from post-extraction cleanup (which is Tier 2b/Mission Failure domain): the Ecological Carrying Cost prices the continuing loss of living ecosystem services during the recovery period, which can span generations. The Chesapeake Bay oyster population, once filtering the entire Bay in three days, now filters it in a year — a permanent reduction in ecological carrying capacity affecting every downstream use. Partially priced under short regulatory timelines; structurally underpriced for multi-generational recovery periods.
Tier 7 · Abundance dimension: Demographic
Knowledge and Culture Cost
Never priced
The cost of accumulated community knowledge, skill, and cultural continuity lost when an extraction-dependent community disperses below the threshold needed to maintain collective expertise. Three generations of technical mining knowledge, traditional ecological knowledge, and social capital — accumulated over a century in McDowell County and destroyed when population fell from 100,000 to 18,000. Revealed by stripping demographic abundance: cultural transmission requires a minimum viable community size; below that threshold, knowledge systems collapse non-linearly. These costs do not appear in any ledger. The loss is irreversible in the same way a language death is irreversible: once dispersed below the critical threshold, the knowledge does not reconstitute itself when conditions improve.
Tier 8 · Abundance dimension: Political
Political Capture Cost
Never priced
The cost imposed on democratic and institutional systems by extraction industries using a fraction of their captured value to suppress honest pricing of their own costs — lobbying expenditures, regulatory capture, suppression of safety research, narrative capture through funded media, and broader institutional corrosion. Both a consequence of cost severance and a cause of its persistence: the industries that benefit from invisible costs invest in keeping them invisible. W.R. Grace concealed Libby asbestos contamination for 27 years; coal interests have spent billions suppressing the social cost of carbon. Revealed by stripping political abundance — the cost of maintaining honest democratic accounting infrastructure becomes visible when that infrastructure is deliberately degraded. Explains why all other tiers have remained underpriced: the parties benefiting from their invisibility have invested in keeping them invisible.

Key Revisions — v10 (April 20, 2026)

  1. Canonical eight-tier list updated per Decision A (April 19, 2026) — full replacement of pre-v10 tier names
  2. Tier 2a renamed from "Individual Risk Cost" to "Actuarial Risk Cost" — more precise framing, aligns with how public health and insurance systems price population-level health distributions
  3. Tier 2b (Mission Failure Reserve) formalized as sub-tier of Actuarial Risk Cost
  4. Abundance Inversion Test established as canonical primary name; "Abundance Test" is shorthand only
  5. Abundance Dimension added as standalone entry (distinct from Abundance Masking: masking = mechanism; dimension = dimension)
  6. Terminology corrections — "Value Capture" consistent throughout (not "Value Extraction")
  7. ESG removed — all references use CSG (Civilizational Substitutability Gap)
  8. V_civ, Solidarity Architecture, Departure Liability removed (retired under Option C)

Retired terms (not in this glossary): Horizon Cost (duplicates FGC), Immediate Survival Cost (pre-v10 Tier 1 name), Lifetime Health Cost (pre-v10 Tier 2 name), Community Stability Cost, Relationship/Family Cost, Opportunity Cost, Environmental/Ecological Cost, Existential/Meaning Cost (all pre-v10 tier names replaced by canonical v10 list above).
