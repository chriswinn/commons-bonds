What this workstream does

Trims Chapter 8 ("What Things Actually Cost") from a full-economy simulation to a single worked example with a brief aggregation signal pointing at the forthcoming Book Two. Moves the trimmed-out material — full-economy simulation, named-industry critique, GoFundMe / tax-gap / housing data clusters — to a Book Two folder as notes. Updates the publishing strategy doc to reflect the staged approach.

This is the uncomfortable workstream. The full-economy simulation is the most intellectually exciting material in the book. It is also what would get the book labeled as advocacy, which would retroactively label the whole framework as advocacy, which would reduce the framework's uptake, which would undermine the book's entire reason for existing. The two-book analysis concluded this with enough rigor that the argument should not be re-litigated here; it should be implemented.

The strategic logic, stated once

From the two-book analysis:

  1. Book One protects Book Two. Once the framework has years of intellectual circulation behind it, Book Two's opponents cannot dismiss the underlying framework as "advocacy." They have to engage with it. That is a structurally weaker position to attack from.
  2. Book Two protects Book One. Book One avoids being pre-labeled as advocacy by keeping beneficiary-naming for a separate volume. A reader who would engage with the framework but bounce off advocacy can engage with Book One, and some will be ready for Book Two when it appears.
  3. Timing allows institutional changes. By the time Book Two is ready, credentials (completed CRNA, PhD, research fellowship) or affiliations (university, foundation, NGO, think tank) provide defensive infrastructure that does not currently exist.
  4. Coauthorship becomes available. Book Two benefits enormously from a coauthor with economics credentials, policy research affiliation, or institutional standing. Book One does not require this. Writing Book One first creates the reputational base that makes coauthor recruitment for Book Two feasible.

The trim

What Chapter 8 should be after this workstream

  1. One fully-worked example through the eight-tier framework. Continue the coal case from Chapter 2. Walk through each of the eight tiers applied to McDowell County coal. Show what each tier priced at, why, and what the Full Generational Cost aggregates to. This demonstrates the framework's operational usability on a case the reader already knows.
  2. A brief aggregation signal. One paragraph, roughly: "This pattern, applied globally, implies a subsidy of approximately $10–15T annually. What happens when we price it honestly — the distributional consequences, the transition pathways, the universal floor that becomes fiscally possible when the subsidy is converted to explicit revenue — is the subject of a separate work." Signal Book Two's existence without writing it.
  3. The counterargument engagement that belongs in Chapter 8. Two of the nine steelmanned counterarguments land here:
    • "This is just degrowth." Response: repricing vs. contraction distinction; GDP vs. welfare distinction; the leaded-gasoline precedent.
    • "This would prevent developing-world industrialization." Response: differentiated transition timelines as a structural requirement of the framework itself; solar already cheaper than coal; leapfrog precedents.
    These belong in Chapter 8 because they are responses to full pricing, which is what Chapter 8 introduces.
  4. The transition to Chapter 9 (Renewable Imperative). The Renewable Imperative is the constructive vision. Chapter 8 ends pointing there.

Target length after trim: 4,000–5,000 words. The Make It Better doc had Chapter 8 scaffolded at ~5,000–6,000 words covering the full simulation. After the trim, a single worked example + aggregation signal + counterarguments + transition fits in 4,000–5,000.

The uncomfortable truth, restated once

Your intellectual contribution may be most powerful if you write the framework that lets others do the political work safely, rather than trying to do both the framework and the politics yourself. Sometimes the most valuable thing an individual author can do is hand the tools to people with more resources and let them do the dangerous work. That is not diminishment. It is strategic humility about what one person in your current position can accomplish without endangering themselves.

This reframes the project identity from "I will fix this" to "I will build the instrument others use to fix this." Sit with it before executing the trim. If after sitting with it you decide the framework-only framing is correct, the trim is straightforward. If you decide you want both framework and politics in one book, revisit Decision D in Workstream 01 before proceeding.

Watch items

  • Do not half-trim. Either commit to the trim or don't. A half-trimmed Chapter 8 — worked example plus partial simulation — carries the Book Two exposure without the Book Two persuasive weight. Either/or.
  • Do not delete the trimmed material. It moves to /_One Day Maybe/Book-Two-Subsidy-Economy/. Everything is source material for the book that may get written later.
  • Do not lose the aggregation signal sentence. "This pattern, applied globally, implies a subsidy of approximately $10–15T annually; what happens when we price it honestly is the subject of a separate work." This single sentence preserves the "jaw-dropping insight" without requiring Book One to deliver the full blueprint.
  • Do not expand the single worked example into a second case study. One example, extended through the eight tiers. The extension is the chapter's main work.
  • Do not describe the forthcoming Book Two in detail. One sentence signal, no more. Overdescribing a not-yet-written book invites dismissal.
  • Do not change Chapter 9. The Renewable Imperative stays as planned — it is the constructive vision Chapter 8 transitions into.
