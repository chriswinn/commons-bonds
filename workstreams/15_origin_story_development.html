Workstream 15 — Origin Story Development

Commons Bonds project · Prepared April 19, 2026 · Personal foundation for framework credibility · Essential for Chapter 1 and closing

What this workstream is

Readers want to know how you arrived at this framework. Not the abstract intellectual genealogy (Harvey, Ostrom, Rawls) but the personal one. What moment made you see the gap between price and cost? The most enduring frameworks in social science have origin stories that give readers reason to trust the author's perspective. This workstream develops your personal narrative of discovery.

Current architecture after Workstream 01 decisions:
  • Two Pillars: (1) Cost Severance — naming the mechanism. (2) Residual Commons Value (RCV) — pricing what was unpriceable.
  • Two Methodologies: (1) Abundance Inversion Test — discovery. (2) Universality Test — validation.
  • Personal positioning: Healthcare IT executive who saw institutional cost externalization, chose nursing over continued consulting, lives on sailboat in Hampton.

Your structural position and credibility

Why your background matters for this framework:
  • Inside view of cost externalization: Healthcare IT consulting showed you how institutions manage costs by pushing them onto whoever can't push back
  • Personal cost severance experience: Left IT consulting despite financial success because you were absorbing personal costs the work imposed
  • Understanding of both sides: Corporate decision-making logic AND impacts on communities and individuals
  • Choice against extraction: Nursing career prioritizes care over maximum extraction of value
  • Sailing/waterman connection: Direct experience with sustainable vs. extractive approaches to natural resources

Key narrative moments to develop

Moment 1: The Plane (Chapter 1 opening)

Current version: Healthcare IT executive on plane, marriage conversation, personal cost that was yours alone to carry.

Deepen this moment:
  • The work context: What exactly were you doing in healthcare IT? Which systems? What costs were being managed and how?
  • The personal realization: What made you recognize that your personal cost (marriage strain, travel exhaustion, work stress) wasn't being captured in the consulting fee?
  • The structural insight: When did you first see this as a pattern rather than personal complaint?

Framework connection: This is cost severance at the individual scale. The client captured the value of your work; you bore the cost to your health, relationships, and personal life. The contract didn't price what it actually cost you to deliver.

Moment 2: The Recognition (early Chapter 1)

Narrative need: The moment when individual experience became structural understanding.

Potential scenes to develop:
  • Healthcare system cost management: Specific project where you saw how hospitals externalize costs onto patients, families, or communities
  • Client interaction: Conversation where the structural logic became clear — "We need to manage costs" meant "We need to push costs somewhere else"
  • First use of the language: When did you first think or say "cost severance" or recognize the mechanism?

Framework connection: The same mechanism operating at institutional scale. Hospitals sever costs onto families (unpaid care work), patients (travel for treatment), communities (closure of local facilities). The pattern is identical to what extractive industries do to extraction communities.

Moment 3: The Choice (mid Chapter 1)

Narrative need: Decision to leave IT consulting for nursing, framed as refusing to participate in cost severance.

Story elements to develop:
  • The financial sacrifice: What you gave up to make the switch
  • The value alignment: Why nursing represents the opposite of cost severance
  • The personal integrity question: Could you continue participating in systems whose costs you now understood?

Framework connection: Nursing is care work — absorbing costs rather than severing them. The career choice embodies the alternative to cost severance at the personal level. This gives you moral standing to critique cost severance at larger scales.

Moment 4: The Sailboat (Chapter 1 extension)

Narrative need: Living on sailboat in Hampton as embodiment of sustainable resource use.

Connections to develop:
  • Sustainable vs. extractive relationship with water: Sailing works with natural systems rather than depleting them
  • Community of watermen: Direct observation of people whose livelihood depends on sustainable resource management
  • Chesapeake Bay decline: Personal witness to cost severance operating on renewable resources

Framework connection: The Bay is the renewable resource case study. You're not an outside academic studying it — you're someone who chose to live in relationship with it and can see what sustainable vs. extractive approaches look like in practice.

Moment 5: The Grandfather (Chapter 10 callback)

Narrative need: Connection to grandfather at NASA Langley as model maker in wind tunnels.

Story development needed:
  • What he actually did: Specific work creating precise instruments that measured what the real world does
  • The lesson learned: Build small, precise instruments that tell you how things actually work
  • The inheritance: Commons Bonds continues his work — building instruments that measure what extraction actually costs

Framework connection: The model makers in the wind tunnels built instruments of honest measurement. They didn't tell engineers what airplanes should look like — they told them what different designs actually do. Commons Bonds is that kind of instrument for resource extraction.

Thematic development across the narrative

Progression of understanding

  1. Personal experience: Individual cost severance (consulting work)
  2. Institutional pattern: Organizational cost severance (healthcare systems)
  3. Systemic recognition: Extractive industry cost severance (McDowell County, Chesapeake Bay)
  4. Universal framework: Cost severance as general mechanism (Mars test)
  5. Actionable response: Instruments for honest measurement (grandfather's legacy)

Credibility building throughout

  • Inside knowledge: You've been inside the machine and understand how it works
  • Personal cost: You've paid the price for refusing to participate in cost severance
  • Lived experience: You've seen the mechanism operating at multiple scales
  • Value alignment: Your life choices embody the alternative to cost severance

Integration with framework development

Personal story must parallel intellectual story:
  • Chapter 1: Personal discovery parallels introduction of cost severance concept
  • Chapters 2-3: Case studies connect to your direct experience (healthcare systems, Chesapeake Bay)
  • Chapter 6: Mathematical framework reflects precision you learned to value from grandfather
  • Chapter 10: Closing returns to personal foundation but now with universal implications

Voice and tone considerations

What to avoid

  • Heroic narrative: You're not the person who discovered a hidden truth others missed
  • Conversion story: This isn't about seeing the light but about building tools
  • Academic distance: Don't retreat into abstract analysis when personal story gets difficult
  • Self-congratulation: The story serves the framework, not your personal brand

What to emphasize

  • Honest grappling: Real person working through real problems and making difficult choices
  • Institutional understanding: Someone who has worked inside systems and understands their logic
  • Personal stakes: Someone whose life choices reflect framework principles
  • Intellectual humility: Framework as ongoing work, not final answer
