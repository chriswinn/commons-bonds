## The landing (to be written in your voice)

The villain-analysis source file proposes that Chapter 10 should land on a specific closing sentence, roughly:

> Establish the prior claim of future generations on commons resources as a first principle of political economy, expressible as a financial instrument, enforceable wherever institutions exist to enforce it, and nameable as a debt wherever they do not.

The prose leading into that sentence does three things:

1. **Names the villain correctly.** Not any specific actor. Not any generation. Not capitalism or socialism as systems. The villain is the deliberate or accidental absence of institutional legibility infrastructure — the accounting, budgeting, and reporting choices that make intergenerational cost severance invisible to the people whose commons are being severed. Norway's genius was not fiscal virtue; it was building the Government Pension Fund Global mechanism that made cost severance visible before it happened. The U.S. had no such mechanism for Social Security, and the unified budget rendered the surplus consumption forensically untraceable.
2. **Arrives at the uncomfortable frontier.** Cost severance at the intergenerational scale may be structurally inevitable in democratic systems that operate on election cycles shorter than the compounding horizon of the resources they manage. If that is true, the institutional prescription is one of three: change the time horizon of democratic accountability (extraordinarily difficult), remove certain intergenerational commons from democratic control entirely (politically explosive, arguably what Norway did), or price the severance explicitly and in advance — which is what Commons Bonds as a financial instrument does. The third path is the most original contribution the framework makes.
3. **Explains why this survives even when institutions fail.** Commons Bonds names the debt even when no one is enforcing payment. Human rights frameworks name rights violated constantly. Commons Bonds names obligations breached constantly. The naming matters. It changes what future generations can demand when conditions allow.

## Watch items

- **Do not let Chapter 10 become a policy manual.** The villain-analysis prescription is one paragraph, maybe two. The chapter's primary work is emotional and philosophical — the common bonds resolution, the miner / waterman / colony administrator identification, the grandfather at Langley. The prescription lands *into* those, not instead of them.
- **Do not resurrect retired terms in prose.** "Solidarity architecture" was retired because it was vague and not falsifiable. If the impulse to use it returns, it probably means the sentence wants to say "shared identity" or "common bonds" or "institutional legibility" — each of which is more precise.
- **Do not make the villain explicitly structural in a way that lets the reader off the hook.** The villain is institutional absence, but the stress-test (Hole 2 in the villain analysis) is clear: contemporaneous voters had some consent, and intergenerational cost severance has structural consent problems that are harder. The chapter acknowledges this honestly rather than dodging it.
- **Do not conflate Chapter 10 with the author's note.** The Author's Note: On Wind Tunnels and AI is a separate document. The grandfather-at-Langley image threads both, but they are distinct pieces.
- **Do not attempt to mention every earlier chapter in the close.** Pick two or three images: the miner, the waterman, the harbor at Hampton, the wind tunnels. Let them do the work.
