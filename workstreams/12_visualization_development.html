What this workstream does

The backtesting results, three-model convergence, CSG ranking, and sensitivity analysis cry out for charts, tables, and diagrams. Well-designed figures communicate in seconds what paragraphs of prose struggle to convey. But the original scope for this workstream conflated three different jobs on different budgets and different timelines. This revision splits them.

The controlling insight: professional visualization design is a post-contract operation. Publishers have specific print and digital specifications that cannot be anticipated pre-contract (dimensions, color profiles, font licensing, accessibility palette, in-house design overrides). Serious trade and academic publishers frequently redo author-supplied figures to match house style regardless of quality. Commissioning a $2,000–$5,000 design package before a publisher is attached is one of the classic self-publishing-pattern mistakes that looks like seriousness and is actually wasted capital.

What is needed pre-contract is a different, cheaper, much smaller package: data compilation, sketch-level figures, and one signature figure polished to proposal quality (most likely the IPG master table). Those are Phase 1.

Phase 1 — Pre-contract minimum

Phase 1 scope

Everything needed to (a) produce a credible proposal package for agents and acquiring editors, (b) provide the Chapter 6 integration (Workstream 03) with the data it needs to write around, and (c) support Noema / Berggruen / other near-term submissions that may reference the framework visually.

Deliverables

  1. Data compilation. Organize all numerical content from backtesting, case studies, and sensitivity analyses into a single working spreadsheet. This is project infrastructure; no design required.
  2. IPG master table (Figure 1), produced as a clean styled table. This is the single most citation-worthy visual in the book. A styled Google Sheets or Excel version with color-coded IPG ratio cells (green <5x, yellow 5–15x, orange 15–50x, red >50x) is fully adequate for the proposal. Cost: zero.
  3. Sketch-level concepts for the other essential figures (three-model convergence diagram, McDowell County timeline, CSG ranking spectrum, spatial cost severance map). Hand-drawn or rough digital; purpose is to let the proposal describe what the figures will look like, not to ship finished art.
  4. One signature figure polished to proposal quality. Likely the three-model convergence diagram, since the IPG table already does the heavy lifting at #2. Can be produced in Illustrator trial, Affinity Designer, or through a $200–$500 Upwork contractor for a cleanup pass.

Budget

  • Phase 1 cost ceiling: $500, and likely $0 if Chris handles the signature figure himself.
  • The IT-consulting and automotive backgrounds mean the Illustrator / Sheets / basic design work is well within existing skills.

Timeline

2–4 weeks, running parallel with Chapter 6 integration (Workstream 03) and proposal development. Must be complete before the proposal goes to agents.

Phase 2 — Post-contract production

Phase 2 scope

Does not begin until a publisher or literary agent is attached. This is the full figure package produced to publisher specs, ready to ship as final manuscript art.

Why this cannot be done pre-contract

  • Publishers have specifications. Print dimensions, trim sizes, bleed, color profiles (CMYK vs. RGB), font licensing, resolution requirements, accessibility palettes (color-blind friendly, screen-reader compatible alt text). These vary by publisher and often by imprint.
  • Publishers often have in-house design. Academic and serious trade publishers frequently have design teams who will redo author-supplied figures to match house style, or will insist on specific designers. Paying for a professional package pre-contract often means paying twice.
  • Capital is better deployed post-contract. Advance or grant money (if any) is the right source for this spend. Personal capital pre-contract is capital at risk.

Deliverables

  1. Full 5-figure Essential tier production (Figures 1–5 below) to publisher specs.
  2. High-value tier (Figures 6–8 below) if budget and publisher interest support them.
  3. Accessibility-compliant alt text and caption pass for each figure.
  4. Vector source files deliverable to publisher.

Budget

  • Phase 2 budget: $2,000–$5,000 depending on figure count and designer rate. If publisher provides design, $0.
  • Do not commit this budget until contract terms are known.

Timeline

6–8 weeks after contract signing, running parallel with final manuscript preparation.

Content: Visual spectrum showing major resource classes positioned by Civilizational Substitutability Gap, from coal (CSG ≈ 0) to helium-3 (conditionally maximum CSG). Color coded: green (extract freely), yellow (moderate reserves), orange (strategic reserves), red (maximum protection).

Content: Heat map showing cost concentration in Appalachia, with arrows showing coal distribution nationally. Value dispersing while costs concentrate. Potentially iconic if well-designed.

Watch items

  • Do not commission professional design before a publisher or literary agent is attached. If the proposal lands and an acquiring editor asks for finished figures, that is when Phase 2 starts. Not before.
  • Do not produce Phase 1 figures to publication quality. The signature figure is the exception; everything else stays sketch-level for the proposal. Over-polishing pre-contract is wasted effort that will likely be redone.
  • Do not lose the data compilation. The underlying spreadsheet is project infrastructure and is reused across workstreams. Keep it in a single well-named file, not scattered across chapter drafts.
  • Do not let figure design drive narrative. Figures supplement the argument; they don't replace it. If a figure isn't strengthening a specific paragraph, cut the figure before cutting the paragraph.
  • Do not skip accessibility. Even sketch-level Phase 1 figures should use color-blind-friendly palettes and should not encode critical information in color alone. Carrying this discipline into Phase 2 saves rework.
