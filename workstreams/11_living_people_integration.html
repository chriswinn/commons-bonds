Workstream 11 — Living People Integration

Commons Bonds project · Prepared April 19, 2026 · High priority for book credibility · Requires fieldwork

What this workstream is

The single biggest structural weakness in the current book draft is the absence of real people whose stories the reader follows. The framework has data, theory, and mathematical rigor, but lacks the human stories that made books like Evicted win Pulitzers. This workstream adds the flesh to the bones — interviews with real people who have lived cost severance personally.

Current architecture after Workstream 01 decisions:
  • Two Pillars: (1) Cost Severance — naming the mechanism. (2) Residual Commons Value (RCV) — pricing what was unpriceable.
  • Two Methodologies: (1) Abundance Inversion Test — discovery. (2) Universality Test — validation.
  • Book spine: Mechanism → Math → Universal Validation → Actionable Policy.

Target outcomes

Primary Goal: Each major chapter anchors its argument in one or two real people's stories told with names, faces, and lived detail. The reader cares about the argument because they care about the people first.

Specific integration points:

Chapter 2 (The Miner) — McDowell County

Target interviews: Former coal miner or miner's family member who lived through the boom-to-bust cycle. Ideal: someone who can speak to health impacts, community collapse, and the gap between coal company promises and actual outcomes.

Story arc needed: Personal cost severance — the health, community, and family costs they bore while the value went elsewhere. Not victim narrative, but agency and dignity within structural constraints.

Chapter role: This person's story becomes the human proof of the $100 barrel calculation. The IPG isn't abstract — it's the gap between what coal was worth and what this person was compensated for what they sacrificed.

Chapter 3 (The Waterman) — Chesapeake Bay

Target interviews: Working waterman or recently-retired waterman who witnessed the Bay's decline from abundance to scarcity. Ideally someone with 30+ years on the water.

Story arc needed: Renewable resource cost severance — watching harvest volumes crash while market prices stayed roughly flat. The widening gap between what the Bay could sustainably provide and what the industry extracted.

Chapter role: This person embodies the framework's application to renewable resources. The Bay could have been managed sustainably; it wasn't. The cost was severance onto the waterman and the ecosystem.

Chapter 4 (Norway) — Optional but powerful

Target interviews: Norwegian connected to the Government Pension Fund Global — politician who helped create it, fund manager, or citizen who understands what it means for future generations.

Story arc needed: Institutional resistance to cost severance — how a society deliberately chose to capture value rather than sever costs. What that choice required politically and culturally.

Chapter role: Existence proof that cost severance is a policy choice, not natural law. This person represents the institutional alternative.

Counterargument Integration — Extraction industry voice

Target interviews: Mine operator, energy company engineer, or industry professional who genuinely tried to do right by communities but couldn't make the economics work under current pricing.

Story arc needed: Sympathetic portrait of someone on the extraction side — shows the problem isn't bad people but missing instruments for pricing externalities.

Chapter role: Strengthens counterargument engagement by putting objections in a real person's mouth rather than presenting them as abstractions.

Interview strategy and approach

Priority order: McDowell County miner (essential) → Chesapeake waterman (essential) → Industry voice (highly valuable) → Norway contact (valuable if accessible).

Finding contacts

  • McDowell County: Local historical societies, United Mine Workers of America local chapters, West Virginia University archives, Appalachian studies programs.
  • Chesapeake watermen: Virginia Institute of Marine Science, Chesapeake Bay Foundation, working waterfronts in Tangier Island, Virginia Beach, Hampton.
  • Industry voices: Professional networks from IT consulting days, energy industry conferences, mining equipment suppliers.
  • Norway: Norwegian consulate, academic contacts at Norwegian School of Economics, Government Pension Fund Global public communications office.

Interview protocol

  1. Life story first: Let them tell their story before introducing the framework. What brought them to this work? What did they love about it? What did they lose?
  2. Structural awareness: Do they see the pattern? The gap between what they provided/sacrificed and what they received? How do they explain it?
  3. Agency and dignity: What choices did they make? What would they do differently? What do they want people to understand about their community/work?
  4. Framework validation: Does "cost severance" resonate as a description? Does it name something they experienced?
Critical ethical boundaries:
  • Never present anyone as a victim without agency
  • Honor the pride and skill in their work even while critiquing the system
  • Get explicit permission before using names and stories
  • Share relevant sections before publication for accuracy verification

Integration into existing chapters

Narrative technique

Follow the Evicted model: open chapters with the person's story, weave their experience through the analytical content, return to their story for chapter endings. The reader experiences the framework through their eyes.

Chapter modification approach

  1. Identify interview targets (this workstream)
  2. Conduct interviews with careful attention to story arc and framework validation
  3. Write person-centered opening for each relevant chapter
  4. Thread their story through existing analytical content
  5. Close chapters with their perspective on what the framework means for their community

Location integration

Geographic anchoring: Your Hampton location provides natural access to Chesapeake watermen. McDowell County is a day trip from Virginia. Use the sailboat context — you understand working on the water, dealing with natural systems, and the difference between extractive and sustainable approaches to common resources.

Personal positioning

You're not an outside academic studying these communities. You're someone who chose nursing over continued IT consulting, who lives on a sailboat, who understands the decision to prioritize meaning over maximum income. That choice embodies the alternative to cost severance — absorbing costs yourself rather than severing them onto others.

Success metrics

  • Emotional resonance: Does the reader care about these people as individuals?
  • Framework validation: Do their stories make cost severance feel real rather than theoretical?
  • Political bridge-building: Do extraction community members recognize dignity in their portraits?
  • Universal recognition: Can urban, suburban, and rural readers all see themselves in some aspect of these stories?

Timeline and dependencies

Critical path: This workstream must be completed before Chapter 2, 3, and 4 final drafts. The interview material shapes the narrative structure of these chapters fundamentally.

Estimated time investment: 2-3 months including contact development, interviews, transcription, and integration. McDowell County and Chesapeake interviews are highest priority.

Budget considerations: Travel costs to McDowell County, potential honoraria for interview subjects, transcription services if needed.

Risk mitigation

  • Access challenges: Have backup contact strategies for each target group. Local newspapers often know key community figures.
  • Exploitation concerns: Emphasize that this is about honoring their experience, not using their suffering. Offer to share any relevant book proceeds with community organizations.
  • Legal issues: Standard interview consent forms, clear attribution agreements, fact-checking protocols.
