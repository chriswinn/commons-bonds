Commons Bonds — C6 Decision Memo

Date: 2026-04-20 · Version 1.0.0 · Subject: commons_bonds_integrated_framework.html taxonomy reconciliation

RECOMMENDATION: Option (c-refined) — Collapse via manifestations pattern. The v10 RCV eight-tier decomposition is canonical. The older individual-level FGC taxonomy ("Immediate Survival Cost," "Relationship/Family Cost," "Existential/Meaning Cost," etc.) is retired as a parallel taxonomy. Its surviving phenomenological content is preserved by folding it into the individual-scale manifestations of the six scale-abstract RCV tiers already in the WS02/WS03 revision queue. The term "Full Generational Cost (FGC)" is retained as the umbrella name for the sum of all tiers — as already defined in eight-tier-v10.html — but not as a separate 8-tier list. This threads the needle the three posed options miss: it is neither erasure (a), nor parallel-taxonomy fragmentation (b), nor abstract reconciliation (c). It is structural reconciliation via an architectural move already in flight.

1. The question, restated

commons_bonds_integrated_framework.html uses an older 8-tier taxonomy:

  2. Lifetime Health Cost
  3. Community Stability Cost
  4. Relationship/Family Cost
  5. Dynastic Labor Cost
  6. Opportunity Cost
  7. Environmental/Ecological Cost

The v10 canonical RCV taxonomy (from eight-tier-v10.html, Decision A ratified) is:

  2. Actuarial Risk Cost (2a; formerly Individual Risk Cost)
  3. Mission Failure Reserve (2b)

  8. Knowledge and Culture Cost
  9. Political Capture Cost

The handoff posed three options: (a) retire FGC, adopt v10 everywhere; (b) keep FGC as a separate analytical layer; (c) reconcile as complementary lenses. The memo tests each against the ten-critic rigor battery, the Abundance Inversion Test, and the stated success criteria — then proposes a fourth option (c-refined) that emerges from the tests as clearly dominant.

2. The Abundance Inversion Test (gatekeeping)

The AIT, per eight-tier-v10.html, is the falsifiability criterion for tierhood. A proposed tier must become visible when a specific abundance layer is stripped and disappear when that layer is restored. Tiers that do not survive the AIT are not tiers — they are something else (prose concepts, phenomenological descriptions, or already-priced costs).

Before running the critics, we should ask: do the four "non-mapping" FGC tiers pass the AIT?

Relationship/Family CostNo (as tier)None distinctPhenomenological dimension of Tier 3 (Dynastic Labor) and Tier 7 (Knowledge/Culture). Does not become visible by stripping its own unique abundance layer.

AIT verdict: Three of four non-mapping FGC tiers fail the AIT and one (Immediate Survival) is redundant with Tier 1. The older individual FGC taxonomy was not built using the AIT methodology. This alone disqualifies keeping it as a parallel canonical taxonomy. It cannot survive the framework's own falsifiability standard.

This does not mean the content those tiers captured is worthless. It means the taxonomic status those tiers held is unearned. The content can (and should) be preserved elsewhere. The list-of-eight parallel structure cannot.

3. The four options (with (c-refined) explicit)

Option (a) — Retire FGC entirely. Delete the individual 8-tier list from integrated_framework.html. Use only v10 RCV vocabulary throughout. Phenomenological content from the older tiers is not preserved in any structured form.
Option (b) — Keep FGC as separate analytical layer with own naming. Two canonical 8-tier decompositions coexist: RCV (structural) and FGC (individual/phenomenological). Each has its own glossary entries, its own citations, its own application contexts.
Option (c) — Reconcile as complementary lenses. Keep both taxonomies. Add explicit mapping language ("at the individual scale, Tier X of RCV corresponds to…"). Retain both sets of tier names as co-canonical.
Option (c-refined) — Collapse via manifestations pattern. RCV 8-tier is the only canonical taxonomy. FGC is retained as an umbrella term (the sum of all tiers, already defined this way in eight-tier-v10.html) but not as a separate 8-tier list. The phenomenological content of the older FGC tier names (Relationship/Family, Existential/Meaning, etc.) is preserved by folding it into the "individual-scale manifestations" paragraphs being added to the six scale-locked RCV tiers (1, 2a, 3, 5, 7, 8) in WS02/WS03. The older tier list ceases to exist as a structure. Its content survives as prose within the canonical structure.

4. Ten-critic rigor battery

The ten critics validated for architectural decisions per eight-tier-v10.html: Academic Economist, Analytic Philosopher, AI Adversary, Writerly Editor, Future-Proofer, Novelty Critic, Falsifiability, Completeness, Libertarian, Leftist. Each is run below with strongest-form attack and verdict across the four options.

4.1 Academic Economist

Attack: "You have two 8-tier decompositions of the same object — the full cost of extraction. They disagree on four of eight categories. Which is right? If both are right, you're not measuring anything; you're storytelling. Peer review will not accept a framework where the author can shift to the alternative taxonomy when the primary one fails to fit a case. Either the older FGC is superseded and retired, or the newer RCV is not yet canonical. Pick one."
Defense under (c-refined): One canonical structural taxonomy; phenomenological dimensions captured as scale-specific manifestations, not as competing tiers. The framework says what it measures and how.
Verdict: Strongly favors (a) or (c-refined). Rejects (b) outright. Rejects (c) as "reconciliation without commitment."

4.2 Analytic Philosopher

Attack: "RCV and FGC operate at different ontological levels. RCV tiers are structural allocations (types of cost-bearing asymmetry). FGC tiers, as originally written, are phenomenological experiences (what the worker feels). Treating these as two lists of the same length labeled 'eight tiers' is a category error. The relationship between structural cause and phenomenological experience is a real philosophical question — but it cannot be resolved by running parallel lists in the same document."
Defense under (c-refined): Structure is canonical; phenomenology is a consequence of structure at the individual scale and is expressed as such ("manifestations"). No category conflation; the hierarchy is explicit.
Verdict: Rejects (b) and (c) as category-conflating. Accepts (a) as philosophically clean but notes it loses phenomenological content. (c-refined) is the only option that preserves content without the category error.

4.3 AI Adversary (reducibility)

Attack: "Run the irreducibility test on each FGC tier against v10 RCV. Immediate Survival Cost → reducible to Tier 1. Lifetime Health Cost → reducible to Tier 1. Community Stability Cost → reducible to Tier 5. Relationship/Family Cost → reducible to Tier 3 + Tier 7. Dynastic Labor Cost → identical. Opportunity Cost → reducible to Tier 4 + Tier 3. Environmental/Ecological → reducible to Tier 6. Existential/Meaning → phenomenological, not a tier. Result: eight of eight reducible to RCV or demoted to non-tier status. The older taxonomy is fully subsumed. It is not a separate lens; it is an earlier, lower-resolution draft of the same lens."
Defense under (c-refined): Accepts the reducibility finding. Preserves FGC content at the only place it is structurally appropriate — inside the individual-scale manifestation of the tier it reduces to.
Verdict: Strong support for (a) or (c-refined). Rejects (b) and (c) — parallel structure is empirically unearned once reducibility is run.

4.4 Writerly Editor

Attack: "Readers can track two or three central concepts through a long argument. You already have cost severance, residual commons value, mechanism/shield, the abundance inversion test, and eight tiers. You want to add a second eight-tier list with overlapping-but-different names? The reader will close the book. Worse, the reviewer will close the book. A framework that cannot be held in one memory across three hundred pages cannot enter discourse. The success criterion you set — vocabulary traveling beyond the text — dies the moment there are two sets of eight terms that do almost-but-not-quite the same work."
Defense under (c-refined): One list of eight. One glossary entry per concept. Individual manifestations are prose, not indexed entries. Reader cognitive load drops from sixteen labels to eight.
Verdict: Strongest rejection of (b) and (c) of any critic. Supports (a) or (c-refined). Mild preference for (c-refined) because it saves phenomenological texture that aids reader recognition.

4.5 Future-Proofer

Attack: "Ten years from now, which vocabulary is cited? Policy people, labor lawyers, journalists — they will cite what is compact, portable, and structurally precise. 'Community Transition Reserve' travels into a bill. 'Existential/Meaning Cost' does not. If the book ships with two taxonomies, the structural one will survive and the individual one will be forgotten, regardless of your intent. You will have sunk revision time into preserving vocabulary that discourse will discard anyway. Worse: having two taxonomies in print creates a citation hazard — secondary writers pick whichever names serve their argument, and the framework fragments as it spreads."
Defense under (c-refined): Ship with the vocabulary that travels. Preserve the phenomenological texture as prose, where it belongs, where it supports the reader's recognition without competing for the citation slot.
Verdict: Strong support for (c-refined) over (a). (a) loses recognition-supporting prose that helps readers apply the framework to their own lives. (b) and (c) create the citation hazard.

4.6 Novelty Critic

Attack: "Two separate taxonomies means two separate novelty claims. If both are Winn's original contribution, the claim is inflated. If the FGC taxonomy predates RCV and RCV supersedes it, say so explicitly and retire FGC. What you cannot do is ship with both taxonomies in the same framework document and claim each is a distinct contribution. Reviewers will read the integrated framework document, see two parallel 8-tier lists with different names, and conclude the author does not yet know what their own framework is."
Defense under (c-refined): One novelty claim: the RCV decomposition + AIT methodology. FGC is an umbrella term, not a second claim. The development history (FGC was an earlier draft; v10 is the ratified decomposition) is documented in the session log, not in the published framework.
Verdict: Decisively rejects (b) and (c). Indifferent between (a) and (c-refined), but (c-refined) satisfies the reviewer while preserving texture.

4.7 Falsifiability Critic

Attack: "If two taxonomies coexist and disagree on four of eight tiers, the framework becomes unfalsifiable by taxonomy-switching. Any case where the RCV structural lens fails to produce the predicted cost severance can be rescued by shifting to FGC and calling the cost 'Relationship/Family' or 'Existential/Meaning.' This is exactly the vice Popper identified: an explanatory scheme that cannot fail because it has two incompatible versions. The AIT you built to prevent tier proliferation is undermined the moment a second taxonomy exists that was never itself subjected to the AIT."
Defense under (c-refined): One taxonomy, AIT-validated. Manifestations are explications of the canonical tier, not alternatives to it. A case that fails to show cost severance at a canonical tier is a case that falsifies the framework, not a case that gets rescued by a synonym.
Verdict: Strongly rejects (b) and (c) as unfalsifiability risks. Supports (a) or (c-refined) equally.

4.8 Completeness Critic

Attack: "The older FGC taxonomy captured something the structural RCV misses: the lived phenomenological experience of severance. The miner's sense of diminished meaning. The waterman's broken family structure. The worker's sense that the tradeoff was never explained. These are real costs. If you retire them entirely under option (a), the framework loses the grip on individual experience that makes it recognizable to the reader who is supposed to apply it to their own life. A purely structural framework reaches the policy audience and loses the personal one."
Defense under (c-refined): Phenomenological content is preserved precisely — in the individual-scale manifestations section of the canonical tiers. The miner who reads the book will find the sense of diminished meaning described under Lifetime Survival Cost (psychological and social dimension) and Dynastic Labor Cost (lineage-level loss). The content does not vanish; its taxonomic status changes from "parallel tier" to "scale-specific manifestation."
Verdict: Decisively rejects (a). Supports (c-refined) over (b) and (c) because (c-refined) has a specific mechanism for content preservation, not just intention.

4.9 Libertarian / Property Rights

Attack: "Your framework already claims eight structural tiers — already a maximalist structure. Adding a parallel individual-level 8-tier decomposition doubles the claim surface. Every tier name is a political demand. Sixteen demands is not a framework; it is a manifesto. Either trim to the structural eight or admit this is a wish list and drop the pretense of parsimony."
Defense under (c-refined): Eight tiers, not sixteen. Parsimony preserved. Content depth via manifestations does not enlarge the taxonomic footprint.
Verdict: Supports (a) or (c-refined). Rejects (b) and (c) as scope creep.

4.10 Leftist / Anti-Capitalist

Attack: "Retiring the individual-scale FGC framing in favor of pure structural analysis over-institutionalizes the argument and loses the voice of the worker. The whole point of the framework is that cost severance is not felt by the capital-holder but is felt by the worker and the community. Option (a) erases the phenomenological grounding and hands the framework to economists, who will neuter it. Option (b) keeps the voice. Option (c) equivocates."
Defense under (c-refined): The voice of the worker is preserved in prose and in the individual-scale manifestations that make each tier recognizable. What is retired is a parallel list structure with different labels; what is kept is the content those labels tried to capture, now located inside the canonical framework rather than beside it.
Verdict: Splits between (b) and (c-refined) depending on whether the manifestations pattern is executed with enough phenomenological grain. If WS02/WS03 manifestation paragraphs are rich, (c-refined) satisfies. If they are thin, this critic returns to (b). This is the execution-risk critic.

6. The mechanism/shield architecture check

Per the standing architectural refinement (Decision A ratification), mechanism is structural allocation; shield is distance. Applying this lens to C6:

  • Mechanism side: Cost severance operates structurally — value capture to one party, cost bearing to another. The canonical taxonomy of severance must be a taxonomy of the allocation structure. That is what RCV is. FGC at the individual level is not a taxonomy of allocation; it is a taxonomy of experience downstream of allocation.
  • Shield side: The distances that prevent recognition are captured by the seven abundance layers, which generate the eight RCV tiers via AIT. FGC's tier names (Relationship/Family, Existential/Meaning) do not map to specific abundance layers.

This is an independent confirmation of the AIT finding: the older FGC taxonomy is not organized around either mechanism or shield. It is organized around phenomenology, which is a legitimate subject but not a taxonomic basis for the structural framework.
