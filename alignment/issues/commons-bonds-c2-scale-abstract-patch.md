## Canonical Scale-Abstract Language — Six Tiers

---

### Tier 1 — Lifetime Survival

**Core (scale-abstract):**  
The irreducible metabolic floor — the minimum resource throughput required to maintain a living system's biological viability across its natural lifespan. When extraction captures value without funding this floor, the cost appears as compressed productive years, elevated all-cause mortality, and shortened life expectancy at the population level. The mechanism operates identically whether the living system in question is a person, a household, a community, or a species.

**Manifestations by scale:**

| Scale | How Tier 1 appears |
|---|---|
| Individual | Food security, shelter, potable water, sleep, basic medical access |
| Household | Cumulative poverty trap when one member's survival cost is externalized to family labor |
| Community | Emergency-room displacement of primary care; public health burden on municipal budgets |
| Regional | Rural hospital closure, food desert formation, water infrastructure disinvestment |
| National | Workforce productivity loss, defense capacity erosion, demographic compression |
| Planetary | Species-level viability thresholds; civilization metabolic floor |

**Pricing status:** Partially priced in advanced economies through public health systems; systematically underpriced in extraction-intensive sectors and export-dependent periphery economies.

---

### Tier 2a — Actuarial Risk Cost

**Core (scale-abstract):**  
The expected-value cost of probabilistic harm — the population-weighted sum of adverse outcomes that a complete actuarial accounting would price into a transaction. When extraction captures value without absorbing the actuarial liability it creates, the risk is displaced to a counterparty who cannot price it, hedge it, or decline it. The mechanism is identical at every scale: value flows to the extractor; risk is socialized to whoever cannot exit.

**Manifestations by scale:**

| Scale | How Tier 2a appears |
|---|---|
| Individual | Uninsured medical events, occupational injury, disability onset |
| Household | Catastrophic out-of-pocket health expenditure; medical bankruptcy |
| Community | Local insurance market dysfunction; adverse selection pool formation |
| Regional | Rural underinsurance, occupational disease clusters in extraction zones |
| National | Medicare/Medicaid unfunded liabilities; workers' compensation burden; uncompensated care |
| Planetary | Pandemic preparedness debt; climate-attributable health burden; zoonotic spillover cost |

**Pricing status:** Partially priced through insurance markets and employer mandates; systematically externalized for long-latency harms (occupational disease, environmental exposure, climate health).

**Note on Tier 2b (Mission Failure Reserve):** Tier 2b is the catastrophic tail of Tier 2a — the low-probability, high-consequence events that actuarial pricing often truncates or excludes. See Tier 2b entry for sub-tier specification.

### Tier 3 — Dynastic Labor

**Core (scale-abstract):**  
The intergenerational cost of human capital formation — the resource investment required to develop a productive participant in the next production cycle. When extraction captures value without funding the reproduction of its labor supply, the cost is displaced to families, public institutions, and future production cycles. The mechanism is structural: extraction benefits from the labor commons while systematically defunding its renewal.

**Manifestations by scale:**

| Scale | How Tier 3 appears |
|---|---|
| Individual | Education, skills formation, apprenticeship, professional licensing |
| Household | Child-rearing cost, elder care, caregiver displacement from labor force |
| Community | School district funding gaps, public childcare deficit, youth infrastructure disinvestment |
| Regional | Workforce pipeline failure, brain drain from extraction-dependent economies |
| National | Human capital gap, credential inflation as cost-absorption signal |
| Planetary | Demographic transition costs in extraction-depleted regions; global human capital inequality |

**Pricing status:** Largely externalized. Education and childcare costs are borne by families and public budgets, not by industries whose production depends on the resulting labor supply. The RCV formula captures this as an unpriced input to extraction profitability.

---

### Tier 5 — Community Transition Reserve

**Core (scale-abstract):**  
The cost of maintaining social cohesion during structural economic change — the resource investment required to absorb displacement, retrain labor, rebuild institutional capacity, and preserve community viability when an extraction-dependent economic sector contracts, relocates, or becomes obsolete. When extractors exit without funding transition, the cost socializes instantly and often permanently. The mechanism is predictable: extraction creates path dependence; exit creates stranded communities.

**Manifestations by scale:**

| Scale | How Tier 5 appears |
|---|---|
| Individual | Retraining cost, relocation, extended unemployment duration |
| Household | Asset value collapse (homes, local businesses), debt burden during transition |
| Community | Tax base erosion, municipal service degradation, school funding collapse |
| Regional | Industrial corridor decline, infrastructure stranding, persistent unemployment zones |
| National | Rust belt dynamics, opioid crisis amplification in deindustrialized regions |
| Planetary | Petro-state transition risk, single-commodity economy fragility, global just-transition funding gap |

**Pricing status:** Systematically externalized. "Just transition" frameworks acknowledge the cost category but have produced no reliable pricing mechanism. In the RCV model, this tier is the most politically visible but least reliably bonded cost.

---

### Tier 7 — Knowledge and Culture

**Core (scale-abstract):**  
The cost of preserving, transmitting, and renewing the non-material commons — language, memory, craft, practice, art, and institutional knowledge — that defines the possibility space for future problem-solving and collective meaning-making. Extraction that erases cultural commons closes options it cannot price and destroys value it cannot restore. The mechanism: extraction monetizes the material substrate of culture (land, airwaves, buildings, time) without funding the cultural commons it displaces.

**Manifestations by scale:**

| Scale | How Tier 7 appears |
|---|---|
| Individual | Loss of craft knowledge, language displacement, intergenerational memory rupture |
| Community | Cultural institution defunding, library closures, arts desert formation |
| Indigenous | Irreversible language extinction, ceremony discontinuity, traditional ecological knowledge loss |
| Regional | Archive degradation, museum funding collapse, local journalism extinction |
| National | Epistemic commons erosion, institutional memory loss, democratic deliberation cost |
| Planetary | Biodiversity of ideas — global epistemic commons loss; foreclosure of future problem-solving options |

**Pricing status:** Largely unpriced. Cultural preservation is treated as discretionary spending in most public budgets, despite its function as irreversible-option-value commons. The RCV formula treats this as a long-horizon externality tail.

---

### Tier 8 — Political Capture

**Core (scale-abstract):**  
The cost of governance distortion — the resource investment required to restore honest institutional accounting when extraction industries have successfully captured regulatory, legislative, or judicial processes. Political capture is the *shield mechanism* at its most systematic: when extraction can write the accounting rules, all other tier costs can be rendered formally invisible. The cost of capture includes not only the resources spent on lobbying and regulatory arbitrage, but the compounded cost of all tier pricing suppression that capture enables.

**Manifestations by scale:**

| Scale | How Tier 8 appears |
|---|---|
| Firm | Lobbying expenditure, revolving-door personnel costs, regulatory arbitrage investment |
| Municipal | Zoning capture, local utility regulatory failure, permitting corruption |
| State | Legislative capture, weakened enforcement capacity, agency underfunding by captured legislature |
| National | Agency capture (EPA, OSHA, SEC, FDA), subsidy entrenchment, accounting standard manipulation |
| International | Treaty capture, carbon accounting manipulation, trade agreement regulatory chill |
| Planetary | IPCC report suppression, climate negotiation obstruction, multilateral institution capture |

**Pricing status:** Essentially unpriced as a cost category. Political capture is typically measured only as a firm expenditure (lobbying cost), not as a socialized cost of governance distortion. In the RCV model, Tier 8 is the cost that suppresses the B term (bond value) toward zero — making it the meta-tier that enables all other externalization.

**Note on mechanism/shield relationship:** Tier 8 is both a cost tier *and* the primary institutional expression of the shield mechanism (distance). When political capture is active, the distance between the site of harm and the site of accounting is legislated rather than merely structural. See C3 content patch for mechanism/shield architecture detail.
