**What this is:** A coherent framework that produces calculable, testable numbers from three independent methods, connects cleanly to the existing Commons Bonds vocabulary, and can be validated across diverse cases. The kind of structure an economist could engage with seriously — not because the formula is settled, but because it's falsifiable.

**What this isn't:** A solved problem. The parameters σ and V_option require empirical estimation that doesn't currently exist at scale. The formula is a functional form that behaves correctly at known boundary conditions, but the specific shape (the exponent α, the multiplicative structure) is a modeling choice, not a derivation from first principles. An economist would want to see why this functional form rather than another one that also satisfies the boundary conditions.

**Why that's fine for the project:** The essay and even the book don't need a solved formula. They need a framed problem. The contribution is demonstrating that the pricing problem exists, naming it, and showing that the absence of a solution has real and measurable consequences. The formal model makes the argument rigorous enough to engage economists. It doesn't need to be the final word. It needs to be a credible first word.

**Practical advantage of the three-method triangulation:** It can be presented in the essay in one or two paragraphs without the math, using Norway as the intuitive anchor, and reserve the formal expression for a technical appendix or follow-up paper. The argument works at both levels — for a general audience and for specialists — which is the dual-register structure the project needs.
