The Drive used *Commons Bonds* as the book title. The April 19 session produced a conceptual shift: *commons bonds* may also function as the **master term** for the entire framework — the thing the mechanism destroys — rather than just a title.

**The distinction that matters:** Every other candidate term — cost severance, accountability severance, bond severance, departure liability — names what happens *when the mechanism fires*. *Commons bonds* names *what the mechanism destroys*. That is a fundamentally different rhetorical position, and potentially a more powerful one.

## The double meaning doing real work

- **The bonds owed to the commons** — obligations, debts, duties created by extraction. The legal/moral instrument that should exist but doesn't.
- **The bonds within a commons** — the connective tissue of community, the shared future, the relationships that extraction dissolves.

Both meanings are present simultaneously without forcing it. The title is intentionally one letter from *common bonds* — the thing we all share that the mechanism severs.

## Proposed conceptual architecture

If *commons bonds* is the master concept, other terms become components beneath it:

| Term | Role |
|---|---|
| **Commons bonds** | The natural obligations extraction creates — currently without legal standing or enforcement mechanism. What the system is designed to sever and sidestep. |
| **Cost severance / bond severance** | The mechanism. The knife. The act of cutting obligation from profit. |
| **Residual commons value (RCV)** | The measurement of what severed bonds actually cost. How to price permanent foreclosure. |
| **Commons architecture** | Institutional design that makes severance structurally impossible. The solution. (Replaces earlier term "solidarity architecture.") |

**Note on "commons architecture":** suggested in this session as a replacement for *solidarity architecture*. It ties the solution directly to what is being protected and creates a cleaner vocabulary family: commons bonds → commons value → commons architecture.
