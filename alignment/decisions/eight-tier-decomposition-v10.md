# Commons Bonds – Eight-Tier Decomposition and Abundance Inversion Test

**Proposed addition to Chapter 6 (Three Ways of Counting) and Technical Appendix**  
**Session:** April 19, 2026

## Purpose

Residual Commons Value (RCV) as currently formalized in the Technical Appendix is a single integral representing the substitutability-weighted foreclosure cost plus the externality tail. This is mathematically correct but operationally opaque to a non-economist reader. The decomposition below makes RCV legible at the individual level — a coal miner, a nurse, an asteroid miner, any worker — by breaking it into eight named, separable components.

Additionally, this section introduces the **Abundance Inversion Test** as the methodological principle that generates tiers rather than allowing them to proliferate arbitrarily. This prevents the framework from becoming a growing list of ad-hoc costs and grounds each tier in a falsifiable test.

## The Methodological Principle: Abundance Inversion Test

Earth hides most cost severance tiers behind layers of abundance. Oxygen in the atmosphere is not priced because it is functionally unlimited. A remote mining town with contaminated water is not priced into coal because most consumers never see it. The reduced lifespan of miners is not priced into the market rate because the miners are geographically and demographically separated from the consumers whose electricity they powered.

The asteroid miner scenario is clarifying not because it is exotic but because it strips every layer of abundance simultaneously. When abundance is removed, the tiers become visible. When abundance is restored, they disappear back into invisibility — but the costs themselves do not disappear. They are simply borne by parties who have no mechanism to price them.

The Abundance Inversion Test, stated formally:

> To identify hidden tiers in any extraction transaction, strip each layer of abundance sequentially and ask what cost becomes visible. A cost that becomes visible when a specific abundance is stripped, and disappears when that abundance is restored, is a tier. A cost that is visible regardless of abundance layer is already priced by existing frameworks. A cost that does not become visible under any abundance stripping is not a tier — it is something else.

This test serves three functions. It prevents tier proliferation by requiring each proposed tier to survive a specific falsifiable test. It generates new tiers systematically rather than by ad-hoc addition. It demonstrates why the framework is universal — because the same abundance-stripping methodology applied to any commons on any world produces the correct tier set for that context.

### The Abundance Layers

Seven abundance layers have been identified. Each corresponds to specific tiers that become visible when the layer is stripped.

| Abundance Layer | What Hides It | Tiers Revealed When Stripped |
|---|---|---|
| Atmospheric | Functionally infinite atmosphere | Oxygen cost, radiation shielding cost, atmospheric processing cost |
| Geographic | Ability to travel, relocate, return home | Spatial displacement cost, transport cost, community immobility cost |
| Temporal | Extraction rates slower than regeneration or substitution | Rate-of-extraction cost, acceleration premium, temporal displacement cost |
| Institutional | Legal enforcement infrastructure | Legibility cost, enforcement cost, accountability mechanism cost |
| Demographic | Communities large enough to sustain knowledge transmission | Knowledge and culture cost, apprenticeship cost, intergenerational expertise cost |
| Ecological | Functional ecosystem services (pollination, water cycling, soil fertility) | Ecological carrying cost, ecosystem service cost, biodiversity cost |
| Political | Democratic infrastructure that permits honest accounting | Political capture cost, suppression cost, narrative capture cost |

## The Eight Tiers (Decomposition of RCV)

The decomposition below integrates tiers identified through the Abundance Inversion Test. Each tier is named, defined, and mapped to the abundance layer whose stripping makes it visible. The original RCV formula remains mathematically correct; these are its components.

### Tier 1: Lifetime Survival Cost
*Previously called "Human Survival Overhead"*

**Definition:** The full biological, psychological, and social cost of working conditions across the worker's entire life, including latent health consequences that emerge after the contract ends. Not just the food, water, shelter, and healthcare provided during the period of extraction, but the cumulative impact on the worker's life expectancy and health-adjusted lifespan.

**Abundance layer:** Atmospheric and ecological. On Earth, most workers can breathe freely, drink water, and find shelter without pricing. The tier becomes visible when the worker's life expectancy is measurably reduced by the extraction work (black lung in coal miners, burnout and cardiovascular disease in knowledge workers, asbestosis in Libby residents).

**Currently priced by:** Partially priced by workers' compensation and life insurance, but only for acute injuries. Chronic and latent costs are systematically unpriced.

### Tier 2a: Individual Risk Cost

**Definition:** The true actuarial price of personal harm from the extraction work, including acute injury, chronic disease, and mortality risk — priced across the full exposure timeline rather than only the contract period.

**Abundance layer:** Institutional. Becomes visible when the institution's ability to absorb or suppress risk data is removed.

### Tier 2b: Mission Failure Reserve

**Definition:** The cost of the operation not returning its expected value, borne by workers and communities who planned around it. Includes the cost of being stranded (asteroid miner without fuel to return), the cost of a mine producing less than expected (community whose economy depended on projected revenues), and the compounding contingency stack (the cost of needing to try a second time, a third time, and so on).

**Abundance layer:** Geographic and temporal. Becomes visible when the worker cannot return home or try again cheaply.

**Currently priced by:** Nearly no existing framework. Mission Failure Reserve is structurally absent from most extraction contracts.

### Tier 3: Dynastic Labor Cost

**Definition:** The cost imposed on a worker's lineage by the extraction of their productive capacity at below Full Generational Cost. Includes lost earnings trajectory, lost intergenerational wealth accumulation, and the compounding cost of a generation's productive contribution being consumed in the present rather than held for the future.

**Abundance layer:** Temporal. Becomes visible when the worker's descendants can be identified and the wealth trajectory they would have inherited can be calculated. The Social Security case is Dynastic Labor Cost made fully quantitative across 88 years of American workers.

**Why the name matters:** Capital owners have always understood that their transactions affect their lineages. "Dynastic" names the same claim for workers. It refuses the asymmetry in which capital compounds across generations but labor does not.

### Tier 4: Foreclosure Cost
*Renaming "Future Option Foreclosure Cost" for accessibility; acronym FOFC retained for formal use*

**Definition:** The permanent elimination of all future uses the resource might have served, valued at a conservative compound rate from the moment of extraction forward. Weighted by the probability that no substitute exists for each future use.

**Abundance layer:** Temporal. The tier becomes visible when the future is made real — when specific future uses are imagined and costed rather than dismissed as speculative.

**Currently priced by:** Existing RCV integral prices this explicitly. This tier is the core of the framework's mathematical contribution.

### Tier 5: Community Transition Reserve
*Previously called "Systemic Reserve"*

**Definition:** A pre-committed, independently held fund sized to finance the complete economic transition of any community whose existence became structurally dependent on the extraction operation. Calculated at inception of extraction, held outside the control of the extracting entity, and required to fund full transition of the dependent community when extraction concludes.

**Abundance layer:** Geographic and demographic. Becomes visible when the community cannot simply absorb the loss of the extraction operation through internal redistribution or migration.

**Distinct from:** Existing decommissioning funds (which pay for physical cleanup only) and existing severance packages (which pay individual workers only). The Community Transition Reserve pays for the town, not just the mine or the miner.

**Policy-actionable:** This is the most concrete, immediately implementable policy recommendation in the framework. It can be added to extraction contracts directly without requiring the rest of the framework to be accepted first.

### Tier 6: Ecological Carrying Cost

**Definition:** The cost of the extraction process on the surrounding ecology, priced across the full recovery timeline. Distinct from Foreclosure Cost (which prices the absence of the extracted resource itself). Ecological Carrying Cost prices the secondary damage to hydrological function, biodiversity, carbon sequestration capacity, flood mitigation, pollination services, and other ecosystem services across centuries or millennia as appropriate.

**Abundance layer:** Ecological. Becomes visible when ecosystem services that are currently free become expensive or unavailable — which in most cases only happens after significant damage has occurred.

### Tier 7: Knowledge and Culture Cost

**Definition:** The cost of accumulated community knowledge, skill, and cultural continuity that is lost when an extraction-dependent community disperses below the threshold needed to maintain its collective expertise. Traditional fishing knowledge, mining expertise, land management practice, language, oral history. Once the carrying community disperses, reconstruction is often impossible.

**Abundance layer:** Demographic. Becomes visible when communities fall below the minimum demographic threshold for knowledge transmission.

### Tier 8: Political Capture Cost

**Definition:** The cost imposed on democratic and institutional systems by extraction industries that use a fraction of their captured value to prevent honest pricing of their costs. Includes lobbying expenditures, regulatory capture, suppression of safety research, narrative capture through funded media, and the broader institutional corrosion these activities produce.

**Abundance layer:** Political. Becomes visible when the democratic infrastructure that would permit honest accounting is itself compromised by the party benefiting from false accounting.

**Why this tier matters:** It is the tier that explains why the other tiers have remained invisible. Political Capture Cost is both a consequence of cost severance and a cause of its persistence. Adding this tier gives the framework an internal explanation for why honest accounting has systematically failed to develop.

## Integration with Existing Framework

The eight tiers are not a replacement for the RCV formula in the Technical Appendix. They are a decomposition of it. The integral still integrates. What changes is that non-economist readers can now see *what is being integrated* — which is the difference between a framework that can be engaged by the general public and one that can only be engaged by mathematically trained economists.

For Chapter 6 (Three Ways of Counting), the decomposition becomes a fourth section: "RCV Decomposed." It sits after the three convergent approaches (bottom-up damage accounting, real options, RCV) and shows how the same total can be broken into eight columns that a coal miner, a nurse, or an asteroid miner can identify in their own situation.

## For the Glossary

Proposed additions to the existing Vocabulary Glossary:

- **Full Generational Cost (FGC)** — The sum of all tiers that have systematically been excluded from the price of extraction. Companion term: **Horizon Cost** (accessible form).
- **Dynastic Labor Cost** — The cost imposed on a worker's lineage by below-FGC compensation.
- **Community Transition Reserve** — A pre-committed fund sized to finance complete economic transition of an extraction-dependent community.
- **Political Capture Cost** — The cost imposed on democratic systems by extraction industries that suppress honest accounting of their own costs.
- **Abundance Inversion Test** — The methodological principle used to identify hidden tiers by stripping layers of abundance and observing which costs become visible.
