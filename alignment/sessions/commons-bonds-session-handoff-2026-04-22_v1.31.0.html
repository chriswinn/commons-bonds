Why Kindred over Kinship (from this session's work)

  • Kindred covers chosen family cleanly. "Kindred spirits" is a stock phrase for chosen-family-close friends. "Kinship" in lay English reads blood-family-first.
  • Chosen family is book-worthy scope. Not just a process note. Weston 1991 is foundational sociological scholarship; a major target-reader population (LGBTQ+, adoptees, estranged-from-family, divorced-and-remarried, immigrant second-generation) recognizes chosen family as the realest thing in their lives. Framework mechanism (cost severance) operates on chosen family identically to blood family. Excluding via narrow "kinship = blood" framing fails Test 17 for these readers.
  • Chris's specific resistance: "Kinship feels too familial." This surfaces a real semantic gap — lay reading defaults family-narrow even though anthropological usage extends to fictive kin. Kindred sidesteps this.
  • Chris's intuition that Cohesion captures "community ties": partly correct for civic-generic community (neighbors, citizens, professional peers). But NOT correct for specific-identity-deep community (ethnic community, religious community as identity-not-just-weekly-service, chosen-family networks). The framework needs BOTH dimensions to price BOTH meanings of "community." Kindred vs Cohesion distinction: gemeinschaft vs gesellschaft (Tönnies 1887) — use this in methodology appendix only, not as canonical names.
