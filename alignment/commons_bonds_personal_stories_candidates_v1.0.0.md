## §0. Why this file exists

Personal stories surface organically during working sessions — often when a framework concept reminds Chris of a lived experience that demonstrates the mechanism at individual scale. Without a capture doc, these stories risk being lost between sessions or showing up as ad-hoc references in scattered guidance docs. This file is the collection point.

Each candidate is captured with:
- Story summary (enough to recognize and draft from later)
- Framework connections (where in the framework this story demonstrates mechanism)
- Potential placements in the book (which chapters could host it)
- Status (candidate / drafted / integrated / rejected)
- Author notes (anything Chris wants to remember when drafting)

Per the project's personal-stories-drafting gate: Chris drafts under Path F (no pre-fit-to-framework concern) — stories come first, framework-fit is tested per-story once 3–5 drafts exist.

### Candidate #1 — The commute trade

- **Captured:** 2026-04-24 by Chris Winn. Expanded 2026-04-24 with additional framework-connections surfaced in conversation.
- **Status:** candidate (framework-connections rich; first drafting candidate)
- **Story summary:** During Chris's IT consulting career, he moved to an apartment that saved $300/month in rent. He'd driven the route at 1am and the drive took 40 minutes. He didn't realize that during actual working hours the commute would stretch to 2.5 hours each way — totaling 5 hours per day of commute time for a 5–7 day/week job over the full year of the lease. The $300/month "savings" cost approximately 1800 hours of his year — enough time to learn a language, take a college class, or learn an instrument. At the moment of signing the lease, past-Chris didn't have the information that future-Chris would need. The value-extractor and cost-bearer were the same person, separated by an information gap across time. The apartment manager had no ill will — they were pricing the unit on standard real-estate logic. The rental market had no mechanism to price commute-time cost. The severance happened through information architecture failure, not through bad actor.
- **Framework connections (expanded 2026-04-24):**
  - **Spatial abundance mechanics at individual scale.** Spatial abundance (commute distance) produced abundance-dependent costs (time) that weren't visible at the moment of decision. Worked example that abundance-cost mechanics operate at individual scale, not just extraction-economy scale. (Builds on Chris's "Spatial Cost Severance was misnamed" insight 2026-04-24.)
  - **Information-asymmetric self-severance.** Cost Severance's core definition (value-extractor separated from cost-bearer) operates here across an information gap within a single agent. Past-self extracted value; future-self bore cost. Novel worked example for reaching knowledge-worker readers. No second party required for severance — the information architecture creates the separation.
  - **Framework as decision-time tool, not just ex-post analysis.** The story demonstrates the framework's most valuable application: *before the severance happens*, not after. Chris's comment 2026-04-24: *"I didn't have a way to realize the true cost to my life of living in that apartment. Even if I didn't use a math formula to compute all variables, the quick mental what would a day in the life of living here and getting to/from my office be like/entail..."* This is informal AIT + informal RCV operating at decision-time. AIT: *"if time were abundant, would the commute cost vanish?"* — yes → time-scarcity is the abundance condition → cost is scarcity-grounded → AIT passes. Informal RCV: mental multiplication of hours/day × days/week × weeks/year = 1800 hours. The framework gives readers a systematic way to see costs they'd otherwise miss — **a decision-time severance-detection tool.**
  - **No-villain framing — information architecture is the adversary.** The apartment manager wasn't extracting value maliciously; the rental market doesn't price commute-time cost; information about day-in-the-life-of-this-commute wasn't available to past-Chris. The severance happened in an honest, mutually-consenting transaction. This is the structural argument the book makes about McDowell County too (the coal company isn't evil; the pricing mechanism is broken) — now demonstrated at individual scale with a universally-recognizable example. Reinforces Ch 1's "villain who isn't a villain" thread directly.
  - **Time as the most severe commons.** Time cannot be recovered even if framework is applied retrospectively. The story illustrates why time-as-resource extraction is particularly devastating — the only defense is decision-time application, not ex-post correction.
  - **Generalization to other decisions.** The mechanism (information asymmetry + missing-price-instrument + cost-bearer-lacks-foresight) applies to: employment contracts without burnout accounting; medical decisions without long-term cost visibility; home purchases without true ownership-cost computation; marriages entered without understanding distribution of domestic labor; career pivots without seeing compounding costs. The framework's decision-time application is a broad domain the book can either foreground (new chapter / appendix) or thread through multiple chapters.
- **Potential placements (expanded):**
  - **Ch 1 — the 120-hour-week thread.** Fits naturally alongside the billable-hours arc. Could extend the consent-problem passage: "you had options a coal miner in McDowell County does not have — and you still ended up on a plane while your wife recovered from dying. You had information a coal miner doesn't have either — better information about your employment contract, your drive time, your apartment specs — and you still got extracted from because the information you had wasn't the information that would have changed the decision."
  - **Ch 7 — Mars colony parallel.** The asteroid miner's teleport to work is the inverse of Chris's 2.5-hour Earth commute. Same mechanism (spatial abundance producing cost severance), vector reversed. Ch 7 guidance could reference as Earth-analog.
  - **Ch 8 — individual-scale worked example.** Non-resource-extraction case demonstrating framework universality.
  - **Ch 10 (reflective) or an Applications section** — the commute story as lead for a "framework as decision-time tool" discussion showing informal AIT + RCV for major life decisions (employment, medical, housing).
  - **Possible new chapter / appendix** — "How to Apply the Framework Before Signing" (or similar): a practical section showing readers how to use informal AIT + RCV at decision-time. The commute story is the opening example; other worked examples follow. This would be a significant new book scope item — see Open Insight #9 for the decision.
- **Author notes:**
  - Personal narrative; no consent or privacy issues.
  - Top candidate for early drafting — rich framework-connections + multiple potential placements.
  - Ch 1 placement should coordinate with other Ch 1 guidance-doc items (wife's illness, plane, co-founded company, nursing pivot).
  - Demonstrates the "no villain, just information architecture failure" structural argument at a scale most knowledge-worker readers will recognize personally.
  - The decision-time-tool framing (informal AIT + RCV) may warrant its own treatment — see Open Insight #9.

- **Two framework-connections surfaced 2026-04-24 session** *(preserved verbatim for drafting continuity; author asked these be captured explicitly — the language itself was noted as worth keeping)*:

  **(1) Spatial-abundance mechanics operating in reverse at individual scale.** *"You identified Spatial Cost Severance as misnamed because it was actually abundance-cost mechanics (costs that appear or vanish with spatial distance). Your commute story is the same mechanics operating at a personal scale: a spatial-abundance condition (commute distance) became a time-scarcity cost (5 hours/day) that you didn't see at the moment of the lease-signing choice. Same mechanism, individual scale. This is a worked example demonstrating that Path F abundance-cost mechanics operate at the individual scale, not just the extraction-economy scale."*

  **(2) Information-asymmetric self-severance** (framework concept that doesn't need a named term but names a novel worked-example class). *"In classical Cost Severance, value-capturer ≠ cost-bearer (coal company ≠ McDowell County). In your commute story, value-capturer = cost-bearer = you, but at different times with different information. Past-you-at-lease-signing captured $300/month without understanding what future-you-at-commute-hour would pay. The severance is across an information gap within a single life. This isn't a new framework concept that needs a named term (the Cost Severance definition still fits — just with an informational rather than interpersonal gap), but it's a worked example that may help reach knowledge-worker readers who recognize the pattern in their own lives."*

- **How it helps the book** *(why this story is load-bearing not just illustrative)*:

  - **Audience-recognition bridge:** the commute pattern is the universally-recognizable knowledge-worker experience of severance. McDowell County is distant for most readers; a commute you underestimated is not. The story gives the framework a knowledge-worker beachhead — readers who recognize themselves in the commute story are primed to recognize the same mechanism at larger scales.
  - **Framework-universality demonstration:** the framework's Ring-1 mechanisms apply at personal-life scale, not only at extraction-economy scale. Cases like this demonstrate universality without requiring a sentence that CLAIMS universality — the worked example does the work.
  - **Consent-problem thread extension (Ch 1):** *"you had options a coal miner in McDowell County does not have — and you still ended up on a plane while your wife recovered from dying. You had information a coal miner doesn't have either — better information about your employment contract, your drive time, your apartment specs — and you still got extracted from because the information you had wasn't the information that would have changed the decision."* The commute story gives Ch 1 a second-layer consent analysis: not just options but also information.
  - **No-villain structural argument at individual scale:** reinforces the book's structural argument (the adversary is information architecture + missing-pricing-instrument, not bad actors). Readers who absorb this at individual scale transfer the pattern to industrial/policy scales more readily than readers who only encounter the argument at industrial scale first.
  - **Ch 7 Mars-colony inversion:** *"the asteroid miner's 40-minute teleport to work in zero-g is the inverse of a two-and-a-half-hour Earth commute — same mechanism (spatial abundance producing cost severance) with the vector reversed."* Chris's own asteroid-teleport thought experiment from the Spatial CS discussion already sets up this frame. Ch 7 guidance can reference the commute story as the Earth analog, strengthening both directions of the argument.
  - **Decision-time tool demonstration:** the story illustrates what "framework as decision-time tool" (Open Insight #9) would look like. A reader who absorbs the commute story learns what informal AIT + RCV at decision-time feels like without needing a dedicated how-to passage. If a how-to passage/chapter is written later, the commute story is the lead example.

### Candidate #2 — Neeraj / "Mirage" — IT mentor, visa-leverage, and the leverage flip

- **Captured:** 2026-05-01 by Chris Winn (live conversational dump in chat).
- **Status:** drafted (raw voice-to-text dump at `research/story-drafts/ch1_neeraj_2026-05-01.md`; awaiting review).
- **Story summary:** Chris's first regular IT job, age 23, in DC. The head of architecture & R&D — Indian-Canadian-American colleague named Neeraj (workplace nickname "Mirage") — pulls Chris onto the building's roof overlooking the Capitol Mall and offers him a cigarette. Over the next several years Neeraj becomes Chris's mentor and the primary source of his understanding of how the IT labor market actually works. Six-to-eight months in, Chris discovers Neeraj is paid one-third of Chris's junior-developer salary despite holding rare certifications worth millions in federal contracts — the company is exploiting his work-visa status (he can't quit without losing the visa and being separated from his wife and daughter). Neeraj teaches Chris the meet-or-beat strategy: always have your résumé out, always interview, leverage outside offers to extract market-rate compensation from employers. Multiple times Neeraj plays this game himself; the CEO matches each offer at the eleventh hour. The leverage finally flips when a competing company offers not just salary parity but full sponsorship of Neeraj's US citizenship process — *"not only pay you what you're worth; will also make it so no one can take advantage of you again."* The CEO matches the salary but refuses the citizenship support. Neeraj takes the other offer. Two weeks later the original CEO comes back desperate to retain him on the contracts; Neeraj negotiates a Saturday-mornings-only retainer at higher pay than his old five-day week — a brilliant inversion of the leverage that had been used against him.
- **Framework connections (preliminary; review-session refines):**

Methodology emerges through Neeraj's voice + Chris's behavioral acquisition of it across years; never codified in Chris's analytical voice. Mirrors the Pattern 2 register the framework requires for external layer.
  - **Leverage-flip moment as accountability-instrument-finally-arriving microcosm:** the CEO matches salary but refuses citizenship — that's the moment the framework's mechanism becomes visible. The CEO's refusal proves the constraint *was* the leverage; once the visa-as-leverage is removable, the CEO loses the asymmetry. Neeraj then reverses the asymmetry (Saturday-mornings retainer at premium rates).
  - **Villain-who-isn't-a-villain (per Ch 1 GuidanceDoc lines 70-93):** the CEO isn't malicious — he's responding to standard incentives, including the visa-leverage that made the labor extraction structurally available. The mechanism is the missing accountability instrument (work-visa-portability; fair-pay-for-equivalent-work enforcement); the CEO is the local optimizer.
  - **Origin-story function (per Ch 1 GuidanceDoc lines 102-108):** this is where Chris's understanding of the labor market's leverage-architecture forms. The framework Chris later names was already operating on Neeraj in his presence; Chris was learning its shape through observation of someone else absorbing the cost.
